# Clinical Entry: Myopia

**Title:** Myopia  
**ICD-10 Code:** H52.1  
**Synonyms:** Nearsightedness, Shortsightedness  

---

### 1. Definition and Overview
Myopia is a common ametropic refractive error of the eye where the optical power of the eye is too strong relative to its axial length. In a myopic eye, light from a distant object focuses at a focal point anterior to the photoreceptor layer of the retina rather than directly on it. This results in blurred distance vision while near vision typically remains clear, provided the object is within the eye’s far point.

---

### 2. Epidemiology
The global prevalence of myopia is increasing at an alarming rate, often referred to as the "Myopia Pandemic." It is estimated that by the year 2050, approximately 50% of the world’s population (nearly 5 billion people) will be myopic. Prevalence is highest in East and Southeast Asian urban populations, where rates among school-leavers can reach 80–90%. Risk factors include genetics (parental myopia), limited time spent outdoors, and prolonged intensive near-work activities.

---

### 3. Classification
Myopia is clinically categorized based on degree and etiology:

*   **By Severity:**
    *   **Low Myopia:** -0.50 D to -3.00 D.
    *   **Moderate Myopia:** -3.00 D to -6.00 D.
    *   **High Myopia:** Greater than -6.00 D or an axial length $\ge$ 26.5 mm.
*   **By Clinical Type:**
    *   **Axial Myopia:** Attributed to an increase in the eye's axial length (the most common form).
    *   **Refractive Myopia:** Attributed to changes in the refractive components (e.g., increased corneal curvature or lenticular nuclear sclerosis).
    *   **Degenerative (Pathologic) Myopia:** High myopia associated with progressive retinal/choroidal changes and posterior staphyloma.

---

### 4. Pathophysiology
The primary mechanism of myopia is **axial elongation**. During emmetropization, the eye coordinates its growth to match its optical power. In myopic progression, this process is disrupted.

*   **Peripheral Defocus Theory:** Current research suggests that while the central retina may be in focus, the peripheral retina often experiences hyperopic defocus (light focusing behind the retina). This peripheral blur serves as a biochemical signal for the sclera to remodel and the eye to elongate further to "reach" the light.
*   **Scleral Remodeling:** Myopia involves the thinning of the scleral extracellular matrix and a reduction in collagen cross-linking, making the posterior pole of the eye more susceptible to expansion.
*   **Biochemical Regulation:** Neurotransmitters such as dopamine, released by retinal dopaminergic cells in response to light, are believed to inhibit axial growth, explaining the protective effect of outdoor light exposure.

---

### 5. Clinical Symptoms
Patients with myopia typically present with the following:
*   **Distance Blur:** Primary complaint of blurred vision when looking at distant objects (e.g., whiteboards, road signs).
*   **Asthenopia:** Eye strain or headaches associated with squinting.
*   **Squinting:** The patient may squint to create a "pinhole effect," which reduces the blur circle on the retina and temporarily improves acuity.
*   **Nystagmus (Rare):** May be seen in cases of high congenital myopia.

---

### 6. Diagnosis
A comprehensive ophthalmic examination is required to diagnose and quantify myopia:

1.  **Visual Acuity:** Assessment of uncorrected distance acuity using Snellen or LogMAR charts.
2.  **Refraction:**
    *   **Manifest Refraction:** Subjective determination of the best sphere-cylinder combination.
    *   **Cycloplegic Refraction:** Mandatory in children and adolescents (using agents like Cyclopentolate 1% or Atropine) to paralyze the ciliary muscle and eliminate accommodative spasm (pseudomyopia).
3.  **Biometry:** Measurement of **Axial Length (AL)** using optical biometry (e.g., IOLMaster or Lenstar). Monitoring AL is the current gold standard for tracking myopia progression.
4.  **Ocular Health Assessment:** Dilated fundus examination (DFE) to assess for peripheral retinal degenerations, such as lattice degeneration, snailtrack degeneration, or retinal holes, which are more prevalent in myopic eyes.

---

### 7. Management and Treatment
Treatment is divided into optical correction and modern myopia control (progression management).

#### A. Optical Correction
*   **Spectacles:** Concave (minus) lenses to diverge light rays before they enter the eye.
*   **Contact Lenses:** Soft or Gas Permeable (GP) lenses. They provide better peripheral vision and eliminate the minification effects of high-minus spectacle lenses.

#### B. Myopia Control (Slowing Progression)
In pediatric populations, the goal is to slow axial elongation to prevent future pathology.
*   **Pharmacological:** Low-dose Atropine (0.01%, 0.02%, or 0.05%) drops administered nightly.
*   **Orthokeratology (Ortho-K):** Rigid gas-permeable lenses worn overnight to temporarily reshape the cornea, creating peripheral myopic defocus.
*   **Dual-Focus/Multifocal Soft Contacts:** Lenses like the MiSight® lens designed with treatment zones to signal the eye to slow growth.
*   **DIMS/HALT Spectacle Lenses:** Specialty spectacle lenses (e.g., Hoya MiyoSmart) utilizing Defocus Incorporated Multiple Segments to inhibit elongation.

#### C. Refractive Surgery (For Adults)
Once the refractive error has stabilized (typically after age 21):
*   **Laser In Situ Keratomileusis (LASIK)** or **Photorefractive Keratectomy (PRK).**
*   **Small Incision Lenticule Extraction (SMILE).**
*   **Phakic Intraocular Lenses (pIOL):** Such as the Implantable Collamer Lens (ICL) for patients with high myopia or thin corneas.

---

### 8. Complications and Prognosis
Unmanaged myopia, particularly high myopia, significantly increases the lifetime risk of sight-threatening conditions:
*   **Retinal Detachment:** Risk increases exponentially with axial length.
*   **Myopic Macular Degeneration (MMD):** Choroidal neovascularization and macular atrophy.
*   **Glaucoma:** Higher prevalence of primary open-angle glaucoma (POAG) in myopic eyes.
*   **Cataracts:** Earlier onset of nuclear and posterior subcapsular cataracts.

**Prognosis:** With early intervention and modern myopia management strategies, the rate of progression can be reduced by 40–60%, significantly lowering the risk of permanent visual impairment in later life.
