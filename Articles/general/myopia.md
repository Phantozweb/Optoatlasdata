# Posterior Vitreous Detachment

---

## Overview

**Posterior vitreous detachment (PVD)** is a common age-related condition in which the [[vitreous humor]]—the gel-like substance filling the posterior segment of the eye—separates from the [[internal limiting membrane (ILM)]] of the [[retina]]. This process represents one of the most frequent causes of acute-onset [[floaters]] and [[photopsia]] encountered in clinical [[ophthalmology]] and [[optometry]] practice. While PVD is often a benign, self-limiting phenomenon, it carries significant clinical importance because of its well-established association with sight-threatening complications, including [[retinal tears]], [[retinal detachment]], and [[vitreous hemorrhage]].

PVD is estimated to occur in over 75% of individuals older than 65 years of age. Understanding its pathophysiology, clinical presentation, diagnostic evaluation, and management is essential for every eye care professional, as timely identification of complications can be the difference between preserved vision and irreversible visual loss.

## Anatomy and Physiology of the Vitreous

The [[vitreous body]] occupies approximately 80% of the total volume of the globe, constituting roughly 4 mL in the adult human eye. It is a transparent, avascular, gel-like structure composed of approximately 98–99% water, with the remaining fraction consisting of [[collagen]] fibrils (predominantly [[type II collagen]], with smaller amounts of types V, IX, and XI) and [[hyaluronic acid]] (hyaluronan), a large glycosaminoglycan responsible for maintaining the gel state through water-binding properties.

### Structural Organization

The vitreous is not a homogeneous structure. It possesses a cortical shell (the **vitreous cortex**) that is denser and more collagen-rich than the central vitreous. The **posterior vitreous cortex** is of particular clinical significance because it is intimately adherent to the [[internal limiting membrane]] of the retina. The vitreous cortex is approximately 100–300 μm thick and contains a higher concentration of collagen fibrils and [[hyalocytes]]—specialized cells of monocyte/macrophage lineage that play roles in hyaluronic acid synthesis, phagocytosis, and wound healing.

### Vitreoretinal Adhesion

The vitreous is attached to the retina at several key anatomical sites, with varying degrees of adhesion strength:

- **[[Vitreous base]]**: The strongest attachment, a 3–4 mm wide annular zone straddling the [[ora serrata]] (approximately 2 mm anterior and 1–3 mm posterior). This attachment is so firm that it is virtually never separated without surgical intervention or trauma.
- **[[Optic disc]] margin**: A ring of adhesion around the [[optic nerve head]], known as the **peripapillary adhesion** or **Martegiani ring** (the space of Martegiani being the funnel-shaped bursa premacularis overlying the disc).
- **[[Macula]] / [[Fovea]]**: Adhesion at the macular region, which is clinically significant in conditions such as [[vitreomacular traction (VMT)]] and [[macular hole]] formation.
- **Retinal blood vessels**: The vitreous cortex adheres to the walls of major [[retinal vessels]], which explains the occasional occurrence of [[vitreous hemorrhage]] during PVD when a vessel is avulsed.

### Age-Related Changes

With aging, the vitreous undergoes two simultaneous degenerative processes:

1. **Liquefaction (synchysis)**: Enzymatic and oxidative degradation of hyaluronic acid and collagen cross-links leads to the formation of liquid-filled lacunae (pockets of liquefied vitreous, or **synchysis senilis**). By age 80, more than half of the vitreous may be liquefied.
2. **Aggregation (syneresis)**: Collagen fibrils aggregate into visible bundles and sheets, which cast shadows on the retina perceived as floaters.

These two processes—liquefaction creating fluid and syneresis causing structural collapse—are the fundamental prerequisites for PVD.

## Pathophysiology

The pathogenesis of PVD involves a complex interplay of biochemical, structural, and mechanical factors.

### Biochemical Mechanisms

The gel state of the vitreous depends on the interaction between collagen fibrils and hyaluronic acid molecules. Hyaluronic acid molecules are interspersed among the collagen network, maintaining spacing and hydration. With aging:

- **Hyaluronic acid redistribution**: Hyaluronic acid migrates from the gel phase into the liquid phase, destabilizing the collagen network.
- **Collagen cross-linking and aggregation**: Non-enzymatic glycation and oxidative damage promote collagen fibril aggregation, reducing the structural integrity of the gel.
- **Enzymatic activity**: [[Matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs)]], particularly MMP-2 and MMP-9, produced by [[hyalocytes]] and [[retinal pigment epithelium (RPE)]] cells, degrade the collagen and extracellular matrix components at the vitreoretinal interface, weakening the adhesion between the posterior vitreous cortex and the ILM.

### Mechanical Separation

As liquefaction progresses, liquid vitreous dissects through weakened areas of the posterior vitreous cortex, entering the **retrocortical (preretinal) space**. This process, termed **vitreoschisis** by some authors when it involves splitting of the vitreous cortex itself, leads to progressive separation of the posterior hyaloid face from the retinal surface.

The separation typically begins in the **perifoveal** region, where the vitreous cortex is thinnest, and extends peripherally. The final stage involves detachment from the [[optic disc]], which is often perceived by the patient as a sudden increase in floaters—classically a ring-shaped floater known as a **[[Weiss ring]]** (or **peripapillary glial ring**), representing the detached annular attachment of vitreous to the optic nerve head margin.

### Anomalous PVD

When the vitreous liquefies but the vitreoretinal adhesion remains abnormally strong (a concept championed by J. Sebag and termed **anomalous PVD**), the mechanical forces generated during eye movements and attempted vitreous separation are transmitted to the retina rather than resulting in clean separation. This can lead to:

- [[Vitreomacular traction syndrome]]
- [[Macular hole]] (full-thickness or lamellar)
- [[Retinal tears]] and [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]]
- [[Vitreous hemorrhage]]
- [[Vitreopapillary traction]]

## Epidemiology and Risk Factors

### Prevalence

PVD is exceedingly common. Autopsy and clinical studies have demonstrated the following age-related prevalence:

| Age Group | Approximate Prevalence of PVD |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| < 40 years | Rare (unless predisposing factors) |
| 40–49 years | ~10% |
| 50–59 years | ~27% |
| 60–69 years | ~53–63% |
| ≥ 70 years | ~63–87% |

The landmark study by Foos and Wheeler (1982) using autopsy eyes found complete PVD in 27% of eyes in the seventh decade and 63% in the eighth decade.

### Risk Factors

Numerous factors predispose to earlier or more complicated PVD:

- **Age**: The single most important risk factor.
- **Female sex**: Women develop PVD earlier than men, possibly related to hormonal influences on vitreous biochemistry (estrogen may have a protective role on vitreous collagen).
- **[[Myopia]]**: Myopic eyes undergo vitreous liquefaction earlier. Eyes with axial length > 26 mm have significantly higher rates of PVD at younger ages.
- **Prior [[cataract surgery]] ([[pseudophakia]]/[[aphakia]])**: Removal of the [[crystalline lens]] alters the biochemical milieu of the vitreous, accelerating liquefaction. PVD occurs in up to 56–59% of pseudophakic eyes within months to years of surgery.
- **Ocular trauma**: Blunt or penetrating trauma can precipitate acute PVD.
- **[[Ocular inflammation]] ([[uveitis]])**: Inflammatory mediators and enzymatic activity accelerate vitreous degeneration.
- **[[Nd:YAG laser capsulotomy]]**: Post-capsulotomy PVD has been reported.
- **[[Diabetes mellitus]]**: While diabetic vitreopathy involves abnormal vitreoretinal adhesion, diabetic patients may also develop PVD, sometimes complicated by hemorrhage.
- **Hereditary [[vitreoretinal dystrophies]]**: Conditions such as [[Stickler syndrome]], [[Wagner syndrome]], and [[Ehlers-Danlos syndrome]] predispose to early vitreous degeneration and PVD.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

The hallmark symptoms of acute PVD are:

1. **[[Floaters]]**: Patients typically describe the sudden onset of one or more floaters, which may appear as:
   - A single large floater ("cobweb," "ring," "fly," or "tadpole")
   - A shower of small floaters (may indicate [[vitreous hemorrhage]] or [[pigment cells (Shafer's sign)]])
   - The classic **[[Weiss ring]]** floater (ring-shaped opacity representing the detached peripapillary glial tissue)

2. **[[Photopsia]] (flashing lights)**: Caused by mechanical traction on the retina during the process of vitreous separation. Flashes are typically:
   - Brief (fraction of a second)
   - Located in the temporal visual field (corresponding to nasal retinal traction)
   - More noticeable in dim illumination or with eye movements
   - May persist for weeks as the vitreous continues to separate

3. **Visual field defect / curtain or veil**: This is an **alarming symptom** suggesting a complication such as [[retinal detachment]] or significant [[vitreous hemorrhage]], and warrants urgent evaluation.

4. **Decreased visual acuity**: Generally not a feature of uncomplicated PVD. If present, consider [[vitreous hemorrhage]], [[macular hole]], [[vitreomacular traction]], or [[retinal detachment]].

### Signs on Examination

- **Anterior segment**: Typically normal. Occasionally, [[pigmented cells]] may be seen in the anterior vitreous (**tobacco dust** or **[[Shafer's sign]]**), which is highly suggestive of a [[retinal tear]] and should prompt urgent [[dilated fundus examination]].
- **Vitreous**: Visible collapse of the vitreous gel, with a mobile posterior hyaloid membrane. The detached posterior hyaloid may be seen undulating with eye movements on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] or [[indirect ophthalmoscopy]].
- **[[Weiss ring]]**: A ring-shaped opacity visible in the anterior vitreous or mid-vitreous, representing the detached peripapillary glial tissue. Its presence confirms complete PVD from the optic disc.
- **Fundus**: In uncomplicated PVD, the retina appears normal. However, careful examination must rule out [[retinal tears]], [[retinal holes]], [[retinal detachment]], [[vitreous hemorrhage]], and [[vitreomacular traction]].

## Classification

PVD can be classified in several ways:

### By Completeness

- **Complete PVD**: The posterior vitreous cortex has separated from the entire retinal surface posterior to the vitreous base, including the optic disc (Weiss ring present).
- **Incomplete (partial) PVD**: The vitreous has separated from some areas but remains attached at one or more sites (e.g., macula, optic disc, or areas of lattice degeneration). Incomplete PVD carries a higher risk of complications due to persistent traction.

### By Etiology

- **Degenerative (age-related) PVD**: The most common type.
- **Secondary PVD**: Caused by trauma, inflammation, surgery, or systemic disease.

### By Complications

- **Uncomplicated PVD**: No retinal tears, hemorrhage, or macular pathology.
- **Complicated PVD**: Associated with retinal tears, retinal detachment, vitreous hemorrhage, or vitreomacular traction/macular hole.

### Imaging-Based Classification (OCT)

With the advent of [[spectral-domain optical coherence tomography (SD-OCT)]] and [[swept-source OCT (SS-OCT)]], PVD can be staged based on the degree and location of vitreous separation:

- **Stage 0**: No PVD; vitreous attached.
- **Stage 1**: Perifoveal PVD with persistent foveal attachment (may cause VMT).
- **Stage 2**: PVD with persistent attachment to the optic disc only.
- **Stage 3**: Complete PVD with no residual attachment posterior to the vitreous base.

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical Examination

1. **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: Using a narrow beam and high magnification, the examiner can visualize the detached posterior hyaloid face, Weiss ring, and vitreous opacities. A **Hruby lens**, **+78D** or **+90D** [[condensing lens]], or **[[Goldmann three-mirror contact lens]]** enhances visualization of the vitreous and retina.

2. **[[Dilated fundus examination (DFE)]]**: Essential in every patient presenting with symptoms of acute PVD. Both [[binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy (BIO)]] with [[scleral depression]] and slit-lamp biomicroscopy with a contact or non-contact lens should be performed to examine the peripheral retina for tears, holes, or detachment.

3. **[[Scleral depression]]**: Indentation of the sclera with a depressor allows visualization of the far peripheral retina and vitreous base, where retinal tears are most commonly found (especially in the superotemporal quadrant).

### Imaging

1. **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]**: The gold standard for confirming PVD when the view is obscured (e.g., by dense [[vitreous hemorrhage]] or [[cataract]]). On B-scan:
   - The detached posterior hyaloid appears as a thin, mobile, hyperechoic membrane in the vitreous cavity.
   - Complete PVD shows the membrane attached only at the vitreous base.
   - Incomplete PVD shows residual attachment points.
   - B-scan also helps differentiate PVD from [[retinal detachment]] (the retina is thicker, less mobile, and attached at the optic disc and ora serrata).
   - **Kinetic echography** (asking the patient to move the eye during scanning) demonstrates the characteristic undulating after-movement of the detached hyaloid.

2. **[[Optical coherence tomography (OCT)]]**: [[SD-OCT]] and [[SS-OCT]] provide high-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the vitreoretinal interface. OCT is particularly valuable for:
   - Detecting subtle [[vitreomacular adhesion (VMA)]] or [[vitreomacular traction (VMT)]]
   - Identifying early or impending [[macular holes]]
   - Confirming complete vs. incomplete PVD at the macula
   - Monitoring progression of vitreous separation over time
   - **Limitation**: Standard OCT may not visualize the posterior hyaloid if it has separated significantly from the retinal surface, as it moves beyond the imaging window.

3. **[[OCT angiography (OCTA)]]**: While not primarily used for PVD diagnosis, OCTA can detect secondary vascular changes such as retinal hemorrhage or neovascularization.

4. **[[Widefield fundus photography]] and [[fundus autofluorescence]]**: Useful for documentation and detection of peripheral retinal pathology.

5. **[[Dynamic light scattering (DLS)]]**: An experimental technique that can quantify vitreous liquefaction and molecular changes in vivo, though not yet widely available clinically.

## Differential Diagnosis

When a patient presents with acute floaters and/or photopsia, the differential diagnosis includes:

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|-----------------------------|
| **[[Retinal tear]]** | Photopsia, pigmented vitreous cells (Shafer's sign), visible tear on DFE |
| **[[Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]]** | Visual field defect, relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) if extensive, elevated retina on examination |
| **[[Vitreous hemorrhage]]** | Dense floaters, reduced red reflex, blood on B-scan |
| **[[Vitreomacular traction]]** | Metamorphopsia, decreased VA, traction visible on OCT |
| **[[Macular hole]]** | Central scotoma, decreased VA, positive Watzke-Allen test |
| **[[Asteroid hyalosis]]** | Chronic, usually asymptomatic, calcium-lipid opacities |
| **[[Vitreous amyloidosis]]** | Bilateral, progressive, associated with systemic [[amyloidosis]] |
| **[[Posterior uveitis]] / [[vitritis]]** | Inflammatory cells, haze, possible pain, redness |
| **[[Retinal migraine]] / [[ocular migraine]]** | Scintillating scotoma lasting 15–30 min, no vitreous pathology |
| **[[Optic neuritis]]** | Photopsia with eye movement, pain, decreased VA, RAPD |
| **[[Intraocular lymphoma]]** | Vitreous cells, "aurora borealis" appearance, older patients |

## Complications

Approximately **10–15%** of acute symptomatic PVDs are associated with retinal tears at initial presentation, and an additional **2–5%** may develop tears in the weeks following the initial event.

### Retinal Tears

- **Horseshoe (flap) tears**: The most clinically significant type. They occur when the vitreous remains adherent to a focal area of retina and exerts sufficient traction to tear the retinal tissue. The flap remains attached anteriorly. These tears are most common in the **superotemporal quadrant** and carry a high risk of progression to [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]] if untreated.
- **Operculated holes**: The torn retinal fragment is completely avulsed and floats freely in the vitreous. These carry a lower risk of retinal detachment because the traction has been relieved.
- **Giant retinal tears**: Tears extending ≥90° of the circumference; rare but devastating.

### Retinal Detachment

Untreated horseshoe tears can allow liquefied vitreous to pass through the tear and accumulate in the subretinal space, causing [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment (RRD)]]. RRD is a sight-threatening emergency requiring surgical intervention ([[pneumatic retinopexy]], [[scleral buckle]], or [[pars plana vitrectomy]]).

### Vitreous Hemorrhage

During PVD, traction on a [[retinal blood vessel]] (particularly at sites of strong vitreoretinal adhesion or in areas of [[lattice degeneration]]) can avulse the vessel, causing bleeding into the vitreous cavity. Vitreous hemorrhage may obscure the fundus view, necessitating [[B-scan ultrasonography]] to rule out underlying retinal tears or detachment.

### Vitreomacular Traction (VMT) and Macular Hole

When PVD is incomplete and the vitreous remains focally adherent to the [[fovea]], persistent anteroposterior and tangential traction can cause:

- **[[Vitreomacular traction syndrome]]**: Distortion of the foveal architecture, causing metamorphopsia and decreased visual acuity.
- **[[Full-thickness macular hole (FTMH)]]**: Progressive traction leads to dehiscence of the foveal tissue. Classified by the Gass staging system (Stages 1–4) or the International Vitreomacular Traction Study (IVTS) Group classification.
- **[[Lamellar macular hole]]**: Partial-thickness defect of the fovea.
- **[[Epiretinal membrane (ERM)]]**: Residual vitreous cortex or hyalocytes left on the retinal surface after PVD can proliferate and contract, forming an epiretinal membrane.

## Management

### Uncomplicated PVD

The majority of PVDs are uncomplicated and require **reassurance and observation**:

1. **Patient education**: Explain the nature of the condition, the expected course (floaters typically become less noticeable over weeks to months due to gravitational settling and neural adaptation), and the importance of recognizing warning symptoms.

2. **Follow-up examination**: A repeat [[dilated fundus examination]] is recommended at **4–6 weeks** after the initial presentation to detect delayed retinal tears, which occur in approximately 2–5% of cases. Some practitioners advocate an additional examination at **3 months** if symptoms persist or change.

3. **Return precautions**: Patients must be instructed to return **immediately** if they experience:
   - A sudden increase in floaters (especially a "shower" of new floaters)
   - New or increased flashing lights
   - A curtain, shadow, or veil in the peripheral vision
   - Sudden decrease in central vision

### Complicated PVD

#### Retinal Tears Without Detachment

- **[[Laser photocoagulation]] (barrage laser)**: The standard treatment for retinal tears without associated retinal detachment. Two to three rows of confluent [[argon laser]] or equivalent burns are applied around the tear to create a chorioretinal adhesion that prevents fluid from passing through the tear into the subretinal space. Treatment is typically performed using an [[indirect ophthalmoscope delivery system]] or a [[slit-lamp with a contact lens]].
- **[[Cryopexy]] (transconjunctival cryotherapy)**: An alternative to laser, particularly useful for tears in the far periphery or when media opacity limits laser delivery. A cryoprobe is applied to the sclera overlying the tear, creating a chorioretinal adhesion through a freeze-thaw cycle.

#### Retinal Detachment

Requires surgical intervention:
- **[[Pneumatic retinopexy]]**: Injection of an expansile gas bubble ([[SF6]] or [[C3F8]]) into the vitreous cavity, combined with laser or cryopexy. Suitable for selected cases with superior tears.
- **[[Scleral buckle]]**: Placement of a silicone band or sponge on the external scleral surface to indent the wall and relieve vitreoretinal traction.
- **[[Pars plana vitrectomy (PPV)]]**: Surgical removal of the vitreous, relief of traction, drainage of subretinal fluid, and endolaser or cryopexy, with gas or [[silicone oil]] tamponade.

#### Vitreous Hemorrhage

- **Observation**: If the hemorrhage is mild and the retina can be visualized and confirmed to be intact, observation with serial B-scan monitoring is appropriate. Most vitreous hemorrhages clear spontaneously over weeks to months.
- **Surgical intervention ([[pars plana vitrectomy]])**: Indicated if the hemorrhage is dense and non-clearing (typically after 1–3 months), if an underlying retinal tear or detachment is suspected, or if the fellow eye has a history of retinal detachment.

## Pharmacological Interventions and Emerging Therapies

### Pharmacologic Vitreolysis

The concept of **pharmacologic vitreolysis**—using enzymatic agents to induce or facilitate PVD—has been an area of active research:

1. **[[Ocriplasmin]] (Jetrea®, ThromboGenics/Oxurion)**: A truncated form of human [[plasmin]] (microplasmin) that cleaves [[fibronectin]] and [[laminin]] at the vitreoretinal interface. FDA-approved in 2012 for the treatment of symptomatic [[vitreomacular adhesion (VMA)]]. Administered as a single intravitreal injection (0.125 mg/0.1 mL). Clinical trials (MIVI-TRUST) demonstrated:
   - VMA resolution in ~26.5% of treated eyes vs. ~10.1% of placebo
   - Macular hole closure (non-surgical) in ~40.6% of treated eyes with small (≤250 μm) holes
   - Side effects include transient decreased vision, photopsia, floaters, and rare cases of [[retinal detachment]], [[lens subluxation]], and [[ellipsoid zone disruption]].

2. **[[Tissue plasminogen activator (tPA)]]**: Intravitreal tPA has been used off-label to facilitate PVD induction, particularly as an adjunct during [[vitrectomy]] surgery. It activates plasminogen to plasmin, which degrades extracellular matrix proteins at the vitreoretinal interface.

3. **[[Hyaluronidase]]**: Enzymatic degradation of hyaluronic acid to liquefy the vitreous. Investigated in clinical trials but not widely adopted due to variable efficacy.

4. **[[RGD peptides]]**: Synthetic peptides that competitively inhibit integrin-mediated vitreoretinal adhesion. Still in preclinical stages.

### Symptomatic Treatment of Floaters

For patients with persistent, visually significant floaters after PVD:

1. **[[Nd:YAG laser vitreolysis]]**: A relatively recent application of the [[Nd:YAG laser]] to photodisrupt vitreous opacities. Using a specially designed contact lens (e.g., Karickhoff lens) or non-contact technique, the laser is focused on the floater to vaporize it into gas microbubbles. Studies report symptomatic improvement in 54–93% of patients. Risks include [[retinal damage]], [[cataract]], and [[elevated intraocular pressure]]. Best suited for single, well-defined Weiss ring floaters located in the mid-vitreous, away from the retina and lens.

2. **[[Pars plana vitrectomy]] for floaters**: Considered a definitive treatment for debilitating floaters refractory to observation. Modern small-gauge (23G, 25G, 27G) vitrectomy has reduced surgical morbidity. However, risks include [[iatrogenic retinal tears]], [[retinal detachment]], [[cataract progression]], and [[endophthalmitis]], so the risk-benefit ratio must be carefully discussed.

## Surgical Approaches

### Pars Plana Vitrectomy (PPV)

[[Pars plana vitrectomy]] is the primary surgical intervention for complications of PVD:

- **Indications related to PVD**:
  - Non-clearing vitreous hemorrhage
  - Retinal detachment
  - Vitreomacular traction syndrome unresponsive to observation or ocriplasmin
  - Full-thickness macular hole
  - Epiretinal membrane
  - Debilitating symptomatic floaters

- **Technique**: Small-gauge (23G, 25G, or 27G) transconjunctival sutureless vitrectomy is the modern standard. The procedure involves:
  1. Creation of three sclerotomy ports (infusion, illumination, and instrument)
  2. Core vitrectomy
  3. Induction of PVD if not already complete (using the vitrectomy cutter with aspiration, or with the aid of [[triamcinolone acetonide]] to visualize the posterior hyaloid)
  4. Shaving of the vitreous base
  5. Treatment of any retinal pathology (endolaser, membrane peeling with [[ILM peeling]] using dyes such as [[Brilliant Blue G]] or [[indocyanine green]])
  6. Fluid-air exchange and gas or silicone oil tamponade as needed

- **Visualization of the posterior hyaloid**: [[Triamcinolone acetonide]] (preservative-free, intravitreal) is commonly used intraoperatively to stain the vitreous cortex and confirm complete PVD induction, as residual vitreous cortex can be difficult to visualize.

## Prognosis and Follow-Up

### Uncomplicated PVD

The prognosis for uncomplicated PVD is excellent. Floaters typically become less bothersome over **3–6 months** as they settle inferiorly and the brain adapts (neural adaptation). Photopsia usually resolves within **weeks to a few months** as the vitreous completes its separation.

### Risk of Complications Over Time

- **At initial presentation**: ~10–15% of symptomatic PVDs have an associated retinal tear.
- **Within 6 weeks**: An additional ~2–5% develop delayed retinal tears.
- **Bilateral involvement**: If PVD occurs in one eye, the fellow eye has a **~90% chance** of developing PVD within **18 months to 2 years**.
- **Retinal detachment risk**: Approximately **1 in 300** acute PVDs leads to retinal detachment.

### Follow-Up Schedule

| Timing | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| Initial visit | Comprehensive DFE, rule out tears/detachment |
| 4–6 weeks | Repeat DFE to detect delayed tears |
| 3 months (optional) | If symptoms persist or change |
| Immediately | If new symptoms (increased floaters, flashes, curtain/veil) |

## Special Populations

### Myopic Patients

Patients with [[high myopia]] (≥ -6.00 D or axial length ≥ 26 mm) are at increased risk for:
- Earlier onset of PVD
- Higher incidence of retinal tears (especially at areas of [[lattice degeneration]])
- Greater risk of [[retinal detachment]]
- [[Myopic macular schisis]] and [[myopic traction maculopathy]]

### Diabetic Patients

In [[diabetic retinopathy]], the vitreoretinal adhesion is often abnormally strong due to glycation of vitreous proteins and neovascularization. PVD in diabetic patients may be complicated by:
- Severe [[vitreous hemorrhage]] from avulsion of [[neovascularization]] at the disc (NVD) or elsewhere (NVE)
- [[Tractional retinal detachment]]
- Combined tractional-rhegmatogenous retinal detachment

### Pediatric Patients

PVD is rare in children and young adults unless predisposing conditions exist (e.g., [[Stickler syndrome]], trauma, prior surgery, or high myopia). PVD in a young patient should prompt investigation for underlying vitreoretinal dystrophy or systemic connective tissue disorder.

### Post-Surgical Patients

Patients who have undergone [[cataract surgery]], [[intravitreal injections]], or [[YAG capsulotomy]] are at increased risk for PVD and should be counseled about symptoms.

## Patient Education

Effective patient communication is a cornerstone of PVD management. Key points to convey include:

1. **What is happening**: "The gel inside your eye is naturally shrinking and pulling away from the back of your eye. This is very common with aging."
2. **Floaters**: "The spots or cobwebs you see are shadows cast by the gel as it moves. They usually become less noticeable over time."
3. **Flashes**: "The flashing lights occur because the gel is tugging on the retina. They should decrease as the gel separates completely."
4. **Warning signs**: "Return immediately if you notice a sudden shower of new floaters, a curtain or shadow in your vision, or a sudden decrease in your eyesight. These could indicate a retinal tear or detachment, which requires urgent treatment."
5. **Activity**: There is no strong evidence that activity restriction prevents complications of PVD. However, some practitioners advise avoiding heavy lifting or jarring activities during the acute phase, though this remains controversial and not evidence-based.
