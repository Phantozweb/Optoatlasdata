

# Uveitis

**Uveitis** is a broad term encompassing a group of inflammatory diseases that affect the [[uveal tract]] of the eye, comprising the [[iris]], [[ciliary body]], and [[choroid]]. It represents one of the leading causes of preventable blindness worldwide, accounting for approximately 5–20% of legal blindness in developed countries and up to 25% in the developing world. Uveitis can occur as an isolated ocular condition or as a manifestation of a systemic inflammatory, infectious, or autoimmune disease. Prompt recognition, accurate classification, and appropriate management by eye care professionals—including [[optometrists]] and [[ophthalmologists]]—are essential to preserving visual function and preventing sight-threatening complications such as [[cystoid macular edema]], [[glaucoma]], [[posterior synechiae]], [[band keratopathy]], and [[cataract]] formation.

The condition affects individuals of all ages but has a peak incidence between 20 and 60 years, placing a disproportionate burden on the working-age population. The annual incidence is estimated at 17–52 per 100,000 persons, with a prevalence of 38–714 per 100,000 depending on geographic region and population studied.

## Classification

The Standardization of Uveitis Nomenclature (SUN) Working Group established the internationally accepted classification system for uveitis based on the **primary anatomical site of inflammation**. This framework is critical for guiding differential diagnosis, workup, and treatment.

| **Type** | **Primary Site of Inflammation** | **Includes** | **Common Etiologies** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Anterior Uveitis]]** | [[Anterior chamber]] | [[Iritis]], [[iridocyclitis]], anterior [[cyclitis]] | [[HLA-B27]]-associated, [[herpes simplex virus]], [[herpes zoster virus]], [[Fuchs heterochromic iridocyclitis]], [[sarcoidosis]], idiopathic |
| **[[Intermediate Uveitis]]** | [[Vitreous]] | [[Pars planitis]], posterior cyclitis, [[hyalitis]] | [[Multiple sclerosis]], sarcoidosis, [[Lyme disease]], idiopathic |
| **[[Posterior Uveitis]]** | [[Retina]] and/or [[choroid]] | [[Choroiditis]], [[retinitis]], [[chorioretinitis]], [[retinochoroiditis]], [[neuroretinitis]] | [[Toxoplasmosis]], [[cytomegalovirus]] retinitis, [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]], [[Behçet disease]], [[birdshot chorioretinopathy]] |
| **[[Panuveitis]]** | Anterior chamber, vitreous, and retina/choroid | Diffuse uveitis | Sarcoidosis, Behçet disease, [[sympathetic ophthalmia]], [[tuberculosis]], [[syphilis]] |

### Additional Classification Parameters (SUN Criteria)

Uveitis is further classified by:

- **Onset**: Sudden or insidious
- **Duration**: Limited (≤3 months) or persistent (>3 months)
- **Course**: Acute (sudden onset, limited duration), recurrent (repeated episodes separated by periods of inactivity without treatment ≥3 months), or chronic (persistent uveitis with relapse within 3 months of discontinuing treatment)
- **Laterality**: Unilateral, bilateral, or alternating

## Epidemiology

Anterior uveitis is by far the most common form, representing approximately 50–60% of all uveitis cases in tertiary referral centers and up to 90% in community-based studies. Intermediate uveitis accounts for roughly 5–15%, posterior uveitis for 15–22%, and panuveitis for 5–15% of cases.

Approximately 30–50% of uveitis cases remain **idiopathic** after thorough investigation. Among identifiable causes, [[HLA-B27]]-associated anterior uveitis is the single most common specific diagnosis in many Western populations. Infectious etiologies predominate in certain geographic regions—[[toxoplasmosis]] is the leading cause of posterior uveitis globally, and [[tuberculosis]]-associated uveitis is highly prevalent in endemic regions of South Asia, Southeast Asia, and sub-Saharan Africa.

Pediatric uveitis deserves special mention: [[juvenile idiopathic arthritis]] (JIA)-associated chronic anterior uveitis is the most common form in children, often presenting insidiously and asymptomatically, necessitating routine screening [[slit-lamp examination]] in all children diagnosed with JIA.

## Anatomy and Pathophysiology

### Relevant Anatomy

The [[uveal tract]] (also called the **uvea**) is the middle vascular coat of the eye, situated between the [[sclera]] externally and the [[retina]] internally. It consists of three contiguous structures:

1. **[[Iris]]**: The most anterior portion, controlling [[pupil]] size and regulating light entry. It is richly vascularized and innervated.
2. **[[Ciliary Body]]**: Located posterior to the iris, it produces [[aqueous humor]] and contains the [[ciliary muscle]] responsible for [[accommodation]]. The [[pars plana]] is the flat posterior portion, and the [[pars plicata]] is the anterior folded portion.
3. **[[Choroid]]**: The posterior and largest component of the uvea, a highly vascular layer that supplies oxygen and nutrients to the outer layers of the [[retina]], including the [[photoreceptors]] and [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE).

The uveal tract is one of the most vascularized tissues in the body, receiving blood supply from the [[ophthalmic artery]] via the [[long posterior ciliary arteries]], [[short posterior ciliary arteries]], and [[anterior ciliary arteries]]. This rich vascularity, combined with the relatively slow blood flow through the choroidal vasculature, creates an environment conducive to immune complex deposition and inflammatory cell infiltration.

### Immune Privilege and the Blood-Ocular Barrier

The eye is an [[immune-privileged]] organ, maintained by the **[[blood-aqueous barrier]]** (formed by the tight junctions of the non-pigmented [[ciliary epithelium]] and the [[iris]] vascular endothelium) and the **[[blood-retinal barrier]]** (formed by the tight junctions of the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] and retinal vascular endothelium). These barriers restrict the passage of immune cells and large molecules into the intraocular environment.

Additional mechanisms of ocular immune privilege include:

- **Anterior chamber-associated immune deviation ([[ACAID]])**: A phenomenon whereby antigens introduced into the anterior chamber induce systemic immune tolerance rather than inflammation.
- **Immunosuppressive microenvironment**: The aqueous humor contains immunosuppressive factors including [[transforming growth factor-beta]] (TGF-β), [[alpha-melanocyte-stimulating hormone]] (α-MSH), [[vasoactive intestinal peptide]] (VIP), and [[calcitonin gene-related peptide]] (CGRP).
- **Fas ligand (FasL) expression**: Ocular tissues express [[FasL]], which induces apoptosis of infiltrating Fas-expressing inflammatory cells.

### Pathophysiological Mechanisms

Uveitis occurs when the blood-ocular barriers are disrupted, allowing inflammatory mediators and immune cells to infiltrate intraocular tissues. The pathophysiology varies depending on the etiology:

#### Autoimmune/Autoinflammatory Mechanisms
In non-infectious uveitis, the predominant mechanism involves a **T-cell-mediated autoimmune response** directed against ocular autoantigens. Key steps include:

1. **Molecular mimicry or bystander activation**: Systemic infection or environmental triggers activate T-cells that cross-react with ocular antigens (e.g., [[retinal S-antigen]], [[interphotoreceptor retinoid-binding protein]] (IRBP)).
2. **T-cell trafficking**: Activated [[CD4+ T-helper cells]] (particularly [[Th1]] and [[Th17]] subsets) express adhesion molecules that facilitate their migration across the blood-ocular barrier.
3. **Cytokine release**: Infiltrating T-cells release pro-inflammatory cytokines including [[interferon-gamma]] (IFN-γ), [[interleukin-17]] (IL-17), [[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] (TNF-α), and [[interleukin-6]] (IL-6), which amplify the inflammatory cascade.
4. **Recruitment of innate immune cells**: [[Macrophages]], [[neutrophils]], and [[dendritic cells]] are recruited, further perpetuating tissue damage.
5. **Barrier breakdown**: Inflammatory mediators increase vascular permeability, leading to protein and cellular exudation into the aqueous humor and vitreous, clinically manifested as [[flare]] and [[cells]].

#### Infectious Mechanisms
In infectious uveitis, the inflammation may result from:
- **Direct tissue invasion** by the pathogen (e.g., [[Toxoplasma gondii]] tachyzoites in the retina)
- **Immune response to pathogen antigens** within ocular tissues
- **Toxin-mediated damage** (e.g., certain bacterial endotoxins)

#### Genetic Predisposition
Strong genetic associations exist, most notably [[HLA-B27]] with acute anterior uveitis (relative risk 10–20×), [[HLA-A29]] with [[birdshot chorioretinopathy]] (relative risk >50×), and [[HLA-B51]] with [[Behçet disease]].

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Anterior Uveitis

Acute anterior uveitis typically presents with:

- **Pain**: Moderate to severe, deep, aching [[ocular pain]], often worse with [[accommodation]]
- **[[Photophobia]]**: Often marked; consensual photophobia (pain in the affected eye when light is shone in the fellow eye) is characteristic
- **Redness**: [[Ciliary flush]] (circumcorneal/perilimbal injection) rather than diffuse [[conjunctival injection]]
- **Tearing**: [[Lacrimation]] secondary to pain and photophobia
- **Blurred vision**: Variable; may be mild or significant depending on the degree of inflammation and complications
- **[[Miosis]]**: The pupil is often constricted due to [[iris sphincter]] spasm

Chronic anterior uveitis (e.g., JIA-associated, [[Fuchs heterochromic iridocyclitis]]) may be **asymptomatic or minimally symptomatic**, discovered only on routine examination—a critical point for screening protocols.

### Intermediate Uveitis

- **Floaters**: The predominant symptom, caused by [[vitreous cells]] and [[vitreous opacities]]
- **Blurred vision**: Often due to [[cystoid macular edema]] (CME), the most common cause of visual loss in intermediate uveitis
- **Typically painless**: Minimal or no pain, photophobia, or redness
- **Bilateral** in many cases

### Posterior Uveitis

- **Decreased vision**: Often the primary complaint; may be profound if the [[macula]] or [[optic nerve]] is involved
- **Floaters**: From vitreous cells or inflammatory debris
- **[[Scotomata]]**: Visual field defects corresponding to areas of retinal or choroidal inflammation
- **[[Metamorphopsia]]**: Distortion of vision if the macula is affected
- **Typically painless**: Unless associated with anterior segment inflammation

### Panuveitis

Presents with a combination of symptoms from all anatomical compartments, often with significant visual morbidity.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

A systematic, thorough ophthalmic examination is the cornerstone of uveitis diagnosis.

#### Visual Acuity
[[Best-corrected visual acuity]] (BCVA) should be documented at every visit as a key outcome measure.

#### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy
The [[slit-lamp examination]] is the single most important diagnostic tool for anterior uveitis:

| **Finding** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|
| **[[Anterior chamber cells]]** | Graded 0 to 4+ per SUN criteria (field size 1mm × 1mm slit beam at 45°); hallmark of active anterior inflammation |
| **[[Flare]]** | Protein exudation from disrupted blood-aqueous barrier; graded 0 to 4+ per SUN; indicates barrier breakdown |
| **[[Keratic precipitates]] (KPs)** | Inflammatory cell deposits on the [[corneal endothelium]]; **fine/stellate KPs** suggest non-granulomatous inflammation; **mutton-fat (large, greasy) KPs** suggest [[granulomatous uveitis]] (sarcoidosis, tuberculosis, syphilis, VKH) |
| **[[Posterior synechiae]]** | Adhesions between the iris and [[anterior lens capsule]]; indicate current or prior inflammation |
| **[[Iris nodules]]** | **[[Koeppe nodules]]** (pupillary margin) and **[[Busacca nodules]]** (iris stroma) suggest granulomatous uveitis |
| **[[Hypopyon]]** | Layered white blood cells in the inferior anterior chamber; seen in [[HLA-B27]] uveitis, [[Behçet disease]], [[endophthalmitis]], and [[rifabutin]]-associated uveitis |
| **[[Fibrin]]** | Indicates severe barrier breakdown; may form a pupillary membrane |
| **[[Iris heterochromia]]** | Characteristic of [[Fuchs heterochromic iridocyclitis]] |
| **[[Band keratopathy]]** | Calcium deposition in [[Bowman's layer]]; seen in chronic uveitis, especially JIA-associated |

##### SUN Grading of Anterior Chamber Cells

| **Grade** | **Cells in Field** |
|---|---|
| 0 | <1 |
| 0.5+ | 1–5 |
| 1+ | 6–15 |
| 2+ | 16–25 |
| 3+ | 26–50 |
| 4+ | >50 |

##### SUN Grading of Anterior Chamber Flare

| **Grade** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| 0 | None |
| 1+ | Faint |
| 2+ | Moderate (iris and lens details clear) |
| 3+ | Marked (iris and lens details hazy) |
| 4+ | Intense (fibrin or plastic aqueous) |

#### Intraocular Pressure (IOP)
[[Intraocular pressure]] may be **elevated** (due to [[trabeculitis]], trabecular meshwork obstruction by inflammatory cells/debris, [[steroid-induced glaucoma]], or [[peripheral anterior synechiae]]) or **reduced** (due to ciliary body shutdown/[[hypotony]] from inflammation of the [[ciliary body]]).

#### Dilated Fundus Examination
Essential for detecting intermediate, posterior, and panuveitis findings:

- **[[Vitreous cells]]** and **[[vitreous haze]]**: Graded 0 to 4+ using the Nussenblatt/NEI scale or SUN photographic standards
- **[[Snowbanking]]**: Whitish exudative material over the [[pars plana]], particularly inferiorly; characteristic of [[pars planitis]]
- **[[Snowballs]]**: White vitreous opacities, typically inferiorly; seen in intermediate uveitis, sarcoidosis
- **[[Retinal vasculitis]]**: Perivascular sheathing (periphlebitis more common than periarteritis); seen in sarcoidosis, Behçet disease, multiple sclerosis, tuberculosis
- **[[Chorioretinal lesions]]**: Focal or multifocal areas of choroiditis or retinitis; pattern and distribution aid in differential diagnosis
- **[[Cystoid macular edema]]**: The most common structural cause of vision loss in uveitis
- **[[Optic disc edema]]**: May indicate posterior uveitis, neuroretinitis, or associated [[optic neuritis]]
- **[[Retinal detachment]]**: Exudative (serous) detachment in VKH, or [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]] from retinal breaks

### Ancillary Diagnostic Testing

#### Ocular Imaging

| **Modality** | **Utility in Uveitis** |
|---|---|
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)** | Gold standard for detecting and monitoring [[cystoid macular edema]], [[epiretinal membrane]], subretinal fluid, and retinal/choroidal thickening. Enhanced depth imaging OCT (EDI-OCT) evaluates choroidal thickness. |
| **[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA)** | Detects [[retinal vasculitis]] (vessel wall staining, leakage), CME (petaloid hyperfluorescence), optic disc leakage, capillary non-perfusion, and [[neovascularization]] |
| **[[Indocyanine green angiography]] (ICGA)** | Superior for evaluating choroidal inflammation; detects hypofluorescent dark dots (active choroidal granulomas in sarcoidosis, VKH), choroidal vasculitis, and subclinical choroidal involvement |
| **[[Fundus autofluorescence]] (FAF)** | Identifies areas of RPE damage (hypoautofluorescence) and active inflammation/RPE stress (hyperautofluorescence); useful in monitoring [[birdshot chorioretinopathy]], [[serpiginous choroiditis]] |
| **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** | Evaluates posterior segment when media opacity precludes fundoscopy; detects vitreous opacities, retinal detachment, choroidal thickening, and posterior scleritis |
| **[[OCT angiography]] (OCTA)** | Non-invasive evaluation of retinal and choroidal vasculature; detects [[choroidal neovascularization]], areas of capillary dropout, and flow changes in inflammatory conditions |
| **[[Anterior segment OCT]]** | Quantifies anterior chamber inflammation, evaluates iris and angle structures, and documents synechiae |
| **[[Laser flare photometry]]** | Objective, quantitative measurement of anterior chamber flare; more sensitive than clinical grading |

#### Laboratory Investigation

The laboratory workup should be **guided by the clinical presentation**, anatomical classification, laterality, granulomatous vs. non-granulomatous character, and associated systemic symptoms. A "shotgun" approach to testing is discouraged.

**Tailored Laboratory Workup by Clinical Scenario:**

| **Clinical Scenario** | **Recommended Investigations** |
|---|---|
| **First episode, unilateral, acute, non-granulomatous anterior uveitis** | Often no workup needed if classic HLA-B27 pattern; consider [[HLA-B27]] testing, [[syphilis serology]] (RPR/VDRL + FTA-ABS or TP-PA) |
| **Recurrent or bilateral anterior uveitis** | HLA-B27, syphilis serology, [[chest X-ray]], [[ACE]] (angiotensin-converting enzyme), [[lysozyme]], [[CBC]], [[ESR]]/[[CRP]], [[tuberculin skin test]] (TST) or [[interferon-gamma release assay]] (IGRA/QuantiFERON) |
| **Granulomatous anterior uveitis** | Syphilis serology, chest X-ray (or [[chest CT]]), ACE, lysozyme, TST/IGRA, consider [[serum calcium]] |
| **Intermediate uveitis** | Syphilis serology, chest X-ray, ACE, lysozyme, TST/IGRA, [[Lyme serology]] (in endemic areas), consider [[MRI brain]] (to evaluate for [[multiple sclerosis]] or [[CNS lymphoma]]) |
| **Posterior uveitis/Panuveitis** | Syphilis serology, chest X-ray/CT, ACE, lysozyme, TST/IGRA, [[Toxoplasma serology]] (IgG/IgM), CBC, consider [[HIV testing]], [[CMV PCR]] (aqueous/vitreous), and disease-specific testing based on clinical pattern |
| **Suspected infectious uveitis** | [[Aqueous humor]] or [[vitreous]] sampling for [[PCR]] (herpes simplex, herpes zoster, CMV, Toxoplasma, tuberculosis), [[Goldmann-Witmer coefficient]], cultures |
| **Pediatric uveitis** | [[Antinuclear antibody]] (ANA), [[rheumatoid factor]], CBC, ESR, consider HLA-B27 in older males |

#### Aqueous and Vitreous Sampling

**[[Anterior chamber paracentesis]]** and **[[diagnostic vitrectomy]]** or **vitreous tap** are invaluable when infectious etiologies are suspected or when the diagnosis remains uncertain despite non-invasive workup:

- **[[Polymerase chain reaction]] (PCR)**: Highly sensitive and specific for detecting viral DNA (HSV, VZV, CMV, EBV), *Toxoplasma gondii*, and *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*
- **[[Goldmann-Witmer coefficient]]**: Compares intraocular antibody production to serum levels; a coefficient >3 is considered positive for local antibody production
- **[[Cytology]]**: Essential when [[intraocular lymphoma]] (masquerade syndrome) is suspected; IL-10:IL-6 ratio >1 suggests lymphoma
- **Culture**: For suspected bacterial or fungal [[endophthalmitis]]

## Differential Diagnosis and Masquerade Syndromes

Certain non-inflammatory conditions can mimic uveitis and must be excluded:

| **Masquerade Syndrome** | **Key Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| **[[Primary intraocular lymphoma]] (PIOL)** | Vitreous cells refractory to corticosteroids; "aurora borealis" sub-RPE deposits; elevated vitreous IL-10 |
| **[[Retinoblastoma]]** | Pediatric; leukocoria; calcification on imaging |
| **[[Leukemia]]** | Hypopyon, retinal infiltrates; systemic findings |
| **[[Retained intraocular foreign body]]** | History of trauma; metallic foreign body on imaging |
| **[[Retinal detachment]]** (chronic) | Pigmented cells ("tobacco dust") in vitreous; no true anterior chamber inflammation |
| **[[Pigment dispersion syndrome]]** | Pigmented cells on endothelium; [[Krukenberg spindle]]; mid-peripheral iris transillumination defects |
| **[[UGH syndrome]]** ([[Uveitis-Glaucoma-Hyphema]]) | History of [[intraocular lens]] implantation; mechanical iris chafing |

## Complications

Uveitis can lead to numerous sight-threatening complications, many of which require independent management:

| **Complication** | **Mechanism** | **Prevalence** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Cystoid macular edema]]** | Breakdown of blood-retinal barrier; accumulation of fluid in Henle's layer | 20–40% of uveitis patients |
| **[[Cataract]]** | Chronic inflammation and/or prolonged [[corticosteroid]] use | 20–60% in chronic uveitis |
| **[[Secondary glaucoma]]** | Trabeculitis, trabecular obstruction, peripheral anterior synechiae, steroid response | 10–20% |
| **[[Posterior synechiae]]** | Iris adhesion to lens capsule; may cause [[iris bombé]] and [[angle-closure glaucoma]] | Common in anterior uveitis |
| **[[Band keratopathy]]** | Calcium deposition in Bowman's layer from chronic inflammation | Especially in JIA-associated uveitis |
| **[[Epiretinal membrane]]** | Fibrocellular proliferation on internal limiting membrane | Common in intermediate/posterior uveitis |
| **[[Choroidal neovascularization]]** | Disruption of Bruch's membrane and RPE from choroidal inflammation | Seen in punctate inner choroidopathy, multifocal choroiditis |
| **[[Optic atrophy]]** | Chronic optic nerve inflammation or ischemia | Variable |
| **[[Hypotony]] and [[phthisis bulbi]]** | Ciliary body destruction from chronic inflammation | End-stage complication |
| **[[Retinal detachment]]** | Exudative (VKH, posterior scleritis) or tractional/rhegmatogenous | Variable |

## Treatment and Management

### Principles of Management

The goals of uveitis treatment are:

1. **Eliminate active inflammation** (achieve grade 0 cells or ≤0.5+ cells)
2. **Prevent structural complications** and vision loss
3. **Minimize treatment-related side effects**
4. **Achieve steroid-sparing remission** whenever possible
5. **Identify and treat underlying systemic disease**

### Pharmacological Treatment

#### Corticosteroids

[[Corticosteroids]] remain the first-line treatment for non-infectious uveitis due to their broad anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive properties.

**Routes of Administration:**

| **Route** | **Examples** | **Indications** | **Key Considerations** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Topical** | [[Prednisolone acetate]] 1%, [[difluprednate]] 0.05%, [[dexamethasone]] 0.1% | Anterior uveitis (mainstay of treatment) | Frequency titrated to inflammation severity; aggressive initial dosing (e.g., every 1–2 hours) with slow taper; monitor [[IOP]] |
| **Periocular injection** | [[Sub-Tenon's triamcinolone acetonide]] (40 mg), [[orbital floor triamcinolone]] | Unilateral intermediate/posterior uveitis, CME refractory to topical therapy | Risk of IOP elevation, ptosis, orbital fat atrophy; effect lasts 3–6 months |
| **Intravitreal injection** | [[Intravitreal triamcinolone acetonide]] (2–4 mg), [[dexamethasone intravitreal implant]] (Ozurdex® 0.7 mg), [[fluocinolone acetonide implant]] (Retisert® 0.59 mg, Yutiq® 0.18 mg, Iluvien® 0.19 mg) | Posterior uveitis, intermediate uveitis, uveitic CME | Ozurdex lasts ~3–6 months; Retisert lasts ~30 months; Yutiq lasts ~36 months; significant risk of cataract and glaucoma with long-acting implants |
| **Oral systemic** | [[Prednisone]]/[[prednisolone]] (typically 1 mg/kg/day initial, max 60–80 mg) | Bilateral disease, posterior/panuveitis, systemic disease | Taper to ≤7.5 mg/day or discontinue within 3 months; long-term use (>7.5 mg/day) unacceptable due to systemic side effects |
| **Intravenous pulse** | [[Methylprednisolone]] 1 g IV daily × 3 days | Severe sight-threatening inflammation (e.g., VKH, Behçet with retinal vasculitis) | Followed by oral taper; inpatient monitoring |

#### Cycloplegic/Mydriatic Agents

[[Cycloplegic]] agents are essential adjuncts in anterior uveitis:

- **[[Cyclopentolate]] 1%** (2–3 times daily): Moderate cycloplegia; commonly used
- **[[Homatropine]] 2–5%** (2–3 times daily): Intermediate duration
- **[[Atropine]] 1%** (1–3 times daily): Maximum cycloplegia; reserved for severe inflammation

**Purposes:**
- Relieve pain from [[ciliary spasm]]
- Prevent and break [[posterior synechiae]]
- Stabilize the [[blood-aqueous barrier]]

#### Immunomodulatory Therapy (Steroid-Sparing Agents)

Immunomodulatory therapy (IMT) is indicated when:
- Corticosteroids cannot be tapered to ≤7.5 mg/day prednisone equivalent
- Corticosteroid side effects are intolerable
- The disease is chronically relapsing
- Specific diseases known to require long-term immunosuppression (e.g., Behçet disease, sympathetic ophthalmia, VKH)

The **stepladder approach** is commonly employed:

**First-Line Immunomodulators (Antimetabolites):**

| **Drug** | **Mechanism** | **Dose** | **Key Monitoring** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Methotrexate]]** | [[Dihydrofolate reductase]] inhibitor; inhibits purine synthesis | 7.5–25 mg/week (oral or subcutaneous) + folic acid 1 mg/day | CBC, LFTs, renal function every 4–8 weeks; avoid in pregnancy (teratogenic); pneumonitis risk |
| **[[Mycophenolate mofetil]]** | [[Inosine monophosphate dehydrogenase]] inhibitor; selective lymphocyte antiproliferative | 1–3 g/day in divided doses | CBC, LFTs; GI side effects common; teratogenic |
| **[[Azathioprine]]** | [[Purine analogue]]; inhibits DNA synthesis | 1–3 mg/kg/day | Check [[TPMT]] genotype/phenotype before initiation; CBC, LFTs; risk of myelosuppression |

**Second-Line Immunomodulators (T-cell Inhibitors):**

| **Drug** | **Mechanism** | **Dose** | **Key Monitoring** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Cyclosporine A]]** | [[Calcineurin inhibitor]]; inhibits T-cell activation and IL-2 production | 2–5 mg/kg/day in divided doses | Blood pressure, renal function (creatinine), trough levels; nephrotoxicity is dose-limiting |
| **[[Tacrolimus]]** | Calcineurin inhibitor (more potent than cyclosporine) | 0.05–0.15 mg/kg/day | Similar to cyclosporine; diabetes risk |

**Third-Line: Alkylating Agents (rarely used due to toxicity):**
- **[[Cyclophosphamide]]**: Reserved for severe, refractory, sight-threatening disease (e.g., Behçet with retinal vasculitis, severe scleritis)
- **[[Chlorambucil]]**: Historically used; largely replaced by biologics

#### Biologic Agents

Biologic therapies have revolutionized the management of refractory non-infectious uveitis:

| **Drug** | **Target** | **Indication/Evidence** | **Administration** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Adalimumab]]** (Humira®) | [[TNF-alpha]] | FDA-approved for non-infectious intermediate, posterior, and panuveitis (VISUAL I & II trials); first-line biologic for many uveitis specialists | 40 mg subcutaneous every 2 weeks |
| **[[Infliximab]]** | TNF-alpha (chimeric monoclonal antibody) | Behçet disease (strong evidence); refractory posterior/panuveitis | 5–10 mg/kg IV at weeks 0, 2, 6, then every 4–8 weeks |
| **[[Tocilizumab]]** | [[IL-6 receptor]] | Refractory uveitic CME (STOP-Uveitis trial); JIA-associated uveitis | 162 mg SC weekly or 8 mg/kg IV monthly |
| **[[Rituximab]]** | [[CD20]] (B-cell depletion) | Refractory uveitis, especially granulomatosis with polyangiitis-associated | 1000 mg IV × 2 doses, 2 weeks apart; repeat every 6 months |
| **[[Secukinumab]]** | [[IL-17A]] | Under investigation; mixed results in uveitis trials | Subcutaneous |
| **[[Interferon-alpha 2a]]** | Immunomodulatory | Behçet disease-associated uveitis (strong European evidence) | 3–6 million IU SC daily, tapered |
| **[[Sarilumab]]** | IL-6 receptor | Emerging evidence in refractory uveitis | Subcutaneous |

**Pre-biologic screening** must include:
- [[Tuberculosis]] screening (TST or IGRA)
- [[Hepatitis B]] and [[Hepatitis C]] serology
- [[HIV]] testing
- Assessment for [[demyelinating disease]] (contraindication for anti-TNF agents)
- Age-appropriate cancer screening

#### Treatment of Specific Infectious Etiologies

| **Infection** | **Treatment** |
|---|---|
| **[[Toxoplasmic retinochoroiditis]]** | Classic triple therapy: [[pyrimethamine]] + [[sulfadiazine]] + [[folinic acid]]; alternatives: [[trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole]], [[azithromycin]] + pyrimethamine, [[clindamycin]] + pyrimethamine; corticosteroids added 24–48 hours after antimicrobials if macula/optic nerve threatened |
| **[[Herpetic anterior uveitis]] (HSV/VZV)** | [[Oral valacyclovir]] 500 mg–1 g TID (HSV) or 1 g TID (VZV); topical corticosteroids; long-term antiviral prophylaxis to prevent recurrence |
| **[[CMV retinitis]]** | [[Valganciclovir]] oral, [[ganciclovir]] IV or intravitreal, [[foscarnet]] IV or intravitreal; immune reconstitution in HIV patients with [[antiretroviral therapy]] |
| **[[Ocular syphilis]]** | [[IV penicillin G]] 18–24 million units/day × 10–14 days (neurosyphilis protocol); mandatory regardless of CSF findings |
| **[[Tubercular uveitis]]** | [[Anti-tubercular therapy]] (ATT): isoniazid, rifampicin, pyrazinamide, ethambutol (initial phase) followed by isoniazid + rifampicin (continuation); systemic corticosteroids as adjunct |
| **[[Toxocara]] uveitis** | [[Albendazole]] with systemic corticosteroids; periocular/intravitreal steroids for vitreous inflammation |

### Surgical Management

Surgical intervention may be necessary for complications of uveitis or for diagnostic purposes:

- **[[Cataract surgery]]**: Requires meticulous preoperative control of inflammation (quiescent for ≥3 months); aggressive perioperative corticosteroid use; [[posterior chamber intraocular lens]] (PCIOL) implantation in the [[capsular bag]] is preferred; acrylic hydrophobic IOLs are generally recommended
- **[[Glaucoma surgery]]**: [[Trabeculectomy]] with [[mitomycin C]], [[glaucoma drainage devices]] (e.g., [[Ahmed valve]], [[Baerveldt implant]]), or [[minimally invasive glaucoma surgery]] (MIGS) when medical therapy fails; drainage devices may have better long-term success in uveitic glaucoma
- **[[Pars plana vitrectomy]] (PPV)**: Diagnostic (vitreous biopsy for lymphoma, infection) or therapeutic (epiretinal membrane removal, vitreous opacification, tractional retinal detachment)
- **[[EDTA chelation]]**: For band keratopathy removal
- **[[Laser photocoagulation]]**: For retinal neovascularization secondary to ischemic retinal vasculitis
- **[[Intravitreal implant surgery]]**: Placement of sustained-release corticosteroid devices (Retisert)

## Prognosis

The prognosis of uveitis varies significantly based on anatomical classification, etiology, chronicity, and complications:

- **Acute anterior uveitis** (e.g., HLA-B27-associated): Generally favorable with appropriate treatment; most episodes resolve within 6–8 weeks; recurrences are common but typically manageable
- **Chronic anterior uveitis** (e.g., JIA-associated): Guarded prognosis; high rates of complications including cataract, band keratopathy, glaucoma, and amblyopia in children
- **Intermediate uveitis**: Variable; CME is the primary cause of visual morbidity; many patients require long-term immunosuppression
- **Posterior uveitis/Panuveitis**: Prognosis depends heavily on etiology and macular involvement; Behçet disease and VKH can cause severe visual loss without aggressive treatment

Overall, approximately 35% of uveitis patients develop significant visual impairment, and 5–10% become legally blind, underscoring the importance of early, aggressive, and sustained treatment.

## Role of the Optometrist

[[Optometrists]] play a critical role in the management of uveitis:

1. **Early detection and diagnosis**: Recognizing the signs and symptoms of uveitis during routine and urgent care examinations
2. **Initiation of treatment**: Prescribing topical corticosteroids and cycloplegics for acute anterior uveitis (within scope of practice in most jurisdictions)
3. **Monitoring**: Serial assessment of anterior chamber cells and flare, IOP, and posterior segment findings
4. **Corticosteroid taper management**: Guiding the slow, systematic reduction of topical corticosteroids to prevent rebound inflammation
5. **Referral**: Timely referral to a uveitis specialist or [[retina specialist]] for posterior/panuveitis, recurrent or chronic disease, suspected infectious etiology, or cases requiring systemic immunosuppression
6. **Screening**: Performing routine slit-lamp screening in at-risk populations (e.g., children with JIA)
7. **Co-management**: Collaborating with rheumatologists, infectious disease specialists, and ophthalmologists in multidisciplinary care
8. **Patient education**: Counseling patients on the chronic nature of the disease, medication compliance, and warning signs of recurrence

---

## Summary

Uveitis is a complex, heterogeneous group of intraocular inflammatory conditions that demands a systematic approach to classification, diagnosis, and management. The SUN classification system provides a standardized framework for communication and research. A targeted laboratory and imaging workup, guided by clinical findings, is essential for identifying underlying etiologies and excluding masquerade syndromes. Treatment follows a stepwise approach from topical and local corticosteroids to systemic immunomodulatory and biologic therapies, with the overarching goal of achieving steroid-sparing remission and preventing irreversible structural damage. The optometrist serves as a vital frontline provider in the detection, initial management, and long-term co-management of uveitis patients.
