# Glaucoma

**Glaucoma** is a progressive, multifactorial group of [[optic neuropathy|optic neuropathies]] characterized by the degeneration of [[retinal ganglion cells]] (RGCs) and their [[axons]], resulting in a distinctive pattern of [[optic nerve head]] cupping and corresponding [[visual field]] loss. It is the leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide, affecting an estimated 80 million people globally, with projections exceeding 111 million by 2040. Elevated [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) remains the most significant and only modifiable risk factor, although glaucomatous damage can occur at statistically normal IOP levels, a condition termed [[normal-tension glaucoma]]. The disease is frequently asymptomatic in its early stages, earning it the clinical moniker "the silent thief of sight," which underscores the critical importance of routine comprehensive eye examinations by [[optometrists]] and [[ophthalmologists]] for early detection and intervention.

---

## Classification

Glaucoma is broadly classified based on the anatomy of the [[anterior chamber angle]], the underlying etiology, and the temporal course of the disease. Accurate classification is essential for guiding appropriate management strategies.

### Primary Glaucomas

Primary glaucomas occur in the absence of identifiable secondary causes.

| Type | Mechanism | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma]] (POAG)** | Increased resistance to [[aqueous humor]] outflow through the [[trabecular meshwork]], particularly the [[juxtacanalicular tissue]] | Open angle on [[gonioscopy]]; chronic, bilateral, often asymmetric; most common form in Western populations |
| **[[Normal-Tension Glaucoma]] (NTG)** | Glaucomatous optic neuropathy with IOP consistently ≤21 mmHg | Subtype of POAG; vascular dysregulation, systemic hypotension, and vasospasm implicated; disc hemorrhages more common |
| **[[Primary Angle-Closure Glaucoma]] (PACG)** | Mechanical obstruction of the [[trabecular meshwork]] by the peripheral [[iris]], impeding aqueous outflow | Narrow or closed angle on gonioscopy; may present acutely or chronically; more prevalent in Asian and Inuit populations |
| **[[Primary Congenital Glaucoma]] (PCG)** | Developmental anomaly of the anterior chamber angle ([[trabeculodysgenesis]]) | Presents in infancy; classic triad of [[epiphora]], [[photophobia]], and [[blepharospasm]]; [[buphthalmos]] |

### Secondary Glaucomas

Secondary glaucomas arise from identifiable ocular or systemic conditions that impair aqueous humor dynamics.

| Type | Associated Condition/Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **[[Pseudoexfoliation Glaucoma]]** | Deposition of [[pseudoexfoliative material]] in the [[anterior segment]], obstructing the trabecular meshwork |
| **[[Pigmentary Glaucoma]]** | Liberation of [[iris pigment]] from the [[iris pigment epithelium]] due to irido-zonular contact ([[concave iris configuration]]), clogging the trabecular meshwork |
| **[[Neovascular Glaucoma]] (NVG)** | Fibrovascular membrane proliferation over the angle secondary to retinal ischemia (e.g., [[proliferative diabetic retinopathy]], [[central retinal vein occlusion]]) |
| **[[Uveitic Glaucoma]]** | Inflammatory debris, [[posterior synechiae]], [[peripheral anterior synechiae]] (PAS), and [[trabeculitis]] secondary to [[uveitis]] |
| **[[Steroid-Induced Glaucoma]]** | Increased outflow resistance due to [[glycosaminoglycan]] accumulation and structural changes in the trabecular meshwork from [[corticosteroid]] use |
| **[[Traumatic Glaucoma]]** | [[Angle recession]], [[hyphema]], [[lens subluxation]], or inflammatory mechanisms following ocular trauma |
| **[[Lens-Related Glaucoma]]** | Includes [[phacomorphic glaucoma]] (intumescent lens causing pupillary block), [[phacolytic glaucoma]] (leakage of high-molecular-weight proteins from a mature [[cataract]]), and [[lens particle glaucoma]] |
| **[[Iridocorneal Endothelial Syndrome]] (ICE)** | Abnormal [[corneal endothelial]] cell proliferation forming a membrane over the angle |

### Glaucoma Suspect and Ocular Hypertension

A **[[glaucoma suspect]]** is an individual with clinical findings or risk factors that suggest an increased likelihood of developing glaucoma but who does not yet meet definitive diagnostic criteria. **[[Ocular hypertension]]** (OHT) refers to IOP consistently above 21 mmHg without detectable optic nerve damage or visual field loss. The [[Ocular Hypertension Treatment Study]] (OHTS) demonstrated that topical hypotensive therapy reduces the risk of conversion from OHT to POAG by approximately 50%.

## Epidemiology

Glaucoma represents a major global public health burden. POAG is the most prevalent form worldwide, though PACG accounts for a disproportionately large share of bilateral blindness due to its potential for acute, severe IOP elevation and its higher prevalence in densely populated regions of East and Southeast Asia.

### Key Epidemiological Data

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Global Prevalence (2020)** | ~80 million affected (POAG ~68 million; PACG ~12 million) |
| **Projected Prevalence (2040)** | ~111.8 million |
| **Bilateral Blindness** | ~11.2 million people globally |
| **POAG Prevalence by Ethnicity** | Highest in individuals of African descent (up to 4–5× higher than Caucasians) |
| **PACG Prevalence by Ethnicity** | Highest in East Asian, Southeast Asian, and Inuit populations |
| **Age** | Prevalence increases significantly after age 40; exponential rise after age 60 |
| **Sex** | PACG more common in females (shorter [[axial length]], shallower [[anterior chamber depth]]); POAG roughly equal or slightly male-predominant |

### Risk Factors

Risk factors for glaucoma are numerous and vary by subtype:

- **Elevated [[Intraocular Pressure]]**: The most significant modifiable risk factor for all forms of glaucoma. Each 1 mmHg reduction in IOP confers approximately a 10% reduction in the risk of progression in POAG.
- **Age**: Risk increases substantially with advancing age.
- **Race/Ethnicity**: African descent for POAG; Asian descent for PACG.
- **Family History**: First-degree relatives of glaucoma patients have a 4–10× increased risk.
- **[[Central Corneal Thickness]] (CCT)**: Thinner CCT is an independent risk factor for POAG (as demonstrated by OHTS). A CCT below 555 µm is associated with increased risk.
- **[[Myopia]]**: High myopia is a risk factor for POAG due to structural susceptibility of the [[lamina cribrosa]] and [[peripapillary sclera]].
- **[[Hyperopia]]**: A risk factor for PACG due to shorter axial length and crowded anterior segment anatomy.
- **Systemic Conditions**: [[Diabetes mellitus]], systemic [[hypertension]], systemic [[hypotension]] (especially nocturnal dips), [[migraine]], [[Raynaud phenomenon]], and [[obstructive sleep apnea]].
- **Disc Hemorrhages**: Particularly associated with NTG and predictive of progressive visual field loss.
- **Corneal Hysteresis**: Lower [[corneal hysteresis]] (a measure of corneal biomechanical properties measured by the [[Ocular Response Analyzer]]) is an independent risk factor for glaucoma development and progression.

## Anatomy and Aqueous Humor Dynamics

A thorough understanding of [[aqueous humor]] production, circulation, and drainage is fundamental to comprehending glaucoma pathophysiology.

### Aqueous Humor Production

Aqueous humor is produced by the [[ciliary body]], specifically the [[ciliary epithelium]] of the [[ciliary processes]], through three mechanisms:

1. **Active Secretion** (~80–90%): Energy-dependent transport involving the enzyme [[carbonic anhydrase]] and the [[Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase]] pump in the non-pigmented ciliary epithelium.
2. **Ultrafiltration**: Pressure-dependent movement of plasma across the ciliary epithelium.
3. **Diffusion**: Passive movement of lipid-soluble substances along concentration gradients.

The normal rate of aqueous humor production is approximately **2.0–2.5 µL/min** during waking hours, with a significant reduction (up to 50%) during sleep due to decreased sympathetic tone.

### Aqueous Humor Outflow Pathways

| Pathway | Contribution | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Conventional (Trabecular) Outflow]]** | ~70–90% of total outflow | Aqueous flows from the [[posterior chamber]] → through the [[pupil]] → into the [[anterior chamber]] → through the [[trabecular meshwork]] → into [[Schlemm's canal]] → via [[collector channels]] → into [[aqueous veins]] → [[episcleral venous plexus]]. This pathway is IOP-dependent and pressure-sensitive. |
| **[[Unconventional (Uveoscleral) Outflow]]** | ~10–30% of total outflow | Aqueous passes through the [[ciliary muscle]] interstitial spaces → into the [[supraciliary space]] and [[suprachoroidal space]] → absorbed by choroidal vasculature or exits through the [[sclera]]. This pathway is relatively IOP-independent. |

### Intraocular Pressure

[[Intraocular pressure]] is determined by the balance between aqueous humor production and outflow, described by the **[[Goldmann equation]]**:

> **IOP = (F/C) + Pv**

Where:
- **F** = rate of aqueous humor formation
- **C** = facility of outflow
- **Pv** = [[episcleral venous pressure]] (normally ~8–10 mmHg)

Normal IOP is statistically defined as **10–21 mmHg** (mean ~15.5 mmHg ± 2.6 mmHg), though this range is derived from population studies and does not define a safe threshold for any individual patient. IOP exhibits [[diurnal variation]], typically peaking in the early morning hours and reaching its nadir in the afternoon/evening, with a normal fluctuation range of 2–6 mmHg.

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of glaucoma is complex and multifactorial, involving both mechanical and vascular insults to the [[retinal ganglion cells]] and their axons at the level of the [[optic nerve head]], particularly the [[lamina cribrosa]].

### Mechanical Theory

Elevated IOP exerts direct compressive force on the RGC axons as they pass through the [[lamina cribrosa]], a sieve-like structure of connective tissue plates within the [[scleral canal]]. This compression leads to:

- **Axoplasmic flow obstruction**: Disruption of both anterograde and retrograde [[axonal transport]], depriving RGCs of essential [[neurotrophic factors]] such as [[brain-derived neurotrophic factor]] (BDNF) from the [[lateral geniculate nucleus]] (LGN).
- **Lamina cribrosa deformation**: Posterior bowing and remodeling of the laminar plates, contributing to the characteristic cupping of the optic disc.
- **Extracellular matrix remodeling**: Activation of [[astrocytes]] and [[lamina cribrosa cells]], leading to increased production of [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs) and altered collagen and [[elastin]] composition.

### Vascular Theory

Vascular insufficiency at the optic nerve head contributes to glaucomatous damage, particularly in NTG:

- **Reduced [[ocular perfusion pressure]] (OPP)**: OPP = mean arterial blood pressure – IOP. Low OPP, especially during nocturnal blood pressure dips, compromises optic nerve head perfusion.
- **Vascular dysregulation**: Impaired autoregulation of blood flow to the optic nerve head, potentially mediated by [[endothelin-1]] (a potent vasoconstrictor found at elevated levels in glaucoma patients) and reduced [[nitric oxide]] bioavailability.
- **Vasospasm**: Associated with conditions such as [[migraine]] and [[Raynaud phenomenon]], contributing to intermittent ischemia.

### Cellular and Molecular Mechanisms

At the cellular level, multiple interconnected pathways contribute to RGC death:

- **[[Excitotoxicity]]**: Excessive [[glutamate]] release in the retina leads to overstimulation of [[NMDA receptors]], causing calcium influx and neuronal death.
- **[[Oxidative Stress]]**: Increased production of [[reactive oxygen species]] (ROS) and decreased antioxidant defenses damage RGCs and trabecular meshwork cells.
- **[[Mitochondrial Dysfunction]]**: Impaired mitochondrial function in RGCs leads to energy failure and apoptosis.
- **Neuroinflammation**: Activation of [[microglia]] and [[astrocytes]] in the retina and optic nerve head, with release of pro-inflammatory [[cytokines]] such as [[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] (TNF-α) and [[interleukin-1β]].
- **[[Apoptosis]]**: The primary mode of RGC death in glaucoma, mediated by both intrinsic (mitochondrial) and extrinsic (death receptor) pathways, involving [[caspase]] activation.
- **Trabecular Meshwork Pathology (in POAG)**: Increased deposition of [[extracellular matrix]] material, loss of trabecular meshwork cells, fusion of trabecular beams, collapse of [[Schlemm's canal]], and increased outflow resistance, particularly in the [[juxtacanalicular tissue]] (the region immediately adjacent to the inner wall of Schlemm's canal).

### Angle-Closure Mechanisms

In [[angle-closure glaucoma]], the pathophysiology involves physical obstruction of the trabecular meshwork:

- **[[Pupillary Block]]**: The most common mechanism (~75% of cases). Apposition of the [[iris]] to the [[anterior lens capsule]] at the pupil impedes aqueous flow from the posterior to the anterior chamber, creating a pressure gradient that bows the peripheral iris forward (iris bombé), occluding the trabecular meshwork.
- **[[Plateau Iris Configuration/Syndrome]]**: Anteriorly positioned [[ciliary processes]] push the peripheral iris root forward against the trabecular meshwork, even after elimination of pupillary block by [[laser peripheral iridotomy]] (LPI).
- **[[Phacomorphic Mechanism]]**: An enlarged or anteriorly displaced [[crystalline lens]] (e.g., intumescent cataract) pushes the iris forward, narrowing or closing the angle.
- **Posterior Segment Mechanisms**: Conditions such as [[aqueous misdirection]] ([[malignant glaucoma]]), choroidal effusions, or posterior segment tumors can push the lens-iris diaphragm anteriorly.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma (POAG)

POAG is characteristically **asymptomatic** in its early and moderate stages. Patients typically do not notice visual field loss until significant, irreversible damage has occurred because:

- The disease preferentially affects the **mid-peripheral visual field** initially, sparing central [[visual acuity]] until advanced stages.
- The fellow eye compensates for monocular field defects in binocular viewing.
- The brain fills in scotomas through perceptual completion.

Late-stage symptoms include:
- Difficulty with peripheral vision ("tunnel vision")
- Difficulty with tasks requiring peripheral awareness (driving, navigating stairs)
- Eventual loss of central visual acuity in end-stage disease

### Acute Primary Angle-Closure (APAC)

In contrast to POAG, **[[acute primary angle-closure]]** presents with dramatic, unmistakable symptoms:

| Symptom | Description |
|---|---|
| **Severe ocular pain** | Intense, deep, aching pain in the affected eye |
| **Headache** | Ipsilateral, often severe; may mimic intracranial pathology |
| **Nausea and vomiting** | Vagal response to severe pain and elevated IOP |
| **Blurred vision** | Marked reduction in visual acuity |
| **Halos around lights** | Due to [[corneal edema]] from acutely elevated IOP |
| **Red eye** | [[Conjunctival]] and [[ciliary injection]] |
| **Mid-dilated, fixed pupil** | Non-reactive or poorly reactive pupil in a mid-dilated position |
| **Corneal edema** | [[Microcystic corneal edema]] causing a hazy, steamy cornea |

APAC is an **ophthalmic emergency** requiring immediate IOP reduction to prevent permanent optic nerve damage and [[ischemic]] injury to the [[iris]], [[trabecular meshwork]], and [[lens]].

### Primary Congenital Glaucoma

The classic presentation in infants includes the **triad** of:
1. **[[Epiphora]]** (excessive tearing)
2. **[[Photophobia]]** (light sensitivity)
3. **[[Blepharospasm]]** (involuntary eyelid squeezing)

Additional signs include [[buphthalmos]] (enlarged globe), [[Haab striae]] (horizontal breaks in [[Descemet's membrane]]), and corneal edema/clouding.

## Diagnosis

The diagnosis of glaucoma requires a comprehensive evaluation integrating multiple clinical findings. No single test is diagnostic in isolation. The key components of the glaucoma evaluation include:

### 1. Intraocular Pressure Measurement ([[Tonometry]])

| Tonometry Method | Principle | Clinical Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Goldmann Applanation Tonometry]] (GAT)** | Measures force required to flatten a 3.06 mm diameter area of the cornea | Gold standard; affected by CCT, corneal curvature, and corneal biomechanics |
| **[[Non-Contact Tonometry]] (NCT / Air-Puff)** | Uses a calibrated air pulse to applanate the cornea | Useful for screening; less accurate at extreme IOP values |
| **[[Tono-Pen]]** | Portable electronic applanation tonometer | Useful for irregular corneas, bedside measurements, and pediatric patients |
| **[[iCare Tonometry]] (Rebound Tonometry)** | Measures deceleration of a small probe upon contact with the cornea | No topical anesthesia required; excellent for pediatric and screening use |
| **[[Dynamic Contour Tonometry]] (Pascal)** | Measures IOP independent of corneal properties using a contour-matching principle | Less influenced by CCT; also measures [[ocular pulse amplitude]] |
| **[[Ocular Response Analyzer]] (ORA)** | Measures corneal biomechanical properties ([[corneal hysteresis]], [[corneal resistance factor]]) and provides corneal-compensated IOP | Corneal hysteresis is an independent risk factor for glaucoma progression |

**Important**: IOP must be interpreted in the context of CCT. Thinner corneas may lead to underestimation of true IOP by GAT, while thicker corneas may lead to overestimation. However, CCT is also an independent risk factor beyond its effect on tonometry.

### 2. Gonioscopy

**[[Gonioscopy]]** is the definitive method for evaluating the [[iridocorneal angle]] (anterior chamber angle) and is essential for classifying glaucoma as open-angle or angle-closure.

- **Grading Systems**: The [[Shaffer grading system]] (grades 0–4) and the [[Spaeth gonioscopic grading system]] (which assesses iris insertion, angular approach, and iris configuration) are commonly used.
- **Indentation Gonioscopy**: Differentiates appositional angle closure (reversible) from synechial angle closure ([[peripheral anterior synechiae]], PAS; irreversible).
- **Key Structures Identified**: [[Schwalbe's line]], [[trabecular meshwork]] (non-pigmented and pigmented), [[scleral spur]], [[ciliary body band]], and [[iris root]].
- **Pathological Findings**: PAS, [[angle recession]], [[neovascularization of the angle]], [[pseudoexfoliative material]], increased [[trabecular pigmentation]] ([[Sampaolesi line]] in pseudoexfoliation), and [[angle narrowing]].

### 3. Optic Nerve Head (ONH) Assessment

Clinical evaluation of the [[optic nerve head]] is a cornerstone of glaucoma diagnosis and monitoring.

#### Stereoscopic Examination

Performed with a **[[slit-lamp biomicroscope]]** using a high-powered condensing lens (e.g., [[78D lens]], [[90D lens]], or [[66D lens]]) or with a [[direct ophthalmoscope]] (limited stereopsis).

#### Key ONH Parameters

| Parameter | Normal | Glaucomatous |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Cup-to-Disc Ratio]] (C/D)** | Typically 0.0–0.4 (varies with disc size) | Enlarged, progressive increase; asymmetry >0.2 between eyes is suspicious |
| **[[Neuroretinal Rim]]** | Follows the **[[ISNT rule]]** (Inferior > Superior > Nasal > Temporal thickness) | Thinning or notching, particularly inferiorly and superiorly; violation of ISNT rule |
| **[[Disc Hemorrhage]]** | Absent | Flame-shaped or splinter hemorrhage at the disc margin (Drance hemorrhage); highly specific for glaucoma; associated with progression |
| **[[Retinal Nerve Fiber Layer]] (RNFL)** | Intact, visible striations | Diffuse or focal RNFL defects (wedge-shaped defects); best seen with red-free (green) illumination |
| **[[Peripapillary Atrophy]]** | Minimal [[Zone Alpha]] | Enlarged [[Zone Beta]] (absence of RPE); associated with glaucoma progression |
| **Laminar Dot Sign** | Not visible | Visible laminar pores due to posterior displacement of the lamina cribrosa |
| **Bayoneting of Vessels** | Absent | Vessels appear to bend sharply (bayonet-like) at the disc rim due to deep cupping |
| **Nasalization of Vessels** | Absent | Central retinal vessels displaced nasally due to loss of temporal neuroretinal rim |

### 4. Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

**[[Optical Coherence Tomography]]** (OCT) has revolutionized glaucoma diagnosis and monitoring by providing objective, quantitative, high-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the optic nerve head, [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL), and [[macular ganglion cell complex]] (GCC).

#### Key OCT Parameters in Glaucoma

- **[[Peripapillary RNFL Thickness]]**: Measured in a 3.46 mm diameter circle around the optic disc. Thinning, particularly in the inferior and superior quadrants, is characteristic of glaucoma. Results are compared to age-matched normative databases.
- **[[Ganglion Cell-Inner Plexiform Layer]] (GCIPL) / [[Ganglion Cell Complex]] (GCC) Analysis**: Macular scans measuring the thickness of the [[ganglion cell layer]] and [[inner plexiform layer]]. Macular GCC loss may be detected earlier than peripapillary RNFL loss in some patients, particularly those with central visual field defects.
- **[[Optic Nerve Head Parameters]]**: Disc area, rim area, cup volume, C/D ratio, and [[minimum rim width]] ([[Bruch's Membrane Opening – Minimum Rim Width]], BMO-MRW), which is considered a more anatomically accurate measure of neuroretinal rim tissue.
- **[[OCT Angiography]] (OCTA)**: A newer modality that provides non-invasive visualization of the [[peripapillary]] and [[macular microvasculature]] without dye injection. Reduced vessel density in the peripapillary and macular regions has been demonstrated in glaucoma.

#### Progression Analysis

Serial OCT measurements enable detection of structural progression through:
- **Trend-based analysis**: Evaluates the rate of change over time (e.g., µm/year of RNFL thinning).
- **Event-based analysis**: Detects statistically significant change from a baseline measurement.

### 5. Visual Field Testing ([[Perimetry]])

**[[Standard Automated Perimetry]]** (SAP) is the primary method for detecting and monitoring functional (visual field) loss in glaucoma.

#### Common Testing Strategies

| Strategy | Description |
|---|---|
| **[[Humphrey Visual Field Analyzer]] (HVF)** | Most widely used; [[SITA Standard]] and [[SITA Fast]] algorithms; [[24-2]] and [[30-2]] test patterns for general glaucoma assessment; [[10-2]] test pattern for advanced glaucoma or suspected macular involvement |
| **[[Swedish Interactive Thresholding Algorithm]] (SITA)** | Adaptive thresholding algorithm that reduces test time while maintaining accuracy |
| **[[Frequency Doubling Technology]] (FDT) Perimetry** | Targets the [[magnocellular pathway]]; useful for screening and detecting early glaucomatous damage |
| **[[Short-Wavelength Automated Perimetry]] (SWAP)** | Uses blue stimulus on yellow background to isolate the [[koniocellular pathway]]; may detect damage earlier than SAP but has higher variability |

#### Characteristic Glaucomatous Visual Field Defects

| Defect Pattern | Description |
|---|---|
| **[[Paracentral Scotoma]]** | Small, isolated depressed points within the central 10° of fixation, often near the [[Bjerrum area]] |
| **[[Arcuate Scotoma]] (Bjerrum Scotoma)** | Arc-shaped defect following the arcuate nerve fiber bundle pattern, extending from the blind spot and arching above or below fixation toward the nasal meridian |
| **[[Nasal Step]] (Roenne Nasal Step)** | Asymmetric depression across the nasal horizontal midline, reflecting differential damage to superior and inferior arcuate nerve fiber bundles |
| **[[Temporal Wedge]]** | Wedge-shaped defect in the temporal visual field corresponding to damage to the nasal nerve fiber bundle ([[papillomacular bundle]] region) |
| **[[Generalized Depression]]** | Diffuse reduction in sensitivity across the visual field |
| **[[Tubular Vision]]** | End-stage glaucoma with only a small central island and/or temporal island of vision remaining |

#### Reliability Indices

Clinicians must assess reliability indices to ensure valid test results:
- **[[Fixation Losses]]**: Should be <20%
- **[[False Positives]]**: Should be <15%
- **[[False Negatives]]**: Should be <33% (elevated false negatives may indicate advanced disease)

#### Global Indices

- **[[Mean Deviation]] (MD)**: Overall average deviation from age-matched normal values. More negative values indicate greater overall field loss.
- **[[Pattern Standard Deviation]] (PSD)**: Measures localized or irregular field loss. Elevated PSD suggests focal defects characteristic of glaucoma.
- **[[Visual Field Index]] (VFI)**: Expressed as a percentage (100% = normal; 0% = perimetrically blind); weighted toward central visual field; useful for rate of progression analysis.
- **[[Glaucoma Hemifield Test]] (GHT)**: Compares corresponding superior and inferior hemifield zones; results reported as "within normal limits," "borderline," or "outside normal limits."

### 6. Pachymetry

**[[Pachymetry]]** measures [[central corneal thickness]] (CCT), which is essential for interpreting IOP measurements and assessing glaucoma risk. Measured using [[ultrasound pachymetry]] (contact; gold standard) or [[optical pachymetry]] (non-contact; e.g., via OCT, [[Scheimpflug imaging]], or [[specular microscopy]]).

- Average CCT: ~545 µm (varies by ethnicity)
- Thinner CCT (<555 µm): Independent risk factor for POAG; associated with IOP underestimation by GAT
- Thicker CCT (>588 µm): May lead to IOP overestimation by GAT

### 7. Anterior Segment Imaging

- **[[Anterior Segment OCT]] (AS-OCT)**: Provides cross-sectional imaging of the anterior chamber angle, useful for assessing angle width, detecting angle closure, and measuring anterior chamber depth.
- **[[Ultrasound Biomicroscopy]] (UBM)**: High-frequency ultrasound (50 MHz) imaging of the anterior segment, including structures posterior to the iris (ciliary body, zonules, posterior chamber). Essential for evaluating [[plateau iris]], [[ciliary body tumors]], and other posterior mechanisms of angle closure.
- **[[Scheimpflug Imaging]] (e.g., [[Pentacam]])**: Provides anterior chamber depth, angle measurements, and corneal thickness mapping.

## Staging and Severity Classification

Glaucoma severity is typically staged based on visual field loss using the **[[Hodapp-Parrish-Anderson]] (HPA) classification**:

| Stage | Mean Deviation (MD) | Visual Field Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| **Early** | Better than -6 dB | <25% of points depressed below the 5% level; <10% below the 1% level; no point within 5° of fixation with sensitivity <15 dB |
| **Moderate** | -6 to -12 dB | 25–50% of points depressed below the 5% level; <20% below the 1% level; no point within 5° of fixation with sensitivity <0 dB; only one hemifield with a point within 5° of fixation with sensitivity <15 dB |
| **Severe/Advanced** | Worse than -12 dB | >50% of points depressed below the 5% level or >20% below the 1% level; any point within 5° of fixation with sensitivity of 0 dB; points within 5° of fixation with sensitivity <15 dB in both hemifields |

The **[[International Classification of Diseases]] (ICD)** and the **[[American Academy of Ophthalmology]] (AAO) Preferred Practice Patterns** also provide staging guidelines incorporating structural and functional criteria.

## Management and Treatment

The primary goal of glaucoma management is to **preserve visual function and quality of life** by lowering IOP to a level that prevents further optic nerve damage. This individualized target is known as the **[[target IOP]]**. Treatment decisions are guided by the severity of disease, rate of progression, patient life expectancy, risk factors, and patient preferences.

### Target IOP

The target IOP is not a single number but a range, individualized for each patient:

- **General guideline**: A 20–30% reduction from baseline IOP for early glaucoma; greater reductions (30–50%) for moderate to advanced disease or rapidly progressing disease.
- **The [[Collaborative Normal-Tension Glaucoma Study]] (CNTGS)** demonstrated that a 30% IOP reduction slowed progression in NTG.
- **The [[Advanced Glaucoma Intervention Study]] (AGIS)** showed that eyes maintaining IOP consistently below 18 mmHg had minimal visual field progression over 6 years; those with IOP consistently below 14 mmHg had virtually no progression.
- **The [[Early Manifest Glaucoma Trial]] (EMGT)** demonstrated that each 1 mmHg of IOP reduction conferred approximately a 10% reduction in the risk of progression.

### Pharmacological Treatment

Topical [[ocular hypotensive]] medications are the first-line treatment for most forms of glaucoma. They lower IOP by either decreasing aqueous humor production or increasing aqueous humor outflow.

| Drug Class | Mechanism of Action | Examples | IOP Reduction | Key Side Effects |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Prostaglandin Analogues]] (PGAs)** | Increase uveoscleral outflow (and some trabecular outflow) by remodeling the extracellular matrix of the ciliary muscle | [[Latanoprost]] 0.005%, [[Travoprost]] 0.004%, [[Bimatoprost]] 0.01%/0.03%, [[Tafluprost]] 0.0015% | 25–35% | [[Conjunctival hyperemia]], iris pigmentation change, [[periorbital fat atrophy]], [[hypertrichosis]], [[prostaglandin-associated periorbitopathy]] (PAP), potential [[cystoid macular edema]] (CME) in at-risk eyes |
| **[[Beta-Adrenergic Antagonists]] (Beta-Blockers)** | Decrease aqueous humor production by blocking β₂-receptors on the ciliary epithelium | [[Timolol]] 0.25%/0.5%, [[Betaxolol]] 0.25%/0.5% (β₁-selective), [[Levobunolol]] 0.25%/0.5%, [[Carteolol]] 1% | 20–27% | [[Bradycardia]], [[bronchospasm]] (contraindicated in [[asthma]]/[[COPD]]), [[hypotension]], [[depression]], [[fatigue]], reduced exercise tolerance; betaxolol has fewer pulmonary side effects |
| **[[Alpha-Adrenergic Agonists]]** | Decrease aqueous production and increase uveoscleral outflow | [[Brimonidine]] 0.1%/0.15%/0.2%, [[Apraclonidine]] 0.5%/1% | 20–27% | [[Allergic conjunctivitis]] (up to 25% with brimonidine), [[follicular conjunctivitis]], [[dry mouth]], [[fatigue]], [[CNS depression]] in children (brimonidine contraindicated in children <2 years) |
| **[[Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors]] (CAIs)** | Decrease aqueous humor production by inhibiting carbonic anhydrase in the ciliary epithelium | **Topical**: [[Dorzolamide]] 2%, [[Brinzolamide]] 1%; **Oral**: [[Acetazolamide]], [[Methazolamide]] | Topical: 15–20%; Oral: 25–40% | Topical: stinging, [[metallic taste]], [[corneal endothelial toxicity]] (caution in low endothelial cell counts); Oral: [[paresthesias]], [[metabolic acidosis]], [[hypokalemia]], [[kidney stones]], [[aplastic anemia]] (rare), [[sulfonamide allergy]] |
| **[[Rho Kinase Inhibitors]] (ROCK Inhibitors)** | Increase trabecular/conventional outflow by relaxing trabecular meshwork and Schlemm's canal cells; reduce episcleral venous pressure | [[Netarsudil]] 0.02% | 15–20% | [[Conjunctival hyperemia]] (most common), [[cornea verticillata]], [[conjunctival hemorrhage]] |
| **[[Nitric Oxide-Donating PGA]]** | Combines PGA (uveoscleral outflow) with nitric oxide donation (trabecular outflow via relaxation of trabecular meshwork and Schlemm's canal) | [[Latanoprostene bunod]] 0.024% | 30–36% | Similar to PGAs |
| **[[Cholinergic Agonists]] (Miotics)** | Increase trabecular outflow by contracting the [[ciliary muscle]], which opens the trabecular meshwork; also constrict the pupil (useful in angle closure) | [[Pilocarpine]] 1%–4% | 20–25% | [[Miosis]] (reduced vision in dim light), [[accommodative spasm]], [[brow ache]], [[myopic shift]], increased risk of [[retinal detachment]] (rare) |
| **[[Combination Products]]** | Improve adherence by combining two agents in one bottle | [[Dorzolamide/Timolol]] (Cosopt), [[Brimonidine/Timolol]] (Combigan), [[Brinzolamide/Brimonidine]] (Simbrinza), [[Netarsudil/Latanoprost]] (Rocklatan) | Varies | Combined side effect profiles |

#### Prescribing Principles

- **First-line therapy**: [[Prostaglandin analogues]] are generally preferred due to superior efficacy, once-daily dosing, and favorable systemic safety profile.
- **Adjunctive therapy**: Added when monotherapy is insufficient to achieve target IOP.
- **Maximum medical therapy**: Typically involves a PGA + beta-blocker + alpha-agonist + topical CAI (or appropriate combinations).
- **Adherence**: A major challenge; studies show that up to 50% of patients are non-adherent within 6 months. Simplifying regimens, patient education, and fixed-combination products improve adherence.
- **Ocular surface disease**: Chronic use of preserved topical medications (particularly those containing [[benzalkonium chloride]], BAK) can cause or exacerbate [[ocular surface disease]], leading to discomfort and reduced adherence. Preservative-free formulations should be considered when available.

### Laser Treatment

Laser procedures play an important role in glaucoma management across various subtypes.

| Procedure | Indication | Mechanism | Details |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty]] (SLT)** | POAG, OHT, pseudoexfoliation glaucoma, pigmentary glaucoma | Selectively targets pigmented trabecular meshwork cells using a frequency-doubled Q-switched [[Nd:YAG laser]] (532 nm); stimulates biological remodeling and improved outflow | Repeatable; can be used as primary therapy or adjunct; ~20–30% IOP reduction; the [[LiGHT trial]] demonstrated SLT as an effective first-line treatment, with 74.2% of patients achieving target IOP without drops at 36 months |
| **[[Argon Laser Trabeculoplasty]] (ALT)** | POAG (largely replaced by SLT) | Thermal coagulation of the trabecular meshwork using an [[argon laser]] | Less repeatable than SLT due to coagulative tissue damage; ~20–25% IOP reduction |
| **[[Laser Peripheral Iridotomy]] (LPI)** | Angle closure (acute, chronic, or prophylactic in fellow eye); pupillary block | Creates a full-thickness opening in the peripheral [[iris]] using [[Nd:YAG laser]], bypassing pupillary block and equalizing pressure between posterior and anterior chambers | Definitive treatment for pupillary block; performed prophylactically in the fellow eye of APAC patients and in primary angle-closure suspects (PACS) with high-risk features |
| **[[Laser Iridoplasty]] (Argon Laser Peripheral Iridoplasty, ALPI)** | Plateau iris syndrome; acute angle closure unresponsive to LPI and medications | Argon laser burns applied to the peripheral iris stroma cause contraction and thinning of the iris, pulling it away from the trabecular meshwork | Useful when pupillary block is not the primary mechanism |
| **[[Cyclophotocoagulation]]** | Refractory glaucoma; eyes with poor visual potential | Destroys [[ciliary epithelium]] to reduce aqueous production using [[diode laser]] (810 nm) | **[[Transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]] (TSCPC)**: Applied externally through the sclera; **[[Endoscopic cyclophotocoagulation]] (ECP)**: Applied internally under direct visualization; **[[Micropulse transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]] (MP-TSCPC)**: Subthreshold, repetitive micropulse delivery; considered less destructive with potentially fewer complications |

### Minimally Invasive Glaucoma Surgery (MIGS)

**[[Minimally Invasive Glaucoma Surgery]]** (MIGS) represents a paradigm shift in glaucoma surgical management, offering a safer alternative to traditional filtering surgery with a more favorable risk-benefit profile, particularly for mild-to-moderate glaucoma. MIGS procedures are typically performed in conjunction with [[cataract surgery]] ([[phacoemulsification]]).

### Incisional (Traditional) Glaucoma Surgery

Traditional filtering surgeries remain essential for moderate-to-advanced glaucoma or cases refractory to medical and laser therapy.

#### [[Trabeculectomy]]

- **The gold standard** of glaucoma filtering surgery.
- Creates a guarded fistula (partial-thickness [[scleral flap]]) allowing aqueous humor to drain from the anterior chamber into the subconjunctival space, forming a **[[filtering bleb]]**.
- **Antifibrotic agents**: [[Mitomycin C]] (MMC) and [[5-Fluorouracil]] (5-FU) are applied intraoperatively or postoperatively to modulate wound healing and prevent bleb fibrosis, significantly improving surgical success rates.
- **IOP Reduction**: 30–50% or more; can achieve very low target IOPs.
- **Complications**: [[Hypotony]] (overfiltration), [[bleb leak]], [[bleb-related infection]] ([[blebitis]], [[bleb-associated endophthalmitis]]), [[choroidal effusion]]/[[choroidal detachment]], [[suprachoroidal hemorrhage]], [[cataract]] acceleration, [[hyphema]], [[malignant glaucoma]], and [[encapsulated bleb]] (Tenon's cyst).

#### [[Glaucoma Drainage Devices]] (GDDs) / Tube Shunts

- Implants consisting of a silicone tube connected to an equatorial plate that is sutured to the [[sclera]].
- Aqueous drains from the anterior chamber (or [[vitreous cavity]] / [[pars plana]] in specific cases) through the tube to the plate, where a fibrous capsule forms around the plate, regulating outflow.
- **Types**: [[Ahmed Glaucoma Valve]] (flow-restricting valve mechanism), [[Baerveldt Glaucoma Implant]] (non-valved), [[Molteno Implant]] (non-valved).
- **Indications**: Failed trabeculectomy, neovascular glaucoma, uveitic glaucoma, prior conjunctival surgery, and other complex or refractory glaucomas.
- **The [[Tube Versus Trabeculectomy]] (TVT) Study** demonstrated that tube shunt surgery had a higher success rate than trabeculectomy with MMC at 5 years in eyes with prior cataract or glaucoma surgery, though both achieved similar IOP reduction.
- **Complications**: [[Tube erosion]], [[tube obstruction]], [[corneal endothelial decompensation]] (from tube-cornea touch), [[diplopia]] (from plate-related restriction), hypotony, and complications similar to trabeculectomy.

#### [[Goniotomy]] and [[Trabeculotomy]] (Pediatric Glaucoma)

- **Primary surgical treatments** for [[primary congenital glaucoma]].
- **Goniotomy**: Ab interno incision of the trabecular meshwork under direct gonioscopic visualization.
- **Trabeculotomy**: Ab externo approach; [[Schlemm's canal]] is cannulated and the trabecular meshwork is ruptured by rotating the instrument (e.g., [[Harms trabeculotome]]) into the anterior chamber. **[[360° trabeculotomy]]** (using a suture or illuminated microcatheter) provides circumferential treatment.

## Monitoring and Follow-Up

Glaucoma is a chronic, lifelong disease requiring regular monitoring to detect progression and adjust treatment.

### Recommended Follow-Up Schedule

| Scenario | Follow-Up Interval |
|---|---|
| **Newly diagnosed / treatment initiation** | 1–3 months to assess IOP response |
| **Stable, controlled glaucoma** | Every 3–6 months for IOP; every 6–12 months for visual fields and OCT |
| **Advanced or progressing glaucoma** | Every 1–3 months |
| **Glaucoma suspect / OHT** | Every 6–12 months |
| **Post-surgical** | Day 1, Week 1, Months 1, 3, 6, then as indicated |

### Detecting Progression

Progression is assessed by integrating:

1. **Structural progression**: Serial OCT showing progressive RNFL thinning, GCC thinning, or neuroretinal rim loss beyond measurement variability.
2. **Functional progression**: Serial visual field testing showing deepening or expansion of scotomas, confirmed on repeat testing. **Guided Progression Analysis (GPA)** on the Humphrey Visual Field Analyzer and **VFI rate of progression** are commonly used tools.
3. **Clinical assessment**: Progressive optic disc cupping, new disc hemorrhages, or new RNFL defects on clinical examination or photography.

The **structure-function relationship** in glaucoma is complex. Structural changes (detectable on OCT) often precede functional changes (detectable on perimetry) in early disease, while functional changes may be more informative in advanced disease (due to the "floor effect" on OCT, where RNFL thickness reaches a minimum measurable level composed of residual non-neural tissue).

## Emerging Therapies and Future Directions

Research in glaucoma is rapidly evolving, with several promising avenues:

- **[[Neuroprotection]]**: Agents aimed at protecting RGCs independent of IOP lowering, including [[memantine]] (NMDA receptor antagonist; clinical trials showed limited benefit), [[brimonidine]] (potential neuroprotective properties via upregulation of neurotrophic factors), [[citicoline]], and [[nicotinamide]] (vitamin B3; the [[Glaucoma Nicotinamide Trial]] showed improved inner retinal function).
- **[[Gene Therapy]]**: Targeting specific genetic mutations associated with glaucoma (e.g., [[MYOC]] gene in juvenile open-angle glaucoma); viral vector-mediated delivery of neuroprotective factors.
- **[[Sustained-Release Drug Delivery]]**: Intracameral implants (e.g., [[Bimatoprost Sustained-Release Implant]] / [[Durysta]]), punctal plugs with drug elution, and contact lens-based delivery systems to improve adherence and reduce ocular surface toxicity.
- **[[Stem Cell Therapy]]**: Regeneration of trabecular meshwork cells and potential RGC replacement.
- **[[Artificial Intelligence]] (AI) in Glaucoma**: Deep learning algorithms for automated detection of glaucomatous optic disc changes, RNFL defects on OCT, and visual field progression, with potential to improve screening efficiency and diagnostic accuracy.
- **[[Implantable IOP Sensors]]**: Continuous IOP monitoring devices to capture 24-hour IOP profiles and detect peaks missed by office-based measurements.
- **[[Rho Kinase Inhibitors]] and [[Nitric Oxide Donors]]**: Continued development of agents targeting the trabecular outflow pathway.

## Landmark Clinical Trials in Glaucoma

| Trial | Key Finding |
|---|---|
| **[[Ocular Hypertension Treatment Study]] (OHTS)** | Topical treatment reduced conversion from OHT to POAG by ~50%; identified CCT as an independent risk factor |
| **[[Early Manifest Glaucoma Trial]] (EMGT)** | Treatment (laser + betaxolol) reduced progression risk by ~50%; each 1 mmHg IOP reduction ≈ 10% risk reduction |
| **[[Advanced Glaucoma Intervention Study]] (AGIS)** | IOP consistently <18 mmHg associated with minimal progression; IOP <14 mmHg associated with near-zero progression |
| **[[Collaborative Normal-Tension Glaucoma Study]] (CNTGS)** | 30% IOP reduction slowed progression in NTG |
| **[[Collaborative Initial Glaucoma Treatment Study]] (CIGTS)** | Initial surgery (trabeculectomy) and initial medicine achieved similar visual field outcomes at 5 years; surgery achieved lower IOP |
| **[[Tube Versus Trabeculectomy]] (TVT) Study** | Tube shunt had higher success rate than trabeculectomy with MMC at 5 years in previously operated eyes |
| **[[LiGHT Trial]]** | SLT as first-line treatment was at least as effective as eye drops at 36 months, with better cost-effectiveness and quality of life |
| **[[Primary Tube Versus Trabeculectomy]] (PTVT) Study** | In eyes without prior incisional surgery, trabeculectomy with MMC achieved lower IOP than tube shunt at 3 years, with similar safety profiles |
| **[[United Kingdom Glaucoma Treatment Study]] (UKGTS)** | Latanoprost significantly reduced visual field progression compared to placebo in newly diagnosed POAG, providing the first placebo-controlled evidence for treatment benefit in POAG |
