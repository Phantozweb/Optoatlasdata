# Long Ciliary Nerves

---

The **long ciliary nerves** (Latin: *nervi ciliares longi*) are a set of two to three slender sensory and sympathetic nerve branches that arise directly from the [[nasociliary nerve]], a major division of the [[ophthalmic nerve]] (V₁), which itself is the first division of the [[trigeminal nerve]] (cranial nerve V). These nerves traverse the [[orbit]] alongside the [[optic nerve]], pierce the [[sclera]] near the posterior pole of the [[globe]], and course anteriorly within the [[suprachoroidal space]] to provide critical sensory innervation to the [[cornea]], [[iris]], and [[ciliary body]]. In addition to their afferent (sensory) role, the long ciliary nerves carry [[sympathetic nervous system|postganglionic sympathetic fibers]] originating from the [[superior cervical ganglion]] that innervate the [[dilator pupillae muscle]], thereby contributing to [[pupil]] dilation ([[mydriasis]]). Unlike the [[short ciliary nerves]], the long ciliary nerves bypass the [[ciliary ganglion]] entirely, making them anatomically and functionally distinct. A thorough understanding of the long ciliary nerves is indispensable for optometrists, ophthalmologists, and ophthalmic surgeons, as these structures are clinically relevant in [[corneal sensitivity]], [[pupillary reflex]] pathways, ocular pain syndromes, regional [[anesthesia]], and various surgical procedures.

---

## Embryology and Development

The long ciliary nerves develop from the [[neural crest]] cells that contribute to the formation of the [[trigeminal nerve]] and its branches during the fourth to sixth weeks of [[embryogenesis]]. The sensory neurons whose axons constitute the long ciliary nerves have their cell bodies in the [[trigeminal ganglion]] (also known as the [[Gasserian ganglion]] or semilunar ganglion), located in [[Meckel's cave]] on the floor of the [[middle cranial fossa]].

The sympathetic component of the long ciliary nerves develops in concert with the [[sympathetic trunk]]. Preganglionic sympathetic neurons originate in the [[ciliospinal center of Budge]] (intermediolateral cell column at spinal cord levels C8–T2). Their axons ascend in the [[sympathetic chain]] to synapse in the [[superior cervical ganglion]]. Postganglionic fibers then travel along the [[internal carotid artery]] as the [[internal carotid plexus]], enter the [[cavernous sinus]], and join the [[nasociliary nerve]] before being distributed via the long ciliary nerves to the [[dilator pupillae muscle]].

During fetal development, the long ciliary nerves penetrate the developing sclera and establish their characteristic intrascleral and suprachoroidal course. The corneal nerve plexus, which is the terminal distribution of the long ciliary nerves, undergoes progressive maturation during the first years of postnatal life, which is clinically relevant to understanding [[corneal sensitivity]] in neonates and infants.

## Gross Anatomy

### Origin

The long ciliary nerves originate from the [[nasociliary nerve]] within the [[orbit]]. The nasociliary nerve itself branches from the [[ophthalmic nerve]] (V₁) after the latter passes through the [[superior orbital fissure]] and enters the orbit within the [[annulus of Zinn]] (common tendinous ring). Typically, **two to three** long ciliary nerves arise from the nasociliary nerve as it crosses above the [[optic nerve]] (cranial nerve II) in the posterior orbit.

### Course

After their origin, the long ciliary nerves pursue a course that can be divided into several segments:

| Segment | Description |
|---|---|
| **Orbital segment** | The nerves travel anteriorly along the medial and lateral aspects of the [[optic nerve]], embedded within the orbital fat and loosely adherent to the optic nerve sheath. They are accompanied by the [[long posterior ciliary arteries]], branches of the [[ophthalmic artery]]. |
| **Scleral penetration** | The nerves pierce the posterior [[sclera]] approximately 3.5–4.0 mm from the [[optic disc]], typically at the horizontal meridian (3 o'clock and 9 o'clock positions). The penetration sites are medial and lateral to the optic nerve. |
| **Suprachoroidal segment** | After penetrating the sclera, the nerves course anteriorly within the [[suprachoroidal space]] (the potential space between the sclera and the [[choroid]]), running in a relatively straight path toward the anterior segment. |
| **Anterior segment distribution** | The nerves reach the [[ciliary body]] and then ramify extensively to supply the [[iris]] and the [[cornea]]. |

### Relationship to Adjacent Structures

The long ciliary nerves maintain important anatomical relationships:

- **[[Optic nerve]]**: The nerves run parallel to and on either side of the optic nerve in the posterior orbit.
- **[[Long posterior ciliary arteries]]**: These arteries, branches of the [[ophthalmic artery]], accompany the long ciliary nerves throughout their orbital and intrascleral course. This neurovascular bundle is a critical landmark in posterior segment surgery.
- **[[Short ciliary nerves]]**: In contrast to the long ciliary nerves, the 6–10 [[short ciliary nerves]] arise from the [[ciliary ganglion]] and carry postganglionic [[parasympathetic]] fibers (for the [[sphincter pupillae muscle]] and [[ciliary muscle]]) in addition to sensory and sympathetic fibers. The long and short ciliary nerves converge and intermingle at the level of the [[ciliary body]] and form the **ciliary nerve plexus**.
- **[[Ciliary ganglion]]**: The long ciliary nerves notably **bypass** the ciliary ganglion. This is a defining anatomical feature that distinguishes them from the short ciliary nerves.

## Fiber Composition and Neurophysiology

The long ciliary nerves are mixed nerves containing two distinct fiber populations:

### Sensory (Afferent) Fibers

The majority of fibers within the long ciliary nerves are **sensory afferents** whose cell bodies reside in the [[trigeminal ganglion]]. These fibers are responsible for:

- **[[Corneal sensation]]**: The long ciliary nerves are the primary source of sensory innervation to the [[cornea]]. Upon reaching the limbal region, the nerve fibers lose their [[myelin sheath]] (at approximately 1–2 mm from the [[limbus]]) and enter the corneal [[stroma]] as unmyelinated fibers. They then penetrate [[Bowman's layer]] and form the **sub-basal nerve plexus** (also known as the subepithelial plexus), which is one of the most densely innervated tissues in the human body, with an estimated density of 300–600 nerve terminals per square millimeter.
- **Iris and ciliary body sensation**: Sensory fibers also supply the [[iris]] stroma and the [[ciliary body]], mediating pain and other sensory modalities from these structures.

The sensory fibers include:

| Fiber Type | Function | Conduction Velocity |
|---|---|---|
| **Aδ fibers** (thinly myelinated) | Sharp, well-localized pain; mechanical and thermal nociception | 5–30 m/s |
| **C fibers** (unmyelinated) | Dull, diffuse pain; polymodal nociception (mechanical, thermal, chemical) | 0.5–2 m/s |
| **Aβ fibers** (myelinated) | Touch and pressure sensation (less abundant in cornea) | 30–70 m/s |

The corneal nerve terminals express a variety of [[nociceptor]] receptors, including [[TRPV1]] (transient receptor potential vanilloid 1), [[TRPA1]], and [[TRPM8]] channels, which are activated by thermal, chemical, and cold stimuli, respectively. These receptors are critical to the [[corneal reflex]] and to the sensation of ocular surface discomfort.

### Sympathetic (Efferent) Fibers

The long ciliary nerves carry **postganglionic sympathetic fibers** that originate in the [[superior cervical ganglion]]. These fibers:

- Innervate the **[[dilator pupillae muscle]]** (radial smooth muscle of the iris), mediating [[mydriasis]] (pupil dilation).
- Release **[[norepinephrine]]** (noradrenaline) as their primary neurotransmitter, acting on **[[alpha-1 adrenergic receptor|α₁-adrenergic receptors]]** on the dilator pupillae smooth muscle cells.
- May also contribute to the regulation of [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) through effects on [[aqueous humor]] dynamics, although this role is less well characterized compared to parasympathetic innervation.

The sympathetic pathway to the eye via the long ciliary nerves is a **three-neuron arc**:

1. **First-order neuron**: Hypothalamus → [[ciliospinal center of Budge]] (C8–T2) via the brainstem.
2. **Second-order neuron**: Ciliospinal center → [[superior cervical ganglion]] (preganglionic fiber ascends in the sympathetic chain).
3. **Third-order neuron**: Superior cervical ganglion → eye via the [[internal carotid plexus]], [[nasociliary nerve]], and long ciliary nerves (postganglionic fiber).

Disruption at any point along this pathway results in **[[Horner syndrome]]**, characterized by [[miosis]] (due to loss of dilator pupillae innervation via the long ciliary nerves), [[ptosis]], and [[anhidrosis]].

## Pathophysiology

Pathological processes affecting the long ciliary nerves can arise from damage at any point along their course—from the trigeminal ganglion to the terminal corneal nerve endings. The pathophysiology can be broadly categorized as follows:

### 1. Corneal Denervation and Neurotrophic Keratopathy

Damage to the long ciliary nerves results in loss of [[corneal sensitivity]], which can lead to **[[neurotrophic keratopathy]]** (also known as [[neurotrophic keratitis]]). The corneal nerves are not merely sensory conduits; they release trophic factors including **[[substance P]]**, **[[calcitonin gene-related peptide]]** (CGRP), and **[[nerve growth factor]]** (NGF) that are essential for maintaining corneal epithelial health, promoting wound healing, and sustaining the [[tear film]].

The Mackie classification of neurotrophic keratopathy describes three stages:

| Stage | Clinical Features |
|---|---|
| **Stage 1** | Corneal epithelial irregularity, punctate epithelial erosions ([[punctate keratopathy]]), [[rose bengal]] staining, decreased [[tear break-up time]] |
| **Stage 2** | Persistent [[epithelial defect]] (PED), typically oval, located in the superior or central cornea, with smooth rolled edges; surrounding epithelial edema |
| **Stage 3** | Corneal [[ulceration]], stromal melting ([[keratolysis]]), and potential [[corneal perforation]] |

Causes of long ciliary nerve damage leading to neurotrophic keratopathy include:

- **[[Herpes simplex keratitis]]** and **[[herpes zoster ophthalmicus]]** (viral damage to the trigeminal ganglion and nasociliary nerve)
- **Surgical trauma**: [[LASIK]], [[photorefractive keratectomy]] (PRK), [[penetrating keratoplasty]], and retinal detachment surgery (particularly [[scleral buckle]] procedures that may compress or transect the long ciliary nerves at their scleral entry points)
- **Tumors**: [[Acoustic neuroma]], [[meningioma]], or other lesions affecting the trigeminal nerve or ganglion
- **[[Diabetes mellitus]]**: Diabetic [[peripheral neuropathy]] can affect the long ciliary nerves, leading to reduced corneal sensitivity (diabetic neurotrophic keratopathy)
- **Topical medication toxicity**: Chronic use of [[topical anesthetics]], [[beta-blockers]], or [[preservatives]] (e.g., [[benzalkonium chloride]]) can damage corneal nerve terminals

### 2. Sympathetic Denervation

Interruption of the sympathetic fibers within the long ciliary nerves (or their proximal pathway) results in loss of [[dilator pupillae muscle]] function, manifesting as **[[miosis]]** on the affected side. This is a cardinal feature of **[[Horner syndrome]]**. Third-order (postganglionic) Horner syndrome specifically involves the sympathetic fibers after they leave the superior cervical ganglion, and damage at the level of the long ciliary nerves themselves would produce a postganglionic pattern.

### 3. Irritation and Pain Syndromes

Irritation or inflammation of the long ciliary nerves can produce significant **ocular pain**, **[[photophobia]]**, and **referred pain** to the distribution of the [[ophthalmic nerve]] (forehead, upper eyelid, nose). Conditions that may irritate the long ciliary nerves include:

- **[[Anterior uveitis]]** (iritis/iridocyclitis): Inflammation of the iris and ciliary body directly irritates the sensory terminals of the long ciliary nerves, producing deep aching pain, photophobia, and consensual photophobia.
- **[[Acute angle-closure glaucoma]]**: Sudden elevation of [[intraocular pressure]] can compress and irritate the long ciliary nerves, contributing to the severe pain characteristic of this condition.
- **[[Scleritis]]**: Inflammation of the sclera, particularly posterior scleritis, can involve the long ciliary nerves at their scleral penetration sites.
- **Trauma**: Blunt or penetrating ocular trauma can damage the long ciliary nerves, producing acute pain and subsequent corneal hypoesthesia.

### 4. Aberrant Regeneration

Following damage, regenerating long ciliary nerve fibers may establish aberrant connections, leading to phenomena such as:

- **Corneal [[dysesthesia]]**: Abnormal sensations (burning, stinging, foreign body sensation) despite an apparently normal corneal surface, due to misdirected regenerating nerve fibers.
- **Neuroma formation**: Rarely, traumatic neuromas may form at sites of nerve transection, producing localized pain.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Symptoms attributable to long ciliary nerve dysfunction depend on whether the pathology involves sensory fibers, sympathetic fibers, or both:

### Sensory Fiber Dysfunction

| Symptom/Sign | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Decreased corneal sensitivity** | Loss of Aδ and C fiber function; measurable by [[Cochet-Bonnet esthesiometry]] or non-contact [[corneal esthesiometry]] |
| **Absent or diminished [[corneal reflex]]** | Loss of the afferent limb of the blink reflex arc (long ciliary nerve → nasociliary nerve → trigeminal nucleus → [[facial nerve]] → [[orbicularis oculi]]) |
| **Recurrent [[corneal erosions]]** | Loss of trophic support to the corneal epithelium |
| **Persistent [[epithelial defect]]** | Impaired epithelial healing due to loss of neuropeptides (substance P, CGRP) |
| **[[Neurotrophic keratopathy]]** | Progressive corneal degeneration (see Mackie classification above) |
| **Paradoxical comfort** | Patients with severe corneal denervation may report minimal pain despite significant corneal pathology ("quiet eye with a sick cornea") |
| **Ocular pain / [[photophobia]]** | Irritation of intact long ciliary nerve fibers (e.g., in uveitis, trauma) |

### Sympathetic Fiber Dysfunction

| Symptom/Sign | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **[[Miosis]]** (ipsilateral) | Loss of dilator pupillae innervation |
| **[[Dilation lag]]** | Delayed pupil dilation in dim illumination; best observed in the first 4–5 seconds after lights are dimmed |
| **[[Iris heterochromia]]** (in congenital cases) | Failure of melanocyte stimulation in the iris stroma (sympathetic fibers influence melanogenesis) |
| **Component of [[Horner syndrome]]** | Miosis + ptosis + anhidrosis (if preganglionic or postganglionic lesion) |

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Assessment of Corneal Sensitivity

Assessment of long ciliary nerve function is primarily performed through evaluation of **[[corneal sensitivity]]**:

1. **Cotton wisp test**: A simple bedside test where a fine wisp of cotton is drawn across the cornea from the periphery toward the center. The examiner observes for a blink response and asks the patient to compare sensation between the two eyes. This is a qualitative screening test.

2. **[[Cochet-Bonnet esthesiometer]]**: The gold standard quantitative instrument. It uses a retractable nylon monofilament of variable length (0–60 mm). Shorter filament lengths exert greater pressure. Normal corneal sensitivity threshold is typically achieved with a filament length of ≥50 mm centrally. Values below 40 mm are considered abnormal.

3. **Non-contact corneal esthesiometry (Belmonte esthesiometer)**: A more sophisticated device that delivers controlled air pulses of varying temperature, flow rate, and chemical composition (CO₂ concentration) to selectively stimulate mechanical, thermal, and chemical nociceptors. This allows differentiation of specific fiber-type dysfunction.

4. **[[In vivo confocal microscopy]]** (IVCM): Instruments such as the **Heidelberg Retina Tomograph with Rostock Corneal Module (HRT-RCM)** or **Nidek ConfoScan** allow direct visualization of the **sub-basal nerve plexus** at the level of [[Bowman's layer]]. Quantitative parameters include:
   - **Nerve fiber density** (total length of nerves per unit area, typically mm/mm²)
   - **Nerve fiber length**
   - **Nerve branch density**
   - **Nerve fiber tortuosity**
   
   Reduced sub-basal nerve density is a hallmark of long ciliary nerve damage and correlates with decreased corneal sensitivity.

### Pupillary Examination

Assessment of the sympathetic component of the long ciliary nerves involves:

1. **Pupil examination in light and dark**: Observation for [[anisocoria]] that is greater in dim illumination (characteristic of sympathetic deficit/Horner syndrome).

2. **Dilation lag test**: Photographing the pupils at 5 seconds and 15 seconds after dimming room lights. In Horner syndrome, the affected pupil dilates more slowly (dilation lag at 5 seconds that equalizes by 15 seconds).

3. **Pharmacological testing for [[Horner syndrome]]**:

| Test | Agent | Mechanism | Result in Horner Syndrome |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Confirmation** | [[Cocaine]] 4–10% (or [[apraclonidine]] 0.5–1%) | Cocaine blocks norepinephrine reuptake; apraclonidine is a weak α₁-agonist | Cocaine: affected pupil fails to dilate. Apraclonidine: reversal of anisocoria (affected pupil dilates due to denervation supersensitivity) |
| **Localization** | [[Hydroxyamphetamine]] 1% (Paredrine) | Releases stored norepinephrine from intact postganglionic nerve terminals | Fails to dilate in **postganglionic** (third-order) lesion (i.e., at the level of the long ciliary nerves); dilates in preganglionic lesions |

### Imaging

- **[[MRI]] of the brain and orbit**: To evaluate for lesions affecting the trigeminal nerve, nasociliary nerve, or orbital structures that may compromise the long ciliary nerves.
- **[[CT scan]] of the orbit**: Useful for evaluating bony orbital pathology, fractures, or tumors.
- **[[MR angiography]]** or **[[CT angiography]]**: May be indicated when Horner syndrome is suspected to evaluate for [[carotid artery dissection]] (which can damage the sympathetic plexus around the internal carotid artery proximal to the long ciliary nerves).
- **[[Optical coherence tomography]]** (OCT): While not directly imaging the long ciliary nerves, [[anterior segment OCT]] can document corneal epithelial and stromal changes secondary to neurotrophic keratopathy.

## Treatment and Management

Management of conditions related to long ciliary nerve dysfunction is directed at the specific clinical manifestation:

### Management of Neurotrophic Keratopathy

Treatment is staged according to the severity of corneal involvement:

| Stage | Treatment Approach |
|---|---|
| **Stage 1** (epithelial irregularity) | Preservative-free [[artificial tears]], [[lubricating ointments]], discontinuation of toxic topical medications, treatment of underlying cause (e.g., antiviral therapy for [[herpes zoster ophthalmicus]]) |
| **Stage 2** (persistent epithelial defect) | Preservative-free tears (frequent), [[autologous serum tears]] (20% concentration), [[bandage contact lens]], [[amniotic membrane transplantation]], [[tarsorrhaphy]] (temporary or permanent), [[botulinum toxin]]-induced ptosis |
| **Stage 3** (ulceration/perforation) | [[Tissue adhesive]] (cyanoacrylate glue), [[amniotic membrane graft]], [[conjunctival flap]], [[tectonic keratoplasty]], [[corneal transplantation]] |

#### Cenegermin (Oxervate)

**[[Cenegermin]]** (recombinant human [[nerve growth factor]], rhNGF) is the first FDA-approved pharmacological treatment specifically for [[neurotrophic keratitis]]. It is administered as a topical ophthalmic solution (0.002%, 20 μg/mL) six times daily for eight weeks. Cenegermin promotes corneal nerve regeneration and epithelial healing by binding to the [[TrkA receptor]] on corneal epithelial cells and residual nerve fibers. Clinical trials (REPARO and NGF0214) demonstrated complete corneal healing in approximately 70% of patients with stage 2 or 3 neurotrophic keratopathy.

#### Corneal Neurotization

**[[Corneal neurotization]]** is an emerging surgical technique that aims to restore corneal sensation by transferring a functioning donor nerve to the denervated cornea. Approaches include:

- **Direct neurotization**: Transfer of the [[supratrochlear nerve]] or [[supraorbital nerve]] (branches of V₁ from the contralateral or ipsilateral side) directly to the corneal limbus via a sural nerve graft or direct coaptation.
- **Indirect neurotization**: Placement of nerve grafts in the subconjunctival or corneal stromal plane to encourage nerve ingrowth.

Studies have demonstrated significant improvement in corneal sensitivity and resolution of neurotrophic keratopathy following corneal neurotization, with measurable nerve regeneration on [[in vivo confocal microscopy]].

### Management of Sympathetic Dysfunction (Horner Syndrome)

Treatment of [[Horner syndrome]] is directed at the underlying cause (e.g., treatment of [[carotid dissection]], removal of a [[Pancoast tumor]], etc.). The miosis itself is generally not treated unless cosmetically bothersome, in which case low-dose [[phenylephrine]] 2.5% drops can be used to dilate the pupil.

### Pain Management

For pain syndromes related to long ciliary nerve irritation:

- **[[Anterior uveitis]]**: [[Cycloplegic agents]] (e.g., [[atropine]] 1%, [[cyclopentolate]] 1%) paralyze the ciliary muscle and reduce iris sphincter spasm, alleviating pain mediated by the long ciliary nerves. [[Topical corticosteroids]] (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate]] 1%) reduce inflammation.
- **Neuropathic ocular pain**: [[Gabapentin]], [[pregabalin]], [[amitriptyline]], or [[duloxetine]] may be used for chronic neuropathic pain involving the long ciliary nerves. Topical [[autologous serum tears]] may also provide symptomatic relief.
- **[[Corneal neuralgia]]**: In cases of persistent corneal pain with normal slit-lamp findings (sometimes termed "corneal neuropathic pain" or "phantom corneal pain"), treatment may include systemic neuropathic pain medications, [[low-dose naltrexone]], or [[transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation]] (TENS).

### Regional Anesthesia Considerations

The long ciliary nerves are a critical target in **ophthalmic regional anesthesia**:

- **[[Retrobulbar block]]**: Injection of local anesthetic (e.g., [[lidocaine]], [[bupivacaine]]) into the [[retrobulbar space]] (intraconal space) blocks the long ciliary nerves (along with other orbital nerves), producing corneal and intraocular anesthesia and [[akinesia]].
- **[[Peribulbar block]]**: Injection into the extraconal space; anesthetic diffuses to block the long ciliary nerves, though onset may be slower than retrobulbar block.
- **[[Sub-Tenon's block]]**: Anesthetic delivered into the [[sub-Tenon's space]] (episcleral space) can block the long ciliary nerves as they course through the suprachoroidal space.
- **[[Topical anesthesia]]**: Agents such as [[proparacaine]] 0.5% or [[tetracaine]] 0.5% block the terminal sensory endings of the long ciliary nerves in the cornea, providing surface anesthesia for procedures such as [[tonometry]], [[gonioscopy]], and foreign body removal.

| Anesthetic Technique | Nerves Blocked | Onset | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Retrobulbar block** | Long ciliary, short ciliary, [[oculomotor nerve]], [[abducens nerve]] | 5–10 min | 2–6 hours |
| **Peribulbar block** | Same as retrobulbar (by diffusion) | 10–15 min | 2–6 hours |
| **Sub-Tenon's block** | Long ciliary, short ciliary nerves | 5–10 min | 1–3 hours |
| **Topical anesthesia** | Terminal long ciliary nerve endings in cornea | 15–30 sec | 15–20 min |

## Surgical Relevance

The long ciliary nerves are of significant importance in multiple ophthalmic surgical contexts:

### Refractive Surgery

- **[[LASIK]]**: Creation of the corneal flap and excimer laser ablation sever the sub-basal nerve plexus (terminal branches of the long ciliary nerves). This results in temporary corneal hypoesthesia and contributes to **post-LASIK [[dry eye]]**, which is the most common complication of the procedure. Corneal nerve regeneration occurs over 6–12 months but may remain incomplete for years.
- **[[PRK]]** and **[[SMILE]]**: PRK removes the epithelium and superficial stroma, disrupting the sub-basal plexus. SMILE (small incision lenticule extraction) causes less nerve disruption than LASIK due to the smaller incision and preservation of the anterior stroma, resulting in less post-operative dry eye.

### Cataract Surgery

- **[[Phacoemulsification]]**: The clear corneal incision transects corneal nerve fibers (terminal branches of the long ciliary nerves) at the incision site. This can contribute to transient corneal hypoesthesia and dry eye symptoms postoperatively.

### Retinal Surgery

- **[[Scleral buckle]]** surgery: Placement of an encircling band or segmental buckle can compress or damage the long ciliary nerves at their scleral penetration sites, potentially leading to sector corneal hypoesthesia.
- **[[Pars plana vitrectomy]]**: Sclerotomy sites are typically placed 3.5–4.0 mm posterior to the limbus, which is anterior to the scleral entry points of the long ciliary nerves. However, extensive dissection or cryotherapy may still affect these nerves.

### Corneal Transplantation

- **[[Penetrating keratoplasty]]** (PK): The full-thickness trephination severs all corneal nerve fibers within the graft. Reinnervation of the graft occurs slowly (over months to years) from the host corneal nerve plexus, and full recovery of corneal sensitivity is often incomplete.
- **[[Deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]]** (DALK): Preserves the host [[Descemet's membrane]] and [[endothelium]] but still disrupts the stromal nerve plexus. Nerve recovery may be slightly better than PK.
- **[[Endothelial keratoplasty]]** ([[DSAEK]]/[[DMEK]]): These procedures preserve the anterior corneal stroma and sub-basal nerve plexus, resulting in minimal disruption of long ciliary nerve terminal branches and better preservation of corneal sensitivity.

## Comparative Anatomy: Long vs. Short Ciliary Nerves

| Feature | Long Ciliary Nerves | [[Short Ciliary Nerves]] |
|---|---|---|
| **Number** | 2–3 | 6–10 |
| **Origin** | [[Nasociliary nerve]] (V₁) | [[Ciliary ganglion]] |
| **Relationship to ciliary ganglion** | Bypass (do not synapse) | Arise from ganglion |
| **Fiber types** | Sensory (V₁) + sympathetic (postganglionic) | Sensory (V₁) + parasympathetic (postganglionic) + sympathetic |
| **Parasympathetic fibers** | **Absent** | Present (to [[sphincter pupillae]] and [[ciliary muscle]]) |
| **Sympathetic target** | [[Dilator pupillae muscle]] | Ocular blood vessels (vasomotor) |
| **Primary sensory territory** | [[Cornea]], [[iris]], [[ciliary body]] | [[Choroid]], [[ciliary body]], [[iris]], [[cornea]] (overlap) |
| **Scleral penetration** | ~3.5–4.0 mm from optic disc, horizontal meridian | Around the optic nerve, more scattered |
| **Clinical significance** | Corneal sensation, mydriasis pathway, neurotrophic keratopathy | Accommodation, miosis, IOP regulation |

## Research and Future Directions

Current research involving the long ciliary nerves includes:

1. **Corneal nerve regeneration**: Studies are investigating the use of [[nerve growth factor]] (NGF), [[semaphorin 7A]], [[pigment epithelium-derived factor]] (PEDF), and other neurotrophic agents to accelerate corneal nerve regeneration after surgery or disease.

2. **Biomarkers for systemic neuropathy**: The sub-basal nerve plexus (terminal distribution of the long ciliary nerves) is increasingly recognized as a surrogate biomarker for systemic [[peripheral neuropathy]], particularly in [[diabetes mellitus]]. [[In vivo confocal microscopy]] of the corneal nerves can detect subclinical neuropathy before conventional nerve conduction studies become abnormal.

3. **Gene therapy**: Experimental approaches using viral vectors to deliver neurotrophic factor genes to the cornea are being explored to promote long ciliary nerve terminal regeneration.

4. **Corneal neurotization techniques**: Refinement of surgical techniques for corneal neurotization, including minimally invasive approaches and the use of processed nerve allografts, continues to evolve.

5. **Neuropathic pain mechanisms**: Research into the molecular mechanisms of corneal neuropathic pain (involving [[TRPV1]], [[TRPA1]], and [[Nav1.7]] sodium channels in long ciliary nerve fibers) may lead to targeted analgesic therapies.

---

## Summary

The long ciliary nerves are essential mixed nerve branches that provide sensory innervation to the [[cornea]], [[iris]], and [[ciliary body]], and carry sympathetic fibers to the [[dilator pupillae muscle]]. Their unique anatomical course—bypassing the [[ciliary ganglion]] and traversing the [[suprachoroidal space]]—makes them vulnerable to damage from infection, surgery, trauma, and systemic disease. Dysfunction of the long ciliary nerves manifests as decreased [[corneal sensitivity]], [[neurotrophic keratopathy]], [[miosis]] (as part of [[Horner syndrome]]), and ocular pain syndromes. Diagnosis relies on corneal esthesiometry, [[in vivo confocal microscopy]], pupillary pharmacological testing, and neuroimaging. Treatment ranges from lubrication and [[autologous serum tears]] to [[cenegermin]] (recombinant NGF) and [[corneal neurotization]] surgery. A comprehensive understanding of the long ciliary nerves is fundamental to the practice of optometry and ophthalmology.
