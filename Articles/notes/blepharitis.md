

# Blepharitis

---

**Blepharitis** is a common, chronic inflammatory condition of the [[eyelid]] margins that affects patients across all age groups and demographics. It represents one of the most frequently encountered diagnoses in both [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] clinical practice, with prevalence estimates suggesting that it affects up to 37–47% of patients seen by eye care professionals. Blepharitis is characterized by inflammation, irritation, and often colonization or infestation of the lid margin structures, leading to a constellation of symptoms including ocular discomfort, redness, crusting, and visual disturbance. Although rarely sight-threatening in isolation, blepharitis is a significant source of chronic morbidity, reduced quality of life, and is intimately associated with [[dry eye disease]] (DED), [[meibomian gland dysfunction]] (MGD), and secondary ocular surface complications. Effective management requires a thorough understanding of its multifactorial pathophysiology, accurate classification, and a sustained, multimodal therapeutic approach.

---

## Classification

Blepharitis is broadly classified based on the anatomical location of the predominant inflammation along the [[eyelid]] margin:

| **Type** | **Anatomical Location** | **Primary Etiology** | **Key Features** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Anterior Blepharitis** | Base of the [[eyelashes]] (anterior lid margin) | [[Staphylococcus]] species (staphylococcal blepharitis); [[Seborrheic dermatitis]] (seborrheic blepharitis) | Crusting, collarettes around lashes, lash loss ([[madarosis]]), lid margin erythema |
| **Posterior Blepharitis** | [[Meibomian glands]] (posterior lid margin) | [[Meibomian gland dysfunction]] (MGD), altered lipid secretion | Meibomian gland orifice plugging, turbid or thickened meibum, lid margin telangiectasia, evaporative [[dry eye]] |
| **Mixed Blepharitis** | Both anterior and posterior lid margins | Combination of above | Features of both anterior and posterior forms; most common clinical presentation |
| **Demodex Blepharitis** | Lash follicles and meibomian glands | Infestation by [[Demodex folliculorum]] and/or [[Demodex brevis]] | Cylindrical dandruff (collarettes) at lash base, lash misdirection, chronic recalcitrant symptoms |

Additional, less common forms include **angular blepharitis** (involving the lateral or medial [[canthus]], often associated with [[Moraxella lacunata]] infection) and **allergic blepharitis** (secondary to contact hypersensitivity to topical medications, cosmetics, or environmental allergens).

---

## Epidemiology

Blepharitis is among the most prevalent ocular conditions worldwide. Large-scale surveys of eye care practitioners in the United States have reported that blepharitis is observed in approximately 37% to 47% of all patients, though true population-based prevalence is difficult to ascertain due to underreporting and the chronic, fluctuating nature of the disease. The condition affects all races and both sexes, though certain subtypes show demographic predilections:

- **Staphylococcal blepharitis** is more common in women and younger patients.
- **Seborrheic blepharitis** is more prevalent in older adults and those with concurrent [[seborrheic dermatitis]] of the scalp and face.
- **Posterior blepharitis / MGD** increases in prevalence with age and is particularly common in Asian populations.
- **Demodex blepharitis** prevalence increases markedly with age, with studies demonstrating [[Demodex]] mite colonization in nearly 100% of individuals over 70 years of age, though not all colonized individuals are symptomatic.

Blepharitis is a leading contributing factor to [[dry eye disease]], and the two conditions frequently coexist, creating a vicious cycle of ocular surface inflammation.

---

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of blepharitis is multifactorial and varies according to the subtype, though common themes include microbial colonization, biofilm formation, lipid abnormalities, inflammatory cascades, and disruption of the [[tear film]].

### Anterior Blepharitis

#### Staphylococcal Blepharitis

The pathogenesis of staphylococcal blepharitis centers on chronic colonization and infection of the anterior lid margin by [[Staphylococcus aureus]] and, more commonly, [[Staphylococcus epidermidis]]. These organisms produce exotoxins (including alpha-toxin, delta-toxin, and toxic shock syndrome toxin), enzymes (lipases, proteases), and superantigens that directly damage the [[eyelid]] epithelium, lash follicles, and adjacent [[conjunctiva]]. The bacterial exotoxins provoke a cell-mediated and humoral [[immune response]], leading to chronic inflammation, marginal [[corneal]] infiltrates, and [[phlyctenulosis]] in severe cases. Staphylococcal lipases also degrade the lipid layer of the [[tear film]], contributing to tear instability and evaporative [[dry eye]].

A critical concept in staphylococcal blepharitis is the formation of **bacterial biofilms** along the lid margin. Biofilms are structured communities of bacteria encased in a self-produced extracellular polymeric matrix that confers resistance to both host immune defenses and topical antimicrobial agents. The biofilm serves as a persistent reservoir for bacterial toxins and inflammatory mediators, perpetuating the chronic nature of the disease.

#### Seborrheic Blepharitis

Seborrheic blepharitis is associated with [[seborrheic dermatitis]], a chronic inflammatory skin condition mediated in part by the lipophilic yeast [[Malassezia]] species (formerly *Pityrosporum*). In seborrheic blepharitis, there is hyperkeratinization and excessive desquamation of the lid margin skin, producing greasy, waxy scales along the lash bases. The inflammatory response is less intense than in staphylococcal blepharitis, and the condition is often bilateral and symmetric.

### Posterior Blepharitis (Meibomian Gland Dysfunction)

Posterior blepharitis is fundamentally a disorder of the [[meibomian glands]]—modified sebaceous glands embedded in the [[tarsal plate]] that produce the lipid (oil) layer of the [[tear film]]. In MGD, there is obstruction of the meibomian gland terminal ducts by hyperkeratinized epithelial cells, leading to stasis of meibum within the glands. The stagnant meibum undergoes qualitative changes: it becomes more viscous, has an elevated melting point, and contains increased saturated fatty acids and cholesterol esters. These altered lipids are less effective at stabilizing the [[tear film]], resulting in increased tear evaporation and evaporative [[dry eye disease]].

The obstructed glands become colonized by commensal bacteria (particularly [[Staphylococcus]], [[Propionibacterium acnes]], and [[Corynebacterium]] species), which produce lipases that further degrade meibomian lipids into free fatty acids. These free fatty acids are directly irritating to the [[ocular surface]] and stimulate inflammatory cytokine release, perpetuating a cycle of glandular inflammation (meibomitis), progressive gland dropout (atrophy), and worsening tear film instability.

Posterior blepharitis is strongly associated with dermatologic conditions such as [[rosacea]] (ocular rosacea), [[seborrheic dermatitis]], and [[atopic dermatitis]].

### Demodex Blepharitis

[[Demodex]] mites are obligate ectoparasites of human hair follicles and sebaceous glands. Two species are clinically relevant:

- **[[Demodex folliculorum]]**: Resides in the lash follicle; associated with anterior blepharitis, cylindrical dandruff (collarettes), lash misdirection ([[trichiasis]]), and [[madarosis]].
- **[[Demodex brevis]]**: Inhabits the [[meibomian glands]] and sebaceous glands of Zeis; associated with posterior blepharitis and MGD.

Demodex mites cause disease through several mechanisms:
1. **Mechanical damage**: Physical blockage of follicles and gland orifices; direct epithelial erosion by mite claws and mouthparts.
2. **Vector for bacteria**: Demodex mites carry bacteria (notably [[Bacillus oleronius]] and [[Staphylococcus]] species) on their surfaces and within their gastrointestinal tracts, introducing these organisms deep into follicles and glands.
3. **Immune-mediated inflammation**: Mite chitin, waste products, and associated bacterial antigens stimulate innate and adaptive immune responses, including [[T-cell]]-mediated granulomatous inflammation.
4. **Reactive oxygen species**: Demodex infestation has been associated with increased oxidative stress on the lid margin.

The pathognomonic clinical sign of Demodex blepharitis is **cylindrical dandruff**—waxy, translucent collarettes that sleeve the base of the lashes, representing a mixture of mite waste, keratin, and lipid debris.

### The Blepharitis–Dry Eye Vicious Cycle

Blepharitis and [[dry eye disease]] are inextricably linked. Lid margin inflammation disrupts the [[tear film]] lipid layer (via MGD and bacterial lipase activity), leading to increased tear evaporation, [[hyperosmolarity]], and ocular surface inflammation. The resulting ocular surface damage further stimulates inflammatory mediators that exacerbate lid margin disease, creating a self-perpetuating cycle. This concept is central to the modern understanding of blepharitis management and is reflected in the [[TFOS DEWS II]] classification of dry eye.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Blepharitis presents with a wide spectrum of symptoms and signs, which may be acute, subacute, or chronic. Symptoms are often bilateral but may be asymmetric.

### Symptoms

| **Symptom** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| Burning / stinging | Persistent burning or stinging sensation along the lid margins and ocular surface |
| Itching | Particularly prominent in Demodex and allergic blepharitis |
| Foreign body sensation | Gritty, sandy feeling; often worse upon waking |
| Tearing ([[epiphora]]) | Reflex tearing secondary to ocular surface irritation |
| Crusting of lids | Especially upon waking; flakes or scales on lashes |
| Red, swollen eyelids | Lid margin erythema and edema |
| Photophobia | Sensitivity to light, especially with corneal involvement |
| Blurred vision | Fluctuating vision due to tear film instability; may improve with blinking |
| Contact lens intolerance | Reduced wearing time, discomfort, and increased deposits |
| Eyelash loss ([[madarosis]]) | Chronic inflammation damages lash follicles |
| Eyelash misdirection ([[trichiasis]]) | Lashes grow inward, potentially abrading the [[cornea]] |

### Clinical Signs

#### Anterior Blepharitis

- **Collarettes**: Hard, fibrinous scales encircling the base of the lashes (staphylococcal); greasy, waxy scales (seborrheic)
- **Lid margin erythema and edema**
- **[[Madarosis]]**: Loss of eyelashes
- **[[Poliosis]]**: Whitening/depigmentation of lashes
- **[[Trichiasis]]**: Misdirected lashes
- **Ulceration of lid margin** (severe staphylococcal disease)
- **Cylindrical dandruff**: Pathognomonic for [[Demodex]] infestation

#### Posterior Blepharitis

- **Meibomian gland orifice capping/plugging**: Inspissated, waxy plugs occluding gland orifices
- **Turbid, thickened, or toothpaste-like meibum** on expression
- **Lid margin telangiectasia**: Dilated blood vessels along the posterior lid margin
- **Lid margin irregularity and notching**: Chronic scarring and architectural distortion
- **Foam in the [[tear film]]**: Saponification of abnormal lipids
- **Reduced or absent meibum expression**

#### Secondary Ocular Surface Findings

Chronic blepharitis may lead to a range of secondary complications involving the [[conjunctiva]] and [[cornea]]:

- **[[Conjunctival hyperemia]]**: Diffuse or papillary injection
- **[[Papillary conjunctivitis]]**: Particularly on the upper tarsal conjunctiva
- **Inferior [[punctate epithelial erosions]]**: Staining with [[fluorescein]] on the inferior cornea and conjunctiva
- **Marginal corneal infiltrates**: Sterile, immune-mediated infiltrates near the [[limbus]], often with a clear zone between the infiltrate and limbus; associated with staphylococcal hypersensitivity
- **[[Phlyctenulosis]]**: Nodular, immune-mediated lesion at the limbus or conjunctiva; represents a delayed-type hypersensitivity reaction to staphylococcal antigens
- **[[Corneal pannus]]**: Superficial vascularization of the cornea in chronic cases
- **[[Corneal ulceration]]**: Rare but serious; may occur with severe staphylococcal disease or secondary infection
- **[[Chalazion]]**: Chronic lipogranulomatous inflammation of an obstructed meibomian gland
- **[[Hordeolum]]** (stye): Acute, focal infection of a lash follicle (external) or meibomian gland (internal)
- **Tear film instability**: Reduced [[tear break-up time]] (TBUT)

## Diagnosis

The diagnosis of blepharitis is primarily clinical, based on a thorough history and [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] examination. However, several ancillary diagnostic modalities can aid in classification, severity grading, and identification of underlying etiologies.

### History

A comprehensive history should include:

- Duration and chronicity of symptoms
- Laterality and symmetry
- Aggravating and relieving factors
- History of skin conditions ([[rosacea]], [[seborrheic dermatitis]], [[atopic dermatitis]], [[psoriasis]])
- Contact lens wear history
- Cosmetic and eyelid hygiene practices
- Topical and systemic medication use
- Previous treatments and their efficacy
- Ocular surgical history (particularly [[cataract surgery]], [[refractive surgery]])

### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] is the cornerstone of blepharitis diagnosis. A systematic examination should evaluate:

1. **Lid margins**: Erythema, edema, telangiectasia, irregularity, ulceration, scarring
2. **Lashes**: Crusting, collarettes, cylindrical dandruff, madarosis, trichiasis, poliosis
3. **Meibomian gland orifices**: Capping, plugging, pouting, retroplacement
4. **Meibum quality and expressibility**: Assessed by digital expression of the lower lid meibomian glands; graded from clear (normal) to inspissated/toothpaste-like (severe MGD)
5. **Tear film**: Stability ([[tear break-up time]]), debris, foam, meniscus height
6. **Conjunctiva**: Hyperemia, papillae, follicles, phlyctenules
7. **Cornea**: Punctate epithelial erosions (with [[fluorescein]] staining), marginal infiltrates, pannus, ulceration, scarring

### Meibomian Gland Expression and Grading

Diagnostic expression of the meibomian glands is performed by applying gentle digital pressure to the lower (and/or upper) eyelid and observing the quality and quantity of expressed meibum. Several grading scales exist:

| **Grade** | **Meibum Quality** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Clear, easily expressed | Normal |
| 1 | Cloudy, mildly viscous | Mild MGD |
| 2 | Cloudy with granular debris | Moderate MGD |
| 3 | Thick, toothpaste-like, or no expression | Severe MGD |

### Meibography

[[Meibography]] is an imaging technique that allows direct visualization of the meibomian gland morphology through the everted eyelid using infrared transillumination or dedicated meibography devices (e.g., LipiView®, Oculus Keratograph®). Meibography reveals:

- Gland dropout (atrophy)
- Gland shortening or truncation
- Gland tortuosity and dilation
- Ghost glands (complete atrophy)

Meibography is invaluable for staging MGD severity and monitoring disease progression over time.

### Demodex Identification

When Demodex blepharitis is suspected (cylindrical dandruff, recalcitrant symptoms, older patients), definitive diagnosis can be achieved by:

- **Lash epilation and microscopy**: Epilating 2–4 lashes from each eyelid and examining them under light microscopy. Demodex mites, eggs, and larvae can be directly visualized at the lash root.
- **In vivo [[confocal microscopy]]**: Allows non-invasive, real-time visualization of Demodex mites within lash follicles and meibomian glands.

A count of >3 mites per 4 epilated lashes is generally considered clinically significant.

### Tear Film Assessment

- **[[Tear break-up time]] (TBUT)**: Measured with fluorescein; values <10 seconds suggest tear film instability.
- **[[Schirmer's test]]**: Assesses aqueous tear production; helps differentiate evaporative from aqueous-deficient dry eye.
- **[[Tear osmolarity]]**: Elevated osmolarity (>308 mOsm/L) supports a diagnosis of concurrent dry eye disease.
- **Inflammatory biomarkers**: [[Matrix metalloproteinase-9]] (MMP-9) testing (e.g., InflammaDry®) can detect elevated inflammatory markers on the ocular surface.

### Microbiological Culture

Lid margin cultures are not routinely performed but may be indicated in cases of:

- Severe or recalcitrant anterior blepharitis
- Suspected infectious keratitis secondary to blepharitis
- Atypical presentations
- Pre-operative assessment in patients with chronic blepharitis undergoing [[intraocular surgery]]

### Differential Diagnosis

The differential diagnosis of blepharitis includes:

- [[Sebaceous gland carcinoma]] (masquerading as chronic unilateral blepharitis or recurrent chalazion—**must be excluded in atypical, unilateral, or treatment-resistant cases**)
- [[Squamous cell carcinoma]] of the eyelid
- [[Basal cell carcinoma]] of the eyelid
- [[Allergic contact dermatitis]]
- [[Herpes simplex]] or [[herpes zoster]] blepharitis
- [[Pemphigoid]] or other cicatrizing conjunctival diseases
- [[Pediculosis]] (Phthirus pubis infestation of lashes)
- [[Psoriasis]] of the eyelids

> **Clinical Pearl**: Any unilateral, chronic, treatment-resistant blepharitis—especially with recurrent chalazia, lash loss, or lid margin destruction—warrants a **full-thickness eyelid biopsy** to exclude [[sebaceous gland carcinoma]], which can masquerade as blepharitis for months to years.

## Management and Treatment

Blepharitis is a chronic condition that requires long-term management rather than a definitive cure. Patient education regarding the chronic, relapsing nature of the disease and the importance of sustained lid hygiene is paramount. Treatment is multimodal and tailored to the specific subtype and severity.

### Lid Hygiene (Foundation of Therapy)

Lid hygiene is the cornerstone of blepharitis management across all subtypes and severities.

#### Warm Compresses

Application of warm compresses to the closed eyelids for **10–15 minutes, 1–2 times daily** serves to:

- Soften and liquefy inspissated meibum
- Facilitate meibomian gland expression
- Loosen crusts and debris from the lid margin
- Improve blood flow to the lid margin

Commercially available heated eye masks (e.g., Bruder mask, MGDRx EyeBag) provide more consistent and sustained therapeutic temperatures (approximately 40–45°C) compared to traditional warm washcloths.

#### Lid Scrubs / Lid Margin Cleansing

Following warm compresses, mechanical cleansing of the lid margin removes crusts, biofilm, collarettes, and debris. Options include:

- **Dilute baby shampoo** on a cotton-tipped applicator or clean washcloth (traditional but less favored due to potential irritation)
- **Commercial lid scrub pads/foams**: Hypochlorous acid-based cleansers (e.g., Avenova®, HypoChlor™), tea tree oil-based cleansers (e.g., Cliradex®, We Love Eyes®), or surfactant-based lid scrubs (e.g., OCuSOFT®)
- **Manuka honey-based products** (e.g., Optimel®): Possess antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties

#### Meibomian Gland Expression

Manual expression of the meibomian glands can be performed in-office by the clinician using digital pressure, a cotton-tipped applicator, or specialized expression forceps (e.g., Mastrota paddle). This evacuates stagnant, thickened meibum and promotes gland patency.

### Pharmacological Interventions

#### Topical Antibiotics

| **Agent** | **Formulation** | **Indication** | **Notes** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Erythromycin]] ointment (0.5%) | Ophthalmic ointment | Anterior staphylococcal blepharitis | Applied to lid margins BID–QID; well-tolerated |
| [[Bacitracin]] ointment | Ophthalmic ointment | Anterior staphylococcal blepharitis | Alternative to erythromycin |
| [[Azithromycin]] ophthalmic (1%) | Ophthalmic solution (AzaSite®) | Anterior and posterior blepharitis | Anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial; applied to lid margin BID × 2 days, then QD × remaining days of a 1-month course |
| [[Tobramycin]] / [[Tobramycin-dexamethasone]] | Ophthalmic ointment/drops | Moderate-severe blepharitis with significant inflammation | Short-term use; steroid component addresses inflammation |
| [[Metronidazole]] gel (0.75%) | Topical gel | Posterior blepharitis associated with [[rosacea]] | Applied to lid margins or periocular skin |

#### Topical Anti-inflammatory Agents

- **[[Corticosteroids]]**: Short-term (1–2 weeks) use of low-potency topical corticosteroids (e.g., [[loteprednol etabonate]] 0.5%, [[fluorometholone]] 0.1%) can rapidly reduce lid margin and ocular surface inflammation during acute exacerbations. Prolonged use is avoided due to risks of [[steroid-induced glaucoma]], [[posterior subcapsular cataract]], and secondary infection.
- **[[Cyclosporine A]] ophthalmic emulsion** (Restasis® 0.05%, Cequa® 0.09%): Calcineurin inhibitor that suppresses T-cell-mediated inflammation on the ocular surface. Useful for concurrent blepharitis-associated [[dry eye disease]]. Requires 4–6 weeks for onset of effect.
- **[[Lifitegrast]] ophthalmic solution** (Xiidra® 5%): Lymphocyte function-associated antigen-1 (LFA-1) antagonist that inhibits T-cell activation and inflammatory cytokine release. Indicated for dry eye disease signs and symptoms associated with blepharitis.

#### Oral Antibiotics (Anti-inflammatory Doses)

Oral antibiotics, particularly [[tetracycline]]-class agents, are a mainstay of treatment for moderate-to-severe posterior blepharitis and MGD due to their combined antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties:

| **Agent** | **Dose** | **Duration** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Doxycycline]] | 50–100 mg PO BID (or 40 mg modified-release QD) | 6–12 weeks; may be extended | Inhibits bacterial lipases, MMP activity, and pro-inflammatory cytokines; modifies meibomian lipid composition |
| [[Minocycline]] | 50–100 mg PO QD–BID | 6–12 weeks | Similar to doxycycline; may have superior CNS penetration |
| [[Azithromycin]] (oral) | 500 mg QD × 3 days, repeated monthly × 3 cycles (pulse therapy) | 3 months | Accumulates in tissues; anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial |

Tetracyclines are contraindicated in pregnant women, nursing mothers, and children under 12 years of age due to risks of dental staining and bone growth inhibition. [[Erythromycin]] or [[azithromycin]] may be used as alternatives in these populations.

#### Omega-3 Fatty Acid Supplementation

Oral supplementation with [[omega-3 essential fatty acids]] (EPA and DHA, typically 2000–3000 mg/day combined) has been shown in multiple studies to improve meibomian gland function, reduce lid margin inflammation, and improve tear film stability. The anti-inflammatory mechanism involves competitive inhibition of pro-inflammatory [[omega-6 fatty acid]] metabolites (e.g., prostaglandins, leukotrienes) and promotion of anti-inflammatory resolvins and protectins. However, the DREAM study (2018) showed equivocal results for omega-3 supplementation in dry eye, and clinical benefit may vary among patients.

### Treatment of Demodex Blepharitis

Demodex blepharitis requires targeted acaricidal therapy in addition to standard lid hygiene:

- **[[Tea tree oil]] (TTO)**: The active acaricidal component is **terpinen-4-ol** (T4O). In-office lid scrubs with 50% TTO followed by daily home use of diluted TTO-containing lid wipes or foams (e.g., Cliradex®, containing T4O) have demonstrated efficacy in reducing Demodex counts and improving symptoms. Full-strength TTO can cause significant ocular surface irritation and should only be applied in-office with careful technique.
- **[[Lotilaner]] ophthalmic solution 0.25% (Xdemvy®)**: FDA-approved in 2023 specifically for the treatment of Demodex blepharitis. Lotilaner is an isoxazoline-class parasiticide that inhibits Demodex mite gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA)-gated chloride channels, causing mite paralysis and death. Dosing is one drop in each eye BID for 6 weeks. Clinical trials (Saturn-1 and Saturn-2) demonstrated significant reduction in collarettes and lid margin erythema compared to vehicle.
- **[[Ivermectin]]**: Oral ivermectin (200 mcg/kg, single dose, repeated at 1 week) has been used off-label for severe or recalcitrant Demodex blepharitis. Topical ivermectin cream (1%) applied to the periocular skin has also shown efficacy.
- **[[Hypochlorous acid]] (HOCl) spray**: Antimicrobial and anti-biofilm properties; may reduce Demodex-associated bacterial load.

### In-Office Procedural Treatments

A growing array of in-office technologies and procedures are available for blepharitis and MGD management:

| **Procedure** | **Mechanism** | **Indication** |
|---|---|---|
| **BlephEx®** | Mechanical micro-exfoliation of the lid margin using a rotating medical-grade micro-sponge | Removal of biofilm, scurf, and debris from the lid margin; anterior blepharitis |
| **[[Thermal pulsation therapy]] (LipiFlow®)** | Vectored thermal pulsation: simultaneous application of controlled heat (42.5°C) to the inner eyelid and pulsatile pressure to the outer eyelid | Obstructive MGD; evacuates meibomian gland contents |
| **[[Intense pulsed light]] (IPL) therapy (e.g., OptiLight®)** | Broad-spectrum light pulses applied to the periocular skin; reduces telangiectasia, decreases inflammatory mediators, eradicates Demodex, and improves meibomian gland function | Posterior blepharitis, MGD, ocular rosacea |
| **Meibomian gland probing (Maskin probing)** | Insertion of a fine stainless-steel probe into meibomian gland orifices to relieve intraductal obstruction and fibrosis | Obstructive MGD refractory to conservative therapy |
| **iLux®** | Handheld device delivering LED-based heat and compression directly to individual meibomian glands | Obstructive MGD |
| **TearCare®** | Open-eye, wearable thermal device that delivers sustained heat to the meibomian glands, followed by manual expression | Obstructive MGD |
| **MiBoFlo Thermoflo®** | Thermoelectric device applying continuous controlled heat to the outer eyelid | MGD |
| **Zest (Zocular Eyelid System Treatment)** | In-office okra-based gel exfoliation of the lid margin and lashes | Anterior blepharitis, Demodex blepharitis, biofilm removal |

### Adjunctive Therapies

- **Artificial tears and lubricants**: Preservative-free [[artificial tears]] are recommended to supplement the tear film and alleviate symptoms of concurrent dry eye. Lipid-containing artificial tears (e.g., Systane Complete®, Refresh Optive Mega-3®) may be particularly beneficial in posterior blepharitis/MGD.
- **Punctal occlusion**: [[Punctal plugs]] or [[punctal cautery]] may be considered in patients with significant aqueous tear deficiency coexisting with blepharitis, to increase tear retention on the ocular surface.
- **Environmental and lifestyle modifications**: Humidifiers, avoidance of direct airflow (fans, air conditioning), reduction of screen time, and adequate hydration.
- **Cosmetic counseling**: Patients should be advised to remove all eye makeup thoroughly before sleep, avoid waterproof mascara (which is more difficult to remove and may occlude meibomian glands), and replace cosmetics regularly to prevent microbial contamination.

## Complications

Chronic, inadequately managed blepharitis can lead to a number of significant ocular complications:

- **[[Chalazion]]**: Chronic granulomatous inflammation of an obstructed meibomian gland; may require incision and curettage or intralesional [[triamcinolone]] injection
- **[[Hordeolum]]**: Acute bacterial infection (external: lash follicle/gland of Zeis; internal: meibomian gland)
- **[[Dry eye disease]]**: Evaporative and/or aqueous-deficient
- **[[Corneal neovascularization]] and [[pannus]]**
- **Marginal [[corneal]] infiltrates and [[phlyctenulosis]]**
- **[[Corneal ulceration]]** and scarring (rare)
- **[[Conjunctival scarring]] and [[symblepharon]]** (rare, in severe cicatrizing forms)
- **[[Madarosis]] and [[trichiasis]]**: Chronic lash follicle damage
- **Lid margin scarring, notching, and architectural distortion**
- **Reduced visual acuity**: Due to corneal irregularity, tear film instability, or corneal scarring
- **Impact on [[intraocular surgery]] outcomes**: Blepharitis is a significant risk factor for post-operative [[endophthalmitis]] following [[cataract surgery]] and must be aggressively treated pre-operatively

---

## Prognosis

Blepharitis is a chronic, relapsing condition. While symptoms can be effectively controlled with sustained lid hygiene and appropriate medical therapy, complete and permanent resolution is uncommon. Patients should be counseled that blepharitis management is analogous to managing a chronic dermatologic condition—ongoing maintenance therapy is essential to prevent flares. Early identification and treatment of complications (particularly corneal involvement and meibomian gland atrophy) are critical to preserving long-term ocular surface health and visual function.

With the advent of newer targeted therapies (e.g., [[lotilaner]] for Demodex blepharitis), advanced in-office procedures (e.g., [[thermal pulsation therapy]], [[intense pulsed light]]), and improved understanding of the blepharitis–dry eye continuum, the therapeutic landscape for blepharitis continues to evolve, offering improved outcomes for patients.
