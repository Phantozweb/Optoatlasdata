

# Accommodative Esotropia

**Accommodative Esotropia** is a convergent [[strabismus]] in which an inward deviation of the eyes is driven, in whole or in part, by the [[accommodation]]-[[convergence]] synkinesis. It is the most common form of childhood [[esotropia]], typically presenting between the ages of 6 months and 7 years (peak onset 2–3 years), and represents a significant proportion of all pediatric strabismus cases encountered in clinical [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] practice. The condition arises because of the physiological linkage between the accommodative effort required to overcome [[hyperopia]] (or to focus at near) and the reflexive convergence response mediated through the [[medial rectus]] muscles. When this synkinesis is excessive relative to the patient's fusional divergence reserves, manifest esotropia results.

Accommodative esotropia is broadly classified into three subtypes: **refractive accommodative esotropia**, **non-refractive accommodative esotropia** (high accommodative convergence/accommodation ratio, or high [[AC/A ratio]]), and **partially accommodative esotropia** (mixed mechanism). Accurate classification is essential because management strategies differ substantially among subtypes. Left untreated, accommodative esotropia may lead to [[amblyopia]], loss of [[binocular vision]], and permanent disruption of [[stereopsis]].

---

## Epidemiology

Accommodative esotropia accounts for approximately **40–55%** of all esotropia cases in the pediatric population. The overall prevalence of esotropia in the general population is estimated at 1–2%, making accommodative esotropia one of the most frequently encountered binocular vision disorders in primary eye care.

| Epidemiological Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Proportion of all childhood esotropia | 40–55% |
| Typical age of onset | 6 months – 7 years |
| Peak age of onset | 2–3 years |
| Gender predilection | None (equal M:F) |
| Mean hyperopic refractive error at presentation | +3.00 to +6.00 D |
| Prevalence of amblyopia at presentation | 30–50% |
| Family history of strabismus | ~25–40% of cases |

There is a strong association with moderate-to-high [[hyperopia]]. A positive family history of strabismus or significant refractive error is present in a substantial minority of patients, suggesting a polygenic hereditary component. Accommodative esotropia is more common in Caucasian populations compared to African and Asian populations, though it occurs across all ethnic groups.

## Pathophysiology

### The Accommodation-Convergence Synkinesis

The pathophysiology of accommodative esotropia is rooted in the neurophysiological coupling between [[accommodation]] and [[convergence]], two components of the [[near triad]] (which also includes [[miosis]]). When the [[ciliary muscle]] contracts to increase the refractive power of the [[crystalline lens]] for near focus, a proportional convergence response is simultaneously generated via the [[medial rectus]] muscles of both eyes. This linkage is mediated through the [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]] and the [[oculomotor nucleus]] (cranial nerve III, [[CN III]]) in the [[midbrain]].

In a visually normal, emmetropic individual, the amount of convergence elicited by a given level of accommodation is appropriate for the viewing distance, and any residual misalignment is corrected by [[fusional vergence]] mechanisms. In accommodative esotropia, this balance is disrupted by one or both of the following mechanisms:

### 1. Refractive (Hyperopic) Mechanism

In **refractive accommodative esotropia**, the patient has uncorrected [[hyperopia]], typically ranging from +2.00 to +6.00 diopters (D) or more. To achieve clear retinal imagery, the hyperopic eye must exert accommodative effort even for distance viewing. This excessive accommodation triggers a correspondingly excessive convergence response. When the magnitude of this convergence exceeds the patient's [[fusional divergence]] (negative fusional vergence) amplitude, the eyes deviate inward, producing manifest esotropia.

The key relationships can be expressed as:

> **Convergence demand = Accommodation × (AC/A ratio)**

In refractive accommodative esotropia, the [[AC/A ratio]] is typically **normal** (3:1 to 5:1, with a mean of approximately 4 prism diopters of convergence per diopter of accommodation). The excessive convergence is driven by the abnormally high accommodative demand imposed by the uncorrected hyperopia, not by an abnormal gain in the AC/A synkinesis itself.

### 2. Non-Refractive (High AC/A Ratio) Mechanism

In **non-refractive accommodative esotropia**, the patient may have little or no significant hyperopia, or the hyperopia is fully corrected, yet esotropia persists at near fixation. The underlying abnormality is an elevated [[AC/A ratio]]—typically **greater than 6:1**—meaning that each diopter of accommodation generates a disproportionately large convergence response. These patients characteristically demonstrate orthophoria or a small esophoria at distance with a significantly larger esotropia at near.

### 3. Mixed (Partially Accommodative) Mechanism

**Partially accommodative esotropia** occurs when optical correction of the full hyperopic refractive error reduces but does not eliminate the esotropia. In these cases, there is an accommodative component superimposed on a non-accommodative (structural or innervational) component. The residual deviation after full optical correction may be due to:

- Contracture or structural changes in the [[medial rectus]] muscle
- Anomalous innervational patterns
- Decompensation of a pre-existing [[esophoria]]
- Secondary motor adaptations from prolonged misalignment

### Neural Adaptation and Sensory Consequences

Prolonged accommodative esotropia in a young child triggers a cascade of sensory adaptations within the [[visual cortex]]:

- **[[Suppression]]**: The cortex actively inhibits the image from the deviating eye to avoid [[diplopia]] and [[visual confusion]]. In children, this occurs rapidly due to cortical plasticity.
- **[[Amblyopia]]**: Chronic suppression of one eye (particularly if the deviation is unilateral or predominantly affects one eye) leads to reduced [[visual acuity]] in the suppressed eye—termed **strabismic amblyopia**.
- **[[Anomalous retinal correspondence]] (ARC)**: The visual system may remap the spatial relationship between the two eyes' retinal images, establishing a non-corresponding binocular relationship.
- **Loss of [[stereopsis]]**: Disruption of normal binocular cortical development results in degraded or absent stereoscopic depth perception.

These sensory adaptations are more likely to become permanent if the esotropia is not corrected during the critical period of visual development (approximately birth to 8–10 years of age), underscoring the urgency of early diagnosis and treatment.

## Classification

| Subtype | Refractive Error | AC/A Ratio | Distance Deviation | Near Deviation | Response to Full Optical Correction |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Refractive Accommodative ET** | Moderate-high hyperopia (+2.00 to +6.00 D or more) | Normal (3:1 to 5:1) | Esotropia | Esotropia (similar to distance) | Full correction of deviation |
| **Non-Refractive (High AC/A) Accommodative ET** | Low hyperopia or emmetropia | High (>6:1) | Orthophoria or small esophoria | Significant esotropia | Distance alignment corrected; near esotropia persists |
| **Partially Accommodative ET** | Variable hyperopia | Normal or high | Esotropia (reduced with correction) | Esotropia (reduced with correction) | Partial correction; residual deviation remains |
| **Decompensated Accommodative ET** | Variable | Variable | Esotropia (constant, large) | Esotropia (constant, large) | Minimal or no response |

### Decompensated Accommodative Esotropia

A subset of patients with initially well-controlled accommodative esotropia may experience **decompensation** over time. In these cases, the deviation becomes constant and no longer responds to optical correction. This may occur due to:

- Progressive loss of fusional vergence reserves
- Development of medial rectus contracture
- Inadequate compliance with spectacle wear
- Progression of the underlying refractive error without corresponding optical update

Decompensated accommodative esotropia often requires surgical intervention in addition to continued optical correction.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### History

The typical patient is a child between 1 and 4 years of age whose parents notice intermittent or constant inward turning of one or both eyes. Key historical features include:

- **Intermittent crossing** of the eyes, often more noticeable during near tasks (reading, coloring, screen use) or when the child is fatigued or ill
- **Gradual progression** from intermittent to constant deviation if untreated
- **Closing or squinting** one eye, particularly in bright light or during near work
- **Head tilt or face turn** (less common than in [[superior oblique palsy]] but may occur)
- **Clumsiness** or difficulty with depth-related tasks
- **Asthenopia** (eye strain) in older, verbal children—though young children rarely report this symptom
- **Diplopia**: Older children or adults with acute-onset accommodative esotropia may report frank [[diplopia]]

### Signs

| Clinical Sign | Description |
|---|---|
| Manifest esotropia | Inward deviation of one or both eyes, variable or constant |
| Unilateral or alternating | May consistently affect one eye (unilateral) or alternate between eyes |
| Increased deviation at near | Especially prominent in high AC/A ratio subtype |
| Hyperopic refractive error | Detected on [[cycloplegic refraction]] |
| Amblyopia | Reduced [[best-corrected visual acuity]] in one eye (30–50% of cases at presentation) |
| Reduced or absent stereopsis | Demonstrated on [[stereoacuity]] testing |
| Suppression | Detected on [[Worth 4-dot test]], [[Bagolini striated lenses]], or similar tests |
| Accommodative excess signs | Possible accommodative spasm or variability in refraction findings |

## Diagnosis

### Comprehensive Examination Protocol

A systematic diagnostic evaluation is essential for accurate classification and management planning. The following components are critical:

#### 1. Visual Acuity Assessment

- Age-appropriate [[visual acuity]] testing (e.g., [[Teller acuity cards]], [[Lea symbols]], [[HOTV]], [[Snellen chart]])
- Monocular and binocular acuity
- Assessment for [[amblyopia]] (interocular acuity difference ≥2 lines)

#### 2. Cycloplegic Refraction

**[[Cycloplegic refraction]]** is the single most critical diagnostic test in the evaluation of accommodative esotropia. It must be performed to reveal the full magnitude of the hyperopic refractive error, which is often masked by the child's active accommodation during non-cycloplegic (manifest) refraction.

- **Preferred cycloplegic agents**:
  - [[Cyclopentolate]] 1% (most commonly used; instill 1 drop, repeat in 5 minutes, refract 30–40 minutes after last drop)
  - [[Atropine]] 1% (gold standard for complete cycloplegia; used as 1 drop BID for 3 days prior to examination, particularly in darkly pigmented irides or when cyclopentolate is insufficient)
- **Retinoscopy** is the preferred objective technique, supplemented by autorefraction when available

#### 3. Cover Testing

- **[[Cover-uncover test]]**: Identifies the presence and laterality of a [[tropia]]
- **[[Alternating cover test]]** with [[prism bar]] or loose prisms: Quantifies the total deviation (tropia + phoria) at distance (6 meters) and near (33–40 cm)
- Measurements should be obtained **with and without** the full hyperopic correction in place to determine the accommodative component
- **[[Simultaneous prism and cover test]]**: Differentiates the tropia from the total deviation

#### 4. AC/A Ratio Determination

The [[AC/A ratio]] can be measured by two methods:

- **Heterophoria method (calculated AC/A)**:

> AC/A = IPD (cm) + (Near deviation − Distance deviation) / Accommodative demand (D)

- **Gradient method**: Measure the change in deviation at a fixed distance when lenses of known power (e.g., +3.00 D or −3.00 D) are introduced. The gradient AC/A ratio is calculated as:

> AC/A (gradient) = Change in deviation (Δ) / Lens power (D)

A gradient AC/A ratio **>6:1** is considered elevated and supports a diagnosis of non-refractive (high AC/A) accommodative esotropia.

#### 5. Sensory Testing

- **[[Stereoacuity]]**: [[Titmus stereo test]], [[Randot stereo test]], [[Lang stereo test]], or [[Frisby stereo test]]
- **[[Worth 4-dot test]]**: Assesses fusion and suppression at distance and near
- **[[Bagolini striated lenses]]**: Evaluates sensory fusion status and anomalous correspondence
- **[[4-base-out prism test]]**: Screens for the presence of suppression and monofixation

#### 6. Ocular Motility and Alignment

- Full [[extraocular motility]] assessment in all nine diagnostic positions of gaze
- Evaluation for [[overaction]] or [[underaction]] of any extraocular muscle
- Assessment for [[inferior oblique overaction]] or [[dissociated vertical deviation]] (DVD), which may coexist

#### 7. Anterior and Posterior Segment Examination

- [[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] and [[dilated fundus examination]] to rule out organic causes of reduced vision (e.g., [[retinoblastoma]], [[optic nerve hypoplasia]], [[cataract]])

#### 8. Additional Testing

- **[[Krimsky test]]** or **[[Hirschberg test]]**: Useful in preverbal or uncooperative children for estimating the angle of deviation
- **Post-atropinization alignment assessment**: In cases where the full cycloplegic refraction is prescribed, re-evaluation of alignment with spectacles in place confirms the accommodative nature of the deviation

### Diagnostic Criteria Summary

| Criterion | Refractive Type | High AC/A Type | Partially Accommodative |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hyperopia | ≥+2.00 D (usually +3.00 to +6.00 D) | Variable (may be low) | Variable |
| AC/A ratio | Normal (3:1–5:1) | Elevated (>6:1) | Normal or elevated |
| Deviation at distance (without Rx) | Esotropia | Ortho/small esophoria | Esotropia |
| Deviation at near (without Rx) | Esotropia (≈ distance) | Large esotropia | Esotropia |
| Response to full hyperopic Rx | Full correction of ET | Distance corrected; near ET persists | Partial correction; residual ET |

## Differential Diagnosis

It is essential to distinguish accommodative esotropia from other forms of esotropia, as management differs significantly:

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| [[Infantile esotropia]] | Onset <6 months; large, constant angle (>40Δ); not corrected by hyperopic lenses; associated with [[latent nystagmus]], DVD, cross-fixation |
| [[Sensory deprivation esotropia]] | Secondary to unilateral visual loss (e.g., cataract, retinoblastoma); organic cause identified |
| [[Sixth nerve palsy]] ([[CN VI palsy]]) | Limitation of [[abduction]]; incomitant deviation (larger in the field of action of the paretic lateral rectus); may have neurological signs |
| [[Duane retraction syndrome]] | Limitation of abduction with globe retraction and narrowing of the palpebral fissure on adduction |
| [[Cyclic esotropia]] | Esotropia present on alternate days (48-hour cycle); rare |
| [[Convergence excess]] (non-accommodative) | Near esotropia with normal AC/A ratio; not responsive to plus lenses |
| [[Acute acquired comitant esotropia]] | Sudden onset in older children/adults; diplopia; may be associated with neurological pathology |

## Management

### Principles of Treatment

The goals of management in accommodative esotropia are:

1. **Achieve ocular alignment** (eliminate or minimize the esotropia)
2. **Treat and prevent [[amblyopia]]**
3. **Restore and maintain [[binocular vision]] and [[stereopsis]]**
4. **Provide clear, comfortable vision at all distances**

### Optical Correction

#### Full Hyperopic Correction

The cornerstone of treatment for refractive accommodative esotropia is the prescription of the **full cycloplegic hyperopic refractive error**. This eliminates the excessive accommodative demand and, consequently, the excessive convergence response.

- **Spectacles** are the primary mode of optical correction in young children
- The full plus should be prescribed initially, even if the child has never worn glasses and the prescription seems high
- **Do not empirically reduce the plus power** at the initial prescription; the full cycloplegic finding should be given to maximize the anti-accommodative effect
- Re-evaluation should occur **6–8 weeks** after dispensing to assess alignment with correction in place
- If the deviation is fully corrected with spectacles, the diagnosis of refractive accommodative esotropia is confirmed

#### Bifocal or Progressive Addition Lenses

For patients with **non-refractive (high AC/A ratio) accommodative esotropia**, the distance deviation may be controlled with the full hyperopic correction, but a significant near esotropia persists. In these cases, **[[bifocal lenses]]** with a near addition (typically **+2.50 to +3.00 D**) are prescribed.

- **Executive (flat-top) bifocals** are preferred over round-segment bifocals because the segment line can be set at the level of the pupil center, ensuring the child looks through the near addition during all near tasks
- **[[Progressive addition lenses]]** (PALs) may be used in older, cosmetically conscious children, though the narrower near zone may be less effective in younger patients
- The near add is titrated to the minimum power that achieves alignment at near

#### Contact Lenses

**[[Contact lenses]]** may be considered in select cases:

- Children with high hyperopia who experience significant spectacle magnification or prismatic effects
- Anisometropic patients in whom spectacle correction induces unacceptable [[aniseikonia]]
- Older children and adolescents who prefer contact lenses for cosmetic or functional reasons
- Contact lenses do not provide a bifocal effect as easily, though [[multifocal contact lenses]] have been explored in limited studies

### Amblyopia Treatment

Amblyopia is present in **30–50%** of patients with accommodative esotropia at the time of diagnosis and must be treated concurrently with optical correction.

- **[[Occlusion therapy]] (patching)**: The sound eye is patched for a prescribed number of hours per day (typically 2–6 hours/day depending on severity and age) to stimulate the amblyopic eye
- **[[Atropine penalization]]**: Instillation of [[atropine]] 1% in the sound eye (typically 1 drop on weekends or daily) blurs the near vision of the dominant eye, forcing use of the amblyopic eye. This is an effective alternative for children who are non-compliant with patching
- **Optical penalization**: Deliberate undercorrection or overcorrection of the sound eye's refractive error to blur its vision
- Treatment should continue until visual acuity is equalized or a stable plateau is reached, with monitoring for recurrence

### Pharmacological Interventions

#### Miotics

**[[Phospholine iodide]] (echothiophate iodide) 0.125%** and other [[miotic]] agents (e.g., [[pilocarpine]]) have been used historically as adjunctive treatment for accommodative esotropia, particularly the high AC/A ratio subtype.

- Mechanism: Miotics stimulate the [[ciliary muscle]] directly, reducing the accommodative effort (and thus the convergence demand) required for near focus
- Echothiophate iodide is a long-acting [[anticholinesterase]] agent instilled once daily
- **Side effects** include:
  - [[Iris cysts]] (mitigated by concurrent use of [[phenylephrine]] 2.5%)
  - Brow ache
  - Potential for prolonged action of [[succinylcholine]] during general anesthesia (important surgical consideration)
  - Retinal detachment risk (theoretical)
- Miotics are now used infrequently due to side effects and the availability of effective optical and surgical alternatives, but they remain a viable option in select cases where bifocals are refused or impractical

#### Atropine (Diagnostic and Therapeutic)

- **Diagnostic**: Atropine cycloplegia is used to determine the full hyperopic refractive error
- **Therapeutic**: Atropine penalization of the sound eye is used for amblyopia treatment (as described above)

### Orthoptic and Vision Therapy

**[[Orthoptics]]** and **[[vision therapy]]** may play a supportive role in the management of accommodative esotropia, particularly in older children and adults:

- **[[Fusional vergence training]]**: Exercises to increase [[negative fusional vergence]] (divergence) amplitudes, which may help compensate for residual esophoria or small-angle residual esotropia
- **Anti-suppression therapy**: Techniques to reduce cortical suppression and promote binocular integration
- **Accommodative facility training**: Exercises to improve the flexibility and accuracy of the accommodative response

Vision therapy is generally considered adjunctive rather than primary treatment and is most effective when the accommodative component has been addressed optically.

### Surgical Management

Surgery is indicated in accommodative esotropia under specific circumstances:

#### Indications for Surgery

1. **Partially accommodative esotropia**: A significant residual esotropia (typically ≥10 prism diopters) persists despite full optical correction and amblyopia treatment
2. **Decompensated accommodative esotropia**: The deviation no longer responds to optical correction
3. **High AC/A ratio esotropia unresponsive to bifocals**: When bifocal lenses fail to control the near deviation or are not tolerated
4. **Cosmetically or functionally significant residual deviation**: Even small residual deviations may warrant surgery if they impair binocular function or cause psychosocial distress

#### Surgical Procedures

| Procedure | Description | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| **Bilateral [[medial rectus recession]]** | Weakening procedure; both medial recti are recessed (moved posteriorly on the globe) | Most common procedure for partially accommodative ET with equal deviation at distance and near |
| **Recession-resection procedure** | Recession of the medial rectus and resection of the [[lateral rectus]] of the same eye | Preferred when deviation is predominantly unilateral |
| **Augmented bilateral medial rectus recession with posterior fixation suture ([[Faden procedure]])** | Medial rectus recession combined with a suture fixing the muscle to the sclera posterior to its arc of contact | High AC/A ratio esotropia with significant near-distance disparity; the Faden procedure selectively weakens the muscle's effectiveness at near |
| **Bilateral medial rectus recession with adjustable sutures** | Recession with sutures that allow postoperative adjustment | Older children and adults; allows fine-tuning of alignment |

#### Surgical Principles

- **The full hyperopic correction must continue to be worn postoperatively**. Surgery addresses only the non-accommodative component; removing spectacles after surgery will result in recurrence of the accommodative deviation.
- Surgical dosing is based on the **residual deviation measured with full optical correction in place**, not the total deviation without glasses.
- Overcorrection should be avoided, as consecutive [[exotropia]] in a child may be more difficult to manage than residual esotropia.

### Prism Therapy

**[[Fresnel prisms]]** or ground-in [[prism]] may be used in specific situations:

- Temporary management of residual deviation while awaiting surgery
- Small residual deviations insufficient to warrant surgery but causing symptoms
- Diagnostic trial to assess potential for binocular fusion before surgical intervention
- Base-out prism is prescribed to compensate for the esotropic deviation

## Natural History and Prognosis

### Favorable Prognostic Factors

- Early diagnosis and prompt initiation of full optical correction
- Good compliance with spectacle wear
- Alternating (rather than unilateral) fixation pattern
- Successful amblyopia treatment
- Presence of some degree of binocular function at presentation

### Long-Term Outcomes

- **Approximately 20–25%** of patients with refractive accommodative esotropia may eventually "outgrow" the need for hyperopic correction as the hyperopia decreases with age (emmetropization), though this is unpredictable and should not be relied upon as a management strategy
- Many patients require lifelong optical correction to maintain alignment
- Gradual reduction of the hyperopic correction may be attempted in adolescence or adulthood under careful monitoring; any reduction that causes recurrence of the deviation should be reversed
- Patients with high AC/A ratio esotropia may eventually tolerate reduction or elimination of the bifocal add as accommodative demand decreases with [[presbyopia]]
- Long-term binocular outcomes are best when treatment is initiated early and compliance is maintained

### Complications of Untreated or Poorly Managed Accommodative Esotropia

- **Dense [[amblyopia]]** with permanent visual acuity loss
- **Loss of [[binocular vision]]** and [[stereopsis]]
- **[[Anomalous retinal correspondence]]** (ARC) with abnormal binocular sensory adaptation
- **Psychosocial impact**: Strabismus in children and adults is associated with reduced self-esteem, social stigma, and impaired quality of life
- **Decompensation** to a non-accommodative, constant esotropia requiring surgical intervention

## Special Considerations

### Accommodative Esotropia in Adults

While classically a pediatric condition, accommodative esotropia may present or recur in adults:

- **Late-onset accommodative esotropia**: Rare; may occur in previously emmetropic adults who develop hyperopia or lose fusional vergence reserves
- **Recurrence after childhood treatment**: Adults who discontinue spectacle wear or whose refractive error changes may experience recurrence
- Adults typically present with **diplopia** rather than suppression, as the mature visual cortex lacks the plasticity for sensory adaptation
- Management follows the same principles: full hyperopic correction, prism if needed, and surgery for residual non-accommodative components

### Accommodative Esotropia and Myopia Development

Some children with accommodative esotropia who are prescribed full hyperopic correction may later develop [[myopia]]. The relationship between prolonged plus lens wear and myopia development is debated, but clinicians should monitor refractive changes carefully and adjust prescriptions accordingly.

### Role of the Optometrist

The [[optometrist]] plays a central role in the detection, diagnosis, and ongoing management of accommodative esotropia:

- **Primary detection**: Often the first clinician to identify the strabismus during routine pediatric eye examinations
- **Cycloplegic refraction and prescription**: Determining and prescribing the full hyperopic correction
- **Amblyopia management**: Directing and monitoring occlusion or penalization therapy
- **Co-management with ophthalmology**: Referring for surgical evaluation when indicated and providing postoperative optical and orthoptic care
- **Long-term monitoring**: Tracking refractive changes, alignment stability, binocular function, and visual acuity over years of follow-up

---

## Summary

Accommodative esotropia is a common, treatable form of childhood strabismus driven by the physiological link between [[accommodation]] and [[convergence]]. Accurate diagnosis requires thorough [[cycloplegic refraction]], careful measurement of the deviation at distance and near with and without optical correction, and determination of the [[AC/A ratio]]. The mainstay of treatment is full hyperopic optical correction, supplemented by bifocal lenses for high AC/A ratio subtypes, amblyopia therapy as needed, and surgical intervention for non-accommodative residual deviations. Early detection and consistent management are critical to preserving [[binocular vision]], preventing [[amblyopia]], and optimizing long-term visual outcomes.
