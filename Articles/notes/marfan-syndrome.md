# Marfan Syndrome

**Marfan syndrome** (MFS; OMIM #154700) is a heritable, autosomal dominant [[connective tissue disorder]] caused by pathogenic variants in the ***FBN1*** gene encoding [[fibrillin-1]], a critical glycoprotein component of extracellular [[microfibrils]]. The condition is multisystemic, with cardinal manifestations involving the [[ocular]], [[cardiovascular]], and [[musculoskeletal]] systems. Marfan syndrome holds particular significance in optometric and ophthalmologic practice because ocular findings—most notably [[ectopia lentis]] (subluxation of the [[crystalline lens]])—are among the earliest and most diagnostically important features of the disease, frequently prompting the initial clinical suspicion that leads to systemic diagnosis.

First described by French pediatrician Antoine-Bernard Marfan in 1896, the syndrome affects approximately 1 in 5,000 to 1 in 10,000 individuals worldwide, with no predilection for sex, race, or ethnicity. Roughly 75% of cases are inherited from an affected parent, while approximately 25% arise from *de novo* mutations. The phenotypic expression of Marfan syndrome is remarkably variable, even within the same family, ranging from mild isolated features to severe neonatal presentations with life-threatening cardiovascular compromise.

## Genetics and Molecular Biology

Marfan syndrome is caused by heterozygous loss-of-function or dominant-negative mutations in the ***FBN1*** gene, located on chromosome 15q21.1. Over 3,000 distinct pathogenic variants have been catalogued in the Universal Mutation Database for *FBN1*. The gene encodes **[[fibrillin-1]]**, a large (~350 kDa) cysteine-rich glycoprotein that polymerizes to form 10–12 nm [[microfibrils]] in the [[extracellular matrix]] (ECM). These microfibrils serve two fundamental roles:

1. **Structural scaffolding**: Microfibrils provide tensile strength and elasticity to tissues, particularly those subjected to mechanical stress (e.g., the [[zonular fibers]] of the lens, the [[aortic wall]], [[periosteum]], and [[dura mater]]).
2. **Regulation of [[transforming growth factor-beta]] (TGF-β) signaling**: Fibrillin-1 microfibrils sequester the latent form of TGF-β in the ECM. When fibrillin-1 is deficient or dysfunctional, excessive release and activation of TGF-β occurs, driving pathological tissue remodeling, [[apoptosis]], and [[matrix metalloproteinase]] (MMP) upregulation.

The inheritance pattern is **[[autosomal dominant]]** with complete [[penetrance]] but highly variable [[expressivity]]. Genotype–phenotype correlations are complex; however, certain generalizations have emerged:

| Mutation Type | Typical Phenotypic Association |
|---|---|
| Missense mutations affecting cysteine residues in cbEGF domains | Classic Marfan syndrome with ectopia lentis |
| Premature termination codons (haploinsufficiency) | More severe cardiovascular disease; less frequent ectopia lentis |
| In-frame exon skipping in exons 24–32 | Neonatal Marfan syndrome (severe, early-onset) |
| Mutations in the TGF-β binding protein-like (TB) domains | Variable; may affect TGF-β sequestration preferentially |

Genetic testing via next-generation sequencing panels or whole-exome sequencing is clinically available and is recommended for confirmation, genetic counseling, and family screening.

## Pathophysiology

### Systemic Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of Marfan syndrome is driven by two interrelated mechanisms:

1. **Structural microfibrillar deficiency**: Reduced quantity or quality of fibrillin-1 microfibrils leads to weakened connective tissue architecture. Tissues with high mechanical demand—the [[aortic root]], [[zonular fibers]], [[sclera]], and [[skeletal ligaments]]—are disproportionately affected.

2. **Dysregulated TGF-β signaling**: The landmark work of Bhatt, Dietz, and colleagues demonstrated that excessive TGF-β activation is a central driver of Marfan pathology. Upregulated TGF-β signaling promotes:
   - [[Smooth muscle cell]] apoptosis and [[elastin]] fragmentation in the aortic media (leading to [[aortic root dilation]] and [[aortic dissection]])
   - Abnormal [[chondrocyte]] and [[osteoblast]] signaling (contributing to skeletal overgrowth)
   - Altered [[extracellular matrix]] remodeling in ocular tissues

These two mechanisms are synergistic: structural weakness of the ECM is compounded by active, TGF-β–mediated tissue degradation.

### Ocular Pathophysiology

The eye is one of the most consistently and significantly affected organ systems in Marfan syndrome. The ocular pathophysiology is directly attributable to the critical dependence of several ocular structures on fibrillin-1–rich microfibrils:

- **[[Zonular fibers]] (Zonules of Zinn)**: These suspensory ligaments of the [[crystalline lens]] are composed almost entirely of fibrillin-1 microfibrils. In Marfan syndrome, zonular fibers are structurally weakened, reduced in number, and prone to progressive stretching and rupture. This results in **[[ectopia lentis]]**—the hallmark ocular finding.

- **[[Sclera]]**: The sclera contains fibrillin-1 microfibrils that contribute to its structural rigidity. Deficiency leads to increased [[axial length]] of the globe and progressive [[myopia]], as well as a predisposition to [[scleral thinning]].

- **[[Cornea]]**: Fibrillin-1 is present in the corneal [[stroma]] and [[Descemet's membrane]]. Patients with Marfan syndrome characteristically have a **flat cornea** (reduced [[keratometry]] readings), which is one of the minor diagnostic criteria.

- **[[Ciliary body]]**: Abnormal zonular tension alters the biomechanics of [[accommodation]] and may contribute to [[ciliary body]] detachment in severe cases.

- **[[Retina]]**: Increased axial length predisposes to [[peripheral retinal degeneration]], [[lattice degeneration]], [[retinal tears]], and [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]].

## Clinical Presentation

### Ocular Manifestations

Ocular findings are present in approximately **60–80%** of patients with Marfan syndrome and are frequently the presenting complaint that leads to diagnosis. The eye care professional is therefore often the first clinician to suspect the condition.

#### Ectopia Lentis

**[[Ectopia lentis]]** is the single most specific ocular finding in Marfan syndrome and is present in approximately **50–80%** of affected individuals. Key clinical features include:

- **Direction of subluxation**: The lens characteristically subluxates **superiorly and temporally** (superotemporally), distinguishing it from the inferonasal subluxation seen in [[homocystinuria]]. However, subluxation in any direction can occur.
- **Bilateral involvement**: Ectopia lentis in Marfan syndrome is almost always bilateral, though it may be asymmetric.
- **Progressive nature**: Zonular weakness is progressive; a lens that appears well-centered in childhood may become increasingly subluxated over time.
- **Slit-lamp findings**: On [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]], the examiner may observe:
  - Visible [[lens equator]] or lens edge within the undilated or dilated [[pupil]]
  - [[Iridodonesis]] (tremulousness of the [[iris]] due to loss of lens support)
  - [[Phacodonesis]] (tremulousness of the lens itself)
  - Visible stretched, broken, or absent [[zonular fibers]]
  - [[Anterior chamber]] depth asymmetry
- **Visual consequences**: Subluxation induces significant [[lenticular astigmatism]], [[monocular diplopia]], and fluctuating [[refractive error]]. If the lens edge bisects the pupillary axis, the patient effectively becomes [[aphakic]] in that portion of the visual axis, creating a dual-focus optical system.

#### Myopia

**High [[myopia]]** is extremely common in Marfan syndrome, occurring in over 50% of patients. The myopia is typically **axial** in origin, resulting from increased [[axial length]] due to scleral weakness and progressive globe elongation. Additional lenticular myopia may result from increased lens curvature secondary to zonular laxity (the lens assumes a more spherical shape—[[microspherophakia]]-like changes). Myopia in Marfan syndrome is often:

- Early in onset (childhood)
- Progressive
- High in magnitude (frequently exceeding −6.00 D)
- Resistant to stabilization with standard myopia control strategies

#### Flat Cornea

A **flat [[cornea]]** (mean [[keratometry]] < 43.00 D, or more specifically < 42.00 D in some diagnostic criteria) is a recognized minor criterion in the revised Ghent nosology. This finding is paradoxical given the high myopia, underscoring that the myopia is predominantly axial rather than corneal in origin.

#### Retinal Complications

The combination of increased axial length and connective tissue fragility places Marfan patients at significantly elevated risk for:

| Retinal Finding | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|
| [[Lattice degeneration]] | Present in up to 30% of Marfan patients; predisposes to retinal breaks |
| [[Retinal tears]] and [[retinal holes]] | May be asymptomatic; require vigilant peripheral retinal examination |
| [[Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]] | Lifetime risk significantly elevated; may be bilateral |
| [[Peripheral retinal degeneration]] | Various forms including [[pavingstone degeneration]] |
| [[Giant retinal tears]] | Rare but reported; associated with poor surgical prognosis |

#### Other Ocular Findings

- **[[Glaucoma]]**: Both [[open-angle glaucoma]] and [[angle-closure glaucoma]] may occur. Angle closure can result from anterior lens subluxation causing [[pupillary block]]. Open-angle glaucoma may be related to abnormal [[trabecular meshwork]] architecture or secondary to chronic inflammation.
- **[[Cataract]]**: Early-onset [[cortical cataract]] and [[posterior subcapsular cataract]] are more common in Marfan patients than in age-matched controls.
- **[[Strabismus]] and [[amblyopia]]**: Particularly in children with significant anisometropia or lens subluxation.
- **Increased [[axial length]]**: Measurable by [[A-scan ultrasonography]] or [[optical biometry]]; often exceeds 26 mm.
- **[[Hypoplasia of the iris dilator muscle]]**: Leading to [[miosis]] and poor pharmacological dilation.
- **[[Blue sclera]]**: Occasionally observed due to scleral thinning allowing visualization of underlying [[uveal tissue]].

### Systemic Manifestations

While the focus of this article is ocular, the eye care professional must be aware of the systemic features of Marfan syndrome to facilitate appropriate referral and co-management.

#### Cardiovascular System

Cardiovascular complications are the leading cause of morbidity and mortality in Marfan syndrome:

- **[[Aortic root dilation]]**: Progressive dilation of the [[aortic root]] at the [[sinuses of Valsalva]] is the most dangerous feature, predisposing to [[aortic dissection]] (Type A) and [[aortic rupture]].
- **[[Mitral valve prolapse]]** (MVP): Present in up to 75% of patients; may cause [[mitral regurgitation]].
- **[[Aortic regurgitation]]**: Secondary to aortic root dilation.
- **[[Aortic dissection]]**: The most feared complication; can be fatal if not surgically managed.

#### Musculoskeletal System

- **Disproportionately long limbs** ([[dolichostenomelia]]): Arm span-to-height ratio > 1.05
- **[[Arachnodactyly]]**: Abnormally long, slender fingers and toes; assessed by the **Steinberg sign** (thumb extends beyond ulnar border of hand when enclosed in fist) and **Walker-Murdoch sign** (thumb and fifth finger overlap when encircling the opposite wrist)
- **[[Pectus excavatum]]** or **[[pectus carinatum]]**
- **[[Scoliosis]]** (≥20°)
- **[[Joint hypermobility]]**
- **Reduced upper-to-lower segment ratio**
- **[[Pes planus]]** (flat feet)
- **[[Protrusio acetabuli]]** (medial displacement of the acetabulum)

#### Other Systems

- **[[Dural ectasia]]**: Widening of the dural sac, most commonly in the lumbosacral region; present in 60–90% of patients and is a major diagnostic criterion.
- **[[Spontaneous pneumothorax]]**: Due to apical blebs.
- **[[Striae distensae]]** (stretch marks): Occurring without significant weight change.
- **Recurrent [[inguinal hernia]]**

## Diagnosis

### Revised Ghent Nosology (2010)

The diagnosis of Marfan syndrome is based on the **Revised Ghent Nosology** (Loeys et al., 2010), which places primary emphasis on **[[aortic root dilation]]/dissection** and **[[ectopia lentis]]** as the cardinal diagnostic features, with a **systemic score** capturing additional manifestations.

#### Diagnostic Criteria Summary

In the **absence of family history**, diagnosis requires any of the following:

1. **Aortic root dilation** (Z-score ≥ 2) **AND** **ectopia lentis** → Marfan syndrome diagnosed
2. **Aortic root dilation** (Z-score ≥ 2) **AND** ***FBN1* mutation** → Marfan syndrome diagnosed
3. **Aortic root dilation** (Z-score ≥ 2) **AND** **systemic score ≥ 7** → Marfan syndrome diagnosed
4. **Ectopia lentis AND *FBN1* mutation known to be associated with aortic disease** → Marfan syndrome diagnosed

In the **presence of family history** (first-degree relative meeting criteria independently):

5. **Ectopia lentis** → Marfan syndrome diagnosed
6. **Systemic score ≥ 7** → Marfan syndrome diagnosed
7. **Aortic root dilation** (Z-score ≥ 2 in adults, ≥ 3 in children) → Marfan syndrome diagnosed

#### Systemic Score Calculation

| Feature | Points |
|---|---|
| Wrist AND thumb sign | 3 |
| Wrist OR thumb sign | 1 |
| [[Pectus carinatum]] | 2 |
| [[Pectus excavatum]] or chest asymmetry | 1 |
| Hindfoot deformity | 2 |
| Plain [[pes planus]] | 1 |
| [[Pneumothorax]] | 2 |
| [[Dural ectasia]] | 2 |
| [[Protrusio acetabuli]] | 2 |
| Reduced upper/lower segment ratio AND increased arm span/height AND no severe [[scoliosis]] | 1 |
| [[Scoliosis]] or thoracolumbar [[kyphosis]] | 1 |
| Reduced elbow extension (≤170°) | 1 |
| Facial features (3/5: [[dolichocephaly]], [[enophthalmos]], downslanting [[palpebral fissures]], [[malar hypoplasia]], [[retrognathia]]) | 1 |
| Skin [[striae]] | 1 |
| [[Myopia]] > 3 diopters | 1 |
| [[Mitral valve prolapse]] | 1 |
| **Maximum systemic score** | **20** |

A systemic score of **≥ 7** is considered diagnostically significant.

### Ophthalmic Diagnostic Evaluation

The comprehensive ophthalmic examination in suspected or confirmed Marfan syndrome should include:

1. **[[Visual acuity]] assessment**: Best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA) with manifest [[refraction]]; note high myopia, lenticular astigmatism, or monocular diplopia.

2. **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**:
   - Careful evaluation of lens position before and after pharmacological [[mydriasis]] (essential—subluxation may only be apparent with a dilated pupil)
   - Assessment for [[iridodonesis]] and [[phacodonesis]]
   - Visualization of [[zonular fibers]]
   - Evaluation for [[cataract]]
   - [[Anterior chamber]] depth assessment
   - [[Gonioscopy]] if angle closure is suspected

3. **[[Keratometry]] / [[Corneal topography]]**: To document flat cornea (K readings < 43.00 D).

4. **[[Intraocular pressure]] (IOP) measurement**: Screening for [[glaucoma]]; note that flat cornea and thin [[central corneal thickness]] may affect [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]] readings.

5. **[[Axial length]] measurement**: Via [[A-scan ultrasonography]] or [[IOLMaster]] [[optical biometry]]; values > 26 mm support the diagnosis.

6. **[[Dilated fundus examination]]**: With careful **peripheral [[retinal]] examination** using [[scleral depression]] or wide-field imaging:
   - Document [[lattice degeneration]], retinal holes, tears, or detachment
   - Evaluate [[optic nerve head]] for [[glaucomatous]] changes
   - Assess [[macula]] for any secondary changes

7. **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)**: May be useful to evaluate [[zonular]] integrity, [[ciliary body]] anatomy, and anterior segment relationships.

8. **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)**: For [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL) and [[macular]] assessment.

9. **[[Wide-field fundus photography]] / [[Optomap]]**: For documentation and monitoring of peripheral retinal pathology.

### Differential Diagnosis of Ectopia Lentis

The differential diagnosis of ectopia lentis is critical, as several conditions can mimic the ocular findings of Marfan syndrome:

| Condition | Direction of Lens Subluxation | Key Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Marfan syndrome** | Superotemporal | Tall stature, arachnodactyly, aortic root dilation, *FBN1* mutation |
| **[[Homocystinuria]]** | Inferonasal | Intellectual disability, thromboembolic events, elevated plasma homocysteine, *CBS* mutation |
| **[[Weill-Marchesani syndrome]]** | Variable (often anterior) | Short stature, brachydactyly, [[microspherophakia]], *FBN1*, *ADAMTS10*, or *LTBP2* mutations |
| **[[Ehlers-Danlos syndrome]]** | Variable | Skin hyperextensibility, joint hypermobility, tissue fragility |
| **[[Sulfite oxidase deficiency]]** | Variable | Severe neurological impairment, seizures |
| **[[Trauma]]** | Variable | History of ocular or head trauma |
| **[[Hyperlysinemia]]** | Variable | Rare metabolic disorder |

## Management and Treatment

Management of Marfan syndrome requires a **multidisciplinary approach** involving [[optometry]], [[ophthalmology]], [[cardiology]], [[genetics]], [[orthopedics]], and [[primary care]]. The eye care professional plays a central role in both diagnosis and long-term ocular management.

### Ophthalmic Management

#### Refractive Correction

- **[[Spectacles]]**: First-line optical correction for myopia and astigmatism. Frequent prescription updates may be necessary due to progressive myopia and changing lens position.
- **[[Contact lenses]]**: May be used when spectacles are inadequate; [[rigid gas permeable]] (RGP) lenses may provide superior optical correction for irregular astigmatism.
- **Aphakic correction**: If the lens is sufficiently subluxated that the visual axis passes through the aphakic zone, an aphakic spectacle or contact lens correction may be required for that eye. Some patients benefit from alternating between phakic and aphakic corrections depending on gaze position.
- **[[Low vision]] aids**: In cases of severe visual impairment from complications.

#### Monitoring Schedule

Patients with Marfan syndrome require **regular, lifelong ophthalmic surveillance**:

| Age Group | Recommended Examination Frequency |
|---|---|
| Children (0–18 years) | Every 6–12 months (more frequently if lens subluxation is progressing or complications arise) |
| Adults (stable) | Annually |
| Adults (progressive subluxation, glaucoma, or retinal pathology) | Every 3–6 months |
| Post-surgical | Per surgeon protocol; typically frequent in the first year, then annually |

#### Management of Ectopia Lentis

**Conservative management** is preferred when visual acuity is adequate with optical correction:

- Optimize refraction (spectacles or contact lenses)
- Monitor for progressive subluxation
- Educate patients about symptoms of [[retinal detachment]] (flashes, floaters, curtain-like visual field loss) and [[acute angle-closure glaucoma]] (pain, redness, halos, nausea)

**Surgical intervention** ([[lensectomy]] / [[lens extraction]]) is indicated when:

- Best-corrected visual acuity is inadequate for the patient's functional needs despite optimal refractive correction
- The lens is causing secondary complications (e.g., [[pupillary block glaucoma]], [[lens-induced uveitis]], lens dislocation into the [[anterior chamber]] or [[vitreous cavity]])
- Progressive subluxation threatens imminent complete dislocation

Surgical options include:

| Procedure | Description | Considerations |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Pars plana lensectomy]]** with [[vitrectomy]] | Removal of the subluxated lens via a posterior approach with vitreous removal | Often preferred for significantly subluxated lenses; requires [[aphakic]] correction or secondary [[IOL]] implantation |
| **[[Intracapsular cataract extraction]] (ICCE)** | Removal of the entire lens with capsule | Rarely performed today; risk of vitreous loss |
| **[[Phacoemulsification]] with capsular tension ring ([[CTR]])** | Standard cataract surgery technique with a CTR or modified CTR (e.g., Cionni ring) to stabilize the capsular bag | Feasible when zonular support is sufficient; allows [[posterior chamber IOL]] implantation in the bag |
| **Scleral-fixated [[IOL]]** | IOL sutured or glued to the [[sclera]] | Used when capsular support is inadequate for in-the-bag IOL placement |
| **Iris-fixated IOL** (e.g., [[Artisan]]/[[Verisyse]]) | IOL clipped to the [[iris]] | Alternative when scleral fixation is not feasible |
| **[[Aphakic contact lens]]** correction | No IOL implanted; optical correction with contact lens | May be preferred in young children to allow for growth and future surgical options |

#### Management of Glaucoma

- **Medical therapy**: [[Topical]] [[beta-blockers]], [[prostaglandin analogs]], [[alpha-agonists]], and [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]] as appropriate. **[[Miotics]]** (e.g., [[pilocarpine]]) should be used with caution—while they can relieve pupillary block in anterior lens subluxation, they may worsen pupillary block in some configurations.
- **Laser therapy**: [[Laser peripheral iridotomy]] (LPI) for pupillary block; [[selective laser trabeculoplasty]] (SLT) for open-angle glaucoma.
- **Surgical therapy**: [[Trabeculectomy]], [[glaucoma drainage device]] implantation, or [[minimally invasive glaucoma surgery]] (MIGS) as indicated.

#### Management of Retinal Complications

- **Prophylactic [[laser photocoagulation]]** or **[[cryopexy]]**: For symptomatic retinal tears or high-risk lattice degeneration.
- **[[Pars plana vitrectomy]]** (PPV) with or without [[scleral buckle]]: For [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]]. Surgical outcomes may be less favorable than in the general population due to underlying connective tissue weakness.
- **Patient education**: All Marfan patients must be educated about the symptoms of retinal detachment and instructed to seek immediate ophthalmic evaluation if symptoms occur.

#### Amblyopia Management in Children

- Early detection and treatment of [[amblyopia]] is critical in pediatric Marfan patients.
- [[Occlusion therapy]] (patching) and/or [[atropine penalization]] should be initiated promptly when amblyopia is identified.
- Refractive correction must be optimized and updated frequently.

### Systemic Management

While systemic management is directed by cardiology and other specialists, the eye care professional should be aware of current therapeutic strategies:

#### Pharmacological Interventions

| Medication | Mechanism / Rationale | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Beta-blockers]]** (e.g., [[atenolol]], [[propranolol]]) | Reduce heart rate and aortic wall shear stress; slow rate of aortic root dilation | Standard of care since 1994 (Shores et al.) |
| **[[Angiotensin II receptor blockers]]** (ARBs; e.g., [[losartan]]) | Block TGF-β signaling via the AT1 receptor; reduce aortic root dilation in animal models | Promising in murine models (Habashi et al., 2006); mixed results in human trials (COMPARE, Pediatric Heart Network trials) |
| **[[ACE inhibitors]]** (e.g., [[enalapril]]) | Reduce afterload; may modulate TGF-β | Some evidence of benefit; used as alternative to ARBs |
| **[[Calcium channel blockers]]** (e.g., [[verapamil]]) | Alternative for patients intolerant of beta-blockers | Less evidence than beta-blockers |
| **[[Doxycycline]]** | MMP inhibition; reduces aortic wall degradation in animal models | Experimental; not standard of care |

#### Surgical Cardiovascular Interventions

- **Prophylactic [[aortic root replacement]]**: Indicated when aortic root diameter reaches approximately **5.0 cm** (or 4.5 cm in patients with family history of dissection, rapid growth > 0.5 cm/year, or significant aortic regurgitation). The **Bentall procedure** (composite graft with mechanical valve) or **valve-sparing root replacement** (David or Yacoob procedure) are the primary surgical options.

#### Lifestyle Modifications

- **Activity restrictions**: Patients should avoid **contact sports**, **isometric exercise**, and **competitive athletics** that involve intense physical exertion or risk of chest trauma. Moderate aerobic exercise at submaximal intensity is generally encouraged.
- **Pregnancy counseling**: Women with Marfan syndrome face increased risk of [[aortic dissection]] during pregnancy, particularly in the third trimester and peripartum period. Pre-conception counseling with cardiology and genetics is essential.

### Genetic Counseling

- Marfan syndrome follows [[autosomal dominant]] inheritance with a **50% recurrence risk** for offspring of an affected individual.
- Genetic testing of the ***FBN1*** gene should be offered to confirm diagnosis and enable predictive testing of at-risk family members.
- Prenatal diagnosis and [[preimplantation genetic testing]] (PGT) are available for families with known pathogenic variants.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of Marfan syndrome has improved dramatically over the past several decades due to advances in cardiovascular surveillance, pharmacological management, and prophylactic aortic surgery. **Life expectancy**, which was historically reduced to approximately 40 years, now approaches that of the general population with appropriate management, with many patients living into their 70s and beyond.

From an ocular standpoint, the prognosis depends on:

- **Degree of lens subluxation** and its impact on visual function
- **Presence and severity of myopia**
- **Occurrence of retinal detachment** (which carries a guarded visual prognosis, particularly if involving the [[macula]])
- **Development of [[glaucoma]]** and response to treatment
- **Timeliness of amblyopia treatment** in children
- **Surgical outcomes** following lensectomy or retinal surgery

Early diagnosis, regular multidisciplinary follow-up, and patient education are the cornerstones of optimizing both systemic and ocular outcomes.

## Role of the Optometrist

The optometrist occupies a uniquely important position in the care of patients with Marfan syndrome:

1. **Primary detection**: The optometrist may be the first clinician to identify ectopia lentis, high myopia, flat cornea, or other ocular features that raise suspicion for Marfan syndrome. Prompt referral to genetics and cardiology can be life-saving.

2. **Refractive management**: Ongoing optimization of visual function through spectacles, contact lenses, or low vision aids.

3. **Surveillance**: Regular monitoring for progressive lens subluxation, glaucoma, cataract, and retinal pathology.

4. **Co-management**: Collaboration with [[ophthalmology]] for surgical planning and postoperative care; coordination with cardiology, genetics, and other specialists.

5. **Patient and family education**: Counseling regarding the hereditary nature of the condition, the importance of regular follow-up, activity restrictions, and warning signs of acute complications (retinal detachment, aortic dissection).

6. **Pediatric screening**: Examination of children of affected parents, even in the absence of symptoms, to enable early diagnosis and intervention.
