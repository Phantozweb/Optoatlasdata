

# Refraction

**Refraction** is the fundamental optical process by which light rays are bent as they pass through the various transparent media of the [[eye]], ultimately converging upon the [[retina]] to form a focused image. In clinical [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]], the term "refraction" also refers to the diagnostic procedure performed to determine the optical power of corrective lenses required to compensate for [[refractive error]]. Refraction is arguably the most frequently performed clinical assessment in eye care and serves as the cornerstone of visual correction, influencing virtually every aspect of ophthalmic practice from routine vision care to pre-surgical planning.

The refractive state of the eye—known as its **ametropia** or **emmetropia**—is determined by the complex interplay between the [[cornea]]l curvature, [[crystalline lens]] power, [[anterior chamber]] depth, and [[axial length]] of the globe. When these components are harmoniously matched, light from a distant object focuses precisely on the retinal photoreceptor layer, a condition termed [[emmetropia]]. Any deviation from this optical equilibrium results in a [[refractive error]], necessitating clinical refraction to quantify and correct the deficiency.

## Optical Principles of Refraction

### Snell's Law and Ocular Optics

The bending of light at the interface between two media of differing [[refractive index]] is governed by **[[Snell's Law]]**, expressed as:

> **n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂**

where *n₁* and *n₂* represent the refractive indices of the first and second media, and *θ₁* and *θ₂* represent the angles of incidence and refraction, respectively. In the human eye, light traverses several refractive interfaces:

| **Ocular Structure** | **Refractive Index** | **Approximate Dioptric Power** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Cornea]] (anterior surface) | 1.376 | +48.00 D |
| [[Aqueous humor]] | 1.336 | — |
| [[Crystalline lens]] (gradient index) | 1.386–1.406 | +15.00 to +20.00 D |
| [[Vitreous humor]] | 1.336 | — |
| **Total eye** | — | **~+60.00 D** |

The [[cornea]] contributes approximately two-thirds of the total refractive power of the eye (~+43.00 D when considering the air-cornea interface), while the [[crystalline lens]] contributes the remaining one-third (~+15.00 to +20.00 D). The lens is unique in possessing a **gradient refractive index**, meaning its refractive index increases progressively from the cortex to the nucleus, which reduces [[spherical aberration]] and enhances image quality.

### Vergence and the Far Point Concept

The refractive state of the eye can be described using the concept of **vergence**. Parallel light rays from optical infinity (conventionally defined as 6 meters or 20 feet) have zero vergence. The **[[far point]]** of the eye is the conjugate point of the retina—the point in space from which light originates and is brought to a perfect focus on the [[fovea]] without [[accommodation]]. In emmetropia, the far point is at optical infinity. In [[myopia]], the far point is at a finite distance anterior to the eye. In [[hyperopia]], the far point is a virtual point located behind the retina.

The relationship between the far point and the required corrective lens power is:

> **Corrective lens power (D) = 1 / far point distance (m)**

### Accommodation and Its Role in Refraction

[[Accommodation]] is the dynamic process by which the [[crystalline lens]] increases its dioptric power to focus on near objects. Mediated by contraction of the [[ciliary muscle]] and relaxation of the [[zonules of Zinn]], accommodation causes the lens to assume a more convex shape, increasing its refractive power. The amplitude of accommodation diminishes progressively with age—a condition known as [[presbyopia]]—due to age-related sclerosis and loss of elasticity of the lens substance.

During clinical refraction, it is essential to control accommodation to obtain an accurate measurement of the eye's static refractive error. This is achieved through the use of [[cycloplegic]] agents (pharmacological paralysis of the ciliary muscle) or through fogging techniques during [[subjective refraction]].

## Pathophysiology of Refractive Errors

Refractive errors arise from a mismatch between the optical power of the eye and its axial length. The pathophysiology of each type of refractive error is distinct:

### Myopia (Nearsightedness)

In [[myopia]], parallel light rays from a distant object converge at a focal point **anterior to the retina**, resulting in a blurred retinal image. This may occur due to:

- **Axial myopia**: Excessive [[axial length]] of the globe (most common mechanism). The average emmetropic eye has an axial length of approximately 23.5 mm; each additional millimeter of axial length corresponds to approximately –3.00 D of myopia.
- **Refractive myopia**: Excessive refractive power of the cornea or lens (e.g., [[keratoconus]], [[lenticonus]], nuclear [[cataract]]).
- **Index myopia**: Increase in the refractive index of the crystalline lens, commonly seen in nuclear sclerotic cataracts ("second sight" phenomenon).

The global prevalence of myopia has reached epidemic proportions, particularly in East Asian populations, where prevalence rates exceed 80–90% among young adults. Environmental factors such as prolonged near work, reduced outdoor time, and higher educational attainment have been implicated alongside genetic predisposition. [[Pathological myopia]] (typically > –6.00 D) is associated with sight-threatening complications including [[myopic macular degeneration]], [[posterior staphyloma]], [[retinal detachment]], and [[myopic choroidal neovascularization]].

### Hyperopia (Farsightedness)

In [[hyperopia]], parallel light rays converge at a theoretical focal point **posterior to the retina**. Mechanisms include:

- **Axial hyperopia**: Shortened axial length of the globe (most common).
- **Refractive hyperopia**: Insufficient corneal or lenticular refractive power (e.g., flat cornea post-[[hyperopic LASIK]], [[aphakia]]).
- **Index hyperopia**: Decrease in the refractive index of the lens (e.g., cortical changes in diabetes).

Hyperopia is classified as:

| **Classification** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Latent hyperopia** | Compensated by tonic accommodation; revealed only with [[cycloplegia]] |
| **Manifest hyperopia** | Measurable without cycloplegia |
| **Facultative hyperopia** | Portion of manifest hyperopia correctable by accommodation |
| **Absolute hyperopia** | Portion that cannot be compensated by accommodation |
| **Total hyperopia** | Latent + manifest hyperopia (measured under full cycloplegia) |

Uncorrected hyperopia in children is a significant risk factor for [[accommodative esotropia]] and [[amblyopia]]. In adults, it may contribute to [[angle-closure glaucoma]] due to the anatomically shorter eye and shallower [[anterior chamber]].

### Astigmatism

[[Astigmatism]] occurs when the refractive power of the eye varies along different meridians, preventing light from converging to a single focal point. Instead, two focal lines are formed (the **[[Sturm's conoid]]**), and the retinal image is blurred in one or more orientations. The interval between the two focal lines is termed the **[[circle of least confusion]]**.

Astigmatism is classified by:

**Axis orientation:**
- **With-the-rule (WTR)**: Steepest meridian near 90° (vertical); more common in younger individuals.
- **Against-the-rule (ATR)**: Steepest meridian near 180° (horizontal); more common in older individuals.
- **Oblique**: Steepest meridian between 30°–60° or 120°–150°.

**Regularity:**
- **Regular astigmatism**: Principal meridians are perpendicular; correctable with spherocylindrical lenses.
- **Irregular astigmatism**: Principal meridians are not perpendicular or curvature varies within a meridian; seen in [[keratoconus]], [[corneal scarring]], [[pellucid marginal degeneration]]; often requires [[rigid gas permeable]] (RGP) contact lenses or [[scleral lenses]] for optimal correction.

**Focal relationship to the retina:**
- Simple myopic/hyperopic astigmatism
- Compound myopic/hyperopic astigmatism
- Mixed astigmatism

### Presbyopia

[[Presbyopia]] is the age-related, progressive loss of accommodative amplitude, typically becoming clinically significant around age 40–45 years. The pathophysiology involves:

- Progressive sclerosis and hardening of the [[crystalline lens]] nucleus and cortex
- Loss of lens capsule elasticity
- Possible age-related changes in [[ciliary muscle]] function and geometry
- Alterations in [[zonular]] fiber mechanics

The Duane-Donders table provides normative values for accommodative amplitude by age:

| **Age (years)** | **Expected Amplitude of Accommodation (D)** |
|---|---|
| 10 | 14.0 |
| 20 | 11.0 |
| 30 | 8.0 |
| 40 | 5.5 |
| 45 | 3.5 |
| 50 | 2.5 |
| 55 | 1.5 |
| 60 | 1.0 |

## Symptoms and Clinical Presentation

The symptoms of uncorrected refractive error vary depending on the type, magnitude, and age of the patient:

### General Symptoms of Uncorrected Refractive Error

- **Blurred vision**: Distance blur (myopia), near blur (hyperopia/presbyopia), or blur at all distances (high astigmatism)
- **[[Asthenopia]]** (eye strain): Particularly associated with uncorrected hyperopia and astigmatism due to sustained accommodative effort
- **Headaches**: Frontal or temporal, often exacerbated by prolonged visual tasks
- **Squinting or partial lid closure**: Employed as a pinhole effect to reduce blur circle diameter
- **Difficulty with night driving**: Especially in myopia and astigmatism due to increased pupil size and higher-order aberrations
- **Diplopia (monocular)**: May occur with irregular astigmatism
- **Reading difficulty and reduced near visual acuity**: Hallmark of presbyopia
- **Photophobia**: Occasionally reported with high refractive errors

### Specific Presentations by Refractive Error Type

| **Refractive Error** | **Distance Vision** | **Near Vision** | **Key Symptoms** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Myopia]] | Blurred | Clear (within far point) | Squinting at distance, difficulty seeing board/signs |
| [[Hyperopia]] (low-moderate) | May be clear (if accommodated) | Blurred or strained | Asthenopia, headaches, difficulty reading |
| [[Hyperopia]] (high) | Blurred | Blurred | Constant blur, amblyopia risk in children |
| [[Astigmatism]] | Blurred/distorted | Blurred/distorted | Ghosting, shadowing of images, asthenopia |
| [[Presbyopia]] | Clear (if distance Rx correct) | Blurred | Difficulty reading, holding material farther away |

### Pediatric Considerations

In children, refractive errors may present atypically. Young children rarely complain of blurred vision, and the condition may manifest as:

- Poor academic performance
- Behavioral issues or inattention
- Abnormal head posture or tilting
- Sitting close to screens or holding books very close
- [[Strabismus]] (particularly [[accommodative esotropia]] in hyperopia)
- Failed vision screening

Early detection through comprehensive pediatric eye examinations is critical to prevent [[amblyopia]] and ensure normal visual development during the critical and sensitive periods of [[visual maturation]].

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Objective Refraction

Objective refraction determines the refractive error without requiring patient responses and is essential in non-verbal patients, children, and as a starting point for subjective refinement.

#### Retinoscopy

[[Retinoscopy]] (also known as skiascopy) remains the gold standard of objective refraction. The examiner projects a streak or spot of light into the patient's eye and observes the movement of the retinal reflex (the "red reflex" or retinoscopic reflex) in the pupil:

- **"With" movement**: The reflex moves in the same direction as the streak → indicates the eye is more hyperopic (or less myopic) than the working distance
- **"Against" movement**: The reflex moves opposite to the streak → indicates the eye is more myopic than the working distance
- **Neutralization**: No apparent movement; the pupil fills uniformly with light → the refractive error has been neutralized at the working distance

The examiner adds lenses to achieve neutralization, then subtracts the working distance lens (e.g., –1.50 D for a 67 cm working distance) to determine the net refractive error. Retinoscopy is particularly valuable in:

- Pediatric refraction (especially under [[cycloplegia]])
- Patients with intellectual disabilities or communication barriers
- Verification of autorefractor findings
- Detection of media opacities and irregular astigmatism (via the quality of the reflex)

#### Autorefraction

[[Autorefraction]] uses automated instruments to objectively measure the refractive error. Modern autorefractors employ infrared light and wavefront sensing or image analysis to determine sphere, cylinder, and axis. While highly convenient and reproducible, autorefractors have limitations:

- Tendency toward instrument-induced myopia (proximal accommodation artifact)
- Reduced accuracy in high refractive errors, irregular astigmatism, and media opacities
- Should always be refined with subjective refraction

#### Wavefront Aberrometry

[[Wavefront aberrometry]] (e.g., Hartmann-Shack sensor) measures both lower-order aberrations (sphere, cylinder) and [[higher-order aberrations]] (coma, trefoil, spherical aberration). This technology is essential for:

- Custom [[wavefront-guided LASIK]]
- Evaluation of optical quality beyond conventional refraction
- Research into the optical performance of the eye

### Subjective Refraction

Subjective refraction refines the objective findings based on patient responses and is the definitive method for determining the final spectacle prescription.

#### Monocular Subjective Refraction

The standard protocol includes:

1. **Best sphere determination**: Using the [[Jackson cross-cylinder]] (JCC) or trial lenses, the most plus (or least minus) sphere that provides best visual acuity is identified (maximum plus to maximum visual acuity, or MPMVA principle).

2. **Cylinder axis refinement**: The JCC is placed with its axis at 45° to the estimated cylinder axis. The patient compares two positions (flipped orientations), and the axis is rotated toward the preferred position until equality is achieved.

3. **Cylinder power refinement**: The JCC is placed with its axes aligned with and perpendicular to the cylinder axis. Power is adjusted based on patient preference until equality is achieved.

4. **Spherical endpoint refinement**: Final adjustment of sphere power to maintain MPMVA.

5. **Duochrome (bichrome) test**: Red-green balance test exploiting [[chromatic aberration]]; used to verify the spherical endpoint. Equal clarity of letters on red and green backgrounds indicates accurate correction (in a non-presbyopic patient, slight preference for green/clearer on red indicates slight over-minus).

#### Binocular Balancing

After monocular refraction, binocular balancing ensures equal accommodative demand between the two eyes. Techniques include:

- **Alternate occlusion with prism dissociation**: A vertical prism (e.g., 3Δ base-up before one eye) dissociates the images; sphere is adjusted until both eyes see equally blurred targets.
- **Humphriss immediate contrast method**: One eye is fogged while the other is refined, then reversed.
- **Turville infinity balance**: Uses a septum to present different portions of the chart to each eye simultaneously.

### Cycloplegic Refraction

[[Cycloplegic refraction]] involves pharmacological paralysis of the [[ciliary muscle]] to eliminate accommodation, revealing the total (latent + manifest) hyperopia and providing the most accurate objective refraction. It is indicated in:

- All children, especially those with [[strabismus]] or suspected [[amblyopia]]
- Young hyperopic patients with asthenopia
- Patients with variable or inconsistent refractive findings
- Pre-operative assessment for [[refractive surgery]]

Commonly used [[cycloplegic agents]]:

| **Agent** | **Concentration** | **Onset** | **Duration of Cycloplegia** | **Notes** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [[Atropine]] sulfate | 0.5%–1.0% | 30–60 min | 7–14 days | Gold standard; used in children with dark irides, accommodative esotropia |
| [[Cyclopentolate]] | 0.5%–1.0% | 30–45 min | 24 hours | Most commonly used in clinical practice |
| [[Tropicamide]] | 0.5%–1.0% | 20–30 min | 4–6 hours | Weak cycloplegic; primarily a mydriatic; insufficient for pediatric refraction |
| [[Homatropine]] | 2%–5% | 30–60 min | 1–3 days | Intermediate duration |

### Keratometry and Corneal Topography

[[Keratometry]] measures the curvature of the central cornea (typically the central 3 mm zone) and is essential for:

- Quantifying corneal astigmatism
- [[Contact lens]] fitting
- [[Intraocular lens]] (IOL) power calculation
- Monitoring corneal changes (e.g., [[keratoconus]] progression)

[[Corneal topography]] and [[corneal tomography]] (e.g., [[Placido disc]]-based systems, [[Scheimpflug imaging]] such as [[Pentacam]], and [[optical coherence tomography]]-based systems) provide comprehensive mapping of anterior and posterior corneal surfaces, pachymetry, and elevation data. These are indispensable in:

- Detecting subclinical [[keratoconus]] and [[ectasia]]
- Planning [[refractive surgery]]
- Fitting specialty contact lenses
- Evaluating irregular astigmatism

### Biometry

[[Optical biometry]] (e.g., [[IOLMaster]], [[Lenstar]]) and [[A-scan ultrasonography]] measure axial length, anterior chamber depth, lens thickness, and corneal curvature for IOL power calculation prior to [[cataract surgery]]. Modern formulas (e.g., [[Barrett Universal II]], [[Hill-RBF]], [[Kane formula]], [[SRK/T]], [[Holladay]], [[Haigis]]) use these biometric parameters to predict the optimal IOL power for the desired postoperative refractive outcome.

## Management and Treatment

### Spectacle Correction

[[Spectacles]] remain the most common, safest, and most accessible method of correcting refractive errors worldwide. The spectacle prescription is expressed in standard notation:

> **Sphere / Cylinder × Axis**
> Example: –2.50 / –1.25 × 180

Key considerations in spectacle prescribing:

- **Vertex distance**: The distance from the posterior spectacle lens surface to the corneal apex (typically 12–14 mm). Vertex distance correction is necessary for prescriptions exceeding ±4.00 D, as the effective power at the corneal plane differs from the spectacle plane.
- **Pantoscopic tilt**: The angle of the spectacle frame relative to the visual axis; affects the effective cylinder and sphere.
- **Lens materials**: [[CR-39]], [[polycarbonate]], [[Trivex]], high-index plastics (1.60, 1.67, 1.74)
- **Lens designs**: Single vision, [[bifocal]], [[trifocal]], [[progressive addition lenses]] (PALs)
- **Coatings**: Anti-reflective (AR), scratch-resistant, UV-blocking, blue-light filtering
- **Prism**: Incorporated when indicated for [[binocular vision]] anomalies

#### Prescribing for Presbyopia

The near addition (Add) is determined based on the patient's age, working distance, and remaining accommodative amplitude. The commonly used formula is:

> **Add = Dioptric demand of working distance – (Accommodative amplitude / 2)**

For a 40 cm working distance (2.50 D demand) in a patient with 4.00 D of accommodation:
> Add = 2.50 – (4.00 / 2) = 2.50 – 2.00 = +0.50 D

Progressive addition lenses provide a seamless transition from distance to near correction and are the most commonly prescribed multifocal design in modern practice.

### Contact Lens Correction

[[Contact lenses]] offer several optical advantages over spectacles, including:

- Elimination of vertex distance effects (particularly beneficial in high myopia and anisometropia)
- Wider field of view
- Reduced spectacle magnification/minification
- Cosmetic preference
- Superior correction of irregular astigmatism (with RGP or scleral designs)

| **Contact Lens Type** | **Indications** | **Key Features** |
|---|---|---|
| Soft spherical | Myopia, hyperopia | Comfortable, disposable options |
| Soft toric | Regular astigmatism | Stabilization designs (prism ballast, thin zones) |
| Soft multifocal | Presbyopia | Simultaneous vision designs (center-near, center-distance) |
| [[Rigid gas permeable]] (RGP) | High astigmatism, keratoconus, irregular corneas | Superior optics, tear lens corrects corneal astigmatism |
| [[Scleral lenses]] | Keratoconus, severe dry eye, irregular corneas, post-surgical | Vault over cornea, fluid reservoir |
| [[Orthokeratology]] (Ortho-K) | Myopia control, refractive correction | Overnight wear reshapes cornea; temporary effect |
| Hybrid lenses | Irregular astigmatism with comfort needs | RGP center with soft skirt |

### Pharmacological Interventions Related to Refraction

While refractive errors are not treated pharmacologically per se, several pharmacological agents are directly relevant to the refractive process:

- **Cycloplegic agents** (discussed above): Essential for accurate refraction in specific populations
- **Low-dose [[atropine]]** (0.01%–0.05%): Demonstrated efficacy in slowing [[myopia progression]] in children (ATOM, LAMP studies); mechanism involves non-accommodative pathways, possibly scleral or retinal signaling
- **Miotics** (e.g., low-dose [[pilocarpine]] 1.25%): Used in [[Vuity]]® (pilocarpine 1.25%) for pharmacological treatment of presbyopia by inducing miosis and increasing depth of focus

### Refractive Surgery

[[Refractive surgery]] encompasses a range of procedures designed to permanently alter the refractive state of the eye:

#### Corneal Refractive Surgery

| **Procedure** | **Mechanism** | **Typical Range** | **Key Considerations** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[LASIK]] (Laser-Assisted In Situ Keratomileusis) | Excimer laser ablation of corneal stroma under a flap | Myopia to –12.00 D; hyperopia to +6.00 D; astigmatism to 6.00 D | Most common procedure; rapid recovery; flap-related risks |
| [[PRK]] (Photorefractive Keratectomy) | Surface ablation after epithelial removal | Similar to LASIK | No flap; preferred in thin corneas; slower recovery |
| [[SMILE]] (Small Incision Lenticule Extraction) | Femtosecond laser creates intrastromal lenticule removed through small incision | Myopia to –10.00 D; astigmatism to 5.00 D | Flapless; preserves anterior stromal biomechanics |
| [[LASEK]] / Epi-LASIK | Surface ablation variants | Similar to PRK | Epithelial flap preservation |
| [[Conductive keratoplasty]] (CK) | Radiofrequency energy steepens peripheral cornea | Low hyperopia, presbyopia | Effect may regress over time |

#### Lenticular Refractive Surgery

| **Procedure** | **Mechanism** | **Indications** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Phakic IOL]] (e.g., [[ICL]] – Implantable Collamer Lens, [[Artisan]]/[[Artiflex]]) | Implantation of an additional lens in the anterior or posterior chamber | High myopia (–6.00 to –20.00 D), high hyperopia, thin corneas unsuitable for LASIK |
| [[Refractive lens exchange]] (RLE) / Clear lens extraction | Removal of the natural crystalline lens and replacement with an IOL | High hyperopia, presbyopia, pre-cataract patients |
| [[Multifocal IOL]] / [[Extended depth of focus (EDOF) IOL]] / [[Toric IOL]] | Premium IOLs implanted during cataract surgery or RLE | Presbyopia correction, astigmatism correction, spectacle independence |

#### Corneal Inlays

[[Corneal inlays]] (e.g., [[KAMRA]] inlay) are small devices implanted into the corneal stroma of the non-dominant eye to increase depth of focus for near vision in presbyopic patients. Their use has declined due to variable outcomes and complications.

### Myopia Control and Management

Given the global myopia epidemic, [[myopia control]] strategies have become a critical component of refractive management in pediatric populations:

| **Intervention** | **Mechanism** | **Approximate Efficacy (Axial Length Reduction)** |
|---|---|---|
| Low-dose atropine (0.01%–0.05%) | Likely non-accommodative retinal/scleral signaling | 30%–50% reduction in progression |
| [[Orthokeratology]] | Peripheral myopic defocus via corneal reshaping | 40%–60% |
| [[Multifocal soft contact lenses]] (e.g., [[MiSight]] 1 day) | Peripheral myopic defocus | 40%–59% (MiSight FDA-approved) |
| [[Defocus incorporated multiple segments (DIMS)]] spectacle lenses (e.g., [[MiyoSmart]]) | Peripheral myopic defocus via lenslets | ~50%–60% |
| [[Highly aspherical lenslet (HAL)]] spectacle lenses (e.g., [[Stellest]]) | Peripheral myopic defocus | ~55%–60% |
| Increased outdoor time | Dopamine-mediated retinal signaling; higher light intensity | 30%–50% reduction in myopia onset |
| Combination therapy | Atropine + ortho-K or atropine + multifocal CLs | Potentially additive; under investigation |

## Special Considerations in Refraction

### Anisometropia

[[Anisometropia]] is a significant difference in refractive error between the two eyes (typically >1.00 D). It poses challenges including:

- **Aniseikonia**: Unequal retinal image sizes between the two eyes when corrected with spectacles (approximately 1% image size change per diopter of spectacle correction)
- **Amblyopia risk**: Particularly in children with unilateral high hyperopia or astigmatism
- Contact lenses minimize aniseikonia due to their position at the corneal plane

### Post-Surgical Refraction

Refraction following [[cataract surgery]], [[corneal transplantation]] ([[penetrating keratoplasty]], [[DALK]], [[DSAEK]], [[DMEK]]), or refractive surgery requires special attention:

- Refractive stability may take weeks to months
- Irregular astigmatism may necessitate specialty contact lenses or secondary surgical intervention
- Accurate IOL power calculation in post-refractive surgery eyes requires modified formulas (e.g., [[Haigis-L]], [[Barrett True-K]], clinical history method) due to altered keratometric readings

### Refraction in Systemic Disease

Certain systemic conditions can transiently or permanently alter the refractive state:

- **[[Diabetes mellitus]]**: Fluctuating blood glucose levels cause osmotic changes in the crystalline lens, resulting in transient refractive shifts (hyperopic shift with falling glucose; myopic shift with rising glucose). Refraction should be deferred until glycemic control is stable for at least 2–4 weeks.
- **Pregnancy**: Hormonal changes may cause transient myopic shifts due to corneal edema and lens changes.
- **Medications**: [[Topiramate]], sulfonamides, and other drugs may cause acute myopic shifts via ciliary body edema and anterior lens displacement.

## Notation and Recording of Refractive Data

The refractive prescription is recorded in standardized notation:

- **Minus cylinder form** (preferred in optometry in many countries):
  > –2.00 / –1.50 × 090

- **Plus cylinder form** (traditionally used in ophthalmology):
  > –3.50 / +1.50 × 180

Transposition between forms follows the rule:
1. Algebraically add sphere and cylinder to obtain new sphere
2. Change the sign of the cylinder
3. Rotate the axis by 90°

The **[[spherical equivalent]]** is calculated as:
> SE = Sphere + (Cylinder / 2)

This is useful for approximating the overall refractive error and is commonly used in epidemiological studies and IOL calculations.

## Prognosis and Outcomes

With appropriate correction, the vast majority of patients with refractive errors achieve excellent visual outcomes. Uncorrected refractive error remains the leading cause of preventable visual impairment globally, affecting an estimated 2.7 billion people worldwide (WHO, 2023). Key prognostic considerations include:

- Early detection and correction in children is essential to prevent [[amblyopia]]
- Progressive myopia in children requires active management to reduce lifetime risk of pathological complications
- Presbyopia is universal and progressive; patient education regarding the need for updated near corrections is important
- Refractive surgery outcomes are generally excellent, with >95% of LASIK patients achieving 20/20 uncorrected visual acuity
- Regular follow-up and re-refraction are necessary, as refractive errors may change with age, disease, or surgical intervention
