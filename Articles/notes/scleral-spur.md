# Scleral Spur

The **scleral spur** is a critical anatomical landmark of the [[anterior segment]] of the [[eye]], representing a ridge or protrusion of the inner [[sclera]] at the junction between the sclera and the [[cornea]]. It serves as a key structural anchor for the [[trabecular meshwork]], the [[ciliary body]], and the longitudinal fibers of the [[ciliary muscle]]. Understanding the scleral spur is essential for clinicians in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]], as it plays a pivotal role in [[aqueous humor]] outflow dynamics, [[gonioscopy]] interpretation, [[anterior segment imaging]], and the pathophysiology of [[glaucoma]]. The scleral spur is not a pathological entity in itself but rather a normal anatomical structure whose identification, morphology, and clinical significance are of paramount importance in the diagnosis and management of numerous ocular conditions, particularly those involving the [[iridocorneal angle]].

---

## Gross and Microscopic Anatomy

### Location and Structural Relationships

The scleral spur is located at the internal aspect of the [[corneoscleral junction]] (the [[limbus]]), forming a circumferential ridge of dense [[collagen]] fibers that projects inward toward the [[anterior chamber]]. It is situated immediately posterior to the [[trabecular meshwork]] and [[Schlemm's canal]], and immediately anterior to the attachment of the [[ciliary body]]. In cross-section, the scleral spur appears as a wedge-shaped or triangular projection of scleral tissue, with its apex directed anteriorly and inward.

The key anatomical relationships of the scleral spur include:

| **Structure** | **Relationship to Scleral Spur** |
|---|---|
| [[Trabecular meshwork]] | Attaches to the anterior and inner aspect of the scleral spur |
| [[Schlemm's canal]] | Located just anterior and external to the scleral spur |
| [[Ciliary body]] (longitudinal muscle fibers) | Attaches to the posterior aspect of the scleral spur |
| [[Iris]] root | Inserts near or at the level of the scleral spur (variable) |
| [[Schwalbe's line]] | Located anteriorly, marking the termination of [[Descemet's membrane]] |
| [[Sclera]] (proper) | The scleral spur is a direct extension of the inner scleral stroma |

### Histological Composition

Histologically, the scleral spur is composed of densely packed, circularly oriented [[collagen]] fibers (predominantly [[type I collagen]] and [[type III collagen]]) interspersed with [[elastic fibers]] and [[ground substance]]. The collagen fibers of the scleral spur are arranged in a circumferential pattern, distinguishing them from the more randomly oriented fibers of the adjacent scleral stroma. This circular arrangement provides the structural rigidity necessary for the spur to serve as an anchoring point for the [[ciliary muscle]] and the [[trabecular meshwork]].

[[Elastic tissue]] is notably concentrated at the scleral spur, particularly at the attachment zone of the longitudinal fibers of the ciliary muscle. These elastic fibers are thought to play a role in the recoil mechanism of the ciliary muscle during [[accommodation]] and in maintaining the patency of [[Schlemm's canal]] and the [[trabecular meshwork]].

[[Fibroblasts]] and occasional [[melanocytes]] are found within the scleral spur tissue. The degree of [[pigmentation]] at the scleral spur varies among individuals and is influenced by factors such as [[race]], [[age]], and the presence of [[pigment dispersion syndrome]].

### Developmental Embryology

The scleral spur develops from [[neural crest]]-derived [[mesenchyme]] during the embryonic and fetal periods. During the third trimester of gestation, the angle structures, including the scleral spur, undergo a process of maturation and remodeling. The scleral spur differentiates as a condensation of collagenous tissue at the inner scleral wall, coinciding with the development of [[Schlemm's canal]] and the [[trabecular meshwork]]. Abnormalities in the development of the scleral spur and adjacent angle structures are implicated in [[congenital glaucoma]] (also known as [[primary congenital glaucoma]] or [[buphthalmos]]), where the [[anterior chamber angle]] fails to mature properly, leading to impaired [[aqueous humor]] outflow.

## Pathophysiology

### Role in Aqueous Humor Outflow

The scleral spur is integral to the [[conventional outflow pathway]] of [[aqueous humor]]. The trabecular meshwork, which is the primary site of aqueous outflow resistance, is anchored anteriorly to [[Schwalbe's line]] and posteriorly to the scleral spur. The longitudinal fibers of the [[ciliary muscle]] insert into the posterior aspect of the scleral spur. When the ciliary muscle contracts (as during [[accommodation]]), it exerts a posterior and inward pull on the scleral spur, which in turn opens the [[intertrabecular spaces]] and widens [[Schlemm's canal]], thereby facilitating aqueous humor outflow and reducing [[intraocular pressure]] ([[IOP]]).

This biomechanical relationship is the basis for the pharmacological action of [[parasympathomimetic]] agents (e.g., [[pilocarpine]]) in the treatment of [[open-angle glaucoma]]. Pilocarpine stimulates contraction of the ciliary muscle, pulling on the scleral spur, opening the trabecular meshwork, and enhancing aqueous outflow.

Conversely, when the ciliary muscle relaxes (e.g., under the influence of [[cycloplegic]] agents such as [[atropine]]), the tension on the scleral spur is reduced, the trabecular meshwork becomes more compact, and aqueous outflow resistance increases. This mechanism explains the potential for cycloplegic agents to transiently elevate IOP in susceptible individuals.

### Scleral Spur and Angle-Closure Mechanisms

The position and morphology of the scleral spur are critical in the assessment of [[angle closure]]. In [[primary angle-closure glaucoma]] ([[PACG]]), the [[peripheral iris]] may appose or adhere to the trabecular meshwork at or anterior to the scleral spur, obstructing aqueous outflow. The scleral spur serves as the key landmark for distinguishing between:

- **Appositional closure**: The iris is in contact with the trabecular meshwork but can be displaced posteriorly (e.g., by [[indentation gonioscopy]]).
- **Synechial closure** ([[peripheral anterior synechiae]], [[PAS]]): The iris is permanently adhered to the angle structures at or anterior to the scleral spur.

The level at which PAS extend relative to the scleral spur is clinically significant. PAS that extend anterior to the scleral spur (i.e., onto the trabecular meshwork or beyond) are more likely to impair aqueous outflow and elevate IOP than those confined to the [[ciliary body band]] posterior to the spur.

### Age-Related and Pathological Changes

With advancing [[age]], the scleral spur may undergo structural changes, including increased [[collagen cross-linking]], decreased [[elasticity]], and accumulation of [[extracellular matrix]] material. These changes can reduce the effectiveness of ciliary muscle contraction in opening the trabecular meshwork, contributing to the age-related increase in [[outflow resistance]] and the higher prevalence of [[primary open-angle glaucoma]] ([[POAG]]) in older adults.

In conditions such as [[pigment dispersion syndrome]] and [[pseudoexfoliation syndrome]], abnormal deposition of [[pigment granules]] or [[pseudoexfoliative material]] at the scleral spur and trabecular meshwork can further compromise aqueous outflow. Heavy pigmentation of the scleral spur is a characteristic gonioscopic finding in [[pigmentary glaucoma]].

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

The scleral spur itself does not produce symptoms, as it is a normal anatomical structure. However, pathological processes involving or adjacent to the scleral spur may present with clinical signs and symptoms related to altered aqueous humor dynamics or angle anatomy:

| **Associated Condition** | **Symptoms/Signs** |
|---|---|
| [[Primary open-angle glaucoma]] | Typically asymptomatic until advanced; elevated [[IOP]], [[optic nerve]] damage, [[visual field loss]] |
| [[Primary angle-closure glaucoma]] | Acute: eye pain, headache, nausea, blurred vision, halos; Chronic: may be asymptomatic |
| [[Pigment dispersion syndrome]] | Blurred vision, halos after exercise; [[Krukenberg spindle]], heavy angle pigmentation |
| [[Pseudoexfoliation syndrome]] | Often asymptomatic; [[pseudoexfoliative material]] on [[lens capsule]], [[pupil margin]], and angle structures |
| [[Congenital glaucoma]] | [[Epiphora]], [[photophobia]], [[blepharospasm]], [[corneal enlargement]] ([[buphthalmos]]) |
| [[Peripheral anterior synechiae]] | Variable; may cause elevated IOP and angle narrowing |
| [[Angle recession]] | History of [[ocular trauma]]; widened [[ciliary body band]], irregular angle anatomy |

## Diagnosis and Identification

### Gonioscopy

[[Gonioscopy]] is the gold standard for clinical identification of the scleral spur. Using a [[goniolens]] (e.g., [[Goldmann three-mirror lens]], [[Zeiss four-mirror lens]], [[Posner lens]], [[Sussman lens]]), the clinician can directly visualize the structures of the [[iridocorneal angle]], including the scleral spur.

#### Gonioscopic Appearance

On gonioscopy, the scleral spur appears as a prominent, whitish or pale band located between the pigmented [[trabecular meshwork]] (anteriorly) and the darker [[ciliary body band]] (posteriorly). It is one of the most consistently identifiable angle landmarks and serves as the reference point for grading the depth and openness of the angle.

The classic gonioscopic landmarks, from anterior to posterior, are:

1. **[[Schwalbe's line]]** — the anterior border of the trabecular meshwork; termination of [[Descemet's membrane]]
2. **[[Trabecular meshwork]]** — the functional filtration tissue; may be pigmented (especially the posterior, or functional, portion)
3. **[[Scleral spur]]** — the whitish ridge between the trabecular meshwork and the ciliary body band
4. **[[Ciliary body band]]** — the gray or brown band representing the anterior face of the ciliary body
5. **[[Iris root]]** — the most peripheral portion of the iris

#### Grading Systems

The scleral spur is a critical reference point in several angle grading systems:

| **Grading System** | **Role of Scleral Spur** |
|---|---|
| [[Shaffer grading system]] | Angle width graded based on the angular approach of the iris to the trabecular meshwork; scleral spur visibility indicates at least a Grade 2 (20°) open angle |
| [[Scheie classification]] | Angle graded by the most posterior structure visible; scleral spur visibility indicates a moderately open angle |
| [[Spaeth gonioscopic grading system]] | Iris insertion level described relative to the scleral spur (e.g., at, anterior to, or posterior to the spur); includes iris configuration and degree of pigmentation |

If the scleral spur is not visible on gonioscopy, the angle is considered narrow or potentially occludable, raising concern for [[angle-closure glaucoma]].

#### Indentation Gonioscopy

[[Indentation gonioscopy]] (also called dynamic or compression gonioscopy) involves applying gentle pressure to the cornea with a small-footprint goniolens (e.g., [[Zeiss lens]] or [[Posner lens]]), which displaces aqueous humor posteriorly and mechanically opens the angle. This technique is invaluable for distinguishing between appositional angle closure (which opens with indentation, revealing the scleral spur and other angle structures) and synechial closure (which does not open, as the iris is permanently adhered to the angle wall at or anterior to the scleral spur).

### Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography (AS-OCT)

[[Anterior segment optical coherence tomography]] ([[AS-OCT]]) is a non-contact, high-resolution imaging modality that provides cross-sectional images of the anterior segment, including the [[cornea]], [[anterior chamber]], [[iris]], [[lens]], and [[iridocorneal angle]]. The scleral spur is a key anatomical landmark on AS-OCT images and is used as a reference point for numerous quantitative measurements.

#### Identification on AS-OCT

On AS-OCT, the scleral spur is identified as the inward projection of the sclera at the junction between the trabecular meshwork and the ciliary body. It appears as a prominent, hyperreflective point or ridge at the inner scleral wall. Accurate identification of the scleral spur is essential for reliable measurement of angle parameters.

#### Quantitative Parameters Referenced to the Scleral Spur

| **Parameter** | **Definition** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|---|
| **Angle opening distance (AOD)** | The perpendicular distance from the trabecular meshwork to the anterior iris surface at a specified distance from the scleral spur (e.g., AOD500, AOD750) | Narrow AOD values indicate a narrow angle and increased risk of [[angle closure]] |
| **Trabecular-iris space area (TISA)** | The trapezoidal area bounded by the AOD line, the anterior iris surface, the inner corneoscleral wall, and a perpendicular from the scleral spur | Reduced TISA values correlate with narrow angles |
| **Scleral spur angle (SSA)** | The angle formed at the scleral spur between the inner corneoscleral wall and the anterior iris surface | Smaller SSA values indicate narrower angles |
| **Angle recess area (ARA)** | The area of the angle recess bounded by the iris, cornea, and a line from the scleral spur | Used to quantify angle width |
| **Iris-lens contact distance (ILCD)** | The extent of contact between the posterior iris surface and the anterior lens surface | Relevant to [[pupillary block]] mechanism |

#### Challenges in Scleral Spur Identification

Despite its importance, identification of the scleral spur on AS-OCT can be challenging, particularly in eyes with:

- Heavy [[iris pigmentation]] or thick iris tissue obscuring the angle recess
- [[Peripheral anterior synechiae]] obliterating the angle
- Poor image quality due to media opacities or patient movement
- Anatomical variations in scleral spur prominence

Studies have shown that inter-observer and intra-observer variability in scleral spur localization on AS-OCT can affect the reliability of angle measurements. Training and standardized protocols are recommended to improve consistency.

### Ultrasound Biomicroscopy (UBM)

[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] ([[UBM]]) is a high-frequency (35–100 MHz) [[ultrasound]] imaging technique that provides detailed cross-sectional images of the anterior segment, including structures posterior to the iris that are not visible on AS-OCT (e.g., the [[ciliary body]], [[ciliary processes]], [[zonules]], and [[pars plana]]). The scleral spur is readily identifiable on UBM as a hyperechoic (bright) inward projection of the sclera.

UBM is particularly useful for:

- Evaluating the relationship between the scleral spur, ciliary body, and iris in cases of [[plateau iris syndrome]] or [[plateau iris configuration]]
- Assessing the mechanism of angle closure (e.g., [[pupillary block]] vs. non-pupillary block mechanisms)
- Identifying [[cyclodialysis cleft]] (separation of the ciliary body from the scleral spur), which can occur after [[ocular trauma]] or [[glaucoma surgery]]
- Measuring angle parameters in eyes where gonioscopy or AS-OCT is limited

### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

While the scleral spur cannot be directly visualized on routine [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] without a goniolens, the slit-lamp is used in conjunction with gonioscopy lenses to examine the angle. The [[Van Herick technique]] (slit-lamp estimation of the peripheral anterior chamber depth relative to the corneal thickness) provides an indirect assessment of angle width but does not directly visualize the scleral spur.

## Clinical Significance in Specific Conditions

### Cyclodialysis Cleft

A [[cyclodialysis cleft]] is a separation of the [[ciliary body]] from its attachment at the scleral spur, typically resulting from blunt [[ocular trauma]] or as a complication of [[intraocular surgery]]. This separation creates a direct communication between the [[anterior chamber]] and the [[suprachoroidal space]], leading to increased [[uveoscleral outflow]], [[hypotony]] (abnormally low IOP), and potentially [[choroidal effusion]], [[maculopathy]], or [[phthisis bulbi]].

Diagnosis of cyclodialysis cleft requires careful gonioscopic examination (a deep angle recess with visible scleral spur and a gap between the ciliary body and sclera) and is confirmed by [[UBM]] or [[AS-OCT]]. Management may include [[atropine]] (to promote ciliary body apposition to the scleral spur), [[laser photocoagulation]], [[cryotherapy]], or [[surgical repair]] (direct cyclopexy).

### Angle Recession

[[Angle recession]] refers to a traumatic tear in the face of the [[ciliary body]], typically between the longitudinal and circular muscle fibers, resulting in a widened ciliary body band on gonioscopy. The scleral spur remains intact but may appear more prominent due to the posterior displacement of the iris root and ciliary body. Angle recession is a risk factor for the development of [[secondary open-angle glaucoma]] (angle-recession glaucoma), which may occur months to years after the initial trauma.

### Plateau Iris Configuration and Syndrome

In [[plateau iris configuration]], the [[ciliary processes]] are anteriorly positioned and push the peripheral iris forward against the trabecular meshwork, causing angle narrowing or closure despite a patent [[iridotomy]]. On UBM, the scleral spur serves as a reference point for assessing the position of the ciliary processes relative to the angle structures. In [[plateau iris syndrome]], angle closure persists or recurs after [[laser peripheral iridotomy]] ([[LPI]]) due to the non-pupillary block mechanism.

### Glaucoma Surgery and the Scleral Spur

The scleral spur is an important surgical landmark in several glaucoma procedures:

- **[[Goniotomy]]** and **[[trabeculotomy]]**: These procedures, used primarily in [[congenital glaucoma]], involve incising or disrupting the trabecular meshwork at the level of the scleral spur to improve aqueous outflow.
- **[[Trabectome]]** and **[[gonioscopy-assisted transluminal trabeculotomy]] ([[GATT]])**: Minimally invasive glaucoma surgery ([[MIGS]]) procedures that target the trabecular meshwork and [[Schlemm's canal]] at the scleral spur.
- **[[iStent]]** and other [[MIGS]] devices: These micro-bypass stents are implanted through the trabecular meshwork into Schlemm's canal at the level of the scleral spur to enhance aqueous outflow.
- **[[Ab interno canaloplasty]] ([[ABiC]])**: Involves catheterization of Schlemm's canal, with the scleral spur serving as a landmark for canal identification.

Accurate identification of the scleral spur during these procedures is essential to avoid complications such as [[cyclodialysis]], [[hemorrhage]], or damage to adjacent structures.

## Management and Treatment Considerations

As the scleral spur is a normal anatomical structure, it does not require treatment per se. However, conditions affecting or involving the scleral spur necessitate specific management strategies:

| **Condition** | **Management** |
|---|---|
| [[Primary open-angle glaucoma]] | [[IOP]]-lowering medications (e.g., [[prostaglandin analogs]], [[beta-blockers]], [[alpha agonists]], [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]], [[Rho kinase inhibitors]]); [[laser trabeculoplasty]]; [[MIGS]]; [[trabeculectomy]]; [[glaucoma drainage devices]] |
| [[Primary angle-closure glaucoma]] | [[Laser peripheral iridotomy]]; [[pilocarpine]]; [[lens extraction]]; [[goniosynechialysis]]; IOP-lowering medications |
| [[Cyclodialysis cleft]] | [[Atropine]]; [[laser photocoagulation]]; [[cryotherapy]]; [[direct cyclopexy]]; [[vitrectomy]] with gas tamponade |
| [[Angle recession glaucoma]] | IOP-lowering medications; [[laser trabeculoplasty]] (often less effective); [[filtering surgery]] |
| [[Plateau iris syndrome]] | [[Laser peripheral iridotomy]]; [[argon laser peripheral iridoplasty]] ([[ALPI]]); long-term [[pilocarpine]]; [[lens extraction]] |
| [[Pigmentary glaucoma]] | IOP-lowering medications; [[laser trabeculoplasty]]; [[pilocarpine]] (controversial); [[filtering surgery]] |

### Pharmacological Agents Acting at the Scleral Spur

Several pharmacological agents exert their IOP-lowering effects through mechanisms involving the scleral spur and its associated structures:

| **Drug Class** | **Mechanism Related to Scleral Spur** |
|---|---|
| [[Parasympathomimetics]] (e.g., [[pilocarpine]]) | Contract the [[ciliary muscle]], pulling on the scleral spur, opening the [[trabecular meshwork]], and enhancing conventional outflow |
| [[Prostaglandin analogs]] (e.g., [[latanoprost]], [[travoprost]], [[bimatoprost]]) | Primarily enhance [[uveoscleral outflow]] by remodeling the [[extracellular matrix]] of the ciliary muscle; indirect effects on scleral spur tension |
| [[Rho kinase inhibitors]] (e.g., [[netarsudil]]) | Reduce outflow resistance at the trabecular meshwork and [[Schlemm's canal]], structures anchored to the scleral spur |

## Morphological Variations and Biometric Considerations

The morphology of the scleral spur varies among individuals and is influenced by several factors:

- **[[Age]]**: The scleral spur may become less prominent with age due to changes in collagen and elastic tissue.
- **[[Refractive error]]**: [[Hyperopic]] eyes tend to have shorter [[axial lengths]], shallower anterior chambers, and narrower angles, making the scleral spur more difficult to visualize on gonioscopy. [[Myopic]] eyes generally have wider angles and more easily identifiable scleral spurs.
- **[[Ethnicity]]**: Studies have demonstrated differences in anterior segment anatomy, including angle width and scleral spur morphology, among different ethnic groups. [[East Asian]] populations, for example, have a higher prevalence of narrow angles and [[angle-closure glaucoma]].
- **[[Sex]]**: Females tend to have shallower anterior chambers and narrower angles than males, potentially affecting scleral spur visibility.

---

## Summary

The scleral spur is a fundamental anatomical landmark of the [[anterior chamber angle]], serving as the structural anchor for the [[trabecular meshwork]] and the [[ciliary muscle]]. Its identification is essential for accurate gonioscopic assessment, anterior segment imaging interpretation, and the diagnosis and management of [[glaucoma]] and other angle-related conditions. Clinicians in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] must possess a thorough understanding of the scleral spur's anatomy, histology, biomechanical function, and clinical significance to provide optimal patient care.
