

# Adie's Tonic Pupil

---

**Adie's Tonic Pupil** (also known as **Adie's pupil**, **tonic pupil**, or **Holmes-Adie pupil**) is a [[neuro-ophthalmological]] condition characterized by a tonically dilated [[pupil]] that demonstrates light-near dissociation, segmental [[iris sphincter]] palsy, and denervation supersensitivity to dilute cholinergic agents. The condition results from damage to the [[postganglionic parasympathetic]] nerve fibers originating from the [[ciliary ganglion]] or the short [[posterior ciliary nerves]] that innervate the [[iris sphincter muscle]] and the [[ciliary muscle]]. First comprehensively described by British neurologist William John Adie in 1932, the condition is predominantly unilateral, benign, and most commonly affects young women between the ages of 20 and 40. When accompanied by diminished or absent [[deep tendon reflexes]], the condition is referred to as **[[Holmes-Adie syndrome]]** (or Adie's syndrome).

---

## Epidemiology

Adie's tonic pupil is a relatively common cause of [[anisocoria]] in clinical practice. The estimated annual incidence is approximately 4.7 per 100,000 population. Key epidemiological features include:

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Age of onset** | Typically 20–40 years; can occur at any age |
| **Sex predilection** | Female predominance (~70% of cases) |
| **Laterality** | Unilateral in ~80% of initial presentations; bilateral involvement may develop over time (~4% per year become bilateral) |
| **Prevalence of Holmes-Adie syndrome** | Approximately 50–90% of patients with tonic pupil also demonstrate hyporeflexia |
| **Ethnicity** | No significant racial or ethnic predilection |
| **Familial occurrence** | Rare; sporadic cases are the norm, though rare familial clustering has been reported |

Over time, the initially dilated tonic pupil tends to become progressively miotic ("the little old Adie's"), and the contralateral pupil may become involved, eventually leading to bilateral tonic pupils in a significant minority of patients.

## Anatomy and Neural Pathway

Understanding Adie's tonic pupil requires a thorough knowledge of the [[parasympathetic]] pathway controlling [[pupillary constriction]] and [[accommodation]].

### Parasympathetic Pupillary Pathway

1. **[[Preganglionic neurons]]**: The parasympathetic pathway for pupillary constriction begins at the [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]] in the dorsal [[midbrain]]. Preganglionic parasympathetic fibers travel with the [[oculomotor nerve]] (cranial nerve III) along its inferior division to reach the [[ciliary ganglion]] located in the posterior [[orbit]].

2. **[[Ciliary ganglion]]**: This small parasympathetic ganglion, approximately 1–2 mm in diameter, is situated within the [[orbital apex]], lateral to the [[optic nerve]]. It contains approximately 30,000 neurons. Critically, roughly **97%** of the postganglionic fibers from the ciliary ganglion innervate the [[ciliary muscle]] (responsible for [[accommodation]]), while only approximately **3%** innervate the [[iris sphincter muscle]] (responsible for [[miosis]]).

3. **[[Short posterior ciliary nerves]]**: Postganglionic fibers exit the ciliary ganglion via 6–10 short posterior ciliary nerves, which penetrate the [[sclera]] around the [[optic nerve head]] and travel within the [[suprachoroidal space]] to reach the [[ciliary body]] and [[iris]].

4. **Target tissues**: The [[iris sphincter muscle]] contracts to produce [[miosis]], while the [[ciliary muscle]] contracts to increase the refractive power of the [[crystalline lens]] during [[accommodation]].

### Relevance to Adie's Tonic Pupil

In Adie's tonic pupil, the lesion is localized to the **ciliary ganglion** or the **short posterior ciliary nerves** (i.e., the postganglionic parasympathetic pathway). Because 97% of the ciliary ganglion neurons serve accommodation and only 3% serve the pupil, aberrant regeneration of damaged fibers preferentially re-innervates the iris sphincter with fibers originally destined for the ciliary muscle. This aberrant regeneration is the fundamental mechanism underlying the characteristic **light-near dissociation** observed clinically.

## Etiology

In the majority of cases, Adie's tonic pupil is **idiopathic**. However, numerous etiologies have been identified that can damage the ciliary ganglion or short posterior ciliary nerves:

### Idiopathic
- The most common presentation; presumed to be secondary to a viral or inflammatory insult to the ciliary ganglion (possibly [[herpes simplex virus]] or [[varicella-zoster virus]])

### Infectious Causes
- [[Herpes zoster ophthalmicus]]
- [[Herpes simplex virus]] infection
- [[Syphilis]] (tertiary)
- [[Lyme disease]]
- [[HIV/AIDS]]
- [[Measles]]
- [[Diphtheria]]
- Viral [[upper respiratory tract infection]] (preceding illness commonly reported)

### Inflammatory and Autoimmune Causes
- [[Sarcoidosis]]
- [[Giant cell arteritis]]
- [[Systemic lupus erythematosus]]
- [[Sjögren syndrome]]
- [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]]
- Post-[[orbital inflammation]]

### Traumatic and Surgical Causes
- Blunt or penetrating [[orbital trauma]]
- Post-[[cataract surgery]] (damage to short posterior ciliary nerves)
- Post-[[laser photocoagulation]] (particularly [[panretinal photocoagulation]])
- Post-[[orbital surgery]]
- Post-[[scleral buckle]] surgery for [[retinal detachment]]

### Neoplastic Causes
- [[Orbital tumors]] compressing the ciliary ganglion
- [[Paraneoplastic]] syndromes (rare)

### Other Associations
- [[Diabetes mellitus]] (autonomic neuropathy)
- [[Alcoholism]]
- [[Migraine]]
- [[Dysautonomia]] (e.g., [[Ross syndrome]] — Adie's pupil + hyporeflexia + segmental anhidrosis)

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of Adie's tonic pupil is centered on **postganglionic parasympathetic denervation** followed by **aberrant reinnervation** and **cholinergic denervation supersensitivity**.

### Stage 1: Denervation

Damage to the ciliary ganglion or short posterior ciliary nerves results in loss of parasympathetic innervation to the iris sphincter muscle and ciliary muscle. Clinically, this manifests as:

- **Mydriasis**: The pupil dilates because the [[iris dilator muscle]] (innervated by the [[sympathetic nervous system]]) acts unopposed.
- **Impaired pupillary light reflex**: The denervated iris sphincter cannot contract in response to light stimulation.
- **Impaired accommodation**: Loss of ciliary muscle innervation leads to accommodative paresis, which may cause significant [[near vision]] blur, particularly in younger patients.

### Stage 2: Aberrant Reinnervation

Over weeks to months, surviving or regenerating postganglionic fibers undergo **aberrant reinnervation**. Because 97% of ciliary ganglion neurons originally innervated the ciliary muscle, the regenerating fibers that reach the iris sphincter are predominantly accommodative fibers. This produces:

- **Light-near dissociation**: The pupil constricts poorly to light (because the original pupillary fibers are lost) but constricts more robustly to a near stimulus (because the aberrant accommodative fibers now innervating the sphincter are activated during the [[near triad]] — accommodation, convergence, and miosis).
- **Segmental iris sphincter contraction**: Aberrant reinnervation is patchy and incomplete, resulting in sectors of the iris sphincter that contract while adjacent sectors remain paralyzed. This produces the characteristic **vermiform** (worm-like) movements of the iris visible on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]].
- **Tonic near response**: The aberrantly reinnervated sphincter contracts slowly during near effort and, critically, **relaxes very slowly** (tonically) when the near stimulus is removed. This tonic redilation is the hallmark feature that gives the condition its name.

### Stage 3: Denervation Supersensitivity (Cannon's Law)

Following postganglionic denervation, the denervated iris sphincter muscle develops **cholinergic supersensitivity** in accordance with **[[Cannon's law of denervation]]**. The denervated muscle upregulates [[muscarinic receptors]] (primarily [[M3 receptors]]) on its surface, making it exquisitely sensitive to low concentrations of [[acetylcholine]] and cholinergic agonists. This principle forms the basis of the **dilute pilocarpine test** used in diagnosis.

### Progressive Miosis

Over years, the ongoing process of aberrant reinnervation results in increasing numbers of accommodative fibers reaching the iris sphincter. The cumulative tonic input causes the pupil to become progressively smaller. Thus, a long-standing Adie's pupil may actually be **miotic** rather than mydriatic — the so-called **"little old Adie's"**. This can create diagnostic confusion, as the anisocoria may reverse over time (the affected pupil becoming smaller than the normal pupil).

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

Patients with Adie's tonic pupil may present with a variety of symptoms, though some are entirely asymptomatic and the condition is discovered incidentally:

| Symptom | Mechanism | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| **Anisocoria** noticed by patient or others | Unilateral mydriasis | Very common |
| **Photophobia / glare** | Mydriasis allowing excessive light entry | Common, especially early |
| **Blurred near vision** (ipsilateral eye) | Accommodative paresis | Common in young patients |
| **Difficulty with reading** | Accommodative paresis | Common |
| **Brow ache or periorbital discomfort** | Accommodative effort | Occasional |
| **Asymptomatic** | Incidental finding | Not uncommon |

Notably, patients do **not** typically report diplopia, ptosis, or extraocular motility disturbance, which helps distinguish Adie's tonic pupil from a [[third nerve palsy]].

### Signs

#### Pupillary Findings

- **Unilateral mydriasis** (in acute/early cases): The affected pupil is larger than the contralateral pupil, and the anisocoria is **greater in bright illumination** (because the affected pupil cannot constrict).
- **Poor or absent direct and consensual [[pupillary light reflex]]** on the affected side.
- **Intact but slow and tonic pupillary near response**: The pupil constricts slowly with sustained near effort and **redilates very slowly** (over 10–30 seconds or more) when the near stimulus is removed. This **light-near dissociation** is the clinical hallmark.
- **Segmental iris sphincter palsy**: On slit-lamp examination with high magnification, sectors of the iris sphincter can be seen contracting while adjacent sectors remain immobile. This produces characteristic **vermiform (worm-like) movements** of the pupillary margin.
- **Sector pupil redilation**: The pupil may redilate in a segmental, irregular fashion.

#### Accommodative Findings

- **Accommodative paresis** (early): Reduced [[amplitude of accommodation]] on the affected side, measurable with a [[RAF rule]] or [[accommodative target]].
- **Tonic accommodation**: Slow relaxation of accommodation after sustained near work, causing transient distance blur.
- **Accommodative recovery** (late): Accommodation often recovers more fully than the pupillary light reflex over time due to the 97:3 ratio of accommodative-to-pupillary fibers.

#### Deep Tendon Reflexes (Holmes-Adie Syndrome)

- Diminished or absent [[Achilles tendon reflex]] (ankle jerk) is the most commonly affected reflex.
- [[Patellar reflex]] (knee jerk) may also be diminished.
- Upper extremity reflexes are less commonly affected.
- The hyporeflexia is thought to result from a concurrent process affecting [[dorsal root ganglia]] or [[spinal cord]] synapses, though the exact mechanism remains debated.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

The diagnosis of Adie's tonic pupil is primarily **clinical**, based on the characteristic triad of:

1. Tonically dilated pupil with poor light reaction
2. Light-near dissociation with slow, tonic near response
3. Segmental iris sphincter contraction on slit-lamp examination

### Pharmacological Testing: Dilute Pilocarpine Test

The **dilute [[pilocarpine]] test** is the most important confirmatory diagnostic test for Adie's tonic pupil. It exploits the principle of **cholinergic denervation supersensitivity**.

#### Protocol

| Step | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Agent** | [[Pilocarpine]] 0.0625%–0.125% (dilute) — prepared by diluting standard 1% pilocarpine |
| **Preparation** | Mix 1 part pilocarpine 1% with 7–15 parts sterile [[normal saline]] or [[balanced salt solution]] to achieve 0.0625%–0.125% concentration |
| **Administration** | Instill 1–2 drops in **both** eyes |
| **Observation period** | 30–45 minutes |
| **Positive result** | The affected (tonic) pupil constricts significantly (≥0.5 mm more than the normal pupil), demonstrating denervation supersensitivity |
| **Normal eye response** | The normal pupil shows minimal or no constriction, as the dilute concentration is subthreshold for a normally innervated iris sphincter |

#### Interpretation

- **Positive test (constriction of the affected pupil)**: Confirms postganglionic parasympathetic denervation — consistent with Adie's tonic pupil.
- **Negative test (no constriction)**: Does not exclude Adie's tonic pupil in very early cases (denervation supersensitivity may take 1–2 weeks to develop) or in cases with extensive iris sphincter atrophy.
- **Both pupils constrict equally**: Suggests the anisocoria is not due to a tonic pupil.

> **Important**: Full-strength pilocarpine (1%) will constrict **both** normal and tonic pupils and therefore does **not** differentiate Adie's tonic pupil from a normal pupil. However, 1% pilocarpine is useful to rule out pharmacological mydriasis (e.g., from [[atropine]] or [[tropicamide]] exposure), in which case neither pupil would constrict.

### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] at high magnification (16x–25x or greater) is essential to observe:

- **Segmental iris sphincter palsy**: Sectors of the sphincter that contract versus sectors that remain paralyzed.
- **Vermiform movements**: Slow, worm-like undulations of the pupillary margin.
- **Iris stromal atrophy**: May develop in long-standing cases.

### Infrared Pupillography

[[Infrared pupillography]] (computerized pupillometry) can provide objective, quantitative documentation of:

- Reduced amplitude of pupillary constriction to light
- Prolonged latency of constriction
- Slow redilation velocity (tonic behavior)
- Asymmetric pupillary dynamics between the two eyes

This modality is particularly useful in research settings and for longitudinal monitoring.

### Additional Investigations

In most cases of isolated Adie's tonic pupil in a young, otherwise healthy patient, **no further workup is necessary**. However, additional investigations should be considered when:

- The presentation is **bilateral**
- The patient is **male** or **older**
- There are **associated neurological findings** beyond hyporeflexia
- An underlying systemic etiology is suspected

| Investigation | Indication |
|---|---|
| **[[MRI]] of the brain and orbits** | To exclude structural lesion if atypical features present |
| **[[Syphilis serology]]** (RPR/VDRL, FTA-ABS) | Bilateral tonic pupils, especially with other neurological signs (Argyll Robertson pupil differential) |
| **[[Lyme disease]] serology** | Endemic area, history of tick bite, associated symptoms |
| **[[Fasting blood glucose]] / [[HbA1c]]** | Suspected diabetic autonomic neuropathy |
| **[[Chest X-ray]] / [[ACE level]]** | Suspected [[sarcoidosis]] |
| **[[HIV testing]]** | Risk factors present |
| **[[Autonomic function testing]]** | Suspected widespread dysautonomia (e.g., [[Ross syndrome]]) |

## Differential Diagnosis

The differential diagnosis of a unilateral dilated pupil with poor light reaction includes several important conditions that must be distinguished from Adie's tonic pupil:

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| **[[Third nerve palsy]]** | [[Ptosis]], [[extraocular motility]] deficits (especially adduction, elevation, depression), possible [[diplopia]]; pupil involvement suggests compressive etiology (e.g., [[posterior communicating artery aneurysm]]) |
| **Pharmacological mydriasis** | History of exposure to [[mydriatic]] or [[cycloplegic]] agents; pupil does NOT constrict to 1% pilocarpine |
| **[[Argyll Robertson pupil]]** | Bilateral, small, irregular pupils with light-near dissociation; associated with [[neurosyphilis]]; pupils are miotic, not mydriatic |
| **[[Traumatic mydriasis]]** | History of blunt [[ocular trauma]]; iris sphincter tears visible on slit-lamp; pupil may be irregular |
| **[[Iris sphincter rupture]]** | Visible tears at pupillary margin on slit-lamp; history of trauma or surgery |
| **[[Physiological anisocoria]]** | Anisocoria ≤1 mm; both pupils react normally to light and near; anisocoria remains constant in light and dark |
| **[[Sympathetic pathway lesion]] ([[Horner syndrome]])** | Miosis (not mydriasis) on the affected side; associated [[ptosis]] and possible [[anhidrosis]]; anisocoria greater in dim illumination |

## Management and Treatment

### Patient Education and Reassurance

The cornerstone of management is **reassurance**. Patients should be informed that:

- Adie's tonic pupil is a **benign condition** that does not threaten vision or indicate serious underlying disease in the vast majority of cases.
- The condition is **not progressive** in a dangerous sense, though the pupil may change size over years.
- The accommodative difficulty typically **improves over time** as aberrant reinnervation progresses.
- The cosmetic anisocoria may diminish over years as the affected pupil gradually becomes smaller.

### Pharmacological Management

#### For Symptomatic Photophobia

- **Tinted lenses** or **[[photochromic lenses]]**: Reduce glare and photophobia caused by the dilated pupil.
- **Dilute [[pilocarpine]]** (0.0625%–0.125%): Can be used therapeutically (not just diagnostically) to constrict the tonic pupil and reduce photophobia. However, this may induce accommodative spasm and is generally reserved for significantly symptomatic patients.

#### For Accommodative Symptoms

- **[[Reading glasses]]** or **[[progressive addition lenses]]**: Prescribed to compensate for accommodative paresis, particularly in younger patients who are symptomatic for near tasks.
- **Dilute pilocarpine**: May partially restore accommodative function, though the effect is variable and may cause brow ache or induced myopia.

#### Considerations for Pilocarpine Use

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Concentration** | 0.0625%–0.125% for therapeutic use |
| **Frequency** | 1–3 times daily as needed |
| **Side effects** | Brow ache, induced [[myopia]], dimming of vision (miosis), potential for [[retinal detachment]] in predisposed individuals (rare at low concentrations) |
| **Monitoring** | Periodic assessment of pupil size, accommodation, and symptoms |

### Optical Management

| Optical Intervention | Indication |
|---|---|
| **Tinted spectacle lenses** | Photophobia and glare |
| **Photochromic lenses** | Variable lighting conditions |
| **Near addition (reading glasses)** | Accommodative paresis |
| **[[Progressive addition lenses]]** | Combined distance and near correction with accommodative deficit |
| **[[Contact lenses]] with artificial pupil** | Severe cosmetic concern or debilitating photophobia (rarely needed) |
| **Prosthetic iris devices** | Extreme cases with intolerable photophobia (exceedingly rare) |

### Surgical Management

Surgical intervention is **not indicated** for Adie's tonic pupil. The condition is benign and self-limited in terms of functional impact. No surgical procedure is recommended to alter pupil size or restore the light reflex.

### Long-Term Follow-Up

- **Annual or biannual ophthalmic examination** is recommended to:
  - Monitor for development of bilateral involvement
  - Reassess accommodative function
  - Update optical correction as needed
  - Evaluate for any atypical progression that might suggest an alternative diagnosis
- **Deep tendon reflex assessment** should be performed at initial evaluation and periodically to document the presence or progression of [[Holmes-Adie syndrome]].

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis for Adie's tonic pupil is **excellent** from a visual and systemic standpoint:

- **Visual acuity**: Remains normal in the vast majority of patients. Accommodative symptoms typically improve over months to years.
- **Pupil size**: The affected pupil tends to become progressively smaller over years due to ongoing aberrant reinnervation. Eventually, the tonic pupil may become smaller than the contralateral normal pupil.
- **Bilateral involvement**: Occurs at a rate of approximately 4% per year. Long-term follow-up studies suggest that up to 30–50% of patients may develop bilateral tonic pupils over decades.
- **Systemic health**: Isolated Adie's tonic pupil (or Holmes-Adie syndrome) carries no increased risk of mortality or serious systemic disease.
- **Quality of life**: Most patients adapt well; photophobia and accommodative symptoms are the primary sources of morbidity and are generally manageable with optical correction.

## Associated Conditions

### Holmes-Adie Syndrome

The combination of **Adie's tonic pupil** and **diminished or absent deep tendon reflexes** constitutes **[[Holmes-Adie syndrome]]**. The hyporeflexia is thought to result from a degenerative process affecting the [[dorsal root ganglia]] or spinal reflex arcs, possibly sharing a common pathogenic mechanism (viral or autoimmune) with the ciliary ganglionopathy.

### Ross Syndrome

**[[Ross syndrome]]** is the triad of:
1. Adie's tonic pupil
2. Hyporeflexia
3. Segmental [[anhidrosis]] (with compensatory [[hyperhidrosis]] in unaffected areas)

This rare condition suggests a more widespread autonomic neuropathy affecting postganglionic neurons in multiple systems.

### Autonomic Neuropathy

Some patients with Adie's tonic pupil may have subclinical evidence of more widespread [[autonomic dysfunction]], including:
- [[Orthostatic hypotension]]
- [[Cardiovascular autonomic neuropathy]]
- [[Gastrointestinal dysmotility]]
- [[Sudomotor dysfunction]]

These findings are uncommon but should be considered in patients with bilateral tonic pupils or additional autonomic symptoms.

## Historical Perspective

The tonic pupil was first described by several clinicians in the late 19th and early 20th centuries:

- **Strasburger (1902)** and **Saenger (1902)**: Early descriptions of unilateral mydriasis with tonic pupillary behavior.
- **Markus (1906)**: Described the tonic near response.
- **Gordon Holmes (1931)**: Published a detailed clinical description emphasizing the association with absent tendon reflexes.
- **William John Adie (1932)**: Provided the most comprehensive clinical characterization, establishing the syndrome as a distinct clinical entity. His seminal paper in the *British Medical Journal* described the complete syndrome of tonic pupil with areflexia.

The pharmacological basis of denervation supersensitivity was later elucidated through the work of **Scheie and Adler**, who demonstrated the constriction of tonic pupils to dilute cholinergic agents, providing both a diagnostic tool and insight into the underlying pathophysiology.

## Key Clinical Pearls

1. **Adie's tonic pupil is the most common cause of a unilateral, fixed, dilated pupil in a young woman** — always consider it before ordering expensive neuroimaging.
2. **Slit-lamp examination for segmental sphincter palsy** is one of the most valuable bedside diagnostic maneuvers and should be performed before pharmacological testing.
3. **The dilute pilocarpine test confirms postganglionic denervation** but may be falsely negative in the first 1–2 weeks before denervation supersensitivity develops.
4. **Light-near dissociation is not pathognomonic for Adie's pupil** — it also occurs in [[Argyll Robertson pupil]], dorsal midbrain lesions ([[Parinaud syndrome]]), and aberrant regeneration of the [[third cranial nerve]].
5. **Long-standing Adie's pupils become small**, not large — the "little old Adie's" phenomenon can cause diagnostic confusion if the clinician expects mydriasis.
6. **Always check deep tendon reflexes** in a patient with a tonic pupil to document Holmes-Adie syndrome.
7. **Bilateral tonic pupils should prompt investigation** for syphilis, diabetes, autonomic neuropathy, and other systemic causes.
