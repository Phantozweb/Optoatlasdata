

# Lacrimal Gland

---

The **lacrimal gland** (Latin: *glandula lacrimalis*) is the principal exocrine gland responsible for the production of the aqueous component of the [[tear film]]. Situated in the superotemporal aspect of the [[orbit]], the lacrimal gland plays an indispensable role in maintaining [[ocular surface]] homeostasis, providing lubrication, immunological defense, and optical clarity to the [[cornea]]. Dysfunction, inflammation, neoplasia, or structural abnormalities of the lacrimal gland can lead to a wide spectrum of clinical conditions ranging from [[dry eye disease]] to sight-threatening malignancies. A thorough understanding of the lacrimal gland's anatomy, physiology, pathophysiology, and associated clinical entities is essential for every optometrist and ophthalmologist.

---

## Embryology

The lacrimal gland develops from the [[surface ectoderm]] of the superolateral conjunctival fornix during the sixth to seventh week of gestation. Solid epithelial buds proliferate into the surrounding [[mesenchyme]], which is derived from [[neural crest]] cells. These buds undergo branching morphogenesis and canalization to form the tubuloalveolar architecture characteristic of the mature gland. The process is regulated by a complex interplay of signaling molecules including [[fibroblast growth factor]] (FGF), [[bone morphogenetic protein]] (BMP), and [[Pax6]] transcription factor. The gland becomes functionally mature in the postnatal period, with full reflex tearing capacity developing within the first few weeks to months of life. Congenital absence ([[alacrima]]) of the lacrimal gland is exceedingly rare and may occur in isolation or as part of syndromes such as [[alacrima-achalasia-adrenal insufficiency syndrome]] (Triple-A syndrome or [[Allgrove syndrome]]).

---

## Gross Anatomy

The lacrimal gland is a bilobed, almond-shaped serous gland located within the **lacrimal fossa** of the [[frontal bone]], in the superotemporal quadrant of the orbit. It is divided by the lateral horn of the [[levator palpebrae superioris]] aponeurosis into two contiguous lobes:

| Lobe | Location | Size | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Orbital lobe** (superior) | Within the lacrimal fossa, deep to the orbital septum | Approximately 20 × 12 × 5 mm | Not visible on external examination; accessed via a superotemporal orbitotomy |
| **Palpebral lobe** (inferior) | Inferior to the levator aponeurosis, within the superotemporal conjunctival fornix | Approximately 10 × 8 × 3 mm | Visible upon eversion of the upper eyelid; site of clinical inspection |

The gland weighs approximately 0.78 g and has a pinkish-gray, lobulated appearance. The two lobes are continuous posteriorly around the lateral edge of the levator aponeurosis. Approximately **10–12 excretory ductules** drain from both lobes into the superior conjunctival fornix. The ductules of the orbital lobe traverse through or adjacent to the palpebral lobe; therefore, excision of the palpebral lobe effectively eliminates secretory function of the entire gland.

### Anatomical Relations

- **Superiorly:** [[Frontal bone]] (lacrimal fossa), [[periorbita]]
- **Inferiorly:** [[Levator palpebrae superioris]], [[superior conjunctival fornix]]
- **Medially:** [[Lateral rectus muscle]], [[orbital fat]]
- **Laterally:** [[Zygomatic bone]], lateral orbital wall
- **Anteriorly:** [[Orbital septum]]

### Suspensory Apparatus

The lacrimal gland is supported by:
- **Whitnall's ligament** ([[superior transverse ligament]]): provides superior support
- **Lateral horn of the levator aponeurosis**: divides and supports the gland
- **Lockwood's ligament** (indirectly): contributes to inferior orbital support
- Connective tissue attachments to the [[periosteum]] of the lacrimal fossa

Weakening of these suspensory structures can result in **lacrimal gland prolapse** (ptosis of the lacrimal gland), presenting as a palpable, non-tender mass in the superotemporal eyelid.

## Vascular Supply, Innervation, and Lymphatic Drainage

### Arterial Supply

The lacrimal gland receives its primary blood supply from the **[[lacrimal artery]]**, a branch of the [[ophthalmic artery]]. In some individuals, a recurrent meningeal branch from the [[middle meningeal artery]] provides an anastomotic or alternative supply.

### Venous Drainage

Venous blood drains via the **[[lacrimal vein]]** into the [[superior ophthalmic vein]], which subsequently empties into the [[cavernous sinus]].

### Lymphatic Drainage

Lymphatic drainage is directed to the **[[preauricular lymph nodes]]** (superficial parotid nodes) and subsequently to the [[deep cervical lymph nodes]]. This lymphatic pathway is clinically relevant in the staging and spread of lacrimal gland malignancies.

### Innervation

The lacrimal gland receives a complex tripartite innervation:

| Type | Nerve Pathway | Neurotransmitter | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sensory** | [[Lacrimal nerve]] (branch of V₁, [[ophthalmic division]] of [[trigeminal nerve]]) | Substance P, CGRP | General sensation; afferent limb of reflex tearing |
| **Parasympathetic (secretomotor)** | [[Facial nerve]] (CN VII) → [[greater petrosal nerve]] → [[nerve of the pterygoid canal]] (Vidian nerve) → synapse in [[pterygopalatine ganglion]] → postganglionic fibers travel via [[zygomatic nerve]] → communicating branch to [[lacrimal nerve]] → gland | [[Acetylcholine]] (muscarinic M3 receptors), [[vasoactive intestinal peptide]] (VIP) | Primary stimulation of aqueous tear secretion |
| **Sympathetic** | Preganglionic fibers from T1–T3 → [[superior cervical ganglion]] → postganglionic fibers travel along the [[internal carotid artery]] plexus → join lacrimal nerve | [[Norepinephrine]] (adrenergic receptors) | Modulation of protein secretion; minor role in basal secretion |

The **parasympathetic pathway** is the dominant secretomotor supply. Disruption at any point along this pathway—from the [[facial nerve]] nucleus in the [[pons]] to the postganglionic fibers—can result in diminished reflex tearing and [[aqueous-deficient dry eye]].

## Histology and Microanatomy

The lacrimal gland is classified as a **tubuloacinar (tubuloalveolar) exocrine gland** with predominantly serous secretion. Key histological features include:

- **Acinar cells:** Columnar to pyramidal epithelial cells arranged around a central lumen. The apical cytoplasm contains abundant **secretory granules** (zymogen-like), while the basal aspect is rich in [[rough endoplasmic reticulum]] and [[mitochondria]]. These cells secrete water, electrolytes, and proteins including [[lysozyme]], [[lactoferrin]], [[secretory IgA]], [[lipocalin]], and [[tear-specific prealbumin]].
- **Myoepithelial cells:** Stellate, contractile cells located between the acinar cells and the [[basement membrane]]. They express [[smooth muscle actin]] and facilitate expulsion of secretory products into the ductal system upon neural stimulation.
- **Ductal cells:** Line the intralobular and interlobular ducts. They modify the primary secretion by ion and water transport. The terminal excretory ducts are lined by stratified columnar epithelium transitioning to non-keratinized stratified squamous epithelium near the conjunctival surface.
- **Interstitial tissue:** Contains [[plasma cells]] (predominantly IgA-secreting), [[lymphocytes]], [[mast cells]], [[dendritic cells]], and a rich capillary network. The presence of organized lymphoid tissue ([[MALT]]—mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue) underscores the gland's immunological function.
- **Connective tissue septa:** Divide the gland into lobules and carry blood vessels, nerves, and ducts.

## Physiology of Lacrimal Secretion

The lacrimal gland is the primary source of the **aqueous layer** of the [[tear film]], contributing approximately 95% of the aqueous component. Tear production occurs via two mechanisms:

### Basal Secretion

Continuous, low-level secretion that maintains the tear film during non-stimulated conditions. This is regulated primarily by tonic parasympathetic activity and local factors. The **accessory lacrimal glands** of [[Krause]] (approximately 42 glands in the conjunctival fornices) and [[Wolfring]] (approximately 2–5 glands near the superior tarsal border) also contribute to basal tear volume, though the relative contribution of the main lacrimal gland versus accessory glands to basal secretion remains debated.

### Reflex Secretion

Stimulated secretion in response to:
- **Sensory stimulation:** Corneal or conjunctival irritation (via [[trigeminal nerve]] afferents), bright light, wind, foreign bodies
- **Emotional stimulation:** Psychogenic tearing mediated by higher cortical centers acting on the [[lacrimal nucleus]] in the pons

### Composition of Lacrimal Gland Fluid

| Component | Function |
|---|---|
| Water and electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻) | Hydration of the ocular surface; maintenance of tear osmolarity (~302 mOsm/L) |
| [[Lysozyme]] | Antimicrobial enzyme; cleaves bacterial cell wall peptidoglycan |
| [[Lactoferrin]] | Iron-binding glycoprotein; bacteriostatic and anti-inflammatory |
| [[Secretory IgA]] (sIgA) | Immune exclusion; prevents microbial adhesion to the ocular surface |
| [[Lipocalin]] (tear-specific prealbumin) | Lipid-binding; scavenges potentially harmful lipid molecules |
| [[Epidermal growth factor]] (EGF) | Promotes corneal epithelial wound healing |
| [[Transforming growth factor-beta]] (TGF-β) | Immunomodulatory; maintains immune privilege |
| [[Defensins]] | Antimicrobial peptides |
| [[Trefoil factors]] | Mucin-associated peptides; promote epithelial repair |
| [[Matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs) | Tissue remodeling |

The lacrimal gland thus serves as a critical component of the **[[ocular surface]] immune defense system** (the so-called "lacrimal functional unit"), which also includes the [[cornea]], [[conjunctiva]], [[meibomian glands]], [[nasolacrimal duct]], and their interconnecting innervation.

## Pathophysiology

Pathological processes affecting the lacrimal gland can be broadly categorized as inflammatory, infectious, autoimmune, lymphoproliferative, neoplastic, degenerative, or structural. The pathophysiology varies by etiology:

### 1. Inflammatory and Autoimmune Pathophysiology

**[[Dacryoadenitis]]** refers to inflammation of the lacrimal gland, which may be acute or chronic.

- **Acute dacryoadenitis:** Most commonly infectious (viral or bacterial). Viral etiologies include [[Epstein-Barr virus]] (EBV), [[mumps]], [[herpes zoster]], and [[adenovirus]]. Bacterial causes include [[Staphylococcus aureus]], [[Streptococcus]] species, and [[Neisseria gonorrhoeae]] (especially in neonates and young adults). The inflammatory cascade involves neutrophilic infiltration, edema, and glandular swelling.

- **Chronic dacryoadenitis:** Often autoimmune or granulomatous. Key etiologies include:
  - **[[Sjögren syndrome]]:** The most common autoimmune cause. Lymphocytic infiltration (predominantly CD4+ T cells) of the lacrimal gland leads to progressive acinar destruction, fibrosis, and aqueous tear deficiency. Sjögren syndrome may be primary or secondary (associated with [[rheumatoid arthritis]], [[systemic lupus erythematosus]], or other connective tissue diseases). Autoantibodies including [[anti-Ro/SSA]] and [[anti-La/SSB]] are characteristic.
  - **[[IgG4-related ophthalmic disease]]:** A fibroinflammatory condition characterized by dense lymphoplasmacytic infiltration rich in IgG4-positive plasma cells, storiform fibrosis, and obliterative phlebitis. It is a major cause of bilateral lacrimal gland enlargement and must be distinguished from lymphoma.
  - **[[Sarcoidosis]]:** Non-caseating granulomatous inflammation of the lacrimal gland. Lacrimal gland involvement occurs in 7–69% of patients with ocular sarcoidosis.
  - **[[Granulomatosis with polyangiitis]]** (formerly Wegener's granulomatosis): Necrotizing granulomatous vasculitis that may involve the lacrimal gland.
  - **[[Thyroid eye disease]]:** Rarely, the lacrimal gland may be enlarged in [[Graves orbitopathy]] due to lymphocytic infiltration and edema.

### 2. Neoplastic Pathophysiology

Lacrimal gland tumors account for approximately **5–13% of orbital space-occupying lesions**. They are broadly classified as:

| Category | Tumor Type | Approximate Frequency | Behavior |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Epithelial (50%)** | [[Pleomorphic adenoma]] (benign mixed tumor) | ~25% of all lacrimal gland tumors | Benign; risk of malignant transformation if incompletely excised or recurrent |
| | [[Adenoid cystic carcinoma]] | ~12–15% | Highly malignant; perineural invasion; poor long-term prognosis |
| | [[Mucoepidermoid carcinoma]] | Rare | Malignant |
| | [[Adenocarcinoma]] (pleomorphic adenocarcinoma / carcinoma ex pleomorphic adenoma) | ~5% | Malignant; arises from pre-existing pleomorphic adenoma |
| **Lymphoid (50%)** | [[Lymphoma]] (MALT lymphoma, DLBCL, follicular lymphoma) | ~25–30% | Variable; MALT lymphoma is indolent; DLBCL is aggressive |
| | Reactive lymphoid hyperplasia (benign lymphoepithelial lesion) | ~10% | Benign; may progress to lymphoma |

The pathophysiology of epithelial tumors involves dysregulated proliferation of ductal and/or acinar epithelial cells. [[Adenoid cystic carcinoma]] is particularly notorious for its propensity for **perineural invasion** along branches of the [[trigeminal nerve]], leading to pain, numbness, and intracranial extension. [[Pleomorphic adenoma]] has a pseudocapsule that, if violated during surgery, leads to tumor seeding and recurrence with potential malignant transformation (carcinoma ex pleomorphic adenoma).

### 3. Age-Related and Degenerative Changes

With aging, the lacrimal gland undergoes progressive **acinar atrophy**, **periductal fibrosis**, **fatty infiltration (lipomatous change)**, and **lymphocytic infiltration**. These changes correlate with the age-related decline in tear production observed clinically and contribute to the high prevalence of [[dry eye disease]] in the elderly population. Hormonal changes, particularly declining [[androgen]] levels in postmenopausal women, exacerbate lacrimal gland dysfunction, as androgens have a trophic and anti-inflammatory effect on the gland.

### 4. Lacrimal Gland Prolapse

Dehiscence or laxity of the suspensory ligaments (particularly the lateral horn of the levator aponeurosis and Whitnall's ligament) allows the orbital lobe to prolapse anteriorly and inferiorly, presenting as a painless, soft, mobile mass in the superotemporal eyelid. This is most common in elderly patients and is a benign condition, though it must be differentiated from neoplasia.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

The clinical presentation of lacrimal gland pathology depends on the underlying etiology:

### Acute Dacryoadenitis
- Rapid onset of **pain**, **swelling**, and **erythema** over the superotemporal eyelid and orbit
- **S-shaped ptosis** (characteristic): the lateral portion of the upper eyelid is more ptotic than the medial portion due to the superotemporal location of the swelling
- [[Chemosis]] and [[conjunctival injection]], particularly in the superotemporal fornix
- Tenderness on palpation
- Possible [[preauricular lymphadenopathy]]
- Restricted ocular motility (especially upgaze and lateral gaze) if significant orbital inflammation is present
- Fever and malaise (in systemic infections such as mumps)
- Purulent discharge (bacterial etiology)

### Chronic Dacryoadenitis
- Painless or mildly tender, firm, non-erythematous enlargement of the lacrimal gland
- Progressive [[proptosis]] or globe displacement (inferomedially)
- Gradual onset of [[dry eye]] symptoms: foreign body sensation, burning, tearing (paradoxical reflex tearing), blurred vision
- Bilateral involvement is suggestive of systemic disease (e.g., [[Sjögren syndrome]], [[sarcoidosis]], [[IgG4-related disease]], [[lymphoma]])

### Lacrimal Gland Neoplasms
- **Pleomorphic adenoma:** Painless, slowly progressive, firm mass in the superotemporal orbit; globe displacement inferomedially; proptosis developing over months to years
- **Adenoid cystic carcinoma:** Pain (often early and prominent due to perineural invasion), rapid progression, numbness in the distribution of V₁ or V₂, proptosis, motility restriction, bony destruction on imaging
- **Lymphoma:** Painless, rubbery, bilateral or unilateral lacrimal gland enlargement; may mold to the globe without bony erosion; associated systemic symptoms (B symptoms: fever, night sweats, weight loss) in some cases

### Dry Eye Due to Lacrimal Gland Dysfunction
- Ocular surface irritation, burning, stinging, grittiness
- Fluctuating vision
- Reflex [[epiphora]] (paradoxical tearing)
- Mucous discharge
- Photophobia
- Contact lens intolerance
- Corneal complications: [[punctate epithelial erosions]], [[filamentary keratitis]], [[corneal ulceration]] in severe cases

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical Examination

- **External inspection:** Evaluate for eyelid swelling, erythema, S-shaped ptosis, proptosis, globe displacement
- **Palpation:** Assess the lacrimal gland through the superotemporal eyelid; note size, consistency (soft, firm, hard, rubbery), tenderness, mobility, and fixation to underlying structures
- **Eversion of the upper eyelid:** The palpebral lobe can be directly visualized in the superotemporal conjunctival fornix
- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]:** Evaluate the [[tear meniscus height]], [[tear film break-up time]] (TBUT), [[corneal]] and [[conjunctival]] staining with [[fluorescein]] and [[lissamine green]], and look for signs of ocular surface disease
- **[[Schirmer test]]:**
  - **Schirmer I (without anesthesia):** Measures total (basal + reflex) tear secretion. A Whatman #41 filter paper strip is placed in the lower conjunctival fornix at the junction of the lateral and middle thirds. <10 mm of wetting in 5 minutes suggests aqueous deficiency; <5 mm is strongly suggestive.
  - **Schirmer I with anesthesia (basic secretion test):** Measures basal secretion after topical anesthesia. <5 mm in 5 minutes is abnormal.
  - **Schirmer II (nasal stimulation test):** Measures reflex secretion by stimulating the nasal mucosa. Useful in differentiating lacrimal gland hyposecretion from nasolacrimal reflex arc dysfunction.
- **[[Tear osmolarity]]:** Elevated tear osmolarity (>308 mOsm/L) is a hallmark of [[dry eye disease]] and may indicate lacrimal gland insufficiency.
- **[[Phenol red thread test]]:** An alternative to the Schirmer test; a phenol red-impregnated cotton thread is placed in the lower fornix for 15 seconds. <10 mm of wetting is considered abnormal.
- **[[Tear film break-up time]] (TBUT):** <10 seconds is abnormal and suggests tear film instability, which may be secondary to aqueous deficiency from lacrimal gland dysfunction.
- **[[Ocular surface staining]]:** [[Fluorescein]], [[rose bengal]], and [[lissamine green]] staining patterns help assess the severity of ocular surface damage secondary to lacrimal gland dysfunction.

### Laboratory Investigations

| Test | Indication |
|---|---|
| Complete blood count (CBC), ESR, CRP | Systemic inflammation, infection |
| [[Anti-Ro/SSA]] and [[anti-La/SSB]] antibodies | [[Sjögren syndrome]] |
| [[Antinuclear antibody]] (ANA) | Autoimmune connective tissue diseases |
| [[Rheumatoid factor]] (RF) | [[Rheumatoid arthritis]]-associated Sjögren syndrome |
| Serum [[IgG4]] levels | [[IgG4-related disease]] |
| [[Angiotensin-converting enzyme]] (ACE), lysozyme, chest X-ray | [[Sarcoidosis]] |
| [[ANCA]] (c-ANCA, p-ANCA) | [[Granulomatosis with polyangiitis]] |
| Serum protein electrophoresis, LDH, β2-microglobulin | [[Lymphoma]] workup |
| Viral serology (EBV, mumps, VZV) | Acute viral dacryoadenitis |
| Blood cultures | Bacterial dacryoadenitis with systemic sepsis |

### Imaging

- **[[Computed tomography]] (CT) of the orbits:**
  - Best for evaluating bony changes (erosion, remodeling, destruction)
  - Pleomorphic adenoma: well-circumscribed, oval mass with bony fossa remodeling (scalloping) without destruction
  - Adenoid cystic carcinoma: irregular, poorly defined mass with bony destruction and calcification
  - Lymphoma: diffuse, molding mass conforming to the globe and orbital structures without bony erosion
  - Dacryoadenitis: diffuse gland enlargement with or without adjacent fat stranding

- **[[Magnetic resonance imaging]] (MRI) of the orbits:**
  - Superior soft tissue contrast compared to CT
  - Useful for evaluating perineural spread (adenoid cystic carcinoma along CN V)
  - T1-weighted: lacrimal gland tumors are typically isointense to muscle
  - T2-weighted: variable signal; pleomorphic adenoma is often hyperintense; adenoid cystic carcinoma may show intermediate signal
  - Gadolinium enhancement: most lacrimal gland lesions enhance; pattern and homogeneity help differentiate benign from malignant

- **[[Ultrasonography]] (B-scan):**
  - Can demonstrate lacrimal gland enlargement, internal reflectivity, and vascularity
  - Useful as an adjunct but limited by operator dependence and inability to evaluate deep orbital structures

- **[[Dacryoscintigraphy]] and [[dacryocystography]]:** Primarily used for nasolacrimal drainage assessment; not routinely used for lacrimal gland evaluation but may be relevant in comprehensive tear system workup.

### Histopathological Evaluation

- **Incisional biopsy** of the palpebral lobe: Indicated for suspected lymphoma, IgG4-related disease, sarcoidosis, or other inflammatory conditions where tissue diagnosis is required.
- **Excisional biopsy (en bloc resection):** Mandatory for suspected pleomorphic adenoma to avoid tumor seeding. Incisional biopsy of a suspected pleomorphic adenoma is **contraindicated** due to the risk of recurrence and malignant transformation.
- **Immunohistochemistry:** Essential for lymphoma subtyping (CD20, CD3, CD5, CD10, BCL-2, BCL-6, Ki-67, kappa/lambda light chains) and IgG4-related disease (IgG4/IgG ratio >40%).
- **Flow cytometry:** For lymphoma diagnosis and classification.
- **Minor salivary gland biopsy (lip biopsy):** Used in the diagnosis of [[Sjögren syndrome]] (focus score ≥1 per 4 mm² is diagnostic).

### Specialized Tear Function Tests

- **[[Tear lactoferrin]] assay:** Reduced lactoferrin levels correlate with lacrimal gland dysfunction.
- **[[Tear lysozyme]] assay:** Decreased in lacrimal gland insufficiency.
- **[[Impression cytology]]:** Evaluates conjunctival goblet cell density and epithelial metaplasia secondary to chronic aqueous deficiency.
- **[[MMP-9]] (InflammaDry):** Point-of-care test for tear film [[matrix metalloproteinase-9]]; elevated levels indicate ocular surface inflammation.
- **[[Meibography]]:** While primarily for [[meibomian gland]] assessment, it is useful in the comprehensive evaluation of dry eye to differentiate aqueous-deficient from evaporative dry eye.

## Management and Treatment

### 1. Acute Dacryoadenitis

- **Viral:** Typically self-limiting. Supportive care with warm compresses, oral analgesics ([[NSAIDs]] or [[acetaminophen]]), and artificial tears. Specific antiviral therapy (e.g., oral [[acyclovir]] or [[valacyclovir]]) is indicated for [[herpes zoster]] ophthalmicus-related dacryoadenitis.
- **Bacterial:** Empiric systemic antibiotics targeting common pathogens:
  - Oral [[amoxicillin-clavulanate]] or [[cephalexin]] for mild cases
  - Intravenous [[ceftriaxone]] or [[nafcillin]] for severe cases or immunocompromised patients
  - Incision and drainage if abscess formation occurs
  - Gonococcal dacryoadenitis: intramuscular [[ceftriaxone]] with concurrent treatment for [[Chlamydia]]

### 2. Chronic Dacryoadenitis and Autoimmune Conditions

- **[[Sjögren syndrome]]:**
  - **Topical therapy:** Preservative-free [[artificial tears]] (frequent use), [[hydroxypropyl cellulose]] inserts ([[Lacrisert]]), [[autologous serum tears]] (20% concentration), topical [[cyclosporine A]] 0.05% ([[Restasis]]) or 0.09% ([[Cequa]]), topical [[lifitegrast]] 5% ([[Xiidra]]), topical [[varenicline]] nasal spray ([[Tyrvaya]]) to stimulate reflex tearing
  - **Punctal occlusion:** [[Punctal plugs]] (temporary collagen or semi-permanent silicone) or [[punctal cautery]] to reduce tear drainage and increase tear film residence time
  - **Systemic therapy:** Oral [[pilocarpine]] (5 mg TID) or [[cevimeline]] (30 mg TID) as muscarinic agonists to stimulate lacrimal and salivary secretion; systemic immunosuppression ([[hydroxychloroquine]], [[methotrexate]], [[rituximab]]) for severe systemic disease
  - **Environmental modifications:** Humidifiers, moisture chamber spectacles, avoidance of desiccating environments
  - **Omega-3 fatty acid supplementation:** May provide anti-inflammatory benefit (evidence is mixed per the DREAM study)

- **[[IgG4-related ophthalmic disease]]:**
  - First-line: Systemic [[corticosteroids]] (oral [[prednisone]] 0.6–1.0 mg/kg/day with gradual taper)
  - Steroid-sparing agents for relapsing disease: [[rituximab]], [[azathioprine]], [[mycophenolate mofetil]], [[methotrexate]]
  - Monitoring of serum IgG4 levels and imaging for treatment response

- **[[Sarcoidosis]]:**
  - Systemic [[corticosteroids]] for active disease
  - Steroid-sparing agents: [[methotrexate]], [[azathioprine]], [[mycophenolate mofetil]], [[infliximab]] for refractory cases
  - Topical corticosteroids and lubricants for associated ocular surface disease

- **[[Granulomatosis with polyangiitis]]:**
  - Systemic immunosuppression: [[cyclophosphamide]] or [[rituximab]] with corticosteroids for induction; [[azathioprine]] or [[methotrexate]] for maintenance

### 3. Neoplastic Lesions

#### Pleomorphic Adenoma (Benign Mixed Tumor)
- **Treatment of choice:** Complete **en bloc excision** via a lateral orbitotomy approach with intact pseudocapsule. The tumor must NOT be biopsied or debulked, as violation of the capsule leads to seeding, recurrence (up to 30%), and risk of malignant transformation.
- Long-term follow-up with serial imaging is recommended.

#### Adenoid Cystic Carcinoma
- **Surgical excision:** Wide local excision or radical orbitotomy; [[orbital exenteration]] may be necessary for advanced disease with bony invasion
- **Adjuvant radiation therapy:** External beam radiation or [[proton beam therapy]]; often used postoperatively
- **Chemotherapy:** Role is limited; regimens including [[cisplatin]], [[doxorubicin]], and [[cyclophosphamide]] have been used for metastatic disease
- **Intra-arterial chemotherapy:** Investigational
- **Prognosis:** Poor long-term survival; 5-year survival approximately 50–70%, but 10-year survival drops to 20–30% due to late recurrences and distant metastases (lung, bone, brain)

#### Lymphoma
- **Low-grade [[MALT lymphoma]]:** External beam [[radiation therapy]] (20–30 Gy) is the standard treatment for localized disease; excellent local control rates (>95%)
- **Aggressive lymphomas (DLBCL):** Systemic chemotherapy ([[R-CHOP]]: [[rituximab]], [[cyclophosphamide]], [[doxorubicin]], [[vincristine]], [[prednisone]]) with or without radiation
- **Staging workup:** Full-body [[PET-CT]], bone marrow biopsy, and hematology/oncology consultation are mandatory

### 4. Lacrimal Gland Prolapse
- **Observation** if asymptomatic
- **Surgical repositioning:** Dacryopexy—the prolapsed gland is repositioned into the lacrimal fossa and secured to the periosteum, often performed in conjunction with [[blepharoplasty]]
- Excision of the prolapsed gland is generally avoided to preserve tear production

### 5. Dacryops (Lacrimal Duct Cyst)
- **Observation** for small, asymptomatic cysts
- **Marsupialization** or **excision** via a transconjunctival approach for symptomatic lesions

### 6. Pharmacological Agents Targeting Lacrimal Gland Function

| Agent | Mechanism | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| [[Cyclosporine A]] 0.05%/0.09% (Restasis/Cequa) | Calcineurin inhibitor; reduces T-cell-mediated inflammation in the lacrimal gland and conjunctiva; may increase tear production | [[Aqueous-deficient dry eye]], Sjögren syndrome |
| [[Lifitegrast]] 5% (Xiidra) | [[LFA-1]] antagonist; blocks T-cell activation and cytokine release | Dry eye disease |
| [[Varenicline]] nasal spray (Tyrvaya) | Cholinergic agonist (nicotinic acetylcholine receptor); stimulates trigeminal parasympathetic reflex arc to increase tear production | Dry eye disease |
| Oral [[pilocarpine]] (Salagen) | Muscarinic (M3) agonist; directly stimulates lacrimal and salivary gland secretion | Sjögren syndrome |
| Oral [[cevimeline]] (Evoxac) | Muscarinic (M1, M3) agonist; stimulates exocrine gland secretion | Sjögren syndrome |
| [[Diquafosol]] tetrasodium 3% (Diquas) | P2Y2 purinergic receptor agonist; stimulates aqueous and mucin secretion from conjunctival goblet cells and lacrimal gland | Dry eye disease (available in Japan and some Asian countries) |
| [[Rebamipide]] 2% (Mucosta) | Mucin secretagogue; increases goblet cell density and mucin expression | Dry eye disease (available in Japan and some Asian countries) |
| [[Autologous serum tears]] | Contains growth factors (EGF, TGF-β), fibronectin, and immunoglobulins that mimic natural tears | Severe aqueous-deficient dry eye refractory to conventional therapy |

## Surgical Approaches to the Lacrimal Gland

| Approach | Indication | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Transconjunctival (palpebral lobe)** | Biopsy of palpebral lobe, dacryops excision, small inflammatory lesions | Incision through the superotemporal conjunctival fornix; provides access to the palpebral lobe |
| **Lateral orbitotomy (with or without bone flap)** | Pleomorphic adenoma, large orbital lobe tumors | Lateral canthotomy and periosteal incision; removal of lateral orbital wall segment for access to the orbital lobe; allows en bloc excision |
| **Transcutaneous (upper eyelid crease)** | Biopsy, dacryopexy, small lesions | Skin incision in the upper eyelid crease; dissection through the orbicularis to reach the gland |
| **Orbital exenteration** | Advanced malignant tumors (adenoid cystic carcinoma with extensive invasion) | Removal of all orbital contents including the globe, extraocular muscles, fat, and periosteum |

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis of lacrimal gland disorders varies widely:

- **Acute dacryoadenitis:** Excellent prognosis with appropriate treatment; most cases resolve completely
- **Sjögren syndrome:** Chronic, progressive condition; lifelong management required; increased risk of [[lymphoma]] (approximately 5–10% lifetime risk of non-Hodgkin lymphoma)
- **IgG4-related disease:** Generally responsive to corticosteroids, but relapses are common; long-term immunosuppression may be needed
- **Pleomorphic adenoma:** Excellent prognosis if completely excised en bloc; recurrence rate is high (up to 30%) if capsule is violated
- **Adenoid cystic carcinoma:** Guarded to poor prognosis; high rates of local recurrence and late distant metastases despite aggressive treatment
- **MALT lymphoma:** Excellent prognosis with radiation therapy; 5-year survival >90%
- **DLBCL:** Variable prognosis depending on stage; 5-year survival 50–70% with R-CHOP

---

## Key Clinical Pearls

1. The **S-shaped ptosis** is a classic clinical sign of lacrimal gland enlargement and should prompt further investigation.
2. **Never perform an incisional biopsy** of a suspected pleomorphic adenoma—always plan for en bloc excision via lateral orbitotomy.
3. **Pain** in a lacrimal gland mass is a red flag for malignancy, particularly [[adenoid cystic carcinoma]].
4. **Bilateral lacrimal gland enlargement** should raise suspicion for systemic disease: [[Sjögren syndrome]], [[sarcoidosis]], [[IgG4-related disease]], or [[lymphoma]].
5. The **parasympathetic secretomotor pathway** (CN VII → greater petrosal nerve → pterygopalatine ganglion → lacrimal nerve) is the most clinically relevant neural pathway for lacrimal gland function.
6. Age-related lacrimal gland atrophy is a significant contributor to the high prevalence of [[dry eye disease]] in the elderly.
7. Patients with [[Sjögren syndrome]] require long-term monitoring for the development of [[lymphoma]].
