

# Posterior Vitreous Detachment

---

**Posterior Vitreous Detachment (PVD)** is a common age-related ophthalmological condition defined by the separation of the [[posterior vitreous cortex]] from the [[internal limiting membrane (ILM)]] of the [[retina]]. It represents one of the most frequent causes of acute-onset [[floaters]] and [[photopsia]] encountered in clinical optometric and ophthalmological practice. While PVD is often a benign, self-limiting process, its clinical significance lies in its potential to precipitate sight-threatening complications, including [[retinal tears]], [[retinal detachment]], and [[vitreous hemorrhage]]. A thorough understanding of the pathophysiology, clinical presentation, diagnostic evaluation, and management of PVD is essential for all eye care practitioners.

---

## Epidemiology

PVD is overwhelmingly an age-related phenomenon. Autopsy and clinical studies have demonstrated the following epidemiological patterns:

| **Factor** | **Detail** |
|---|---|
| **Prevalence (age 60–69)** | Approximately 24–27% |
| **Prevalence (age >70)** | Approximately 57–63%; approaches 80–87% in the ninth decade |
| **Sex predilection** | Slightly more common in females, potentially related to postmenopausal [[estrogen]] decline and its effects on [[collagen]] metabolism |
| **Laterality** | Bilateral in the majority of cases; fellow eye involvement typically occurs within 6–24 months of the first eye |
| **Myopia association** | Occurs approximately 10 years earlier in eyes with [[axial myopia]] (≥ −3.00 D) |
| **Post-surgical incidence** | Markedly accelerated following [[cataract surgery]] ([[phacoemulsification]]), [[YAG capsulotomy]], and intravitreal injections |

Additional risk factors include [[diabetes mellitus]] (particularly in the absence of significant [[diabetic retinopathy]]), ocular inflammation ([[uveitis]]), and ocular trauma. The condition is rare in children and young adults unless associated with pathological states such as [[Stickler syndrome]], [[Marfan syndrome]], or [[Ehlers-Danlos syndrome]].

## Anatomy of the Vitreous Body

A comprehensive understanding of PVD requires detailed knowledge of [[vitreous humor]] anatomy and its relationship to surrounding ocular structures.

### Composition

The [[vitreous body]] occupies approximately 80% of the globe's volume (~4 mL) and is a transparent, avascular, gel-like structure composed of:

- **Water**: ~98–99% of total vitreous mass
- **[[Collagen]]**: Predominantly [[type II collagen]] fibrils (with smaller amounts of types V/XI, IX, and VI), which provide the structural scaffold
- **[[Hyaluronic acid]] (hyaluronan)**: A [[glycosaminoglycan]] (GAG) that fills the spaces between collagen fibrils, maintaining gel hydration and spacing
- **Other components**: Trace amounts of [[ascorbic acid]], inorganic salts, lipids, and non-collagenous proteins

### Structural Organization

The vitreous is not homogeneous. It possesses a denser outer shell known as the **[[vitreous cortex]]**, which is approximately 100–300 μm thick. The posterior vitreous cortex is of particular clinical importance in PVD, as it is the layer that separates from the retinal surface. The cortex contains a higher concentration of collagen fibrils and [[hyalocytes]]—specialized cells of monocyte/macrophage lineage that play roles in hyaluronan synthesis, phagocytosis, and wound healing.

### Vitreoretinal Adhesions

The vitreous is attached to the retina at several key anatomical sites, and the strength of these adhesions directly influences the clinical behavior and complications of PVD:

| **Adhesion Site** | **Strength** | **Clinical Significance in PVD** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Vitreous base]]** | Strongest; essentially inseparable | PVD does not typically involve the vitreous base; persistent adhesion here can cause traction on the [[peripheral retina]] |
| **[[Optic disc]] margin** | Strong | The peripapillary adhesion is often the last to release; its separation produces the classic **[[Weiss ring]]** |
| **[[Macula]] / [[fovea]]** | Moderate | Anomalous or incomplete separation can lead to [[vitreomacular traction (VMT)]] or [[macular hole]] formation |
| **Retinal blood vessels** | Moderate | Traction on vessels during PVD can cause [[vitreous hemorrhage]] |
| **Lattice degeneration sites** | Variable; often strong | Traction at areas of [[lattice degeneration]] increases risk of [[retinal tears]] |
| **Peripheral retinal thinning / cysts** | Variable | May predispose to [[retinal breaks]] during vitreous separation |

## Pathophysiology

The development of PVD is a multistep degenerative process driven primarily by biochemical changes within the vitreous gel. The two fundamental processes are **[[vitreous liquefaction]] (synchysis)** and **weakening of vitreoretinal adhesion (syneresis)**.

### 1. Vitreous Liquefaction (Synchysis)

With advancing age, the delicate balance between [[collagen]] fibrils and [[hyaluronic acid]] is progressively disrupted:

- **Collagen fibril aggregation**: Type II collagen fibrils lose their uniform spacing and aggregate into thicker, visible bundles. This process is thought to be driven by enzymatic degradation (including [[matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs)]]) and oxidative damage (loss of [[ascorbic acid]] antioxidant protection).
- **Hyaluronic acid redistribution**: Hyaluronan dissociates from the collagen network, leading to pockets of liquid vitreous (**[[lacunae]]**) interspersed with denser collagen aggregates.
- **Progressive liquefaction**: By the age of 80, more than 50% of the vitreous volume may be liquefied. These liquid pockets coalesce over time, forming larger cavities, particularly in the central and posterior vitreous.

### 2. Weakening of Vitreoretinal Adhesion

Simultaneously, the molecular adhesion between the [[posterior vitreous cortex]] and the [[internal limiting membrane]] weakens:

- The extracellular matrix molecules mediating adhesion—including [[laminin]], [[fibronectin]], and [[type IV collagen]]—undergo age-related degradation.
- [[Hyalocytes]] in the posterior cortex may contribute to enzymatic weakening of the interface.
- The combination of a progressively liquefied vitreous cavity and weakened adhesion creates the mechanical conditions for separation.

### 3. Acute Separation Event

PVD typically begins with separation of the posterior vitreous cortex from the [[perifoveal]] retina, often initiated by a sudden mechanical event (e.g., saccadic eye movement, head trauma, or Valsalva maneuver). Liquid vitreous from the central cavity dissects through a break in the thin posterior cortex and enters the **[[retro-hyaloid space]]** (the potential space between the posterior cortex and the ILM), propagating the detachment anteriorly toward the [[vitreous base]].

The sequence of separation is generally:

1. **Perifoveal detachment** → 2. **Macular detachment** → 3. **Equatorial extension** → 4. **Peripapillary detachment** (release from the optic disc margin, producing the Weiss ring) → 5. **Extension to the vitreous base** (where the vitreous remains firmly attached)

This process may occur acutely over hours to days, or may progress gradually over weeks to months. An **incomplete PVD**—where the vitreous has separated posteriorly but remains focally adherent at one or more sites—is a particularly important clinical entity, as persistent vitreoretinal traction at these adhesion points is the primary mechanism for complications.

### Molecular and Enzymatic Mediators

Recent research has elucidated several molecular mediators involved in PVD:

- **[[Matrix metalloproteinase-2 (MMP-2)]]** and **[[MMP-9]]**: Degrade type IV collagen and other extracellular matrix components at the vitreoretinal interface.
- **[[Tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases (TIMPs)]]**: Counterbalance MMP activity; an imbalance favoring MMPs promotes separation.
- **Oxidative stress**: Decreased [[ascorbic acid]] concentration in the aging vitreous reduces antioxidant protection, accelerating collagen cross-linking and fibril aggregation.
- **[[Advanced glycation end-products (AGEs)]]**: Particularly relevant in [[diabetes mellitus]], AGEs cross-link collagen fibrils, paradoxically both stiffening the gel and promoting liquefaction in adjacent areas.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

The hallmark symptoms of acute PVD are sudden-onset [[floaters]] and [[photopsia]]. The clinical history is often highly characteristic:

| **Symptom** | **Description** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|---|
| **Floaters** | Sudden appearance of new floaters, often described as a "cobweb," "ring," "fly," "tadpole," or "cloud" of spots in the visual field | Condensed collagen fibrils, detached posterior vitreous cortex, and/or [[Weiss ring]] casting shadows on the retina |
| **Photopsia (light flashes)** | Brief, arc-like or lightning-like flashes of light, typically in the temporal visual field; often more noticeable in dim illumination or with eye movement | Mechanical stimulation of [[photoreceptors]] by vitreoretinal traction during vitreous separation |
| **"Shower" of floaters** | Sudden onset of numerous small, dark floaters resembling "pepper" or "soot" | Strongly suggestive of [[vitreous hemorrhage]] from a torn retinal vessel; warrants urgent evaluation |
| **Visual field defect / "curtain" or "shadow"** | Progressive peripheral visual field loss | Highly suggestive of [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]]; requires emergent referral |
| **Decreased visual acuity** | Blurred or reduced central vision | May indicate [[vitreous hemorrhage]], [[vitreomacular traction]], [[macular hole]], or [[retinal detachment]] involving the macula |

It is critical to note that many PVDs are **asymptomatic** and discovered incidentally during routine [[dilated fundus examination]].

### Signs

Clinical examination findings in PVD include:

- **[[Weiss ring]]**: A ring-shaped or oval opacity visible in the anterior vitreous, representing the detached peripapillary glial tissue (annular attachment around the [[optic nerve head]]). Its presence is pathognomonic for complete PVD at the posterior pole.
- **Vitreous syneresis**: Visible collapse and mobility of the vitreous gel, with the detached posterior hyaloid membrane often seen undulating with eye movements on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]].
- **[[Shafer's sign]] (tobacco dust)**: The presence of pigmented cells ([[pigment granules]]) in the anterior vitreous, detected on slit-lamp examination. This sign is highly suggestive of a [[retinal tear]] and mandates thorough peripheral retinal examination. Shafer's sign has a reported positive predictive value of approximately 50–70% for retinal breaks.
- **Vitreous hemorrhage**: Blood in the vitreous cavity, ranging from trace to dense, obscuring the fundus view.
- **Retinal findings**: [[Retinal tears]] (horseshoe/flap tears, operculated holes), [[retinal detachment]], [[lattice degeneration]], or areas of vitreoretinal traction may be identified on [[dilated fundus examination]].

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

The diagnosis of PVD is primarily clinical, based on a combination of patient history and comprehensive ocular examination:

1. **Detailed history**: Onset, duration, and character of symptoms (floaters, flashes, visual field changes, vision loss). Inquire about risk factors (age, myopia, prior surgery, trauma, family history of retinal detachment).

2. **[[Visual acuity]] testing**: Baseline and essential for monitoring.

3. **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: Examination of the anterior vitreous for Weiss ring, pigmented cells (Shafer's sign), red blood cells, or inflammatory cells. Use of a high-magnification preset lens (e.g., [[90D lens]], [[78D lens]], or [[Goldmann three-mirror lens]]) allows detailed evaluation of the vitreoretinal interface.

4. **[[Dilated fundus examination]]**: Mandatory in all patients presenting with symptoms suggestive of PVD. [[Binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy (BIO)]] with [[scleral indentation]] is the gold standard for evaluating the peripheral retina for tears, holes, detachment, and lattice degeneration. A [[Goldmann three-mirror contact lens]] examination provides excellent stereoscopic views of the vitreoretinal interface and peripheral retina.

5. **[[Intraocular pressure (IOP)]] measurement**: To rule out associated conditions; IOP may be slightly lower in eyes with retinal detachment ([[Schwartz-Matsuo syndrome]]).

### Imaging Modalities

| **Modality** | **Role in PVD Evaluation** | **Key Findings** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** | Gold standard imaging for PVD confirmation, especially when media opacities (e.g., dense vitreous hemorrhage, cataract) preclude fundoscopy | Detached posterior hyaloid membrane visible as a hyperechoic membrane in the vitreous cavity, mobile with eye movements, attached at the optic disc (incomplete PVD) or free-floating (complete PVD). Differentiates PVD from [[retinal detachment]] (retinal detachment shows a taut, less mobile membrane attached at the [[ora serrata]] and [[optic disc]]). |
| **[[Optical coherence tomography (OCT)]]** | High-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the vitreoretinal interface at the posterior pole | Demonstrates vitreous separation from the ILM, residual vitreoretinal adhesions, [[vitreomacular traction (VMT)]], [[epiretinal membrane (ERM)]], [[macular hole]] (full-thickness or lamellar), and subretinal fluid. [[Spectral-domain OCT (SD-OCT)]] and [[swept-source OCT (SS-OCT)]] provide superior resolution. |
| **[[OCT angiography (OCTA)]]** | Adjunctive role | May help identify vascular changes associated with vitreoretinal traction or neovascularization. |
| **[[Widefield fundus photography]] / [[Optomap]]** | Documentation and monitoring | Captures peripheral retinal pathology, retinal tears, hemorrhages, and lattice degeneration in a single image. |
| **[[Fundus autofluorescence (FAF)]]** | Limited direct role | May identify associated retinal pigment epithelial changes. |

### Differential Diagnosis

It is essential to differentiate PVD from other conditions that may present with similar symptoms:

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| **[[Retinal detachment]]** | Visual field defect ("curtain"), reduced VA if macula involved; taut retinal membrane on B-scan; subretinal fluid on OCT |
| **[[Vitreous hemorrhage]]** (without PVD) | May occur from [[proliferative diabetic retinopathy]], [[retinal vein occlusion]], or other vascular causes without PVD |
| **[[Posterior vitreous schisis]]** | Splitting of the posterior vitreous cortex rather than true separation from the ILM; may mimic PVD on B-scan; OCT can differentiate |
| **[[Asteroid hyalosis]]** | Chronic, usually asymptomatic; calcium-lipid deposits in vitreous; characteristic appearance on slit-lamp and B-scan |
| **[[Vitritis]] / [[Intermediate uveitis]]** | Inflammatory cells in vitreous; associated with pain, redness, photophobia; underlying systemic or infectious etiology |
| **[[Ocular migraine]]** | Photopsia without floaters; visual aura typically bilateral, scintillating, and lasting 15–30 minutes; normal fundus examination |
| **[[Retinal tear]] without PVD** | Rare; most retinal tears are associated with PVD |

## Complications

The primary clinical concern with PVD is the risk of associated retinal complications. Approximately **10–15%** of patients presenting with acute symptomatic PVD will have a [[retinal tear]] at initial examination, and an additional **2–5%** may develop a tear in the subsequent weeks.

### Retinal Tears and Retinal Detachment

- **[[Horseshoe (flap) tears]]**: The most clinically significant type of retinal break associated with PVD. They occur when persistent vitreoretinal traction avulses a flap of retina. The flap remains attached anteriorly, and ongoing traction can lead to [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment (RRD)]].
- **[[Operculated holes]]**: Occur when the vitreous completely avulses a piece of retina (the operculum), which floats freely in the vitreous. These carry a lower risk of retinal detachment because traction is relieved.
- **[[Giant retinal tears]]**: Tears extending ≥90° of the circumference; rare but devastating complication.
- **Risk of RRD**: Approximately 33–46% of untreated symptomatic horseshoe tears progress to retinal detachment.

### Vitreous Hemorrhage

- Occurs in approximately **3–7%** of acute PVD cases.
- Results from tearing of a [[retinal blood vessel]] (typically a bridging vessel at the site of a retinal tear) during vitreous separation.
- The presence of vitreous hemorrhage in the setting of acute PVD significantly increases the probability of an underlying retinal tear (reported in **50–70%** of such cases).

### Vitreomacular Traction (VMT) and Macular Hole

- **[[Vitreomacular traction syndrome]]**: Occurs when the vitreous separates peripherally but remains focally adherent to the [[fovea]] or [[perifoveal]] area, exerting anteroposterior and/or tangential traction. This can cause macular edema, distortion, and reduced visual acuity.
- **[[Macular hole]]**: Full-thickness macular holes (FTMH) can develop as a consequence of persistent vitreomacular traction. The Gass classification describes the stages of macular hole formation in relation to PVD progression.

### Epiretinal Membrane

- **[[Epiretinal membrane (ERM)]]** formation may occur following PVD due to proliferation of residual [[hyalocytes]], [[glial cells]], and [[retinal pigment epithelial (RPE)]] cells on the retinal surface.

## Classification

PVD can be classified based on completeness and clinical significance:

| **Classification** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Complete PVD** | Full separation of the posterior vitreous cortex from the ILM, from the macula to the optic disc and periphery (up to the vitreous base). Weiss ring present. |
| **Incomplete (partial) PVD** | Vitreous has separated in some areas but remains attached at one or more sites (e.g., fovea, optic disc, peripheral retina). Higher risk of traction-related complications. |
| **Anomalous PVD** | A concept proposed by J. Sebag describing PVD occurring in the setting of advanced vitreous liquefaction but with abnormally strong vitreoretinal adhesion, leading to pathological traction and complications (tears, VMT, macular holes, vitreous hemorrhage). |

## Management

### General Principles

Management of PVD is guided by the presence or absence of complications at initial presentation and the patient's risk profile.

### Uncomplicated PVD (No Retinal Tears, No Hemorrhage)

- **Patient education**: Reassure the patient that PVD is a common, usually benign condition. Explain that floaters typically become less noticeable over weeks to months as they settle inferiorly and the brain adapts ([[neural adaptation]]).
- **Symptom monitoring instructions**: Provide clear, written instructions to return **immediately** if they experience:
  - A sudden increase in floaters (especially a "shower" of new floaters)
  - New or increased flashing lights
  - A shadow, curtain, or veil in the peripheral vision
  - Any decrease in central vision
- **Follow-up schedule**:

| **Timing** | **Purpose** |
|---|---|
| **Initial visit** | Comprehensive dilated examination with scleral indentation |
| **4–6 weeks** | Repeat dilated examination to rule out delayed retinal tears (highest risk period) |
| **3 months (optional)** | Additional follow-up if symptoms persist or change |
| **Annual** | Routine dilated examination thereafter |

### Complicated PVD

#### Retinal Tears Without Detachment

- **Urgent referral** to a [[vitreoretinal surgeon]] or management by the treating practitioner if trained in [[laser retinopexy]].
- **[[Laser photocoagulation (retinopexy)]]**: Barrage laser (typically [[argon laser]] or [[532 nm green laser]]) applied around the tear to create a chorioretinal adhesion, sealing the tear and preventing fluid from entering the subretinal space. This is the standard of care for acute symptomatic horseshoe tears.
- **[[Cryopexy]]**: An alternative to laser, using a [[cryoprobe]] applied transsclerally to freeze the retina around the tear, inducing a chorioretinal adhesion. Often used when media opacities preclude laser delivery or for anterior tears.
- **Follow-up**: Close monitoring at 1 week, 1 month, and 3 months post-treatment to ensure adequate seal and no progression to retinal detachment.

#### Retinal Detachment

- **Emergent referral** to a [[vitreoretinal surgeon]].
- Surgical options include:
  - **[[Pneumatic retinopexy]]**: Injection of an expansile gas bubble ([[SF6]] or [[C3F8]]) into the vitreous cavity combined with laser or cryopexy. Suitable for selected superior retinal detachments with a single break.
  - **[[Scleral buckle]]**: Placement of a silicone band or sponge on the external scleral surface to indent the wall of the eye, relieving vitreoretinal traction and supporting the retinal break.
  - **[[Pars plana vitrectomy (PPV)]]**: Surgical removal of the vitreous gel, relief of traction, drainage of subretinal fluid, endolaser retinopexy, and tamponade with gas or [[silicone oil]]. Increasingly the preferred approach for complex or recurrent detachments.

#### Vitreous Hemorrhage

- If the fundus view is obscured, **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** is mandatory to rule out retinal detachment.
- Mild hemorrhage: Observation with close follow-up; hemorrhage often clears spontaneously over weeks.
- Dense hemorrhage or associated retinal detachment: **[[Pars plana vitrectomy]]** for hemorrhage clearance and treatment of underlying pathology.

#### Vitreomacular Traction (VMT)

- **Observation**: Many cases of VMT resolve spontaneously with completion of PVD.
- **[[Ocriplasmin]] (Jetrea®)**: A recombinant truncated form of human [[plasmin]] (a serine protease) approved for intravitreal injection in the treatment of symptomatic VMT. Ocriplasmin enzymatically cleaves [[laminin]] and [[fibronectin]] at the vitreoretinal interface, promoting pharmacological vitreolysis. Efficacy for VMT resolution is approximately 26–37% in clinical trials (MIVI-TRUST studies). It is indicated for VMT with or without a small (≤400 μm) full-thickness macular hole.
- **[[Pars plana vitrectomy]]**: Definitive surgical treatment for persistent, symptomatic VMT unresponsive to observation or pharmacological vitreolysis. Also indicated for full-thickness macular holes.

#### Macular Hole

- **[[Pars plana vitrectomy]]** with [[ILM peeling]] and gas tamponade is the standard surgical treatment for full-thickness macular holes, with anatomical closure rates exceeding 90%.

## Pharmacological Vitreolysis

The concept of **pharmacological vitreolysis**—using enzymatic agents to induce or facilitate PVD—has been an area of active research. The goal is to achieve a complete, atraumatic PVD, thereby preventing traction-related complications.

| **Agent** | **Mechanism** | **Status** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Ocriplasmin]] (Jetrea®)** | Truncated plasmin; cleaves laminin and fibronectin | FDA-approved (2012) for symptomatic VMT ± small macular hole |
| **[[Autologous plasmin enzyme (APE)]]** | Full-length plasmin derived from patient's blood; cleaves fibronectin and laminin | Investigational; used as surgical adjunct |
| **[[Microplasmin]]** | Recombinant truncated plasmin | Precursor to ocriplasmin; studied in clinical trials |
| **[[Tissue plasminogen activator (tPA)]]** | Converts plasminogen to plasmin | Used intravitreally as adjunct in vitrectomy; investigational for PVD induction |
| **[[Hyaluronidase]] (Vitrase®)** | Degrades hyaluronic acid, promoting vitreous liquefaction | Studied in clinical trials; not FDA-approved for this indication |
| **[[Chondroitinase ABC]]** | Degrades chondroitin sulfate at the vitreoretinal interface | Preclinical/early clinical investigation |
| **[[RGD peptides]]** | Competitive inhibitors of integrin-mediated vitreoretinal adhesion | Preclinical investigation |

## Special Populations and Considerations

### PVD in Myopia

Patients with [[axial myopia]] (axial length >26 mm or refractive error ≥ −6.00 D) experience PVD at a younger age and have a significantly higher risk of retinal tears and [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]]. The elongated globe produces thinner retina, more extensive [[lattice degeneration]], and stronger vitreoretinal adhesions at pathological sites. These patients warrant particularly vigilant examination and follow-up.

### PVD Following Cataract Surgery

[[Cataract surgery]] ([[phacoemulsification]]) accelerates PVD development, likely due to alterations in vitreous biochemistry (increased oxygen exposure, inflammatory mediators, and disruption of the [[lens]]-vitreous interface). Studies report PVD incidence of 40–60% within 1–2 years following uncomplicated cataract surgery. Post-surgical PVD carries a similar risk of retinal tears as spontaneous PVD and requires the same vigilance.

### PVD in Diabetes

In patients with [[diabetes mellitus]], the vitreous undergoes paradoxical changes: accelerated liquefaction centrally but abnormally strong vitreoretinal adhesion due to AGE-mediated collagen cross-linking. This creates conditions for anomalous PVD, which can exacerbate [[diabetic retinopathy]] by inducing vitreous hemorrhage from [[neovascularization]] or tractional complications.

### Pediatric and Young Adult PVD

PVD in young patients is rare and should raise suspicion for underlying connective tissue disorders ([[Stickler syndrome]], [[Marfan syndrome]], [[Ehlers-Danlos syndrome]]), high myopia, prior ocular trauma, or prior intraocular surgery. These patients require comprehensive systemic evaluation.

## Prognosis

The prognosis for uncomplicated PVD is excellent. Floaters typically become less symptomatic over 3–6 months due to gravitational settling and neural adaptation. Photopsia usually resolves within weeks to months as vitreoretinal traction ceases.

For complicated PVD:

| **Complication** | **Prognosis with Treatment** |
|---|---|
| **Retinal tear (treated with laser/cryo)** | Excellent; >95% success in preventing retinal detachment |
| **Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment** | Good with timely surgery; anatomical success 85–95% with single procedure; visual prognosis depends on macular involvement and duration |
| **Vitreous hemorrhage (without RD)** | Good; most clear spontaneously; vitrectomy if persistent |
| **Vitreomacular traction** | Variable; many resolve spontaneously; ocriplasmin or vitrectomy effective for persistent cases |
| **Full-thickness macular hole** | Good with vitrectomy; >90% anatomical closure; visual recovery depends on hole size and duration |

## Patient Counseling and Education

Effective patient communication is a cornerstone of PVD management. Key counseling points include:

1. **Nature of the condition**: PVD is a normal aging process of the eye, not a disease in itself.
2. **Floaters**: Will likely persist but become less noticeable over time. Avoid fixating on them.
3. **Warning signs**: Emphasize the critical importance of seeking **immediate** evaluation for sudden increase in floaters, new flashes, visual field shadow/curtain, or vision loss.
4. **Activity**: No specific activity restrictions are necessary for uncomplicated PVD.
5. **Fellow eye**: Inform the patient that PVD is likely to occur in the other eye in the future, and the same warning signs apply.
6. **Follow-up compliance**: Stress the importance of attending scheduled follow-up appointments, even if symptoms improve.

## Emerging Research and Future Directions

- **Advanced vitreous imaging**: [[Swept-source OCT]] and enhanced depth imaging techniques are improving visualization of the vitreoretinal interface and early detection of anomalous PVD.
- **Artificial intelligence (AI)**: Machine learning algorithms are being developed to automatically detect PVD, vitreoretinal traction, and retinal tears on OCT and widefield imaging, potentially improving screening efficiency.
- **Novel pharmacological agents**: Research continues into safer and more effective enzymatic agents for pharmacological vitreolysis, including next-generation plasmin derivatives and targeted molecular therapies.
- **Vitreous biomarkers**: Identification of biochemical markers in the vitreous (e.g., MMP levels, collagen degradation products) that may predict PVD onset or complication risk.
- **Floater treatment**: **[[YAG vitreolysis]]** (Nd:YAG laser applied to symptomatic vitreous opacities) is an emerging but controversial treatment for persistent, visually significant floaters following PVD. Limited evidence supports its efficacy and safety, and it is not yet considered standard of care. **[[Pars plana vitrectomy]]** for floaters (floaterectomy) is effective but carries surgical risks and is reserved for severely symptomatic cases.

---

## Summary

Posterior vitreous detachment is a ubiquitous age-related condition that represents one of the most common acute presentations in eye care practice. While the majority of cases are benign and self-limiting, the potential for sight-threatening complications—particularly [[retinal tears]] and [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]]—mandates a systematic, thorough clinical approach. Every patient presenting with acute-onset floaters and/or photopsia should receive a comprehensive [[dilated fundus examination]] with [[scleral indentation]], appropriate imaging when indicated, clear patient education regarding warning signs, and timely follow-up. Advances in imaging technology, pharmacological vitreolysis, and surgical techniques continue to refine our ability to diagnose, monitor, and manage PVD and its complications.
