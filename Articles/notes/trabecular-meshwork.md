

# Trabecular Meshwork

---

The **trabecular meshwork** (TM) is a highly specialized, porous, sieve-like tissue located in the [[iridocorneal angle]] of the [[anterior chamber]] of the eye. It serves as the principal site of [[aqueous humor]] outflow and is the critical determinant of [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) homeostasis. The trabecular meshwork is situated at the junction of the [[cornea]], [[sclera]], and [[iris]] root, spanning the circumference of the [[anterior chamber angle]]. Dysfunction, degeneration, or obstruction of this tissue is the primary pathological mechanism underlying the elevated IOP observed in most forms of [[glaucoma]], the leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide. A thorough understanding of the trabecular meshwork—its anatomy, embryology, physiology, pathophysiology, and clinical significance—is indispensable for every optometrist and ophthalmologist engaged in the diagnosis and management of glaucomatous disease and related anterior segment disorders.

---

## Anatomy and Histology

### Gross Anatomy

The trabecular meshwork is a triangular, band-like structure in cross-section, with its apex located anteriorly at [[Schwalbe's line]] (the termination of [[Descemet's membrane]]) and its base extending posteriorly toward the [[scleral spur]] and the root of the [[iris]]. It bridges the internal aspect of the [[corneoscleral junction]] and forms the inner wall of [[Schlemm's canal]]. The tissue is approximately 350–500 µm wide anteriorly and narrows posteriorly. It is visible on [[gonioscopy]] as a pigmented or non-pigmented band between Schwalbe's line and the scleral spur.

### Histological Layers

The trabecular meshwork is classically divided into three distinct histological regions, each with unique structural and functional characteristics:

| **Layer** | **Location** | **Structure** | **Function** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Uveal meshwork** | Innermost layer, adjacent to the [[anterior chamber]] | Cord-like trabecular beams (1–3 layers) with large intertrabecular spaces (25–75 µm) | Minimal resistance to aqueous outflow; acts as a coarse filter |
| **Corneoscleral meshwork** | Middle layer | Flattened, perforated sheets (8–15 layers) with smaller elliptical openings (5–50 µm) | Moderate resistance; progressive filtration of aqueous humor |
| **Juxtacanalicular tissue (JCT) / Cribriform layer** | Outermost layer, directly adjacent to the inner wall of [[Schlemm's canal]] | Loose connective tissue with embedded cells, [[extracellular matrix]] (ECM), and no distinct beam structure; 2–20 µm thick | **Primary site of aqueous outflow resistance**; critical in IOP regulation |

#### Trabecular Beams

Each trabecular beam consists of a central core of [[collagen]] (types I, III, IV, V, and VI) and [[elastic fibers]], surrounded by a [[basement membrane]] and a monolayer of [[trabecular meshwork endothelial cells]] (TM cells). These cells are metabolically active, phagocytic, and contractile, playing a vital role in maintaining tissue homeostasis, clearing debris, and modulating outflow resistance.

#### Juxtacanalicular Tissue (JCT)

The JCT is the most clinically significant region of the trabecular meshwork. It is composed of a sparse population of fibroblast-like cells embedded in a loose [[extracellular matrix]] rich in [[glycosaminoglycans]] (GAGs), [[fibronectin]], [[laminin]], [[proteoglycans]], and various collagens. The JCT interfaces directly with the inner wall [[endothelium]] of [[Schlemm's canal]], and together these structures generate the majority (approximately 50–75%) of the total resistance to aqueous humor outflow through the [[conventional outflow pathway]].

### Schlemm's Canal

[[Schlemm's canal]] is a circumferential, endothelium-lined channel located within the [[scleral sulcus]], immediately external to the JCT. The inner wall endothelium of Schlemm's canal is unique in the body: it possesses giant vacuoles and transcellular pores that facilitate the bulk flow of aqueous humor from the JCT into the canal lumen. From Schlemm's canal, aqueous humor drains via 25–35 [[collector channels]] into the [[aqueous veins]] (veins of Ascher) and ultimately into the [[episcleral venous plexus]] and systemic venous circulation.

## Embryology and Development

The trabecular meshwork is derived from [[neural crest]] cells and, to a lesser extent, [[mesoderm]]. During embryonic development, the anterior chamber angle structures differentiate from a mass of mesenchymal tissue. The process of angle maturation involves progressive rarefaction and cavitation of this tissue, a process that continues postnatally and is largely complete by 8–12 months of age.

Developmental arrest or incomplete maturation of the trabecular meshwork is the underlying mechanism in [[primary congenital glaucoma]] (also known as [[infantile glaucoma]] or [[buphthalmos]]). In these cases, a membrane of immature tissue (the "Barkan membrane") may overlie the trabecular meshwork, impeding aqueous outflow and leading to elevated IOP in infancy. Conditions such as [[Axenfeld-Rieger syndrome]], [[Peters anomaly]], and [[aniridia]] also involve maldevelopment of the trabecular meshwork and iridocorneal angle, collectively classified as [[anterior segment dysgenesis]].

## Physiology of Aqueous Humor Outflow

### Conventional (Trabecular) Outflow Pathway

The trabecular meshwork is the central structure of the **conventional outflow pathway**, which accounts for approximately 70–90% of total aqueous humor drainage in the human eye. The pathway proceeds as follows:

1. [[Aqueous humor]] is produced by the [[ciliary body]] epithelium.
2. It flows from the [[posterior chamber]], through the [[pupil]], into the [[anterior chamber]].
3. Aqueous humor percolates through the uveal meshwork, corneoscleral meshwork, and JCT.
4. It crosses the inner wall endothelium of [[Schlemm's canal]] via transcellular pores and giant vacuoles.
5. It enters Schlemm's canal, then drains through [[collector channels]] into [[aqueous veins]] and the [[episcleral venous system]].

### Regulation of Outflow Resistance

The trabecular meshwork is not a passive filter. It actively regulates outflow resistance through several mechanisms:

- **Extracellular matrix (ECM) turnover:** TM cells continuously synthesize and degrade ECM components (e.g., [[fibronectin]], [[collagen]], [[hyaluronic acid]], [[proteoglycans]]). The balance between [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs) and [[tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases]] (TIMPs) determines ECM composition and, consequently, outflow resistance.
- **Cellular contractility:** TM cells and cells of the [[ciliary muscle]] are contractile. Contraction of the [[longitudinal fibers of the ciliary muscle]] pulls on the scleral spur, opening the trabecular meshwork and increasing outflow facility. This is the mechanism of action of [[parasympathomimetic]] agents such as [[pilocarpine]].
- **Phagocytosis:** TM cells actively phagocytose debris, pigment granules, and cellular fragments, maintaining the patency of the outflow channels.
- **Cytoskeletal dynamics:** The [[actin]] cytoskeleton of TM cells modulates cell shape, stiffness, and intercellular junctions, influencing tissue permeability. [[Rho kinase]] (ROCK) signaling is a key regulator of this process and is the target of [[ROCK inhibitor]] drugs such as [[netarsudil]].

### Uveoscleral (Unconventional) Outflow Pathway

The remaining 10–30% of aqueous humor exits via the [[uveoscleral outflow pathway]], which bypasses the trabecular meshwork entirely. Aqueous humor passes through the [[ciliary muscle]] interstitial spaces, into the [[suprachoroidal space]], and is absorbed by the [[choroidal vasculature]] or exits through the [[sclera]]. [[Prostaglandin analogs]] such as [[latanoprost]] primarily enhance uveoscleral outflow, though some evidence suggests they may also affect the trabecular pathway.

## Pathophysiology

### Age-Related Changes

The trabecular meshwork undergoes significant age-related degenerative changes that contribute to the increased prevalence of [[primary open-angle glaucoma]] (POAG) in older adults:

- **Loss of TM cells:** There is a progressive decline in TM cellularity with age, estimated at approximately 0.58% per year. By the eighth decade, the TM may have lost up to 50% of its cells compared to younger eyes.
- **Thickening and cross-linking of trabecular beams:** Accumulation of [[basement membrane]] material, increased collagen cross-linking, and deposition of [[sheath-derived plaques]] reduce tissue porosity.
- **Increased ECM deposition:** Accumulation of [[fibronectin]], [[collagen type IV]], and [[glycosaminoglycans]] in the JCT increases outflow resistance.
- **Decreased phagocytic activity:** Reduced ability to clear debris leads to obstruction of outflow channels.
- **Increased tissue stiffness:** Age-related stiffening of the TM, measurable by [[atomic force microscopy]], correlates with increased outflow resistance.

### Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma (POAG)

[[Primary open-angle glaucoma]] is the most common form of glaucoma and is characterized by an open, normal-appearing iridocorneal angle on [[gonioscopy]], yet with pathologically elevated outflow resistance localized to the trabecular meshwork—specifically the JCT and inner wall of Schlemm's canal. Key pathological findings in POAG include:

- **Excessive ECM accumulation in the JCT**, including increased deposition of fibrillar material, [[cochlin]], and cross-linked [[glycoproteins]].
- **Reduced TM cellularity** beyond that expected for age.
- **Collapse or narrowing of Schlemm's canal**, reducing the surface area available for aqueous drainage.
- **Increased expression of [[transforming growth factor-beta]] (TGF-β)** in the aqueous humor, which stimulates ECM production and fibrosis in the TM.
- **Oxidative stress and mitochondrial dysfunction** in TM cells, leading to cellular senescence and apoptosis.
- **Altered [[Wnt signaling]], [[bone morphogenetic protein]] (BMP) signaling, and [[Rho/ROCK pathway]] activity**, all of which contribute to increased tissue stiffness and outflow resistance.

### Pigmentary Dispersion and Pseudoexfoliation

In [[pigment dispersion syndrome]] and [[pigmentary glaucoma]], liberated [[melanin]] granules from the [[iris pigment epithelium]] accumulate within the trabecular meshwork, physically obstructing the intertrabecular spaces and inducing a secondary inflammatory and degenerative response in TM cells.

In [[pseudoexfoliation syndrome]] (PXF) and [[pseudoexfoliative glaucoma]], abnormal fibrillar [[pseudoexfoliative material]] is deposited throughout the anterior segment, including within the trabecular meshwork and JCT. This material obstructs outflow and is associated with markedly elevated IOP and a more aggressive glaucoma phenotype. [[Lysyl oxidase-like 1]] (LOXL1) gene polymorphisms are strongly associated with PXF.

### Steroid-Induced Glaucoma

Chronic use of [[corticosteroids]]—whether topical, periocular, intravitreal, or systemic—can induce increased IOP by altering the trabecular meshwork. Steroids increase ECM deposition (particularly [[myocilin]] and [[fibronectin]]), inhibit phagocytosis, reduce MMP activity, and promote cytoskeletal rearrangement in TM cells, all of which increase outflow resistance. This condition is known as [[steroid-induced glaucoma]] or [[steroid response]].

### Trabecular Meshwork in Angle-Closure Glaucoma

In [[angle-closure glaucoma]], the trabecular meshwork itself may be structurally normal, but it is physically obstructed by apposition of the peripheral [[iris]] (appositional closure) or by the formation of [[peripheral anterior synechiae]] (PAS), which permanently adhere the iris to the trabecular meshwork. Chronic angle closure can lead to secondary degenerative changes in the TM, even after the angle is reopened.

### Inflammatory and Traumatic Damage

In [[uveitic glaucoma]], inflammatory cells, [[fibrin]], and proteinaceous debris can obstruct the trabecular meshwork. Chronic inflammation may lead to [[trabeculitis]]—direct inflammation of the TM tissue—and subsequent scarring. Blunt [[ocular trauma]] can cause [[angle recession]], in which the face of the [[ciliary body]] is torn, disrupting the trabecular meshwork and predisposing to late-onset [[traumatic glaucoma]].

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

The trabecular meshwork itself does not produce symptoms directly perceptible to the patient. Dysfunction of the trabecular meshwork manifests clinically through the consequences of elevated [[intraocular pressure]]:

- **Asymptomatic IOP elevation:** The most common presentation; detected only through routine [[tonometry]].
- **Gradual, painless loss of [[peripheral vision]]:** Characteristic of [[primary open-angle glaucoma]].
- **Acute symptoms (in angle-closure or severe IOP spikes):** [[Ocular pain]], [[headache]], [[nausea]], [[vomiting]], [[halos around lights]], and [[blurred vision]].
- **Optic nerve damage:** Progressive [[optic disc cupping]], [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL) thinning, and [[visual field]] defects.

Because TM dysfunction is typically insidious and painless, regular comprehensive eye examinations with [[gonioscopy]], [[tonometry]], [[optic nerve head]] evaluation, and [[visual field testing]] are essential for early detection.

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Gonioscopy

[[Gonioscopy]] is the gold standard for direct visualization of the trabecular meshwork and the iridocorneal angle. Using a goniolens (e.g., [[Goldmann three-mirror lens]], [[Zeiss four-mirror lens]], [[Posner lens]], [[Sussman lens]]), the clinician can assess:

- **Angle width and configuration** (using the [[Shaffer grading system]], [[Spaeth grading system]], or [[Scheie classification]])
- **Degree of TM pigmentation** (graded 0–4+; increased pigmentation may indicate [[pigment dispersion syndrome]], [[pseudoexfoliation]], or chronic [[uveitis]])
- **Presence of [[peripheral anterior synechiae]] (PAS)**
- **Presence of abnormal vessels ([[neovascularization of the angle]])**
- **Presence of [[pseudoexfoliative material]], [[blood]], or [[inflammatory debris]]**
- **Identification of [[Sampaolesi's line]]** (a line of pigment anterior to Schwalbe's line, associated with PXF)
- **Angle recession** (widened [[ciliary body band]])

### Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography (AS-OCT)

[[Anterior segment OCT]] provides high-resolution, cross-sectional imaging of the iridocorneal angle and can quantify angle width, [[angle opening distance]] (AOD), [[trabecular-iris space area]] (TISA), and [[scleral spur angle]]. While it does not resolve individual TM layers, it is valuable for screening and monitoring angle configuration, particularly in [[angle-closure]] suspects.

### Ultrasound Biomicroscopy (UBM)

[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (typically 35–50 MHz) provides detailed imaging of the angle structures, including the trabecular meshwork, [[ciliary body]], [[scleral spur]], and [[Schlemm's canal]]. UBM is particularly useful for evaluating the angle in eyes with opaque corneas or for detecting [[plateau iris configuration]], [[ciliary body tumors]], or [[cyclodialysis clefts]].

### Tonometry

[[Goldmann applanation tonometry]] (GAT) remains the clinical standard for measuring IOP. Elevated IOP is the hallmark of trabecular meshwork dysfunction. Other tonometric methods include [[non-contact tonometry]] (NCT), [[iCare rebound tonometry]], [[Tono-Pen]], [[dynamic contour tonometry]] (DCT), and [[ocular response analyzer]] (ORA), which also measures [[corneal hysteresis]]—a biomechanical property that may reflect aspects of scleral and TM tissue compliance.

### Outflow Facility Measurement

[[Tonography]] (using a [[Schiøtz tonometer]] or electronic tonographer) can estimate the facility of aqueous outflow (C value), providing a functional assessment of trabecular meshwork function. Normal outflow facility is approximately 0.22–0.30 µL/min/mmHg. Reduced outflow facility is a hallmark of TM dysfunction in POAG. Although less commonly performed in routine clinical practice today, tonography remains a valuable research tool.

### Optic Nerve and Visual Field Assessment

While not directly imaging the TM, assessment of the [[optic nerve head]] (via [[fundoscopy]], [[OCT]] of the [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] and [[ganglion cell complex]]) and [[visual field testing]] (e.g., [[Humphrey visual field]], [[Octopus perimetry]]) are essential for determining the clinical consequences of TM dysfunction—namely, [[glaucomatous optic neuropathy]].

## Management and Treatment

The management of trabecular meshwork dysfunction is directed at lowering IOP to prevent or slow [[glaucomatous optic neuropathy]]. Therapeutic strategies can be broadly categorized into pharmacological, laser, and surgical approaches.

### Pharmacological Interventions

Most topical [[antiglaucoma medications]] exert their IOP-lowering effect by either reducing aqueous humor production or enhancing outflow through the trabecular or uveoscleral pathways.

| **Drug Class** | **Examples** | **Mechanism Related to TM** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Prostaglandin analogs]] | [[Latanoprost]], [[travoprost]], [[bimatoprost]], [[tafluprost]] | Primarily enhance [[uveoscleral outflow]]; some evidence of increased trabecular outflow via ECM remodeling and MMP upregulation |
| [[Beta-adrenergic antagonists]] | [[Timolol]], [[betaxolol]], [[levobunolol]] | Reduce [[aqueous humor]] production by the [[ciliary body]]; no direct TM effect |
| [[Alpha-adrenergic agonists]] | [[Brimonidine]], [[apraclonidine]] | Reduce aqueous production; may enhance uveoscleral outflow |
| [[Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]] | [[Dorzolamide]], [[brinzolamide]], [[acetazolamide]] (oral) | Reduce aqueous production; no direct TM effect |
| [[Parasympathomimetics]] (Miotics) | [[Pilocarpine]], [[echothiophate]] | Contract [[ciliary muscle]], pulling on [[scleral spur]], mechanically opening the trabecular meshwork and increasing conventional outflow facility |
| [[Rho kinase (ROCK) inhibitors]] | [[Netarsudil]], [[ripasudil]] | **Directly target the TM**: relax TM and JCT cells, reduce ECM deposition, decrease TM cell stiffness, increase outflow through the conventional pathway, and reduce [[episcleral venous pressure]] |
| [[Nitric oxide (NO)-donating agents]] | [[Latanoprostene bunod]] | Releases NO, which relaxes TM cells and increases trabecular outflow, in addition to prostaglandin-mediated uveoscleral outflow enhancement |
| Fixed combinations | [[Netarsudil/latanoprost]] (Rocklatan), [[timolol/dorzolamide]] (Cosopt), [[brimonidine/timolol]] (Combigan) | Dual mechanisms; some combinations directly and indirectly affect TM outflow |

**ROCK inhibitors** and **NO-donating agents** represent a paradigm shift in glaucoma pharmacotherapy because they are the first drug classes to directly target the trabecular meshwork outflow pathway, addressing the fundamental site of pathology in POAG.

### Laser Procedures Targeting the Trabecular Meshwork

#### Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty (SLT)

[[Selective laser trabeculoplasty]] (SLT) uses a frequency-doubled [[Nd:YAG laser]] (532 nm) with short pulse durations (3 nanoseconds) to selectively target pigmented TM cells without causing thermal or structural damage to the surrounding tissue. SLT stimulates a biological response—including cytokine release, macrophage recruitment, ECM remodeling, and increased MMP activity—that enhances trabecular outflow facility. SLT is effective as a first-line treatment or adjunct to medical therapy, is repeatable, and has been validated by the landmark [[LiGHT trial]] as a primary treatment for open-angle glaucoma.

#### Argon Laser Trabeculoplasty (ALT)

[[Argon laser trabeculoplasty]] (ALT) applies thermal laser energy to the TM, causing localized coagulative damage and contraction of the trabecular beams. This mechanically opens adjacent intertrabecular spaces and stimulates biological remodeling. ALT is effective but causes more structural damage than SLT and is generally not repeatable. It has been largely supplanted by SLT in contemporary practice.

#### Micropulse Laser Trabeculoplasty (MLT)

[[Micropulse laser trabeculoplasty]] delivers sub-threshold, repetitive short pulses of laser energy (typically 577 nm or 532 nm) to the TM, aiming to stimulate a biological response with even less tissue damage than SLT. Early evidence suggests comparable efficacy, though long-term data are still emerging.

### Minimally Invasive Glaucoma Surgery (MIGS)

The advent of [[minimally invasive glaucoma surgery]] (MIGS) has revolutionized the surgical management of glaucoma by providing procedures that specifically target the trabecular meshwork and Schlemm's canal with minimal tissue disruption and favorable safety profiles.

| **MIGS Procedure** | **Mechanism** | **Key Features** |
|---|---|---|
| [[iStent]] / [[iStent inject W]] | Titanium micro-bypass stent inserted through the TM into [[Schlemm's canal]], creating a direct conduit for aqueous outflow | Ab interno; often combined with [[cataract surgery]]; FDA-approved |
| [[Hydrus Microstent]] | Nitinol scaffold placed within Schlemm's canal, dilating approximately 90° of the canal and scaffolding the TM | Ab interno; dilates Schlemm's canal; shown to reduce medication burden |
| [[Kahook Dual Blade]] (KDB) [[Goniotomy]] | Excises a strip of TM tissue (goniotomy), creating a direct opening into Schlemm's canal | Ab interno; removes diseased TM; can be combined with cataract surgery |
| [[Trabectome]] | Electrosurgical ablation of TM and inner wall of Schlemm's canal | Ab interno; removes TM tissue; established long-term data |
| [[OMNI Surgical System]] | Combines [[viscodilation]] of Schlemm's canal (canaloplasty) with [[trabeculotomy]] (360°) | Ab interno; addresses both TM and distal outflow resistance |
| [[Goniotomy]] (traditional) | Incision through the TM to open Schlemm's canal | Historically used in [[congenital glaucoma]]; now adapted for adult POAG |
| [[Ab interno canaloplasty]] (ABiC) | Viscodilation of Schlemm's canal and collector channels without removing TM tissue | Restores outflow without tissue destruction |
| [[GATT]] ([[Gonioscopy-assisted transluminal trabeculotomy]]) | 360° trabeculotomy using a suture or microcatheter passed through Schlemm's canal | Ab interno; effective in various glaucoma types; cost-effective |

MIGS procedures are generally indicated for mild-to-moderate [[open-angle glaucoma]] and are frequently performed in conjunction with [[phacoemulsification]] cataract surgery. They offer the advantage of targeting the precise anatomical site of outflow resistance—the trabecular meshwork and Schlemm's canal—while preserving the [[conjunctiva]] for potential future filtering surgery.

### Traditional Glaucoma Surgery

When TM-targeted therapies are insufficient, more invasive surgical options may be considered:

- **[[Trabeculectomy]]:** Creates a fistula from the anterior chamber to the subconjunctival space, bypassing the TM entirely. Remains the gold standard for advanced glaucoma.
- **[[Glaucoma drainage devices]]** (e.g., [[Ahmed valve]], [[Baerveldt implant]]): Shunt aqueous humor to an external reservoir, bypassing the TM.
- **[[Cyclodestructive procedures]]** (e.g., [[transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]], [[endoscopic cyclophotocoagulation]]): Reduce aqueous production by destroying [[ciliary body]] tissue.

These procedures do not directly target the TM but are employed when TM-directed interventions fail to achieve adequate IOP control.

## Emerging Research and Future Directions

### Stem Cell and Regenerative Therapies

Research into [[trabecular meshwork stem cells]] (TM stem cells), identified in the [[insert region]] (transition zone between the TM and corneal endothelium near Schwalbe's line), has opened the possibility of regenerative therapies. Transplantation of TM stem cells or induced pluripotent stem cell (iPSC)-derived TM cells has shown promise in animal models for repopulating the TM, restoring outflow facility, and lowering IOP. Clinical translation remains in early stages.

### Gene Therapy

[[Gene therapy]] targeting the TM is under investigation. Viral vectors (e.g., [[adeno-associated virus]], [[lentivirus]]) can be used to deliver therapeutic genes directly to TM cells, potentially correcting molecular defects (e.g., [[myocilin]] mutations in [[juvenile open-angle glaucoma]]) or modulating ECM turnover, [[TGF-β signaling]], or [[Rho/ROCK pathway]] activity.

### Biomarkers and Molecular Profiling

Advances in [[proteomics]], [[genomics]], and [[single-cell RNA sequencing]] are enabling detailed molecular profiling of the trabecular meshwork in health and disease. Identification of novel biomarkers (e.g., [[cochlin]], [[myocilin]], [[LOXL1]], [[ANGPTL7]]) may improve early diagnosis and enable personalized therapeutic strategies.

### Nanotechnology and Sustained Drug Delivery

Nanoparticle-based drug delivery systems and sustained-release implants targeting the trabecular meshwork are being developed to improve drug bioavailability, reduce dosing frequency, and enhance patient compliance. [[Bimatoprost sustained-release implant]] (Durysta) is an early example of intracameral sustained-release technology.

### Advanced Imaging

Emerging imaging modalities, including [[OCT angiography]] (OCTA) of the anterior segment, [[adaptive optics imaging]], and [[phase-sensitive OCT]], may enable in vivo visualization of Schlemm's canal, collector channels, and even individual TM structures, facilitating earlier and more precise diagnosis of TM dysfunction.
