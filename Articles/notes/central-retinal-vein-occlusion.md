# Central Retinal Vein Occlusion

---

**Central Retinal Vein Occlusion (CRVO)** is a significant sight-threatening [[retinal vascular disorder]] characterized by the obstruction of the [[central retinal vein]] at or posterior to the [[lamina cribrosa]] of the [[optic nerve]]. It represents the second most common retinal vascular disease after [[diabetic retinopathy]] and is a leading cause of unilateral, painless vision loss, particularly in older adults. CRVO results in impaired venous outflow from the [[retina]], leading to widespread retinal hemorrhages, venous dilation and tortuosity, [[macular edema]], and, in severe cases, retinal and [[optic disc]] ischemia with subsequent [[neovascularization]]. The condition is broadly classified into two principal subtypes—**non-ischemic (perfused) CRVO** and **ischemic (non-perfused) CRVO**—which differ substantially in prognosis, clinical features, and risk of devastating neovascular complications. Comprehensive understanding of CRVO is essential for all [[optometrists]] and [[ophthalmologists]] involved in the detection, monitoring, and co-management of retinal disease.

---

## Epidemiology

CRVO is estimated to affect approximately 2.5 million people worldwide. The pooled prevalence from population-based studies is approximately 0.1% to 0.5%, with incidence increasing markedly with age. The condition most commonly presents in individuals over the age of 50, though it can occur in younger patients, particularly those with underlying [[thrombophilia]], [[hyperviscosity syndromes]], or inflammatory conditions.

| Epidemiological Factor | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Global prevalence** | ~2.5 million affected individuals |
| **Age-standardized prevalence** | 0.1–0.5% of the general population |
| **Peak age of onset** | >65 years |
| **Sex predilection** | Slight male predominance in some studies; overall roughly equal |
| **Laterality** | Typically unilateral; bilateral CRVO occurs in ~5–10% of cases |
| **Conversion rate (non-ischemic to ischemic)** | ~15–34% within 3 years |

Risk factors for CRVO overlap significantly with those for systemic [[cardiovascular disease]], and the condition is frequently a marker for underlying vascular morbidity.

## Anatomy and Pathophysiology

### Relevant Vascular Anatomy

The [[central retinal vein]] (CRV) is the sole venous drainage pathway for the inner retinal layers. It courses alongside the [[central retinal artery]] (CRA) within the substance of the [[optic nerve]], exiting through the [[lamina cribrosa]] before draining into the [[superior ophthalmic vein]] or the [[cavernous sinus]]. At the level of the lamina cribrosa, the CRV and CRA share a common adventitial sheath within a confined, rigid bony-fibrous canal. This anatomical arrangement is of critical pathophysiological importance: any process that compresses, distorts, or narrows the venous lumen at this site can precipitate occlusion.

### Pathophysiological Mechanisms

The pathogenesis of CRVO is multifactorial and is best understood through the framework of [[Virchow's triad]], which describes three broad categories of factors predisposing to thrombosis:

1. **Hemodynamic changes (venous stasis):** Reduced blood flow velocity in the CRV may result from systemic [[hypotension]], increased [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) as seen in [[glaucoma]] or [[ocular hypertension]], or external compression of the vein. The shared adventitial sheath means that [[atherosclerotic]] changes in the adjacent CRA can mechanically compress the CRV, reducing its lumen and promoting stasis.

2. **Endothelial injury/dysfunction:** Damage to the venous endothelium—whether from chronic [[hypertension]], [[diabetes mellitus]], [[hyperlipidemia]], or inflammatory vasculitis—exposes subendothelial [[collagen]] and [[tissue factor]], triggering the [[coagulation cascade]] and platelet adhesion.

3. **Hypercoagulability:** Inherited or acquired thrombophilic states, including [[Factor V Leiden mutation]], [[prothrombin gene mutation]] (G20210A), [[antiphospholipid syndrome]], [[hyperhomocysteinemia]], [[protein C deficiency]], [[protein S deficiency]], and [[antithrombin III deficiency]], increase the propensity for thrombus formation within the CRV.

### Downstream Consequences of Venous Occlusion

Once the CRV is occluded, venous pressure rises dramatically throughout the retinal venous system. This leads to:

- **Retinal capillary distension and rupture**, producing the characteristic widespread, flame-shaped and dot-blot [[retinal hemorrhages]] in all four quadrants.
- **Increased [[hydrostatic pressure]]** in retinal capillaries, causing transudation of fluid into the retinal tissue and the development of [[macular edema]]—the most common cause of vision loss in CRVO.
- **Retinal ischemia** due to reduced perfusion pressure (the difference between arterial inflow pressure and elevated venous outflow pressure). In severe (ischemic) CRVO, extensive capillary non-perfusion leads to upregulation of [[vascular endothelial growth factor]] ([[VEGF]]) and other pro-angiogenic cytokines, which drive [[neovascularization]] of the [[iris]] ([[rubeosis iridis]]), [[anterior chamber]] angle ([[neovascular glaucoma]]), [[optic disc]] (NVD), and [[retina]] (NVE).
- **Breakdown of the inner [[blood-retinal barrier]]**, further exacerbating macular edema and retinal exudation.

The degree of capillary non-perfusion is the principal determinant of whether CRVO is classified as non-ischemic or ischemic, and this distinction has profound implications for visual prognosis and the risk of neovascular complications.

## Classification

CRVO is classified into two major subtypes based on the extent of retinal capillary non-perfusion as assessed by [[fluorescein angiography]] (FA):

| Feature | Non-Ischemic (Perfused) CRVO | Ischemic (Non-Perfused) CRVO |
|---|---|---|
| **Frequency** | ~75% of all CRVO cases | ~25% of all CRVO cases |
| **Visual acuity at presentation** | Often ≥20/200; may be mildly reduced | Usually <20/200; often ≤20/400 |
| **Relative afferent pupillary defect ([[RAPD]])** | Absent or mild | Present (≥0.9 log units) |
| **Retinal hemorrhages** | Mild to moderate; predominantly flame-shaped | Extensive, dense, involving all four quadrants; deep dot-blot and flame-shaped |
| **Cotton-wool spots** | Few or absent | Numerous |
| **Capillary non-perfusion on FA** | <10 disc areas | ≥10 disc areas (some authorities use ≥30 disc areas) |
| **Macular edema** | Common; primary cause of vision loss | Common, but ischemia also contributes to vision loss |
| **Risk of neovascularization** | Low (~2–5%) | High (~40–60% within 6–12 months) |
| **Prognosis** | Better; ~50% recover to ≥20/40 spontaneously | Poor; <20% achieve ≥20/200 without treatment |
| **Conversion** | ~15–34% convert to ischemic type over 3 years | N/A |

An intermediate or "indeterminate" category is sometimes recognized for cases that do not clearly fit either subtype at initial presentation and require close follow-up.

## Risk Factors

Identification and management of systemic risk factors is a critical component of the clinical evaluation of any patient presenting with CRVO.

### Systemic Risk Factors

| Risk Factor | Mechanism / Association |
|---|---|
| **[[Systemic hypertension]]** | Most common associated systemic condition (~64–75% of CRVO patients); promotes [[atherosclerosis]] of the CRA, compressing the CRV |
| **[[Diabetes mellitus]]** | Endothelial dysfunction, hypercoagulability, microvascular disease |
| **[[Hyperlipidemia]]** | Accelerates [[atherosclerosis]] |
| **[[Cardiovascular disease]]** | Shared vascular risk factors |
| **[[Cerebrovascular disease]]** | Shared vascular risk factors |
| **[[Smoking]]** | Endothelial damage, hypercoagulability |
| **[[Obesity]]** | Promotes metabolic syndrome and hypercoagulability |
| **[[Hyperviscosity syndromes]]** (e.g., [[polycythemia vera]], [[Waldenström macroglobulinemia]], [[multiple myeloma]]) | Increased blood viscosity promotes venous stasis |
| **[[Thrombophilia]]** (inherited or acquired) | Hypercoagulable state |
| **[[Oral contraceptive]] use** | Hypercoagulable state, especially in younger patients |
| **[[Dehydration]]** | Hemoconcentration and stasis |

### Ocular Risk Factors

| Risk Factor | Mechanism / Association |
|---|---|
| **[[Glaucoma]] / [[Ocular hypertension]]** | Elevated [[IOP]] compresses the CRV at the lamina cribrosa; present in ~25–40% of CRVO patients |
| **[[Optic disc drusen]]** | Mechanical compression of the CRV |
| **[[Papilledema]]** | Compression of the CRV |
| **Orbital or retrobulbar mass** | External compression |

In patients under 50 years of age presenting with CRVO, a thorough workup for [[thrombophilia]], [[autoimmune disease]] (e.g., [[systemic lupus erythematosus]], [[Behçet disease]], [[sarcoidosis]]), and [[hyperviscosity syndromes]] is strongly recommended.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

The hallmark symptom of CRVO is **sudden, painless, unilateral decrease in [[visual acuity]]**. The severity of vision loss at presentation varies widely depending on the subtype:

- **Non-ischemic CRVO:** Patients may report mild blurring of vision, metamorphopsia, or a relative [[scotoma]]. Visual acuity may range from 20/20 to 20/200.
- **Ischemic CRVO:** Patients typically present with more profound vision loss, often counting fingers or worse. A dense central or paracentral scotoma may be reported.

Additional symptoms may include:
- [[Photopsia]] (flashes of light) — less common
- Floaters — due to vitreous hemorrhage in advanced cases
- Visual field defects

Notably, CRVO is **painless** unless complicated by [[neovascular glaucoma]], which may cause significant ocular pain, redness, and headache due to markedly elevated IOP.

### Signs (Clinical Examination Findings)

A comprehensive [[dilated fundus examination]] is essential. The classic fundoscopic appearance of CRVO has been described as a "blood and thunder" fundus or "tomato ketchup" fundus in severe cases.

#### Anterior Segment Findings

- **[[Relative afferent pupillary defect]] (RAPD):** A key differentiating sign. A significant RAPD (≥0.9 log units) strongly suggests ischemic CRVO. Its absence favors the non-ischemic subtype.
- **[[Rubeosis iridis]] (neovascularization of the iris):** Seen in ischemic CRVO, typically developing 2–4 months after onset ("90-day glaucoma" or "100-day glaucoma"). Requires careful [[gonioscopy]] to assess for angle neovascularization.
- **Elevated [[IOP]]:** May be present at baseline (if [[glaucoma]] is a predisposing factor) or may develop secondary to [[neovascular glaucoma]].
- **Anterior chamber cells/flare:** May be present due to breakdown of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]].

#### Posterior Segment Findings

| Finding | Description |
|---|---|
| **Retinal hemorrhages** | Widespread in all four quadrants; flame-shaped (nerve fiber layer) and dot-blot (deeper retinal layers); more extensive and denser in ischemic CRVO |
| **Venous dilation and tortuosity** | Marked engorgement of all retinal veins |
| **[[Optic disc]] edema** | Swelling and hyperemia of the optic disc; may be prominent |
| **[[Cotton-wool spots]]** | Indicate focal retinal nerve fiber layer ischemia; more numerous in ischemic CRVO |
| **[[Macular edema]]** | Thickening of the macula due to fluid accumulation; the most common cause of reduced vision |
| **Hard exudates** | Lipid deposits from chronic leakage; may form a circinate pattern around the macula |
| **Retinal edema** | Diffuse retinal thickening |
| **Capillary non-perfusion** | Not directly visible on fundoscopy; assessed by FA or [[optical coherence tomography angiography]] ([[OCTA]]) |
| **Neovascularization** | NVD, NVE, or NVI in ischemic CRVO; may lead to [[vitreous hemorrhage]] or [[tractional retinal detachment]] |
| **Optociliary shunt vessels** | Collateral vessels on the optic disc; develop over weeks to months as the venous system attempts to bypass the obstruction |

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical Assessment

- **Best-corrected [[visual acuity]] (BCVA):** Baseline and serial measurements are essential for monitoring.
- **[[Pupil]] examination:** Assessment for RAPD using the swinging flashlight test.
- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]:** Anterior segment evaluation for rubeosis iridis, anterior chamber reaction, and lens status.
- **[[Gonioscopy]]:** Critical for detecting angle neovascularization, especially in ischemic CRVO.
- **[[Intraocular pressure]] measurement ([[Tonometry]]):** Baseline and follow-up IOP assessment.
- **[[Dilated fundus examination]]:** Using [[binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy]] (BIO) and/or slit-lamp [[fundoscopy]] with a [[78D]] or [[90D]] lens.

### Ancillary Testing

#### Optical Coherence Tomography ([[OCT]])

[[OCT]] is the gold standard for the detection, quantification, and monitoring of [[macular edema]] in CRVO. [[Spectral-domain OCT]] (SD-OCT) provides high-resolution cross-sectional images of the retina, allowing precise measurement of [[central macular thickness]] (CMT) and identification of:

- **Cystoid macular edema (CME):** Intraretinal cystic spaces, most prominent in the outer plexiform and inner nuclear layers.
- **Subretinal fluid (SRF):** Fluid accumulation beneath the [[neurosensory retina]].
- **Disruption of the [[ellipsoid zone]] (EZ) / [[external limiting membrane]] (ELM):** Indicators of photoreceptor damage and poorer visual prognosis.
- **Disorganization of retinal inner layers (DRIL):** A biomarker associated with worse visual outcomes.
- **Epiretinal membrane (ERM):** May develop secondary to chronic CRVO.

Serial OCT is indispensable for guiding treatment decisions, particularly the need for and response to anti-[[VEGF]] therapy.

#### Fluorescein Angiography ([[FA]])

[[Fluorescein angiography]] remains a critical tool for:

- **Determining the extent of capillary non-perfusion:** The key criterion for classifying CRVO as ischemic vs. non-ischemic. Ischemic CRVO is defined by ≥10 disc areas (and by some criteria ≥30 disc areas) of capillary non-perfusion.
- **Identifying macular ischemia:** Non-perfusion involving the [[foveal avascular zone]] (FAZ) portends a poor visual prognosis regardless of macular edema treatment.
- **Detecting neovascularization:** NVD, NVE, and NVI demonstrate characteristic hyperfluorescent leakage.
- **Assessing macular edema:** Petaloid pattern of leakage in CME; diffuse leakage in more severe cases.
- **Identifying collateral vessels:** Optociliary shunt vessels on the disc.

Typical FA findings in CRVO include delayed venous filling, widespread retinal hemorrhage blocking fluorescence, extensive capillary non-perfusion (in ischemic type), and diffuse leakage from damaged capillaries.

#### Optical Coherence Tomography Angiography ([[OCTA]])

[[OCTA]] is a non-invasive, dye-free imaging modality that provides depth-resolved visualization of retinal and choroidal vasculature. In CRVO, OCTA can:

- Delineate areas of capillary non-perfusion in the superficial and deep capillary plexuses.
- Assess FAZ enlargement and irregularity.
- Detect neovascularization.
- Monitor vascular changes over time without the need for dye injection.

While OCTA has limitations (smaller field of view compared to widefield FA, susceptibility to artifacts), it is increasingly used as a complementary tool in CRVO evaluation.

#### Widefield Imaging

**Ultra-widefield [[fundus photography]]** and **ultra-widefield FA** (e.g., using [[Optos]] devices) allow visualization of the peripheral retina, which is important for detecting peripheral non-perfusion and neovascularization that may be missed on standard imaging.

### Laboratory Workup

A targeted systemic workup is recommended, particularly in younger patients (<50 years), bilateral CRVO, or recurrent CRVO:

| Test Category | Specific Tests |
|---|---|
| **Cardiovascular risk assessment** | Blood pressure, fasting lipid panel, fasting blood glucose / [[HbA1c]], [[ECG]] |
| **Hematologic** | Complete blood count (CBC) with differential, [[erythrocyte sedimentation rate]] (ESR), [[C-reactive protein]] (CRP) |
| **Coagulation / Thrombophilia** | [[Prothrombin time]] (PT), [[activated partial thromboplastin time]] (aPTT), [[Factor V Leiden]], [[prothrombin gene mutation]], [[antiphospholipid antibodies]] (anticardiolipin, lupus anticoagulant, anti-β2 glycoprotein I), [[homocysteine]] levels, [[protein C]], [[protein S]], [[antithrombin III]] |
| **Autoimmune / Inflammatory** | [[ANA]], [[anti-dsDNA]], [[ANCA]], [[ACE level]] (if sarcoidosis suspected) |
| **Hyperviscosity** | Serum protein electrophoresis (SPEP), serum viscosity |
| **Thyroid function** | TSH (thyroid disease associated with vascular risk) |

Referral to an [[internist]] or [[hematologist]] may be warranted based on findings.

## Differential Diagnosis

Several conditions may mimic the fundoscopic appearance of CRVO and must be considered:

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| **[[Ocular ischemic syndrome]]** | Caused by [[carotid artery]] stenosis; hemorrhages are typically mid-peripheral and dot-blot; veins are dilated but not tortuous; low perfusion pressure; may have anterior segment ischemia |
| **[[Papilledema]]** | Bilateral disc edema; hemorrhages concentrated around the disc; associated with elevated [[intracranial pressure]] |
| **[[Diabetic retinopathy]]** | Usually bilateral; microaneurysms are a hallmark; hemorrhages may be asymmetric but typically not as acute in onset |
| **[[Hypertensive retinopathy]]** | Usually bilateral; arteriolar narrowing, AV nicking, flame hemorrhages; cotton-wool spots |
| **[[Papillophlebitis]]** | CRVO variant in young, healthy patients; mild disc edema and venous engorgement; generally excellent prognosis |
| **[[Radiation retinopathy]]** | History of radiation exposure; cotton-wool spots, hemorrhages, microaneurysms |
| **[[Hemi-retinal vein occlusion]]** | Hemorrhages confined to superior or inferior hemifield; considered a variant of CRVO by some authorities |
| **[[Branch retinal vein occlusion]] ([[BRVO]])** | Hemorrhages confined to the distribution of a single branch retinal vein |

## Natural History and Prognosis

### Non-Ischemic CRVO

- Approximately **50%** of patients with non-ischemic CRVO will experience spontaneous improvement in visual acuity to 20/40 or better.
- The primary cause of persistent vision loss is chronic [[macular edema]].
- Approximately **15–34%** of non-ischemic CRVO cases will convert to the ischemic subtype within 3 years, necessitating vigilant follow-up.
- Neovascular complications are uncommon (~2–5%) but possible.

### Ischemic CRVO

- Visual prognosis is significantly worse. Fewer than **20%** of patients achieve visual acuity of 20/200 or better without treatment.
- The risk of [[neovascularization of the iris]] (NVI) and/or [[neovascular glaucoma]] (NVG) is **40–60%** within 6–12 months.
- [[Vitreous hemorrhage]] and [[tractional retinal detachment]] may occur secondary to neovascularization.
- The term **"100-day glaucoma"** historically refers to the typical time frame for the development of NVG following ischemic CRVO, though this can occur earlier or later.

## Management and Treatment

The management of CRVO is multifaceted, addressing both the ocular complications and the underlying systemic risk factors.

### General Principles

1. **Identify and manage systemic risk factors:** Blood pressure control, glycemic control, lipid management, smoking cessation, and treatment of any identified thrombophilia or hyperviscosity syndrome.
2. **Treat vision-threatening complications:** Primarily macular edema and neovascularization.
3. **Monitor for conversion:** Non-ischemic CRVO patients require regular follow-up (every 1–2 months initially) to detect conversion to the ischemic subtype.

### Pharmacological Treatment

#### Anti-VEGF Therapy

Intravitreal anti-[[VEGF]] agents are the **first-line treatment** for macular edema secondary to CRVO. The upregulation of VEGF in CRVO drives vascular permeability and macular edema, and anti-VEGF therapy directly targets this pathway.

| Agent | Landmark Trial | Key Findings |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Ranibizumab]]** (Lucentis®) | **CRUISE** (Central Retinal Vein Occlusion Study: Evaluation of Efficacy and Safety) | Monthly intravitreal ranibizumab (0.3 mg or 0.5 mg) resulted in significant improvement in BCVA compared to sham at 6 months. Mean gain of ~15 letters (0.5 mg group). FDA-approved for CRVO-related macular edema. |
| **[[Aflibercept]]** (Eylea®) | **COPERNICUS** and **GALILEO** | Monthly intravitreal aflibercept (2 mg) demonstrated significant BCVA improvement vs. sham at 6 months. Mean gain of ~18 letters (COPERNICUS) and ~18 letters (GALILEO). FDA-approved for CRVO-related macular edema. |
| **[[Bevacizumab]]** (Avastin®) | Multiple smaller studies and real-world data | Used off-label; generally considered effective and more cost-effective, though not FDA-approved for this indication. |
| **[[Brolucizumab]]** (Beovu®) | **RAVEN** and other studies | Emerging data; approved for other indications; potential for extended dosing intervals. |
| **[[Faricimab]]** (Vabysmo®) | **COMINO** | Dual-pathway inhibitor targeting both VEGF-A and [[angiopoietin-2]] (Ang-2). FDA-approved for macular edema secondary to RVO. Potential for extended treatment intervals up to 16 weeks. |

**Treatment Protocol:** Anti-VEGF therapy is typically initiated with a loading phase of monthly injections (often 3–6 monthly injections), followed by a maintenance phase using a **treat-and-extend** (TAE) or **pro re nata** (PRN/as-needed) regimen guided by OCT findings and visual acuity.

#### Intravitreal Corticosteroids

Corticosteroids address the inflammatory component of macular edema in CRVO by suppressing multiple inflammatory mediators, stabilizing the [[blood-retinal barrier]], and downregulating VEGF expression.

| Agent | Details |
|---|---|
| **[[Dexamethasone intravitreal implant]]** (Ozurdex®) | Biodegradable sustained-release implant (0.7 mg). Approved for macular edema secondary to CRVO. Duration of action ~4–6 months. Landmark trial: **GENEVA**. Significant improvement in BCVA; however, associated with increased risk of [[cataract]] progression and IOP elevation. Often considered second-line or for patients who are pseudophakic, have contraindications to anti-VEGF, or are suboptimal responders to anti-VEGF. |
| **[[Triamcinolone acetonide]]** (intravitreal) | Off-label use; effective for macular edema but associated with significant rates of IOP elevation and cataract. Evaluated in the **SCORE** (Standard Care vs. Corticosteroid for Retinal Vein Occlusion) study, which found 1 mg intravitreal triamcinolone comparable to observation for CRVO-related macular edema, with fewer side effects than 4 mg. |
| **[[Fluocinolone acetonide intravitreal implant]]** (Iluvien®) | Not specifically approved for CRVO but used off-label in refractory cases. Provides sustained drug release for up to 36 months. |

#### Comparison: Anti-VEGF vs. Corticosteroids

| Parameter | Anti-VEGF | Intravitreal Corticosteroids |
|---|---|---|
| **Efficacy for macular edema** | High | High |
| **First-line status** | Yes | Generally second-line |
| **Treatment frequency** | Monthly initially; can be extended | Every 4–6 months (Ozurdex) |
| **Risk of IOP elevation** | Low | Significant (~30–40%) |
| **Risk of cataract** | Negligible | Significant in phakic patients |
| **Risk of endophthalmitis** | Low (~0.02–0.05% per injection) | Low (~0.02–0.05% per injection) |
| **Systemic safety** | Theoretical cardiovascular risk (low) | Minimal systemic absorption |

### Laser Photocoagulation

#### Panretinal Photocoagulation ([[PRP]])

[[Panretinal photocoagulation]] is indicated for the treatment or prevention of neovascular complications in **ischemic CRVO**. The landmark **Central Vein Occlusion Study (CVOS)** established the following guidelines:

- **PRP is NOT recommended prophylactically** in ischemic CRVO before neovascularization develops. The CVOS found no benefit to prophylactic PRP in preventing NVI/NVG.
- **PRP IS recommended promptly** when neovascularization of the iris (NVI), angle neovascularization, or posterior segment neovascularization (NVD/NVE) is detected.
- PRP works by destroying ischemic retinal tissue, thereby reducing the overall VEGF load and the stimulus for neovascularization.

**Important caveat:** In the era of anti-VEGF therapy, intravitreal anti-VEGF injections are often used as an adjunct or even as initial treatment for neovascularization, with PRP applied subsequently or concurrently. Anti-VEGF agents can cause rapid regression of neovascularization, providing a window for PRP application.

#### Macular Grid Laser

The CVOS found that macular grid laser photocoagulation **did NOT improve visual acuity** in eyes with macular edema secondary to CRVO, although it did reduce angiographic macular edema. Therefore, macular grid laser is **not recommended** as a treatment for CRVO-related macular edema. This stands in contrast to [[branch retinal vein occlusion]], where grid laser has demonstrated benefit.

### Surgical Interventions

#### Pars Plana Vitrectomy ([[PPV]])

[[Pars plana vitrectomy]] may be considered in specific circumstances:

- **Non-clearing [[vitreous hemorrhage]]:** Secondary to neovascularization.
- **[[Tractional retinal detachment]]** or combined tractional-rhegmatogenous retinal detachment.
- **[[Epiretinal membrane]]** causing tractional macular edema.
- **Refractory macular edema:** Some evidence suggests that vitrectomy with [[internal limiting membrane]] (ILM) peeling may benefit select cases of chronic, refractory macular edema, though this remains controversial.

#### Radial Optic Neurotomy (RON)

This surgical technique involves making an incision at the nasal edge of the optic disc to theoretically decompress the CRV at the lamina cribrosa. Initial reports were promising, but subsequent studies have not consistently demonstrated benefit, and the procedure carries significant risks (e.g., visual field defects, hemorrhage). **RON is not widely practiced and is not considered standard of care.**

#### Laser-Induced Chorioretinal Venous Anastomosis

This technique attempts to create an alternative venous outflow pathway by inducing an anastomosis between a retinal vein and the [[choroidal]] circulation. Results have been inconsistent, and complications (including choroidal neovascularization, vitreous hemorrhage, and retinal detachment) are significant. **This procedure is largely experimental and not standard of care.**

### Management of Neovascular Glaucoma ([[NVG]])

[[Neovascular glaucoma]] is a devastating complication of ischemic CRVO requiring urgent management:

1. **Intravitreal anti-VEGF injection:** Causes rapid regression of neovascularization; serves as a temporizing measure.
2. **Panretinal photocoagulation (PRP):** Definitive treatment to reduce VEGF drive; applied as soon as media clarity permits.
3. **Topical and systemic IOP-lowering agents:** [[Topical beta-blockers]], [[alpha-agonists]], [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]] (topical and oral), and [[topical corticosteroids]] (to reduce inflammation). [[Miotics]] (e.g., [[pilocarpine]]) are generally **avoided** as they can worsen inflammation and promote posterior synechiae.
4. **[[Glaucoma drainage device]] (GDD) surgery** (e.g., [[Ahmed valve]], [[Baerveldt implant]]): May be necessary if IOP is uncontrolled despite maximal medical therapy and PRP.
5. **[[Transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]]:** For refractory cases or eyes with poor visual potential.

### Emerging and Investigational Therapies

| Therapy | Mechanism / Status |
|---|---|
| **[[Faricimab]]** | Bispecific antibody targeting VEGF-A and Ang-2; FDA-approved for RVO-related macular edema; potential for extended dosing |
| **[[KSI-301]] (Tarcocimab tedromer)** | Anti-VEGF antibody biopolymer conjugate designed for extended durability; Phase III trials ongoing |
| **Port Delivery System (PDS)** | Surgically implanted reservoir for sustained ranibizumab delivery; investigated for various retinal conditions |
| **Gene therapy** | Investigational approaches to provide sustained intraocular anti-VEGF protein expression |
| **Sustained-release anti-VEGF implants** | Various platforms under development to reduce injection burden |

## Follow-Up and Monitoring

Appropriate follow-up is critical for detecting complications and guiding treatment:

| Time Point | Recommended Evaluation |
|---|---|
| **Initial presentation** | Comprehensive exam, OCT, consider FA, RAPD assessment, gonioscopy, systemic workup |
| **1 month** | BCVA, OCT, IOP, anterior segment exam (rubeosis check), dilated fundus exam |
| **Monthly for first 6 months** | Especially for ischemic CRVO or those on active treatment; BCVA, OCT, IOP, rubeosis/NV check |
| **Every 1–3 months thereafter** | Based on clinical status, treatment response, and ischemic vs. non-ischemic classification |
| **FA** | Repeat as needed to assess perfusion status, especially if clinical worsening or suspected conversion to ischemic type |
| **Gonioscopy** | At each visit for ischemic CRVO; periodically for non-ischemic CRVO |

Patients should be educated about symptoms of worsening (further vision loss, new floaters, pain, redness) and instructed to seek prompt evaluation.

## Prognosis

The visual prognosis in CRVO depends on several factors:

- **Subtype (ischemic vs. non-ischemic):** The single most important prognostic factor.
- **Baseline visual acuity:** Better presenting VA is associated with better final VA.
- **Presence and severity of macular edema:** Chronic, refractory macular edema leads to permanent photoreceptor damage.
- **Macular ischemia:** Enlargement and irregularity of the FAZ on FA or OCTA indicates macular ischemia and portends poor visual recovery regardless of macular edema treatment.
- **OCT biomarkers:** Integrity of the [[ellipsoid zone]] and [[external limiting membrane]], presence of DRIL, and subfoveal thickness are prognostic indicators.
- **Timeliness and adequacy of treatment:** Early initiation of anti-VEGF therapy for macular edema is associated with better visual outcomes.
- **Development of neovascular complications:** NVG carries a particularly poor prognosis.

---

## Key Clinical Pearls

- Always check for a **[[relative afferent pupillary defect]]** in any patient with suspected CRVO—it is the most reliable clinical indicator of ischemic CRVO.
- **Gonioscopy** should be performed at every follow-up visit for ischemic CRVO to detect early angle neovascularization.
- **Do not perform prophylactic PRP** in ischemic CRVO; treat neovascularization when it develops (per CVOS guidelines), though anti-VEGF may be used as a bridge.
- **Macular grid laser is not effective** for CRVO-related macular edema (unlike BRVO).
- **Anti-VEGF therapy is first-line** for macular edema in CRVO; intravitreal corticosteroids are an important alternative, especially in pseudophakic patients.
- **Systemic workup** is essential—CRVO is often a sign of underlying cardiovascular disease.
- **Young patients** (<50 years) with CRVO warrant a thorough thrombophilia and autoimmune workup.
- **Bilateral CRVO** should raise suspicion for hyperviscosity syndromes, thrombophilia, or systemic vasculitis.
