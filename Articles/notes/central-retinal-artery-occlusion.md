

# Central Retinal Artery Occlusion

---

**Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO)** is an ophthalmic emergency characterized by the sudden, painless loss of vision resulting from the obstruction of blood flow through the [[central retinal artery]] (CRA). Often referred to as a "retinal stroke" or "eye stroke," CRAO represents one of the most devastating vascular events affecting the [[retina]], with profound implications for visual prognosis. The condition is analogous to an [[ischemic stroke]] of the brain and shares many of the same risk factors, etiological mechanisms, and systemic associations. CRAO accounts for approximately 1 in 10,000 ophthalmology outpatient visits and has an estimated annual incidence of 1–2 per 100,000 persons, with a strong predilection for individuals over the age of 60 and a slight male predominance. Prompt recognition, urgent systemic workup, and emergent intervention are critical, as the retinal tissue has an extremely limited window of tolerance to ischemia—typically estimated at 90 to 240 minutes before irreversible neuronal damage ensues.

---

## Anatomy and Vascular Supply of the Retina

A thorough understanding of CRAO requires familiarity with the vascular anatomy of the [[retina]] and [[optic nerve head]].

### The Central Retinal Artery

The [[central retinal artery]] is a branch of the [[ophthalmic artery]], which itself arises from the [[internal carotid artery]] (ICA). The CRA enters the [[optic nerve]] approximately 10–15 mm posterior to the globe, traveling within the substance of the nerve before emerging at the [[optic disc]]. At the disc, it bifurcates into superior and inferior branches, which further divide into temporal and nasal arcades, supplying the inner two-thirds of the retina (from the [[inner limiting membrane]] to the [[inner nuclear layer]]).

### The Cilioretinal Artery

A [[cilioretinal artery]], present in approximately 15–32% of the general population, arises from the [[posterior ciliary arteries]] (part of the [[choroidal circulation]]) rather than the CRA. When present, this vessel supplies a variable portion of the inner retina, most commonly the [[papillomacular bundle]]. The presence of a cilioretinal artery is of critical clinical significance in CRAO, as it may preserve central macular perfusion and thus spare central visual acuity even in the setting of complete CRA obstruction.

### The Choroidal Circulation

The [[choroid]], supplied by the [[posterior ciliary arteries]], nourishes the outer retinal layers, including the [[photoreceptor]] layer and the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE). In CRAO, the choroidal circulation typically remains intact, which accounts for the characteristic [[cherry-red spot]] observed on [[fundoscopy]]—the fovea, being thinnest and deriving its nutrition from the underlying choroid, retains its reddish appearance against the surrounding pale, edematous, ischemic inner retina.

---

## Classification

CRAO is classified into several subtypes based on clinical presentation, mechanism, and the presence or absence of associated arteritic disease:

| **Classification** | **Description** | **Approximate Frequency** |
|---|---|---|
| **Non-arteritic CRAO (NA-CRAO)** | The most common form; caused by [[thromboembolism]], [[atherosclerosis]], or in situ [[thrombosis]]. No associated [[giant cell arteritis]]. | ~67% of cases |
| **Non-arteritic CRAO with cilioretinal artery sparing** | CRA is occluded, but a patent [[cilioretinal artery]] preserves macular perfusion, potentially sparing central acuity. | ~15% of cases |
| **Transient non-arteritic CRAO (Retinal TIA)** | Temporary obstruction with spontaneous reperfusion; vision may recover partially or fully. Analogous to a [[transient ischemic attack]] (TIA). | ~15% of cases |
| **Arteritic CRAO (A-CRAO)** | Caused by [[giant cell arteritis]] (GCA/temporal arteritis); associated with inflammation and occlusion of the ophthalmic artery or CRA. Constitutes an ophthalmic and systemic emergency. | ~3–5% of cases |

---

## Epidemiology

- **Incidence:** Approximately 1–2 per 100,000 persons per year.
- **Age:** Mean age at presentation is approximately 60–65 years. CRAO in younger patients (<45 years) should prompt investigation for [[hypercoagulable states]], [[cardiac valvular disease]], [[vasculitis]], or other uncommon etiologies.
- **Sex:** Slight male predominance (approximately 58–66% male).
- **Laterality:** Typically unilateral; bilateral CRAO is exceedingly rare and should raise suspicion for [[giant cell arteritis]], severe [[atherosclerotic disease]], or [[hypercoagulable disorders]].
- **Systemic associations:** CRAO is strongly associated with systemic cardiovascular disease. Studies have demonstrated that patients with CRAO have a significantly elevated risk of subsequent [[cerebrovascular accident]] (stroke), [[myocardial infarction]], and cardiovascular mortality, particularly within the first week to months following the ocular event.

---

## Etiology and Risk Factors

The etiology of CRAO is multifactorial and closely mirrors the risk factor profile for systemic [[atherosclerosis]] and [[cerebrovascular disease]].

### Common Etiologies

| **Mechanism** | **Examples** |
|---|---|
| **Thromboembolism** | [[Carotid artery]] atherosclerotic plaque (most common source), [[cardiac valvular disease]], [[atrial fibrillation]], [[endocarditis]], [[aortic arch atheroma]] |
| **In situ thrombosis** | [[Atherosclerosis]] of the CRA at the level of the [[lamina cribrosa]], [[vasculitis]] |
| **Arteritis** | [[Giant cell arteritis]] (GCA), [[polyarteritis nodosa]], [[systemic lupus erythematosus]] (SLE), [[Takayasu arteritis]] |
| **Vasospasm** | [[Migraine]], [[cocaine]] use, [[ergot]] derivatives |
| **Hypercoagulable states** | [[Antiphospholipid syndrome]], [[Factor V Leiden mutation]], [[protein C deficiency]], [[protein S deficiency]], [[hyperhomocysteinemia]], [[sickle cell disease]] |
| **Dissection** | [[Carotid artery dissection]], [[ophthalmic artery dissection]] |
| **Iatrogenic/Traumatic** | Retrobulbar injection, [[cosmetic filler injection]] (particularly periorbital hyaluronic acid or fat), orbital/facial trauma, surgical complications |
| **Other** | [[Oral contraceptive]] use, [[pregnancy]], [[sickle cell disease]], [[Fabry disease]], intravenous drug use |

### Systemic Risk Factors

- [[Hypertension]] (present in 60–75% of patients)
- [[Diabetes mellitus]] (present in 25–35%)
- [[Hyperlipidemia]] / [[dyslipidemia]]
- [[Coronary artery disease]]
- [[Carotid artery stenosis]]
- [[Atrial fibrillation]] and other [[cardiac arrhythmias]]
- [[Smoking]]
- [[Obesity]]
- History of [[transient ischemic attack]] or [[stroke]]

## Pathophysiology

### Mechanism of Ischemic Injury

The [[central retinal artery]] is an end artery with minimal anastomotic connections. Occlusion of this vessel results in acute cessation of blood flow to the inner retinal layers, which are metabolically highly active and exquisitely sensitive to ischemia. The inner retina has among the highest oxygen consumption rates of any tissue in the body.

**Sequence of pathophysiological events:**

1. **Acute vascular obstruction:** An embolus (most commonly a [[cholesterol embolus]] [Hollenhorst plaque], [[platelet-fibrin embolus]], or [[calcific embolus]]) lodges at the narrowest point of the CRA, typically at or near the [[lamina cribrosa]] of the [[optic nerve head]]. Alternatively, in situ thrombosis or vasculitis may occlude the vessel.

2. **Retinal ischemia:** Within seconds of occlusion, the inner retinal layers (including the [[retinal ganglion cell]] layer, [[inner plexiform layer]], [[inner nuclear layer]], and portions of the [[outer plexiform layer]]) are deprived of oxygen and glucose.

3. **Cellular energy failure:** Aerobic metabolism ceases rapidly. Intracellular ATP stores are depleted within minutes, leading to failure of the [[Na+/K+ ATPase]] pump and subsequent cytotoxic edema (intracellular swelling).

4. **Excitotoxicity and calcium influx:** Glutamate accumulates in the extracellular space, activating [[NMDA receptors]] and [[AMPA receptors]], leading to massive calcium influx into neurons. This triggers a cascade of destructive enzymatic processes.

5. **Irreversible neuronal death:** Experimental studies in primate models (Hayreh et al.) have demonstrated that complete CRAO lasting **90 minutes** results in significant but potentially reversible retinal damage, while occlusion lasting **240 minutes (4 hours)** results in **massive, irreversible** retinal infarction. The clinical window for meaningful intervention is therefore extremely narrow.

6. **Retinal edema and opacification:** The ischemic inner retina becomes edematous and opaque (whitish), producing the classic fundoscopic appearance. The [[fovea]], which is devoid of inner retinal layers and nourished by the [[choriocapillaris]], remains transparent, revealing the underlying [[retinal pigment epithelium]] and choroidal vasculature as a [[cherry-red spot]].

7. **Late atrophy:** Over weeks to months, the edema resolves, and the inner retina undergoes atrophy and gliosis. The retinal arterioles may become attenuated and sclerotic. [[Optic disc pallor]] develops due to loss of the [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL).

### Embolic Sources

The most common embolic sources in non-arteritic CRAO include:

- **[[Carotid artery]] atherosclerotic plaque** — the single most common source
- **Cardiac sources** — [[atrial fibrillation]], [[valvular heart disease]] (including [[mitral valve prolapse]], [[prosthetic heart valves]]), [[patent foramen ovale]] (PFO) with paradoxical embolism, [[left atrial myxoma]], [[endocarditis]]
- **Aortic arch atheroma**

### Arteritic CRAO (Giant Cell Arteritis)

In [[giant cell arteritis]] (GCA), the mechanism differs fundamentally. GCA is a systemic [[granulomatous vasculitis]] affecting medium and large arteries, with a predilection for branches of the [[external carotid artery]] (particularly the [[temporal artery]]) and the [[ophthalmic artery]]. Arteritic CRAO results from inflammatory occlusion of the CRA or the ophthalmic artery itself, often accompanied by concurrent [[anterior ischemic optic neuropathy]] (AION) or [[posterior ciliary artery]] occlusion. Arteritic CRAO carries a high risk of bilateral involvement and requires emergent treatment with high-dose systemic [[corticosteroids]].

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

The hallmark symptom of CRAO is **sudden, painless, profound monocular vision loss**. Key features include:

- **Onset:** Acute, often described as occurring "within seconds" or upon awakening.
- **Pain:** Typically absent. The presence of pain should raise suspicion for [[giant cell arteritis]] (headache, jaw claudication, scalp tenderness), [[orbital disease]], or [[angle-closure glaucoma]].
- **Severity:** Vision is typically reduced to [[counting fingers]] (CF), [[hand motions]] (HM), or [[light perception]] (LP). In rare cases with a patent [[cilioretinal artery]], central acuity may be relatively preserved (e.g., 20/50 or better), though the peripheral visual field remains severely constricted.
- **Visual field:** A dense [[central scotoma]] or near-total field loss is typical. An [[afferent pupillary defect]] (APD/[[relative afferent pupillary defect]] [RAPD]) is present in the affected eye.
- **Preceding symptoms:** Some patients report antecedent episodes of [[amaurosis fugax]] (transient monocular vision loss lasting seconds to minutes), which represent retinal TIAs and are a critical warning sign of impending permanent occlusion.

### Signs

#### Anterior Segment Examination

- The anterior segment is typically **unremarkable** in the acute phase.
- [[Intraocular pressure]] (IOP) is usually normal.
- A [[relative afferent pupillary defect]] (RAPD) is present in the affected eye (unless bilateral disease exists).
- Weeks to months later, [[neovascularization of the iris]] ([[rubeosis iridis]]) may develop in a subset of patients (approximately 15–20%), potentially leading to [[neovascular glaucoma]] (NVG).

#### Fundoscopic Examination

The classic fundoscopic findings of acute CRAO include:

| **Finding** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Retinal whitening/opacification** | Diffuse pallor and edema of the posterior pole retina, most prominent in the [[macula]] and along the major vascular arcades, due to ischemic swelling of the inner retinal layers. |
| **[[Cherry-red spot]]** | A bright red or reddish-orange spot at the [[fovea]], contrasting with the surrounding pale retina. This is not a true pathological lesion but rather the normal foveal appearance visible through the thin, transparent foveal tissue against the intact [[choroidal]] blood supply. Present in approximately 90% of cases. |
| **Retinal arterial attenuation** | The retinal arterioles appear narrowed, thread-like, or segmented ("boxcarring" or "cattle-trucking" of the blood column due to fragmented flow). |
| **Visible emboli** | A [[Hollenhorst plaque]] (refractile, golden-yellow [[cholesterol]] crystal), [[platelet-fibrin embolus]] (dull, white, elongated), or [[calcific embolus]] (white, non-refractile) may be visible at an arteriolar bifurcation, most commonly at the [[optic disc]]. |
| **Optic disc appearance** | The [[optic disc]] may appear normal acutely, mildly edematous, or pale. In arteritic CRAO, disc edema is more common due to concurrent [[anterior ischemic optic neuropathy]]. |
| **Venous changes** | Retinal veins may appear mildly dilated or normal. |
| **Cilioretinal artery sparing** | If a [[cilioretinal artery]] is present and patent, the area of retina it supplies (typically the papillomacular bundle) may appear normal and perfused, surrounded by the pale ischemic retina. |

#### Temporal Evolution of Fundoscopic Findings

- **Hyperacute (0–1 hours):** Fundus may appear deceptively normal or show only subtle retinal whitening. The cherry-red spot may not yet be fully developed.
- **Acute (1–24 hours):** Classic findings of diffuse retinal whitening and cherry-red spot become fully apparent.
- **Subacute (days to weeks):** Retinal edema gradually resolves. Arteriolar attenuation persists.
- **Chronic (weeks to months):** Retinal whitening resolves, replaced by inner retinal atrophy and thinning. [[Optic disc pallor]] (optic atrophy) develops. Retinal arterioles remain attenuated and may show sheathing. The cherry-red spot disappears as the surrounding edema resolves.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis

CRAO is primarily a **clinical diagnosis** based on the characteristic history (sudden, painless, profound monocular vision loss) and fundoscopic findings (retinal whitening, cherry-red spot, arteriolar attenuation). However, ancillary testing is essential to confirm the diagnosis, assess the extent of ischemia, identify the underlying etiology, and evaluate for systemic vascular risk.

### Ancillary Ophthalmic Investigations

#### Fluorescein Angiography (FA)

[[Fluorescein angiography]] is the gold standard for confirming retinal vascular occlusion and is highly informative in CRAO:

- **Delayed or absent retinal arterial filling:** The most characteristic finding. The arm-to-retina circulation time is markedly prolonged (normal: 10–15 seconds). In complete CRAO, there may be no filling of the retinal arterial tree for an extended period.
- **"Leading edge" or "boxcar" pattern:** Slow, segmented filling of the retinal arterioles with alternating columns of dye and non-perfused segments.
- **Delayed arteriovenous transit time:** The time from first arteriolar filling to complete venous filling is markedly prolonged.
- **Choroidal filling:** Typically normal (unless concurrent ophthalmic artery occlusion or arteritic disease is present, in which case choroidal filling may also be delayed or patchy).
- **Cilioretinal artery filling:** If present, the cilioretinal artery fills normally with the choroidal phase, while the CRA branches remain unfilled—a pathognomonic finding.
- **Late staining:** The optic disc may show late staining or leakage.

#### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

[[Optical coherence tomography]] provides high-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the retina and is invaluable in CRAO:

- **Acute phase:** Increased reflectivity and thickening of the inner retinal layers (ganglion cell layer, inner plexiform layer, inner nuclear layer) due to cytotoxic edema. The outer retinal layers remain relatively normal.
- **Hyperreflective inner retina:** The ischemic inner retina appears hyperreflective on OCT, corresponding to the clinical whitening seen on fundoscopy.
- **Foveal sparing:** The foveal depression may show relatively preserved architecture, as the fovea lacks inner retinal layers.
- **Chronic phase:** Progressive thinning and atrophy of the inner retinal layers, with loss of the normal laminar architecture. The [[ganglion cell complex]] (GCC) becomes markedly thinned.

#### OCT Angiography (OCTA)

[[OCT angiography]] is a non-invasive imaging modality that can demonstrate:

- Reduced or absent flow signal in the superficial and deep retinal capillary plexuses
- Preserved choroidal flow (in non-arteritic cases)
- Visualization of the area of non-perfusion
- Monitoring of reperfusion over time

#### Visual Field Testing

- [[Automated perimetry]] (e.g., [[Humphrey visual field]]) typically demonstrates dense central or diffuse depression, often with residual temporal island of vision (corresponding to preserved nasal retinal function from collateral or choroidal supply).
- In cases with cilioretinal artery sparing, a central island of preserved field may be present.

#### Electroretinography (ERG)

- [[Full-field electroretinography]] may show a reduced b-wave amplitude with a relatively preserved a-wave, reflecting inner retinal dysfunction (the b-wave is generated primarily by [[Müller cells]] and [[bipolar cells]] in the inner retina, while the a-wave reflects photoreceptor function in the outer retina).
- The b/a ratio is characteristically reduced ("negative ERG" pattern).

### Systemic Workup

Given the strong association between CRAO and systemic vascular disease, a comprehensive systemic evaluation is **mandatory** and should be initiated urgently:

| **Investigation** | **Purpose** |
|---|---|
| **[[Erythrocyte sedimentation rate]] (ESR)** | Screen for [[giant cell arteritis]] (markedly elevated in GCA, often >50 mm/hr, frequently >100 mm/hr) |
| **[[C-reactive protein]] (CRP)** | Acute-phase reactant; elevated in GCA and other inflammatory conditions |
| **[[Complete blood count]] (CBC)** | Evaluate for [[polycythemia]], [[thrombocytosis]], [[leukocytosis]], [[sickle cell disease]] |
| **[[Carotid Doppler ultrasound]]** | Assess for [[carotid artery stenosis]] or plaque—the most common embolic source |
| **[[Echocardiography]] (TTE/TEE)** | Evaluate for cardiac embolic sources: [[valvular disease]], [[intracardiac thrombus]], [[patent foramen ovale]], [[atrial myxoma]], [[vegetations]] |
| **[[Electrocardiogram]] (ECG)** | Screen for [[atrial fibrillation]] and other [[arrhythmias]] |
| **[[Holter monitor]]** | Detect paroxysmal [[atrial fibrillation]] |
| **[[Fasting lipid panel]]** | Assess [[dyslipidemia]] |
| **[[Fasting blood glucose]] / [[HbA1c]]** | Screen for [[diabetes mellitus]] |
| **[[Blood pressure]] measurement** | Screen for [[hypertension]] |
| **[[Coagulation studies]]** | [[PT]], [[PTT]], [[INR]]; consider [[hypercoagulability panel]] in younger patients or those without traditional risk factors |
| **[[Hypercoagulability panel]]** | [[Antiphospholipid antibodies]], [[lupus anticoagulant]], [[Factor V Leiden]], [[prothrombin gene mutation]], [[protein C]], [[protein S]], [[antithrombin III]], [[homocysteine]] levels |
| **[[Temporal artery biopsy]]** | Gold standard for diagnosis of [[giant cell arteritis]]; should be performed urgently if GCA is suspected (do not delay corticosteroid treatment while awaiting biopsy) |
| **Neuroimaging ([[MRI]]/[[MRA]] brain)** | Evaluate for concurrent cerebral ischemia; studies show 5–25% of CRAO patients have concurrent acute cerebral infarction on diffusion-weighted MRI |

### Differential Diagnosis

While the clinical presentation of CRAO is usually distinctive, the following conditions should be considered in the differential diagnosis:

- [[Ophthalmic artery occlusion]] — more severe; involves both retinal and choroidal circulation; no cherry-red spot (entire retina and choroid are ischemic)
- [[Branch retinal artery occlusion]] (BRAO) — localized retinal whitening in the distribution of a single arteriolar branch
- [[Central retinal vein occlusion]] (CRVO) — distinguished by widespread retinal hemorrhages, dilated tortuous veins, and disc edema ("blood and thunder" fundus)
- [[Anterior ischemic optic neuropathy]] (AION) — disc edema with sectoral or diffuse pallor; visual field defect typically altitudinal
- [[Optic neuritis]] — pain with eye movement; younger demographic; MRI findings
- [[Retinal detachment]] — may present with sudden vision loss but has distinct fundoscopic and OCT findings
- [[Vitreous hemorrhage]] — obscured fundus view; B-scan ultrasonography diagnostic

## Management

### Emergency Classification

CRAO is classified as an **ophthalmic emergency**. The American Academy of Ophthalmology (AAO) and multiple stroke guidelines now recommend that CRAO be treated with the same urgency as an acute ischemic stroke, given the shared pathophysiology and the narrow therapeutic window.

### Acute Management (Within 4.5–6 Hours of Onset)

#### Traditional Measures (Limited Evidence)

Historically, several interventions have been employed in the acute setting, though evidence for their efficacy remains limited:

| **Intervention** | **Mechanism** | **Evidence Level** |
|---|---|---|
| **Ocular massage** | Mechanical dislodgement of embolus; transient IOP fluctuation may promote distal migration of the clot | Low; anecdotal benefit |
| **Anterior chamber paracentesis** | Rapid reduction of [[intraocular pressure]] to increase perfusion pressure gradient across the CRA | Low; may provide transient IOP reduction but no proven visual benefit |
| **Topical IOP-lowering agents** ([[timolol]], [[acetazolamide]]) | Reduce IOP to improve ocular perfusion pressure | Low; adjunctive measure |
| **Intravenous [[acetazolamide]]** (500 mg) | Systemic IOP reduction | Low |
| **Carbogen inhalation** (95% O₂ / 5% CO₂) | Vasodilation of retinal vessels; increased oxygen delivery | Low; largely abandoned |
| **Sublingual [[isosorbide dinitrate]]** | Systemic vasodilation | Very low |
| **Hyperbaric oxygen therapy** | Increase dissolved oxygen in plasma to sustain outer retinal viability and potentially inner retinal viability via diffusion from choroid | Moderate; some studies suggest benefit if initiated within 8–24 hours; may serve as a bridge therapy |

#### Intravenous Thrombolysis (tPA)

Systemic intravenous [[tissue plasminogen activator]] (tPA/[[alteplase]]) has been investigated as a treatment for acute CRAO, analogous to its use in acute ischemic stroke:

- **EAGLE Study (European Assessment Group for Lysis in the Eye):** This multicenter randomized controlled trial was terminated early due to safety concerns (increased adverse events, including intracranial hemorrhage, in the treatment group) without demonstrating a statistically significant visual benefit. However, the study had significant limitations, including delayed treatment times.
- **Current evidence:** Despite the EAGLE trial results, subsequent observational studies and meta-analyses have suggested potential benefit of IV tPA when administered within **4.5 hours** of symptom onset, consistent with the stroke treatment window. The benefit appears to diminish rapidly with increasing time to treatment.
- **Guidelines:** Several stroke centers and neuro-ophthalmology experts now advocate for consideration of IV tPA within the 4.5-hour window, particularly given the reclassification of CRAO as a stroke equivalent. The decision must be individualized, weighing potential benefits against bleeding risks.

#### Intra-arterial Thrombolysis (IAT)

Selective [[intra-arterial thrombolysis]], involving catheter-directed delivery of tPA or [[urokinase]] directly into the ophthalmic artery via the internal carotid artery, has been explored:

- Some case series have reported visual improvement in a subset of patients.
- However, the procedure carries significant risks, including [[stroke]], [[arterial dissection]], and [[hemorrhage]].
- No large randomized controlled trials have demonstrated definitive benefit.
- Currently not recommended as standard of care but may be considered in specialized centers on a case-by-case basis.

#### Emerging Therapies

- **Mechanical thrombectomy:** Case reports describe endovascular mechanical clot retrieval for CRAO, analogous to thrombectomy for large-vessel cerebral stroke. This remains investigational.
- **Nd:YAG laser embolysis:** [[Nd:YAG laser]] application to visible emboli within retinal arterioles has been reported in case series, with some success in fragmenting and dislodging emboli. This technique is limited to cases with visible emboli and carries risks of vessel wall damage and vitreous hemorrhage.

### Subacute and Chronic Management

#### Stroke Protocol and Systemic Risk Factor Modification

Given that CRAO is now recognized as a **stroke equivalent**, the most critical aspect of management extends beyond the eye:

- **Urgent referral to stroke service/neurology:** Ideally within 24 hours. Studies demonstrate that up to 25% of CRAO patients have concurrent silent cerebral infarcts, and the risk of subsequent stroke is highest in the first days to weeks.
- **Antiplatelet therapy:** [[Aspirin]] (75–325 mg daily) is typically initiated unless contraindicated. [[Clopidogrel]] or dual antiplatelet therapy may be considered based on the clinical scenario.
- **Anticoagulation:** Indicated if a cardioembolic source is identified (e.g., [[atrial fibrillation]] → [[warfarin]] or direct oral anticoagulants [[[DOACs]]]).
- **Statin therapy:** High-intensity [[statin]] therapy (e.g., [[atorvastatin]] 40–80 mg) for [[dyslipidemia]] and plaque stabilization.
- **Antihypertensive therapy:** Blood pressure optimization per current guidelines.
- **Glycemic control:** In patients with [[diabetes mellitus]].
- **Smoking cessation:** Counseling and pharmacotherapy as needed.
- **Carotid endarterectomy or stenting:** If significant ipsilateral [[carotid artery stenosis]] (≥70%) is identified, referral for [[carotid endarterectomy]] (CEA) or [[carotid artery stenting]] (CAS) should be considered per vascular surgery/neurology guidelines.

#### Management of Giant Cell Arteritis (Arteritic CRAO)

If [[giant cell arteritis]] is suspected or confirmed:

- **Immediate high-dose systemic corticosteroids:** [[Intravenous methylprednisolone]] 1 g/day for 3 days, followed by oral [[prednisone]] 1 mg/kg/day (typically 60–80 mg/day), with gradual taper over months guided by symptoms and inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP).
- **Do NOT delay treatment** while awaiting [[temporal artery biopsy]] results. Biopsy should be performed within 1–2 weeks of initiating steroids (histological findings remain positive for at least 2–6 weeks after starting corticosteroids).
- **Steroid-sparing agents:** [[Tocilizumab]] (an [[IL-6 receptor]] antagonist) has been approved for GCA and may be used as adjunctive therapy to reduce steroid burden and relapse risk. [[Methotrexate]] is an alternative steroid-sparing agent.
- **Monitor for complications of long-term corticosteroid use:** [[Osteoporosis]], [[diabetes]], [[hypertension]], [[adrenal insufficiency]], [[cataracts]], [[glaucoma]].

#### Monitoring for Ocular Neovascularization

- [[Neovascularization]] of the retina ([[NVE]]), optic disc ([[NVD]]), or iris ([[NVI]]/[[rubeosis iridis]]) develops in approximately **15–20%** of eyes following CRAO, typically within **4–12 weeks**.
- This is driven by the release of [[vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF) and other pro-angiogenic cytokines from the ischemic retina.
- **Follow-up schedule:** Patients should be monitored at regular intervals (e.g., every 2–4 weeks for the first 3 months) with careful [[gonioscopy]], [[iris examination]], and [[dilated fundus examination]].
- **Treatment of neovascularization:**
  - **[[Panretinal photocoagulation]] (PRP):** The standard treatment for retinal and disc neovascularization; ablates ischemic retina to reduce VEGF production.
  - **Intravitreal [[anti-VEGF]] injections:** ([[Bevacizumab]], [[ranibizumab]], [[aflibercept]]) may be used as adjunctive therapy, particularly for [[neovascular glaucoma]] or as a temporizing measure before PRP.
  - **[[Neovascular glaucoma]] management:** May require [[glaucoma drainage device]] implantation, [[cyclophotocoagulation]], or other surgical interventions if IOP is uncontrolled.

## Prognosis

The visual prognosis of CRAO is generally **poor**:

| **Subtype** | **Visual Outcome** |
|---|---|
| **Non-arteritic CRAO (complete)** | Final visual acuity of CF or worse in approximately 80% of cases. Spontaneous improvement to 20/40 or better occurs in only ~8–10% of patients. |
| **CRAO with cilioretinal artery sparing** | Significantly better prognosis for central acuity; approximately 60–80% may retain 20/50 or better acuity, though peripheral field loss persists. |
| **Transient CRAO** | Variable; some patients recover fully, but many have residual visual deficits. These patients are at high risk for recurrent permanent CRAO. |
| **Arteritic CRAO** | Extremely poor visual prognosis for the affected eye. Urgent treatment is aimed at preventing involvement of the fellow eye. |

### Systemic Prognosis

- Patients with CRAO have a **significantly increased risk** of cardiovascular and cerebrovascular morbidity and mortality.
- Studies report a **2- to 3-fold increased risk** of stroke within the first 3 years following CRAO.
- The risk of acute stroke is highest in the **first 7 days** after CRAO onset.
- All-cause mortality is increased compared to age-matched controls.
- Comprehensive cardiovascular risk factor management is therefore essential and arguably the most impactful intervention for the patient's overall health.

---

## Summary of Key Clinical Pearls

1. **CRAO is a stroke equivalent.** Treat with the same urgency as an acute cerebral ischemic event.
2. **Time is retina.** The window for potential visual recovery is extremely narrow (90–240 minutes of complete ischemia).
3. **Always rule out [[giant cell arteritis]]** in patients over 50 years of age. Obtain ESR and CRP urgently. Do not delay corticosteroid treatment if GCA is suspected.
4. **Comprehensive systemic workup is mandatory**, including carotid imaging, echocardiography, ECG, and laboratory studies.
5. **Urgent stroke service referral** is recommended, ideally within 24 hours.
6. **Monitor for neovascular complications** (rubeosis iridis, neovascular glaucoma) for at least 3 months following the event.
7. **The cherry-red spot is not pathognomonic for CRAO alone**—it can also be seen in [[Tay-Sachs disease]], [[Niemann-Pick disease]], and other [[sphingolipidoses]] (storage diseases), though the clinical context is entirely different.
8. **Counsel patients** about the systemic implications of their diagnosis and the importance of cardiovascular risk factor modification.
