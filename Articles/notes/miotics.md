# Miotics

**Miotics** are a class of [[pharmacological agents]] that induce [[miosis]]—constriction of the [[pupil]]—by stimulating the [[sphincter pupillae]] muscle of the [[iris]] or by inhibiting the [[dilator pupillae]] muscle. These agents have been foundational in the history of [[ophthalmic pharmacology]] and remain clinically relevant in the management of [[glaucoma]], [[accommodative esotropia]], and various other ocular conditions. Miotics act primarily through the [[parasympathetic nervous system]], either as direct-acting [[cholinergic agonists]] or as indirect-acting [[anticholinesterase]] agents. Despite the advent of newer [[intraocular pressure]]-lowering medications, miotics continue to occupy an important niche in the therapeutic armamentarium of [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]].

---

## Classification

Miotics are broadly classified into two major pharmacological categories based on their mechanism of action:

### Direct-Acting (Parasympathomimetic) Miotics

These agents directly stimulate [[muscarinic receptors]] (specifically [[M3 receptors]]) on the [[sphincter pupillae]] and the [[ciliary muscle]], mimicking the action of [[acetylcholine]].

| Agent | Concentration | Onset | Duration | Primary Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [[Pilocarpine]] | 0.5%–6% | 10–30 min | 4–8 hours | [[Open-angle glaucoma]], [[angle-closure glaucoma]], reversal of [[mydriasis]] |
| [[Carbachol]] | 0.75%–3% (topical); 0.01% (intraocular) | 10–20 min | 6–8 hours (topical); prolonged (intraocular) | Glaucoma, intraoperative miosis |
| [[Acetylcholine]] (Miochol-E) | 1% (intraocular) | Seconds | ~10 minutes | Intraoperative miosis during [[cataract surgery]] |
| [[Methacholine]] | Rarely used | Minutes | Short | Historical/diagnostic use |

### Indirect-Acting (Anticholinesterase) Miotics

These agents inhibit the enzyme [[acetylcholinesterase]] (or [[pseudocholinesterase]]), preventing the breakdown of endogenous [[acetylcholine]] at the [[neuromuscular junction]] and [[parasympathetic nerve terminals]], thereby prolonging and amplifying its action.

| Agent | Type | Concentration | Onset | Duration | Primary Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| [[Physostigmine]] | Reversible [[cholinesterase inhibitor]] | 0.25%–0.5% | 20–30 min | 12–36 hours | Glaucoma (rarely used) |
| [[Neostigmine]] | Reversible cholinesterase inhibitor | 3%–5% | 15–30 min | 2–4 hours | Diagnostic use |
| [[Demecarium bromide]] | Irreversible cholinesterase inhibitor | 0.125%–0.25% | 15–60 min | Days to weeks | [[Accommodative esotropia]], glaucoma |
| [[Echothiophate iodide]] (Phospholine Iodide) | Irreversible cholinesterase inhibitor | 0.03%–0.25% | 10–45 min | Days to weeks | Accommodative esotropia, glaucoma |
| [[Isoflurophate]] (DFP) | Irreversible cholinesterase inhibitor | 0.025% | 15–60 min | 1–4 weeks | Historical use; largely discontinued |

## Pathophysiology and Mechanism of Action

### Autonomic Innervation of the Pupil

The size of the [[pupil]] is governed by the dynamic balance between two smooth muscles within the [[iris]]:

1. **[[Sphincter pupillae]]**: A ring of smooth muscle fibers arranged circumferentially around the [[pupillary margin]], innervated by [[parasympathetic]] fibers originating from the [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]]. These preganglionic fibers travel along the [[oculomotor nerve]] (cranial nerve III) and synapse in the [[ciliary ganglion]]. Postganglionic fibers reach the sphincter via the [[short ciliary nerves]], releasing [[acetylcholine]] at [[muscarinic receptors]] (predominantly [[M3 subtype]]).

2. **[[Dilator pupillae]]**: Radially oriented smooth muscle fibers innervated by [[sympathetic]] fibers originating from the [[superior cervical ganglion]]. These postganglionic fibers travel along the [[long ciliary nerves]] and release [[norepinephrine]] at [[alpha-1 adrenergic receptors]].

### Mechanism of Direct-Acting Miotics

Direct-acting miotics such as [[pilocarpine]] and [[carbachol]] bind to and activate [[muscarinic receptors]] on the sphincter pupillae, causing contraction and resultant [[miosis]]. Simultaneously, these agents stimulate the [[ciliary muscle]], causing it to contract. Ciliary muscle contraction has two critically important physiological consequences:

- **Accommodation**: Contraction of the [[ciliary muscle]] relaxes the [[zonular fibers]] of [[Zinn]], allowing the [[crystalline lens]] to assume a more spherical shape, increasing its refractive power. This is the basis for the [[accommodative spasm]] and [[induced myopia]] commonly experienced as side effects of miotic therapy.

- **Opening of the [[trabecular meshwork]]**: The longitudinal fibers of the ciliary muscle insert into the [[scleral spur]], and their contraction mechanically pulls open the [[trabecular meshwork]] and [[Schlemm's canal]], enhancing [[aqueous humor]] outflow through the [[conventional (trabecular) outflow pathway]]. This is the primary mechanism by which miotics reduce [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP).

In [[angle-closure glaucoma]], miosis physically pulls the peripheral [[iris]] away from the [[trabecular meshwork]], relieving [[pupillary block]] and opening the [[iridocorneal angle]].

### Mechanism of Indirect-Acting Miotics

Indirect-acting miotics inhibit [[acetylcholinesterase]], the enzyme responsible for the rapid hydrolysis of acetylcholine in the [[synaptic cleft]]. By preventing acetylcholine degradation, these agents allow endogenous acetylcholine to accumulate and exert a prolonged and intensified effect on muscarinic receptors of the sphincter pupillae and ciliary muscle.

- **Reversible inhibitors** (e.g., [[physostigmine]], [[neostigmine]]) form a temporary bond with the enzyme, with effects lasting hours.
- **Irreversible inhibitors** (e.g., [[echothiophate iodide]], [[demecarium bromide]], [[isoflurophate]]) form a covalent bond with the enzyme's active site (phosphorylation of the serine residue at the [[esteratic site]]), permanently inactivating it. Recovery of enzymatic activity requires synthesis of new enzyme molecules, which may take days to weeks.

### Effect on Aqueous Humor Dynamics

The IOP-lowering effect of miotics is primarily mediated through enhancement of [[aqueous humor outflow]] via the [[trabecular (conventional) pathway]]. The mechanism involves:

1. Contraction of the longitudinal fibers of the [[ciliary muscle]]
2. Traction on the [[scleral spur]]
3. Mechanical opening and widening of the intertrabecular spaces of the [[trabecular meshwork]]
4. Increased patency of [[Schlemm's canal]]
5. Enhanced drainage of aqueous humor into the [[episcleral venous system]]

Some evidence also suggests a minor contribution from increased [[uveoscleral outflow]] in certain conditions, though this is not the predominant mechanism.

## Clinical Indications

### Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma (POAG)

Miotics, particularly [[pilocarpine]], were historically the first-line treatment for [[primary open-angle glaucoma]]. While they have been largely supplanted by [[prostaglandin analogs]], [[beta-blockers]], [[alpha-2 agonists]], and [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]] due to their more favorable side-effect profiles, pilocarpine remains a valuable adjunctive agent, especially in cases refractory to other therapies. The typical regimen involves pilocarpine 1%–4% instilled two to four times daily.

### Angle-Closure Glaucoma

In acute [[angle-closure glaucoma]], pilocarpine is a critical component of the emergency treatment protocol. By inducing miosis, pilocarpine pulls the peripheral iris posteriorly, breaking the [[pupillary block]] mechanism and opening the [[drainage angle]]. However, it is important to note that in the setting of extremely elevated IOP with [[iris sphincter ischemia]], pilocarpine may initially be ineffective until the IOP is partially lowered by other agents (e.g., [[timolol]], [[apraclonidine]], oral or intravenous [[acetazolamide]], intravenous [[mannitol]]). Once the IOP begins to decrease and the sphincter regains function, pilocarpine becomes effective.

### Accommodative Esotropia

Indirect-acting miotics, particularly [[echothiophate iodide]], have been used in the management of [[accommodative esotropia]] in children. By potentiating the action of acetylcholine at the [[ciliary muscle]], these agents enhance the [[accommodative response]] per unit of [[accommodative effort]], thereby reducing the [[accommodative convergence/accommodation (AC/A) ratio]] and decreasing the degree of [[esotropia]]. This pharmacological approach may serve as a temporizing measure or as an alternative to [[bifocal spectacles]] in selected pediatric patients.

### Reversal of Mydriasis

Pilocarpine 1%–2% is commonly used to reverse pharmacologically induced [[mydriasis]] following a dilated [[fundus examination]]. This is particularly useful in patients who are at risk for [[angle closure]] or who experience significant visual disturbance from prolonged dilation (e.g., patients with light-colored irides, patients who must drive).

### Presbyopia

In 2021, the [[U.S. Food and Drug Administration]] (FDA) approved [[pilocarpine]] 1.25% ophthalmic solution (Vuity™) for the treatment of [[presbyopia]] in adults. This low-concentration formulation leverages the miotic effect to create a [[pinhole effect]], increasing [[depth of focus]] and improving near vision without significantly compromising distance vision. The optimized formulation uses a pH-adjusted vehicle to reduce the accommodative spasm and myopic shift associated with higher concentrations.

### Intraoperative Use

- **[[Acetylcholine]] (Miochol-E)**: Used intraocularly during [[cataract surgery]], [[keratoplasty]], [[iridectomy]], and other anterior segment procedures to achieve rapid miosis.
- **[[Carbachol]] (Miostat)**: Used intraocularly when a more sustained intraoperative miosis is required, as it is resistant to degradation by cholinesterase.

## Symptoms and Side Effects

Miotics produce a characteristic constellation of ocular and systemic side effects that are directly related to their parasympathomimetic activity.

### Ocular Side Effects

| Side Effect | Mechanism | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| [[Accommodative spasm]] / [[induced myopia]] | Ciliary muscle contraction | Blurred distance vision; particularly problematic in younger patients with active accommodation |
| [[Brow ache]] / periorbital pain | Sustained ciliary muscle and sphincter contraction | Common in initial weeks of therapy; often diminishes with continued use |
| [[Miosis]] (small pupil) | Sphincter pupillae contraction | Reduced retinal illumination; difficulty with night vision ([[nyctalopia]]); dimming of vision |
| [[Retinal detachment]] | Traction on the [[vitreous base]] via ciliary muscle contraction | Increased risk in patients with pre-existing [[peripheral retinal degeneration]], [[lattice degeneration]], or high [[myopia]] |
| [[Anterior chamber shallowing]] | Forward displacement of the [[lens-iris diaphragm]] | May paradoxically worsen angle closure in susceptible individuals |
| [[Conjunctival injection]] and irritation | Local vasodilation and direct tissue irritation | Chronic use may lead to [[follicular conjunctivitis]] or [[contact dermatitis]] |
| [[Posterior synechiae]] | Prolonged miosis in inflamed eyes | Risk in patients with concurrent [[uveitis]] |
| [[Iris cysts]] | Chronic use of anticholinesterase agents | Particularly with [[echothiophate iodide]]; may be prevented by concurrent use of [[phenylephrine]] 2.5% |
| [[Cataract]] formation | Chronic use of anticholinesterase agents | [[Anterior subcapsular cataracts]] reported with long-term echothiophate use |
| [[Corneal toxicity]] | Preservative-related or direct drug effect | Chronic use may contribute to [[epithelial toxicity]] |

### Systemic Side Effects

Because miotics are [[parasympathomimetic]] agents, systemic absorption (primarily through the [[nasolacrimal drainage system]] and [[nasal mucosa]]) can produce systemic cholinergic effects:

| Systemic Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| [[Bradycardia]] | Vagal stimulation of the [[sinoatrial node]] |
| [[Hypotension]] | Peripheral vasodilation |
| [[Bronchospasm]] | Smooth muscle contraction in [[bronchioles]] |
| [[Increased salivation]] | Stimulation of [[salivary glands]] |
| [[Increased lacrimation]] | Stimulation of [[lacrimal gland]] |
| [[Nausea]], [[vomiting]], [[diarrhea]] | Increased gastrointestinal motility and secretion |
| [[Diaphoresis]] | Stimulation of [[sweat glands]] |
| [[Urinary urgency]] | Detrusor muscle contraction |

Systemic side effects are more common and more severe with indirect-acting (anticholinesterase) miotics, particularly the irreversible agents. [[Punctal occlusion]] and [[eyelid closure]] for 2–3 minutes after instillation can significantly reduce systemic absorption.

### Critical Drug Interaction: Succinylcholine

Patients using irreversible anticholinesterase miotics (e.g., [[echothiophate iodide]]) have depleted levels of [[pseudocholinesterase]] (plasma cholinesterase). Since [[succinylcholine]], a depolarizing [[neuromuscular blocking agent]] commonly used during [[general anesthesia]], is metabolized by pseudocholinesterase, these patients are at risk for **prolonged neuromuscular blockade** and **respiratory paralysis** if succinylcholine is administered. It is imperative that echothiophate iodide be discontinued at least **2–4 weeks** prior to any surgical procedure requiring general anesthesia with succinylcholine, or that the anesthesiologist be informed so that alternative non-depolarizing agents can be used.

## Diagnosis and Clinical Assessment

The term "diagnosis" in the context of miotics pertains to two clinical scenarios: (1) identifying the appropriate clinical indication for miotic therapy, and (2) recognizing and diagnosing the adverse effects of miotic use.

### Pre-Treatment Assessment

Before initiating miotic therapy, a comprehensive ophthalmic examination should include:

1. **[[Visual acuity]]**: Baseline best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA) at distance and near
2. **[[Refraction]]**: Document baseline refractive error, as miotics will induce a myopic shift
3. **[[Pupil examination]]**: Assess baseline pupil size, reactivity, and the presence of an [[afferent pupillary defect]]
4. **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: Evaluate the [[anterior segment]], including the [[cornea]], [[anterior chamber]] depth, [[iris]] architecture, and [[crystalline lens]] for pre-existing [[cataract]]
5. **[[Gonioscopy]]**: Assess the [[iridocorneal angle]] configuration; critical for determining the appropriateness of miotic therapy and for monitoring angle changes
6. **[[Intraocular pressure]] measurement**: Baseline [[tonometry]] (e.g., [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]])
7. **[[Dilated fundus examination]]**: Thorough evaluation of the [[peripheral retina]] to identify [[lattice degeneration]], [[retinal holes]], [[retinal tears]], or other predisposing lesions for [[retinal detachment]]; this is essential before initiating miotic therapy given the increased risk of tractional retinal detachment
8. **[[Optic nerve]] assessment**: [[Cup-to-disc ratio]], [[neuroretinal rim]] evaluation, and documentation with [[fundus photography]] or [[optical coherence tomography]] (OCT) of the [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL)
9. **[[Visual field]] testing**: Baseline [[perimetry]] (e.g., [[Humphrey visual field]]) for glaucoma patients

### Monitoring During Therapy

Patients on chronic miotic therapy require regular follow-up including:

- **IOP measurement** at each visit to assess therapeutic efficacy
- **Periodic gonioscopy** to monitor angle configuration
- **Dilated fundus examination** at least annually (or more frequently if symptomatic) to monitor for [[retinal detachment]] or [[peripheral retinal changes]]
- **Lens evaluation** for development of [[anterior subcapsular cataracts]], particularly with anticholinesterase agents
- **Assessment for iris cysts**, especially in pediatric patients on echothiophate iodide
- **Visual field testing** to monitor glaucoma progression and to differentiate true field loss from artifactual constriction caused by miosis

### Recognizing Miotic-Induced Adverse Effects

Clinicians must maintain a high index of suspicion for miotic-related complications:

- **Sudden onset of [[floaters]], [[photopsia]], or [[visual field curtain]]**: Warrants urgent evaluation for [[retinal detachment]]
- **Progressive dimming of vision or difficulty with night driving**: Related to chronic miosis and reduced retinal illumination
- **Brow ache or headache**: Particularly in the first 2–4 weeks of therapy; usually self-limiting
- **Paradoxical angle narrowing**: Anterior rotation of the ciliary body and forward lens movement may worsen angle closure in [[plateau iris]] configuration

## Treatment and Therapeutic Protocols

### Glaucoma Management with Miotics

#### Pilocarpine in Open-Angle Glaucoma

- **Formulation**: Pilocarpine hydrochloride 0.5%–4% ophthalmic solution; [[pilocarpine]] 4% gel (Pilopine HS®) for once-nightly dosing
- **Dosing**: Typically 1 drop, 2–4 times daily for solutions; once at bedtime for gel formulation
- **Expected IOP reduction**: 15%–25% from baseline
- **Onset**: 10–30 minutes
- **Peak effect**: 30–120 minutes
- **Duration**: 4–8 hours (solution); 18–24 hours (gel)

The gel formulation offers the advantage of once-daily dosing and sustained drug delivery, improving [[patient compliance]]. However, the gel may cause more prolonged blurring of vision.

#### Pilocarpine in Angle-Closure Glaucoma

In acute [[primary angle-closure crisis]]:

1. Initiate systemic IOP-lowering therapy (e.g., intravenous [[mannitol]], oral [[acetazolamide]])
2. Topical IOP-lowering agents ([[timolol]] 0.5%, [[apraclonidine]] 1%)
3. Once IOP begins to decrease: **Pilocarpine 1%–2%**, one drop every 15 minutes for 2–3 doses, then every 4–6 hours
4. Definitive treatment: [[Laser peripheral iridotomy]] (LPI)

**Note**: Higher concentrations of pilocarpine (e.g., 4%–6%) are generally avoided in acute angle closure as they may paradoxically worsen the condition by increasing the anterior-posterior lens thickness and promoting further [[pupillary block]].

#### Pilocarpine Ocular Insert (Ocusert®)

The [[Ocusert]] system was a membrane-controlled drug delivery device placed in the [[conjunctival cul-de-sac]] that released pilocarpine at a constant rate (20 µg/hr or 40 µg/hr) over 7 days. While it offered superior pharmacokinetic control and reduced side effects compared to topical drops, it has been discontinued from the market. It remains historically significant as one of the earliest [[controlled-release ophthalmic drug delivery systems]].

### Accommodative Esotropia Management

- **[[Echothiophate iodide]] 0.03%–0.125%**: One drop in each eye once daily or every other day, typically at bedtime to minimize daytime visual side effects
- **Concurrent [[phenylephrine]] 2.5%**: May be instilled to prevent formation of [[iris cysts]]
- **Duration**: Treatment may continue for months to years; periodic attempts to taper should be made
- **Monitoring**: Regular assessment of [[ocular alignment]], [[accommodative function]], and [[anterior segment]] examination for cataract and iris cyst formation

### Presbyopia Management

- **[[Pilocarpine]] 1.25% (Vuity™)**: One drop in each eye once daily
- **Mechanism**: Creates a small-aperture (pinhole) pupil, increasing depth of focus
- **Expected benefit**: Improvement of approximately 3 lines of near visual acuity
- **Duration of effect**: Approximately 6 hours
- **Considerations**: Patients should be counseled about reduced vision in dim lighting conditions and the potential for a transient myopic shift

### Intraoperative Miosis

| Agent | Route | Dose | Onset | Duration | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| [[Acetylcholine]] 1% (Miochol-E) | Intracameral | 0.5–2.0 mL | Seconds | ~10 min | Must be reconstituted immediately before use; rapidly hydrolyzed |
| [[Carbachol]] 0.01% (Miostat) | Intracameral | 0.5 mL | 2–5 min | ~24 hours | More sustained effect; resistant to cholinesterase |

## Pharmacokinetics and Formulation Considerations

### Corneal Penetration

Miotics must penetrate the [[corneal epithelium]] to reach the [[anterior chamber]]. [[Pilocarpine]], being a tertiary amine, exists in both ionized and non-ionized forms at physiological pH, allowing reasonable penetration through the lipophilic [[corneal epithelium]] and the hydrophilic [[corneal stroma]]. [[Carbachol]], a quaternary ammonium compound, has poor corneal penetration and requires the addition of a wetting agent such as [[benzalkonium chloride]] to enhance transcorneal absorption when used topically.

### Melanin Binding

Pilocarpine binds to [[melanin]] in the [[iris pigment epithelium]]. In patients with heavily pigmented (dark brown) irides, a higher concentration or more frequent dosing may be required to achieve the desired therapeutic effect, as melanin binding creates a drug reservoir that delays onset and reduces peak concentration at the target tissues.

### Preservatives and Ocular Surface Health

Chronic use of preserved miotic formulations (containing [[benzalkonium chloride]] or other preservatives) may contribute to [[ocular surface disease]], including [[dry eye syndrome]], [[punctate epithelial keratopathy]], and [[meibomian gland dysfunction]]. Preservative-free formulations, when available, are preferred for long-term use.

## Contraindications and Precautions

| Contraindication | Rationale |
|---|---|
| [[Uveitis]] or active intraocular inflammation | Risk of [[posterior synechiae]] formation and exacerbation of inflammation |
| [[Neovascular glaucoma]] | Miotics are ineffective and may worsen inflammation |
| Known hypersensitivity to the agent | [[Allergic conjunctivitis]], [[contact dermatitis]] |
| [[Retinal detachment]] or high risk thereof | Ciliary muscle contraction increases vitreoretinal traction |
| [[Pupillary block]] with plateau iris (relative) | May paradoxically worsen angle closure |
| [[Asthma]] or severe [[COPD]] (relative) | Risk of systemic bronchospasm |
| Concurrent use of [[succinylcholine]] (for anticholinesterases) | Risk of prolonged respiratory paralysis |
| Pregnancy (Category C) | Potential systemic cholinergic effects on the fetus |

## Historical Perspective

Miotics hold a distinguished place in the history of [[ophthalmic pharmacology]]. The [[Calabar bean]] (*Physostigma venenosum*), native to West Africa, was the source of [[physostigmine]] (eserine), one of the earliest known miotics. In 1862, Thomas Richard Fraser described the miotic properties of the Calabar bean extract. In 1876, Ludwig Laqueur demonstrated the IOP-lowering effect of physostigmine, marking the beginning of the pharmacological treatment of [[glaucoma]].

[[Pilocarpine]], derived from the leaves of the South American shrub *Pilocarpus jaborandi*, was introduced into ophthalmic practice in 1876 by Adolf Weber. For over a century, pilocarpine remained the mainstay of glaucoma therapy until the introduction of [[timolol]] in 1978 and subsequently [[latanoprost]] in 1996.

The development of irreversible anticholinesterase agents such as [[echothiophate iodide]] in the mid-20th century expanded the therapeutic applications of miotics to include [[accommodative esotropia]] and refractory glaucoma, though their use has been limited by significant side effects.

---

## Summary

Miotics remain an important, though less frequently prescribed, class of ophthalmic medications. Their unique mechanism of action—enhancing trabecular outflow through ciliary muscle contraction and inducing miosis—provides therapeutic benefits that are not replicated by other classes of [[anti-glaucoma agents]]. The recent FDA approval of low-dose pilocarpine for [[presbyopia]] has renewed interest in this venerable drug class. Clinicians must be thoroughly familiar with the pharmacology, indications, contraindications, and adverse effect profiles of miotics to optimize patient outcomes and minimize complications.
