

# Pachymetry

---

**Pachymetry** (from the Greek *pachys*, meaning "thick," and *metron*, meaning "measure") is the clinical measurement of [[corneal thickness]], a fundamental diagnostic parameter in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]]. Corneal pachymetry has become an indispensable component of comprehensive eye examinations, playing a critical role in the accurate assessment of [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP), the screening and monitoring of [[corneal ectatic disorders]], preoperative evaluation for [[refractive surgery]], and the management of [[corneal edema]] and [[endothelial dysfunction]]. The measurement is typically expressed in micrometers (μm), with the average central corneal thickness (CCT) in the adult human population ranging from approximately 530 to 560 μm, though significant variation exists across ethnicities, age groups, and clinical conditions.

---

## Historical Background

The concept of measuring corneal thickness dates to the mid-20th century. Early attempts utilized optical methods derived from [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]], where clinicians estimated corneal thickness by observing the optical section of the [[cornea]]. In 1951, Maurice and Giardini introduced one of the first quantitative optical pachymetry techniques. The development of [[ultrasound pachymetry]] in the 1970s and 1980s revolutionized the field by providing a contact-based, highly reproducible method for measuring CCT. The introduction of [[optical coherence tomography]] (OCT) and [[Scheimpflug imaging]] in the late 1990s and 2000s further expanded the armamentarium, offering non-contact, high-resolution, full-corneal-thickness mapping capabilities. The landmark [[Ocular Hypertension Treatment Study]] (OHTS), published in 2002, cemented the clinical importance of pachymetry by demonstrating that CCT is an independent risk factor for the development of [[primary open-angle glaucoma]] (POAG), fundamentally changing how clinicians interpret IOP measurements.

## Anatomy and Physiology of Corneal Thickness

### Corneal Structure

The [[cornea]] is a transparent, avascular tissue that constitutes the anterior one-sixth of the [[globe]]. It is composed of five distinct layers, each contributing to its overall thickness:

| Layer | Approximate Thickness | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| [[Corneal epithelium]] | 50–55 μm | Stratified squamous, 5–7 cell layers, rapid turnover |
| [[Bowman's layer]] | 8–14 μm | Acellular condensation of anterior [[stroma]] |
| [[Corneal stroma]] | ~470 μm (≈90% of total) | Organized [[collagen]] lamellae, [[keratocytes]], [[glycosaminoglycans]] |
| [[Descemet's membrane]] | 3–12 μm (thickens with age) | Basement membrane of [[endothelium]], composed of [[type IV collagen]] |
| [[Corneal endothelium]] | ~5 μm | Single layer of hexagonal cells, critical pump function |

### Physiological Regulation of Corneal Thickness

Corneal thickness is maintained through a dynamic equilibrium between the passive imbibition of [[aqueous humor]] into the [[stroma]] (driven by the swelling pressure of stromal [[glycosaminoglycans]], primarily [[keratan sulfate]] and [[chondroitin sulfate]]) and the active deturgescing function of the [[corneal endothelium]]. The endothelial pump, primarily mediated by [[Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase]] and [[bicarbonate ion transport]], actively removes fluid from the stroma, maintaining the cornea in a state of relative dehydration (approximately 78% water content) essential for [[corneal transparency]].

The [[corneal epithelium]] also serves as a barrier to fluid influx from the [[tear film]], contributing to the maintenance of stromal hydration. Any disruption to the endothelial pump mechanism, epithelial barrier function, or stromal architecture can result in alterations in corneal thickness, which pachymetry is designed to detect and quantify.

### Normal Corneal Thickness Distribution

The cornea is not uniformly thick. It is thinnest at or near its center and progressively thickens toward the [[limbus]]:

| Region | Approximate Thickness |
|---|---|
| Central cornea | 530–560 μm |
| Paracentral cornea | 560–600 μm |
| Mid-peripheral cornea | 600–650 μm |
| Peripheral cornea (near limbus) | 650–700+ μm |

This physiological thickness gradient is clinically significant, particularly in the evaluation of [[keratoconus]] and other [[ectatic disorders]], where focal thinning deviates from the expected profile.

## Pathophysiology: Conditions Affecting Corneal Thickness

Pachymetry is not a diagnostic endpoint in itself but rather a critical measurement that reflects underlying physiological or pathological processes. Understanding the pathophysiology of conditions that alter corneal thickness is essential for the proper clinical interpretation of pachymetric data.

### Corneal Thinning

Pathological corneal thinning is the hallmark of the [[corneal ectasias]], a group of progressive, non-inflammatory (or minimally inflammatory) disorders characterized by structural weakening and protrusion of the cornea.

- **[[Keratoconus]]**: The most common corneal ectasia, characterized by progressive stromal thinning, typically inferotemporal, leading to conical protrusion. Pachymetry reveals focal thinning, often below 500 μm at the cone apex, and is essential for staging the disease (e.g., using the [[Amsler-Krumeich classification]]). The thinnest corneal point is often displaced from the geometric center. The pathophysiology involves degradation of stromal [[collagen]] cross-links, increased activity of [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs), oxidative stress, and [[keratocyte]] [[apoptosis]].

- **[[Pellucid marginal degeneration]]**: A bilateral, non-inflammatory ectasia characterized by a band of inferior corneal thinning, typically 1–2 mm from the [[limbus]], with the area of maximal protrusion located above the zone of maximal thinning. Pachymetric mapping is crucial for differentiating this condition from keratoconus.

- **[[Keratoglobus]]**: A rare ectasia involving generalized, diffuse corneal thinning (often to 200–300 μm) with globular protrusion. Pachymetry demonstrates uniformly thin cornea from limbus to limbus.

- **[[Post-LASIK ectasia]]**: Iatrogenic ectasia occurring after [[laser in situ keratomileusis]] (LASIK), typically resulting from excessive tissue ablation leaving an inadequate residual stromal bed (generally considered to be less than 250–300 μm). Preoperative pachymetry is the primary tool for preventing this devastating complication.

- **[[Terrien's marginal degeneration]]**: A slowly progressive, peripheral corneal thinning disorder, typically superior, with lipid deposition at the leading edge. Pachymetry can document the extent and progression of thinning.

### Corneal Thickening (Edema)

Increased corneal thickness detected by pachymetry is most commonly indicative of [[corneal edema]], reflecting a failure of the endothelial pump mechanism or disruption of the epithelial barrier.

- **[[Fuchs' endothelial corneal dystrophy]]**: The most common cause of endothelial failure, characterized by progressive loss of [[endothelial cells]] and the formation of [[guttae]] (excrescences on [[Descemet's membrane]]). Pachymetry reveals progressive corneal thickening, often exceeding 600–700 μm, and is used to monitor disease progression. Diurnal variation in pachymetry (thicker upon waking due to nocturnal hypoxia-induced edema, thinning throughout the day) is a classic early finding.

- **[[Pseudophakic bullous keratopathy]]**: Endothelial decompensation following [[cataract surgery]] or [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) implantation, leading to chronic corneal edema. Pachymetry documents the degree of edema.

- **[[Iridocorneal endothelial (ICE) syndrome]]**: A spectrum of disorders involving abnormal endothelial cell proliferation, leading to corneal edema, [[peripheral anterior synechiae]], and [[iris]] abnormalities. Pachymetry aids in assessing the corneal component.

- **[[Contact lens]]-induced edema**: Prolonged wear of contact lenses, particularly low-[[oxygen permeability]] (Dk/t) lenses, can cause chronic corneal hypoxia, leading to stromal and epithelial edema detectable by pachymetry. Overnight swelling of 2–4% is considered physiological, but values exceeding this suggest hypoxic stress.

- **Acute [[hydrops]]**: In advanced [[keratoconus]], a break in [[Descemet's membrane]] allows aqueous humor to flood the stroma, causing acute, dramatic corneal thickening and opacification. Pachymetry in this setting reveals markedly increased thickness at the site of the break.

### Physiological and Demographic Variations

Corneal thickness is influenced by numerous non-pathological factors that must be considered in clinical interpretation:

| Factor | Effect on CCT |
|---|---|
| **Ethnicity** | African Americans tend to have thinner CCT (~521 μm) compared to Caucasians (~550 μm) and Hispanics (~548 μm). Asian populations show variable results. |
| **Age** | Slight decrease with age (approximately 0.3 μm/year after age 20), though some studies show minimal change. |
| **Diurnal variation** | CCT is thickest upon waking (due to overnight lid closure and relative hypoxia) and thins by 5–10 μm over the first few hours of the day. |
| **Sex** | Some studies suggest males have slightly thicker corneas; others show no significant difference. |
| **Diabetes mellitus** | [[Diabetes]] is associated with increased CCT, possibly due to endothelial dysfunction and altered stromal hydration. |
| **Pregnancy** | Hormonal changes can increase CCT transiently. |
| **Altitude** | High altitude may be associated with decreased CCT due to lower atmospheric pressure and reduced corneal hydration. |

## Clinical Significance and Indications

### Pachymetry and Intraocular Pressure Measurement

Perhaps the most clinically impactful application of pachymetry is its role in the interpretation of [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) measurements obtained by [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]] (GAT), the current clinical gold standard for IOP measurement. GAT was calibrated based on an assumed average CCT of 520 μm. Corneas thicker than this value tend to yield artificially elevated IOP readings (overestimation), while thinner corneas tend to yield artificially low IOP readings (underestimation).

The [[Ocular Hypertension Treatment Study]] (OHTS) demonstrated that a thinner CCT is an independent and powerful risk factor for the conversion from [[ocular hypertension]] to [[primary open-angle glaucoma]] (POAG). Specifically, participants with a CCT ≤ 555 μm had a three-fold greater risk of developing glaucoma compared to those with a CCT > 588 μm. This finding has had profound implications for [[glaucoma]] screening and management.

**Approximate IOP Correction Factors for CCT (Goldmann Applanation Tonometry):**

| CCT (μm) | Approximate IOP Adjustment |
|---|---|
| 445 | Subtract ~7 mmHg |
| 475 | Subtract ~5 mmHg |
| 505 | Subtract ~3 mmHg |
| 520 | No adjustment (calibration standard) |
| 535 | Add ~1 mmHg |
| 555 | Add ~2–3 mmHg |
| 585 | Add ~4–5 mmHg |
| 615 | Add ~6–7 mmHg |

> **Clinical Note:** These correction factors are approximations and vary across published algorithms (e.g., Ehlers, Doughty-Zaman, Kohlhaas). No single correction algorithm has been universally validated, and many glaucoma specialists prefer to use CCT as a risk factor rather than applying a direct arithmetic correction to IOP. The relationship between CCT and IOP measurement error is not purely linear and is also influenced by [[corneal biomechanical properties]] such as [[corneal hysteresis]] (CH) and [[corneal resistance factor]] (CRF), as measured by the [[Ocular Response Analyzer]] (ORA) or [[Corvis ST]].

### Pachymetry in Refractive Surgery

Pachymetry is an absolute prerequisite for preoperative evaluation in all forms of [[corneal refractive surgery]]:

- **[[LASIK]]**: The residual stromal bed (RSB) after flap creation and laser ablation must be ≥ 250 μm (many surgeons prefer ≥ 300 μm) to minimize the risk of [[post-LASIK ectasia]]. Preoperative CCT, combined with planned ablation depth and flap thickness, determines surgical candidacy.
- **[[Photorefractive keratectomy]] (PRK)**: Although no flap is created, adequate stromal thickness must remain after surface ablation.
- **[[Small incision lenticule extraction]] (SMILE)**: Pachymetry guides the planning of lenticule thickness and cap thickness.
- **[[Implantable collamer lens]] (ICL)**: While ICL does not alter corneal thickness, preoperative pachymetry is part of the comprehensive anterior segment evaluation.

### Pachymetry in Corneal Ectasia Screening and Monitoring

Pachymetric mapping, particularly when combined with [[corneal topography]] and [[tomography]], is essential for:

- Early detection of [[keratoconus]] and [[forme fruste keratoconus]]
- Monitoring progression of ectatic disease
- Evaluating candidacy for [[corneal collagen cross-linking]] (CXL), which typically requires a minimum corneal thickness of 400 μm at the thinnest point (to prevent endothelial damage from [[ultraviolet-A]] radiation)
- Differentiating between various ectatic disorders based on the pattern and location of thinning

### Pachymetry in Corneal Transplantation

Pachymetry is used extensively in the context of [[keratoplasty]]:

- **Preoperative assessment**: Documenting the degree of corneal edema or thinning in the recipient cornea.
- **Donor tissue evaluation**: [[Eye bank]] assessment of donor corneal thickness as a marker of endothelial viability. Excessively swollen donor tissue may indicate poor endothelial function.
- **Postoperative monitoring**: Tracking graft thickness after [[penetrating keratoplasty]] (PK), [[Descemet's stripping automated endothelial keratoplasty]] (DSAEK), or [[Descemet's membrane endothelial keratoplasty]] (DMEK) to assess graft function and detect early signs of [[graft rejection]] or failure.

## Diagnostic Modalities: Instruments and Techniques

Multiple technologies are available for performing pachymetry, each with distinct advantages, limitations, and clinical applications.

### 1. Ultrasound Pachymetry (Contact Method)

**Principle:** A handheld probe emitting high-frequency [[ultrasound]] waves (typically 20 MHz) is placed perpendicular to the corneal surface after instillation of a topical [[anesthetic]]. The probe measures the time required for the ultrasound pulse to travel from the [[corneal epithelium]] to the [[corneal endothelium]] and return, calculating thickness based on the speed of sound in corneal tissue (approximately 1,640 m/s).

**Advantages:**
- Considered the historical clinical gold standard
- Highly reproducible with proper technique
- Relatively inexpensive and portable
- Provides a single-point measurement (typically central)

**Limitations:**
- Requires corneal contact (risk of [[epithelial abrasion]], infection, patient discomfort)
- Requires topical anesthesia
- Operator-dependent (probe alignment, applanation force)
- Provides only single-point measurements; does not generate a thickness map
- Slight corneal compression by the probe may underestimate true thickness
- Epithelial displacement by the probe tip can introduce measurement variability

**Clinical Pearls:**
- The probe must be held perpendicular to the corneal surface; oblique placement artificially increases the measured thickness.
- Multiple readings (typically 5–10) should be obtained and averaged.
- Measurements should ideally be taken at the same time of day to minimize the effect of [[diurnal variation]].

### 2. Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

**Principle:** [[Optical coherence tomography]] uses low-coherence [[interferometry]] to generate high-resolution, cross-sectional images of the cornea. Both [[time-domain OCT]] (TD-OCT) and [[spectral-domain OCT]] (SD-OCT), as well as [[swept-source OCT]] (SS-OCT), can measure corneal thickness with axial resolutions of 3–7 μm.

**Subtypes:**
- **Anterior segment OCT (AS-OCT)**: Dedicated instruments (e.g., Zeiss Visante, Tomey CASIA) or anterior segment modules on retinal OCT platforms provide detailed corneal cross-sections and pachymetric maps.
- **Spectral-domain OCT**: Instruments such as the [[Heidelberg Spectralis]], [[Zeiss Cirrus]], and [[Optovue Avanti]] can generate high-resolution corneal thickness measurements.

**Advantages:**
- Non-contact (no anesthesia required, no risk of epithelial disruption)
- High resolution and reproducibility
- Generates full-corneal pachymetric maps
- Can separately measure individual corneal layers (epithelial thickness mapping)
- Rapid acquisition

**Limitations:**
- More expensive than ultrasound pachymetry
- Measurements may differ slightly from ultrasound values (OCT typically reads slightly thinner, approximately 10–20 μm less, due to differences in the defined measurement boundaries)
- Requires patient cooperation and fixation

### 3. Scheimpflug Imaging

**Principle:** [[Scheimpflug imaging]] (e.g., [[Pentacam]], [[Galilei]], [[Sirius]]) uses a rotating Scheimpflug camera to capture cross-sectional images of the entire anterior segment. The Scheimpflug principle allows the camera to image a tilted plane in sharp focus, providing detailed images from the anterior corneal surface to the posterior [[crystalline lens]].

**Advantages:**
- Non-contact
- Generates comprehensive pachymetric maps of the entire cornea
- Provides corneal topography, tomography, anterior chamber depth, and lens density simultaneously
- Identifies the thinnest corneal point and its location (critical for ectasia screening)
- Highly reproducible

**Limitations:**
- Expensive
- Measurements may be affected by significant corneal opacities or scarring
- Slight systematic differences compared to ultrasound pachymetry (Scheimpflug values may be slightly higher or lower depending on the instrument and software version)

**Key Pachymetric Parameters from Scheimpflug Imaging:**

| Parameter | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|
| Central corneal thickness (CCT) | IOP correction, refractive surgery planning |
| Thinnest corneal thickness (TCT) | Ectasia screening, CXL candidacy |
| Thinnest point location | Displacement from center suggests ectasia |
| Pachymetric progression index (PPI) | Rate of thickness change from thinnest point to periphery; abnormal in ectasia |
| Percentage thickness increase (PTI) | Corneal thickness spatial profile; abnormal patterns suggest subclinical ectasia |
| Belin/Ambrósio Enhanced Ectasia Display (BAD-D) | Composite index incorporating pachymetric and elevation data for ectasia screening |

### 4. Slit-Lamp Optical Pachymetry

**Principle:** The earliest form of pachymetry, using the optical section of the [[slit-lamp]] to estimate corneal thickness. A calibrated graticule or attachment (e.g., Haag-Streit optical pachymeter) is used to measure the width of the corneal optical section.

**Advantages:**
- Non-contact
- Inexpensive (uses existing slit-lamp)

**Limitations:**
- Low accuracy and reproducibility compared to modern methods
- Operator-dependent
- Largely obsolete for clinical pachymetry but retains historical significance

### 5. Specular Microscopy

**Principle:** [[Specular microscopy]] primarily evaluates [[corneal endothelial cell]] density, morphology ([[polymegethism]], [[pleomorphism]]), and function, but many modern specular microscopes also provide a pachymetric measurement as part of the examination.

**Advantages:**
- Provides endothelial data alongside thickness measurement
- Non-contact (most modern instruments)

**Limitations:**
- Pachymetric measurement is secondary to the primary function
- Less accurate for pachymetry than dedicated instruments

### 6. Corneal Biomechanical Analyzers

**Principle:** Instruments such as the [[Ocular Response Analyzer]] (ORA) and [[Corvis ST]] (a non-contact tonometer integrated with Scheimpflug imaging) measure corneal biomechanical properties ([[corneal hysteresis]], [[corneal resistance factor]]) and also provide pachymetric data. The Corvis ST, in particular, provides dynamic Scheimpflug imaging of the corneal deformation response to an air puff, along with CCT measurement.

**Advantages:**
- Provides biomechanical data that complements pachymetric information
- The [[Tomographic and Biomechanical Index]] (TBI) from the Corvis ST integrates pachymetric, tomographic, and biomechanical data for enhanced ectasia screening

**Limitations:**
- Expensive
- Primarily designed for biomechanical assessment; pachymetry is a secondary output

### Comparison of Pachymetric Instruments

| Feature | Ultrasound | AS-OCT | Scheimpflug | Slit-Lamp Optical |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Contact | Yes | No | No | No |
| Anesthesia required | Yes | No | No | No |
| Resolution | Moderate | Very high | High | Low |
| Pachymetric map | No (single point) | Yes | Yes | No |
| Epithelial mapping | No | Yes (some) | Limited | No |
| Reproducibility | High | Very high | Very high | Low |
| Cost | Low | High | High | Very low |
| Speed | Fast | Fast | Fast | Slow |

## Clinical Protocol and Technique

### Standard Procedure for Ultrasound Pachymetry

1. **Patient preparation**: Explain the procedure. Seat the patient comfortably at the slit-lamp or in a reclined position.
2. **Instill topical anesthetic**: One drop of [[proparacaine]] 0.5% or [[tetracaine]] 0.5% in each eye.
3. **Calibration**: Ensure the pachymeter is calibrated according to the manufacturer's specifications.
4. **Probe placement**: Gently place the probe tip perpendicular to the central corneal surface, avoiding excessive pressure. The probe should lightly touch the cornea without indentation.
5. **Acquire readings**: Obtain a minimum of 5 (preferably 10) consecutive readings. Discard outliers.
6. **Calculate average**: Record the mean value and standard deviation. A standard deviation > 5–10 μm suggests inconsistent probe placement.
7. **Repeat for the fellow eye**.
8. **Document**: Record the values, instrument used, time of day, and any relevant clinical context.

### Standard Procedure for Non-Contact Pachymetry (OCT/Scheimpflug)

1. **Patient preparation**: Explain the procedure. No anesthesia is required.
2. **Positioning**: Align the patient at the instrument chin rest.
3. **Fixation**: Instruct the patient to fixate on the internal target.
4. **Acquisition**: The instrument automatically acquires the scan. Ensure adequate image quality (check for artifacts, incomplete scans, or misalignment).
5. **Review**: Examine the pachymetric map, thinnest point location, and any ectasia screening indices.
6. **Document**: Record all relevant parameters.

## Interpretation of Pachymetric Data

### Normal Values

- **Central corneal thickness (CCT)**: 530–560 μm (population mean approximately 545 μm, though this varies by ethnicity and study)
- **Thinnest corneal thickness (TCT)**: Typically 5–20 μm thinner than CCT, located slightly inferotemporal to the corneal apex

### Abnormal Findings

| Finding | Possible Clinical Significance |
|---|---|
| CCT < 500 μm | Suspect [[keratoconus]], other ectasia, or naturally thin cornea; IOP may be underestimated |
| CCT > 600 μm | Possible [[corneal edema]], [[Fuchs' dystrophy]], or naturally thick cornea; IOP may be overestimated |
| Focal thinning (inferotemporal) | Highly suggestive of [[keratoconus]] |
| Inferior band of thinning near limbus | Suggestive of [[pellucid marginal degeneration]] |
| Diffuse thinning | Consider [[keratoglobus]] |
| Asymmetric CCT between eyes (>30 μm difference) | May indicate unilateral or asymmetric ectasia |
| Progressive thinning over time | Ectasia progression; consider [[corneal collagen cross-linking]] |
| Progressive thickening over time | Endothelial decompensation; monitor [[endothelial cell density]] |

### Epithelial Thickness Mapping

Advanced OCT platforms can separately map [[corneal epithelial thickness]], which provides additional diagnostic information:

- In early [[keratoconus]], the epithelium thins over the cone apex and thickens in the surrounding area, creating a "doughnut" pattern. This compensatory epithelial remodeling can mask early stromal thinning on total pachymetry, making epithelial mapping a more sensitive early detector of ectasia.
- Normal central epithelial thickness is approximately 50–55 μm.
- A thin-over-thick pattern (thin epithelium centrally or inferiorly with thick epithelium superiorly) is suspicious for subclinical ectasia.

## Management Implications

Pachymetry itself is a diagnostic measurement, not a condition requiring treatment. However, the findings of pachymetry directly influence clinical management decisions across multiple domains:

### Glaucoma Management

- **Thin CCT (< 520 μm)**: Increases the risk profile for [[glaucoma]] development and progression. IOP may be underestimated by [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]]. Clinicians should have a lower threshold for initiating treatment or intensifying monitoring.
- **Thick CCT (> 580 μm)**: IOP may be overestimated. Patients with apparent [[ocular hypertension]] and thick corneas may be at lower risk than their IOP readings suggest, potentially avoiding unnecessary treatment.
- Pachymetry should be performed at least once in all patients being evaluated for [[glaucoma]] or [[ocular hypertension]] and should be considered in the context of other risk factors (e.g., [[optic disc]] appearance, [[visual field]] testing, [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] thickness, family history, age, ethnicity).

### Refractive Surgery Planning

- **Minimum CCT for LASIK**: Generally ≥ 500 μm (with adequate RSB after ablation)
- **Residual stromal bed**: ≥ 250 μm (preferably ≥ 300 μm)
- **Abnormal pachymetric patterns**: Contraindicate corneal refractive surgery due to ectasia risk
- **PRK may be preferred over LASIK** in patients with borderline CCT, as no flap is created, preserving more stromal tissue

### Corneal Collagen Cross-Linking (CXL)

- **Minimum corneal thickness at the thinnest point**: ≥ 400 μm for standard (Dresden) protocol CXL
- If the thinnest point is < 400 μm, modified protocols may be employed:
  - Hypotonic [[riboflavin]] solution to swell the cornea above 400 μm before UV-A exposure
  - [[Transepithelial CXL]] (epi-on) to preserve epithelial thickness
  - [[Contact lens]]-assisted CXL (CACXL)
  - [[Customized epithelial debridement]] techniques
- Intraoperative pachymetry (often with ultrasound) is performed after epithelial removal and riboflavin soaking to confirm adequate thickness before UV-A irradiation

### Corneal Transplantation

- Pachymetry guides the decision between [[penetrating keratoplasty]] (PK) and [[endothelial keratoplasty]] (EK) techniques:
  - Isolated endothelial dysfunction with clear stroma → [[DSAEK]] or [[DMEK]]
  - Full-thickness corneal pathology → PK
- Postoperative pachymetry monitors graft function:
  - Increasing thickness suggests endothelial failure or [[graft rejection]]
  - Stable or decreasing thickness (toward normal) indicates good graft function

### Pharmacological Considerations

While no medications directly target corneal thickness as a primary endpoint, several pharmacological agents affect pachymetric measurements:

| Agent | Effect on CCT | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| [[Hypertonic saline]] (5% NaCl) drops/ointment | Decreases CCT | Osmotic dehydration of edematous cornea |
| [[Prostaglandin analogs]] (e.g., [[latanoprost]]) | May slightly decrease CCT | Possible effect on corneal hydration; clinical significance debated |
| [[Rho kinase inhibitors]] (e.g., [[netarsudil]]) | May transiently increase CCT | Effect on endothelial cell function |
| Topical [[corticosteroids]] | May increase CCT | Possible effect on stromal hydration; also associated with IOP elevation |
| [[Riboflavin]] (in CXL) | Transiently increases CCT (with hypotonic formulation) | Osmotic swelling |

## Emerging Technologies and Future Directions

### Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning

[[Artificial intelligence]] (AI) algorithms are being developed to integrate pachymetric data with topographic, tomographic, and biomechanical parameters for enhanced ectasia screening. Deep learning models trained on large datasets of Scheimpflug and OCT images show promise in detecting subclinical keratoconus with higher sensitivity and specificity than any single parameter alone.

### Brillouin Microscopy

[[Brillouin microscopy]] is an emerging non-contact, non-invasive technique that measures the biomechanical stiffness of the cornea at a microscopic level. When combined with pachymetric data, it may provide a more complete picture of corneal structural integrity than thickness alone.

### Very High-Frequency (VHF) Digital Ultrasound

[[VHF digital ultrasound]] (e.g., Artemis Insight 100) operates at frequencies of 35–50 MHz, providing extremely high-resolution imaging of individual corneal layers, including separate epithelial, stromal, and flap thickness measurements. This technology is particularly valuable in refractive surgery planning and post-LASIK evaluation.

### Intraoperative Real-Time Pachymetry

Integration of real-time OCT into surgical microscopes allows intraoperative pachymetric monitoring during procedures such as [[deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]] (DALK), CXL, and [[femtosecond laser]]-assisted surgery, improving safety and precision.

---

## Summary

Pachymetry is a cornerstone diagnostic measurement in modern [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]], with far-reaching implications for the management of [[glaucoma]], [[corneal ectasia]], [[refractive surgery]], [[corneal edema]], and [[corneal transplantation]]. The clinician must understand the principles underlying each measurement technology, the physiological and pathological factors that influence corneal thickness, and the appropriate clinical context for interpreting pachymetric data. As technology continues to advance, the integration of pachymetry with biomechanical assessment, AI-driven analytics, and high-resolution layer-by-layer imaging will further enhance its diagnostic power and clinical utility.
