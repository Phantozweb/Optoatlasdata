

# Divergence Insufficiency

---

**Divergence Insufficiency (DI)** is a relatively uncommon [[binocular vision]] disorder characterized by an acquired or decompensated [[esotropia]] that is greater at distance fixation than at near fixation, in the absence of [[lateral rectus]] muscle [[paresis]] or other identifiable neurological [[cranial nerve]] pathology. It represents a specific subtype of distance [[esodeviation]] that must be carefully differentiated from [[sixth nerve palsy]], [[convergence excess]], and other forms of [[strabismus]]. Divergence insufficiency has been a subject of considerable clinical debate regarding whether it constitutes a true primary entity involving a putative "divergence center" in the [[brainstem]], or whether it is merely a manifestation of subtle bilateral [[abducens nerve]] dysfunction below the threshold of clinical detection. This article provides a comprehensive clinical overview of divergence insufficiency for the practicing [[optometrist]] and [[ophthalmologist]], encompassing its pathophysiology, clinical presentation, diagnostic evaluation, differential diagnosis, and management strategies.

---

## Definition and Terminology

Divergence insufficiency is defined as a comitant or near-comitant [[esotropia]] that is significantly larger at distance fixation (typically ≥6 meters) compared to near fixation (typically 40 cm), with full or near-full [[ductions]] and [[versions]] on clinical examination. The term was first introduced in the ophthalmic literature in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, with early descriptions by **Duane** and **Bielschowsky**, who debated the existence of an active divergence mechanism versus a passive elastic model of the [[extraocular muscles]].

Key terminological distinctions include:

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Divergence Insufficiency** | Comitant esotropia greater at distance than near, with no identifiable lateral rectus paresis |
| **Divergence Paralysis** | An acute-onset variant of divergence insufficiency, often associated with underlying neurological disease |
| **[[Convergence Excess]]** | Esotropia greater at near than at distance, often associated with high [[accommodative convergence/accommodation ratio]] (AC/A ratio) |
| **[[Sixth Nerve Palsy]]** | Incomitant esotropia due to paresis of the [[abducens nerve]] (CN VI), with limited [[abduction]] |
| **Pseudo-divergence Insufficiency** | Distance esotropia mimicking DI but caused by subtle bilateral sixth nerve palsies or other neurological pathology |

The distinction between **divergence insufficiency** and **divergence paralysis** is clinically important. Divergence paralysis is typically acute in onset, may be associated with elevated [[intracranial pressure]], [[Chiari malformation]], [[demyelinating disease]], or other [[central nervous system]] pathology, and warrants urgent neuroimaging. Divergence insufficiency, by contrast, tends to be more insidious or chronic and may occur in the absence of identifiable neurological disease, particularly in the elderly population.

---

## Epidemiology

Divergence insufficiency is most commonly encountered in **older adults**, typically presenting in the sixth to eighth decades of life. However, it can occur at any age, including in children and young adults, particularly when associated with underlying neurological conditions. The exact prevalence is difficult to ascertain because many cases are mild and may go undiagnosed, and because the condition is frequently conflated with other forms of distance esotropia in epidemiological studies.

Key epidemiological observations include:

- **Age of onset**: Most commonly diagnosed in patients over 50 years of age
- **Sex distribution**: No consistent sex predilection has been established, though some series report a slight female predominance
- **Association with aging**: The increased prevalence in elderly patients has led to speculation that age-related changes in [[orbital connective tissue]], [[extraocular muscle]] elasticity, and [[brainstem]] function may contribute to the condition
- **Neurological associations**: A subset of patients presenting with acute divergence insufficiency/paralysis harbor significant intracranial pathology, including [[posterior fossa]] lesions, [[hydrocephalus]], and [[cerebrovascular disease]]

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of divergence insufficiency remains one of the most debated topics in [[neuro-ophthalmology]] and [[strabismology]]. The central question is whether an active **divergence mechanism** exists in the [[brainstem]], and if so, whether divergence insufficiency represents a primary failure of this mechanism.

### The Divergence Center Hypothesis

Early investigators, including **Bielschowsky**, proposed the existence of a supranuclear "divergence center" in the brainstem that actively drives the eyes apart for distance fixation. Under this model, divergence insufficiency would result from a lesion or dysfunction of this center, producing a comitant esotropia at distance without affecting the [[convergence]] system or the individual [[lateral rectus]] muscles.

Evidence supporting an active divergence mechanism includes:

- **Electrophysiological studies** in primates demonstrating neurons in the [[mesencephalic reticular formation]] and [[nucleus reticularis tegmenti pontis]] (NRTP) that fire specifically during divergence movements
- **Clinical observations** that some patients with divergence insufficiency have perfectly comitant deviations in all fields of gaze, which would be difficult to explain by bilateral sixth nerve dysfunction alone
- **Neuroimaging studies** occasionally revealing discrete [[brainstem]] lesions in patients with acute divergence paralysis

### The Bilateral Sixth Nerve Microparesis Hypothesis

An alternative and widely held view is that many or most cases of divergence insufficiency are actually caused by **subtle bilateral [[abducens nerve]] (CN VI) paresis** that is below the threshold of detection on standard clinical examination. Under this model, the lateral rectus muscles are mildly weakened bilaterally, producing a distance esotropia (because the demand on abduction is greatest at distance), while near alignment is preserved because [[convergence]] tone masks the deficit.

Evidence supporting this hypothesis includes:

- **Quantitative eye movement recordings** (e.g., [[scleral search coil]] studies) demonstrating mildly reduced abduction velocities in some patients diagnosed with divergence insufficiency
- **The frequent association** of acute divergence paralysis with conditions known to affect the sixth nerve, such as elevated [[intracranial pressure]], [[meningitis]], and [[posterior fossa]] tumors
- **The clinical difficulty** in reliably distinguishing comitant from mildly incomitant deviations on bedside examination alone

### Age-Related Mechanical Factors

In elderly patients without identifiable neurological disease, age-related changes in the [[orbit]] may contribute to divergence insufficiency:

- **Sagging eye syndrome**: Described by **Rutar and Demer**, this condition involves age-related degeneration of the [[lateral rectus]]-[[superior rectus]] band (LR-SR band), a connective tissue structure that stabilizes the extraocular muscle pulleys. Degeneration of this band allows the lateral rectus muscle to sag inferiorly, reducing its effective abducting force and producing a pattern of esotropia and/or [[hypotropia]] at distance. High-resolution [[orbital MRI]] has confirmed displacement of the LR pulley in affected patients.
- **Changes in orbital fat and connective tissue**: Generalized atrophy of [[orbital fat]] and weakening of the [[intermuscular septum]] and [[Tenon's capsule]] may alter the mechanical equilibrium of the extraocular muscles.

### Summary of Pathophysiological Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Description | Supporting Evidence |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Active divergence center dysfunction | Failure of a supranuclear brainstem divergence mechanism | Primate electrophysiology; discrete brainstem lesions in some patients |
| Bilateral sixth nerve microparesis | Subtle bilateral abducens nerve weakness below clinical detection threshold | Quantitative eye movement studies; association with raised ICP |
| Age-related orbital/mechanical changes | Degeneration of LR-SR band and orbital connective tissues (sagging eye syndrome) | High-resolution orbital MRI; histopathological studies |
| Decompensation of pre-existing [[esophoria]] | Breakdown of previously compensated fusional divergence reserves | History of longstanding esophoria; reduced [[fusional vergence]] amplitudes |

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

The hallmark symptom of divergence insufficiency is **horizontal [[diplopia]]** (double vision) at distance fixation, with single or near-single [[binocular vision]] at near. Patients typically describe:

- **Intermittent or constant horizontal diplopia** when viewing distant objects (e.g., driving, watching television, looking across a room)
- **Absence of diplopia at near** (reading, computer work, eating) — this is a critical distinguishing feature
- **Diplopia that may worsen with fatigue**, illness, or prolonged distance viewing
- **Asthenopia** (eye strain), particularly with sustained distance tasks
- **Difficulty with depth perception** at distance
- **Compensatory head posture**: Some patients may adopt a slight **face turn** to reduce the angle of deviation and achieve [[fusion]], though this is less common than in incomitant strabismus

The onset may be:
- **Acute**: Sudden onset of distance diplopia, raising concern for neurological pathology (divergence paralysis)
- **Gradual/insidious**: Slowly progressive distance diplopia, more typical of the benign/idiopathic form seen in elderly patients
- **Intermittent**: Diplopia that comes and goes, often related to fatigue or general health status

### Signs

On clinical examination, the following signs are characteristic of divergence insufficiency:

1. **Comitant esotropia at distance fixation**: The angle of [[esotropia]] is relatively equal in all horizontal and vertical gaze positions. Typical deviations range from **4 to 20 [[prism diopters]] (PD)** at distance.

2. **Orthophoria or small esophoria at near fixation**: The deviation at near is absent or significantly smaller than at distance. A difference of at least **10 PD** between distance and near measurements is often cited as a diagnostic criterion, though smaller differences may be clinically significant.

3. **Full ductions and versions**: There is no limitation of [[abduction]] (outward eye movement) on clinical examination. This is the critical feature distinguishing divergence insufficiency from [[sixth nerve palsy]].

4. **Normal saccadic velocities**: [[Saccades]] in all directions, including abducting saccades, should be of normal velocity and accuracy. Slowed abducting saccades suggest sixth nerve paresis.

5. **Reduced [[fusional divergence]] amplitudes**: Measurement of fusional vergence ranges at distance typically reveals reduced divergence (base-in) amplitudes.

6. **Normal [[accommodative]] function**: The [[accommodation]] system is typically unaffected, and the [[AC/A ratio]] is normal (distinguishing DI from convergence excess).

7. **Absence of [[nystagmus]]**: The presence of nystagmus, particularly [[abduction]] nystagmus, would suggest alternative diagnoses such as [[internuclear ophthalmoplegia]] or sixth nerve palsy.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination Protocol

A systematic approach to the diagnosis of divergence insufficiency includes the following steps:

#### 1. History

- **Onset and duration** of diplopia (acute vs. gradual)
- **Distance vs. near** characteristics of diplopia
- **Associated symptoms**: headache, nausea, vertigo, limb weakness, speech changes (to screen for neurological disease)
- **Past ocular history**: prior strabismus, amblyopia, strabismus surgery, or history of esophoria
- **Medical history**: hypertension, diabetes, cerebrovascular disease, demyelinating disease, head trauma
- **Medications**: Some medications may affect ocular motility

#### 2. Visual Acuity

- Best-corrected [[visual acuity]] at distance and near in each eye. Visual acuity should be normal unless there is concurrent ocular pathology.

#### 3. Refraction

- A careful [[cycloplegic refraction]] (or manifest refraction in adults) is essential to rule out uncorrected [[hyperopia]], which can cause [[accommodative esotropia]] and mimic divergence insufficiency. Even small amounts of uncorrected hyperopia can contribute to esotropia at distance.

#### 4. Cover Test

- **Unilateral [[cover test]]** and **alternating cover test** performed at both distance (6 meters) and near (40 cm)
- The hallmark finding is a significantly larger esotropia at distance than at near
- The deviation should be measured with [[prism bars]] or loose [[prisms]] using the **[[prism and alternate cover test]]** (PACT)

#### 5. Ocular Motility Assessment

- **Ductions**: Each eye tested individually in all positions of gaze. Must be full, with no limitation of abduction.
- **Versions**: Binocular eye movements in the nine diagnostic positions of gaze. The deviation should be comitant (equal in all gaze positions).
- **Saccadic testing**: Assess velocity and accuracy of horizontal saccades. Slowed abducting saccades suggest sixth nerve involvement.
- **Smooth pursuit**: Assess for asymmetries.

#### 6. Measurement of Deviation

| Fixation Distance | Expected Finding in DI |
|-------------------|----------------------|
| 6 meters (distance) | Esotropia (typically 4–20 PD) |
| 40 cm (near) | Orthophoria or small esophoria (significantly less than distance) |
| Far distance (>6 m or outdoors) | Esotropia may be even larger |

#### 7. Fusional Vergence Testing

- **Base-in (divergence) fusional vergence** at distance: Typically reduced
- **Base-out (convergence) fusional vergence** at near: Typically normal
- **[[Vergence facility]]** testing may reveal difficulty with divergence recovery

#### 8. Sensory Testing

- **[[Worth 4-dot test]]** at distance and near: May reveal suppression or diplopia at distance, with fusion at near
- **[[Bagolini striated lenses]]**: Useful for assessing sensory fusion status
- **[[Stereoacuity]]** testing (e.g., [[Titmus test]], [[Randot test]]): May be reduced at distance but preserved at near

#### 9. Pupillary Examination

- [[Pupil]] examination should be normal. Abnormal pupils (e.g., [[Horner syndrome]], [[third nerve palsy]] signs) suggest alternative diagnoses.

#### 10. Fundoscopy and Slit-Lamp Examination

- Complete [[anterior segment]] and [[posterior segment]] examination to rule out concurrent ocular pathology. [[Papilledema]] on [[fundoscopy]] would raise urgent concern for elevated [[intracranial pressure]].

### Neuroimaging

The decision to obtain neuroimaging is one of the most important clinical judgments in the management of divergence insufficiency. Guidelines include:

- **Acute onset divergence insufficiency/paralysis**: **Neuroimaging is mandatory**. [[MRI]] of the [[brain]] with attention to the [[posterior fossa]], [[brainstem]], and [[cerebellopontine angle]] is the study of choice. [[MR angiography]] (MRA) or [[CT angiography]] (CTA) may be indicated if vascular pathology is suspected.
- **Chronic, stable divergence insufficiency in an elderly patient** with no other neurological signs: Neuroimaging may be deferred if the clinical picture is consistent with benign/idiopathic DI or sagging eye syndrome, though many clinicians still recommend baseline imaging.
- **Any associated neurological signs or symptoms**: Neuroimaging is mandatory.
- **Progressive deviation**: Warrants imaging to exclude evolving pathology.

Conditions that may be identified on neuroimaging in patients presenting with divergence paralysis include:

| Condition | Imaging Findings |
|-----------|-----------------|
| [[Chiari malformation]] | Cerebellar tonsillar herniation below the [[foramen magnum]] |
| [[Hydrocephalus]] | Ventricular enlargement, periventricular edema |
| [[Posterior fossa tumor]] | Mass lesion in the cerebellum, brainstem, or cerebellopontine angle |
| [[Demyelinating disease]] (e.g., [[multiple sclerosis]]) | White matter lesions on [[FLAIR]] and T2-weighted sequences |
| [[Cerebrovascular disease]] | Brainstem infarction or hemorrhage |
| [[Idiopathic intracranial hypertension]] | Empty sella, optic nerve sheath distension, flattening of the posterior globe |

### High-Resolution Orbital MRI

In patients suspected of having **[[sagging eye syndrome]]**, high-resolution orbital MRI with thin-section coronal and quasi-sagittal cuts through the orbit can demonstrate:

- Inferior displacement of the [[lateral rectus]] muscle
- Elongation or disruption of the LR-SR connective tissue band
- Superior displacement of the [[superior rectus]] muscle
- General laxity of orbital connective tissues

### Differential Diagnosis

The differential diagnosis of divergence insufficiency is critical and includes:

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| **Bilateral [[sixth nerve palsy]]** | Limited abduction; incomitant deviation (larger in abduction); slowed abducting saccades |
| **Unilateral sixth nerve palsy** | Unilateral abduction deficit; incomitant deviation; may have abnormal [[head posture]] |
| **[[Convergence excess]]** | Esotropia greater at near than distance; high AC/A ratio |
| **[[Accommodative esotropia]]** | Associated with uncorrected [[hyperopia]]; improves with full hyperopic correction |
| **[[Myasthenia gravis]]** | Variable, fatigable deviation; may have [[ptosis]], [[orbicularis]] weakness; positive [[ice test]] or [[edrophonium test]] |
| **[[Thyroid eye disease]]** | Restrictive pattern; positive [[forced duction test]]; may have [[proptosis]], [[lid retraction]] |
| **[[Internuclear ophthalmoplegia]]** | Adduction deficit on the affected side; abduction nystagmus in the contralateral eye |
| **Sagging eye syndrome** | Often associated with [[hypotropia]] in addition to esotropia; orbital MRI shows LR displacement |
| **Decompensated [[esophoria]]** | Long history of esophoria; may have old photographs showing esotropia |
| **[[Spasm of the near reflex]]** | Intermittent esotropia with [[miosis]] and accommodative spasm; often functional |

## Management and Treatment

The management of divergence insufficiency depends on the severity of symptoms, the magnitude of the deviation, the underlying etiology (if identified), and the patient's visual demands. Treatment options range from conservative optical and prismatic approaches to surgical intervention.

### 1. Observation and Reassurance

In patients with **mild, intermittent symptoms** and small deviations, observation may be appropriate, particularly if the patient can maintain fusion at distance with effort. Periodic monitoring is recommended to detect progression.

### 2. Treatment of Underlying Etiology

If neuroimaging or systemic evaluation reveals an underlying cause (e.g., [[Chiari malformation]], [[hydrocephalus]], [[intracranial mass]]), treatment of the primary condition may resolve the divergence insufficiency. For example:

- **Ventriculoperitoneal shunting** for hydrocephalus
- **Posterior fossa decompression** for Chiari malformation
- **Tumor resection** or **radiation therapy** for intracranial neoplasms
- **Treatment of elevated intracranial pressure** in [[idiopathic intracranial hypertension]]

### 3. Optical Correction

- **Full hyperopic correction**: Even small amounts of uncorrected [[hyperopia]] should be corrected, as the associated [[accommodative convergence]] can exacerbate the esotropia at distance.
- **Minus lens therapy**: In some cases, small amounts of additional minus power (e.g., −0.50 to −1.00 D) added to the distance prescription can stimulate [[accommodative convergence]] and paradoxically reduce the esotropia. However, this approach is limited by the patient's accommodative ability and is less effective in presbyopic patients.

### 4. Prism Therapy

**[[Fresnel prisms]]** or **ground-in [[prisms]]** are the mainstay of conservative management for divergence insufficiency:

- **Base-out prisms** are prescribed to compensate for the esotropia at distance
- Prisms may be prescribed for **distance wear only** (e.g., incorporated into the distance portion of [[progressive addition lenses]] or [[bifocals]]) or for full-time wear if the deviation is also present at intermediate distances
- **Fresnel prisms** (press-on prisms) are useful as a temporary measure to determine the optimal prism power before ordering ground-in prisms. They are also useful when the deviation is unstable or expected to change.
- Typical prism amounts range from **2 to 10 PD base-out**, divided equally between the two eyes or placed entirely before one eye

| Prism Type | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|------------|-----------|---------------|
| [[Fresnel prism]] | Inexpensive; easily adjustable; temporary | Reduces visual acuity and contrast; cosmetically visible |
| Ground-in prism | Excellent optical quality; cosmetically superior | More expensive; not easily changed; limited to ~5–6 PD per lens |

### 5. Orthoptic / Vision Therapy

The role of [[vision therapy]] (orthoptic exercises) in divergence insufficiency is limited compared to its role in [[convergence insufficiency]]. However, some clinicians report benefit from:

- **Fusional divergence training**: Exercises designed to increase base-in fusional vergence amplitudes at distance, using [[prism bars]], [[vectograms]], or computer-based vergence training programs
- **Anti-suppression therapy**: If suppression has developed at distance
- **Facility exercises**: Alternating convergence and divergence demands to improve vergence flexibility

The evidence base for vision therapy in divergence insufficiency is considerably weaker than for convergence insufficiency, and many patients — particularly elderly individuals — may not respond adequately to orthoptic exercises alone.

### 6. Botulinum Toxin Injection

**[[Botulinum toxin]] (Botox)** injection into one or both [[medial rectus]] muscles has been described as a treatment for divergence insufficiency:

- Produces temporary weakening of the medial rectus, reducing the esotropia
- Effects typically last **3 to 6 months**
- May be useful as a diagnostic or temporizing measure, particularly in patients with unstable deviations or those who are poor surgical candidates
- Risks include [[ptosis]], overcorrection (consecutive [[exotropia]]), and vertical deviation due to toxin spread

### 7. Surgical Treatment

**[[Strabismus surgery]]** is indicated for patients with divergence insufficiency who:

- Have a stable deviation that is not adequately controlled with prisms
- Cannot tolerate prisms (e.g., due to optical degradation, cosmetic concerns, or large deviation)
- Have persistent, symptomatic diplopia at distance despite conservative measures

#### Surgical Options

| Procedure | Description | Indications |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| **Bilateral [[lateral rectus]] resection** | Strengthening procedure; shortening of both lateral rectus muscles | Moderate to large comitant esotropia at distance |
| **Bilateral [[medial rectus]] recession** | Weakening procedure; posterior displacement of both medial rectus insertions | Moderate to large comitant esotropia at distance; may be preferred if near alignment is orthophoric |
| **Unilateral medial rectus recession with lateral rectus resection (R&R)** | Combined weakening and strengthening on one eye | Smaller deviations; unilateral surgery preferred |
| **Adjustable suture technique** | Sutures placed during surgery that can be adjusted postoperatively | Allows fine-tuning of alignment; particularly useful in adults |

**Surgical considerations specific to divergence insufficiency:**

- The goal of surgery is to correct the distance esotropia **without inducing an exotropia at near**. This requires careful preoperative planning and conservative surgical dosing.
- **Bilateral lateral rectus resections** are often favored because they preferentially increase the divergence force at distance while having less effect on near alignment.
- **Bilateral medial rectus recessions** are also effective but carry a slightly higher risk of overcorrection at near (consecutive exotropia at near).
- **Adjustable sutures** are strongly recommended in adult patients to allow postoperative titration of alignment.
- Surgical outcomes are generally favorable, with reported success rates of **70–90%** in achieving single binocular vision at distance without diplopia at near.

### 8. Management of Sagging Eye Syndrome

For patients in whom divergence insufficiency is attributed to **[[sagging eye syndrome]]**, management follows similar principles (prisms, surgery). Surgical approaches may be modified to address the specific mechanical pathology:

- **Lateral rectus muscle repositioning** (superotransposition) to correct the inferior displacement of the LR
- Combined horizontal and vertical muscle surgery if both esotropia and hypotropia are present

## Prognosis

The prognosis of divergence insufficiency depends on the underlying etiology:

- **Idiopathic/age-related DI**: Generally stable or slowly progressive. Most patients can be managed successfully with prisms or surgery. Spontaneous resolution is uncommon but has been reported.
- **DI secondary to neurological disease**: Prognosis depends on the underlying condition. Resolution may occur if the primary pathology is treated (e.g., shunting for hydrocephalus).
- **Acute divergence paralysis**: May resolve spontaneously over weeks to months, particularly if related to a self-limited process (e.g., viral illness). Persistent cases may require prisms or surgery.

---

## Special Considerations

### Divergence Insufficiency in Children

While divergence insufficiency is predominantly a condition of older adults, it can occur in children. Pediatric cases warrant thorough neurological evaluation, as the likelihood of underlying pathology (e.g., [[posterior fossa]] tumors, [[hydrocephalus]], [[Chiari malformation]]) is higher than in elderly patients with insidious onset.

### Divergence Insufficiency and Driving

Distance diplopia can significantly impair driving safety. Patients should be counseled about the risks, and appropriate prismatic correction should be provided promptly. In some jurisdictions, patients with uncorrected diplopia may not meet legal visual standards for driving.

### Monitoring Protocol

Patients with divergence insufficiency should be monitored at regular intervals:

| Visit | Assessment |
|-------|-----------|
| Initial | Full neuro-ophthalmic evaluation; neuroimaging if indicated |
| 4–6 weeks | Reassess deviation; adjust prisms if needed |
| 3 months | Stability check; consider definitive management |
| 6–12 months | Long-term follow-up; reassess for progression |
| Annually | Ongoing monitoring in stable patients |

## Summary

Divergence insufficiency is a clinically important cause of distance [[diplopia]], particularly in the elderly population. It is characterized by a comitant [[esotropia]] at distance with preserved alignment at near, full [[ductions]], and normal [[saccadic]] velocities. The pathophysiology likely involves a spectrum of mechanisms, including dysfunction of a brainstem divergence center, subtle bilateral [[abducens nerve]] weakness, and age-related orbital mechanical changes ([[sagging eye syndrome]]). Accurate diagnosis requires careful clinical examination, appropriate neuroimaging when indicated, and systematic exclusion of mimicking conditions — most importantly bilateral [[sixth nerve palsy]]. Management options include optical correction, [[prism]] therapy, [[vision therapy]], [[botulinum toxin]] injection, and [[strabismus surgery]], with treatment individualized based on the severity of symptoms, magnitude of deviation, and patient factors. With appropriate management, the majority of patients with divergence insufficiency can achieve comfortable, single [[binocular vision]] at both distance and near.
