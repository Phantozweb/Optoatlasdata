# Ductions

**Ductions** refer to the monocular rotations of a single eye around its center of rotation, independent of the movement of the fellow eye. They represent the fundamental unit of [[ocular motility]] assessment and are critical to the clinical evaluation of [[extraocular muscle]] function, [[cranial nerve]] integrity, and orbital mechanics. Ductions are distinguished from [[versions]] (conjugate binocular movements) and [[vergences]] (disjunctive binocular movements) in that they describe the movement capacity of one eye tested in isolation, typically with the fellow eye occluded.

The clinical assessment of ductions is a cornerstone of the [[neuro-ophthalmic]] and [[strabismus]] examination. Limitation or excess of ductions provides essential diagnostic information regarding the etiology of [[diplopia]], [[strabismus]], and [[ocular motility disorders]], helping clinicians differentiate between [[paretic strabismus]], [[restrictive strabismus]], and supranuclear gaze palsies.

---

## Terminology and Classification

Ductions are classified according to the direction of the monocular eye movement. The standard nomenclature, based on the primary and secondary actions of the [[extraocular muscles]], is as follows:

| Term | Direction of Movement | Primary Agonist Muscle | Innervation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Adduction]]** | Nasalward (toward the nose) | [[Medial rectus]] | [[Cranial nerve III]] ([[Oculomotor nerve]]) |
| **[[Abduction]]** | Temporalward (toward the temple) | [[Lateral rectus]] | [[Cranial nerve VI]] ([[Abducens nerve]]) |
| **[[Elevation]]** (Supraduction / Sursumduction) | Upward | [[Superior rectus]] (primary position); [[Inferior oblique]] (in adduction) | [[Cranial nerve III]] (SR); [[Cranial nerve III]] (IO) |
| **[[Depression]]** (Infraduction / Deorsumduction) | Downward | [[Inferior rectus]] (primary position); [[Superior oblique]] (in adduction) | [[Cranial nerve III]] (IR); [[Cranial nerve IV]] ([[Trochlear nerve]]) (SO) |
| **[[Intorsion]]** (Incycloduction) | Rotation of the superior pole of the cornea nasalward | [[Superior oblique]]; [[Superior rectus]] | CN IV (SO); CN III (SR) |
| **[[Extorsion]]** (Excycloduction) | Rotation of the superior pole of the cornea temporalward | [[Inferior oblique]]; [[Inferior rectus]] | CN III (IO); CN III (IR) |

### Distinction from Versions and Vergences

It is essential to distinguish ductions from other categories of eye movement:

- **Ductions**: Monocular rotations of a single eye, tested with the fellow eye occluded.
- **[[Versions]]**: Binocular conjugate movements in which both eyes move in the same direction simultaneously (e.g., [[dextroversion]], [[levoversion]], [[supraversion]], [[infraversion]]).
- **[[Vergences]]**: Binocular disjunctive movements in which the eyes move in opposite directions (e.g., [[convergence]], [[divergence]]).

The clinical significance of testing ductions separately from versions lies in the ability to isolate the function of individual extraocular muscles and their neural supply without the confounding influence of [[Hering's law of equal innervation]], which governs yoke muscle pairs during versional movements.

## Anatomy and Physiology of Ductions

### Extraocular Muscles

Each eye is controlled by six [[extraocular muscles]] (EOMs): four [[rectus muscles]] ([[medial rectus]], [[lateral rectus]], [[superior rectus]], [[inferior rectus]]) and two [[oblique muscles]] ([[superior oblique]], [[inferior oblique]]). These muscles originate from the [[annulus of Zinn]] (rectus muscles and the superior oblique) or the orbital floor (inferior oblique) and insert onto the [[sclera]] of the globe.

The actions of each muscle depend on the position of the eye in the orbit. In [[primary gaze]] (looking straight ahead), each muscle has a primary, secondary, and tertiary action:

| Muscle | Primary Action | Secondary Action | Tertiary Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Medial rectus]] | Adduction | — | — |
| [[Lateral rectus]] | Abduction | — | — |
| [[Superior rectus]] | Elevation | Intorsion | Adduction |
| [[Inferior rectus]] | Depression | Extorsion | Adduction |
| [[Superior oblique]] | Intorsion | Depression | Abduction |
| [[Inferior oblique]] | Extorsion | Elevation | Abduction |

The complexity of these actions is critical to understanding duction deficits. For example, a limitation of elevation in adduction implicates the [[inferior oblique]] muscle, whereas a limitation of elevation in abduction implicates the [[superior rectus]] muscle.

### Neural Control

The motor innervation of the extraocular muscles follows a well-defined pattern:

- **[[Oculomotor nerve]] (CN III)**: Innervates the [[medial rectus]], [[superior rectus]], [[inferior rectus]], and [[inferior oblique]], as well as the [[levator palpebrae superioris]] and the parasympathetic fibers to the [[pupillary sphincter]] and [[ciliary muscle]].
- **[[Trochlear nerve]] (CN IV)**: Innervates the [[superior oblique]] muscle exclusively.
- **[[Abducens nerve]] (CN VI)**: Innervates the [[lateral rectus]] muscle exclusively.

Supranuclear control of ductions involves the [[frontal eye fields]], [[parietal-occipital-temporal junction]], [[superior colliculus]], [[paramedian pontine reticular formation]] (PPRF) for horizontal gaze, and the [[rostral interstitial nucleus of the medial longitudinal fasciculus]] (riMLF) for vertical gaze. Lesions at any level of this hierarchy—from the [[cerebral cortex]] to the [[neuromuscular junction]] to the muscle itself—can produce duction deficits.

### Orbital Connective Tissue and Pulleys

Modern understanding of ocular motility has been revolutionized by the discovery of the [[extraocular muscle pulley system]]. These pulleys are rings of collagen, elastin, and smooth muscle located near the [[equator]] of the globe within [[Tenon's capsule]]. They serve as functional origins for the rectus muscles and significantly influence the rotational axes of the globe. Displacement or pathology of these pulleys (e.g., in [[heavy eye syndrome]], [[sagging eye syndrome]], or orbital trauma) can produce duction limitations that mimic cranial nerve palsies.

## Pathophysiology

Duction deficits arise from disruption at any point along the motor pathway or within the orbital mechanical system. The pathophysiology can be broadly categorized as:

### 1. Neurogenic (Paretic) Causes

Neurogenic duction deficits result from lesions affecting the [[cranial nerves]] (III, IV, or VI) or their supranuclear and internuclear pathways.

- **[[Cranial nerve III palsy]]**: Produces deficits in adduction, elevation, and depression, often accompanied by [[ptosis]] and [[mydriasis]] (if the parasympathetic fibers are involved). Etiologies include [[posterior communicating artery aneurysm]], [[diabetes mellitus]] (microvascular ischemia), [[cavernous sinus]] lesions, and [[brainstem]] pathology.
- **[[Cranial nerve IV palsy]]**: Produces a deficit in intorsion and depression (most notable in adduction). The most common cause of isolated cyclovertical duction deficit. Etiologies include [[head trauma]], congenital absence, microvascular ischemia, and rarely, [[tumor]].
- **[[Cranial nerve VI palsy]]**: Produces a deficit in abduction. The most common isolated cranial nerve palsy affecting ocular motility. Etiologies include [[elevated intracranial pressure]], microvascular ischemia, [[pontine]] lesions, [[Gradenigo syndrome]], and [[cavernous sinus thrombosis]].
- **[[Internuclear ophthalmoplegia]] (INO)**: A lesion of the [[medial longitudinal fasciculus]] (MLF) produces impaired adduction on the ipsilateral side during contralateral gaze (a versional deficit), but adduction on duction testing (convergence) may be preserved, helping localize the lesion.

### 2. Restrictive (Mechanical) Causes

Restrictive duction deficits occur when passive mechanical forces prevent the globe from rotating normally, despite intact neural innervation.

- **[[Thyroid eye disease]] (Graves' orbitopathy)**: The most common cause of restrictive duction deficit in adults. [[Fibrosis]] and [[inflammation]] of the [[inferior rectus]] (most commonly) or [[medial rectus]] produce limitation of elevation or abduction, respectively.
- **[[Orbital floor fracture]]**: Entrapment of the [[inferior rectus]] or surrounding [[orbital tissue]] in a [[blowout fracture]] restricts elevation.
- **[[Brown syndrome]]**: Restriction of the [[superior oblique]] tendon-trochlea complex limits elevation in adduction.
- **[[Duane retraction syndrome]]**: Congenital absence or hypoplasia of the [[abducens nucleus]] with aberrant innervation of the [[lateral rectus]] by branches of CN III produces characteristic duction limitations (typically limited abduction in Type I, limited adduction in Type II, or both in Type III) with globe retraction and [[palpebral fissure]] narrowing on attempted adduction.
- **Post-surgical restriction**: Prior [[strabismus surgery]], [[scleral buckle]] placement, or [[orbital surgery]] can produce adhesions or excessive muscle tightening that limits ductions.

### 3. Myogenic Causes

Primary muscle pathology can impair ductions:

- **[[Myasthenia gravis]]**: An [[autoimmune]] disorder affecting the [[neuromuscular junction]] (antibodies against [[acetylcholine receptors]] or [[MuSK]]). Produces variable, fatigable duction deficits that can mimic virtually any pattern of ocular motility disturbance. The hallmark is variability and fatigability.
- **[[Chronic progressive external ophthalmoplegia]] (CPEO)**: A [[mitochondrial myopathy]] producing slowly progressive, symmetric limitation of ductions in all directions, often accompanied by bilateral [[ptosis]].
- **[[Oculopharyngeal muscular dystrophy]]**: A rare [[muscular dystrophy]] affecting the extraocular muscles and pharyngeal muscles.

### 4. Supranuclear Causes

Supranuclear lesions typically affect versions rather than ductions per se, but certain conditions can produce apparent duction limitations:

- **[[Progressive supranuclear palsy]] (PSP)**: Produces vertical gaze palsy (initially downgaze) that affects both versions and ductions.
- **[[Dorsal midbrain syndrome]] (Parinaud syndrome)**: Produces upgaze palsy, [[convergence-retraction nystagmus]], and [[light-near dissociation]] of the pupils.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Symptoms Associated with Duction Deficits

Patients with duction limitations may present with a variety of symptoms depending on the severity, acuity, and etiology:

- **[[Diplopia]] (double vision)**: The most common presenting symptom. Binocular diplopia that resolves with occlusion of either eye is the hallmark of a duction deficit causing misalignment. The diplopia is typically worst in the field of action of the affected muscle.
- **[[Asthenopia]]**: Eye strain, headache, and fatigue, particularly with sustained gaze in the direction of the duction limitation.
- **Abnormal head posture ([[torticollis]])**: Patients may adopt a compensatory [[head turn]], [[head tilt]], or [[chin elevation/depression]] to minimize diplopia by placing the eyes in a gaze position where the duction deficit is least manifest.
- **[[Oscillopsia]]**: A sensation of visual world movement, which may occur with acquired duction deficits, particularly in the acute phase.
- **Blurred vision**: May result from the inability to maintain [[fixation]] in certain gaze positions.
- **Cosmetic concern**: Visible [[strabismus]] may be the primary complaint, particularly in longstanding or congenital duction deficits.

### Signs on Examination

- **Limitation of eye movement** in one or more directions of gaze.
- **[[Strabismus]]**: Manifest deviation ([[tropia]]) that may be comitant or incomitant.
- **[[Incomitant deviation]]**: The hallmark of a duction deficit—the angle of misalignment varies with gaze direction, being greatest in the field of action of the paretic or restricted muscle.
- **Primary and secondary deviation**: In paretic conditions, the secondary deviation (deviation measured when the paretic eye fixates) is greater than the primary deviation (deviation measured when the normal eye fixates), as predicted by [[Hering's law]].
- **Globe retraction or palpebral fissure changes**: Seen in [[Duane retraction syndrome]].
- **[[Ptosis]]**: May accompany CN III palsy or myogenic conditions.
- **[[Pupil]] abnormalities**: Mydriasis in compressive CN III palsy; normal pupils in microvascular CN III palsy.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Assessment of Ductions

#### 1. Monocular Duction Testing

The gold standard for assessing ductions is to occlude the fellow eye and observe the range of movement of the tested eye as the patient follows a target through the diagnostic positions of gaze. The examiner should assess:

- **Adduction**
- **Abduction**
- **Elevation in adduction**
- **Elevation in abduction**
- **Depression in adduction**
- **Depression in abduction**

Ductions are typically graded on a clinical scale:

| Grade | Description |
|---|---|
| **0** | Full duction (normal) |
| **-1** | Slight limitation (approximately 75% of normal range) |
| **-2** | Moderate limitation (approximately 50% of normal range) |
| **-3** | Severe limitation (approximately 25% of normal range) |
| **-4** | No movement past midline / absent duction |

Some clinicians use a positive scale (e.g., +1 to +4) to denote overaction of a muscle, particularly the oblique muscles.

#### 2. Forced Duction Test (Passive Duction Test)

The **[[forced duction test]]** is the definitive clinical method for distinguishing a **restrictive** from a **paretic** duction deficit. After application of topical anesthesia (e.g., [[proparacaine]] or [[tetracaine]]), the examiner grasps the [[conjunctiva]] and [[episclera]] near the [[limbus]] with toothed forceps and attempts to passively rotate the globe in the direction of the duction limitation.

- **Positive forced duction** (resistance to passive rotation): Indicates a **restrictive** etiology (e.g., [[thyroid eye disease]], [[orbital fracture]] with entrapment, fibrosis).
- **Negative forced duction** (no resistance): Indicates a **paretic** or **neurogenic** etiology (e.g., cranial nerve palsy).

#### 3. Force Generation Test (Active Force Generation Test)

The **[[force generation test]]** assesses the contractile force of a suspected paretic muscle. The patient is asked to look in the direction of the duction deficit while the examiner holds the globe with forceps and assesses the force generated. Reduced force generation confirms paresis, while normal force generation in the setting of limited ductions supports a restrictive process.

#### 4. Saccadic Velocity Assessment

Observation of [[saccadic]] velocity in the direction of the duction limitation provides additional diagnostic information:

- **Slow saccades**: Suggest a paretic etiology (reduced neural drive produces slower eye movements).
- **Normal velocity saccades that abruptly stop**: Suggest a restrictive etiology (the muscle generates normal force but is mechanically blocked).

This can be assessed clinically or quantified with [[scleral search coil]] recordings or [[infrared oculography]].

### Diagnostic Positions of Gaze

The **[[nine diagnostic positions of gaze]]** are used to systematically evaluate the function of each extraocular muscle:

| Position | Right Eye Muscle Tested | Left Eye Muscle Tested |
|---|---|---|
| Right and Up | [[Superior rectus]] (OD) | [[Inferior oblique]] (OS) |
| Right | [[Lateral rectus]] (OD) | [[Medial rectus]] (OS) |
| Right and Down | [[Inferior rectus]] (OD) | [[Superior oblique]] (OS) |
| Left and Up | [[Inferior oblique]] (OD) | [[Superior rectus]] (OS) |
| Left | [[Medial rectus]] (OD) | [[Lateral rectus]] (OS) |
| Left and Down | [[Superior oblique]] (OD) | [[Inferior rectus]] (OS) |
| Up (Primary) | SR and IO (both eyes) | SR and IO (both eyes) |
| Primary Gaze | — | — |
| Down (Primary) | IR and SO (both eyes) | IR and SO (both eyes) |

### Ancillary Diagnostic Tests

#### Cover Testing

The **[[cover test]]** and **[[alternate cover test]]** with [[prism]] measurement in multiple gaze positions quantify the deviation and characterize its comitancy. An incomitant pattern with greatest deviation in the field of action of a specific muscle strongly suggests a duction deficit involving that muscle.

#### Hess-Lancaster Test

The **[[Hess-Lancaster test]]** (or **[[Hess screen test]]**) provides a graphical representation of the duction and version status of each eye. Using red-green dissociation, the test maps the excursion of each eye in the nine diagnostic positions, producing a characteristic pattern:

- **Paretic muscle**: Contracted field on the Hess chart in the direction of action of the affected muscle.
- **Contralateral synergist overaction**: Expanded field of the yoke muscle in the fellow eye (per [[Hering's law]]).
- **Ipsilateral antagonist contracture**: Over time, secondary contracture of the antagonist muscle may develop, further limiting ductions.

#### Parks Three-Step Test

The **[[Parks three-step test]]** is specifically designed to identify which cyclovertical muscle is paretic in cases of vertical or torsional duction deficits. The three steps are:

1. **Which eye is hypertropic?**
2. **Is the hypertropia worse in right gaze or left gaze?**
3. **Is the hypertropia worse with right head tilt or left head tilt?** ([[Bielschowsky head tilt test]])

#### Neuroimaging

**[[Magnetic resonance imaging]] (MRI)** of the [[brain]] and [[orbits]] is indicated when a neurogenic etiology is suspected, particularly for:

- CN III palsy with pupil involvement (rule out [[posterior communicating artery aneurysm]])
- CN VI palsy with other neurological signs
- Progressive or atypical duction deficits
- Suspected orbital pathology (e.g., [[orbital tumor]], [[thyroid eye disease]])

High-resolution orbital MRI with thin coronal sections can directly visualize the extraocular muscles and their pulleys, identifying atrophy, enlargement, or displacement.

**[[Computed tomography]] (CT)** of the orbits is preferred for evaluating bony orbital pathology, particularly [[orbital fractures]].

#### Laboratory Testing

- **[[Tensilon test]] (edrophonium test)** or **[[ice pack test]]**: For suspected [[myasthenia gravis]]. Improvement of duction deficit after administration of edrophonium or application of ice to the eyelid supports the diagnosis.
- **[[Acetylcholine receptor antibodies]]** and **[[anti-MuSK antibodies]]**: Serological testing for myasthenia gravis.
- **[[Thyroid function tests]]** and **[[thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulin]] (TSI)**: For suspected [[thyroid eye disease]].
- **[[Erythrocyte sedimentation rate]] (ESR)** and **[[C-reactive protein]] (CRP)**: For suspected [[orbital inflammatory disease]] or [[giant cell arteritis]].
- **[[Hemoglobin A1c]]** and **fasting glucose**: Microvascular ischemia from [[diabetes mellitus]] is a common cause of isolated cranial nerve palsies.

## Management and Treatment

The management of duction deficits is directed at the underlying etiology and may involve observation, optical correction, pharmacological intervention, or surgical treatment.

### 1. Observation and Conservative Management

Many acute duction deficits, particularly those caused by microvascular ischemia (e.g., diabetic or hypertensive cranial nerve palsies), resolve spontaneously within **3 to 6 months**. During this period:

- **Occlusion therapy**: Patching or [[occluder]] use to eliminate diplopia.
- **[[Fresnel prism]]**: Temporary press-on prisms applied to spectacle lenses to compensate for the deviation and relieve diplopia in primary gaze and/or reading position.
- **Reassurance and monitoring**: Serial duction assessments to document recovery.

### 2. Optical and Prismatic Correction

- **[[Prism]] correction**: Ground-in prisms incorporated into spectacle lenses can provide stable relief of diplopia for small, stable deviations. Prisms are most effective for comitant or near-comitant residual deviations.
- **[[Fresnel prisms]]**: Useful for larger or variable deviations, though they reduce visual acuity and contrast sensitivity.
- **Sector occlusion**: Opaque tape or [[Bangerter foil]] applied to a portion of the spectacle lens corresponding to the gaze direction of maximum diplopia.

### 3. Pharmacological Interventions

#### Botulinum Toxin Injection

**[[Botulinum toxin]] (Botox)** injection into the antagonist muscle of a paretic muscle can be used to:

- Prevent contracture of the antagonist muscle during the recovery phase of a cranial nerve palsy.
- Temporarily improve alignment and reduce diplopia.
- Serve as a diagnostic tool to predict the outcome of surgical intervention.

For example, in an acute [[CN VI palsy]] with limited abduction, injection of botulinum toxin into the ipsilateral [[medial rectus]] can prevent medial rectus contracture and improve alignment while awaiting recovery.

#### Treatment of Underlying Conditions

- **[[Corticosteroids]]**: For [[thyroid eye disease]] (active inflammatory phase), [[orbital inflammatory pseudotumor]], or [[giant cell arteritis]].
- **[[Pyridostigmine]]**: An [[acetylcholinesterase inhibitor]] used as first-line treatment for [[myasthenia gravis]].
- **[[Immunosuppressive agents]]**: For refractory myasthenia gravis or orbital inflammatory disease (e.g., [[azathioprine]], [[mycophenolate mofetil]], [[rituximab]]).
- **[[Orbital radiation therapy]]**: For moderate-to-severe thyroid eye disease unresponsive to corticosteroids.
- **[[Teprotumumab]]**: A monoclonal antibody targeting the [[insulin-like growth factor 1 receptor]] (IGF-1R), approved for the treatment of thyroid eye disease, which can improve duction limitations by reducing extraocular muscle volume and fibrosis.

### 4. Surgical Treatment

#### Strabismus Surgery

**[[Strabismus surgery]]** is indicated for stable, non-resolving duction deficits that produce symptomatic diplopia or significant cosmetic deformity. The timing of surgery is generally deferred until the deviation has been stable for at least **6 months** to allow for maximum spontaneous recovery.

Surgical strategies include:

| Strategy | Indication | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Recession** of the antagonist muscle | Restrictive or paretic duction deficit | Recession of the [[medial rectus]] for limited abduction due to CN VI palsy |
| **Resection** of the paretic muscle | Paretic duction deficit with residual muscle function | Resection of the [[lateral rectus]] for CN VI palsy |
| **Transposition procedures** | Complete or near-complete paralysis of a muscle | [[Knapp procedure]] (vertical rectus transposition to the lateral rectus insertion for CN VI palsy); [[Hummelsheim procedure]]; full tendon transposition with or without [[augmentation sutures]] |
| **Adjustable sutures** | To allow postoperative fine-tuning of alignment | Commonly used in adult strabismus surgery for duction deficits |
| **Inferior rectus recession** | Restrictive elevation deficit (e.g., thyroid eye disease) | Recession of the fibrotic [[inferior rectus]] to improve elevation |
| **Superior oblique tenotomy or tenectomy** | [[Brown syndrome]] | Release of the restricted superior oblique tendon |

#### Orbital Surgery

- **[[Orbital decompression]]**: For thyroid eye disease with compressive [[optic neuropathy]] or severe proptosis contributing to duction limitation.
- **[[Orbital fracture repair]]**: Surgical release of entrapped tissue and reconstruction of the orbital floor or wall to restore ductions.

### 5. Rehabilitation and Orthoptic Exercises

In select cases, particularly those involving mild paretic deficits or convergence insufficiency contributing to duction-related symptoms, **[[orthoptic exercises]]** may be beneficial:

- **Convergence exercises**: For convergence insufficiency.
- **Antisuppression therapy**: For patients developing suppression in the field of the duction deficit.

However, orthoptic exercises have limited efficacy for significant paretic or restrictive duction deficits and should not delay definitive treatment.

## Special Considerations

### Ductions in Pediatric Patients

Assessment of ductions in infants and young children requires specialized techniques, as these patients cannot follow verbal commands:

- **Doll's head maneuver ([[vestibulo-ocular reflex]])**: Rotating the child's head to elicit reflexive eye movements in the opposite direction.
- **Spinning test**: Rotating the child in the examiner's arms to elicit vestibular-driven eye movements.
- **Attraction to toys or lights**: Using interesting targets to elicit voluntary ductions.

Congenital duction deficits (e.g., [[Duane retraction syndrome]], [[Möbius syndrome]], congenital [[CN III palsy]], [[Brown syndrome]], [[congenital fibrosis of the extraocular muscles]]) require careful evaluation to distinguish them from acquired conditions.

### Ductions in Thyroid Eye Disease

[[Thyroid eye disease]] deserves special mention as the most common cause of acquired restrictive duction deficit in adults. The typical pattern involves:

1. **Inferior rectus restriction** → limited elevation (most common)
2. **Medial rectus restriction** → limited abduction
3. **Superior rectus restriction** → limited depression (less common)
4. **Lateral rectus restriction** → limited adduction (least common)

The **Clinical Activity Score (CAS)** and **EUGOGO classification** are used to grade disease severity and guide treatment decisions. Duction measurements are a key component of monitoring disease progression and treatment response.

### Ductions and the Medial Longitudinal Fasciculus

[[Internuclear ophthalmoplegia]] (INO) presents a unique scenario in which adduction is impaired during versional (conjugate) gaze but may be preserved during convergence-driven duction testing. This dissociation is pathognomonic for a lesion of the [[medial longitudinal fasciculus]] and helps distinguish INO from a [[medial rectus]] palsy, in which adduction would be impaired in all contexts.

## Prognosis

The prognosis for duction deficits depends entirely on the underlying etiology:

- **Microvascular cranial nerve palsies**: Excellent prognosis; >95% recover fully within 3–6 months.
- **Traumatic cranial nerve palsies**: Variable; partial recovery is common, but complete recovery is less predictable.
- **Compressive lesions**: Prognosis depends on the nature and treatability of the compressive pathology.
- **Thyroid eye disease**: Duction limitations may improve with medical treatment (corticosteroids, teprotumumab) but often require surgical correction in the fibrotic (inactive) phase.
- **Myasthenia gravis**: Duction deficits are often controllable with appropriate immunomodulatory therapy.
- **Congenital duction deficits**: Generally stable and non-progressive; surgical intervention is aimed at improving alignment and head posture rather than restoring normal ductions.
- **CPEO**: Progressive and currently without curative treatment; management is supportive.

---

## Summary

Ductions are the fundamental monocular eye movements that form the basis of ocular motility assessment. A systematic approach to evaluating ductions—including clinical grading, forced duction testing, force generation testing, and ancillary diagnostic studies—enables the clinician to accurately localize the pathology, differentiate paretic from restrictive etiologies, and formulate an appropriate management plan. The integration of duction assessment with the broader [[neuro-ophthalmic]] and [[strabismus]] examination is essential for optimal patient care.
