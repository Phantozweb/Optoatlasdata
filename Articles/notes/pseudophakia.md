

# Pseudophakia

**Pseudophakia** (from Greek *pseudḗs*, "false," and *phakós*, "lens") is the clinical state in which the natural [[Crystalline Lens]] of the eye has been surgically removed and replaced with an artificial [[Intraocular Lens]] (IOL). It is the intended and expected outcome following [[Cataract Surgery]] or, less commonly, [[Refractive Lens Exchange]] (RLE). Pseudophakia is not a disease or pathological condition per se; rather, it is an iatrogenic optical state that fundamentally alters the dioptric system of the eye. Understanding pseudophakia is essential for every eye care practitioner, as the pseudophakic eye presents unique considerations in refraction, biometry, posterior segment evaluation, secondary complications, and long-term management.

---

## Epidemiology

Pseudophakia is one of the most prevalent postoperative ocular states worldwide. [[Cataract Surgery]] is the most frequently performed elective surgical procedure globally, with an estimated **20 million procedures performed annually** and rising due to aging populations. In the United States alone, approximately **3.7–4 million cataract surgeries** are performed each year. Consequently, the pseudophakic population is enormous and continues to grow.

| Statistic | Value |
|---|---|
| Global cataract surgeries per year | ~20 million |
| U.S. cataract surgeries per year | ~3.7–4 million |
| Prevalence of pseudophakia in adults >65 years (U.S.) | ~25–30% |
| Average age at cataract surgery (developed nations) | 65–75 years |
| Bilateral pseudophakia rate among surgical patients | >60% within 5 years |

The prevalence of pseudophakia increases sharply with age and is influenced by access to surgical care, socioeconomic status, and geographic region. In developed nations, the [[Cataract Surgical Rate]] (CSR) exceeds 9,000 per million population per year, whereas in low-income countries it may be below 500 per million.

## Historical Background

The history of pseudophakia is inextricably linked to the development of the intraocular lens. Sir [[Harold Ridley]], a British ophthalmologist, performed the first successful IOL implantation on **November 29, 1949**, at [[St. Thomas' Hospital]] in London. Ridley's observation that fragments of [[Polymethyl Methacrylate]] (PMMA) from shattered aircraft canopies lodged in the eyes of World War II pilots remained biologically inert led him to develop the first IOL from the same material.

Early IOL designs were fraught with complications, including [[Uveitis]], [[Secondary Glaucoma]], [[Corneal Endothelial Decompensation]], and lens dislocation. Over subsequent decades, advances in lens materials (including [[Silicone]], [[Hydrophobic Acrylic]], and [[Hydrophilic Acrylic]]), surgical techniques (transition from [[Intracapsular Cataract Extraction]] to [[Extracapsular Cataract Extraction]] to modern [[Phacoemulsification]]), and IOL designs (from anterior chamber to iris-fixated to [[Posterior Chamber Intraocular Lens|posterior chamber]] lenses) have made pseudophakia an extraordinarily safe and predictable outcome.

## Pathophysiology

### Optical Transformation

The natural [[Crystalline Lens]] is a biconvex, transparent, avascular structure with a refractive index gradient ranging from approximately 1.386 at the periphery to 1.406 at the nucleus. It contributes roughly **+18 to +20 diopters** of the eye's total refractive power (~+60 D). The crystalline lens also provides [[Accommodation]] through changes in shape mediated by the [[Ciliary Muscle]] and [[Zonules of Zinn]].

Upon removal of the crystalline lens (lensectomy via phacoemulsification or extracapsular extraction), the eye becomes profoundly [[Hyperopic]]—typically by +10 to +14 diopters in an average eye—because the refractive contribution of the natural lens is lost. Implantation of an IOL restores the dioptric power of the eye. The IOL power is calculated preoperatively using [[Biometry]] formulas (see Diagnostic Modalities below) to target a specific postoperative refraction, most commonly [[Emmetropia]] or mild [[Myopia]] for distance vision.

### Anatomical Changes in the Pseudophakic Eye

The pseudophakic eye differs from the phakic eye in several critical anatomical and physiological ways:

| Parameter | Phakic Eye | Pseudophakic Eye |
|---|---|---|
| Lens material | Biological crystalline lens | Synthetic polymer (acrylic, silicone, PMMA) |
| Refractive index of lens | Gradient: 1.386–1.406 | Uniform: 1.44–1.55 (material-dependent) |
| Accommodation | Present (age-dependent) | Absent (with monofocal IOL) |
| [[Anterior Chamber Depth]] | ~3.0–3.5 mm | Often deeper (~4.0–4.5 mm) |
| [[Posterior Capsule]] | Intact, transparent | Intact but may opacify ([[PCO]]) |
| Aberrations | Low-order dominant | Higher-order aberrations may differ; depends on IOL design |
| UV protection | Natural UV absorption by lens | Depends on IOL chromophore (UV-blocking, blue-light filtering) |

### Loss of Accommodation

A critical consequence of pseudophakia with a standard [[Monofocal IOL]] is the complete loss of [[Accommodation]]. The natural crystalline lens changes shape in response to ciliary muscle contraction, allowing the eye to focus at varying distances. A conventional IOL is rigid and fixed in position within the [[Capsular Bag]], providing a single focal point. This results in [[Presbyopia]] that is absolute—the patient requires spectacle correction (typically reading glasses or [[Progressive Addition Lenses]]) for near tasks unless a [[Multifocal IOL]], [[Extended Depth of Focus IOL]] (EDOF), or [[Accommodating IOL]] is implanted.

### Capsular Bag Dynamics

After phacoemulsification, the IOL is placed within the remaining [[Capsular Bag]], which is composed of the [[Anterior Capsule]] (with a central opening, the [[Capsulorhexis]]) and the [[Posterior Capsule]]. Over time, residual [[Lens Epithelial Cells]] (LECs) on the capsular bag may proliferate and migrate, leading to:

- **[[Posterior Capsule Opacification]]** (PCO): The most common long-term complication of pseudophakia (see Complications below).
- **Capsular contraction syndrome** ([[Capsular Phimosis]]): Fibrotic contraction of the anterior capsulotomy opening.
- **Anterior capsule opacification**: Whitening and fibrosis of the anterior capsule remnant.

### Altered Vitreous Dynamics

Removal of the crystalline lens and placement of a thinner IOL alters the relationship between the [[Vitreous Body]] and the posterior segment. The pseudophakic eye has a slightly larger vitreous cavity volume, and the posterior capsule provides a less robust barrier than the natural lens. This has implications for:

- Increased risk of [[Posterior Vitreous Detachment]] (PVD)
- Altered risk profile for [[Rhegmatogenous Retinal Detachment]] (discussed under Complications)
- Changes in pharmacokinetics of intravitreal medications

## Types of Intraocular Lenses in Pseudophakia

The specific visual characteristics of the pseudophakic eye depend heavily on the type of IOL implanted. A comprehensive understanding of IOL categories is essential for managing pseudophakic patients.

### By Optical Design

| IOL Type | Description | Key Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| [[Monofocal IOL]] | Single focal point | Excellent distance vision; requires reading glasses |
| [[Multifocal IOL]] | Multiple focal points (bifocal or trifocal) | Simultaneous distance and near vision; may cause [[Dysphotopsia]], halos, glare |
| [[Extended Depth of Focus IOL]] (EDOF) | Elongated focal range | Improved intermediate vision with fewer dysphotopsias than multifocal |
| [[Accommodating IOL]] | Designed to move or flex with ciliary effort | Limited real-world accommodative amplitude |
| [[Toric IOL]] | Incorporates cylindrical correction | Corrects pre-existing [[Corneal Astigmatism]] |
| [[Phakic IOL]] | Implanted without removing natural lens | NOT pseudophakia (lens is retained) |

### By Material

| Material | Refractive Index | Foldability | Common Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Hydrophobic Acrylic]] | ~1.55 | Yes | AcrySof (Alcon), Tecnis (J&J Vision) |
| [[Hydrophilic Acrylic]] | ~1.46 | Yes | Various European designs |
| [[Silicone]] | ~1.41–1.46 | Yes | Less commonly used currently |
| [[PMMA]] | ~1.49 | No (rigid) | Historical; still used in MSICS |

### By Fixation Location

| Fixation Site | Description | Indications |
|---|---|---|
| [[Capsular Bag]] (in-the-bag) | Standard placement within intact capsular bag | Routine phacoemulsification |
| [[Ciliary Sulcus]] | Placed in sulcus anterior to capsular bag | Posterior capsule rupture, inadequate capsular support |
| [[Anterior Chamber IOL]] (ACIOL) | Placed in anterior chamber angle | Absent capsular support |
| [[Iris-Fixated IOL]] | Sutured or clipped to iris | Absent capsular/zonular support |
| [[Scleral-Fixated IOL]] | Sutured or glued to sclera | Absent capsular/zonular support |

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Expected Postoperative Findings

The pseudophakic patient typically presents with the following expected clinical findings:

- **Improved [[Visual Acuity]]**: The primary goal of cataract surgery. Most patients achieve best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA) of 20/20 to 20/40.
- **Stable refraction**: Refraction typically stabilizes within 2–6 weeks postoperatively.
- **IOL visible on [[Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy]]**: The IOL is seen within the capsular bag or at the intended fixation site.
- **Deepened [[Anterior Chamber]]**: Due to the thinner profile of the IOL compared to the natural lens.
- **Absence of [[Accommodation]]** (with monofocal IOL): Patients require near correction.

### Symptoms Reported by Pseudophakic Patients

While most patients are highly satisfied after cataract surgery, a subset may report visual symptoms that are inherent to the pseudophakic state or related to the specific IOL design:

| Symptom | Mechanism | Prevalence |
|---|---|---|
| Need for reading glasses | Loss of accommodation (monofocal IOL) | Nearly universal with monofocal IOL |
| [[Dysphotopsia]] (positive) | Halos, glare, starbursts from IOL edge or optic | 15–50% (higher with multifocal IOLs) |
| [[Dysphotopsia]] (negative) | Dark crescent or shadow in temporal visual field | 0.5–3% (may resolve over months) |
| Floaters | Pre-existing or new [[Posterior Vitreous Detachment]] | Variable |
| Altered color perception | Removal of yellow-tinted crystalline lens; increased blue light transmission | Common; usually adapts neurally |
| Reduced contrast sensitivity | Particularly with multifocal IOLs | Variable by IOL design |
| [[Aniseikonia]] | Unilateral pseudophakia with contralateral phakic eye | Uncommon with modern IOL calculations |

### Positive and Negative Dysphotopsia

**[[Positive Dysphotopsia]]** refers to unwanted light phenomena such as halos, glare, and starbursts. These are caused by light interacting with the IOL edge, surface reflections, or diffractive optics (in multifocal IOLs). Most patients experience neuroadaptation over 3–6 months.

**[[Negative Dysphotopsia]]** is a dark crescent or arc-shaped shadow typically perceived in the temporal visual field. It is thought to result from a gap between the IOL optic edge and the iris/capsular bag, creating a shadow on the peripheral [[Retina]]. It occurs in approximately 0.5–3% of pseudophakic patients and may be persistent. Management options include observation (most resolve), IOL exchange, reverse optic capture, or secondary piggyback IOL implantation.

## Diagnosis

### Confirming Pseudophakic Status

Pseudophakia is diagnosed by clinical examination and history:

1. **Patient history**: Prior cataract surgery or refractive lens exchange.
2. **[[Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy]]**: Direct visualization of the IOL within the eye. The IOL optic and haptics can be observed. The capsulorhexis edge may be visible overlying the IOL optic.
3. **[[Retroillumination]]**: Useful for assessing IOL centration, detecting posterior capsule opacification, and evaluating the capsulotomy.
4. **[[Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography]] (AS-OCT)**: Provides cross-sectional imaging of IOL position, tilt, and decentration.
5. **[[Ultrasound Biomicroscopy]] (UBM)**: Useful for evaluating sulcus-placed or scleral-fixated IOLs, and for assessing haptic position.

### Biometry and IOL Power Calculation

Preoperative biometry is critical for achieving the desired refractive outcome in pseudophakia. Key measurements include:

| Parameter | Measurement Device | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| [[Axial Length]] (AL) | [[Optical Biometry]] (IOLMaster, Lenstar), [[A-scan Ultrasonography]] | Most important variable in IOL power calculation |
| [[Keratometry]] (K) | Autokeratometer, [[Corneal Topography]], optical biometer | Corneal refractive power |
| [[Anterior Chamber Depth]] (ACD) | Optical biometry, AS-OCT | Predicts effective lens position (ELP) |
| [[Lens Thickness]] (LT) | Optical biometry | Used in newer-generation formulas |
| [[White-to-White]] (WTW) | Optical biometry, calipers | Corneal diameter; used in some formulas |
| [[Central Corneal Thickness]] (CCT) | [[Pachymetry]] | Minor role in some formulas |

### IOL Power Calculation Formulas

Modern IOL power calculation uses sophisticated formulas to predict the postoperative refraction. The evolution of these formulas has dramatically improved refractive outcomes in pseudophakia.

| Generation | Formula Examples | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| 1st Generation | Fyodorov, Binkhorst | Theoretical; limited accuracy |
| 2nd Generation | SRK | Regression-based; simple |
| 3rd Generation | [[SRK/T]], [[Holladay 1]], [[Hoffer Q]] | Improved ELP prediction |
| 4th Generation | [[Haigis]], [[Holladay 2]], [[Olsen]] | Multiple variables for ELP |
| Newer/AI-based | [[Barrett Universal II]], [[Hill-RBF]], [[Kane Formula]], [[EVO Formula]] | Machine learning, large datasets; highest accuracy |

**Special considerations** in IOL calculation include:
- **Post-[[LASIK]]/[[PRK]] eyes**: Altered corneal power requires adjusted formulas (e.g., [[Haigis-L]], [[Barrett True-K]], [[ASCRS Post-Refractive Calculator]])
- **High [[Myopia]]**: Longer axial lengths require optimized formulas (Wang-Koch adjustment, Barrett)
- **High [[Hyperopia]]**: Short eyes with higher IOL powers; greater prediction error
- **Pediatric pseudophakia**: Requires consideration of eye growth and refractive shift

## Complications of Pseudophakia

While modern cataract surgery is remarkably safe, the pseudophakic state carries specific risks for both early and late complications.

### Early Postoperative Complications

| Complication | Incidence | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| [[Corneal Edema]] | Common (transient) | Usually resolves within days to weeks |
| [[Cystoid Macular Edema]] (CME / [[Irvine-Gass Syndrome]]) | 1–2% (clinical); up to 20% (subclinical on OCT) | Peak incidence 4–6 weeks postoperatively |
| [[Endophthalmitis]] | 0.03–0.1% | Devastating; requires emergent treatment |
| [[Toxic Anterior Segment Syndrome]] (TASS) | Rare | Sterile inflammation; onset within 24 hours |
| Elevated [[Intraocular Pressure]] (IOP) | Variable | Due to retained viscoelastic, inflammation, or steroid response |
| IOL decentration/dislocation | <1% | May require repositioning or exchange |
| [[Wound Leak]] | Uncommon | Seidel-positive; may require suturing |
| [[Retained Lens Fragments]] | ~1% | May cause inflammation, glaucoma; may require [[Pars Plana Vitrectomy]] |

### Late Postoperative Complications

| Complication | Incidence | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Posterior Capsule Opacification]] (PCO)** | **20–50% within 5 years** (lower with modern IOLs) | Most common late complication; treated with [[Nd:YAG Laser Capsulotomy]] |
| [[Retinal Detachment]] | 0.5–1.5% (higher in myopes) | Increased risk compared to phakic eyes |
| Late IOL dislocation | 0.1–3% over decades | Associated with [[Pseudoexfoliation Syndrome]], prior vitrectomy, trauma |
| [[Capsular Phimosis]] | Variable | Contraction of capsulorhexis; more common with small CCC |
| IOL opacification/calcification | Rare (more common with hydrophilic acrylic) | May require IOL exchange |
| [[Uveitis-Glaucoma-Hyphema Syndrome]] (UGH) | Rare | Caused by IOL chafing on uveal tissue; typically with malpositioned IOL |
| Negative dysphotopsia (persistent) | 0.5–1% | May require surgical intervention |

### Posterior Capsule Opacification (PCO)

**[[Posterior Capsule Opacification]]** deserves special emphasis as the most common long-term complication of pseudophakia. PCO results from proliferation and migration of residual [[Lens Epithelial Cells]] (LECs) across the posterior capsule, causing opacification that degrades visual quality.

**Risk factors for PCO include:**
- Younger age at surgery
- [[Uveitis]]
- Hydrophilic acrylic IOL material (vs. hydrophobic acrylic)
- Round-edged IOL optic (vs. sharp square-edged)
- Incomplete cortical cleanup
- Large capsulorhexis not overlapping IOL optic edge

**Treatment**: [[Nd:YAG Laser Capsulotomy]] is the standard treatment. A focused Nd:YAG laser creates an opening in the opacified posterior capsule, restoring the visual axis. This is a brief outpatient procedure with high success rates. Complications of YAG capsulotomy include transient IOP elevation, IOL pitting, [[Cystoid Macular Edema]], and a small increased risk of [[Retinal Detachment]].

### Pseudophakic Retinal Detachment

The pseudophakic eye has an increased risk of [[Rhegmatogenous Retinal Detachment]] compared to the phakic eye. The cumulative incidence is approximately **0.5–1.5%** over 10–20 years, with higher rates in:
- Highly [[Myopic]] eyes (axial length >26 mm)
- Eyes that have undergone Nd:YAG capsulotomy
- Eyes with intraoperative complications (e.g., posterior capsule rupture, vitreous loss)
- Male patients
- Younger patients at time of surgery

The mechanism involves altered vitreous dynamics, increased vitreous liquefaction, and higher rates of [[Posterior Vitreous Detachment]] following lens removal.

### Pseudophakic Cystoid Macular Edema (Irvine-Gass Syndrome)

**[[Pseudophakic Cystoid Macular Edema]]** (PCME) is characterized by fluid accumulation in the outer plexiform and inner nuclear layers of the [[Macula]] following cataract surgery. It is mediated by surgical inflammation and breakdown of the [[Blood-Retinal Barrier]], with [[Prostaglandins]] playing a central role.

- **Clinical PCME**: Occurs in 1–2% of uncomplicated cases; higher with complicated surgery.
- **Subclinical PCME**: Detectable on [[Optical Coherence Tomography]] (OCT) in up to 20–40% of cases.
- **Peak onset**: 4–6 weeks postoperatively.
- **Risk factors**: [[Diabetes Mellitus]], [[Epiretinal Membrane]], prior [[Uveitis]], complicated surgery, use of [[Prostaglandin Analog]] glaucoma drops.

## Management of the Pseudophakic Patient

### Postoperative Pharmacological Regimen

The standard postoperative regimen following cataract surgery aims to control inflammation and prevent infection:

| Medication Class | Examples | Duration | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Topical Corticosteroid]] | [[Prednisolone Acetate]] 1%, [[Dexamethasone]], [[Difluprednate]] | 2–6 weeks (tapered) | Anti-inflammatory |
| [[Topical NSAID]] | [[Ketorolac]] 0.5%, [[Nepafenac]] 0.1%, [[Bromfenac]] 0.07% | 2–6 weeks | Anti-inflammatory; CME prophylaxis |
| [[Topical Antibiotic]] | [[Moxifloxacin]] 0.5%, [[Gatifloxacin]] 0.3%, [[Ofloxacin]] 0.3% | 1–2 weeks | Infection prophylaxis |
| [[Intracameral Antibiotic]] | [[Cefuroxime]] 1 mg/0.1 mL, [[Moxifloxacin]] | Single intraoperative dose | Endophthalmitis prophylaxis (ESCRS protocol) |

### Refraction and Spectacle Prescription

- **Timing**: Final refraction is typically performed **3–6 weeks** postoperatively, once the wound has healed and refraction has stabilized.
- **Monofocal IOL patients**: Will require spectacle correction for near tasks (and possibly distance if residual refractive error exists). Options include single-vision readers, [[Progressive Addition Lenses]], or [[Bifocal]] spectacles.
- **Toric IOL patients**: Residual astigmatism should be assessed; IOL rotation may be considered if significant residual cylinder is present.
- **Multifocal/EDOF IOL patients**: May achieve spectacle independence for most tasks but should be counseled about potential need for supplemental correction in certain lighting or working conditions.

### Monovision and Blended Vision Strategies

Some pseudophakic patients opt for **[[Monovision]]**, where the dominant eye is targeted for distance and the non-dominant eye for near (typically -1.25 to -2.00 D of myopia). **Mini-monovision** targets -0.75 to -1.25 D in the near eye. **Blended vision** using EDOF IOLs can extend the range of focus while minimizing anisometropia.

### Management of Posterior Capsule Opacification

As discussed, [[Nd:YAG Laser Capsulotomy]] is the definitive treatment for visually significant PCO. Indications include:
- Decreased visual acuity attributable to PCO
- Glare or reduced contrast sensitivity from PCO
- Inability to adequately visualize the [[Fundus]] for monitoring of posterior segment disease (e.g., [[Diabetic Retinopathy]], [[Age-Related Macular Degeneration]])

**Procedure**: The Nd:YAG laser delivers focused pulses to create a central opening (typically 3–4 mm) in the posterior capsule. Energy levels of 1–3 mJ per pulse are typical. The procedure is performed at the slit lamp with a contact lens (e.g., [[Abraham Capsulotomy Lens]]) to stabilize the eye and focus the beam.

### Management of IOL Dislocation

Late IOL dislocation (subluxation or complete dislocation into the [[Vitreous Cavity]]) may occur years to decades after surgery, particularly in patients with:
- [[Pseudoexfoliation Syndrome]]
- History of ocular trauma
- Prior [[Pars Plana Vitrectomy]]
- Connective tissue disorders (e.g., [[Marfan Syndrome]])

Management options include:
- **IOL repositioning** with scleral or iris fixation
- **IOL exchange** with a new scleral-fixated, iris-fixated, or anterior chamber IOL
- **[[Pars Plana Vitrectomy]]** for posteriorly dislocated IOLs

### Management of Pseudophakic CME

| Treatment | Route | Details |
|---|---|---|
| [[Topical NSAIDs]] | Topical | First-line; ketorolac, nepafenac, bromfenac |
| [[Topical Corticosteroids]] | Topical | Prednisolone acetate 1%, difluprednate |
| [[Sub-Tenon's Triamcinolone]] | Periocular injection | For refractory cases |
| [[Intravitreal Anti-VEGF]] | Intravitreal injection | [[Bevacizumab]], [[Ranibizumab]], [[Aflibercept]] for refractory cases |
| [[Intravitreal Dexamethasone Implant]] | Intravitreal | [[Ozurdex]] for chronic/refractory PCME |
| Oral [[Acetazolamide]] | Systemic | Adjunctive; reduces macular edema via carbonic anhydrase inhibition |

## Special Considerations in Pseudophakia

### Pseudophakia in Pediatric Patients

Cataract surgery in children (for [[Congenital Cataract]] or [[Developmental Cataract]]) presents unique challenges. The decision to implant an IOL depends on the child's age:

- **Infants (<1–2 years)**: Primary IOL implantation is controversial. The [[Infant Aphakia Treatment Study]] (IATS) demonstrated that contact lens correction of [[Aphakia]] had comparable visual outcomes to primary IOL implantation in unilateral congenital cataracts, with fewer adverse events in the aphakic group.
- **Children >2 years**: Primary IOL implantation is generally preferred.
- **IOL power selection**: Must account for the expected [[Myopic Shift]] as the eye grows. Undercorrection (targeting hyperopia) is standard practice.
- **Amblyopia management**: Aggressive [[Occlusion Therapy]] and/or [[Atropine Penalization]] of the fellow eye is critical for visual development.

### Pseudophakia and Glaucoma

Pseudophakic patients require ongoing [[Intraocular Pressure]] monitoring. Considerations include:
- **Steroid-induced IOP elevation**: From postoperative topical corticosteroids.
- **Altered IOP measurement**: IOL implantation and corneal changes may affect [[Goldmann Applanation Tonometry]] readings.
- **Angle anatomy changes**: Cataract extraction deepens the anterior chamber and opens the [[Iridocorneal Angle]], which can lower IOP in eyes with [[Angle-Closure Glaucoma]]. Combined [[Phacoemulsification]] and [[Goniosynechialysis]] or [[Minimally Invasive Glaucoma Surgery]] (MIGS) procedures (e.g., [[iStent]], [[Kahook Dual Blade]], [[Hydrus Microstent]]) are increasingly performed.

### Pseudophakia and Retinal Imaging

The pseudophakic state can affect retinal imaging:
- **[[Optical Coherence Tomography]] (OCT)**: Generally excellent image quality through a clear IOL. PCO may degrade signal.
- **[[Fundus Photography]]**: IOL edge reflections may cause artifacts.
- **[[Fluorescein Angiography]]**: Blue-light filtering IOLs may slightly alter fluorescence excitation.
- **[[Fundoscopy]]**: The pseudophakic eye often provides an excellent view of the fundus due to the clear optical media, facilitating monitoring of conditions such as [[Diabetic Retinopathy]], [[Age-Related Macular Degeneration]], and [[Glaucomatous Optic Neuropathy]].

### Pseudophakia and Refractive Surprise

A **refractive surprise** occurs when the postoperative refraction differs significantly from the intended target. Causes include:
- Biometry errors (incorrect axial length or keratometry)
- Incorrect IOL power selection or formula choice
- Effective lens position (ELP) prediction error
- IOL mislabeling (rare)
- Surgically induced astigmatism
- Prior refractive surgery (altered corneal power)

Management of refractive surprise includes:
- Spectacle or [[Contact Lens]] correction
- [[Laser Vision Correction]] ([[LASIK]], [[PRK]], [[SMILE]]) as an enhancement
- IOL exchange (typically within the first few weeks)
- [[Piggyback IOL]] (secondary sulcus-placed IOL to adjust power)
- [[Light Adjustable Lens]] (LAL) technology allows postoperative UV-mediated power adjustment

### Pseudophakic Bullous Keratopathy

**[[Pseudophakic Bullous Keratopathy]]** (PBK) is a condition of [[Corneal Endothelial]] cell loss and subsequent corneal decompensation occurring after cataract surgery and IOL implantation. It was historically more common with anterior chamber IOLs and closed-loop designs but can occur with any IOL type, particularly after complicated surgery. PBK may ultimately require [[Corneal Transplantation]] ([[Descemet's Stripping Automated Endothelial Keratoplasty|DSAEK]], [[Descemet's Membrane Endothelial Keratoplasty|DMEK]], or [[Penetrating Keratoplasty]]).

## Prognosis

The prognosis for pseudophakic patients is excellent. Modern cataract surgery achieves:

- **>95% of patients** achieve BCVA of 20/40 or better
- **>85% of patients** achieve BCVA of 20/20 or better (in eyes without comorbid ocular disease)
- **Patient satisfaction rates** exceed 95% in most large series
- Long-term IOL stability is excellent with modern posterior chamber IOLs

The most common reason for reduced visual outcome in pseudophakia is pre-existing ocular comorbidity, including [[Age-Related Macular Degeneration]], [[Diabetic Retinopathy]], [[Glaucoma]], and [[Corneal Disease]].

## Summary

Pseudophakia represents the successful restoration of the eye's optical system following removal of the natural crystalline lens and implantation of an artificial intraocular lens. It is the most common postoperative ocular state encountered in clinical practice and demands comprehensive understanding by optometrists and ophthalmologists alike. Key clinical considerations include appropriate IOL selection and power calculation, management of expected visual symptoms (particularly loss of accommodation and dysphotopsias), vigilance for complications (especially PCO, CME, retinal detachment, and endophthalmitis), and long-term monitoring of ocular health. Advances in IOL technology, biometry, and surgical technique continue to improve outcomes and expand the functional visual capabilities of pseudophakic patients.
