

# Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion

---

**Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion (BRVO)** is the second most common [[retinal vascular disorder]], surpassed only by [[diabetic retinopathy]], and represents the most prevalent form of [[retinal vein occlusion]] (RVO). BRVO is characterized by the obstruction of blood flow in one or more branches of the [[central retinal vein]], leading to upstream venous congestion, [[retinal hemorrhage]], [[macular edema]], and potential vision loss. It is a significant cause of unilateral visual morbidity, particularly in patients over the age of 50, and its management requires a thorough understanding of retinal vascular anatomy, systemic risk factor modification, and modern intravitreal pharmacotherapy. This article provides an exhaustive clinical reference for eye care professionals, encompassing epidemiology, pathophysiology, clinical presentation, diagnostic modalities, differential diagnosis, management strategies, complications, prognosis, and prevention.

---

## Epidemiology

BRVO is the most common subtype of [[retinal vein occlusion]], accounting for approximately 70–80% of all RVO cases. Population-based studies estimate the prevalence of BRVO at approximately 0.6–1.1% in adults over the age of 40, with incidence increasing significantly with advancing age. The landmark [[Beaver Dam Eye Study]] and the [[Blue Mountains Eye Study]] have provided robust epidemiological data confirming these estimates.

| **Epidemiological Parameter** | **Data** |
|---|---|
| Prevalence (adults >40 years) | 0.6–1.1% |
| Proportion of all RVO cases | ~70–80% |
| Mean age at presentation | 60–70 years |
| Gender predilection | Slight male predominance or equal |
| Bilateral involvement | 5–10% over 5 years |
| Most common site of occlusion | Superotemporal branch |

The superotemporal branch of the [[central retinal vein]] is the most frequently affected site, involved in approximately 63% of cases, followed by the inferotemporal branch (~36%). Nasal branch occlusions are rare and often clinically silent due to their distance from the [[macula]]. There is no strong racial predilection, although some studies suggest a slightly higher prevalence in populations with elevated rates of [[hypertension]] and [[cardiovascular disease]].

## Anatomy and Pathophysiology

### Relevant Retinal Vascular Anatomy

Understanding the pathophysiology of BRVO requires a detailed appreciation of [[retinal vascular anatomy]]. The [[central retinal artery]] and [[central retinal vein]] enter and exit the eye through the [[optic nerve head]]. After emerging from the optic disc, the central retinal vein divides into superior and inferior branches, which further bifurcate into temporal and nasal tributaries. These venous branches travel within the [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] and are responsible for draining deoxygenated blood from the retinal capillary beds.

At [[arteriovenous (AV) crossings]], the retinal artery and vein share a common [[adventitial sheath]]. This anatomical relationship is of paramount importance in the pathogenesis of BRVO. There are typically 15–30 AV crossings in each eye, and in the vast majority of cases (approximately 97–99%), the artery lies anterior to (crosses over) the vein. At these crossings, the rigid arterial wall can exert mechanical compression on the more compliant venous wall, particularly when the artery is affected by [[arteriosclerosis]].

### Pathogenesis

The pathogenesis of BRVO is multifactorial and is best understood through [[Virchow's triad]], which describes three broad categories of factors contributing to thrombosis:

1. **Vessel wall damage (endothelial injury):** [[Arteriosclerotic]] changes in the retinal arteriole at the AV crossing cause thickening and rigidity of the arterial wall. This leads to chronic mechanical compression of the adjacent vein, resulting in endothelial damage, turbulent blood flow, and predisposition to thrombus formation.

2. **Hemodynamic changes (venous stasis):** Compression of the vein at the AV crossing narrows the venous lumen, reducing blood flow velocity and promoting stasis. This stagnation of blood is a potent stimulus for thrombogenesis.

3. **Hypercoagulability:** Systemic conditions that increase blood viscosity or promote a [[hypercoagulable state]]—such as [[hyperlipidemia]], [[polycythemia vera]], [[antiphospholipid syndrome]], [[hyperhomocysteinemia]], and [[Factor V Leiden mutation]]—may further predispose to thrombus formation at the site of venous compression.

The resulting thrombus obstructs venous outflow from the affected retinal sector, leading to a cascade of pathological events:

- **Increased intraluminal venous pressure** proximal to the occlusion
- **Rupture of retinal capillaries**, producing intraretinal hemorrhages in the distribution of the occluded vein (characteristically flame-shaped hemorrhages in the [[nerve fiber layer]] and dot-blot hemorrhages in the deeper retinal layers)
- **Breakdown of the [[blood-retinal barrier]]**, resulting in exudation of fluid and proteins into the retinal tissue and the development of [[macular edema]]
- **Retinal ischemia** in severe cases, due to capillary non-perfusion, which triggers upregulation of [[vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF)]] and other pro-angiogenic cytokines
- **Neovascularization** in ischemic BRVO, which may lead to [[vitreous hemorrhage]], [[tractional retinal detachment]], or [[neovascular glaucoma]] (though the latter is far more common in [[central retinal vein occlusion]])

The upregulation of [[VEGF]] is central to the pathophysiology of both macular edema and neovascularization in BRVO. Elevated levels of VEGF-A, [[interleukin-6 (IL-6)]], and [[interleukin-8 (IL-8)]] have been demonstrated in the [[vitreous humor]] and [[aqueous humor]] of eyes with BRVO, providing the rationale for [[anti-VEGF therapy]].

### Classification: Ischemic vs. Non-Ischemic BRVO

BRVO is clinically classified based on the degree of retinal capillary non-perfusion, as determined by [[fluorescein angiography (FA)]] or [[optical coherence tomography angiography (OCTA)]]:

| **Feature** | **Non-Ischemic (Perfused) BRVO** | **Ischemic (Non-Perfused) BRVO** |
|---|---|---|
| Proportion of cases | ~50–70% | ~30–50% |
| Visual acuity at presentation | Often better (≥20/40) | Often worse (<20/200) |
| Capillary non-perfusion on FA | <5 disc areas | ≥5 disc areas |
| Retinal hemorrhage | Mild to moderate | Extensive, dense |
| Cotton-wool spots | Few or absent | Multiple |
| Risk of neovascularization | Low (~1–2%) | High (up to 40%) |
| Macular edema | Common | Common |
| Prognosis | Generally favorable | Guarded |

It is important to note that non-ischemic BRVO may convert to ischemic BRVO over time, necessitating serial monitoring.

## Risk Factors

BRVO is strongly associated with systemic vascular risk factors. Identification and management of these risk factors are essential components of comprehensive patient care.

| **Risk Factor** | **Mechanism / Association** |
|---|---|
| [[Systemic hypertension]] | Most significant risk factor; promotes arteriosclerosis at AV crossings |
| [[Hyperlipidemia]] | Contributes to arteriosclerotic vessel wall changes |
| [[Diabetes mellitus]] | Promotes microvascular disease and endothelial dysfunction |
| [[Cardiovascular disease]] | Marker of generalized atherosclerosis |
| [[Smoking]] | Endothelial damage, increased blood viscosity |
| [[Obesity]] | Associated with hypertension, diabetes, and hyperlipidemia |
| [[Open-angle glaucoma]] / [[Ocular hypertension]] | Elevated [[intraocular pressure]] may compress the central retinal vein at the [[lamina cribrosa]] |
| [[Hypercoagulable states]] | Factor V Leiden, antiphospholipid antibodies, hyperhomocysteinemia |
| [[Hyperviscosity syndromes]] | Polycythemia vera, Waldenström macroglobulinemia |
| Age >50 years | Increased arteriosclerosis |
| [[Oral contraceptive]] use | Prothrombotic state |
| [[Obstructive sleep apnea]] | Nocturnal hypoxia, endothelial dysfunction |

All patients presenting with BRVO should undergo a thorough systemic evaluation, including measurement of [[blood pressure]], fasting [[blood glucose]] or [[HbA1c]], [[lipid panel]], [[complete blood count (CBC)]], [[erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)]], and, in younger patients or those without traditional risk factors, a [[thrombophilia screen]] (including [[homocysteine]], [[antiphospholipid antibodies]], [[protein C]], [[protein S]], and [[antithrombin III]] levels).

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

The hallmark symptom of BRVO is **sudden, painless, unilateral decrease in visual acuity** or **visual field defect** corresponding to the affected retinal sector. The severity of visual loss depends on the degree of macular involvement:

- **Mild cases:** Patients may be asymptomatic or report only a subtle scotoma or blurred vision, particularly if the occlusion involves a nasal or peripheral branch that does not affect the [[macula]].
- **Moderate to severe cases:** Significant central vision loss occurs when [[macular edema]] or macular hemorrhage is present. Patients may describe metamorphopsia, relative scotoma, or a "curtain" or "shadow" in a portion of their visual field.
- **Acute presentation:** Symptoms typically develop over hours to days. Some patients may notice symptoms upon waking, suggesting a nocturnal onset related to systemic [[hypotension]] and reduced perfusion pressure during sleep.

### Signs (Fundoscopic Findings)

The clinical diagnosis of BRVO is primarily made by [[dilated fundus examination]]. The classic fundoscopic appearance includes:

- **Flame-shaped and dot-blot [[retinal hemorrhages]]** distributed in a sectoral pattern corresponding to the drainage territory of the occluded venous branch. The hemorrhages respect the horizontal [[raphe]] and do not cross the midline.
- **Dilated, tortuous retinal veins** in the affected sector.
- **[[Cotton-wool spots]]** (soft exudates), indicating focal retinal nerve fiber layer ischemia.
- **[[Retinal edema]]**, particularly [[macular edema]], which is the most common cause of vision loss in BRVO.
- **[[Hard exudates]]** (lipid deposits) in chronic cases, often arranged in a circinate pattern around the macula.
- **Visible arteriovenous crossing changes** (AV nicking, [[Gunn sign]], [[Salus sign]]), reflecting underlying [[arteriosclerosis]].
- In ischemic BRVO: **extensive hemorrhage**, **multiple cotton-wool spots**, and, in later stages, **[[retinal neovascularization]]** (NVE—neovascularization elsewhere, or NVD—neovascularization at the disc).

Over weeks to months, the acute hemorrhages gradually resolve, and the fundus appearance may evolve to show:

- Resolution of hemorrhages with residual hard exudates
- Persistent or chronic [[cystoid macular edema (CME)]]
- Development of [[collateral vessels]] (shunt vessels), which are dilated, tortuous venous channels that bypass the site of occlusion—these are a sign of chronicity and should not be confused with neovascularization
- [[Epiretinal membrane]] formation
- [[Retinal neovascularization]] in ischemic cases
- [[Vitreous hemorrhage]] secondary to neovascularization

## Diagnostic Modalities

### 1. Dilated Fundus Examination

The cornerstone of diagnosis. [[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] with a [[78D]] or [[90D]] condensing lens, or [[binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy (BIO)]], allows detailed visualization of the retinal vasculature, hemorrhages, edema, and any neovascularization.

### 2. Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

[[Optical coherence tomography]] is indispensable in the evaluation and management of BRVO. [[Spectral-domain OCT (SD-OCT)]] provides high-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the retina and is the gold standard for:

- **Quantifying [[macular edema]]:** Central subfield thickness (CST) measurements guide treatment decisions. A CST >300 µm (on most platforms) is generally considered clinically significant.
- **Identifying cystoid spaces:** Intraretinal cystic changes characteristic of [[cystoid macular edema]].
- **Detecting subretinal fluid (SRF):** Present in some cases of BRVO-associated macular edema.
- **Assessing outer retinal integrity:** Disruption of the [[ellipsoid zone]] (formerly the IS/OS junction) and the [[external limiting membrane (ELM)]] are associated with poorer visual prognosis.
- **Monitoring treatment response:** Serial OCT is used to track resolution of macular edema following intravitreal injections.

### 3. Fluorescein Angiography (FA)

[[Fluorescein angiography]] remains a critical diagnostic tool for BRVO, providing dynamic information about retinal perfusion:

- **Delayed venous filling** in the affected sector
- **Capillary non-perfusion areas:** Quantification of non-perfusion is essential for classifying BRVO as ischemic vs. non-ischemic. ≥5 disc areas of capillary non-perfusion defines ischemic BRVO (per the [[Branch Vein Occlusion Study (BVOS)]])
- **Macular edema:** Late-phase hyperfluorescence due to leakage from damaged capillaries
- **Neovascularization:** Profuse leakage from new vessels
- **Collateral vessel formation**
- **Microaneurysms** in the affected territory

### 4. Optical Coherence Tomography Angiography (OCTA)

[[OCTA]] is a non-invasive, dye-free imaging modality that provides depth-resolved visualization of the retinal and choroidal vasculature. In BRVO, OCTA is useful for:

- Detecting and quantifying areas of **capillary non-perfusion** in the superficial and deep capillary plexuses
- Identifying **macular ischemia** (enlargement of the [[foveal avascular zone (FAZ)]])
- Detecting subtle **neovascularization**
- Monitoring vascular remodeling and collateral formation over time

OCTA has the advantage of being non-invasive and repeatable, though it has a limited field of view compared to widefield FA and cannot demonstrate leakage.

### 5. Widefield and Ultra-Widefield Imaging

[[Ultra-widefield fundus photography]] (e.g., [[Optos]]) and [[ultra-widefield fluorescein angiography]] allow visualization of the peripheral retina, which is particularly valuable for:

- Detecting peripheral capillary non-perfusion that may be missed on standard FA
- Identifying peripheral neovascularization
- Documenting the full extent of retinal hemorrhage

### 6. Fundus Photography

Standard [[fundus photography]] provides baseline documentation and is useful for monitoring hemorrhage resolution and disease progression over time.

### 7. Visual Field Testing

[[Automated perimetry]] (e.g., [[Humphrey visual field]]) may reveal a scotoma corresponding to the affected retinal sector. This is particularly relevant for BRVO involving the papillomacular bundle or the arcuate nerve fiber bundles.

## Differential Diagnosis

Several conditions may mimic the fundoscopic appearance of BRVO and must be considered:

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| [[Central retinal vein occlusion (CRVO)]] | Hemorrhages in all four quadrants; involves the entire retina |
| [[Hemi-retinal vein occlusion]] | Involves superior or inferior hemisphere; may be classified as a variant of CRVO |
| [[Diabetic retinopathy]] | Bilateral, symmetric; microaneurysms, hard exudates, neovascularization in characteristic patterns |
| [[Hypertensive retinopathy]] | Bilateral; generalized arteriolar narrowing, AV nicking, flame hemorrhages in all quadrants |
| [[Ocular ischemic syndrome]] | Dilated but non-tortuous veins; mid-peripheral dot-blot hemorrhages; low IOP; carotid stenosis |
| [[Papilledema]] | Bilateral disc edema; hemorrhages peripapillary; elevated intracranial pressure |
| [[Radiation retinopathy]] | History of radiation; cotton-wool spots, hemorrhages, microaneurysms |
| [[Retinal macroaneurysm]] | Focal dilation of retinal arteriole; localized hemorrhage |

## Management

The management of BRVO is directed at two primary goals: (1) **treatment of macular edema** to restore and preserve central visual acuity, and (2) **prevention and treatment of neovascular complications** in ischemic disease.

### Observation

Not all patients with BRVO require immediate treatment. According to the [[Branch Vein Occlusion Study (BVOS)]], approximately 50–60% of eyes with BRVO and macular edema will achieve a final visual acuity of 20/40 or better without treatment. However, modern anti-VEGF therapy has significantly improved outcomes, and current practice favors earlier intervention for visually significant macular edema.

**Observation may be appropriate when:**
- Visual acuity is 20/40 or better and the patient is asymptomatic
- Macular edema is mild and not center-involving
- The hemorrhage is acute and may resolve spontaneously

### Pharmacological Treatment

#### Anti-VEGF Therapy (First-Line Treatment)

[[Anti-VEGF]] intravitreal injections are the current **standard of care** for the treatment of macular edema secondary to BRVO. Multiple landmark randomized controlled trials have established their efficacy:

| **Agent** | **Landmark Trial** | **Key Findings** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Ranibizumab]] (Lucentis®) | [[BRAVO Study]] | Significant VA improvement at 6 months; mean gain of 18.3 letters (0.5 mg) vs. 7.3 letters (sham) |
| [[Aflibercept]] (Eylea®) | [[VIBRANT Study]] | Superior to grid laser at 24 weeks; mean gain of 17.0 letters vs. 6.9 letters (laser) |
| [[Bevacizumab]] (Avastin®) | Multiple studies (off-label) | Effective and widely used due to lower cost; comparable efficacy to ranibizumab in many studies |
| [[Brolucizumab]] (Beovu®) | [[RAPTOR/RAVEN]] | Emerging data supporting efficacy in RVO-related macular edema |
| [[Faricimab]] (Vabysmo®) | [[BALATON/COMINO]] | Dual-pathway inhibition (VEGF-A and Ang-2); non-inferior to aflibercept with potential for extended dosing intervals |

**Treatment Protocol:**
- **Loading phase:** Typically 3 monthly intravitreal injections
- **Maintenance phase:** Treat-and-extend (TAE) or pro re nata (PRN) regimen guided by OCT findings and visual acuity
- **Treatment endpoint:** Resolution of macular edema (CST normalization), stabilization of visual acuity
- **Duration:** Many patients require treatment for 2–3 years or longer; some achieve sustained resolution

#### Intravitreal Corticosteroids

[[Intravitreal corticosteroids]] are a second-line option for macular edema in BRVO, particularly in eyes that are **pseudophakic** (to avoid steroid-induced [[cataract]]) or in patients who are **refractory to anti-VEGF therapy**.

| **Agent** | **Formulation** | **Duration of Action** | **Key Considerations** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Dexamethasone intravitreal implant]] (Ozurdex®) | Biodegradable implant (0.7 mg) | ~4–6 months | FDA-approved for RVO-related macular edema; [[GENEVA trial]] demonstrated efficacy; risk of IOP elevation and cataract |
| [[Triamcinolone acetonide]] (Triesence®, Kenalog®) | Intravitreal injection (1–4 mg) | ~3 months | Off-label; effective but higher risk of IOP elevation and cataract than Ozurdex |
| [[Fluocinolone acetonide implant]] (Iluvien®) | Non-biodegradable implant | ~36 months | Primarily studied in [[diabetic macular edema]]; limited data in BRVO |

**Risks of intravitreal corticosteroids include:**
- Elevated [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) / steroid-induced [[glaucoma]] (occurs in ~30% of patients)
- Accelerated [[posterior subcapsular cataract]] formation
- [[Endophthalmitis]] (rare, <0.1%)

#### Combination Therapy

In refractory cases, combination therapy with anti-VEGF agents and intravitreal corticosteroids, or anti-VEGF agents and [[macular laser photocoagulation]], may be considered.

### Laser Photocoagulation

#### Macular Grid Laser

The [[Branch Vein Occlusion Study (BVOS)]] (1984) established that **grid-pattern [[argon laser photocoagulation]]** applied to the area of macular edema (avoiding the foveal avascular zone) improved visual acuity in eyes with BRVO and macular edema with visual acuity of 20/40 or worse, provided the hemorrhage had cleared sufficiently to allow treatment. However, with the advent of anti-VEGF therapy, grid laser is now considered a **second-line or adjunctive treatment** rather than first-line therapy.

**BVOS Criteria for Grid Laser:**
- Visual acuity ≤20/40
- Macular edema present on FA
- Hemorrhage has cleared sufficiently to visualize the macula
- Duration of macular edema ≥3 months (to allow for spontaneous resolution)

#### Scatter (Panretinal) Laser Photocoagulation

The BVOS also demonstrated that **sectoral scatter laser photocoagulation** is effective in preventing [[vitreous hemorrhage]] from retinal neovascularization in ischemic BRVO. Current guidelines recommend:

- **Prophylactic scatter laser is NOT recommended** for ischemic BRVO without neovascularization
- **Scatter laser IS recommended** when neovascularization develops (NVE or NVD in the affected sector)
- Treatment is applied to the ischemic retinal sector, not the entire retina (unlike panretinal photocoagulation for [[proliferative diabetic retinopathy]])

### Surgical Management

#### Pars Plana Vitrectomy (PPV)

[[Pars plana vitrectomy]] may be indicated in BRVO for the following complications:

- **Non-clearing [[vitreous hemorrhage]]:** When hemorrhage from neovascularization obscures the fundus and prevents monitoring or laser treatment
- **[[Tractional retinal detachment]]** or combined tractional-rhegmatogenous detachment involving the macula
- **[[Epiretinal membrane]]** causing significant visual distortion or decline
- **Chronic, refractory macular edema:** Some evidence suggests that vitrectomy with [[internal limiting membrane (ILM) peeling]] may benefit select cases of chronic macular edema unresponsive to pharmacotherapy

#### Arteriovenous Sheathotomy

[[Arteriovenous sheathotomy]] (also called adventitial sheathotomy) is an experimental surgical procedure in which the common adventitial sheath at the AV crossing is incised to decompress the occluded vein. While some case series have reported favorable outcomes, this procedure is not widely adopted due to technical difficulty, risk of complications, and the availability of effective pharmacological alternatives.

#### Radial Optic Neurotomy

[[Radial optic neurotomy]] has been described for CRVO but has no established role in BRVO management.

## Complications

| **Complication** | **Mechanism** | **Management** |
|---|---|---|
| Chronic [[macular edema]] | Persistent VEGF-driven vascular leakage | Anti-VEGF, corticosteroids, laser |
| [[Macular ischemia]] | Capillary non-perfusion involving the fovea | No effective treatment; poor visual prognosis |
| [[Retinal neovascularization]] (NVE/NVD) | VEGF-driven angiogenesis in ischemic BRVO | Scatter laser, anti-VEGF |
| [[Vitreous hemorrhage]] | Bleeding from fragile new vessels | Observation, anti-VEGF, vitrectomy |
| [[Epiretinal membrane]] | Glial proliferation on retinal surface | Observation or vitrectomy with membrane peel |
| [[Tractional retinal detachment]] | Fibrovascular proliferation and contraction | Vitrectomy |
| [[Neovascular glaucoma]] | Rare in BRVO (more common in CRVO); anterior segment neovascularization | Anti-VEGF, PRP, glaucoma surgery |
| [[Macular hole]] | Rare; related to chronic edema or vitreomacular traction | Vitrectomy with ILM peel |

## Prognosis

The visual prognosis of BRVO is generally more favorable than that of [[CRVO]]. Key prognostic factors include:

- **Baseline visual acuity:** Better presenting VA is associated with better final VA
- **Degree of macular ischemia:** Foveal capillary non-perfusion is the strongest predictor of poor visual outcome
- **Integrity of the [[ellipsoid zone]] and [[external limiting membrane]]** on OCT: Disruption correlates with worse visual outcomes
- **Promptness of treatment:** Early initiation of anti-VEGF therapy is associated with superior visual gains
- **Chronicity of macular edema:** Prolonged edema leads to irreversible photoreceptor damage

**Natural history (without treatment):**
- ~50–60% achieve VA ≥20/40 (BVOS data)
- ~25% develop chronic macular edema
- ~40% of ischemic BRVO develop neovascularization within 3 years

**With modern anti-VEGF therapy:**
- Mean visual acuity gains of 15–18 ETDRS letters at 12 months
- >50% of treated patients achieve VA ≥20/40
- Significant reduction in the need for laser photocoagulation

## Systemic Workup and Risk Factor Management

Given the strong association between BRVO and systemic vascular disease, all patients diagnosed with BRVO should undergo a comprehensive systemic evaluation. Coordination with the patient's [[primary care physician]] or [[internist]] is essential.

**Recommended systemic workup:**

| **Test** | **Purpose** |
|---|---|
| [[Blood pressure]] measurement | Screen for [[hypertension]] |
| Fasting [[blood glucose]] / [[HbA1c]] | Screen for [[diabetes mellitus]] |
| [[Lipid panel]] (total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, triglycerides) | Screen for [[hyperlipidemia]] |
| [[Complete blood count (CBC)]] | Screen for [[polycythemia]], [[leukemia]], [[thrombocytosis]] |
| [[Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)]] / [[C-reactive protein (CRP)]] | Screen for inflammatory or vasculitic conditions |
| [[Homocysteine]] level | Screen for [[hyperhomocysteinemia]] (especially in younger patients) |
| [[Thrombophilia screen]] (in patients <50 or without traditional risk factors) | Protein C, Protein S, Antithrombin III, Factor V Leiden, Antiphospholipid antibodies |
| [[Electrocardiogram (ECG)]] | Screen for [[atrial fibrillation]] or other cardiac disease |
| [[Carotid Doppler ultrasound]] | Consider if signs of [[ocular ischemic syndrome]] |

**Risk factor modification includes:**
- Aggressive [[blood pressure control]] (target <130/80 mmHg per current guidelines)
- [[Glycemic control]] in diabetic patients
- [[Statin therapy]] for hyperlipidemia
- [[Smoking cessation]]
- Weight management and regular exercise
- Treatment of [[obstructive sleep apnea]] if present

## Follow-Up Schedule

| **Time Point** | **Assessment** |
|---|---|
| Initial visit | Comprehensive dilated exam, OCT, consider FA/OCTA, systemic workup referral |
| 1 month | OCT, VA; initiate treatment if center-involving macular edema with VA loss |
| Monthly (during loading phase) | OCT, VA; intravitreal injection if indicated |
| Every 1–3 months (maintenance) | OCT, VA; treat-and-extend or PRN protocol |
| Every 3–6 months (stable) | OCT, VA; monitor for recurrence, neovascularization |
| Annually (long-term) | Comprehensive exam; monitor fellow eye; systemic risk factor review |

Patients should be counseled to report any new symptoms (floaters, vision loss, visual field changes) promptly, as these may indicate neovascularization, vitreous hemorrhage, or recurrence of macular edema.

## Special Considerations

### BRVO in Young Patients

BRVO in patients under 50 years of age is uncommon and warrants a more extensive workup for [[hypercoagulable states]], [[autoimmune vasculitis]] (e.g., [[systemic lupus erythematosus]], [[Behçet disease]]), [[sarcoidosis]], and other inflammatory or hematologic conditions. [[Oral contraceptive]] use and [[pregnancy]]-related hypercoagulability should also be considered in young women.

### BRVO and Glaucoma

[[Open-angle glaucoma]] and [[ocular hypertension]] are established risk factors for BRVO. The mechanism is thought to involve compression of the [[central retinal vein]] at the [[lamina cribrosa]] due to elevated IOP, predisposing to venous stasis. Conversely, intravitreal corticosteroid treatment for BRVO-related macular edema may exacerbate or induce [[steroid-induced glaucoma]], requiring careful IOP monitoring.

### Macular Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion

A subtype of BRVO involving only a small macular venule is termed **macular BRVO**. These occlusions affect a limited area of the [[perifoveal capillary network]] and may present with localized macular edema and a small sector of hemorrhage. Visual prognosis is generally good, though chronic macular edema may require treatment.

---

## Summary

Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion is a common, sight-threatening retinal vascular condition with a well-characterized pathophysiology rooted in arteriosclerotic changes at arteriovenous crossings. Its management has been revolutionized by [[anti-VEGF therapy]], which has become the first-line treatment for visually significant macular edema. Comprehensive care requires accurate diagnosis through multimodal imaging (OCT, FA, OCTA), classification of ischemic status, timely pharmacological intervention, vigilant monitoring for neovascular complications, and thorough systemic risk factor assessment and modification. Eye care professionals, including [[optometrists]] and [[ophthalmologists]], play a critical role in the early detection, co-management, and long-term follow-up of patients with BRVO.
