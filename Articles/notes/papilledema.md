# Papilledema

**Papilledema** is the bilateral swelling of the [[optic disc]] resulting from elevated [[intracranial pressure]] (ICP). The term is strictly reserved for optic disc edema caused by raised ICP and must be distinguished from other causes of [[optic disc edema]], such as [[optic neuritis]], [[anterior ischemic optic neuropathy]] (AION), or [[pseudopapilledema]]. Papilledema represents a critical neuro-ophthalmic emergency, as it is frequently the presenting sign of life-threatening intracranial pathology, including [[intracranial neoplasm]], [[cerebral venous sinus thrombosis]], [[meningitis]], and [[idiopathic intracranial hypertension]] (IIH). Early recognition by the [[optometrist]] or [[ophthalmologist]] is paramount, as delayed diagnosis can result in permanent [[visual field]] loss and irreversible [[optic atrophy]].

---

## Etymology and Terminology

The word "papilledema" derives from the Latin *papilla* (nipple, referring to the optic disc) and the Greek *oídēma* (swelling). In clinical practice, the term should be used exclusively when disc swelling is attributable to raised [[intracranial pressure]]. When the etiology is uncertain or non-ICP-related, the more general term "optic disc edema" or "optic disc swelling" is preferred. This distinction is clinically vital because the differential diagnosis, workup, and urgency differ substantially between papilledema and other forms of disc swelling.

---

## Epidemiology

The epidemiology of papilledema is closely linked to its underlying etiologies. [[Idiopathic intracranial hypertension]] (IIH), formerly known as [[pseudotumor cerebri]], is the most common cause of papilledema in the developed world, with an annual incidence of approximately 1–3 per 100,000 in the general population, rising to 12–20 per 100,000 in obese women of childbearing age. Space-occupying [[intracranial lesions]], [[hydrocephalus]], [[cerebral venous sinus thrombosis]], and [[meningitis]] account for the remaining significant etiologies. Papilledema can occur at any age, including in children, though the clinical presentation and differential diagnosis may differ in pediatric populations.

## Anatomy and Pathophysiology

### Relevant Anatomy

Understanding papilledema requires a thorough knowledge of the anatomy of the [[optic nerve]] and its surrounding structures. The optic nerve is a white matter tract of the [[central nervous system]] (CNS), ensheathed by the three [[meninges]]: the [[pia mater]], [[arachnoid mater]], and [[dura mater]]. The [[subarachnoid space]] surrounding the optic nerve is continuous with the intracranial subarachnoid space and is filled with [[cerebrospinal fluid]] (CSF). The [[optic nerve head]] (ONH), or optic disc, is the site where approximately 1.2 million [[retinal ganglion cell]] (RGC) axons converge and exit the globe through the [[lamina cribrosa]], a sieve-like collagenous structure within the [[sclera]].

The [[central retinal vein]] (CRV) and [[central retinal artery]] (CRA) traverse the optic nerve and pass through the lamina cribrosa. The [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL) axons are unmyelinated within the retina and become myelinated posterior to the lamina cribrosa. The optic disc is supplied by the [[short posterior ciliary arteries]] (SPCAs) and branches of the [[ophthalmic artery]], with the surface nerve fiber layer receiving blood from [[retinal arterioles]].

### Mechanism of Disc Swelling

The pathophysiology of papilledema is fundamentally related to the transmission of elevated CSF pressure through the subarachnoid space surrounding the optic nerve to the region of the optic nerve head. The sequence of events is as follows:

1. **Elevated Intracranial Pressure:** Any process that increases ICP—whether mass lesion, obstruction of CSF flow, impaired CSF absorption, or increased CSF production—raises the pressure within the intracranial subarachnoid space.

2. **Transmission to the Optic Nerve Sheath:** Because the subarachnoid space is continuous around the optic nerve, elevated ICP is transmitted to the perioptic subarachnoid space, increasing the pressure surrounding the retrobulbar optic nerve.

3. **Axoplasmic Flow Stasis:** The elevated pressure differential across the [[lamina cribrosa]] impedes both orthograde (toward the [[lateral geniculate nucleus]]) and retrograde axoplasmic transport within the RGC axons. This results in accumulation of axoplasmic material (organelles, mitochondria, and other cellular contents) at the level of the optic disc, causing axonal swelling.

4. **Venous Congestion:** Compression of the [[central retinal vein]] as it traverses the optic nerve sheath and lamina cribrosa leads to venous stasis, capillary dilation, and leakage of fluid into the peripapillary [[retina]] and optic nerve head. This contributes to the visible disc edema, [[peripapillary hemorrhages]], and [[cotton-wool spots]].

5. **Extracellular Edema:** Disruption of the [[blood-brain barrier]] and [[blood-retinal barrier]] at the optic nerve head leads to extracellular fluid accumulation, further contributing to disc swelling.

6. **Secondary Ischemia:** In chronic or severe papilledema, sustained compression of the optic nerve vasculature can lead to ischemic damage to the RGC axons, resulting in progressive [[optic atrophy]] and irreversible visual loss.

### Stages of Papilledema

Papilledema progresses through recognized clinical stages, as described by the **Modified Frisén Scale**:

| **Frisén Grade** | **Clinical Features** |
|---|---|
| **Grade 0 (Normal)** | Normal optic disc; no swelling; clear disc margins; spontaneous [[venous pulsation]] may be present |
| **Grade 1 (Very Early)** | C-shaped halo of disc edema (nasal, superior, inferior); disruption of normal radial RNFL arrangement; temporal margin spared; subtle obscuration of disc margin |
| **Grade 2 (Early)** | Circumferential halo of edema; complete obscuration of disc margin; concentric or radial [[retinal folds]] (Paton's lines) may appear |
| **Grade 3 (Moderate)** | Increased disc diameter; obscuration of one or more major [[retinal vessels]] leaving the disc; peripapillary hemorrhages and cotton-wool spots |
| **Grade 4 (Marked)** | Total obscuration of a major vessel segment on the disc; marked elevation of the optic nerve head; possible [[exudates]] |
| **Grade 5 (Severe)** | Dome-shaped protrusion of the optic nerve head; complete obscuration of all vessels on the disc; peripapillary hemorrhages and exudates may be extensive; possible partial or total obliteration of the [[optic cup]] |

Chronic papilledema may eventually lead to a pale, atrophic disc with gliotic changes, referred to as "vintage" or "atrophic" papilledema, signifying irreversible axonal loss.

## Etiology

Papilledema is always secondary to elevated ICP. The causes of raised ICP can be broadly categorized:

| **Category** | **Examples** |
|---|---|
| **Space-Occupying Lesions** | [[Brain tumor]], [[intracranial abscess]], [[subdural hematoma]], [[epidural hematoma]] |
| **Obstruction of CSF Flow** | [[Hydrocephalus]] (obstructive/non-communicating), [[aqueductal stenosis]], [[Chiari malformation]] |
| **Impaired CSF Absorption** | [[Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis]], [[arachnoid granulation]] dysfunction, post-[[meningitis]] scarring |
| **Increased CSF Production** | [[Choroid plexus papilloma]] (rare) |
| **Idiopathic** | [[Idiopathic intracranial hypertension]] (IIH / [[pseudotumor cerebri]]) |
| **Infectious/Inflammatory** | [[Meningitis]], [[encephalitis]], [[neurosarcoidosis]] |
| **Vascular** | [[Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis]], [[dural arteriovenous fistula]], [[superior vena cava syndrome]] |
| **Medications** | [[Tetracycline]], [[minocycline]], [[vitamin A]] excess, [[isotretinoin]], [[lithium]], [[corticosteroid]] withdrawal |
| **Systemic Conditions** | Severe [[hypertension]] (malignant hypertension), [[renal failure]], [[hypoparathyroidism]] |

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

The symptoms of papilledema are primarily related to the underlying elevated ICP and the secondary effects on the optic nerve:

- **Headache:** The most common symptom; typically diffuse, worse in the morning or upon waking, exacerbated by [[Valsalva maneuver]], coughing, or bending forward. May be positional.
- **Transient Visual Obscurations (TVOs):** Brief episodes (seconds) of monocular or binocular visual dimming or graying, often precipitated by postural changes. These are considered a hallmark symptom and are thought to result from transient ischemia of the optic nerve head.
- **Diplopia:** Horizontal [[diplopia]] due to unilateral or bilateral [[sixth nerve palsy]] ([[abducens nerve]] palsy), which is a "false localizing sign" of raised ICP caused by stretching of the sixth cranial nerve over the [[petrous apex]].
- **Nausea and Vomiting:** Related to elevated ICP, particularly with posterior fossa lesions.
- **Pulsatile Tinnitus:** A rhythmic whooshing sound synchronous with the heartbeat, commonly reported in IIH.
- **Visual Field Loss:** Progressive [[visual field]] constriction, particularly enlargement of the [[blind spot]], inferior nasal field loss, and generalized constriction. In advanced cases, central [[visual acuity]] may be affected.
- **Photopsia:** Flashes of light, occasionally reported.

Importantly, **visual acuity is typically preserved in early papilledema** and may remain normal even with significant disc swelling. Loss of central acuity is a late and ominous finding, suggesting severe or chronic optic nerve damage.

### Signs

On clinical examination, the following signs may be observed:

- **Optic Disc Swelling:** Bilateral (though may be asymmetric); elevated, hyperemic disc with blurred margins, initially nasal then circumferential.
- **Loss of Spontaneous Venous Pulsation (SVP):** Absent SVP at the optic disc is suggestive but not diagnostic of elevated ICP (SVP is absent in approximately 10–20% of normal individuals).
- **Peripapillary Hemorrhages:** Flame-shaped or splinter hemorrhages in the [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] surrounding the disc.
- **Cotton-Wool Spots:** Indicative of focal retinal ischemia in the peripapillary region.
- **Retinal Folds (Paton's Lines):** Concentric or radial folds in the peripapillary retina, caused by displacement of the retina by the swollen disc.
- **Choroidal Folds:** May be visible on [[fundoscopy]] or [[optical coherence tomography]] (OCT).
- **Exudates:** Hard exudates, including a partial or complete [[macular star]], may be seen in chronic cases.
- **Optic Cup Obliteration:** The physiologic cup may be partially or completely filled by swollen axons.
- **Venous Engorgement:** Dilated, tortuous retinal veins.
- **Optociliary Shunt Vessels:** Retinochoroidal collateral vessels may develop in chronic papilledema as a compensatory mechanism for venous outflow obstruction.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

The diagnosis of papilledema begins with a thorough [[dilated fundus examination]] using [[direct ophthalmoscopy]], [[indirect ophthalmoscopy]], or [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] with a [[78D lens]], [[90D lens]], or [[Volk lens]]. Stereoscopic viewing is essential for assessing disc elevation. The examiner should assess:

- Disc color, margins, and elevation
- Presence of hemorrhages, exudates, and cotton-wool spots
- Spontaneous venous pulsation
- Retinal and choroidal folds
- Symmetry between the two eyes

### Visual Field Testing

[[Perimetry]] is critical for monitoring the functional impact of papilledema. [[Humphrey visual field]] (HVF) testing or [[Goldmann perimetry]] should be performed at baseline and serially. Characteristic visual field defects include:

- **Enlarged blind spot** (most common and earliest finding)
- **Inferior nasal step**
- **Generalized constriction**
- **Arcuate scotomas**
- **Central scotoma** (late finding, indicating severe damage)

### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT) is an invaluable tool in the assessment and monitoring of papilledema:

- **Peripapillary RNFL Thickness:** Quantitative measurement of the [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] thickness around the optic disc. In acute papilledema, RNFL thickness is markedly increased. Serial measurements allow objective tracking of disease progression or resolution.
- **Enhanced Depth Imaging OCT (EDI-OCT):** Allows visualization of the [[lamina cribrosa]] and assessment of optic nerve head morphology.
- **Ganglion Cell Analysis (GCA):** [[Ganglion cell-inner plexiform layer]] (GCIPL) thinning on OCT may indicate chronic axonal loss even in the presence of persistent disc edema, helping differentiate active edema from atrophic papilledema.
- **Subretinal Fluid:** OCT may reveal peripapillary subretinal fluid or intraretinal fluid.
- **Choroidal Folds and Bruch's Membrane Morphology:** OCT can detect subtle peripapillary changes including Bruch's membrane deflection and choroidal folds.

### Fundus Photography

Serial [[fundus photography]] provides documentation for comparison over time and is essential for grading papilledema severity using the Frisén scale.

### Ocular Ultrasonography

[[B-scan ultrasonography]] can demonstrate:

- **Optic nerve sheath diameter (ONSD):** An ONSD greater than 5.0–5.5 mm (measured 3 mm behind the globe) is suggestive of elevated ICP.
- **30-degree test (crescent sign):** Distension of the optic nerve sheath that decreases with eccentric gaze, indicating fluid within the sheath.
- **Optic disc elevation:** Protrusion of the disc into the vitreous cavity.

### Fluorescein Angiography

[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA) may be used in equivocal cases and demonstrates:

- **Early hyperfluorescence** of the disc due to capillary dilation
- **Late leakage** of fluorescein from the disc (distinguishing true edema from pseudopapilledema)
- **Peripapillary capillary dilation**

### Neuroimaging

Once papilledema is suspected or confirmed, urgent [[neuroimaging]] is mandatory to exclude a space-occupying lesion or other structural cause of elevated ICP:

- **[[Magnetic resonance imaging]] (MRI) of the brain with and without [[gadolinium]] contrast:** The preferred initial imaging modality. MRI can identify tumors, hydrocephalus, Chiari malformations, and other structural abnormalities. MRI findings suggestive of raised ICP include empty [[sella turcica]], flattening of the posterior globe, distension of the perioptic subarachnoid space, and tortuosity of the optic nerve.
- **[[Magnetic resonance venography]] (MRV):** Essential to exclude [[cerebral venous sinus thrombosis]], particularly in patients with suspected IIH.
- **[[Computed tomography]] (CT) of the brain:** May be used in emergency settings when MRI is unavailable; less sensitive for posterior fossa lesions and venous sinus pathology.

### Lumbar Puncture

If neuroimaging is unremarkable, a [[lumbar puncture]] (LP) is performed to:

- **Measure opening pressure:** An opening pressure >25 cm H₂O in adults (>28 cm H₂O in children) is considered elevated. The LP should be performed in the lateral decubitus position with legs extended.
- **Analyze CSF composition:** To exclude infectious, inflammatory, or neoplastic causes (e.g., meningitis, carcinomatous meningitis).

The combination of papilledema, elevated opening pressure, normal neuroimaging, and normal CSF composition fulfills the **Modified Dandy Criteria** for the diagnosis of [[idiopathic intracranial hypertension]].

## Differential Diagnosis

It is essential to differentiate papilledema from other causes of optic disc swelling:

| **Condition** | **Key Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| **[[Pseudopapilledema]]** | Congenitally anomalous disc (e.g., [[optic disc drusen]]); no true edema; autofluorescence or B-scan may reveal buried drusen; no hemorrhages; SVP often present |
| **[[Optic Neuritis]]** | Typically unilateral; pain with eye movement; reduced visual acuity; [[relative afferent pupillary defect]] (RAPD); younger patients |
| **[[Anterior Ischemic Optic Neuropathy]] (AION)** | Typically unilateral; sudden painless vision loss; altitudinal visual field defect; RAPD; older patients with vascular risk factors |
| **[[Diabetic Papillopathy]]** | Mild disc edema in patients with [[diabetes mellitus]]; relatively preserved vision; may be unilateral or bilateral |
| **[[Hypertensive Retinopathy]]** | Bilateral disc edema in the setting of severe/malignant [[hypertension]]; associated with arteriolar narrowing, AV nicking, flame hemorrhages, cotton-wool spots, and macular star |
| **[[Infiltrative Optic Neuropathy]]** | Disc swelling due to infiltration by [[sarcoidosis]], [[lymphoma]], [[leukemia]], or other granulomatous/neoplastic processes |
| **[[Central Retinal Vein Occlusion]] (CRVO)** | Unilateral; extensive retinal hemorrhages in all quadrants; dilated tortuous veins; disc edema secondary to venous congestion |

## Management and Treatment

The management of papilledema is directed at treating the underlying cause of elevated ICP and protecting visual function. Treatment strategies vary depending on the etiology, severity, and rate of visual deterioration.

### General Principles

- **Urgent referral:** All patients with suspected papilledema require urgent referral to [[neuro-ophthalmology]] and/or [[neurology]] for further evaluation and management.
- **Identify and treat the underlying cause:** Surgical excision of tumors, treatment of infections, anticoagulation for venous sinus thrombosis, etc.
- **Monitor visual function:** Serial [[visual field testing]], [[visual acuity]] assessment, [[OCT]], and [[fundus photography]] are essential for tracking disease progression and treatment response.

### Pharmacological Treatment

#### Acetazolamide

[[Acetazolamide]] (Diamox) is the first-line medical therapy for papilledema secondary to IIH. It is a [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitor]] that reduces CSF production by the [[choroid plexus]].

- **Dosage:** Typically initiated at 500 mg twice daily, with titration up to 2–4 g/day as tolerated.
- **Side effects:** Paresthesias (tingling of extremities), dysgeusia (metallic taste), fatigue, nausea, diarrhea, [[metabolic acidosis]], [[hypokalemia]], and [[nephrolithiasis]].
- **Monitoring:** Serum electrolytes and renal function should be monitored regularly.
- **Evidence:** The **Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension Treatment Trial (IIHTT)** demonstrated that acetazolamide combined with a weight management program significantly improved visual field function, papilledema grade, and quality of life compared to placebo plus weight management.

#### Topiramate

[[Topiramate]] is a second-line agent with weak carbonic anhydrase inhibitory activity and the added benefit of promoting weight loss.

- **Dosage:** 25–100 mg twice daily.
- **Side effects:** Cognitive slowing, word-finding difficulty, paresthesias, [[nephrolithiasis]], and weight loss.

#### Furosemide

[[Furosemide]], a [[loop diuretic]], may be used as an adjunct or alternative in patients who cannot tolerate acetazolamide, though evidence is limited.

#### Corticosteroids

Short-term [[corticosteroid]] therapy (e.g., [[dexamethasone]] or [[methylprednisolone]]) may be used in acute, fulminant papilledema with rapidly deteriorating vision to provide temporary ICP reduction while definitive treatment is arranged. Long-term use is avoided due to side effects and rebound intracranial hypertension upon withdrawal.

### Weight Management

In patients with IIH, weight loss is a critical component of treatment. A weight reduction of 5–10% of total body weight has been shown to significantly reduce ICP and improve papilledema. Referral to a [[dietitian]] and consideration of [[bariatric surgery]] in morbidly obese patients may be appropriate.

### Surgical Treatment

Surgical intervention is indicated in cases of:

- Fulminant papilledema with rapidly progressive visual loss
- Failure of maximal medical therapy
- Intolerance of medical therapy
- Severe visual field loss at presentation

#### Optic Nerve Sheath Fenestration (ONSF)

[[Optic nerve sheath fenestration]] involves creating a window or series of slits in the [[dura mater]] and [[arachnoid mater]] surrounding the retrobulbar optic nerve, allowing CSF to drain and reducing pressure on the optic nerve.

- **Indications:** Primarily for visual preservation in papilledema with progressive visual loss, particularly when headache is not the predominant symptom.
- **Efficacy:** Effective in reducing disc edema and stabilizing or improving visual fields in the operated eye; may have a bilateral effect in some cases.
- **Complications:** Diplopia, pupillary dysfunction, retinal vascular occlusion, and rarely, vision loss.

#### CSF Diversion Procedures

[[Ventriculoperitoneal shunt]] (VP shunt) or [[lumboperitoneal shunt]] (LP shunt) procedures divert CSF from the ventricles or lumbar subarachnoid space to the peritoneal cavity, reducing ICP.

- **Indications:** Papilledema with refractory headache and/or visual loss; hydrocephalus.
- **Complications:** Shunt obstruction, infection, over-drainage (low-pressure headache), shunt migration, and need for revision (revision rates are high, particularly for LP shunts).

#### Venous Sinus Stenting

[[Venous sinus stenting]] is an emerging interventional neuroradiological procedure for patients with IIH and documented [[transverse sinus stenosis]] with a significant pressure gradient.

- **Indications:** IIH with venous sinus stenosis and elevated trans-stenotic pressure gradient, refractory to medical therapy.
- **Efficacy:** Studies have shown significant improvement in ICP, papilledema, headache, and visual function.
- **Complications:** In-stent thrombosis, subdural hematoma, stent migration, and recurrence of stenosis.

#### Therapeutic Lumbar Puncture

Serial [[lumbar puncture]] with CSF drainage may provide temporary relief of ICP and papilledema, particularly in acute settings or during pregnancy when other treatments may be contraindicated. However, this is not a long-term solution due to rapid CSF re-accumulation.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of papilledema depends on the underlying etiology, the severity and duration of disc swelling, and the timeliness of treatment:

- **Acute papilledema** with prompt treatment of the underlying cause generally has a favorable visual prognosis.
- **Chronic papilledema** (lasting weeks to months) carries a significant risk of progressive, irreversible visual field loss and [[optic atrophy]].
- **Fulminant papilledema** (rapidly progressive, severe swelling) is a neuro-ophthalmic emergency with a high risk of permanent vision loss if not treated aggressively.
- In IIH, the **IIHTT** demonstrated that treatment with acetazolamide and weight management significantly improved visual outcomes compared to placebo.
- Patients with papilledema require long-term follow-up, as recurrence is possible, particularly in IIH.

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Papilledema

Papilledema in children may present differently than in adults. Infants with open fontanelles may not develop papilledema despite elevated ICP because the compliant skull can expand. In older children, the etiologic spectrum includes [[posterior fossa tumors]] (e.g., [[medulloblastoma]], [[cerebellar astrocytoma]]), [[hydrocephalus]], and [[meningitis]]. IIH in children may occur in non-obese individuals and in males, unlike the typical adult demographic.

### Papilledema in Pregnancy

Pregnant patients with papilledema (most commonly due to IIH or [[cerebral venous sinus thrombosis]]) require careful multidisciplinary management. Acetazolamide is classified as FDA category C and should be used with caution. Weight management recommendations must be modified. Therapeutic lumbar puncture may be a temporizing measure. Delivery planning should consider the potential for Valsalva-related ICP spikes during labor.

## Role of the Optometrist

The [[optometrist]] plays a critical role in the detection and co-management of papilledema:

1. **Detection:** Papilledema is frequently first identified during routine comprehensive eye examinations. Proficiency in [[fundoscopy]], [[OCT]] interpretation, and [[visual field]] analysis is essential.
2. **Differentiation:** The optometrist must distinguish papilledema from [[pseudopapilledema]], [[optic neuritis]], and other causes of disc swelling through careful clinical assessment and ancillary testing.
3. **Urgent Referral:** Upon suspicion of papilledema, the optometrist must initiate urgent referral for neuroimaging and neurological evaluation.
4. **Monitoring:** In co-management with neurology and neuro-ophthalmology, the optometrist performs serial visual field testing, OCT, and fundus photography to monitor treatment response and detect progression.
5. **Patient Education:** Counseling patients about symptoms of elevated ICP (headache, TVOs, diplopia) and the importance of adherence to treatment and follow-up.

---

## Summary

Papilledema is a sight-threatening and potentially life-threatening condition that demands prompt recognition, thorough investigation, and appropriate management. The hallmark finding is bilateral optic disc swelling secondary to elevated [[intracranial pressure]], and the clinical workup must include neuroimaging, lumbar puncture (when indicated), and comprehensive ophthalmic assessment including [[visual field testing]] and [[OCT]]. Treatment is directed at the underlying cause and may include pharmacological therapy (primarily [[acetazolamide]]), weight management, and surgical intervention ([[optic nerve sheath fenestration]], CSF diversion, or venous sinus stenting) in refractory or fulminant cases. The optometrist is often the first clinician to detect papilledema and plays an indispensable role in its ongoing monitoring and co-management.
