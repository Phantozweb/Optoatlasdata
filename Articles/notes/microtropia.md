

# Microtropia

---

**Microtropia** (also referred to as **microstrabismus**, **monofixation syndrome**, or **microtropia with identity**) is a small-angle [[strabismus]] typically measuring less than 10 prism dioptres (Δ), most commonly involving an [[esotropia]] of 1–5Δ, though small-angle [[exotropia]] and vertical deviations have also been documented. First formally described by Lang in 1966 and further characterized by Parks through the concept of the **[[monofixation syndrome]]** in 1969, microtropia represents a clinically significant yet frequently under-diagnosed entity in which the patient maintains a small manifest deviation with an associated central [[scotoma]] in the deviating eye, [[anomalous retinal correspondence]] (ARC), and reduced or absent central [[stereopsis]], while retaining peripheral [[binocular vision|fusional mechanisms]].

Microtropia occupies a unique position in the spectrum of [[binocular vision]] disorders because it can exist as a **primary** (congenital/developmental) condition or arise **secondarily** (consecutive) following surgical or optical treatment of a larger-angle strabismus, or in association with [[anisometropia]]. Its clinical importance lies in the fact that it is easily overlooked during routine examination, may be associated with significant [[amblyopia]], and has implications for the prognosis of binocular vision rehabilitation.

---

## Epidemiology

Microtropia is more common than historically appreciated. Prevalence estimates vary depending on the diagnostic criteria and population studied, but it is generally reported in approximately **1–2%** of the general population. Key epidemiological features include:

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Prevalence** | ~1–2% of the general population; higher in clinical strabismus populations |
| **Most common type** | Microesotropia (eso-microtropia) |
| **Gender predilection** | No significant gender difference consistently reported |
| **Age of detection** | Often detected in childhood during [[amblyopia]] screening or [[vision screening]] programs; may remain undetected into adulthood |
| **Laterality** | Usually unilateral; the same eye is consistently the deviating eye |
| **Association with anisometropia** | Strong association, particularly with [[hypermetropia|hypermetropic]] anisometropia ≥1.00 D |
| **Familial tendency** | Some evidence of familial clustering, suggesting a genetic component |

Microtropia is frequently encountered in post-surgical populations. Studies have shown that a significant proportion of patients who undergo surgical correction for larger-angle [[infantile esotropia]] or [[accommodative esotropia]] achieve a microtropic outcome rather than true [[orthophoria]], and this is often considered a functionally successful result.

---

## Classification

Microtropia is classified into several subtypes based on its etiology and clinical characteristics:

### Primary Microtropia (Microtropia with Identity)

This is the congenital or early-onset form in which the deviation has been present from early visual development. In **microtropia with identity**, the angle of anomaly of the [[anomalous retinal correspondence]] equals the angle of deviation, meaning the subjective and objective angles are identical. The fovea of the deviating eye is not used for fixation; instead, an eccentric point very close to the fovea (parafoveal) serves as the fixation point. This creates a situation where the deviation is effectively "masked" by the anomalous correspondence, making it extremely difficult to detect on routine [[cover test]].

Key features of primary microtropia with identity:
- No movement (or an extremely small, barely perceptible movement) on [[cover test|cover-uncover test]]
- A small movement may be detected on the **4Δ base-out prism test**
- Central suppression scotoma in the deviating eye
- Harmonious [[anomalous retinal correspondence]]
- Reduced [[stereoacuity]] (typically worse than 60 seconds of arc)
- Often associated with mild [[amblyopia]]

### Primary Microtropia without Identity

In this variant, the angle of anomaly does not fully equal the angle of deviation. A small manifest movement is detectable on cover test, though it remains less than 10Δ. The patient still demonstrates ARC and a central scotoma, but the correspondence is not perfectly harmonious.

### Secondary (Consecutive) Microtropia

This form develops after treatment of a larger-angle strabismus, either through:
- **Surgical correction** of [[infantile esotropia]], [[accommodative esotropia]], or other forms of strabismus
- **Optical correction** (e.g., full hypermetropic correction reducing an accommodative esotropia to a residual microtropia)
- **Botulinum toxin ([[botulinum toxin|Botox]]) injection** for strabismus

Secondary microtropia is often considered a favorable surgical outcome, as it implies the presence of peripheral fusional mechanisms that help maintain ocular alignment and prevent recurrence of a large-angle deviation.

### Microtropia Associated with Anisometropia

A strong association exists between microtropia and [[anisometropia]], particularly hypermetropic anisometropia. It remains debated whether the anisometropia causes the microtropia (through unequal image clarity leading to abnormal binocular development) or whether both conditions share a common developmental etiology. Anisometropic microtropia is frequently accompanied by [[anisometropic amblyopia]].

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of microtropia involves a complex interplay of sensory and motor adaptations that occur during the critical period of visual development. Understanding these mechanisms requires knowledge of normal [[binocular vision]] development and the consequences of early disruption.

### Sensory Adaptations

#### Central Suppression Scotoma

The hallmark sensory adaptation in microtropia is the development of a **facultative central [[suppression]] scotoma** in the deviating eye. This scotoma is small (typically 1–3° in diameter), corresponding roughly to the area of the [[fovea]] and parafoveal region. The scotoma serves an anti-diplopic function, preventing the patient from experiencing [[diplopia]] despite the manifest deviation. Importantly, this scotoma is present only under binocular conditions; monocular visual function of the deviating eye (outside of any coexisting amblyopia) may be relatively preserved.

The scotoma can be demonstrated using the **[[Bagolini striated lens test]]**, where the patient reports a small gap or interruption in the streak corresponding to the deviating eye's central field, while the peripheral portions of the streak remain intact—indicating preserved peripheral fusion.

#### Anomalous Retinal Correspondence (ARC)

In microtropia, the visual system develops [[anomalous retinal correspondence]], wherein a non-foveal point in the deviating eye (a parafoveal locus) is neurologically paired with the fovea of the fixating eye for the purpose of binocular correspondence. In **microtropia with identity**, this anomalous correspondence is **harmonious**—the angle of anomaly precisely matches the angle of deviation, effectively nullifying the subjective perception of misalignment.

This harmonious ARC is deeply embedded in the neural architecture and is extremely resistant to disruption, which is why microtropia with identity is considered a remarkably stable condition. The neural basis is thought to involve cortical remapping within the [[visual cortex]] (areas [[V1]], [[V2]], and higher binocular processing areas), where binocular neurons adjust their receptive field disparities to accommodate the small deviation.

#### Peripheral Fusion

Despite the central scotoma and ARC, patients with microtropia retain **peripheral fusional vergence mechanisms**. This peripheral fusion provides:
- Motor fusion that helps maintain the small angle of deviation and prevents it from increasing
- A degree of gross [[stereopsis]] (typically peripheral stereopsis at disparities >60–100 arc seconds)
- Stability of ocular alignment over time

This preservation of peripheral fusion is what distinguishes microtropia from larger-angle strabismus with complete suppression, and it is the reason microtropia is considered a form of **subnormal binocular vision** rather than absent binocularity.

### Motor Adaptations

#### Eccentric Fixation

In many cases of microtropia, the deviating eye adopts a **parafoveal eccentric fixation** point. This eccentric fixation locus is typically nasal to the fovea in eso-microtropia and temporal to the fovea in exo-microtropia. The eccentricity is small, usually corresponding to the magnitude of the deviation (1–3°).

It is important to distinguish between:
- **Eccentric fixation**: An abnormal monocular fixation behavior where the non-foveal point is used even during monocular viewing
- **Eccentric viewing**: A compensatory strategy used in macular disease

In microtropia, the eccentric fixation is a developmental adaptation and is present during both monocular and binocular viewing conditions.

#### Vergence and Accommodation Interactions

The small deviation in microtropia may interact with the [[accommodation]]-[[convergence]] relationship. In eso-microtropia associated with [[hypermetropia]], the deviation may increase with accommodative effort if there is a high [[AC/A ratio]]. Conversely, appropriate optical correction of the hypermetropia may reduce the deviation, though it rarely eliminates it entirely in primary microtropia.

### Developmental Considerations

Microtropia is believed to develop during the **critical period** of binocular visual development (approximately the first 6–8 years of life, with the most sensitive period being the first 2 years). Factors that may predispose to or trigger the development of microtropia include:

- **Anisometropia**: Unequal refractive errors leading to unequal image clarity between the two eyes
- **Genetic predisposition**: Family studies suggest a hereditary component
- **Early visual deprivation**: Any condition causing asymmetric visual input during the critical period
- **Incomplete recovery from infantile esotropia**: Following surgical or optical treatment

The developing visual cortex responds to the small misalignment by establishing the sensory adaptations (scotoma, ARC) described above, creating a stable but subnormal binocular state.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

Microtropia is frequently **asymptomatic**, which is one of the primary reasons it is often undiagnosed. The sensory adaptations (central scotoma and ARC) effectively eliminate diplopia and visual confusion. However, patients may present with or report:

| Symptom | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Reduced depth perception** | Loss of central (fine) stereopsis; patients may have difficulty with tasks requiring precise depth judgment (e.g., threading a needle, certain sports) |
| **Mild amblyopia** | Reduced [[visual acuity]] in the deviating eye, typically mild (often 6/9 to 6/12, rarely worse than 6/18 unless significant anisometropia coexists) |
| **Asthenopia** | Occasionally reported, particularly with prolonged near work, due to the effort of maintaining peripheral fusion |
| **Cosmetic concern** | Usually absent, as the deviation is too small to be cosmetically noticeable; however, some patients or parents may notice a subtle misalignment |
| **Reading difficulties** | Rarely reported; some children may demonstrate slower reading speeds or reduced reading fluency |
| **Failed vision screening** | Often the initial presentation in children, detected through reduced acuity in one eye during school or pediatric screening |

### Signs

Clinical signs of microtropia include:

- **Small manifest deviation** (<10Δ, most commonly 2–5Δ of esotropia)
- **Amblyopia** in the deviating eye (mild to moderate)
- **Anisometropia** (frequently present, especially hypermetropic)
- **Eccentric fixation** in the deviating eye (parafoveal)
- **Central suppression scotoma** under binocular conditions
- **Anomalous retinal correspondence** (harmonious in microtropia with identity)
- **Reduced stereoacuity** (absent on [[Titmus stereo test|Titmus fly test]] circles or [[TNO stereo test]]; may demonstrate gross stereopsis only)
- **Normal or near-normal peripheral fusion** on [[Bagolini striated lens test]]

## Diagnosis

The diagnosis of microtropia requires a high index of clinical suspicion and the use of specific diagnostic tests, as the condition is easily missed with standard examination techniques. A systematic approach is essential.

### Cover Test

The [[cover test]] (cover-uncover and alternating cover test) is the cornerstone of strabismus assessment, but in microtropia—particularly **microtropia with identity**—the cover test may appear **negative** or show only an equivocal, barely perceptible movement. This is because:

1. The angle of deviation is very small
2. In microtropia with identity, the eccentric fixation point in the deviating eye takes up fixation without a detectable refixation movement when the dominant eye is occluded

The **alternating cover test** may reveal a small movement in microtropia without identity, but the movement is subtle and requires careful observation.

### 4Δ Base-Out Prism Test (Irvine-Jampolsky Test)

The **[[4 prism dioptre base-out test]]** is the single most important diagnostic test for microtropia with identity. The test exploits the central scotoma in the deviating eye.

**Procedure:**
1. A 4Δ base-out prism is placed before the **fixating (dominant) eye**
2. In a normal individual, this induces a version movement of both eyes followed by a fusional vergence recovery movement
3. In microtropia, when the prism is placed before the **fixating eye**, the image is displaced into the scotoma of the deviating eye, and **no recovery vergence movement occurs**
4. When the prism is placed before the **deviating (microtropic) eye**, a normal version-vergence response is observed because the image is displaced out of the scotoma

**Interpretation:**

| Prism Placement | Normal Response | Microtropic Response |
|---|---|---|
| Before fixating eye | Version + vergence recovery | Version movement only; **NO vergence recovery** (image falls in scotoma of deviating eye) |
| Before deviating eye | Version + vergence recovery | Version + vergence recovery (normal response) |

An **asymmetric response** between the two eyes is diagnostic of microtropia.

### Bagolini Striated Lens Test

The [[Bagolini striated lens test]] is invaluable for assessing the binocular status in microtropia. Striated lenses are placed before each eye at complementary orientations (e.g., 45° and 135°).

**Expected findings in microtropia:**
- The patient reports seeing **two streaks** forming an "X" pattern
- The streak corresponding to the deviating eye shows a **small central gap** (indicating the central suppression scotoma)
- The peripheral portions of both streaks are intact (indicating peripheral fusion)

This pattern—two complete peripheral streaks with a central gap in one—is pathognomonic for microtropia with a central scotoma and preserved peripheral fusion.

### Worth Four-Dot Test

The [[Worth four-dot test]] can provide supportive evidence:
- At **distance** (6 meters): The patient may report seeing **four dots** (indicating peripheral fusion at the larger angular subtense) or may suppress
- At **near** (33 cm): The patient may report **fusion (four dots)** or may demonstrate suppression of the deviating eye

The response depends on whether the test targets fall within or outside the central scotoma at the given testing distance.

### Stereoacuity Testing

Quantitative [[stereoacuity]] assessment is essential:

| Test | Expected Finding in Microtropia |
|---|---|
| **[[Titmus stereo test]]** (Wirt circles) | Reduced or absent; typically unable to detect disparities finer than 60–100 arc seconds |
| **[[TNO stereo test]]** | Often reduced or absent (random-dot stereograms are more sensitive to disruption of binocularity) |
| **[[Frisby stereo test]]** | May demonstrate gross stereopsis |
| **[[Lang stereo test]]** | May be reduced or absent |
| **[[Randot stereo test]]** | Reduced; may detect only gross disparities |

The characteristic finding is **absence of fine (central) stereopsis** with **preservation of coarse (peripheral) stereopsis**. Random-dot stereograms (e.g., TNO) are particularly useful because they cannot be passed using monocular cues.

### Visuoscopy / Ophthalmoscopic Assessment of Fixation

**[[Visuoscopy]]** (or direct ophthalmoscopic assessment of fixation) is used to determine whether the deviating eye demonstrates eccentric fixation:

- The patient is asked to fixate on the ophthalmoscope's fixation target with the suspected deviating eye (the other eye is occluded)
- In microtropia, the fixation reflex is displaced from the foveal center, typically **nasally** in eso-microtropia
- The degree of eccentricity is usually small (1–3°)

### Refraction

A thorough [[cycloplegic refraction]] is mandatory in all suspected cases of microtropia to:
- Identify and quantify any [[anisometropia]]
- Determine the full hypermetropic refractive error
- Guide optical management

### Additional Investigations

- **[[Ocular motility]] assessment**: Full extraocular muscle evaluation to rule out incomitant deviations
- **[[Fundus examination]]**: To exclude organic causes of reduced vision
- **[[Visual acuity]] assessment**: Best-corrected acuity in each eye; crowded optotypes are preferred as they are more sensitive to amblyopic deficits (the **[[crowding phenomenon]]** is often prominent in amblyopia associated with microtropia)
- **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)**: May be considered to rule out subtle macular pathology if the clinical picture is atypical

## Differential Diagnosis

Conditions that may mimic or be confused with microtropia include:

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| **[[Orthophoria]] with anisometropic amblyopia** | No manifest deviation on any testing; normal binocular correspondence; normal stereoacuity (if amblyopia is mild) |
| **Small-angle manifest strabismus (>10Δ)** | Larger deviation detectable on cover test; may or may not have ARC |
| **[[Heterophoria]] (latent deviation)** | No manifest deviation; deviation only present when fusion is disrupted; normal retinal correspondence |
| **[[Eccentric fixation]] without strabismus** | Rare; eccentric fixation present monocularly but no manifest deviation under binocular conditions |
| **Macular pathology** | Organic cause of reduced acuity; abnormal fundus appearance; OCT abnormalities |

## Management

The management of microtropia is guided by the specific clinical findings and the patient's age. It is important to recognize that **microtropia itself is a stable binocular adaptation** and the goal of treatment is not to eliminate the microtropia (which is generally not achievable) but rather to:

1. **Treat any associated amblyopia**
2. **Optimize refractive correction**
3. **Monitor for stability of the deviation**
4. **Counsel the patient/family appropriately**

### Optical Correction

Full and accurate [[refractive correction]] is the first-line intervention:

- **Anisometropia**: Must be corrected to equalize image clarity between the two eyes. [[Spectacles]] or [[contact lenses]] may be used. Contact lenses may be preferred in cases of significant anisometropia to minimize [[aniseikonia]].
- **Hypermetropia**: Full cycloplegic hypermetropic correction should be prescribed, particularly in eso-microtropia, to minimize any accommodative component contributing to the deviation.
- **Astigmatism**: Should be fully corrected in both eyes.

### Amblyopia Treatment

If [[amblyopia]] is present (which is common in microtropia), treatment should be initiated, particularly in children within the sensitive period for visual development:

#### Occlusion Therapy (Patching)

**[[Occlusion therapy]]** (patching the dominant/fixating eye) remains the gold standard for amblyopia treatment:

- **Part-time occlusion** (2–6 hours per day) is typically sufficient for the mild to moderate amblyopia seen in microtropia
- Treatment should be monitored regularly (every 4–8 weeks) to assess visual acuity improvement and to watch for **occlusion amblyopia** (iatrogenic amblyopia of the patched eye)
- The goal is to improve visual acuity in the amblyopic eye to the best achievable level

**Important consideration**: In microtropia, aggressive or prolonged occlusion therapy may theoretically disrupt the existing peripheral fusion and destabilize the deviation. Therefore, a **conservative, monitored approach** is recommended. Some clinicians prefer part-time occlusion to minimize the risk of disrupting binocular adaptations.

#### Pharmacological Penalization

**[[Atropine]] penalization** of the dominant eye is an alternative to patching:

- Atropine 1% instilled in the fixating eye once daily or on weekends
- Blurs the near vision of the dominant eye, encouraging use of the amblyopic eye
- May be better tolerated than patching in some children
- Similar efficacy to patching for mild to moderate amblyopia (as demonstrated in the [[Pediatric Eye Disease Investigator Group]] (PEDIG) studies)

#### Optical Penalization

Deliberate under-correction or over-correction of the dominant eye's refractive error to create a relative blur, encouraging fixation with the amblyopic eye.

### Orthoptic / Vision Therapy Considerations

The role of [[orthoptics]] and [[vision therapy]] in microtropia is limited but may include:

- **Monitoring**: Regular orthoptic assessment to ensure stability of the deviation and binocular status
- **Fusional vergence exercises**: Generally not indicated for the microtropia itself, as the condition is a stable adaptation. However, if the patient has decompensating fusional reserves or symptomatic asthenopia, vergence exercises may provide some benefit.
- **Anti-suppression therapy**: Controversial in microtropia. Attempting to eliminate the central scotoma may destabilize the binocular adaptation without providing meaningful clinical benefit. Most authorities advise against aggressive anti-suppression treatment in established microtropia.

### Surgical Management

**Surgery is generally NOT indicated for primary microtropia** because:

1. The deviation is cosmetically insignificant
2. The binocular status (peripheral fusion with ARC) represents a stable adaptation
3. Surgery risks disrupting the existing binocular mechanisms without achieving normal binocularity
4. The deviation is too small for reliable surgical correction

However, surgery may be relevant in the following contexts:

- **Treatment of a larger-angle strabismus** where the goal is to achieve a microtropic outcome (e.g., surgery for [[infantile esotropia]] aiming for a residual microtropia as the best achievable binocular outcome)
- **Secondary microtropia developing after surgery**: This is typically observed and monitored rather than re-operated, as it represents a stable endpoint
- **Decompensation of microtropia into a larger-angle deviation**: If the microtropia decompensates (e.g., loss of peripheral fusion leading to an increasing deviation), surgical intervention may be considered to restore a small angle amenable to peripheral fusion

### Prism Therapy

**[[Prism]] correction** is generally not used in microtropia because:
- The deviation is too small to warrant prismatic correction
- The ARC and central scotoma mean that prisms would not restore normal binocular correspondence
- Prisms may disrupt the existing peripheral fusion

In rare cases, small amounts of prism may be considered if the patient is symptomatic, but this is uncommon.

### Monitoring and Follow-Up

Regular follow-up is essential, particularly in children:

| Age Group | Recommended Follow-Up |
|---|---|
| **Children undergoing amblyopia treatment** | Every 4–8 weeks during active treatment; then every 3–6 months |
| **Children post-amblyopia treatment** | Every 6–12 months until visual maturity (~8–10 years) to monitor for recurrence |
| **Stable microtropia (no active treatment)** | Annual review to monitor acuity, deviation stability, and binocular status |
| **Adults with known microtropia** | As needed; generally stable and requires no ongoing intervention |

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis for microtropia depends on the specific clinical context:

- **Visual acuity**: Amblyopia associated with microtropia is typically mild and responds well to treatment, particularly if detected early. However, complete normalization of acuity may not always be achieved, and recurrence of amblyopia after cessation of treatment is possible.
- **Binocular vision**: Microtropia represents a **stable subnormal binocular state**. Patients retain peripheral fusion and gross stereopsis, which is functionally useful. Restoration of normal bifoveal fixation and fine stereopsis is **not achievable** in established microtropia.
- **Stability of deviation**: Primary microtropia is generally very stable over time due to the peripheral fusional mechanisms. Secondary microtropia following surgery is also typically stable, though monitoring is warranted.
- **Occupational implications**: Patients with microtropia may not meet the binocular vision standards required for certain occupations (e.g., airline pilot, certain military roles) that demand fine stereoacuity. Counseling regarding occupational visual requirements may be appropriate.

---

## Special Considerations

### Microtropia and Driving

In most jurisdictions, the visual acuity requirements for driving can be met by patients with microtropia, as the amblyopia is typically mild. The reduced stereoacuity is generally not a barrier to safe driving, as monocular depth cues compensate adequately at driving distances.

### Microtropia in the Context of Refractive Surgery

Patients with microtropia considering [[refractive surgery]] (e.g., [[LASIK]], [[PRK]]) should be counseled that:
- Correction of anisometropia may not improve the binocular status
- The microtropia and associated sensory adaptations will persist
- Careful preoperative assessment of binocular function is essential

### Microtropia and Cataract Surgery

In patients with known microtropia undergoing [[cataract surgery]], careful [[biometry]] and [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) selection are important to avoid introducing or worsening anisometropia, which could destabilize the binocular adaptation.

## Summary of Key Diagnostic Features

| Feature | Microtropia with Identity | Microtropia without Identity |
|---|---|---|
| **Angle of deviation** | <10Δ (usually 1–5Δ) | <10Δ |
| **Cover test** | Negative or equivocal | Small manifest movement detectable |
| **4Δ base-out prism test** | Asymmetric response (diagnostic) | Asymmetric response |
| **Bagolini lenses** | Central gap in one streak; peripheral fusion intact | Central gap; peripheral fusion intact |
| **Retinal correspondence** | Harmonious ARC (angle of anomaly = angle of deviation) | ARC present but not fully harmonious |
| **Eccentric fixation** | Present (parafoveal) | May be present |
| **Stereoacuity** | Reduced (>60 arc seconds); absent fine stereopsis | Reduced |
| **Amblyopia** | Common (mild to moderate) | Common |
| **Anisometropia** | Frequently associated | Frequently associated |

## Historical Perspective

The concept of microtropia has evolved significantly since the mid-20th century:

- **1966**: **Josef Lang** introduced the term "Mikrostrabismus" (microtropia) to describe small-angle strabismus with specific sensory adaptations, emphasizing the central scotoma and ARC.
- **1969**: **Marshall Parks** described the **[[monofixation syndrome]]**, a closely related concept emphasizing the pattern of bifoveal disruption with preserved peripheral fusion. Parks' monofixation syndrome encompasses microtropia but also includes patients with a small [[heterophoria]] and a central scotoma.
- **1974**: **Helveston and von Noorden** further characterized the clinical features and natural history of microtropia.
- Subsequent decades saw refinement of diagnostic techniques (particularly the 4Δ base-out prism test) and recognition of microtropia as a common and stable endpoint following strabismus surgery.

The relationship between Lang's microtropia and Parks' monofixation syndrome continues to be discussed in the literature. While the terms are often used interchangeably, some authorities consider monofixation syndrome to be a broader category that includes microtropia as a subset.
