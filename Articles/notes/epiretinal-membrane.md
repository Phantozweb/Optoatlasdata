

# Epiretinal Membrane

**Epiretinal Membrane (ERM)**, also known as **macular pucker**, **cellophane maculopathy**, **preretinal macular fibrosis**, or **surface wrinkling retinopathy**, is a semi-translucent, avascular, fibrocellular proliferative membrane that forms on the inner surface of the [[internal limiting membrane]] (ILM) of the [[retina]], most commonly overlying the [[macula]]. This condition represents one of the most prevalent macular disorders encountered in clinical optometric and ophthalmologic practice, with significant implications for central visual function. ERMs can range from clinically insignificant, incidentally discovered findings to visually debilitating pathology requiring surgical intervention.

The membrane exerts tangential and anteroposterior tractional forces on the underlying [[neurosensory retina]], leading to distortion of the normal foveal architecture, retinal thickening, and consequent visual symptoms including [[metamorphopsia]], decreased [[visual acuity]], and [[micropsia]] or [[macropsia]]. Understanding the pathogenesis, clinical presentation, diagnostic evaluation, and management of epiretinal membranes is essential for all eye care practitioners.

---

## Epidemiology

Epiretinal membranes are among the most common macular pathologies, with prevalence increasing significantly with age. Population-based studies have provided robust epidemiological data:

| **Study** | **Population** | **Prevalence** | **Key Findings** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Blue Mountains Eye Study | Australian adults ≥49 years | 7.0% (early ERM), 1.6% (late/preretinal macular fibrosis) | Prevalence increases with age; bilateral in ~19.5% |
| Beaver Dam Eye Study | US adults 43–84 years | 11.8% (cellophane maculopathy), 1.9% (preretinal macular fibrosis) | Strong age association; no significant sex predilection |
| Multi-Ethnic Study of Atherosclerosis (MESA) | US multi-ethnic cohort | 2.2% overall; higher in Chinese (39.0%) and Hispanic (29.3%) participants | Ethnic variation in prevalence |
| Hisayama Study | Japanese adults ≥40 years | 4.0% | Associated with age and posterior vitreous detachment |

The prevalence of ERM rises sharply after the sixth decade of life, with some studies reporting rates exceeding 20% in individuals over 75 years of age. There is no consistent sex predilection, although some studies suggest a slight female predominance. Bilateral involvement occurs in approximately 10–30% of cases, though the severity may be asymmetric.

## Etiology and Classification

Epiretinal membranes are broadly classified into two categories based on their underlying etiology:

### Idiopathic (Primary) Epiretinal Membrane

Idiopathic ERMs constitute the majority of cases (approximately 60–90%) and occur in the absence of any identifiable ocular pathology other than age-related changes. They are strongly associated with [[posterior vitreous detachment]] (PVD), which is considered the principal inciting event. During PVD, residual cortical [[vitreous]] remnants and cellular elements on the retinal surface serve as a scaffold for membrane formation. Idiopathic ERMs are most commonly unilateral at presentation but may become bilateral over time.

### Secondary Epiretinal Membrane

Secondary ERMs arise in the context of pre-existing ocular disease, prior surgical intervention, or ocular trauma. Common secondary causes include:

| **Category** | **Associated Conditions** |
|---|---|
| Vascular | [[Diabetic retinopathy]], [[retinal vein occlusion]] (branch and central), retinal [[macroaneurysm]] |
| Inflammatory | [[Uveitis]] (particularly intermediate and posterior), [[sarcoidosis]], [[toxoplasmosis]] |
| Post-surgical | [[Retinal detachment]] repair (scleral buckle, [[pars plana vitrectomy]]), [[cataract surgery]], retinal [[laser photocoagulation]] |
| Traumatic | Blunt or penetrating [[ocular trauma]] |
| Degenerative | [[Retinal tear]] or [[retinal detachment]] (rhegmatogenous), [[retinal break]] |
| Other | [[Retinitis pigmentosa]], retinal [[cryotherapy]], intraocular tumors |

Secondary ERMs tend to be more clinically aggressive, with thicker membranes, greater tractional distortion, and more pronounced visual symptoms compared to their idiopathic counterparts.

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of epiretinal membrane formation is a complex, multifactorial process involving cellular migration, proliferation, extracellular matrix deposition, and membrane contraction. Understanding this process requires knowledge of the relevant retinal anatomy and the cellular biology underlying membrane development.

### Normal Anatomy of the Vitreomacular Interface

The [[vitreous body]] is a transparent, gel-like structure that fills the posterior segment of the eye. It is composed primarily of water (approximately 98%), [[collagen]] fibrils (predominantly type II), and [[hyaluronic acid]]. The posterior vitreous cortex is attached to the [[internal limiting membrane]] (ILM) of the retina, which is the basement membrane of the [[Müller cells]]. The adhesion between the posterior vitreous cortex and the ILM is strongest at the [[optic disc]], the [[fovea]], along major retinal vessels, and at the [[vitreous base]].

### Posterior Vitreous Detachment and Membrane Initiation

With aging, the vitreous undergoes progressive liquefaction ([[synchysis]]) and collapse ([[syneresis]]), ultimately leading to [[posterior vitreous detachment]] (PVD). During PVD, the posterior hyaloid face separates from the ILM. This process may result in:

1. **Residual vitreous cortex remnants** remaining adherent to the ILM surface, providing a scaffold for cellular proliferation.
2. **Microscopic breaks in the ILM**, which allow migration of glial cells (particularly [[Müller cells]] and [[astrocytes]]) from the inner retina onto the retinal surface.
3. **Release of growth factors and cytokines** that stimulate cellular proliferation and extracellular matrix synthesis.

In cases where PVD is incomplete or anomalous, persistent vitreomacular adhesion ([[vitreomacular adhesion|VMA]]) or [[vitreomacular traction]] (VMT) may coexist with or precede ERM formation, further complicating the clinical picture.

### Cellular Composition

Histopathological and immunohistochemical analyses of surgically excised epiretinal membranes have identified a heterogeneous population of cell types:

| **Cell Type** | **Role in ERM Pathogenesis** |
|---|---|
| [[Müller cells]] (retinal glial cells) | Predominant cell type in idiopathic ERMs; migrate through ILM defects; produce extracellular matrix |
| [[Astrocytes]] | Contribute to glial component; migrate from optic nerve head and retinal nerve fiber layer |
| [[Hyalocytes]] (vitreous macrophages) | Residual vitreous cortex cells; capable of transdifferentiation into myofibroblasts |
| [[Retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE) cells | More prominent in secondary ERMs; migrate through retinal breaks |
| [[Fibroblasts]] / [[Myofibroblasts]] | Key contractile cells; responsible for membrane contraction and retinal distortion |
| [[Macrophages]] / Immune cells | Inflammatory mediators; more prominent in secondary (inflammatory) ERMs |
| [[Fibrous astrocytes]] | Contribute to structural integrity of the membrane |

### Extracellular Matrix and Membrane Contraction

The extracellular matrix (ECM) of epiretinal membranes is composed of [[collagen]] (types I, II, III, IV, and VI), [[fibronectin]], [[laminin]], [[vitronectin]], and [[tenascin]]. The progressive deposition of ECM components, combined with the transdifferentiation of resident cells into contractile [[myofibroblasts]] expressing [[alpha-smooth muscle actin]] (α-SMA), leads to membrane contraction.

This contraction generates tangential tractional forces on the underlying retina, resulting in:

- **Retinal wrinkling and striae** (surface wrinkling retinopathy)
- **Macular thickening and edema** due to disruption of the normal retinal architecture and impaired fluid transport
- **Foveal ectopia** (displacement of the foveal center)
- **Distortion of the inner retinal layers**, particularly the [[inner nuclear layer]] (INL) and [[ganglion cell layer]] (GCL)
- **Pseudohole formation** — a central area of relative thinning surrounded by thickened, contracted membrane, mimicking a [[macular hole]]

### Molecular Mediators

Several growth factors and cytokines have been implicated in ERM pathogenesis:

- **[[Transforming growth factor-beta]] (TGF-β)**: Promotes fibrosis, myofibroblast differentiation, and ECM production
- **[[Platelet-derived growth factor]] (PDGF)**: Stimulates cellular proliferation and migration
- **[[Connective tissue growth factor]] (CTGF)**: Mediates fibrotic response downstream of TGF-β
- **[[Basic fibroblast growth factor]] (bFGF)**: Promotes angiogenesis and cellular proliferation
- **[[Interleukin-6]] (IL-6)** and **[[Interleukin-8]] (IL-8)**: Pro-inflammatory cytokines found in elevated concentrations in vitreous samples from eyes with ERM
- **[[Matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs)**: Involved in ECM remodeling and membrane maturation

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

The clinical symptoms of epiretinal membrane are highly variable and depend on the severity, location, and degree of tractional distortion of the membrane. Many patients with early or mild ERMs are entirely asymptomatic, with the membrane discovered incidentally during routine [[dilated fundus examination]].

Common symptoms include:

| **Symptom** | **Description** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|---|
| **Decreased [[visual acuity]]** | Gradual, painless reduction in central vision; typically mild to moderate (20/25 to 20/60), but can be severe | Macular thickening, foveal distortion, photoreceptor displacement |
| **[[Metamorphopsia]]** | Distortion of straight lines (wavy, bent, or irregular); often the most bothersome symptom | Tangential traction causing displacement of foveal photoreceptors |
| **[[Micropsia]] / [[Macropsia]]** | Objects appear smaller or larger than actual size | Stretching or compression of photoreceptor mosaic |
| **[[Aniseikonia]]** | Perceived difference in image size between the two eyes | Asymmetric macular distortion |
| **Decreased contrast sensitivity** | Difficulty distinguishing objects from background, especially in low-contrast conditions | Disruption of normal foveal architecture and light scatter |
| **Central or paracentral [[scotoma]]** | Subjective awareness of a dim or missing area in central vision | Severe macular thickening or photoreceptor damage |
| **[[Monocular diplopia]]** | Perception of double or ghost images with one eye | Irregular retinal surface causing optical aberration |
| **Difficulty with reading and fine tasks** | Impaired near vision disproportionate to distance acuity | Foveal distortion affecting high-spatial-frequency tasks |

Symptoms are typically slowly progressive over months to years. Rapid visual decline should prompt consideration of concurrent pathology such as [[cystoid macular edema]] (CME), [[macular hole]] formation, or progression of an underlying secondary cause.

### Clinical Signs

On [[dilated fundus examination]] and [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] with a fundus lens (e.g., [[78D lens]], [[90D lens]], or [[Goldmann three-mirror lens]]), the following signs may be observed:

- **Cellophane reflex**: A glistening, translucent sheen on the macular surface, representing an early, thin membrane. This is the hallmark of early or mild ERM (cellophane maculopathy).
- **Retinal striae**: Fine, irregular wrinkling of the inner retinal surface radiating from the membrane's center of contraction.
- **Macular thickening**: Visible thickening of the macula, often with loss of the normal foveal depression ([[foveal reflex]]).
- **Vascular tortuosity and straightening**: Distortion of the perifoveal retinal vasculature, with vessels appearing straightened, dragged, or tortuous due to tangential traction.
- **Macular pseudohole**: A central area of relative retinal thinning surrounded by thickened, contracted membrane, creating the appearance of a full-thickness [[macular hole]] on clinical examination. Unlike a true macular hole, visual acuity is often relatively preserved.
- **Macular edema**: Intraretinal fluid accumulation, sometimes with cystoid changes, secondary to tractional disruption of retinal homeostasis.
- **Foveal ectopia**: Displacement of the foveal center from its normal anatomical position.
- **Retinal hemorrhages**: Rarely, small intraretinal hemorrhages may occur due to vascular distortion.

### Gass Classification

Gass originally classified epiretinal membranes into three grades based on clinical appearance:

| **Grade** | **Clinical Appearance** | **Visual Impact** |
|---|---|---|
| **Grade 0 (Cellophane maculopathy)** | Translucent, thin membrane; cellophane-like reflex; no retinal distortion | Usually asymptomatic; normal or near-normal visual acuity |
| **Grade 1 (Crinkled cellophane maculopathy)** | Thicker membrane with irregular wrinkling of the inner retinal surface | Mild metamorphopsia; mild visual acuity reduction |
| **Grade 2 (Macular pucker / Preretinal macular fibrosis)** | Opaque, dense fibrous membrane with significant retinal distortion, vascular tortuosity, and macular thickening | Significant metamorphopsia; moderate to severe visual acuity loss |

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT), particularly [[spectral-domain OCT]] (SD-OCT) and [[swept-source OCT]] (SS-OCT), is the gold standard diagnostic tool for evaluating epiretinal membranes. OCT provides high-resolution, cross-sectional imaging of the vitreomacular interface and retinal layers, enabling precise characterization of the membrane and its effects on the underlying retina.

Key OCT findings in ERM include:

| **OCT Finding** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|
| **Hyperreflective line on the inner retinal surface** | Direct visualization of the epiretinal membrane |
| **Loss of the normal foveal contour (foveal pit)** | Indicates tractional distortion of the fovea |
| **Increased central macular thickness (CMT)** | Quantitative measure of macular thickening; correlates with visual acuity |
| **Intraretinal cystoid spaces** | Cystoid macular edema secondary to tractional disruption |
| **Inner retinal layer corrugation ("saw-tooth" pattern)** | Tangential traction causing undulation of inner retinal layers |
| **Disruption of the [[ellipsoid zone]] (EZ) / [[external limiting membrane]] (ELM)** | Indicates photoreceptor damage; poor prognostic indicator for visual recovery after surgery |
| **Ectopic inner foveal layers (EIFL)** | Presence of inner retinal layers (GCL, IPL, INL) within the foveal center, which are normally absent; indicates centripetal contraction |
| **Cotton-ball sign** | Focal area of hyperreflectivity within the inner nuclear layer; represents Müller cell proliferation |
| **Schisis-like thickening** | Separation of retinal layers mimicking [[retinoschisis]] |
| **Pseudohole** | Steep foveal contour with near-normal central thickness but thickened perifoveal retina |
| **Lamellar hole** | Irregular foveal contour with intraretinal splitting, often associated with ERM |
| **Vitreomacular traction (VMT)** | Persistent attachment of the posterior hyaloid to the fovea with anteroposterior traction |

**En face OCT** imaging allows visualization of the membrane's extent and morphology in the coronal plane, complementing cross-sectional analysis.

**OCT angiography ([[OCTA]])** can be used to evaluate the perifoveal capillary network, demonstrating vascular distortion, capillary dropout, or enlargement of the [[foveal avascular zone]] (FAZ) in eyes with ERM.

### Fundus Photography

Standard [[fundus photography]] documents the clinical appearance of the membrane, retinal striae, vascular changes, and macular thickening. Serial photography is valuable for monitoring progression over time.

### Fluorescein Angiography (FA)

[[Fluorescein angiography]] is not routinely required for the diagnosis of ERM but may be useful in specific clinical scenarios:

- Differentiating ERM from other causes of macular edema (e.g., [[diabetic macular edema]], [[retinal vein occlusion]])
- Evaluating secondary causes of ERM
- Identifying concurrent [[cystoid macular edema]] (late-phase petaloid hyperfluorescence)
- Assessing vascular distortion and capillary non-perfusion

### Amsler Grid Testing

The [[Amsler grid]] is a simple, office-based screening tool that can detect and monitor [[metamorphopsia]]. Patients with ERM may report distortion, waviness, or irregularity of the grid lines, particularly in the central or paracentral area. While subjective and limited in sensitivity, it is useful for patient self-monitoring.

### Microperimetry

[[Microperimetry]] (fundus-related perimetry) provides a functional map of retinal sensitivity correlated with fundus anatomy. In ERM, microperimetry may reveal:

- Reduced macular sensitivity
- Displacement of the preferred retinal locus (PRL)
- Correlation between areas of retinal thickening and functional loss

### B-scan Ultrasonography

[[B-scan ultrasonography]] may be employed when media opacities (e.g., dense [[cataract]], [[vitreous hemorrhage]]) preclude adequate fundoscopic or OCT examination. It can identify the presence of a PVD, vitreous opacities, or associated retinal detachment but lacks the resolution to directly visualize thin epiretinal membranes.

## Differential Diagnosis

Several conditions may mimic or coexist with epiretinal membrane and must be considered in the differential diagnosis:

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| **[[Vitreomacular traction]] (VMT)** | Anteroposterior traction from persistent vitreous attachment; may coexist with ERM; OCT shows vitreous attachment with retinal distortion |
| **Full-thickness [[macular hole]]** | Complete disruption of all retinal layers at the fovea; OCT shows full-thickness defect with surrounding cuff of subretinal fluid |
| **[[Lamellar macular hole]]** | Partial-thickness foveal defect; irregular foveal contour on OCT; may be associated with ERM |
| **[[Macular pseudohole]]** | Steep foveal contour due to perifoveal ERM contraction; central retinal thickness is near-normal; good visual acuity |
| **[[Cystoid macular edema]] (CME)** | Intraretinal cystic spaces; may be secondary to ERM traction or other causes (post-surgical, inflammatory, vascular) |
| **[[Central serous chorioretinopathy]] (CSC)** | Serous neurosensory detachment; subretinal fluid on OCT; RPE changes on FA |
| **[[Diabetic macular edema]] (DME)** | Macular thickening in the context of [[diabetic retinopathy]]; hard exudates; microaneurysms |
| **[[Age-related macular degeneration]] (AMD)** | Drusen, RPE changes, choroidal neovascularization; distinct OCT and FA findings |

## Natural History and Prognosis

The natural history of epiretinal membrane is generally one of slow progression, though a significant proportion of cases remain stable or even improve spontaneously.

- **Spontaneous separation**: Approximately 2–4% of ERMs may spontaneously separate from the retinal surface, often in association with completion of PVD, leading to visual improvement.
- **Stable disease**: Many ERMs, particularly early cellophane maculopathy (Grade 0), remain stable for years without significant visual deterioration.
- **Progressive disease**: A subset of ERMs, particularly those with thicker, more contractile membranes, will progressively worsen, with increasing metamorphopsia and declining visual acuity.

Factors associated with poorer visual prognosis include:

- Greater baseline macular thickness
- Disruption of the [[ellipsoid zone]] (EZ) and [[external limiting membrane]] (ELM) on OCT
- Presence of ectopic inner foveal layers (EIFL)
- Longer duration of symptoms prior to intervention
- Concurrent macular pathology (e.g., [[macular hole]], [[diabetic retinopathy]])

## Management

### Observation

The majority of epiretinal membranes, particularly those that are mild, asymptomatic, or minimally symptomatic, are managed with observation alone. Periodic monitoring with:

- **Best-corrected [[visual acuity]] (BCVA)** testing
- **[[Amsler grid]]** assessment
- **Serial [[OCT]]** imaging (typically every 6–12 months)

is recommended to detect progression. Patients should be educated about the nature of the condition, the importance of monitoring, and symptoms that should prompt earlier evaluation (e.g., worsening distortion, significant visual decline).

### Pharmacological Interventions

There is currently **no established pharmacological treatment** for epiretinal membrane. However, several investigational approaches have been explored:

| **Agent** | **Mechanism** | **Status** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Ocriplasmin]] (Jetrea®)** | Recombinant truncated form of [[plasmin]]; enzymatic vitreolysis; cleaves fibronectin and laminin at the vitreomacular interface | FDA-approved for symptomatic [[vitreomacular traction]] (VMT); **not approved or effective for established ERM**; may be considered in cases with concurrent VMT |
| **Intravitreal [[anti-VEGF]] agents** | Inhibit [[vascular endothelial growth factor]]; reduce vascular permeability and edema | May be used as adjunctive therapy for concurrent macular edema (e.g., in secondary ERMs associated with diabetic retinopathy or vein occlusion); **not a primary treatment for ERM** |
| **Intravitreal [[corticosteroids]]** | Anti-inflammatory; reduce macular edema | May be considered for secondary ERMs with significant inflammatory or edematous component; risk of [[cataract]] and [[glaucoma]] |

### Surgical Treatment: Pars Plana Vitrectomy (PPV)

**[[Pars plana vitrectomy]] (PPV)** with membrane peeling is the definitive treatment for visually significant epiretinal membrane. It is the most commonly performed vitreoretinal surgical procedure worldwide.

#### Indications for Surgery

There are no universally agreed-upon absolute indications, but surgery is generally considered when:

- **Visual acuity** is reduced to 20/40 or worse (though some surgeons intervene at better acuity levels if metamorphopsia is significant)
- **Symptomatic metamorphopsia** significantly impacts quality of life and daily activities (reading, driving, facial recognition)
- **Progressive visual decline** documented on serial examinations
- **Significant aniseikonia** causing binocular dysfunction
- **Concurrent pathology** requiring vitrectomy (e.g., [[macular hole]], [[retinal detachment]])

#### Surgical Technique

Modern ERM surgery typically involves:

1. **Standard three-port [[pars plana vitrectomy]]** using 23-gauge, 25-gauge, or 27-gauge microincision vitrectomy systems (MIVS)
2. **Core vitrectomy** with removal of the central and peripheral vitreous gel
3. **Induction of [[posterior vitreous detachment]]** if not already present, using active aspiration over the optic disc
4. **Membrane identification and peeling**: The ERM is engaged at its edge using intraocular forceps (e.g., end-gripping or asymmetric forceps) and carefully peeled from the retinal surface in a circumferential or tangential fashion
5. **[[Internal limiting membrane]] (ILM) peeling**: Increasingly performed as an adjunctive step to reduce ERM recurrence. The ILM is stained with a vital dye to enhance visualization:

| **Vital Dye** | **Characteristics** |
|---|---|
| **[[Indocyanine green]] (ICG)** | Excellent ILM staining; concerns regarding potential retinal toxicity at higher concentrations |
| **[[Brilliant blue G]] (BBG) / Brilliant Peel®** | Selective ILM staining; favorable safety profile; widely used |
| **[[Trypan blue]] (TB) / MembraneBlue®** | Stains ERM preferentially; less selective for ILM |
| **[[Triamcinolone acetonide]]** | White crystalline suspension; highlights residual vitreous cortex and membrane remnants; not a true dye |

6. **Fluid-air exchange** (if indicated, e.g., concurrent macular hole)
7. **Sclerotomy closure** (sutureless in most MIVS cases)

#### Surgical Outcomes

Surgical outcomes for ERM peeling are generally favorable:

- **Visual acuity improvement**: Approximately 70–90% of patients experience improvement in BCVA, with a mean improvement of 2–3 Snellen lines
- **Metamorphopsia reduction**: Significant improvement in metamorphopsia is reported in the majority of patients, though complete resolution may not occur
- **Anatomical success**: OCT demonstrates reduction in central macular thickness, restoration of foveal contour, and improvement in retinal layer architecture in most cases

**Prognostic factors for better surgical outcomes include:**

- Shorter duration of symptoms (< 6 months)
- Better preoperative visual acuity
- Intact [[ellipsoid zone]] (EZ) and [[external limiting membrane]] (ELM) on preoperative OCT
- Absence of ectopic inner foveal layers (EIFL)
- Younger patient age
- Idiopathic (vs. secondary) etiology

#### Complications of Surgery

| **Complication** | **Incidence** | **Notes** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Cataract]] progression** | 60–90% within 1–2 years (in phakic eyes) | Most common complication; often necessitates subsequent [[cataract surgery]]; combined phacovitrectomy increasingly performed |
| **ERM recurrence** | 2–7% (lower with ILM peeling) | ILM peeling reduces recurrence to < 2% |
| **[[Retinal detachment]]** | 1–3% | Iatrogenic retinal breaks during surgery |
| **[[Endophthalmitis]]** | < 0.05% | Rare but devastating; requires emergent treatment |
| **[[Cystoid macular edema]] (CME)** | 5–15% | Usually self-limiting; may require topical NSAIDs or corticosteroids |
| **[[Macular hole]] formation** | 1–3% | May occur during or after membrane peeling |
| **Elevated [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP)** | Variable | Transient; related to gas tamponade or steroid use |
| **[[Vitreous hemorrhage]]** | 1–3% | Usually self-limiting |
| **Phototoxicity** | Rare | Related to prolonged intraoperative light exposure |
| **Dissociated optic nerve fiber layer (DONFL)** | Common after ILM peeling | Arcuate, dimpled appearance of the inner retina on OCT; usually visually insignificant |
| **Visual field defects** | Rare | Related to retinal nerve fiber layer damage |

### Combined Phacoemulsification-Vitrectomy (Phacovitrectomy)

In phakic patients, particularly those over 50 years of age, combined [[phacoemulsification]] [[cataract surgery]] with [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) implantation and vitrectomy with membrane peeling (**phacovitrectomy**) is increasingly favored. This approach:

- Eliminates the near-certain need for subsequent cataract surgery
- Provides improved surgical access and visualization during vitrectomy
- Reduces the total number of surgical procedures and associated risks
- Allows optimization of refractive outcome

## Special Considerations

### Epiretinal Membrane and Macular Pseudohole

A **[[macular pseudohole]]** is a clinical entity in which centripetal contraction of an epiretinal membrane creates a steep foveal contour that mimics a full-thickness [[macular hole]] on clinical examination. On OCT, the central foveal thickness is near-normal or only mildly reduced, and all retinal layers remain intact. Visual acuity is often relatively preserved (20/20 to 20/40). Management is typically observation unless significant symptoms develop.

### Epiretinal Membrane and Lamellar Macular Hole

**[[Lamellar macular hole]]** (LMH) may coexist with or develop secondary to epiretinal membrane. LMH is characterized by an irregular foveal contour with partial-thickness tissue loss and intraretinal splitting. The presence of a dense, tractional ERM overlying an LMH may warrant surgical intervention, though outcomes are less predictable than for ERM alone.

### Epiretinal Membrane in Myopic Eyes

Highly [[myopic]] eyes (axial length > 26 mm) may develop ERMs with distinct characteristics, including association with [[myopic foveoschisis]], [[posterior staphyloma]], and [[myopic traction maculopathy]]. Surgical outcomes in myopic eyes may be less favorable due to concurrent structural abnormalities.

### Pediatric Epiretinal Membrane

ERMs in children and young adults are rare and are almost always secondary to ocular inflammation ([[uveitis]]), trauma, or prior retinal surgery. The clinical behavior and surgical outcomes may differ from adult idiopathic ERMs.

## Patient Education and Counseling

Effective patient communication is essential in the management of epiretinal membrane. Key counseling points include:

- **Nature of the condition**: ERM is a common, age-related condition that is not cancerous and does not represent an emergency
- **Visual prognosis**: Many ERMs remain stable; significant visual loss requiring surgery occurs in a minority of cases
- **Monitoring**: Regular follow-up with OCT and visual acuity testing is important to detect progression
- **Self-monitoring**: Patients should be instructed in the use of the [[Amsler grid]] and advised to report any sudden change in vision, new floaters, or flashes of light (which may indicate [[retinal tear]] or [[retinal detachment]])
- **Surgical expectations**: If surgery is indicated, patients should understand that visual improvement is gradual (often over 3–6 months), metamorphopsia may not completely resolve, and cataract progression is expected in phakic eyes
- **Driving and activities**: Patients should be counseled regarding the impact of metamorphopsia and reduced acuity on driving and occupational tasks

---

## Summary

Epiretinal membrane is a prevalent, clinically significant macular disorder characterized by the formation of a fibrocellular membrane on the inner retinal surface. Its pathogenesis involves cellular migration and proliferation following posterior vitreous detachment, with subsequent membrane contraction causing macular distortion and visual impairment. [[Optical coherence tomography]] is the cornerstone of diagnosis and monitoring, providing detailed structural information about the membrane and its effects on retinal architecture. Management ranges from observation for mild, stable cases to [[pars plana vitrectomy]] with membrane peeling for visually significant disease. Surgical outcomes are generally favorable, with the majority of patients experiencing improvement in visual acuity and metamorphopsia. Ongoing research into the molecular mechanisms of membrane formation and novel therapeutic approaches continues to advance our understanding and management of this common condition.
