

# Corneal Abrasion

**Corneal abrasion** is a disruption or defect of the [[corneal epithelium]] resulting from mechanical trauma, foreign body contact, chemical exposure, or other insults to the anterior surface of the [[cornea]]. It is one of the most common ocular injuries encountered in both primary eye care and emergency medicine settings, accounting for approximately 10% of all new presentations to emergency eye services. Corneal abrasions can range from superficial micro-erosions involving only the outermost epithelial cell layers to deeper defects that extend to or through [[Bowman's layer]]. While the majority of simple corneal abrasions heal rapidly and without sequelae due to the remarkable regenerative capacity of the corneal epithelium, improper management or complications can lead to significant morbidity, including [[recurrent corneal erosion syndrome]], [[corneal ulcer|corneal ulceration]], infectious [[keratitis]], and permanent visual impairment. A thorough understanding of the pathophysiology, clinical presentation, diagnostic approach, and evidence-based management of corneal abrasion is essential for all optometrists and ophthalmologists.

---

## Epidemiology

Corneal abrasion is among the most frequently diagnosed ocular conditions in clinical practice. Key epidemiological features include:

- **Incidence**: Estimated at approximately 3 per 1,000 population per year in developed countries. Corneal abrasions represent the most common traumatic ocular injury seen in emergency departments.
- **Age and Sex Distribution**: While corneal abrasions can occur at any age, they are most prevalent in young adults (ages 20–40), with a male predominance (approximately 2:1 male-to-female ratio), largely attributable to occupational and recreational exposures.
- **Occupational Risk**: Workers in construction, metalworking, woodworking, agriculture, and manufacturing are at significantly elevated risk due to exposure to particulate matter, flying debris, and chemical agents. Failure to use appropriate [[protective eyewear]] is a major modifiable risk factor.
- **Contact Lens–Related Abrasions**: [[Contact lens]] wearers constitute a substantial subgroup. Improper lens hygiene, extended wear, poorly fitting lenses, and sleeping in contact lenses all increase the risk of epithelial disruption. Contact lens–related abrasions carry a higher risk of secondary [[infectious keratitis]], particularly from [[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]].
- **Pediatric Population**: Corneal abrasions are common in children, often resulting from fingernail scratches, toys, or accidental pokes. Diagnosis may be challenging in preverbal children who cannot articulate symptoms.
- **Sports-Related Injuries**: Racquet sports, basketball, and combat sports are associated with increased incidence of corneal abrasion.

## Anatomy of the Cornea

A comprehensive understanding of [[corneal anatomy]] is fundamental to appreciating the pathophysiology and clinical significance of corneal abrasion.

The cornea is a transparent, avascular structure that constitutes the anterior one-sixth of the outer fibrous tunic of the [[globe]]. It measures approximately 11.5 mm horizontally and 10.5 mm vertically, with a central thickness of approximately 540 µm. The cornea is composed of five distinct layers (from anterior to posterior):

| Layer | Approximate Thickness | Composition | Relevance to Corneal Abrasion |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Corneal epithelium]]** | 50–60 µm (5–7 cell layers) | Stratified squamous non-keratinized epithelium; basal cells, wing cells, superficial cells | Primary layer involved in corneal abrasion; rapid regeneration (turnover ~7–10 days) |
| **[[Bowman's layer]]** | 8–14 µm | Acellular condensation of anterior [[stroma]] collagen | Does not regenerate if damaged; deeper abrasions may involve this layer |
| **[[Corneal stroma]]** | ~500 µm (90% of corneal thickness) | Regularly arranged [[collagen]] lamellae (primarily Type I), [[keratocytes]], and ground substance | Involvement indicates a deeper injury beyond simple abrasion (i.e., [[corneal laceration]] or [[corneal ulcer]]) |
| **[[Descemet's membrane]]** | 10–15 µm | Basement membrane of the endothelium; Type IV and VIII collagen | Not involved in typical corneal abrasion |
| **[[Corneal endothelium]]** | ~5 µm (single cell layer) | Monolayer of hexagonal cells; maintains corneal deturgescence via Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump | Not involved in typical corneal abrasion |

The corneal epithelium is the most densely innervated tissue in the human body, supplied by the [[ophthalmic division]] (V₁) of the [[trigeminal nerve]] via the [[long ciliary nerves]] and the [[nasociliary nerve]]. This rich sensory innervation accounts for the intense pain associated with even minor epithelial defects. The epithelium is also bathed in the [[tear film]], which provides lubrication, nutrition, antimicrobial defense (via [[lysozyme]], [[lactoferrin]], [[immunoglobulin A]], and [[defensins]]), and a smooth optical surface.

## Etiology and Risk Factors

Corneal abrasions arise from a variety of mechanisms. Clinically, it is important to classify the etiology, as this influences management and prognosis.

### Common Causes

- **Mechanical Trauma**: Fingernail scratches, paper cuts, tree branches, contact with foreign objects (dust, sand, metal filings, glass particles)
- **[[Foreign body]]–Related**: Corneal or subtarsal [[foreign body]] causing secondary epithelial disruption with each blink
- **[[Contact lens]]–Related**: Overwear, poor fit, desiccation, insertion/removal trauma, sleeping in lenses, damaged or deposited lenses
- **Chemical Exposure**: Mild chemical splashes (acids or alkalis) can cause epithelial sloughing; severe chemical injuries extend beyond simple abrasion (see [[chemical burn of the eye]])
- **Thermal Injury**: Exposure to intense heat, welding flash ([[ultraviolet keratitis]]), or tanning beds
- **Iatrogenic**: Intraoperative epithelial defects during [[cataract surgery]], [[LASIK]], [[PRK]], or other anterior segment procedures; exposure during general anesthesia ([[exposure keratopathy]])
- **Spontaneous/Recurrent**: Patients with a history of prior corneal abrasion may develop [[recurrent corneal erosion syndrome]] (RCES) due to defective epithelial [[basement membrane]] adhesion

### Risk Factors

| Risk Factor | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Contact lens wear | Mechanical disruption, hypoxia, microbial colonization |
| Occupational exposure (metalwork, construction) | Flying debris, particulate matter |
| Dry eye disease ([[keratoconjunctivitis sicca]]) | Compromised tear film, poor epithelial hydration |
| [[Epithelial basement membrane dystrophy]] (EBMD/Map-Dot-Fingerprint dystrophy) | Defective hemidesmosomes and basement membrane adhesion complexes |
| [[Diabetes mellitus]] | Impaired corneal wound healing, [[diabetic keratopathy]] |
| [[Lagophthalmos]] / [[nocturnal lagophthalmos]] | Incomplete lid closure leading to exposure and desiccation |
| Neurotrophic cornea ([[neurotrophic keratitis]]) | Reduced corneal sensation, impaired epithelial healing |
| Prior ocular surgery (e.g., [[LASIK]], [[PRK]]) | Disrupted corneal nerves, altered epithelial adhesion |
| Pediatric age / cognitive impairment | Inability to protect eyes, accidental self-injury |

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of corneal abrasion involves a cascade of events beginning with mechanical disruption of the corneal epithelium and culminating in a coordinated wound healing response.

### Initial Injury

The inciting event causes shearing or avulsion of epithelial cells from the underlying [[basement membrane]] and [[Bowman's layer]]. The depth and extent of the defect depend on the nature, force, and duration of the insult. In a simple abrasion, only the epithelium is affected. The disruption exposes the dense network of sub-basal corneal nerve plexus fibers, which are branches of the [[ophthalmic nerve]] (V₁). Stimulation of these unmyelinated C-fibers and thinly myelinated Aδ-fibers produces immediate, intense nociceptive pain, reflex [[lacrimation]], [[blepharospasm]], and [[photophobia]].

### Inflammatory Response

Within minutes of epithelial disruption, a robust inflammatory cascade is initiated:

1. **Cytokine and Growth Factor Release**: Damaged epithelial cells and exposed keratocytes release pro-inflammatory cytokines including [[interleukin-1]] (IL-1), [[interleukin-6]] (IL-6), [[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] (TNF-α), and [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs). [[Epidermal growth factor]] (EGF), [[transforming growth factor-beta]] (TGF-β), [[hepatocyte growth factor]] (HGF), and [[keratinocyte growth factor]] (KGF) from the tear film and limbal vasculature stimulate epithelial repair.
2. **Polymorphonuclear Leukocyte (PMN) Infiltration**: [[Neutrophils]] migrate from the [[limbal vasculature]] and tear film into the wound bed within hours. While essential for antimicrobial defense, excessive neutrophil activity can release reactive oxygen species (ROS) and proteolytic enzymes that may exacerbate tissue damage if the inflammatory response is not properly regulated.
3. **Tear Film Disruption**: The loss of epithelial surface integrity disrupts the [[tear film]], reducing its stability and protective function. This creates a localized environment more susceptible to microbial colonization.

### Epithelial Wound Healing

Corneal epithelial wound healing is a highly organized, multi-phase process:

1. **Latent Phase (0–4 hours)**: Immediately following injury, there is a brief lag period during which cellular reorganization occurs. Residual epithelial cells at the wound margin flatten, lose their hemidesmosomes and desmosomes, and begin to extend lamellipodia.
2. **Cell Migration Phase (4–24 hours)**: Epithelial cells at the wound edge undergo active migration as a coherent sheet sliding over the exposed basement membrane and Bowman's layer. This process is driven by actin cytoskeletal reorganization and is facilitated by [[fibronectin]], [[fibrinogen]], and other extracellular matrix components deposited in the wound bed. Migration occurs at a rate of approximately 60–80 µm/hour. Small abrasions (1–2 mm) may achieve complete epithelial coverage within 24–48 hours.
3. **Cell Proliferation Phase (24–48 hours and beyond)**: [[Limbal stem cells]] located in the [[palisades of Vogt]] at the [[limbus]] undergo mitosis to replenish the migrating cell population. Transit-amplifying cells derived from limbal stem cells proliferate and differentiate as they migrate centripetally toward the wound.
4. **Adhesion and Remodeling Phase (Days to Weeks)**: Newly migrated epithelial cells must re-establish firm adhesion to the underlying basement membrane through the formation of new [[hemidesmosomes]], [[anchoring fibrils]] (Type VII collagen), and [[anchoring plaques]]. This adhesion complex maturation takes approximately 6–8 weeks to reach full strength, which explains the vulnerability to [[recurrent corneal erosion]] during this period.

### Factors Affecting Healing

Several factors can impair or delay corneal epithelial wound healing:

- **Infection**: Bacterial, viral, or fungal colonization of the wound bed
- **Persistent foreign body**: Subtarsal or embedded corneal foreign body causing ongoing mechanical trauma
- **Dry eye**: Inadequate tear film compromises cell migration and nutrition
- **Diabetes mellitus**: Impaired epithelial cell migration, reduced corneal nerve density, and altered growth factor signaling ([[diabetic keratopathy]])
- **Neurotrophic cornea**: Loss of trophic nerve-derived factors (e.g., [[substance P]], [[nerve growth factor]])
- **Topical medications**: Preserved [[artificial tears]], [[aminoglycoside]] antibiotics (e.g., [[gentamicin]], [[tobramycin]]), and [[nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs]] (NSAIDs) can be epitheliotoxic
- **Basement membrane abnormalities**: [[Epithelial basement membrane dystrophy]] predisposes to poor adhesion and recurrent erosion

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

Patients with corneal abrasion typically present acutely with a characteristic constellation of symptoms:

| Symptom | Description |
|---|---|
| **Pain** | Acute onset, sharp or stabbing pain; often severe and disproportionate to the size of the defect due to dense corneal innervation |
| **[[Foreign body sensation]]** | Persistent feeling of "something in the eye," exacerbated by blinking |
| **[[Lacrimation]]** (tearing) | Reflex tearing due to trigeminal nerve stimulation |
| **[[Photophobia]]** | Light sensitivity, often marked; caused by ciliary spasm and iris sphincter irritation |
| **[[Blepharospasm]]** | Involuntary, forceful eyelid closure |
| **Blurred vision** | Variable; depends on location and size of the defect; central abrasions cause more significant visual disturbance |
| **Redness** | [[Conjunctival injection]], particularly [[ciliary flush]] (circumcorneal injection) |
| **Headache** | Referred pain via the trigeminal nerve |

Patients can usually identify a specific inciting event (e.g., "I was poked in the eye," "something flew into my eye while grinding metal"). However, contact lens–related abrasions and those occurring during sleep may present without a clear history of trauma.

### Signs

On clinical examination, the following signs may be observed:

- **Epithelial defect**: Visible as an area of irregular or absent epithelium; may appear as a dull, irregular patch on the otherwise smooth, glistening corneal surface
- **[[Fluorescein]] staining**: The hallmark diagnostic finding. Fluorescein dye pools in areas of epithelial loss and fluoresces bright green under [[cobalt blue light]] or [[Wood's lamp]] illumination
- **Conjunctival injection**: Diffuse or sectoral; [[ciliary flush]] (deep perilimbal injection) may indicate significant anterior segment inflammation
- **[[Anterior chamber]] reaction**: Mild [[anterior uveitis]] (cells and flare in the anterior chamber) may accompany larger or deeper abrasions due to neurogenic inflammation
- **Lid edema**: Periorbital swelling, particularly in children
- **Subtarsal foreign body**: Must be excluded by [[eversion of the upper eyelid]]
- **Corneal edema**: Localized stromal edema surrounding the defect may be present in larger abrasions
- **Linear or vertical abrasion pattern**: Suggests a subtarsal foreign body causing repeated mechanical trauma with each blink; mandates thorough lid eversion

## Diagnosis

### Clinical History

A detailed history is the cornerstone of diagnosis and should include:

- **Mechanism of injury**: Nature of the inciting event, timing, and circumstances
- **Contact lens use**: Type of lens, wearing schedule, hygiene practices, sleeping in lenses
- **Occupational and recreational history**: Exposure to high-velocity projectiles, chemicals, UV radiation
- **Prior episodes**: History of previous corneal abrasion or recurrent erosion
- **Ocular history**: Pre-existing [[dry eye disease]], [[epithelial basement membrane dystrophy]], prior corneal surgery, [[diabetes mellitus]]
- **Symptoms**: Onset, severity, and progression of pain, photophobia, tearing, and visual disturbance
- **Red flags**: History of high-velocity projectile (risk of [[intraocular foreign body]] or [[open globe injury]]), chemical exposure, or significant blunt trauma

### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] is the gold standard for diagnosing and characterizing corneal abrasion. The examination should include:

1. **External examination**: Assess periorbital structures, lid position, and [[proptosis]]
2. **[[Visual acuity]]**: Document best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA) in each eye; may be reduced if the abrasion involves the [[visual axis]]
3. **Diffuse illumination**: Survey the cornea for gross defects, foreign bodies, and infiltrates
4. **Direct focal illumination**: Narrow beam to assess depth of the defect and detect stromal involvement
5. **[[Fluorescein]] staining with cobalt blue filter**: Instill fluorescein sodium dye (using a fluorescein strip moistened with sterile saline). Epithelial defects stain bright green. A [[Wratten #12 yellow filter]] (Burton lamp or slit-lamp yellow barrier filter) enhances contrast and sensitivity.
6. **[[Seidel test]]**: If there is any suspicion of a full-thickness corneal wound or [[corneal perforation]], apply concentrated fluorescein over the wound and observe for a streaming rivulet of aqueous humor (positive Seidel test), which indicates an [[open globe injury]]
7. **Anterior chamber assessment**: Evaluate for [[cells and flare]] indicating traumatic [[anterior uveitis]]; assess [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) if safe to do so (avoid [[applanation tonometry]] over the defect; use [[non-contact tonometry]] or [[Tono-Pen]] on the unaffected area)
8. **Upper lid eversion**: Mandatory in all cases to exclude a retained subtarsal [[foreign body]]
9. **Fundus examination**: [[Dilated fundus examination]] if there is any concern for posterior segment injury (e.g., high-velocity mechanism)

### Classification of Corneal Abrasions

| Classification | Description |
|---|---|
| **Simple/Traumatic** | Clean epithelial defect from mechanical trauma; low risk of infection (unless contact lens–related) |
| **Contact Lens–Related** | Abrasion in a contact lens wearer; higher risk of [[Pseudomonas]] and other gram-negative infections; must be managed more aggressively |
| **Foreign Body–Related** | Abrasion secondary to corneal or subtarsal foreign body; requires foreign body removal |
| **Recurrent Erosion** | Spontaneous epithelial breakdown, often upon waking; history of prior abrasion or underlying [[EBMD]] |
| **Large/Central** | Defects >4 mm or involving the visual axis; higher risk of complications and visual sequelae |

### Differential Diagnosis

It is critical to differentiate corneal abrasion from other conditions that may present similarly:

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| [[Corneal ulcer]] (infectious keratitis) | Stromal infiltrate (white opacity), mucopurulent discharge, anterior chamber reaction; often in contact lens wearers |
| [[Corneal foreign body]] | Visible embedded particle on slit-lamp; may have surrounding rust ring (metallic foreign body) |
| [[Recurrent corneal erosion syndrome]] | Spontaneous onset (often upon waking), history of prior abrasion, may show loose/redundant epithelium, negative [[Descemet's folds]] |
| [[Herpes simplex keratitis]] | [[Dendritic ulcer]] with terminal bulbs on fluorescein staining; reduced corneal sensation |
| [[Ultraviolet keratitis]] (photokeratitis) | Bilateral punctate epithelial erosions; history of UV exposure (welding, tanning bed, snow/sun) |
| [[Chemical burn]] | History of chemical exposure; may show limbal ischemia, conjunctival blanching |
| [[Anterior uveitis]] | Cells and flare without epithelial defect; photophobia, miosis |
| [[Acute angle-closure glaucoma]] | Corneal edema, shallow anterior chamber, elevated IOP, mid-dilated pupil |
| [[Open globe injury]] | Positive Seidel test, irregular pupil, shallow anterior chamber, subconjunctival hemorrhage |

## Management

### General Principles

The goals of corneal abrasion management are:

1. **Pain relief**
2. **Prevention of infection**
3. **Promotion of epithelial healing**
4. **Prevention of complications** (recurrent erosion, scarring, infection)
5. **Identification and treatment of underlying causes** (foreign body removal, contact lens discontinuation)

### First Aid and Initial Management

- **Irrigation**: Copious irrigation with sterile [[saline]] or [[balanced salt solution]] (BSS) if chemical exposure or particulate contamination is suspected
- **Foreign body removal**: If a corneal or subtarsal foreign body is identified, it should be removed under slit-lamp guidance using a sterile needle (25- or 27-gauge), spud, or jeweler's forceps. A [[rust ring]] from a metallic foreign body should be removed with an [[Alger brush]] or burr, either at the initial visit or within 24–48 hours once the surrounding epithelium has loosened
- **Contact lens discontinuation**: All contact lens wear must be immediately discontinued until the abrasion has fully healed and the eye is quiet; typically a minimum of 1–2 weeks after complete re-epithelialization

### Pharmacological Treatment

| Drug Class | Examples | Purpose | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Topical antibiotics (non–contact lens–related)** | [[Erythromycin]] 0.5% ointment; [[Polymyxin B/trimethoprim]] drops | Prophylaxis against secondary bacterial infection | Ointment preferred for lubrication and comfort; applied QID or at bedtime |
| **Topical antibiotics (contact lens–related)** | [[Fluoroquinolone]] drops: [[ofloxacin]] 0.3%, [[moxifloxacin]] 0.5%, [[ciprofloxacin]] 0.3% | Broad-spectrum coverage including [[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]] | Fluoroquinolones are mandatory for contact lens–related abrasions due to elevated risk of gram-negative infection |
| **[[Cycloplegic]] agents** | [[Cyclopentolate]] 1%, [[homatropine]] 5% | Pain relief via ciliary muscle relaxation; reduces photophobia from ciliary spasm | Particularly useful for larger abrasions with significant anterior chamber reaction; use with caution in narrow angles |
| **Oral analgesics** | [[Acetaminophen]], [[ibuprofen]], [[naproxen]] | Systemic pain control | NSAIDs preferred for anti-inflammatory effect; opioids rarely necessary |
| **Topical NSAIDs** | [[Diclofenac]] 0.1%, [[ketorolac]] 0.5% | Adjunctive pain relief | Use with caution; may delay epithelial healing; limit to 24–48 hours; avoid in contact lens–related abrasions or large defects |
| **Preservative-free [[artificial tears]]** | [[Carboxymethylcellulose]], [[hyaluronic acid]], [[polyethylene glycol]] | Lubrication, comfort, support of epithelial healing | Preservative-free formulations preferred to avoid epitheliotoxicity |
| **[[Bandage contact lens]] (BCL)** | Silicone hydrogel therapeutic lens (e.g., [[PureVision]], [[Air Optix Night & Day]]) | Pain relief, mechanical protection of healing epithelium | Must be used with concurrent topical antibiotic prophylaxis; monitor closely for infectious keratitis; remove once epithelium has healed |

### Practices No Longer Recommended

- **Eye patching**: Multiple randomized controlled trials and meta-analyses have demonstrated that pressure patching does **not** accelerate healing and may actually increase pain, reduce binocular vision, impair depth perception, and increase the risk of infection by creating a warm, moist environment. Patching is **no longer recommended** for routine corneal abrasion management.
- **Topical anesthetics for outpatient use**: Agents such as [[proparacaine]] 0.5% and [[tetracaine]] 0.5% are essential for examination but should **not** be prescribed for home use. Chronic topical anesthetic use causes severe epitheliotoxicity, inhibits epithelial healing, and can lead to [[corneal melting]], [[ring infiltrate]], and permanent vision loss. However, recent limited evidence suggests that short-term (24-hour), dilute topical anesthetic use under close supervision may be safe in select cases—this remains controversial and is not standard of care.

### Follow-Up

- **Small, simple abrasions** (<4 mm, non–contact lens–related): Follow-up in 24–48 hours to confirm healing. If symptoms are improving and the defect is closing, the patient may be followed as needed.
- **Large abrasions** (>4 mm), **central abrasions**, or **contact lens–related abrasions**: Follow-up within 24 hours is mandatory. Daily follow-up may be necessary until significant healing is confirmed.
- **Bandage contact lens in situ**: Follow-up within 24 hours; continue daily or every-other-day monitoring until the lens is removed.
- **Persistent epithelial defect** (failure to heal within 5–7 days): Warrants further investigation for underlying causes (e.g., retained foreign body, [[neurotrophic keratitis]], [[limbal stem cell deficiency]], [[EBMD]], [[diabetes mellitus]], medication toxicity). Consider referral to a [[cornea specialist]].

## Complications

| Complication | Description | Risk Factors |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Recurrent corneal erosion syndrome]] (RCES)** | Spontaneous re-opening of the healed abrasion site, typically upon waking; caused by defective epithelial adhesion to the basement membrane | Prior traumatic abrasion (especially fingernail or paper), underlying [[EBMD]], [[diabetes]] |
| **Infectious [[keratitis]]** | Bacterial, fungal, or [[Acanthamoeba]] infection of the corneal stroma | Contact lens wear, contaminated foreign body, agricultural injury, immunosuppression |
| **[[Corneal scarring]]** | Permanent stromal opacity if the injury extends beyond the epithelium or if infection occurs | Deep abrasion, infection, delayed healing |
| **Traumatic [[anterior uveitis]]** | Inflammation of the [[iris]] and [[ciliary body]] secondary to corneal injury | Large or deep abrasions |
| **[[Corneal neovascularization]]** | Ingrowth of blood vessels into the normally avascular cornea | Chronic inflammation, prolonged contact lens wear, [[limbal stem cell deficiency]] |
| **Persistent epithelial defect** | Failure of the epithelium to heal within the expected timeframe (>5–7 days) | Diabetes, neurotrophic cornea, dry eye, medication toxicity |

### Management of Recurrent Corneal Erosion Syndrome

RCES is the most common long-term complication of corneal abrasion and deserves special attention:

- **Conservative measures**: Aggressive lubrication with preservative-free artificial tears during the day and lubricating ointment (e.g., [[Refresh PM]], [[Lacri-Lube]]) at bedtime for 3–6 months; [[hypertonic saline]] drops (e.g., [[Muro 128]] 5%) to promote epithelial adhesion
- **Extended bandage contact lens wear**: Continuous wear therapeutic lens for 8–12 weeks to allow basement membrane remodeling
- **[[Anterior stromal puncture]]**: Office-based procedure using a 25-gauge needle to create focal adhesions between the epithelium and stroma; effective for erosions outside the visual axis
- **[[Phototherapeutic keratectomy]] (PTK)**: [[Excimer laser]] ablation of the superficial cornea to promote new basement membrane formation and stronger epithelial adhesion; treatment of choice for recalcitrant RCES, especially within the visual axis
- **[[Epithelial debridement]] with diamond burr polishing**: Removal of loose epithelium followed by smoothing of Bowman's layer with a rotating diamond burr to enhance adhesion
- **[[Autologous serum tears]]**: May be considered in refractory cases to provide growth factors and promote healing

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Corneal Abrasion

- Children may present with nonspecific symptoms: irritability, eye rubbing, refusal to open the eye
- Examination may require topical anesthesia and, in very young or uncooperative children, a brief examination under sedation
- Fingernail scratches from siblings or self-inflicted injury are common
- Healing is typically rapid in children due to robust regenerative capacity
- Consider [[non-accidental injury]] (child abuse) if the history is inconsistent with the clinical findings

### Corneal Abrasion Under General Anesthesia

- [[Exposure keratopathy]] during general anesthesia is a well-recognized cause of iatrogenic corneal abrasion, occurring in 0.01–0.17% of general anesthetics
- Risk factors include prone positioning, prolonged surgery, inadequate lid taping, and reduced tear production under anesthesia
- Prevention includes careful lid taping, application of lubricating ointment, and avoidance of direct pressure on the globe

### Corneal Abrasion in Diabetes Mellitus

- Patients with [[diabetes mellitus]] are at increased risk of corneal abrasion and delayed healing due to [[diabetic keratopathy]]
- Features include reduced corneal sensitivity, impaired epithelial cell migration, decreased limbal stem cell function, and altered basement membrane composition
- These patients require closer follow-up and may benefit from preservative-free lubricants, [[autologous serum tears]], or [[amniotic membrane transplantation]] for persistent defects

## Prognosis

The prognosis for simple corneal abrasion is excellent. The vast majority of uncomplicated abrasions heal completely within 24–72 hours without residual scarring or visual impairment. Key prognostic factors include:

- **Size and depth**: Smaller, superficial abrasions heal faster
- **Location**: Peripheral abrasions are less likely to affect vision than central abrasions
- **Etiology**: Simple traumatic abrasions have the best prognosis; contact lens–related abrasions carry higher infection risk
- **Patient factors**: Young, healthy patients heal faster; patients with diabetes, dry eye, or neurotrophic cornea may experience delayed healing
- **Compliance**: Adherence to prescribed medications and follow-up appointments is critical

---

## Prevention

Prevention of corneal abrasion is a key component of public health and occupational safety:

- **[[Protective eyewear]]**: ANSI Z87.1-rated safety glasses or goggles should be worn in all high-risk occupational and recreational settings
- **Proper contact lens care**: Adherence to recommended wearing schedules, proper cleaning and disinfection, avoidance of sleeping in lenses (unless specifically approved for extended wear), and regular replacement
- **Nail trimming**: Particularly important in infants and young children to prevent accidental corneal scratches
- **Workplace safety programs**: Education and enforcement of eye protection policies
- **Perioperative eye protection**: Appropriate lid taping and lubrication during general anesthesia

---

## Summary

Corneal abrasion is a ubiquitous ocular injury characterized by disruption of the [[corneal epithelium]]. Its diagnosis relies on a thorough clinical history, [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]], and [[fluorescein]] staining. Management centers on pain control, infection prophylaxis with topical antibiotics (with fluoroquinolone coverage mandatory for contact lens–related cases), promotion of epithelial healing, and prevention of complications such as [[recurrent corneal erosion syndrome]] and infectious [[keratitis]]. Eye patching is no longer recommended, and topical anesthetics should not be dispensed for home use. With appropriate management, the prognosis is excellent, though patients should be counseled about the risk of recurrent erosion and the importance of follow-up.
