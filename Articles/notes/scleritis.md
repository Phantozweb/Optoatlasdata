

# Scleritis

**Scleritis** is a severe, vision-threatening inflammatory disorder characterized by edema and cellular infiltration of the [[sclera]], the dense, white, collagenous outer coat of the eye. Unlike the more benign [[episcleritis]], scleritis involves the deeper scleral tissue, is frequently associated with significant ocular morbidity, and may serve as a sentinel marker for serious underlying systemic disease—most commonly [[autoimmune]] and [[connective tissue disorders]]. Scleritis demands prompt recognition and aggressive management by eye care professionals, as delayed treatment can result in irreversible structural damage, including scleral thinning, [[staphyloma]] formation, and permanent vision loss.

Scleritis accounts for approximately 0.08% of new ophthalmology consultations and has an estimated annual incidence of 3.4–6.3 per 100,000 population. It most commonly affects women between the fourth and sixth decades of life, with a female-to-male ratio of approximately 1.6:1. Up to 50% of patients with scleritis will have an identifiable systemic association, underscoring the importance of a thorough systemic workup in every case.

---

## Classification

The most widely accepted classification system for scleritis was proposed by Watson and Hayreh in 1976 and subsequently refined. This system divides scleritis based on anatomical location and clinical morphology:

| **Type** | **Subtype** | **Approximate Frequency** | **Key Features** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Anterior Scleritis** | Diffuse | ~40–45% | Most common and generally most benign form; widespread scleral inflammation |
| | Nodular | ~20–25% | Discrete, immobile, tender scleral nodule(s) |
| | Necrotizing with inflammation | ~10–14% | Most destructive form; severe pain; scleral necrosis; highest association with systemic vasculitis |
| | Necrotizing without inflammation ([[Scleromalacia perforans]]) | ~4–5% | Painless scleral thinning/melting; almost exclusively in long-standing [[rheumatoid arthritis]] |
| **Posterior Scleritis** | — | ~2–12% | Inflammation posterior to the [[ora serrata]]; often underdiagnosed; may present with [[exudative retinal detachment]], [[choroidal folds]], or [[optic disc edema]] |

Anterior scleritis is far more common than posterior scleritis, comprising approximately 90% of all cases. However, anterior and posterior scleritis may coexist, and posterior scleritis is frequently underdiagnosed due to its more subtle clinical presentation.

## Anatomy of the Sclera

Understanding the anatomy of the [[sclera]] is essential for comprehending the pathophysiology of scleritis. The sclera is a dense, avascular, fibrous connective tissue structure that forms approximately five-sixths of the outer coat of the globe. It is composed primarily of irregularly arranged [[collagen]] fibrils (predominantly types I and III), [[proteoglycans]], and [[glycosaminoglycans]], embedded within a ground substance matrix. The sclera is thinnest at the insertion of the [[rectus muscles]] (approximately 0.3 mm) and thickest at the posterior pole near the [[optic nerve]] (approximately 1.0–1.35 mm).

The sclera is organized into three layers:

1. **[[Episclera]]**: The outermost, vascularized layer of loose connective tissue lying between [[Tenon's capsule]] and the scleral stroma proper. It contains the superficial and deep episcleral vascular plexuses.
2. **Scleral stroma**: The main body of the sclera, composed of dense, interlacing collagen bundles. It is relatively avascular, receiving its nutrition primarily by diffusion from the episcleral and choroidal vasculature.
3. **[[Lamina fusca]]**: The innermost layer, a thin pigmented zone that transitions into the [[suprachoroidal space]].

The relative avascularity of the scleral stroma is clinically significant: once an inflammatory or immune-mediated process is established within the sclera, the limited blood supply impairs both immune clearance and drug delivery, contributing to the chronicity and treatment resistance often observed in scleritis.

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of scleritis is complex and multifactorial, but the predominant mechanism in most cases is **immune-mediated inflammation**. The following pathogenic pathways have been identified:

### Immune Complex–Mediated Vasculitis

The most well-characterized mechanism involves [[immune complex]] deposition within and around scleral and episcleral blood vessels. Circulating immune complexes composed of [[immunoglobulin G]] (IgG), [[immunoglobulin M]] (IgM), and [[complement]] components (particularly [[C3]] and [[C4]]) deposit in vessel walls, activating the [[classical complement pathway]]. This triggers a cascade of events:

- Recruitment of [[neutrophils]] and [[macrophages]] via [[chemotaxis]]
- Release of [[proteolytic enzymes]], including [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs), [[collagenases]], and [[elastases]]
- Generation of [[reactive oxygen species]] (ROS)
- Direct endothelial damage and fibrinoid necrosis of vessel walls

This vasculitic process leads to ischemia, tissue necrosis, and progressive destruction of the scleral collagen matrix—hallmarks of necrotizing scleritis.

### T-Cell–Mediated (Type IV) Hypersensitivity

Histopathological studies of scleritic tissue frequently demonstrate a **granulomatous inflammatory pattern** with [[epithelioid cells]], [[multinucleated giant cells]], [[lymphocytes]] (predominantly [[CD4+ T helper cells]]), and [[plasma cells]] arranged in a zonal pattern around areas of scleral necrosis. This pattern is consistent with a [[delayed-type hypersensitivity]] (DTH) reaction and is particularly prominent in scleritis associated with [[granulomatosis with polyangiitis]] (formerly Wegener's granulomatosis), [[sarcoidosis]], and [[tuberculosis]].

### Role of Matrix Metalloproteinases

Elevated levels of [[MMP-1]], [[MMP-2]], [[MMP-3]], [[MMP-8]], and [[MMP-9]] have been demonstrated in scleritic tissue and in the tears of patients with active scleritis. These enzymes degrade the collagen and proteoglycan components of the scleral extracellular matrix, contributing to scleral thinning and, in severe cases, perforation.

### Cytokine and Inflammatory Mediator Involvement

Proinflammatory cytokines including [[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] (TNF-α), [[interleukin-1]] (IL-1), [[interleukin-6]] (IL-6), [[interleukin-8]] (IL-8), and [[interferon-gamma]] (IFN-γ) are upregulated in scleritic tissue. These mediators amplify the inflammatory cascade, promote leukocyte recruitment, and sustain chronic inflammation. The central role of TNF-α in the pathogenesis of scleritis has been validated by the clinical efficacy of [[anti-TNF biologic agents]] in refractory cases.

### Infectious Pathophysiology

In a minority of cases (approximately 5–10%), scleritis results from direct infectious invasion of the sclera. Organisms implicated include [[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]], [[Staphylococcus aureus]], [[herpes simplex virus]] (HSV), [[varicella-zoster virus]] (VZV), [[Mycobacterium tuberculosis]], [[Treponema pallidum]], and [[Acanthamoeba]]. Infectious scleritis may occur following ocular surgery (particularly [[pterygium]] excision with adjunctive [[mitomycin C]] or [[beta-irradiation]]), trauma, or in the setting of systemic immunosuppression. The pathophysiology in these cases involves direct tissue destruction by the organism, as well as a secondary host inflammatory response.

## Etiology and Systemic Associations

Approximately 40–50% of patients with scleritis have an identifiable underlying systemic disease. The most common associations are with autoimmune and connective tissue disorders:

| **Category** | **Associated Conditions** |
|---|---|
| **Connective Tissue / Autoimmune** | [[Rheumatoid arthritis]] (most common, ~15–20%), [[granulomatosis with polyangiitis]], [[systemic lupus erythematosus]], [[polyarteritis nodosa]], [[relapsing polychondritis]], [[inflammatory bowel disease]], [[ankylosing spondylitis]], [[reactive arthritis]], [[psoriatic arthritis]], [[Behçet disease]], [[IgG4-related disease]] |
| **Vasculitis** | [[Giant cell arteritis]], [[microscopic polyangiitis]], [[eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis]] (Churg-Strauss), [[Cogan syndrome]] |
| **Infectious** | [[Herpes zoster ophthalmicus]], [[herpes simplex]], [[tuberculosis]], [[syphilis]], [[Lyme disease]], [[Pseudomonas]], [[fungal infections]], [[Acanthamoeba]] |
| **Other** | [[Sarcoidosis]], [[gout]], post-surgical (surgically induced necrotizing scleritis, or [[SINS]]), medication-induced (rare; e.g., [[bisphosphonates]]) |

**Necrotizing scleritis** carries the strongest systemic association, with up to 50–80% of patients having an underlying systemic vasculitis. Historically, necrotizing scleritis associated with [[rheumatoid arthritis]] was associated with a significantly increased 5-year mortality rate (up to 25–30% in older studies), largely due to the severity of the underlying systemic vasculitis. Modern immunosuppressive therapy has improved these outcomes.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Symptoms

The cardinal symptom of scleritis is **severe, deep, boring ocular pain** that is characteristically:

- Gradual in onset, worsening over several days
- Described as a deep, aching, or boring quality
- Radiating to the ipsilateral brow, temple, jaw, or sinuses
- Worse at night, frequently awakening the patient from sleep
- Exacerbated by eye movement
- Often poorly responsive to over-the-counter analgesics

Additional symptoms include:

- **Redness**: Deep violaceous (bluish-red) injection of the sclera, as opposed to the bright red, sectoral injection of [[episcleritis]]
- **Tearing** ([[epiphora]])
- **Photophobia**
- **Decreased vision**: Particularly in posterior scleritis or when complications such as [[keratitis]], [[uveitis]], [[cataract]], or [[glaucoma]] develop

**Scleromalacia perforans** is a notable exception: patients with this form of necrotizing scleritis without inflammation are typically **painless**, presenting instead with progressive, asymptomatic scleral thinning and uveal show.

### Signs

| **Type** | **Clinical Signs** |
|---|---|
| **Diffuse Anterior** | Widespread scleral edema and injection; deep violaceous hue; engorged deep episcleral and scleral vessels that do not blanch with topical [[phenylephrine]] 2.5–10% |
| **Nodular Anterior** | One or more discrete, firm, immobile, tender nodules on the scleral surface; deep vessel engorgement |
| **Necrotizing with Inflammation** | Intense pain; scleral avascular patches (vessel dropout); scleral thinning with exposure of underlying dark [[uveal tissue]] (uveal show); may progress to perforation |
| **Scleromalacia Perforans** | Painless; yellow-white scleral necrotic patches; progressive thinning; uveal show; may perforate with minimal trauma |
| **Posterior** | May have minimal external signs; findings include [[proptosis]], restricted motility, [[choroidal folds]], [[exudative retinal detachment]], [[optic disc swelling]], [[macular edema]], and thickening of the posterior sclera on imaging |

### Complications

Scleritis can lead to numerous sight-threatening complications, occurring in up to 40–60% of patients:

- **[[Peripheral ulcerative keratitis]]** (PUK): Corneal stromal melting adjacent to the limbus; strongly associated with necrotizing scleritis and systemic vasculitis
- **[[Anterior uveitis]]**: Occurs in approximately 30–40% of cases
- **[[Secondary glaucoma]]**: Due to trabecular meshwork inflammation, [[peripheral anterior synechiae]], or steroid response
- **[[Cataract]]**: From chronic inflammation or prolonged corticosteroid use
- **Scleral thinning and [[staphyloma]]**
- **[[Globe perforation]]**: Rare but devastating; most common in necrotizing scleritis
- **[[Exudative retinal detachment]]**: Particularly in posterior scleritis
- **[[Optic neuropathy]]**: From posterior scleral inflammation extending to the [[optic nerve sheath]]

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

Diagnosis of scleritis is primarily **clinical**, based on a thorough history and comprehensive [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] examination. Key diagnostic maneuvers include:

1. **Slit-lamp examination**: Assess for scleral edema, vessel engorgement pattern, nodules, necrosis, and uveal show. Evaluate the [[cornea]] for [[keratitis]] or [[peripheral ulcerative keratitis]], and the [[anterior chamber]] for cells and flare indicating concurrent [[uveitis]].

2. **Phenylephrine blanching test**: Instillation of topical [[phenylephrine]] 2.5% or 10% causes blanching of the superficial episcleral vessels but does **not** blanch the deep scleral vascular plexus. In scleritis, the deep scleral vessels remain engorged and visible after phenylephrine application, whereas in [[episcleritis]], the superficial vessels blanch, revealing a white sclera beneath. This test is critical for differentiating scleritis from episcleritis.

3. **Natural daylight examination**: The characteristic violaceous (blue-violet) hue of scleritis is best appreciated in natural daylight rather than under slit-lamp illumination.

4. **Dilated fundus examination**: Essential to evaluate for posterior segment complications including [[choroidal folds]], [[exudative retinal detachment]], [[optic disc edema]], and [[macular edema]].

5. **[[Intraocular pressure]] measurement**: To detect secondary [[glaucoma]] or [[ocular hypotony]].

### Imaging

| **Modality** | **Findings in Scleritis** | **Clinical Utility** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** | Scleral thickening (>2 mm); fluid in [[Tenon's space]] ("T-sign" in posterior scleritis); retrobulbar edema | Gold standard for diagnosing posterior scleritis; readily available |
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)** | Scleral thickening; subretinal fluid; choroidal thickening; macular edema | Useful for monitoring macular complications and treatment response |
| **[[Enhanced depth imaging OCT]] (EDI-OCT)** | Detailed visualization of scleral and choroidal thickness changes | Increasingly used for quantitative monitoring |
| **[[Anterior segment OCT]] (AS-OCT)** | Scleral edema; nodule characterization; scleral thinning measurement | Helpful for anterior scleritis assessment and monitoring |
| **[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA)** | Vascular leakage; non-perfusion areas; disc leakage; macular edema | Useful in posterior scleritis and to assess vascular complications |
| **[[Indocyanine green angiography]] (ICGA)** | Choroidal vascular changes; hypofluorescent dark spots indicating choroidal granulomas | Helpful in posterior scleritis and in differentiating from choroidal tumors |
| **[[Magnetic resonance imaging]] (MRI)** | Scleral thickening and enhancement on T2-weighted and gadolinium-enhanced sequences; retrobulbar inflammation | Useful for posterior scleritis, orbital extension, and differentiating from orbital tumors |
| **[[Computed tomography]] (CT)** | Scleral calcification; thickening; orbital involvement | Less commonly used; helpful when MRI is contraindicated |
| **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | High-resolution imaging of anterior scleral thickness and ciliary body involvement | Useful for anterior scleritis and cyclitis assessment |

### Laboratory Investigations

Given the high prevalence of systemic associations, a comprehensive laboratory workup is recommended for all patients with scleritis, particularly those with bilateral, recurrent, or necrotizing disease:

| **Test** | **Purpose** |
|---|---|
| [[Complete blood count]] (CBC) with differential | Assess for leukocytosis, eosinophilia, anemia of chronic disease |
| [[Erythrocyte sedimentation rate]] (ESR) | Non-specific marker of systemic inflammation |
| [[C-reactive protein]] (CRP) | Acute-phase reactant; marker of active inflammation |
| [[Rheumatoid factor]] (RF) | Screen for [[rheumatoid arthritis]] |
| [[Anti-cyclic citrullinated peptide]] (anti-CCP) antibodies | More specific marker for [[rheumatoid arthritis]] |
| [[Antinuclear antibody]] (ANA) | Screen for [[systemic lupus erythematosus]] and other connective tissue diseases |
| [[Anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies]] (ANCA): c-ANCA ([[PR3-ANCA]]) and p-ANCA ([[MPO-ANCA]]) | Screen for [[granulomatosis with polyangiitis]], [[microscopic polyangiitis]], [[eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis]] |
| [[Uric acid]] | Screen for [[gout]] |
| [[Serum angiotensin-converting enzyme]] (ACE) and [[lysozyme]] | Screen for [[sarcoidosis]] |
| [[HLA-B27]] | Associated with [[ankylosing spondylitis]], [[reactive arthritis]] |
| [[FTA-ABS]] / [[RPR]] / [[VDRL]] | Screen for [[syphilis]] |
| [[QuantiFERON-TB Gold]] / [[tuberculin skin test]] | Screen for [[tuberculosis]] |
| [[Urinalysis]] | Assess for renal involvement in systemic vasculitis |
| [[Chest X-ray]] | Screen for [[sarcoidosis]], [[tuberculosis]], [[granulomatosis with polyangiitis]] |
| [[Serum IgG4]] | Screen for [[IgG4-related disease]] |

Additional testing (e.g., [[Lyme serology]], viral titers for [[HSV]]/[[VZV]], [[blood cultures]]) should be guided by clinical suspicion.

### Scleral Biopsy

[[Scleral biopsy]] is reserved for atypical cases, suspected infectious scleritis (particularly when cultures are needed), cases refractory to standard immunosuppressive therapy, or when malignancy is a diagnostic consideration. Histopathology may reveal granulomatous inflammation, necrotizing vasculitis, fibrinoid necrosis, or infectious organisms. Tissue should be sent for histopathology, Gram stain, culture (bacterial, fungal, mycobacterial), and special stains as indicated.

## Differential Diagnosis

It is essential to differentiate scleritis from conditions that may mimic its presentation:

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| **[[Episcleritis]]** | Mild discomfort (not severe pain); bright red sectoral injection; vessels blanch with phenylephrine; self-limiting; no scleral edema |
| **[[Orbital inflammatory disease]]** ([[orbital pseudotumor]]) | Proptosis, motility restriction, orbital pain; may coexist with posterior scleritis |
| **[[Orbital cellulitis]]** | Fever, proptosis, restricted motility, eyelid edema; infectious etiology |
| **[[Conjunctivitis]]** | Discharge, conjunctival injection without deep scleral involvement |
| **[[Anterior uveitis]]** | Primarily intraocular inflammation; may coexist with scleritis |
| **Ocular surface [[lymphoma]] or [[carcinoma]]** | Salmon-colored patch (lymphoma); may mimic nodular scleritis; biopsy diagnostic |

## Management and Treatment

### General Principles

Management of scleritis is guided by the **type and severity** of inflammation, the presence or absence of systemic disease, and the response to initial therapy. Treatment goals include:

1. Control of ocular inflammation and pain
2. Prevention of structural scleral damage and sight-threatening complications
3. Identification and treatment of underlying systemic disease
4. Minimization of treatment-related side effects

A **multidisciplinary approach** involving the optometrist or ophthalmologist, rheumatologist, and internist is often essential, particularly in cases associated with systemic vasculitis.

### Pharmacological Treatment

#### Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs)

Oral [[NSAIDs]] are the first-line treatment for **diffuse and nodular anterior scleritis** without necrotizing features or vision-threatening complications:

- **[[Indomethacin]]**: 25–50 mg three times daily (most commonly used)
- **[[Naproxen]]**: 250–500 mg twice daily
- **[[Ibuprofen]]**: 400–600 mg three to four times daily
- **[[Flurbiprofen]]**: 100 mg two to three times daily
- **[[Celecoxib]]**: 200 mg twice daily (COX-2 selective; may be preferred in patients with gastrointestinal risk factors)

NSAIDs should be taken with food or a [[proton pump inhibitor]] to reduce gastrointestinal side effects. Response is typically assessed at 1–2 weeks; if inadequate, escalation to corticosteroids is warranted.

> **Note**: Topical NSAIDs and topical corticosteroids alone are **insufficient** for treating scleritis, as they do not adequately penetrate the scleral stroma. However, topical [[prednisolone acetate]] 1% or [[difluprednate]] 0.05% may be used as adjunctive therapy for concurrent [[anterior uveitis]] or surface inflammation.

#### Corticosteroids

Systemic [[corticosteroids]] are indicated when NSAIDs fail, in moderate-to-severe non-necrotizing scleritis, and as initial therapy in necrotizing scleritis or posterior scleritis:

- **Oral [[prednisone]]**: 1 mg/kg/day (typically 40–80 mg/day), tapered gradually over weeks to months based on clinical response
- **Intravenous [[methylprednisolone]]**: 500–1000 mg/day for 3 days ("pulse therapy") for severe or sight-threatening scleritis, followed by oral prednisone taper
- **Sub-Tenon's (periocular) [[triamcinolone acetonide]]** injection: 20–40 mg; may be used as adjunctive therapy in non-necrotizing scleritis. **Contraindicated in necrotizing scleritis** due to risk of scleral perforation.

Long-term systemic corticosteroid use carries significant risks including [[osteoporosis]], [[diabetes mellitus]], [[hypertension]], [[adrenal suppression]], [[peptic ulcer disease]], [[cataracts]], and [[glaucoma]]. Therefore, a steroid-sparing immunosuppressive agent should be introduced early if the prednisone dose cannot be tapered below 7.5–10 mg/day within 2–3 months.

#### Immunosuppressive (Steroid-Sparing) Agents

Immunosuppressive agents are indicated for:
- Necrotizing scleritis (often first-line alongside corticosteroids)
- Scleritis refractory to NSAIDs and corticosteroids
- Scleritis requiring prolonged corticosteroid therapy
- Scleritis associated with systemic autoimmune disease requiring systemic immunosuppression

| **Agent** | **Class** | **Typical Dose** | **Key Considerations** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Methotrexate]]** | Antimetabolite | 7.5–25 mg/week (oral or subcutaneous) | Most commonly used first-line steroid-sparing agent; requires folic acid supplementation; monitor CBC, LFTs, renal function |
| **[[Mycophenolate mofetil]]** | Antimetabolite | 1000–1500 mg twice daily | Alternative to methotrexate; well-tolerated; monitor CBC |
| **[[Azathioprine]]** | Antimetabolite | 1–3 mg/kg/day | Check [[TPMT]] enzyme level before initiation; monitor CBC, LFTs |
| **[[Cyclophosphamide]]** | Alkylating agent | Oral: 1–2 mg/kg/day; IV pulse: 500–1000 mg/m² monthly | Reserved for severe necrotizing scleritis and systemic vasculitis (e.g., [[GPA]]); significant toxicity (hemorrhagic cystitis, bone marrow suppression, malignancy risk) |
| **[[Cyclosporine A]]** | Calcineurin inhibitor | 2–5 mg/kg/day | Monitor renal function and blood pressure; useful in refractory cases |
| **[[Tacrolimus]]** | Calcineurin inhibitor | 0.05–0.1 mg/kg/day | Alternative to cyclosporine |
| **[[Leflunomide]]** | Pyrimidine synthesis inhibitor | 10–20 mg/day | Used in RA-associated scleritis |
| **[[Dapsone]]** | Sulfone | 50–100 mg/day | Occasionally used; check [[G6PD]] before initiation |

#### Biologic Agents

Biologic agents have revolutionized the management of refractory and severe scleritis, particularly in cases associated with systemic autoimmune disease:

| **Agent** | **Target** | **Dose/Route** | **Evidence/Notes** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Infliximab]]** | [[TNF-α]] (chimeric monoclonal antibody) | 3–5 mg/kg IV at weeks 0, 2, 6, then every 4–8 weeks | Strong evidence for refractory scleritis; rapid onset of action |
| **[[Adalimumab]]** | [[TNF-α]] (fully human monoclonal antibody) | 40 mg subcutaneous every 2 weeks | Effective in refractory non-necrotizing and necrotizing scleritis |
| **[[Rituximab]]** | [[CD20]] (B-cell depletion) | 375 mg/m² IV weekly × 4, or 1000 mg IV × 2 doses (days 0 and 14) | Particularly effective in [[GPA]]-associated and refractory necrotizing scleritis; increasingly used as first-line for GPA |
| **[[Tocilizumab]]** | [[IL-6 receptor]] | 4–8 mg/kg IV monthly, or 162 mg subcutaneous weekly/biweekly | Emerging evidence for refractory scleritis |
| **[[Secukinumab]]** | [[IL-17A]] | 150–300 mg subcutaneous | Case reports in refractory scleritis |
| **[[Abatacept]]** | [[T-cell co-stimulation]] (CTLA-4-Ig) | 500–1000 mg IV monthly, or 125 mg subcutaneous weekly | Used in RA-associated refractory scleritis |
| **[[Tofacitinib]]** | [[JAK inhibitor]] | 5–11 mg twice daily (oral) | Emerging evidence; oral small molecule; used in refractory cases |

### Treatment of Infectious Scleritis

Infectious scleritis requires **targeted antimicrobial therapy** based on culture and sensitivity results, in addition to possible surgical debridement. Empiric broad-spectrum coverage (e.g., fortified topical [[vancomycin]] and [[ceftazidime]] or a [[fluoroquinolone]]) may be initiated while awaiting culture results. **Immunosuppressive therapy must be withheld or used with extreme caution** in infectious scleritis, as it may worsen the infection. Specific treatment depends on the causative organism:

- **Bacterial**: Targeted systemic and topical antibiotics
- **Viral (HSV/VZV)**: Systemic [[acyclovir]] (800 mg five times daily) or [[valacyclovir]] (1000 mg three times daily)
- **Fungal**: Systemic and topical [[voriconazole]] or [[natamycin]]
- **Mycobacterial**: Multi-drug anti-tubercular therapy
- **Acanthamoeba**: Topical [[polyhexamethylene biguanide]] (PHMB) and [[propamidine isethionate]] (Brolene)

### Surgical Management

Surgical intervention in scleritis is reserved for specific indications:

1. **Scleral patch grafting**: For impending or actual scleral perforation. Donor materials include preserved [[sclera]], [[pericardium]], [[fascia lata]], or [[corneal tissue]].
2. **Scleral reinforcement**: For progressive scleral thinning with [[staphyloma]] formation.
3. **Debridement**: In infectious scleritis, surgical debridement of necrotic tissue may be necessary to improve antimicrobial penetration and remove the infectious nidus.
4. **[[Cataract surgery]]**: For visually significant cataract secondary to chronic inflammation or corticosteroid use; should be performed only when inflammation is well controlled for a minimum of 3 months.
5. **[[Glaucoma surgery]]**: For medically uncontrolled secondary glaucoma; caution is required due to the risk of wound healing complications in inflamed tissue.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of scleritis varies significantly by subtype:

- **Diffuse anterior scleritis**: Generally favorable with appropriate treatment; recurrence rate approximately 35–50%
- **Nodular anterior scleritis**: Intermediate prognosis; may progress to necrotizing disease in a minority of cases
- **Necrotizing scleritis with inflammation**: Poorest ocular prognosis; significant risk of vision loss (up to 40–80% experience decreased visual acuity); highest association with systemic morbidity and mortality
- **Scleromalacia perforans**: Chronic, progressive; risk of perforation; reflects severe underlying systemic disease
- **Posterior scleritis**: Variable; generally good if diagnosed and treated promptly, but may cause significant vision loss from retinal detachment or optic neuropathy if delayed

Overall, approximately 15–25% of patients with scleritis will experience significant visual loss (defined as ≥2 lines of [[Snellen acuity]]). Early, aggressive treatment—particularly with immunosuppressive and biologic agents in severe cases—has significantly improved outcomes compared to historical data.

---

## Monitoring and Follow-Up

Patients with scleritis require close, regular follow-up:

- **Active disease**: Every 1–4 weeks, depending on severity and treatment
- **Stable/remission**: Every 1–3 months, with gradual extension of intervals
- **Monitoring parameters**: Visual acuity, [[intraocular pressure]], slit-lamp examination (scleral inflammation grading, complications), dilated fundus examination, OCT (for macular edema, choroidal changes), and laboratory monitoring for immunosuppressive drug toxicity (CBC, hepatic and renal function panels, drug levels as appropriate)

Patients on systemic immunosuppression require coordinated care with a rheumatologist or internist for monitoring of systemic side effects, infection risk, and disease activity.

## Special Considerations

### Surgically Induced Necrotizing Scleritis (SINS)

[[Surgically induced necrotizing scleritis]] (SINS) is a rare but devastating complication occurring at the site of prior ocular surgery, most commonly after [[cataract surgery]], [[pterygium excision]], [[strabismus surgery]], or [[retinal detachment repair]]. It typically presents weeks to years after surgery and is strongly associated with underlying autoimmune disease. Management follows the same principles as non-surgical necrotizing scleritis, with aggressive immunosuppression and possible scleral grafting.

### Scleritis in Pediatric Patients

Scleritis is rare in children but may occur in the setting of [[juvenile idiopathic arthritis]] (JIA) or other pediatric autoimmune conditions. Diagnosis may be delayed due to atypical presentations and difficulty in examination. Management principles are similar to adults, with attention to growth and developmental considerations when using systemic immunosuppression.

### Scleritis and Pregnancy

Management of scleritis during pregnancy requires careful consideration of teratogenic risks. [[Methotrexate]], [[mycophenolate mofetil]], and [[cyclophosphamide]] are **contraindicated** in pregnancy. [[Azathioprine]], [[cyclosporine]], and [[corticosteroids]] are generally considered safer options. Biologic agents should be used with caution and in consultation with maternal-fetal medicine specialists.

---

## Summary

Scleritis is a serious, potentially sight-threatening and life-threatening inflammatory condition of the [[sclera]] that requires prompt diagnosis, thorough systemic evaluation, and aggressive, often long-term immunosuppressive therapy. The optometrist plays a critical role in early recognition, appropriate referral, co-management, and long-term monitoring of these patients. A high index of suspicion for underlying systemic disease—particularly [[rheumatoid arthritis]], [[granulomatosis with polyangiitis]], and other systemic vasculitides—is essential, as scleritis may be the presenting manifestation of a potentially fatal systemic condition.
