# Pars Plana

---

The **pars plana** is a critical anatomical region of the [[uveal tract]], forming the posterior portion of the [[ciliary body]]. Situated between the [[pars plicata]] anteriorly and the [[ora serrata]] posteriorly, the pars plana serves as one of the most clinically significant landmarks in [[ophthalmology]] and [[optometry]]. Its relatively avascular nature and strategic location make it the preferred surgical entry site for numerous intraocular procedures, collectively termed **[[pars plana vitrectomy]]** (PPV) and **pars plana approaches**. Beyond its surgical importance, the pars plana is the site of a specific inflammatory condition known as **[[pars planitis]]**, a subset of [[intermediate uveitis]], which carries significant visual morbidity if left untreated. This article provides an exhaustive clinical review of the pars plana, encompassing its anatomy, histology, embryology, pathophysiology of associated diseases, clinical presentation, diagnostic modalities, and comprehensive management strategies.

---

## Gross Anatomy

The pars plana (Latin: "flat part") is the flattened, posterior zone of the [[ciliary body]]. It extends approximately **4 mm** in length and lies between two well-defined anatomical boundaries:

- **Anteriorly:** The [[pars plicata]] (also known as the corona ciliaris), which contains the [[ciliary processes]] responsible for [[aqueous humor]] production.
- **Posteriorly:** The [[ora serrata]], the scalloped junction between the [[retina]] and the ciliary body, marking the anterior termination of the sensory retina.

The pars plana is located approximately **3.0–4.0 mm posterior to the [[limbus]]** on the temporal side and **3.0–3.5 mm posterior to the limbus** on the nasal side. This slight asymmetry is clinically important when planning surgical entry sites. In phakic eyes, the standard entry point for [[pars plana vitrectomy]] is typically **3.5–4.0 mm** posterior to the limbus to avoid the [[crystalline lens]], while in pseudophakic or aphakic eyes, entry can be made at **3.0–3.5 mm** posterior to the limbus.

| **Anatomical Parameter** | **Measurement** |
|---|---|
| Width of pars plana (temporal) | ~4.0–4.5 mm |
| Width of pars plana (nasal) | ~3.5–4.0 mm |
| Distance from limbus to ora serrata (temporal) | ~6.5–7.0 mm |
| Distance from limbus to ora serrata (nasal) | ~5.5–6.0 mm |
| Standard PPV entry (phakic eye) | 3.5–4.0 mm from limbus |
| Standard PPV entry (pseudophakic/aphakic eye) | 3.0–3.5 mm from limbus |

The pars plana is covered externally by the [[sclera]] and internally faces the [[vitreous body]]. The [[vitreous base]], a zone of particularly strong vitreoretinal adhesion, straddles the ora serrata and extends approximately 2 mm anterior and 3 mm posterior to it, overlapping the posterior aspect of the pars plana. This anatomical relationship is of paramount importance in understanding the pathogenesis of [[retinal tears]] and [[retinal detachment]] associated with [[posterior vitreous detachment]].

## Histology and Microanatomy

Histologically, the pars plana consists of two epithelial layers overlying a stroma:

### Epithelial Layers

1. **Non-pigmented ciliary epithelium (NPCE):** The inner layer, facing the [[vitreous cavity]]. These cuboidal cells are continuous with the [[neurosensory retina]] at the ora serrata. The NPCE of the pars plana is relatively flat and lacks the extensive infoldings seen in the pars plicata, reflecting its minimal role in active [[aqueous humor]] secretion.

2. **Pigmented ciliary epithelium (PCE):** The outer layer, resting on the [[basement membrane]] adjacent to the ciliary stroma. These cells are heavily melanin-laden and are continuous with the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE) posteriorly. The PCE provides structural support and contributes to the [[blood-aqueous barrier]].

The apices of the NPCE and PCE face each other, a unique arrangement resulting from the invagination of the [[optic cup]] during [[embryological development]]. The intercellular junctions between these layers, particularly [[tight junctions]] (zonulae occludentes) between NPCE cells, form a critical component of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]].

### Stroma

The underlying stroma of the pars plana is composed of loose [[connective tissue]], [[collagen fibers]], [[fibroblasts]], [[melanocytes]], and a sparse vascular network. Compared to the pars plicata, the pars plana stroma is notably thinner and less vascular, which is precisely why it is favored as a surgical entry point — incisions through this region carry a lower risk of significant [[hemorrhage]] and damage to critical intraocular structures.

### Vitreous Attachments

The [[vitreous base]] has its strongest attachment at the pars plana and the immediately adjacent ora serrata. [[Collagen fibrils]] of the [[vitreous cortex]] insert directly into the basement membrane of the NPCE in this region. These attachments are so robust that they are virtually impossible to separate surgically without causing tissue disruption, which is relevant in the pathogenesis of [[giant retinal tears]] and anterior [[proliferative vitreoretinopathy]] (PVR).

## Embryology

The pars plana develops from the **anterior portion of the [[optic cup]]** during [[embryogenesis]]. The inner layer of the optic cup differentiates into the NPCE, while the outer layer becomes the PCE. This process occurs during the **third to fourth month of gestation** in humans. The differentiation of the ciliary body, including the distinction between pars plicata and pars plana, is influenced by signaling from the surrounding [[mesenchyme]] and involves key developmental pathways including [[Wnt signaling]], [[BMP signaling]], and [[FGF signaling]].

Congenital anomalies of the pars plana are rare but may include:

- **[[Ciliary body coloboma]]:** A developmental defect resulting from incomplete closure of the [[embryonic fissure]], which may involve the pars plana.
- **Cysts of the pars plana:** These are relatively common, often incidental findings, particularly in older adults. They arise from the epithelial layers and are typically benign.

---

## Physiology and Function

While the pars plana is often described as a relatively "quiescent" zone compared to the pars plicata, it does serve several physiological roles:

1. **Contribution to the [[blood-aqueous barrier]]:** The tight junctions of the NPCE in the pars plana region help maintain the immunological privilege of the [[anterior segment]] and [[vitreous cavity]].

2. **Structural continuity:** The pars plana provides a seamless anatomical transition between the metabolically active pars plicata and the neurosensory retina, ensuring structural integrity of the [[globe]].

3. **Vitreous support:** The firm attachment of the vitreous base to the pars plana provides mechanical stability to the [[vitreous gel]], anchoring it within the posterior segment.

4. **Minor role in aqueous dynamics:** Although the pars plicata is the primary site of [[aqueous humor]] production, the pars plana epithelium may contribute marginally to fluid transport across the ciliary body, particularly in the context of the [[uveoscleral outflow]] pathway.

## Pathophysiology of Pars Plana–Related Conditions

### Pars Planitis (Intermediate Uveitis)

**[[Pars planitis]]** is the most clinically significant pathological condition directly involving the pars plana. It is classified as a specific form of **[[intermediate uveitis]]** by the [[Standardization of Uveitis Nomenclature]] (SUN) Working Group. Pars planitis is defined as intermediate uveitis characterized by the presence of **snowbank formation** and/or **snowball opacities** in the absence of an identifiable infectious or systemic etiology — i.e., it is an **idiopathic** condition.

#### Immunopathogenesis

The pathophysiology of pars planitis is believed to involve a **T-cell–mediated autoimmune process** targeting the pars plana region. Key immunological features include:

- **[[CD4+ T helper cells]]** and **[[CD8+ cytotoxic T cells]]** infiltrate the pars plana and vitreous base.
- **[[Cytokines]]** such as [[interleukin-6]] (IL-6), [[interleukin-17]] (IL-17), [[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] (TNF-α), and [[interferon-gamma]] (IFN-γ) are elevated in the vitreous and aqueous humor.
- **Snowbanks** are composed of fibrovascular tissue, [[glial cells]], collapsed vitreous collagen, and inflammatory cells overlying the pars plana and inferior ora serrata. Histopathological studies have demonstrated the presence of [[type II collagen]] within snowbanks, suggesting a possible autoimmune cross-reactivity with vitreous collagen.
- **Snowball opacities** are aggregates of inflammatory cells (predominantly [[macrophages]] and [[epithelioid cells]]) suspended in the inferior vitreous.
- There is an association with **[[HLA-DR15]]** and **[[HLA-DR17]]** haplotypes, supporting a genetic predisposition.
- Breakdown of the [[blood-retinal barrier]] and [[blood-aqueous barrier]] leads to protein and cellular leakage into the vitreous cavity.

#### Vascular Changes

Chronic inflammation in pars planitis leads to:

- **[[Retinal vasculitis]]**, particularly involving peripheral retinal veins ([[periphlebitis]]).
- **[[Cystoid macular edema]]** (CME), the most common cause of visual loss in pars planitis, resulting from breakdown of the perifoveal capillary [[blood-retinal barrier]].
- **[[Neovascularization]]** of the optic disc or retina in severe, chronic cases, driven by [[vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF) release secondary to retinal ischemia.

### Pars Plana Cysts

**[[Pars plana cysts]]** (also called ciliary body cysts) are fluid-filled spaces arising from separation of the pigmented and non-pigmented epithelial layers. They are:

- Extremely common, found in up to **30–50%** of autopsy eyes.
- Usually asymptomatic and discovered incidentally on [[ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM) or [[anterior segment OCT]].
- Rarely, large cysts can cause [[angle closure]] by pushing the [[iris]] forward or can be confused with [[ciliary body melanoma]] on imaging.

### Pars Plana in Neoplasia

Although rare, the pars plana can be involved in:

- **[[Ciliary body melanoma]]:** Tumors arising from the [[uveal melanocytes]] within the ciliary body stroma may extend into or originate from the pars plana region.
- **[[Medulloepithelioma]]:** A rare embryonal tumor of the NPCE that can arise from the pars plana, more commonly seen in children.
- **[[Metastatic disease]]:** Hematogenous metastases to the ciliary body, including the pars plana, have been reported, most commonly from [[breast carcinoma]] and [[lung carcinoma]].

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms of Pars Planitis

Pars planitis typically presents in **children and young adults** (peak onset between ages 5 and 30), with a slight male predominance. Symptoms include:

| **Symptom** | **Frequency** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Floaters]] | Very common (>80%) | Vitreous inflammatory cells and debris |
| Blurred vision | Common (60–80%) | [[Cystoid macular edema]], vitreous haze |
| Decreased visual acuity | Variable | CME, [[epiretinal membrane]], [[cataract]] |
| [[Photophobia]] | Mild to moderate | Anterior segment spillover inflammation |
| Pain | Rare | Typically absent unless secondary [[glaucoma]] develops |
| Redness | Mild or absent | Minimal anterior segment involvement |

A hallmark of pars planitis is the **relative paucity of anterior segment signs** compared to the degree of vitreous inflammation. Patients may have minimal or no [[conjunctival injection]], mild [[anterior chamber cells]], and no [[keratic precipitates]] (or only fine, non-granulomatous KPs), yet demonstrate significant vitreous cellular infiltration.

### Bilateral Involvement

Pars planitis is **bilateral in approximately 70–80%** of cases, although the severity may be markedly asymmetric between the two eyes.

### Complications

Chronic or recurrent pars planitis can lead to sight-threatening complications:

| **Complication** | **Prevalence** | **Notes** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Cystoid macular edema]] | 30–50% | Leading cause of visual morbidity |
| [[Posterior subcapsular cataract]] | 30–50% | Due to chronic inflammation and/or corticosteroid use |
| [[Epiretinal membrane]] | 10–30% | Causes metamorphopsia and reduced acuity |
| [[Vitreous hemorrhage]] | 5–15% | From neovascularization or retinal vasculitis |
| [[Retinal detachment]] | 5–10% | Tractional or rhegmatogenous |
| [[Secondary glaucoma]] | 5–15% | Inflammatory, steroid-induced, or neovascular |
| [[Optic disc edema]] | 10–20% | May indicate severe inflammation |
| [[Band keratopathy]] | 5–10% | Chronic anterior segment inflammation |
| [[Cyclitic membrane]] | Rare | Severe, chronic cases; can cause [[hypotony]] |

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical Examination

A thorough ophthalmic examination is essential:

1. **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]:** Assess for anterior chamber cells and flare, [[keratic precipitates]], [[posterior synechiae]], and lens opacities. In pars planitis, anterior segment findings are typically mild.

2. **[[Dilated fundus examination]]:** Using [[indirect ophthalmoscopy]] and [[scleral depression]], the examiner should carefully evaluate:
   - **Snowbanks:** White-yellow fibrovascular exudates overlying the pars plana and inferior ora serrata. Best visualized with scleral depression.
   - **Snowball opacities:** White, round, fluffy aggregates in the inferior vitreous, often described as resembling "string of pearls" when aligned along the inferior vitreous framework.
   - **Vitreous haze:** Graded using the [[National Eye Institute]] (NEI) / SUN vitreous haze grading scale (0 to 4+).
   - **Peripheral retinal vasculitis:** Perivascular sheathing, particularly of veins.
   - **Macular changes:** CME, epiretinal membrane formation.

3. **[[Goldmann applanation tonometry]]:** To monitor [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP), which may be elevated (steroid response, inflammatory glaucoma) or low ([[hypotony]] from ciliary body shutdown).

### Imaging Studies

| **Modality** | **Findings in Pars Planitis** | **Clinical Utility** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)** | CME (cystoid spaces in outer plexiform and inner nuclear layers), epiretinal membrane, subretinal fluid | Gold standard for monitoring macular edema; quantitative measurement of [[central macular thickness]] |
| **[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA)** | Petaloid pattern of CME, disc leakage, peripheral vascular leakage, retinal neovascularization, capillary non-perfusion | Essential for assessing vascular complications and guiding treatment |
| **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | Snowbanks, cyclitic membranes, pars plana cysts, ciliary body thickening | Visualizes structures behind the iris not accessible to standard imaging |
| **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** | Vitreous opacities, snowbanks, retinal detachment, vitreous membranes | Useful when media opacity precludes fundoscopy |
| **[[OCT angiography]] (OCTA)** | Perifoveal capillary changes, macular ischemia, neovascularization | Emerging non-invasive modality for vascular assessment |
| **[[Anterior segment OCT]]** | Pars plana cysts, anterior chamber depth, angle assessment | Adjunct to UBM for anterior segment evaluation |

### Laboratory Investigations

Since pars planitis is a diagnosis of exclusion (idiopathic intermediate uveitis), a targeted laboratory workup is essential to rule out infectious and systemic inflammatory etiologies that can mimic intermediate uveitis:

| **Test** | **Condition Excluded** |
|---|---|
| [[Chest X-ray]] or [[CT chest]] | [[Sarcoidosis]], [[tuberculosis]] |
| [[Angiotensin-converting enzyme]] (ACE) and [[lysozyme]] | [[Sarcoidosis]] |
| [[QuantiFERON-TB Gold]] / [[Tuberculin skin test]] | [[Tuberculosis]] |
| [[FTA-ABS]] / [[RPR]] / [[VDRL]] | [[Syphilis]] |
| [[Complete blood count]] (CBC) | [[Lymphoma]], [[leukemia]] |
| [[HLA-B27]] | [[Ankylosing spondylitis]], [[reactive arthritis]] |
| [[MRI brain]] with gadolinium | [[Multiple sclerosis]] (strongly associated with intermediate uveitis) |
| [[Lyme serology]] | [[Lyme disease]] |
| [[Toxocara serology]] | [[Ocular toxocariasis]] |
| [[HIV testing]] | [[HIV-associated uveitis]] |
| [[Serum calcium]] | [[Sarcoidosis]] |

If all investigations are negative and the clinical picture is consistent, the diagnosis of **pars planitis** (idiopathic intermediate uveitis with snowbanks/snowballs) is established.

## Differential Diagnosis

Conditions that may mimic pars planitis or involve the pars plana region include:

- **[[Sarcoidosis]]** — Can cause intermediate uveitis with snowball opacities; systemic workup differentiates.
- **[[Multiple sclerosis]]** — Associated with intermediate uveitis; MRI brain is essential.
- **[[Lyme disease]]** — Endemic areas; serological testing required.
- **[[Syphilis]]** — The "great masquerader"; serological screening mandatory.
- **[[Tuberculosis]]** — Particularly in endemic regions.
- **[[Intraocular lymphoma]]** (primary vitreoretinal lymphoma) — Must be considered in patients >50 years with vitreous cells; diagnostic vitrectomy with [[cytology]] and [[IL-10/IL-6 ratio]] may be required.
- **[[Ocular toxocariasis]]** — Especially in children; peripheral granuloma may mimic snowbank.
- **[[Retinitis pigmentosa]]** — Can have vitreous cells; distinguished by ERG and fundus appearance.

## Management and Treatment

### Treatment Philosophy

Treatment of pars planitis is guided by the **severity of inflammation**, the **presence of complications** (particularly CME), and the **visual acuity**. The general approach follows a **stepladder strategy**:

1. **Observation** for mild cases without CME and with good visual acuity (≥20/40).
2. **Corticosteroids** (periocular, intravitreal, or systemic) as first-line therapy.
3. **Immunomodulatory therapy** (steroid-sparing agents) for chronic, recurrent, or steroid-dependent disease.
4. **Biologic agents** for refractory cases.
5. **Surgical intervention** for complications or refractory inflammation.

### Pharmacological Interventions

#### Corticosteroids

| **Route** | **Agent** | **Dose/Regimen** | **Notes** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Periocular (sub-Tenon's) | [[Triamcinolone acetonide]] | 40 mg/mL, 0.5–1.0 mL injection | First-line for unilateral or asymmetric disease; effective for CME; risk of IOP elevation and cataract |
| Intravitreal | [[Triamcinolone acetonide]] | 2–4 mg in 0.05–0.1 mL | Potent anti-inflammatory; higher risk of IOP elevation and cataract than periocular |
| Intravitreal implant | [[Dexamethasone intravitreal implant]] (Ozurdex®) | 0.7 mg sustained-release | Duration ~3–6 months; effective for CME; favorable safety profile |
| Intravitreal implant | [[Fluocinolone acetonide implant]] (Retisert®) | 0.59 mg sustained-release | Duration ~30 months; highly effective but significant risk of IOP elevation (>60%) and cataract (~100%) |
| Intravitreal implant | [[Fluocinolone acetonide insert]] (Yutiq®) | 0.18 mg sustained-release | Duration ~36 months; lower steroid load than Retisert; lower but still significant IOP risk |
| Systemic oral | [[Prednisone]] | 1 mg/kg/day, tapered over weeks to months | Reserved for bilateral severe disease; significant systemic side effects with prolonged use |
| Topical | [[Prednisolone acetate 1%]] | 4–8 times daily | Primarily for anterior segment spillover; limited penetration to posterior segment |

#### Immunomodulatory Therapy (Steroid-Sparing Agents)

Indicated when corticosteroids cannot be tapered below a threshold dose (typically **≤7.5 mg/day prednisone equivalent**) without recurrence, or when steroid side effects are unacceptable.

| **Drug Class** | **Agent** | **Dose** | **Monitoring** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Antimetabolites]] | [[Methotrexate]] | 7.5–25 mg/week (oral or subcutaneous) | CBC, LFTs, renal function every 4–8 weeks; folic acid supplementation |
| Antimetabolites | [[Mycophenolate mofetil]] | 1–3 g/day in divided doses | CBC, LFTs every 4–8 weeks |
| Antimetabolites | [[Azathioprine]] | 1–3 mg/kg/day | [[TPMT]] genotype/phenotype before initiation; CBC, LFTs |
| [[Calcineurin inhibitors]] | [[Cyclosporine A]] | 2.5–5 mg/kg/day | Blood pressure, renal function, drug levels |
| Calcineurin inhibitors | [[Tacrolimus]] | 0.05–0.15 mg/kg/day | Renal function, drug levels, glucose |

#### Biologic Agents

For refractory pars planitis unresponsive to conventional immunomodulatory therapy:

| **Agent** | **Mechanism** | **Route/Dose** | **Evidence** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Adalimumab]] (Humira®) | Anti-[[TNF-α]] monoclonal antibody | 40 mg subcutaneous every 2 weeks | FDA-approved for non-infectious intermediate, posterior, and panuveitis; VISUAL I and VISUAL II trials demonstrated efficacy |
| [[Infliximab]] | Anti-TNF-α chimeric monoclonal antibody | 5–10 mg/kg IV every 4–8 weeks | Off-label; effective in refractory cases |
| [[Rituximab]] | Anti-[[CD20]] monoclonal antibody | 1000 mg IV × 2 doses, 2 weeks apart | Off-label; case series support use in refractory intermediate uveitis |
| [[Tocilizumab]] | Anti-[[IL-6 receptor]] monoclonal antibody | 162 mg subcutaneous weekly or 8 mg/kg IV monthly | Emerging evidence for refractory uveitic CME |
| [[Interferon-alpha]] | Immunomodulatory cytokine | 3–6 million IU subcutaneous daily, tapered | European experience; effective but significant side effects |

### Cryotherapy and Laser Photocoagulation

**[[Peripheral retinal cryotherapy]]** and **[[peripheral scatter laser photocoagulation]]** have been used as adjunctive treatments for pars planitis, particularly in cases with:

- Persistent snowbanking despite medical therapy.
- Peripheral retinal neovascularization.
- Vitreous hemorrhage from neovascularization.

The proposed mechanism involves **ablation of the peripheral retinal tissue** overlying the inflamed pars plana, reducing the antigenic stimulus and the production of inflammatory mediators and [[VEGF]]. Cryotherapy is applied to the snowbank region in a double freeze-thaw technique. Peripheral laser photocoagulation achieves similar goals with potentially less inflammation.

### Surgical Interventions

#### Pars Plana Vitrectomy (PPV)

**[[Pars plana vitrectomy]]** is both a therapeutic and diagnostic procedure in the management of pars planitis and its complications:

**Indications for PPV in pars planitis:**
- Visually significant vitreous opacification unresponsive to medical therapy.
- Non-clearing [[vitreous hemorrhage]].
- [[Epiretinal membrane]] causing significant visual disturbance.
- [[Tractional retinal detachment]] or combined tractional-rhegmatogenous detachment.
- Diagnostic vitreous biopsy to exclude [[intraocular lymphoma]] or infection.
- Refractory CME (debulking of inflammatory mediators).

**Surgical technique considerations:**
- Modern **small-gauge vitrectomy** (23-gauge, 25-gauge, or 27-gauge) systems are preferred, offering faster wound healing and reduced postoperative inflammation.
- Entry sites are placed through the pars plana at the appropriate distance from the limbus (see anatomy section above).
- Core vitrectomy with removal of vitreous opacities, snowballs, and membranes.
- [[Endolaser photocoagulation]] to areas of peripheral neovascularization or ischemia.
- Peeling of [[epiretinal membrane]] and/or [[internal limiting membrane]] (ILM) if indicated.
- Intravitreal corticosteroid or sustained-release implant may be placed at the conclusion of surgery.

#### Cataract Surgery

**[[Posterior subcapsular cataract]]** is a common complication of both chronic pars planitis and prolonged corticosteroid use. [[Phacoemulsification]] with [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) implantation can be performed, ideally when inflammation has been quiescent for at least **3 months**. Perioperative intensification of anti-inflammatory therapy is essential to prevent postoperative flare.

#### Glaucoma Surgery

**[[Secondary glaucoma]]** in pars planitis may require:
- **[[Glaucoma drainage device]]** (e.g., [[Ahmed valve]], [[Baerveldt implant]]) — preferred over [[trabeculectomy]] in uveitic glaucoma due to lower failure rates from scarring.
- **[[Cyclodestructive procedures]]** (e.g., [[transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]], [[endocyclophotocoagulation]]) — reserved for refractory cases.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of pars planitis is generally **favorable** with appropriate management, though it is a chronic condition often requiring years of treatment. Key prognostic factors include:

- **Age of onset:** Younger patients (children) may have a more aggressive course.
- **Presence of CME:** The single most important predictor of visual outcome; chronic CME leads to permanent photoreceptor damage.
- **Snowbank formation:** Associated with more severe disease and higher complication rates compared to snowball-only disease.
- **Bilateral involvement:** More common and may indicate a more systemic immunological process.
- **Response to initial therapy:** Patients who respond well to periocular corticosteroids tend to have a better long-term prognosis.

Studies report that approximately **75–85%** of patients with pars planitis maintain visual acuity of **20/40 or better** with appropriate treatment over long-term follow-up.

## Pars Plana as a Surgical Landmark

Beyond pars planitis, the pars plana is of immense importance as the **standard surgical entry site** for a wide range of posterior segment procedures:

| **Procedure** | **Indication** |
|---|---|
| [[Pars plana vitrectomy]] | [[Retinal detachment]], [[vitreous hemorrhage]], [[macular hole]], [[epiretinal membrane]], [[endophthalmitis]], [[intraocular foreign body]] |
| [[Pars plana lensectomy]] | Pediatric [[cataract]], dislocated [[crystalline lens]] or [[IOL]] |
| [[Intravitreal injections]] | [[Anti-VEGF therapy]], intravitreal corticosteroids, intravitreal antibiotics |
| [[Pars plana glaucoma drainage device]] placement | [[Refractory glaucoma]] with tube placed through pars plana into vitreous cavity |
| [[Pars plana anterior vitrectomy]] | Vitreous loss during complicated [[cataract surgery]] |

The safety of pars plana entry relies on its **relative avascularity**, the **absence of retinal tissue** (the retina terminates at the ora serrata), and the **distance from the [[crystalline lens]]** when appropriate measurements are used.

---

## Special Considerations in Pediatric Patients

Pars planitis in children deserves special mention:

- Children may present late due to inability to articulate visual symptoms.
- **[[Amblyopia]]** is a significant risk in unilateral or asymmetric cases, particularly in younger children.
- Aggressive treatment is often warranted to prevent amblyopia and structural complications.
- Steroid side effects (growth retardation, adrenal suppression) must be carefully monitored.
- Early referral to a [[uveitis specialist]] and close collaboration with [[pediatric ophthalmology]] is recommended.
- The differential diagnosis in children must include **[[ocular toxocariasis]]**, **[[retinoblastoma]]** (in very young children), and **[[leukemia]]**.

---

## Summary

The pars plana is a deceptively simple anatomical structure with profound clinical significance in eye care. Its role as the preferred surgical entry site for posterior segment procedures underscores the importance of understanding its precise anatomical dimensions and relationships. Pars planitis, the inflammatory condition bearing its name, remains a challenging clinical entity requiring a systematic approach to diagnosis, exclusion of mimicking conditions, and a stepladder treatment strategy ranging from observation and corticosteroids to immunomodulatory and biologic therapies. Advances in imaging (OCT, UBM, OCTA), pharmacotherapy (sustained-release corticosteroid implants, anti-TNF biologics), and surgical techniques (small-gauge vitrectomy) have significantly improved outcomes for patients with pars plana–related pathology.
