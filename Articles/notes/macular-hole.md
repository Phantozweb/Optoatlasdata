

# Macular Hole

---

**Macular Hole** is a full-thickness defect of the [[neurosensory retina]] involving the [[fovea]], the central and most visually significant region of the [[macula]]. This condition results in significant central visual impairment, [[metamorphopsia]], and, if untreated, can lead to permanent loss of fine central [[visual acuity]]. Macular holes are most commonly idiopathic, occurring predominantly in women over the age of 60, and are strongly associated with age-related changes in the [[vitreous body]] and its interface with the [[internal limiting membrane (ILM)]] of the retina. Advances in [[optical coherence tomography (OCT)]] have revolutionized the diagnosis, staging, and management of this condition, while modern [[vitreoretinal surgery]] techniques, particularly [[pars plana vitrectomy (PPV)]] with [[ILM peeling]], have yielded anatomical closure rates exceeding 90% in most published series.

---

## Epidemiology

Macular hole is a relatively common macular pathology encountered in clinical optometric and ophthalmologic practice. Key epidemiological features include:

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Prevalence** | Approximately 0.1–0.8 per 1,000 population in large population-based studies |
| **Age of Onset** | Predominantly 55–75 years; peak incidence in the seventh decade |
| **Sex Predilection** | Female-to-male ratio approximately 2–3:1 |
| **Laterality** | Unilateral in the vast majority (~80–90%); bilateral involvement occurs in 10–20% of cases over time |
| **Risk of Fellow Eye Involvement** | Approximately 10–15% lifetime risk if the fellow eye has a complete [[posterior vitreous detachment (PVD)]]; higher if PVD is absent |

The condition is overwhelmingly idiopathic, but secondary macular holes may arise from [[ocular trauma]] (traumatic macular hole), high [[myopia]], chronic [[cystoid macular edema (CME)]], [[epiretinal membrane (ERM)]], [[macular telangiectasia type 2]], [[solar retinopathy]], and [[laser injury]]. Traumatic macular holes tend to occur in younger patients and may have a different natural history and prognosis compared to idiopathic cases.

---

## Anatomy and Relevant Structures

Understanding the anatomy of the [[macula]] and the [[vitreomacular interface]] is essential for comprehending the pathogenesis of macular hole formation.

- **[[Macula]]**: The central area of the [[retina]], approximately 5.5 mm in diameter, responsible for high-acuity central vision and color perception. It contains the highest density of [[cone photoreceptors]].
- **[[Fovea]]**: The central depression of the macula, approximately 1.5 mm in diameter, where the inner retinal layers are displaced laterally, leaving only [[cone photoreceptors]] and [[Müller cell]] processes.
- **[[Foveola]]**: The very center of the fovea, approximately 0.35 mm in diameter, representing the thinnest point of the retina and the area of highest visual acuity.
- **[[Umbo]]**: The central pit of the foveola, the anatomical point most susceptible to hole formation.
- **[[Internal Limiting Membrane (ILM)]]**: The basement membrane of the [[Müller cells]], forming the innermost layer of the retina and the boundary of the [[vitreomacular interface]].
- **[[Vitreous Body]]**: The gel-like structure filling the posterior segment of the eye. With aging, the vitreous undergoes [[syneresis]] (liquefaction) and [[synchysis]], leading to progressive separation from the retinal surface ([[posterior vitreous detachment]]).
- **[[Vitreomacular Interface]]**: The zone of adhesion between the posterior [[vitreous cortex]] and the [[ILM]]. Abnormal or incomplete separation at this interface is the primary pathogenic mechanism in idiopathic macular hole formation.

---

## Pathophysiology

### Vitreomacular Traction Theory

The prevailing and most widely accepted theory of idiopathic macular hole formation centers on abnormal [[vitreomacular traction (VMT)]]. The sequence of events is as follows:

1. **Vitreous Syneresis and Liquefaction**: With aging, the [[vitreous gel]] undergoes progressive liquefaction ([[syneresis]]), forming pockets of liquid vitreous ([[lacunae]]) within the gel structure.
2. **Incomplete Posterior Vitreous Detachment**: As the vitreous separates from the retinal surface, a focal area of persistent adhesion may remain at the [[fovea]], where the vitreous cortex is most firmly attached. This creates a state of [[vitreomacular adhesion (VMA)]] that, when associated with tractional forces, becomes [[vitreomacular traction (VMT)]].
3. **Anteroposterior and Tangential Traction**: The persistent vitreous attachment exerts anteroposterior (front-to-back) traction on the fovea. Additionally, contraction of the residual [[posterior hyaloid face]] or associated [[epiretinal membrane]] may exert tangential (lateral) traction. These combined forces distort the foveal architecture.
4. **Foveal Dehiscence**: Sustained traction leads to progressive stretching and eventual full-thickness disruption of the foveal neurosensory retina, resulting in a macular hole.
5. **Cuff of Subretinal Fluid**: Once the hole forms, fluid from the [[vitreous cavity]] gains access to the [[subretinal space]] through the defect, creating a surrounding cuff of [[subretinal fluid]] and localized [[neurosensory retinal detachment]].

### Role of Müller Cells and Glial Proliferation

[[Müller cells]], the principal glial cells of the retina, play a significant role in the pathophysiology. In the fovea, Müller cell processes form the structural framework. Tractional forces may disrupt these cells, contributing to cystoid changes and tissue loss. Following hole formation, [[glial cell]] proliferation at the edges of the hole may contribute to the characteristic elevated, rolled edges seen clinically and on [[OCT]].

### Tangential Traction and Epiretinal Membrane

In some cases, the formation or contraction of an [[epiretinal membrane (ERM)]] on the macular surface contributes tangential tractional forces that can initiate or enlarge a macular hole. This is particularly relevant in secondary macular holes.

## Classification and Staging

### Gass Classification (Historical)

The original classification by J. Donald Gass (1988, revised 1995) described the stages of idiopathic macular hole formation based on [[biomicroscopic]] findings:

| Stage | Clinical Description | Visual Acuity |
|---|---|---|
| **Stage 1A (Impending Hole – Foveal Detachment)** | Yellow spot at the fovea; loss of foveal reflex; foveal detachment due to VMT | 20/25 to 20/60 |
| **Stage 1B (Impending Hole – Foveal Dehiscence)** | Yellow ring at the fovea; occult dehiscence of the foveal floor | 20/25 to 20/80 |
| **Stage 2 (Small Full-Thickness Hole)** | Small, round or eccentric full-thickness retinal defect, ≤400 µm; may have a pseudo-operculum or attached vitreous | 20/60 to 20/200 |
| **Stage 3 (Full-Thickness Hole without PVD)** | Full-thickness hole >400 µm; posterior vitreous still attached to the optic disc; may have a pseudo-operculum suspended above the hole | 20/80 to 20/400 |
| **Stage 4 (Full-Thickness Hole with Complete PVD)** | Full-thickness hole >400 µm with complete [[posterior vitreous detachment]]; Weiss ring may be visible | 20/80 to 20/400+ |

### International Vitreomacular Traction Study (IVTS) Group Classification (2013)

The modern OCT-based classification system proposed by the IVTS Group provides a more precise anatomical framework:

| Feature | Classification |
|---|---|
| **Size (minimum hole diameter at narrowest point)** | Small: ≤250 µm; Medium: >250 to ≤400 µm; Large: >400 µm |
| **Cause** | Primary (idiopathic) vs. Secondary (traumatic, myopic, associated with other pathology) |
| **Presence of VMT** | With VMT vs. Without VMT |
| **Full-thickness vs. Lamellar** | Full-thickness macular hole (FTMH) vs. [[Lamellar macular hole (LMH)]] |

This classification is clinically important because hole size is the strongest predictor of surgical success and visual outcome.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

Patients with macular hole typically present with the following symptoms, which are usually of gradual onset in idiopathic cases:

- **Decreased Central Visual Acuity**: The hallmark symptom. Visual acuity typically ranges from 20/40 in early stages to 20/200 or worse in advanced full-thickness holes. Peripheral vision remains intact.
- **[[Metamorphopsia]]**: Distortion of central vision, where straight lines appear wavy or bent. This is often the earliest symptom noticed by the patient and can be detected with an [[Amsler grid]].
- **Central [[Scotoma]]**: A central blind spot corresponding to the area of the macular hole. Patients may describe a "missing area" in the center of their vision.
- **Difficulty with Fine Visual Tasks**: Reading, recognizing faces, and other tasks requiring high-resolution central vision become progressively impaired.
- **Micropsia**: Objects may appear smaller than expected in the affected eye due to separation of photoreceptors at the hole margin.

### Signs

On clinical examination, the following signs may be observed:

- **Loss of Foveal Reflex**: An early and subtle finding.
- **Yellow Spot or Ring at the Fovea**: Seen in Stage 1 (impending) holes, representing xanthophyll pigment exposed by foveal detachment or dehiscence.
- **Round, Well-Circumscribed Retinal Defect at the Fovea**: The hallmark sign of a full-thickness macular hole. The base of the hole may appear red due to visibility of the underlying [[retinal pigment epithelium (RPE)]] and [[choroid]].
- **Cuff of Subretinal Fluid**: A surrounding halo of shallow [[neurosensory retinal detachment]] around the hole.
- **Elevated, Rolled Edges**: The margins of the hole are often thickened and elevated due to intraretinal cystic changes and glial proliferation.
- **Pseudo-Operculum**: A small, round piece of tissue (composed of glial tissue and ILM, not full-thickness retina) may be seen floating above the hole, attached to or separated from the posterior hyaloid.
- **Yellow Deposits at the Base**: Drusen-like deposits or RPE changes may be visible at the base of long-standing holes.
- **Positive [[Watzke-Allen Test]]**: When a thin slit beam is projected across the macular hole, the patient reports a break, thinning, or narrowing of the line corresponding to the hole.
- **Positive [[Laser Aiming Beam Test]]**: A small (50 µm) laser aiming beam projected onto the hole is not perceived by the patient, confirming a full-thickness defect.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

- **[[Dilated Fundus Examination]]**: Using a [[slit-lamp biomicroscope]] with a [[78D lens]], [[90D lens]], or [[Goldmann three-mirror contact lens]], the clinician can directly visualize the macular hole, assess its size, and evaluate the vitreomacular interface.
- **[[Amsler Grid Testing]]**: Reveals central metamorphopsia and/or scotoma. Patients typically report distortion or a missing area in the central grid.
- **[[Watzke-Allen Test]]**: A narrow slit beam is projected onto the fovea during biomicroscopy. A positive result (patient reports a gap or thinning in the line) supports the diagnosis of a full-thickness macular hole.

### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

[[Optical coherence tomography (OCT)]], particularly [[spectral-domain OCT (SD-OCT)]] and [[swept-source OCT (SS-OCT)]], is the gold standard diagnostic modality for macular hole. OCT provides:

- **Confirmation of Full-Thickness Defect**: Cross-sectional imaging clearly demonstrates the interruption of all neurosensory retinal layers from the [[ILM]] to the [[photoreceptor layer]].
- **Precise Measurement of Hole Size**: The minimum hole diameter (at the narrowest point, typically at the mid-retinal level) and the base diameter are measured. This is critical for staging and prognostication.
- **Assessment of Vitreomacular Traction**: OCT can visualize the posterior hyaloid and determine whether VMT is present, partially released, or fully detached.
- **Detection of Intraretinal Cystic Changes**: Cystoid spaces within the retinal layers at the hole margins are readily identified.
- **Evaluation of Subretinal Fluid**: The cuff of subretinal fluid surrounding the hole is clearly delineated.
- **Assessment of Photoreceptor Layer Integrity**: The status of the [[ellipsoid zone (EZ)]] (formerly the IS/OS junction) and the [[external limiting membrane (ELM)]] at the hole margins is an important prognostic indicator for postoperative visual recovery.
- **Differentiation from [[Lamellar Macular Hole]]**: OCT distinguishes full-thickness macular holes from lamellar holes, [[macular pseudoholes]], and [[epiretinal membranes]] with foveal thinning.
- **Macular Hole Index (MHI)**: Calculated as the ratio of hole height to base diameter. An MHI >0.5 is generally associated with a better prognosis for anatomical closure and visual recovery.
- **Tractional Hole Index (THI)** and **Diameter Hole Index (DHI)**: Additional OCT-derived metrics used in prognostication.

### Fundus Autofluorescence (FAF)

[[Fundus autofluorescence (FAF)]] imaging may show increased autofluorescence at the site of the macular hole due to unmasking of [[RPE]] [[lipofuscin]] fluorescence in the absence of overlying neurosensory retina. This can help confirm the diagnosis and assess the extent of RPE changes.

### Fluorescein Angiography (FA)

[[Fluorescein angiography]] is not routinely required for the diagnosis of macular hole but may show a central window defect (hyperfluorescence) corresponding to the hole due to transmission of choroidal fluorescence through the absent retinal tissue. It is more useful for excluding other macular pathologies in atypical presentations.

### B-Scan Ultrasonography

[[B-scan ultrasonography]] may be useful in cases with media opacities (e.g., dense [[cataract]]) that preclude adequate OCT imaging, to assess the status of the vitreous and confirm the presence or absence of [[posterior vitreous detachment]].

## Differential Diagnosis

It is essential to differentiate a full-thickness macular hole from other conditions that may mimic its appearance:

| Condition | Key Differentiating Features |
|---|---|
| **[[Lamellar Macular Hole (LMH)]]** | Partial-thickness defect; intact photoreceptor layer on OCT; irregular foveal contour; better visual acuity |
| **[[Macular Pseudohole]]** | Caused by contraction of an [[epiretinal membrane]] creating a steep foveal contour; no actual tissue loss; all retinal layers intact on OCT |
| **[[Epiretinal Membrane (ERM)]]** | Membrane on the retinal surface causing wrinkling and distortion; foveal tissue is present but distorted |
| **[[Cystoid Macular Edema (CME)]]** | Intraretinal cystic spaces without full-thickness tissue loss; may have petaloid pattern on FA |
| **[[Central Serous Chorioretinopathy (CSC)]]** | Serous neurosensory detachment; no full-thickness retinal defect; characteristic FA findings |
| **[[Vitreomacular Traction (VMT) without Hole]]** | Traction present but foveal tissue intact; may show foveal elevation or cystic changes without full-thickness defect |
| **[[Solar Retinopathy]]** | Small foveal lesion; history of sun exposure; may cause a small lamellar or full-thickness defect |

---

## Natural History

The natural history of untreated macular hole depends on the stage:

- **Stage 1 (Impending Hole)**: Approximately 50% of Stage 1 lesions may resolve spontaneously with release of VMT and completion of PVD. However, approximately 40–50% progress to a full-thickness hole.
- **Stage 2 (Small Full-Thickness Hole)**: Without intervention, the majority of Stage 2 holes enlarge over time. Spontaneous closure is rare (estimated at 2–10% in some series), more likely in small holes with persistent VMT that subsequently releases.
- **Stage 3 and 4 (Established Full-Thickness Holes)**: These rarely close spontaneously. Visual acuity typically stabilizes at 20/200 to 20/400 or worse. Long-standing holes may develop [[RPE]] atrophy, [[photoreceptor]] degeneration, and progressive enlargement.

---

## Management

### Observation

- **Stage 1 (Impending Holes)**: Close observation with serial [[OCT]] is appropriate, as many resolve spontaneously. Patients should be educated about symptoms of progression and instructed to monitor with an [[Amsler grid]].
- **Small Holes with Minimal Symptoms**: In select cases, particularly in elderly patients with significant comorbidities, observation may be considered, though surgical intervention is generally recommended for full-thickness holes to prevent further visual decline.

### Pharmacological Intervention: Ocriplasmin (Jetrea®)

**[[Ocriplasmin]]** (Jetrea®, ThromboGenics/Oxurion) is a recombinant truncated form of human [[plasmin]] approved by the FDA in 2012 for the treatment of symptomatic [[vitreomacular traction]], including cases associated with macular holes ≤400 µm.

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Mechanism of Action** | Enzymatic [[vitreolysis]]; cleaves [[fibronectin]] and [[laminin]] at the vitreomacular interface, promoting release of VMT |
| **Administration** | Single [[intravitreal injection]] of 0.125 mg (0.1 mL) |
| **Efficacy for Macular Hole Closure** | Approximately 40% closure rate for holes ≤250 µm with VMT; lower success rates for larger holes |
| **Ideal Candidates** | Small macular holes (≤250 µm, preferably ≤400 µm) with concurrent VMT; absence of [[epiretinal membrane]] |
| **Adverse Effects** | Transient [[floaters]], [[photopsia]], [[blurred vision]], [[vitreous detachment]], [[zonular instability]], [[dyschromatopsia]], [[electroretinographic changes]], risk of [[retinal detachment]] (rare) |

Ocriplasmin offers a non-surgical alternative for select patients but has limited efficacy for larger holes and is not a substitute for vitrectomy in most cases.

### Surgical Management: Pars Plana Vitrectomy (PPV)

**[[Pars plana vitrectomy (PPV)]]** is the definitive and most effective treatment for full-thickness macular holes. The surgical technique has evolved significantly since Kelly and Wendel first reported successful macular hole surgery in 1991.

#### Surgical Technique

1. **Standard Three-Port Pars Plana Vitrectomy**: Performed using [[microincision vitrectomy surgery (MIVS)]] with 23-gauge, 25-gauge, or 27-gauge instrumentation. The [[vitreous gel]] is removed, and the [[posterior hyaloid]] is separated from the retinal surface if not already detached.

2. **[[Internal Limiting Membrane (ILM) Peeling]]**: This is now considered a critical step in macular hole surgery. The ILM is stained with a vital dye (most commonly [[indocyanine green (ICG)]], [[Brilliant Blue G (BBG)]], or [[trypan blue]]) to enhance visualization. The ILM is then grasped with intraocular forceps and peeled circumferentially around the macular hole, typically for a diameter of 2–3 disc diameters. ILM peeling:
   - Removes the scaffold for [[myofibroblast]] proliferation and tangential traction
   - Increases retinal compliance, allowing the hole edges to approximate
   - Significantly improves anatomical closure rates (>90–95% with ILM peeling vs. ~70–80% without)

3. **Inverted ILM Flap Technique**: For large (>400 µm) or chronic macular holes, the **inverted ILM flap technique** (described by Michalewska et al., 2010) involves peeling the ILM but leaving it attached at the hole edge, then inverting (folding) the flap over the hole to serve as a scaffold for [[glial cell]] proliferation and hole closure. This technique has significantly improved closure rates for large and recurrent holes.

4. **Gas Tamponade**: After vitrectomy and ILM peeling, an intraocular gas tamponade is injected to provide surface tension at the macular hole, promoting apposition of the hole edges and facilitating closure. Commonly used gases include:

   | Gas | Concentration | Duration of Tamponade |
   |---|---|---|
   | **[[Sulfur hexafluoride (SF6)]]** | 20% non-expansile | ~2 weeks |
   | **[[Perfluoropropane (C3F8)]]** | 14% non-expansile | ~6–8 weeks |
   | **Room Air** | 100% | ~5–7 days |

   Recent evidence suggests that shorter-acting tamponades (SF6 or even room air) may be sufficient for small and medium-sized holes, reducing the duration of postoperative positioning requirements.

5. **Face-Down Positioning**: Traditionally, patients are instructed to maintain a strict **face-down (prone) position** for 1–2 weeks postoperatively to ensure the gas bubble tamponades the macular hole. However, recent studies have challenged the necessity of prolonged face-down positioning, particularly for small and medium-sized holes, with some surgeons advocating for "non-supine" or minimal positioning protocols with comparable outcomes.

#### Surgical Outcomes

| Parameter | Outcome |
|---|---|
| **Anatomical Closure Rate (Primary Surgery)** | >90–95% for small and medium holes; 80–90% for large holes with ILM peeling; higher with inverted flap technique |
| **Visual Acuity Improvement** | Majority of patients achieve ≥2 lines of improvement; final VA of 20/40 or better in 40–70% of cases depending on hole size and duration |
| **Prognostic Factors for Better Outcome** | Smaller hole size, shorter duration of symptoms, better preoperative VA, intact EZ and ELM on preoperative OCT, younger age |
| **Reoperation for Failed Closure** | Repeat vitrectomy with ILM peeling (if not previously performed), inverted flap, autologous [[retinal transplantation]], or [[autologous ILM transplantation]] may be considered |

#### Complications of Vitrectomy

| Complication | Approximate Incidence |
|---|---|
| **[[Nuclear Sclerotic Cataract]] Progression** | >70–80% within 1–2 years in phakic patients; many surgeons perform combined [[phacoemulsification]] with [[intraocular lens (IOL)]] implantation |
| **[[Retinal Detachment]]** | 1–3% |
| **[[Endophthalmitis]]** | <0.1% |
| **Elevated [[Intraocular Pressure (IOP)]]** | Transient; related to gas tamponade |
| **[[Reopening of Macular Hole]]** | 1–5% |
| **[[Retinal Phototoxicity]]** | Rare; related to operating microscope light exposure |
| **Visual Field Defects** | Reported in some series; may be related to ILM peeling or gas tamponade |
| **[[ICG Toxicity]]** | Potential RPE toxicity with indocyanine green dye; mitigated by using lower concentrations or alternative dyes (BBG) |

### Emerging and Adjunctive Therapies

- **[[Autologous Retinal Transplantation (ART)]]**: For refractory or very large macular holes that fail conventional surgery, a free flap of autologous peripheral retina can be transplanted into the hole as a scaffold. Early results are promising.
- **[[Autologous ILM Transplantation]]**: Similar concept using a free ILM graft harvested from a distant site.
- **[[Human Amniotic Membrane (hAM) Transplantation]]**: Amniotic membrane plugs have been used as a scaffold for closure of large or persistent macular holes.
- **[[Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP)]] and [[Autologous Serum]]**: Used as adjuncts to promote hole closure, particularly in earlier surgical series; less commonly used with modern ILM peeling techniques.
- **[[Lens Capsular Flap Transplantation]]**: The anterior or posterior [[lens capsule]] can be used as a biological scaffold for large or refractory holes.

## Special Considerations

### Myopic Macular Hole

Macular holes in highly [[myopic]] eyes (axial length >26 mm) present unique challenges due to [[posterior staphyloma]], retinal thinning, and the risk of [[macular hole retinal detachment (MHRD)]]. Surgical closure rates are lower, and additional techniques such as the inverted ILM flap, [[macular buckle]], or [[silicone oil]] tamponade may be required.

### Traumatic Macular Hole

[[Traumatic macular hole]] occurs following blunt [[ocular trauma]], typically in younger patients. These holes may close spontaneously in up to 30–50% of cases, particularly if small. Observation for 3–6 months is often recommended before considering surgical intervention.

### Chronic and Large Macular Holes

Holes present for more than 1 year or with a diameter >400 µm have lower anatomical closure rates and poorer visual outcomes. The inverted ILM flap technique and autologous tissue transplantation have significantly improved outcomes in this subgroup.

### Bilateral Macular Holes

Patients with a macular hole in one eye have an approximately 10–15% risk of developing a macular hole in the fellow eye. The risk is higher if the fellow eye lacks a complete [[posterior vitreous detachment]]. Regular monitoring with [[OCT]] and [[Amsler grid]] testing is recommended.

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis for macular hole has improved dramatically with modern surgical techniques:

- **Anatomical closure** is achieved in >90–95% of cases with primary vitrectomy and ILM peeling.
- **Functional visual recovery** depends on preoperative hole size, duration of symptoms, and integrity of the [[photoreceptor]] layer. Patients with smaller holes and shorter symptom duration tend to achieve better final visual acuity.
- **Postoperative OCT monitoring** is essential to confirm closure, assess photoreceptor layer restoration (EZ and ELM reconstitution), and detect reopening.
- **Long-term visual improvement** may continue for 6–12 months or longer after surgery as photoreceptor recovery progresses.

---

## Role of the Optometrist

[[Optometrists]] play a critical role in the detection, diagnosis, monitoring, and co-management of macular hole:

1. **Early Detection**: Identification of metamorphopsia and central scotoma during routine eye examinations using [[Amsler grid]] testing and careful [[fundoscopy]].
2. **Diagnostic Imaging**: Ordering and interpreting [[OCT]] scans to confirm the diagnosis, stage the hole, and differentiate from mimicking conditions.
3. **Timely Referral**: Prompt referral to a [[vitreoretinal specialist]] for surgical evaluation, particularly for full-thickness holes, as earlier intervention is associated with better outcomes.
4. **Postoperative Co-Management**: Monitoring for complications, assessing visual recovery, managing concurrent conditions (e.g., [[cataract]] progression), and providing low vision rehabilitation if needed.
5. **Fellow Eye Monitoring**: Ongoing surveillance of the fellow eye with serial OCT and patient education regarding warning symptoms.

---

## Summary

Macular hole is a well-characterized, sight-threatening condition of the [[fovea]] that results from abnormal [[vitreomacular traction]] and subsequent full-thickness retinal dehiscence. [[Optical coherence tomography]] has transformed the diagnostic accuracy and staging of this condition, while [[pars plana vitrectomy]] with [[ILM peeling]] and gas tamponade remains the gold standard treatment, achieving anatomical closure in the vast majority of cases. Innovations such as the inverted ILM flap technique and autologous tissue transplantation have expanded the surgical armamentarium for complex and refractory cases. Optometrists serve as essential frontline clinicians in the early detection, appropriate referral, and long-term co-management of patients with macular hole.
