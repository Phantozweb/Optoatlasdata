

# Ciliary Muscle

The **ciliary muscle** (musculus ciliaris) is a ring of [[smooth muscle]] located within the [[ciliary body]] of the [[eye]], serving as the principal effector of [[accommodation]]. It is the only intrinsic smooth muscle of the eye that contracts to *decrease* tension on its associated connective tissue apparatus—the [[zonular fibers]] (zonules of Zinn)—thereby allowing the [[crystalline lens]] to assume a more convex shape for near vision. The ciliary muscle is innervated predominantly by [[parasympathetic]] postganglionic fibers originating from the [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]], traversing the [[oculomotor nerve]] (cranial nerve III), and synapsing in the [[ciliary ganglion]]. Its proper function is indispensable for dynamic focusing, and its dysfunction underlies a spectrum of clinically significant conditions ranging from [[presbyopia]] to [[accommodative spasm]].

---

## Gross and Microscopic Anatomy

### Location and Relationships

The ciliary muscle occupies the bulk of the [[ciliary body]], which is a triangular (in cross-section) structure situated between the [[iris]] anteriorly and the [[choroid]] posteriorly. The ciliary body extends from the [[scleral spur]] anteriorly to the [[ora serrata]] posteriorly, spanning approximately 6–7 mm in the meridional plane. The ciliary muscle lies in the outermost (scleral) aspect of the ciliary body, directly internal to the [[supraciliary space]] and the [[sclera]].

Key anatomical relationships include:

| Structure | Relationship to Ciliary Muscle |
|---|---|
| [[Sclera]] | External; separated by the supraciliary lamina |
| [[Scleral spur]] | Anterior attachment point for longitudinal fibers |
| [[Trabecular meshwork]] / [[Schlemm's canal]] | Anteriorly adjacent; longitudinal fibers influence aqueous outflow |
| [[Zonular fibers]] | Originate from the ciliary epithelium overlying the muscle; transmit force to the lens |
| [[Crystalline lens]] | Connected via zonules; shape modulated by ciliary muscle contraction |
| [[Choroid]] | Posterior continuation; longitudinal fibers insert into the choroidal stroma |
| [[Iris]] | Shares vascular supply; iris root attaches near the anterior ciliary body |
| [[Ciliary processes]] | Located on the inner surface of the ciliary body (pars plicata), overlying the muscle |
| [[Vitreous body]] | Internal to the ciliary epithelium; the [[vitreous base]] attaches near the ora serrata |

### Fiber Orientation and Classification

The ciliary muscle is classically described as having three distinct fiber groups, though they represent a continuum rather than truly separate muscles:

#### 1. Longitudinal (Meridional) Fibers — Brücke's Muscle

These are the outermost fibers, running anteroposteriorly from the [[scleral spur]] and the adjacent [[trabecular meshwork]] to the suprachoroidal lamina and choroidal stroma. Contraction of these fibers pulls the choroid anteriorly and opens the [[trabecular meshwork]], thereby facilitating [[aqueous humor]] outflow through the conventional (trabecular) pathway. This is the pharmacological basis for the use of [[pilocarpine]] and other [[miotics]] in the treatment of [[open-angle glaucoma]].

#### 2. Radial (Oblique) Fibers — Ivanoff's Muscle

These intermediate fibers fan out from the scleral spur region toward the ciliary processes in an oblique orientation. They serve as a transitional zone between the longitudinal and circular fibers and contribute to both accommodation and aqueous outflow dynamics.

#### 3. Circular (Sphincteric) Fibers — Müller's Muscle

The innermost fibers form a ring (annular configuration) at the apex of the ciliary body, near the base of the ciliary processes. Their contraction is the primary driver of accommodation: by constricting, they reduce the diameter of the ciliary ring, relaxing the [[zonular fibers]] and allowing the elastic [[lens capsule]] to mold the lens into a more spherical shape.

### Histology

The ciliary muscle is composed of [[smooth muscle]] cells embedded in a connective tissue matrix rich in [[elastic fibers]] and [[collagen]]. The muscle cells are spindle-shaped, uninucleated, and arranged in bundles corresponding to the three fiber orientations described above. With advancing age, there is progressive replacement of muscle fibers by [[connective tissue]] (fibrosis) and accumulation of [[elastic tissue]], which contributes to the age-related decline in accommodative amplitude ([[presbyopia]]). Histochemical studies demonstrate abundant [[mitochondria]] within the muscle cells, reflecting the high metabolic demand of sustained accommodative effort.

### Vascular Supply

The ciliary muscle receives its blood supply from the [[long posterior ciliary arteries]] (two branches of the [[ophthalmic artery]]) and the [[anterior ciliary arteries]] (branches of the muscular arteries supplying the [[extraocular muscles]]). These vessels anastomose to form the [[major arterial circle of the iris]] (circulus arteriosus iridis major), which lies at the root of the iris and the anterior ciliary body. Venous drainage is via the [[vortex veins]] and the [[anterior ciliary veins]].

### Innervation

The ciliary muscle receives a triple innervation, though parasympathetic input is overwhelmingly dominant:

| Innervation | Origin | Neurotransmitter | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Parasympathetic** (primary) | [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]] → [[CN III]] → [[ciliary ganglion]] → [[short posterior ciliary nerves]] | [[Acetylcholine]] (muscarinic M3 receptors) | Contraction → accommodation |
| **Sympathetic** (modulatory) | [[Superior cervical ganglion]] → [[long ciliary nerves]] and via the ciliary ganglion | [[Norepinephrine]] (β2-adrenergic receptors) | Relaxation (facilitates disaccommodation); minor role |
| **Sensory (afferent)** | [[Trigeminal nerve]] (V1, [[nasociliary nerve]]) → [[long ciliary nerves]] | Substance P, CGRP | Nociception; reflex arcs |

The parasympathetic pathway is clinically the most important. Preganglionic fibers travel with the inferior division of [[CN III]] to the [[ciliary ganglion]] in the orbit, where they synapse. Postganglionic fibers reach the ciliary muscle via the [[short posterior ciliary nerves]]. The muscarinic receptors on the ciliary muscle are predominantly of the **M3 subtype**, with some M2 receptors also present.

## Physiology of Accommodation

### The Helmholtz Theory

The prevailing model of accommodation is the **Helmholtz theory** (1855), which describes the following sequence:

1. **Distance vision (unaccommodated state):** The ciliary muscle is relaxed. The [[zonular fibers]] are under tension, pulling the equator of the [[crystalline lens]] radially outward and flattening it. The lens has minimal refractive power in this state (relative to its accommodated form).

2. **Near vision (accommodated state):** Parasympathetic stimulation causes the ciliary muscle (especially the circular fibers) to contract. The ciliary ring decreases in diameter, moving the ciliary body anteriorly and inward. This releases tension on the zonular fibers. The inherent elasticity of the [[lens capsule]] then molds the lens into a more convex (spherical) shape, increasing its dioptric power and enabling focus on near objects.

### Accommodative Triad

Accommodation does not occur in isolation. It is part of the **[[near triad]]** (also called the accommodative triad or near synkinesis):

1. **Accommodation** — increased lens curvature via ciliary muscle contraction
2. **Convergence** — adduction of both eyes by the [[medial rectus muscles]] to maintain binocular fixation on a near target
3. **Pupillary miosis** — constriction of the [[pupil]] via the [[sphincter pupillae]], increasing depth of focus

These three responses are neurologically linked through the [[accommodation-convergence reflex]] mediated by the [[supraoculomotor area]] and the [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]].

### Accommodative Amplitude

Accommodative amplitude is the maximum increase in dioptric power the eye can achieve through ciliary muscle contraction. It is measured in [[diopters]] (D) and declines predictably with age:

| Age (years) | Average Accommodative Amplitude (D) |
|---|---|
| 10 | 13–14 |
| 20 | 10–12 |
| 30 | 7–8 |
| 40 | 4–6 |
| 45 | 3–4 |
| 50 | 1–2 |
| 60 | <1 |

This decline is the basis of [[presbyopia]], which becomes clinically symptomatic typically around age 40–45 when the amplitude falls below approximately 4 D, making sustained near work difficult.

## Pathophysiology

Dysfunction of the ciliary muscle may arise from intrinsic changes within the muscle itself, alterations in its neural control, pharmacological effects, or secondary involvement in ocular or systemic disease.

### Age-Related Changes and Presbyopia

[[Presbyopia]] is the most common condition related to ciliary muscle pathophysiology. Historically, presbyopia was attributed solely to [[lenticular sclerosis]]—progressive hardening and loss of elasticity of the crystalline lens. However, modern research using [[ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM), [[optical coherence tomography]] (OCT) of the anterior segment, and [[magnetic resonance imaging]] (MRI) has demonstrated that the ciliary muscle itself undergoes significant age-related changes:

- **Fibrosis and connective tissue infiltration:** Replacement of contractile smooth muscle fibers with collagen and elastic tissue reduces the force-generating capacity of the muscle.
- **Geometric remodeling:** The ciliary muscle becomes shorter in its anteroposterior dimension and thicker, with an anterior shift of its mass. The circular (Müller's) fibers, most critical for accommodation, are disproportionately affected.
- **Reduced contractile excursion:** Even when pharmacologically stimulated (e.g., with [[pilocarpine]]), the aged ciliary muscle demonstrates diminished movement compared to younger eyes.
- **Altered elastic tissue composition:** Changes in the [[extracellular matrix]], including increased cross-linking of collagen and alterations in [[elastin]], reduce tissue compliance.

Despite these changes, studies using [[anterior segment OCT]] (AS-OCT) and MRI have shown that the ciliary muscle retains some contractile ability even in advanced presbyopia, suggesting that lenticular changes (increased lens stiffness and size) are the predominant cause, with ciliary muscle changes playing a contributory role.

### Accommodative Dysfunction

Disorders of accommodation directly related to ciliary muscle function include:

#### Accommodative Insufficiency

[[Accommodative insufficiency]] is defined as an accommodative amplitude that is significantly below the age-expected norm (typically >2 D below the lower limit for age, as predicted by [[Hofstetter's formulas]]). It may result from:

- Ciliary muscle fatigue or weakness
- Systemic illness (e.g., [[diabetes mellitus]], [[anemia]], [[chronic fatigue syndrome]])
- Medications (e.g., [[anticholinergics]], [[antihistamines]], [[tricyclic antidepressants]])
- Head trauma or [[traumatic brain injury]] (TBI)
- Idiopathic causes

#### Accommodative Spasm (Ciliary Spasm)

[[Accommodative spasm]] (also termed [[ciliary spasm]] or spasm of the near reflex) is a sustained, involuntary contraction of the ciliary muscle resulting in a pseudomyopic shift. It may be accompanied by excessive convergence and miosis (spasm of the entire near triad). Causes include:

- Functional/psychogenic factors (most common in young patients)
- Excessive near work
- Pharmacological agents (e.g., [[cholinergic agonists]], [[organophosphate]] exposure)
- Inflammatory conditions of the anterior segment (e.g., [[anterior uveitis]])
- Central nervous system lesions (rare)

#### Accommodative Infacility

[[Accommodative infacility]] refers to a reduced ability to make rapid, accurate changes in accommodation (i.e., difficulty switching focus between near and far). The ciliary muscle contracts and relaxes sluggishly. This is commonly identified using [[accommodative facility testing]] with ±2.00 D flipper lenses.

### Pharmacological Effects on the Ciliary Muscle

The ciliary muscle is a major target of ophthalmic pharmacology:

| Drug Class | Example | Mechanism | Effect on Ciliary Muscle | Clinical Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [[Parasympathomimetics]] (direct-acting) | [[Pilocarpine]] | Muscarinic agonist (M3) | Contraction | [[Glaucoma]] (opens trabecular meshwork); [[accommodative esotropia]] |
| [[Parasympathomimetics]] (indirect-acting) | [[Echothiophate iodide]] | [[Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor]] | Sustained contraction | Glaucoma; accommodative esotropia |
| [[Cycloplegics]] / [[Parasympatholytics]] | [[Atropine]], [[cyclopentolate]], [[tropicamide]] | Muscarinic antagonist | Relaxation (paralysis) → [[cycloplegia]] | [[Cycloplegic refraction]]; uveitis treatment; myopia control |
| [[Sympathomimetics]] | [[Phenylephrine]] (primarily α1) | Adrenergic agonist | Minimal direct effect on ciliary muscle (primarily mydriasis) | Pupil dilation |
| [[Beta-adrenergic agonists]] | — | β2 agonist | Mild relaxation | Not clinically used for this purpose |

### Ciliary Muscle and Myopia

Emerging research has implicated the ciliary muscle in the pathogenesis and progression of [[myopia]]. Key findings include:

- **Ciliary muscle thickness:** Studies using AS-OCT have demonstrated that myopic eyes tend to have a **thicker ciliary muscle**, particularly in the anterior portion, compared to emmetropic or hyperopic eyes. This thickening may be an adaptive response to increased accommodative demand or a primary structural difference.
- **Accommodative lag:** Myopic individuals often exhibit greater [[accommodative lag]] (under-accommodation for near targets), which results in chronic hyperopic retinal defocus. This defocus is hypothesized to be a stimulus for axial elongation and myopia progression.
- **Atropine for myopia control:** Low-dose [[atropine]] (0.01%–0.05%), which acts on muscarinic receptors in the ciliary muscle (and potentially in the [[retina]] and [[sclera]]), has been shown to significantly slow myopia progression in children. The exact mechanism remains debated—it may involve direct ciliary muscle relaxation, scleral remodeling, or retinal signaling pathways.

### Ciliary Muscle in Ocular Inflammation

In [[anterior uveitis]] (iridocyclitis), inflammation of the [[ciliary body]] directly involves the ciliary muscle. Inflammatory mediators (e.g., [[prostaglandins]], [[cytokines]]) cause ciliary muscle spasm, which manifests clinically as:

- **Pain:** Deep, aching ocular pain exacerbated by accommodation
- **Photophobia:** Partly due to ciliary spasm triggered by pupillary light reflex
- **Pseudomyopia:** From sustained accommodative spasm

This is the rationale for using [[cycloplegic agents]] (e.g., [[atropine]], [[homatropine]], [[cyclopentolate]]) in the management of anterior uveitis—they relax the ciliary muscle, relieve pain, and prevent [[posterior synechiae]] formation by dilating the pupil.

### Ciliary Muscle and Glaucoma

The longitudinal fibers of the ciliary muscle insert into the [[scleral spur]] and the [[trabecular meshwork]]. Contraction of these fibers (e.g., by pilocarpine) pulls the scleral spur posteriorly, opening the trabecular meshwork and [[Schlemm's canal]], thereby enhancing conventional [[aqueous humor]] outflow and reducing [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP). This mechanism is exploited therapeutically in [[open-angle glaucoma]].

Conversely, in [[angle-closure glaucoma]], ciliary muscle contraction (via miotics) can also be used to pull the peripheral iris away from the [[trabecular meshwork]], breaking pupillary block in some cases.

## Symptoms of Ciliary Muscle Dysfunction

Patients with ciliary muscle dysfunction may present with a constellation of symptoms, depending on the underlying etiology:

| Symptom | Associated Condition |
|---|---|
| Blurred near vision | [[Accommodative insufficiency]], [[presbyopia]], [[cycloplegia]] |
| Blurred distance vision (pseudomyopia) | [[Accommodative spasm]] |
| Difficulty shifting focus near-to-far | [[Accommodative infacility]] |
| [[Asthenopia]] (eye strain) | All accommodative disorders |
| Frontal or periorbital [[headache]] | Accommodative excess, spasm, or insufficiency |
| Deep aching eye pain | [[Anterior uveitis]] with ciliary spasm |
| [[Photophobia]] | Ciliary spasm secondary to inflammation |
| Diplopia (intermittent) | Spasm of the near reflex (convergence component) |
| Reading avoidance or reduced reading stamina | Accommodative insufficiency or infacility |
| Brow ache | Sustained accommodative effort |

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical Assessment of Accommodation

#### Accommodative Amplitude Measurement

- **Push-up (Donders') method:** A near target is moved progressively closer to the patient until it first becomes blurred. The reciprocal of the distance (in meters) at the blur point gives the amplitude in diopters. This is the most commonly used clinical method.
- **Push-down method:** The target is moved away from a very close position until it first clears.
- **Minus lens method:** Minus lenses of increasing power are placed before the eye while the patient views a near target at a fixed distance (typically 40 cm). The strongest minus lens through which the target remains clear is added to the accommodative demand (2.50 D at 40 cm) to yield the amplitude.

#### Accommodative Facility Testing

Using ±2.00 D flipper lenses at 40 cm, the patient alternately clears plus and minus lenses. The number of cycles completed per minute (cpm) is recorded. Norms for monocular facility are approximately **11 ± 5 cpm** for adults. Reduced facility suggests [[accommodative infacility]], with difficulty clearing minus lenses suggesting accommodative insufficiency and difficulty clearing plus lenses suggesting accommodative excess.

#### Accommodative Response (Lag/Lead)

Measured using [[dynamic retinoscopy]] (e.g., [[Monocular Estimation Method (MEM)]] or [[Nott retinoscopy]]). A normal accommodative lag at 40 cm is approximately **+0.25 to +0.75 D**. Excessive lag (>+1.00 D) is associated with accommodative insufficiency and may be relevant in myopia progression.

### Imaging of the Ciliary Muscle

#### Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography (AS-OCT)

[[Anterior segment OCT]] is the gold standard for in vivo imaging of the ciliary muscle. High-resolution cross-sectional images allow measurement of:

- **Ciliary muscle thickness (CMT):** Measured at specific distances posterior to the scleral spur (e.g., CMT1 at 1 mm, CMT2 at 2 mm, CMT3 at 3 mm)
- **Ciliary muscle length**
- **Changes in ciliary muscle configuration during accommodation** (by comparing relaxed vs. accommodated states)

Swept-source OCT and enhanced-depth imaging (EDI) techniques have improved visualization of the ciliary muscle through the sclera.

#### Ultrasound Biomicroscopy (UBM)

[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] uses high-frequency ultrasound (35–50 MHz) to image the anterior segment, including the ciliary body and ciliary muscle, with a resolution of approximately 25–50 μm. UBM can visualize:

- Ciliary muscle morphology
- Ciliary body tumors (e.g., [[ciliary body melanoma]])
- Ciliary body detachment or effusion
- Zonular integrity
- Relationship of the ciliary body to the angle structures

#### Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

High-resolution MRI (3T or 7T) has been used in research settings to image the ciliary muscle and lens during accommodation. MRI provides excellent soft tissue contrast and can demonstrate the dynamic changes in ciliary muscle shape, lens curvature, and zonular geometry during the accommodative response.

### Pharmacological Testing

- **[[Cycloplegic refraction]]:** Instillation of a cycloplegic agent (e.g., [[cyclopentolate]] 1%, [[atropine]] 1%) paralyzes the ciliary muscle, revealing the true (latent) refractive error by eliminating accommodative tone. This is essential in pediatric refraction and in cases of suspected accommodative spasm.
- **Pilocarpine stimulation:** Can be used to assess residual ciliary muscle function (e.g., in presbyopia research or pharmacological studies).

## Management and Treatment

### Non-Pharmacological Management

#### Optical Correction

- **Plus lenses (reading glasses, bifocals, progressive addition lenses):** The primary management for [[presbyopia]] and [[accommodative insufficiency]]. Plus lenses supplement the reduced accommodative amplitude, reducing the demand on the ciliary muscle.
- **Prism:** In cases of accommodative esotropia with a high [[AC/A ratio]], plus lenses reduce the accommodative demand and thereby the convergence response.

#### Vision Therapy / Orthoptics

For non-presbyopic accommodative disorders (insufficiency, infacility, excess), [[vision therapy]] (also termed orthoptic exercises or accommodative training) is a well-established treatment:

- **Accommodative facility training:** Repeated practice with ±2.00 D flipper lenses to improve the speed and accuracy of accommodative responses.
- **Push-up accommodative exercises:** Progressive near-point training to improve accommodative amplitude.
- **Hart chart exercises:** Alternating fixation between near and far letter charts to improve accommodative flexibility.
- **Computer-based accommodative training programs**

Evidence supports the efficacy of office-based vision therapy for accommodative dysfunction, particularly in children and young adults with symptomatic accommodative insufficiency or infacility (as demonstrated in the [[Convergence Insufficiency Treatment Trial]] and related studies).

### Pharmacological Management

#### Cycloplegic Agents

Used to relax the ciliary muscle in the following clinical scenarios:

| Agent | Onset | Duration | Primary Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Atropine]] 1% | 30–40 min | 7–14 days | Cycloplegic refraction (gold standard); uveitis; myopia control (low-dose 0.01–0.05%) |
| [[Homatropine]] 2–5% | 30–60 min | 1–3 days | Uveitis management |
| [[Cyclopentolate]] 0.5–2% | 25–75 min | 6–24 hours | Cycloplegic refraction (routine clinical use) |
| [[Tropicamide]] 0.5–1% | 20–40 min | 4–6 hours | Mydriasis (weak cycloplegia); screening refraction |
| [[Scopolamine]] 0.25% | 30–60 min | 3–7 days | Cycloplegic refraction (alternative to atropine) |

#### Miotics (Parasympathomimetics)

Used to contract the ciliary muscle:

- **[[Pilocarpine]]** (0.5–4%): Contracts the longitudinal fibers, opening the trabecular meshwork for glaucoma management. Also used in low concentration in some presbyopia-correcting eye drops (e.g., [[Vuity]]—pilocarpine 1.25%) to create a small-aperture (pinhole) effect via miosis, improving near vision in presbyopes.
- **[[Echothiophate iodide]]** (0.03–0.25%): Irreversible cholinesterase inhibitor; used in accommodative esotropia to reduce the need for bifocals in children.

#### Low-Dose Atropine for Myopia Control

[[Low-dose atropine]] (0.01%–0.05%) has emerged as a leading pharmacological intervention for [[myopia control]] in children. While the exact mechanism is debated, its action on muscarinic receptors in the ciliary muscle, retina, and sclera is believed to modulate the signaling pathways that drive axial elongation. The [[ATOM]] (Atropine for the Treatment of Myopia) studies and subsequent trials have demonstrated significant reduction in myopia progression with minimal side effects at low concentrations.

### Surgical and Device-Based Approaches

#### Presbyopia Surgery Involving the Ciliary Muscle

Several surgical and device-based strategies have targeted the ciliary muscle or ciliary body to restore accommodation in presbyopic patients:

- **Scleral expansion procedures (e.g., anterior ciliary sclerotomy, scleral expansion bands):** Based on the [[Schachar theory]] of accommodation (which posits that presbyopia results from growth of the lens reducing the space between the lens equator and the ciliary muscle), these procedures aim to increase the circumlenticular space. Results have been inconsistent, and the underlying theory remains controversial.
- **[[Accommodating intraocular lenses]] (IOLs):** Lenses designed to move or change shape within the eye in response to ciliary muscle contraction (e.g., [[Crystalens]], [[Synchrony]]). These rely on residual ciliary muscle function to translate force into lens movement. Clinical outcomes have been variable, with limited true accommodative range achieved in most cases.
- **Lens refilling procedures:** Experimental approaches to replace the hardened lens material with a flexible polymer within the intact capsular bag, theoretically restoring accommodation via ciliary muscle action on the zonules and capsule.

#### Ciliary Body Ablation

[[Cyclophotocoagulation]] (transscleral or endoscopic) and [[cyclocryotherapy]] are procedures that destroy ciliary body tissue (including the ciliary muscle and ciliary epithelium) to reduce aqueous humor production in refractory [[glaucoma]]. While these target the ciliary epithelium primarily, the ciliary muscle is inevitably affected, and accommodation is further compromised.

## Ciliary Muscle in Special Populations

### Pediatric Considerations

Children have robust accommodative amplitudes (12–16 D) and highly responsive ciliary muscles. Key clinical considerations include:

- **[[Accommodative esotropia]]:** Excessive accommodative effort (often driven by uncorrected [[hyperopia]]) triggers excessive convergence via the [[AC/A ratio]], resulting in esotropia. Management includes full hyperopic correction, bifocals, and in some cases, miotics (echothiophate).
- **Cycloplegic refraction is mandatory** in pediatric eye examinations to unmask latent hyperopia, as the powerful ciliary muscle in children can mask significant refractive errors through tonic accommodation.
- **Accommodative dysfunction post-concussion:** Children with [[traumatic brain injury]] frequently develop accommodative insufficiency or infacility, requiring targeted vision therapy.

### Geriatric Considerations

In elderly patients, the ciliary muscle is fibrotic and has reduced contractile capacity. Clinically:

- Accommodative amplitude is negligible (< 1 D after age 55–60)
- Cycloplegic refraction adds minimal additional information compared to manifest refraction
- Accommodating IOLs may have limited efficacy due to reduced ciliary muscle function
- Pilocarpine-based presbyopia drops (e.g., Vuity) rely primarily on miosis rather than true accommodation
