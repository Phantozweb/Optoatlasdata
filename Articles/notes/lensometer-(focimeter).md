

# Lensometer (Focimeter)

---

## Definition and Overview

The **Lensometer** (also known as a **Focimeter**, **Lensmeter**, **Vertometer**, or **Lensometer**) is a precision optical instrument used in [[Optometry]] and [[Ophthalmology]] to measure the **back vertex power**, **cylinder axis**, **optical center**, and **prismatic effect** of ophthalmic lenses. It is an indispensable clinical tool in every optical dispensary, optometric practice, and ophthalmological clinic worldwide. The instrument allows clinicians to verify the prescription of existing [[spectacle lenses]], confirm the accuracy of newly fabricated lenses from an optical laboratory, and determine the unknown prescription of a patient's current eyewear.

The term "**Focimeter**" is derived from the Latin *focus* (hearth, focal point) and the Greek *metron* (measure), literally meaning "a device that measures focal points." In North America, the instrument is most commonly referred to as a **Lensometer** (a trade name originally coined by the American Optical Company) or **Lensmeter**, while in the United Kingdom, Europe, and many Commonwealth nations, the term **Focimeter** is preferred. The Reichert Corporation popularized the trade name **Vertometer**. Regardless of nomenclature, all these terms refer to the same fundamental instrument and its operational principle.

The lensometer is considered one of the most essential pieces of equipment in clinical eye care. It is used at virtually every patient encounter where spectacles are involved—from the initial assessment of a patient's habitual correction to the final verification of dispensed eyewear. Mastery of the lensometer is a foundational competency for all [[optometrists]], [[ophthalmologists]], [[opticians]], and [[ophthalmic technicians]].

---

## Historical Development

The concept of measuring the focal power of lenses dates back centuries, but the modern focimeter was developed in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Key milestones include:

- **1848–1876**: Early attempts at lens power measurement relied on the **Geneva Lens Measure** (also called a **lens clock** or **lens gauge**), which estimated lens power by measuring surface curvature. This method was indirect and could not account for lens thickness or index of refraction variations.
- **1912**: **Troppmann** is credited with developing one of the earliest practical focimeters based on the telescope-collimator optical principle.
- **1921**: The **American Optical Company** introduced the first commercially successful instrument under the trade name **Lensometer**, which became the generic term in North American practice.
- **1922**: **B&L (Bausch & Lomb)** introduced competing models, further standardizing the instrument in clinical practice.
- **Mid-20th century**: Refinements in optics, mechanical design, and illumination systems led to the modern manual lensometer designs still in use today.
- **1980s–present**: The advent of **automated (digital) lensometers** revolutionized lens measurement by incorporating electronic sensors, microprocessors, and digital displays, dramatically improving speed, accuracy, and ease of use.

## Optical Principles and Instrumentation

### Fundamental Optical Principle

The lensometer operates on the principle of **Badal's optometer** combined with a **telescope** focused at infinity. The core concept is as follows:

1. A **movable illuminated target** (the mires) is placed at the primary focal point of a **standard (collimating) lens** of known power within the instrument.
2. When the target is precisely at the focal point of the collimating lens, the light rays emerging from the collimating lens are rendered **parallel (collimated)**.
3. The **unknown spectacle lens** (the lens being measured) is placed on the **lens rest** (also called the **lens stop** or **lens table**) at the back focal point of the collimating lens.
4. If the unknown lens has zero power (plano), the parallel rays pass through unchanged and are focused by the **eyepiece telescope** (set for infinity) onto the **reticle** (graticule), producing a sharp image.
5. If the unknown lens has **plus or minus power**, the emergent rays are no longer parallel, and the target must be moved along the optical axis until the image is again sharp through the telescope. The amount and direction of target movement is directly proportional to the **back vertex power** of the unknown lens.
6. The **power drum** (or dial) is calibrated in [[diopters]] and indicates the back vertex power of the lens.

### The Badal Principle

The **Badal principle** states that when a lens is placed at the anterior focal point of an auxiliary (standard) lens, the image magnification remains constant regardless of the power of the test lens. This is critical because it ensures that the target image size does not change as different lens powers are measured, allowing consistent and accurate readings. The mathematical relationship is:

$$F_{unknown} = \frac{-x \cdot F_s^2}{1}$$

Where:
- $F_{unknown}$ = back vertex power of the unknown lens
- $x$ = displacement of the target from the focal point of the standard lens
- $F_s$ = power of the standard lens

In practice, the power drum is pre-calibrated so the clinician simply reads the diopter value directly.

### Key Optical Components

| Component | Function |
|---|---|
| **Light source** | Provides illumination (typically a small incandescent bulb, LED, or halogen lamp) |
| **Target (Mires)** | An illuminated pattern (typically a cross of lines and dots, or a corona of dots) that is projected through the optical system |
| **Collimating (Standard) Lens** | A lens of known power (typically +20 D) that collimates light from the target |
| **Lens Rest / Lens Stop** | The platform where the unknown spectacle lens is placed; positioned at the back focal point of the standard lens |
| **Power Drum / Wheel** | A calibrated dial that moves the target along the optical axis; graduated in diopters (typically –20 D to +20 D) |
| **Axis Wheel** | A rotating wheel calibrated in degrees (0°–180°) for measuring the [[cylinder axis]] of astigmatic lenses |
| **Eyepiece Telescope** | A Galilean or Keplerian telescope focused at infinity; the observer views the target through this |
| **Reticle (Graticule)** | A crosshair or grid pattern in the eyepiece used to center the target and measure [[prism]] |
| **Prism Compensator** | A device (typically a pair of rotary prisms, known as **Risley prisms**) used to measure and neutralize prismatic effects |
| **Lens Clamp / Holder** | A spring-loaded mechanism that holds the spectacle lens securely against the lens rest |
| **Ink Marker / Dotting Device** | Three small ink-tipped pins that can be pressed against the lens to mark the optical center and axis |

## Types of Lensometers

### 1. Manual (Optical) Lensometer

The traditional manual lensometer requires the operator to visually focus the target mires and read the power from a calibrated dial. It demands skill and training but provides excellent accuracy in experienced hands.

**Subtypes by target design:**

- **American Cross Target**: Uses a pattern of **three lines** (one set of triple lines and one set of single lines perpendicular to them, plus a circle of dots). The triple lines are focused for one principal meridian and the single lines for the other. This is the most common design in North America.
- **European Dot Target (Corona Target)**: Uses a **ring of dots** that form a circle when the lens is in focus. When a cylindrical component is present, the dots form an ellipse. This design is common in European and British practice.

### 2. Automated (Digital) Lensometer

Automated lensometers use electronic sensors (typically a **CCD array** or **Hartmann-Shack wavefront sensor**) to measure the lens power without requiring the operator to visually focus mires. Results are displayed on an **LCD screen** and can often be printed or transferred electronically to a patient's record.

**Advantages of automated lensometers:**
- Faster measurement
- Reduced operator error
- Ability to measure [[progressive addition lenses]] (PALs) more easily
- Digital storage and transfer of data
- Measurement of UV transmittance in some models
- Automatic detection of lens type (single vision, bifocal, progressive)

**Disadvantages:**
- Higher cost
- Requires calibration and maintenance
- May have difficulty with heavily scratched, tinted, or coated lenses
- Less tactile feedback for the clinician

### 3. Projection Lensometer

A variant of the manual lensometer that projects the target image onto a screen rather than requiring the operator to look through an eyepiece. This is useful for teaching and demonstration purposes.

## Clinical Applications

The lensometer is used in the following clinical scenarios:

### 1. Verification of New Spectacle Lenses

Before dispensing newly fabricated spectacles to a patient, the clinician or optician **must** verify that the lenses match the written [[prescription]]. This includes confirming:

- **Sphere power** (in diopters)
- **Cylinder power** (in diopters)
- **Cylinder axis** (in degrees)
- **Addition power** (for [[bifocal]] and [[progressive lenses]])
- **Prism** (amount in [[prism diopters]] and base direction)
- **Optical center** location (to ensure proper alignment with the patient's [[pupillary distance]])

The **ANSI Z80.1** standard (American National Standards Institute) and the **ISO 8980** standard (International Organization for Standardization) define acceptable tolerances for spectacle lens power and other parameters.

| Parameter | ANSI Z80.1 Tolerance (Example for Sphere ≤ ±6.50 D) |
|---|---|
| Sphere Power | ± 0.13 D |
| Cylinder Power | ± 0.13 D |
| Cylinder Axis (Cyl ≤ 0.50 D) | ± 7° |
| Cylinder Axis (Cyl 0.50–0.75 D) | ± 5° |
| Cylinder Axis (Cyl 0.75–1.50 D) | ± 3° |
| Cylinder Axis (Cyl > 1.50 D) | ± 2° |
| Addition Power | ± 0.12 D |
| Prismatic Effect (Horizontal) | ± 0.33 Δ per lens |
| Prismatic Effect (Vertical) | ± 0.33 Δ per lens |

### 2. Determination of a Patient's Current (Habitual) Prescription

When a patient presents for an eye examination, the clinician routinely measures the power of the patient's existing spectacles using the lensometer. This provides:

- A **starting point** for [[refraction]]
- Documentation of the patient's **habitual correction**
- Identification of potential sources of visual complaints (e.g., incorrect axis, wrong power, decentered optical centers)

### 3. Detection of Lens Errors and Defects

The lensometer can reveal:

- **Incorrect power** (sphere or cylinder)
- **Incorrect axis orientation**
- **Unwanted prism** (due to decentration or fabrication error)
- **Lens warping** or distortion (the mires will appear distorted or unable to be clearly focused)
- **Surface defects** (scratches, pitting) that scatter light and degrade the target image

### 4. Measurement of Prism

The reticle in the lensometer eyepiece is calibrated in **prism diopters** (Δ). When the optical center of a lens is not aligned with the center of the reticle, the displacement of the target indicates the amount and direction of prismatic effect. This is essential for:

- Verifying **prescribed prism**
- Detecting **unwanted prism** due to lens decentration (per [[Prentice's Rule]]: Δ = c × F, where c = decentration in cm and F = lens power in diopters)
- Measuring **vertical and horizontal prism** components separately

### 5. Measurement of Bifocal and Progressive Addition Lenses

For [[bifocal lenses]], the lensometer is used to measure:
1. The **distance portion** power (sphere, cylinder, axis)
2. The **near segment** (add) power — measured by focusing through the near segment and subtracting the distance sphere power

For [[progressive addition lenses]] (PALs), the process is more complex:
1. The **distance reference point** (usually marked or identifiable by temporary engravings) is measured
2. The **near reference point** is measured
3. The **addition power** is calculated as the algebraic difference between the near and distance sphere readings
4. Automated lensometers with PAL mapping capabilities can scan the entire lens surface and display a power map

### 6. Contact Lens Verification

While specialized instruments exist for [[contact lens]] measurement, a lensometer can be used with a **contact lens adapter** (a small concave holder) to measure the back vertex power of rigid gas permeable ([[RGP]]) contact lenses. Soft contact lenses are more difficult to measure on a standard lensometer due to their flexibility and tendency to dehydrate.

### 7. Marking the Optical Center and Axis

The lensometer's **dotting device** (ink marker) allows the clinician to place three dots on the lens surface:
- The **central dot** marks the **optical center**
- The two **peripheral dots** indicate the **axis orientation** of the cylinder (or the 180° meridian for spherical lenses)

These marks are essential for:
- Verifying proper alignment during frame fitting
- Checking [[pupillary distance]] (PD) alignment
- Identifying the axis for edging and mounting

## Step-by-Step Procedure for Using a Manual Lensometer

### Preparation

1. **Adjust the eyepiece**: Before measuring any lens, the operator must focus the eyepiece reticle for their own eye. This is done by:
   - Turning the eyepiece fully counterclockwise (most plus/fogged position)
   - Slowly rotating clockwise until the reticle crosshairs are **first** sharply focused
   - This compensates for the operator's own [[refractive error]] and prevents [[accommodation]] from introducing measurement error

2. **Clean the lens rest**: Ensure the lens stop is clean and free of debris.

3. **Zero the instrument**: Place no lens on the rest and verify that the target is in focus at the zero (plano) reading on the power drum. If not, recalibrate.

### Measurement of a Single Vision Lens

1. Place the spectacle lens on the lens rest with the **back (ocular) surface** against the lens stop. This ensures measurement of **back vertex power**, which is the standard for spectacle prescriptions.
2. Secure the lens with the lens clamp.
3. Rotate the **power drum** until the target lines (mires) come into focus.
4. **For a spherical lens**: All lines (both sets) will focus simultaneously. Read the power from the drum.
5. **For a sphero-cylindrical lens**:
   - Focus the **first set of lines** (e.g., the triple lines on an American-style target). Record this as the **sphere power**.
   - Rotate the **axis wheel** until these lines are aligned with the axis markings.
   - Focus the **second set of lines** (e.g., the single lines). Record this reading.
   - The **cylinder power** = second reading minus first reading.
   - The **axis** is read from the axis wheel.
6. Center the target on the reticle crosshairs to identify the **optical center**. If the target is displaced, the displacement indicates **prism**.
7. Use the **dotting device** to mark the optical center and axis on the lens.

### Measurement of Bifocal/Progressive Add Power

1. Measure the **distance portion** as described above.
2. Move the lens so the **near segment** (or near reference point for PALs) is centered on the lens stop.
3. Focus the mires and read the sphere power through the near segment.
4. **Add power** = Near sphere reading – Distance sphere reading.

## Sources of Error and Troubleshooting

Accurate lensometry requires attention to several potential sources of error:

| Source of Error | Description | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| **Improper eyepiece adjustment** | Failure to properly focus the reticle leads to accommodation-induced errors | Always adjust the eyepiece before each session; focus from plus toward minus, stopping at first clear image |
| **Lens placed incorrectly** | Placing the front surface against the lens stop measures front vertex power instead of back vertex power | Always place the **back (concave/ocular) surface** against the lens stop |
| **Dirty or scratched lens** | Scattering of light degrades the target image | Clean the lens before measurement; note if scratches prevent accurate reading |
| **Tilted lens** | If the lens is not perpendicular to the optical axis, erroneous readings result | Ensure the lens is flat and properly seated on the lens rest |
| **High-powered lenses** | Very high plus or minus lenses may exceed the instrument's range (typically ±20 D) | Use auxiliary lenses or a specialized high-power lensometer |
| **Progressive lenses** | The power varies continuously across the lens surface; no single point gives the full prescription | Use manufacturer's markings to identify distance and near reference points; consider automated lensometer with PAL mapping |
| **Operator accommodation** | Especially in young operators, accommodation can cause the target to appear focused when it is not | Proper eyepiece adjustment; relax accommodation; use the "first clear focus" technique |
| **Instrument miscalibration** | The zero point may drift over time | Regularly verify calibration with a known test lens (e.g., a plano lens or a lens of known power) |

## Relationship to Clinical Diagnosis and Patient Management

While the lensometer itself does not diagnose ocular disease, it plays a critical role in the **diagnostic process** and **patient management**:

### Diagnostic Relevance

- **Unexplained visual complaints**: A patient presenting with [[asthenopia]] (eye strain), [[headache]], [[diplopia]], or blurred vision may have an incorrect spectacle prescription. Lensometry of the habitual spectacles can immediately identify discrepancies between the prescribed and actual lens powers.
- **Anisometropia detection**: Measuring the existing lenses can reveal significant differences in power between the two eyes ([[anisometropia]]), which may contribute to symptoms.
- **Induced prism identification**: Unwanted prismatic effects from decentered lenses can cause [[binocular vision]] dysfunction, including [[heterophoria]] decompensation and diplopia. Lensometry quantifies this prism.
- **Post-surgical assessment**: After [[cataract surgery]] or [[refractive surgery]], the patient's pre-operative spectacles are measured to document the previous correction and guide post-operative management.

### Therapeutic and Management Relevance

- **Prescription verification ensures therapeutic accuracy**: The spectacle prescription is a therapeutic intervention for [[refractive errors]] ([[myopia]], [[hyperopia]], [[astigmatism]], [[presbyopia]]). The lensometer is the quality control instrument that ensures this therapy is delivered correctly.
- **Monitoring progressive conditions**: Serial lensometry of a patient's spectacles over time, compared with refraction findings, can help track the progression of conditions such as [[nuclear sclerotic cataract]] (which causes a myopic shift) or [[keratoconus]] (which causes increasing irregular astigmatism).
- **Occupational and specialty lens verification**: Lensometry is essential for verifying safety glasses (which must meet [[ANSI Z87.1]] impact resistance standards), sports eyewear, and specialty lenses such as those with therapeutic tints for conditions like [[photophobia]] or [[migraine]].

## Automated Lensometer Technology

Modern automated lensometers represent a significant technological advancement. Key technologies include:

### Hartmann-Shack Wavefront Sensing

Some advanced automated lensometers use a **Hartmann-Shack sensor** (an array of microlenses coupled with a CCD camera) to analyze the wavefront of light passing through the spectacle lens. This allows:
- Rapid, accurate measurement of sphere, cylinder, and axis
- Detection of higher-order aberrations in the lens
- Power mapping across the entire lens surface (especially useful for [[progressive addition lenses]])

### CCD/CMOS Array Detection

Most automated lensometers project a pattern of light through the lens onto a **CCD or CMOS sensor array**. Software algorithms analyze the distortion of the pattern to calculate:
- Sphere power
- Cylinder power and axis
- Prism (amount and base direction)
- Addition power
- UV transmittance (in some models)
- Lens type identification (single vision, bifocal, progressive)

### Features of Modern Automated Lensometers

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| **Digital display** | LCD/LED screen showing sphere, cylinder, axis, add, and prism in real time |
| **Auto-detection of lens type** | Software identifies whether the lens is single vision, bifocal, or progressive |
| **PAL mapping** | Scans the progressive lens and displays a color-coded power map |
| **Data transfer** | Results can be transmitted to electronic health records (EHR) or printed |
| **UV measurement** | Measures the percentage of ultraviolet light transmitted through the lens |
| **Abbe number estimation** | Some models estimate the [[Abbe number]] (chromatic dispersion) of the lens material |
| **Built-in printer** | Prints a label with the measured prescription for record-keeping |
| **Bluetooth/Wi-Fi connectivity** | Wireless data transfer to practice management systems |

## Calibration and Maintenance

### Calibration

- The lensometer should be **calibrated regularly** (at minimum, at the start of each clinical day) using a **plano verification lens** or a **lens of known power** supplied by the manufacturer.
- The power drum should read **zero (plano)** when no lens is on the rest and the mires are in focus.
- If the instrument is out of calibration, the manufacturer's instructions should be followed for adjustment, or the instrument should be sent for professional servicing.

### Maintenance

- **Clean the lens rest** and **eyepiece** regularly with appropriate lens cleaning solutions and lint-free cloths.
- **Replace the light source** (bulb or LED) when it dims or burns out.
- **Protect the instrument** from dust, moisture, and physical shock.
- **Ink markers** should be refilled or replaced when the dots become faint.
- Automated lensometers require periodic **software updates** and **sensor calibration** per manufacturer guidelines.

## Clinical Pearls and Best Practices

1. **Always adjust the eyepiece first.** This is the single most common source of error for novice operators.
2. **Always measure back vertex power** by placing the ocular surface of the lens against the lens stop. This is especially critical for high-powered lenses where front and back vertex powers differ significantly.
3. **Record the prescription in standard notation**: Sphere / Cylinder × Axis (e.g., –2.00 / –1.25 × 090). Be consistent with **minus cylinder** or **plus cylinder** convention as per your practice standard.
4. **For progressive lenses**, locate the manufacturer's **hidden engravings** (usually nasal and temporal etch marks at the 180° line) to identify the distance and near reference points.
5. **When measuring prism**, ensure the optical center is centered on the reticle before reading. If prism is present, use the prism compensator (Risley prisms) to neutralize it and read the amount and base direction.
6. **Compare lensometer findings with the written prescription** and with the patient's subjective complaints. Discrepancies may explain symptoms and guide clinical decision-making.
7. **Document lensometer readings** in the patient's chart at every visit. This creates a longitudinal record that is invaluable for tracking refractive changes and verifying dispensing accuracy.

## Summary

The **Lensometer (Focimeter)** is a cornerstone instrument in [[Optometry]], [[Ophthalmology]], and [[Opticianry]]. Its ability to accurately measure the [[back vertex power]], [[cylinder]] axis, [[optical center]], and [[prismatic effect]] of ophthalmic lenses makes it indispensable for prescription verification, patient assessment, and quality assurance. From the classic manual instruments based on the **Badal optometer principle** to modern **automated digital lensometers** with wavefront sensing and PAL mapping capabilities, the focimeter continues to evolve while remaining fundamental to evidence-based eye care practice. Proficiency in lensometry is a non-negotiable clinical competency for all eye care professionals.
