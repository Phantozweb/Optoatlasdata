

# Keratitis

**Keratitis** is an inflammatory condition of the [[cornea]] that represents one of the most clinically significant and potentially sight-threatening disorders encountered in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] practice. Characterized by disruption of the corneal [[epithelium]], [[stroma]], or [[endothelium]], keratitis may arise from infectious or non-infectious etiologies and, if inadequately managed, can progress to corneal ulceration, [[corneal perforation]], [[endophthalmitis]], and irreversible vision loss. Keratitis remains a leading cause of [[corneal blindness]] worldwide, particularly in developing nations, and demands prompt recognition, accurate etiological classification, and aggressive, targeted therapy.

---

## Epidemiology

The global incidence of keratitis varies significantly by geographic region, climate, socioeconomic status, and prevalence of risk factors. In the United States, the annual incidence of microbial keratitis is estimated at approximately 71,000 cases per year, with [[contact lens]] wear being the single most important predisposing factor in developed countries, accounting for up to 65–70% of all infectious keratitis cases. In tropical and agricultural regions of South and Southeast Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, and Central America, ocular trauma—particularly with vegetable matter—is the predominant risk factor, and fungal keratitis constitutes a disproportionately higher percentage of cases (up to 35–50%) compared to temperate climates (< 5–10%).

| **Epidemiological Factor** | **Developed Nations** | **Developing Nations** |
|---|---|---|
| Primary risk factor | [[Contact lens]] wear | Ocular trauma (agricultural) |
| Most common pathogen | *[[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]]*, *[[Staphylococcus aureus]]* | *[[Fusarium]]* spp., *[[Aspergillus]]* spp., *Pseudomonas* |
| Annual incidence (per 100,000) | 11–28 | 113–799 |
| Contact lens–related proportion | 60–70% | 15–25% |
| Fungal keratitis proportion | 5–10% | 30–50% |

The burden of keratitis-related [[corneal opacity]] is substantial: the World Health Organization estimates that corneal disease is the fourth leading cause of blindness globally, with infectious keratitis being a major contributor. Demographic factors including male sex, younger working age, lower socioeconomic status, and rural habitation are associated with increased risk in many populations.

## Anatomy and Relevant Ocular Structures

A thorough understanding of [[corneal anatomy]] is essential for appreciating the pathophysiology and clinical manifestations of keratitis.

The cornea is an avascular, transparent, dome-shaped structure comprising five distinct layers:

1. **[[Corneal epithelium]]** – A stratified, non-keratinized squamous epithelium approximately 50 µm thick, consisting of 5–7 cell layers. It serves as the primary barrier against microbial invasion and is continuously renewed from [[limbal stem cells]].
2. **[[Bowman's layer]]** – An acellular condensation of the anterior stroma (8–14 µm), composed of randomly arranged [[collagen]] fibrils. Once damaged, Bowman's layer does not regenerate, and its disruption may result in permanent scarring.
3. **[[Corneal stroma]]** – Comprising approximately 90% of corneal thickness (~450 µm), the stroma consists of highly organized [[type I collagen]] lamellae, [[keratocytes]], and a proteoglycan-rich extracellular matrix. The precise lamellar arrangement is critical for corneal transparency.
4. **[[Descemet's membrane]]** – A thick basement membrane (10–15 µm in adults) produced by the endothelium, composed primarily of [[type IV collagen]] and [[type VIII collagen]]. It thickens with age and can regenerate after injury.
5. **[[Corneal endothelium]]** – A monolayer of hexagonal cells on the posterior corneal surface responsible for maintaining corneal deturgescence through active fluid transport via Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pumps. Human corneal endothelial cells have extremely limited proliferative capacity.

The cornea is innervated by the [[ophthalmic division]] (V₁) of the [[trigeminal nerve]] via the [[long ciliary nerves]] and [[nasociliary nerve]], making it one of the most densely innervated tissues in the body. This rich innervation accounts for the severe pain associated with keratitis and plays a critical role in the corneal wound healing response through the release of neurotrophic factors such as [[substance P]] and [[nerve growth factor]].

The [[tear film]], composed of lipid, aqueous, and mucin layers, provides essential antimicrobial defense through [[lysozyme]], [[lactoferrin]], [[secretory immunoglobulin A]] (sIgA), [[defensins]], and [[complement]] components. Disruption of the tear film or epithelial barrier is a prerequisite for most forms of infectious keratitis.

## Classification

Keratitis is broadly classified based on etiology, anatomical depth, and clinical morphology.

### By Etiology

| **Category** | **Subtypes** | **Common Causative Agents / Causes** |
|---|---|---|
| **Bacterial keratitis** | Gram-positive, Gram-negative | *[[Staphylococcus aureus]]*, *[[Streptococcus pneumoniae]]*, *[[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]]*, *[[Serratia marcescens]]*, *[[Moraxella]]* spp. |
| **Fungal keratitis** ([[Keratomycosis]]) | Filamentous, Yeast | *[[Fusarium]]* spp., *[[Aspergillus]]* spp., *[[Candida]]* spp., *[[Curvularia]]* spp. |
| **Viral keratitis** | Epithelial, Stromal, Endothelial | [[Herpes simplex virus]] (HSV-1, HSV-2), [[Varicella-zoster virus]] (VZV), [[Adenovirus]], [[Cytomegalovirus]] (CMV) |
| **Acanthamoeba keratitis** | — | *[[Acanthamoeba]]* spp. (trophozoites and cysts) |
| **Parasitic keratitis** | — | *[[Onchocerca volvulus]]*, *[[Microsporidium]]* spp. |
| **Non-infectious keratitis** | Autoimmune, Neurotrophic, Exposure, Toxic | [[Peripheral ulcerative keratitis]] (PUK), [[Mooren's ulcer]], [[neurotrophic keratitis]], [[exposure keratopathy]], medicamentosa |
| **Interstitial keratitis** | Immune-mediated stromal inflammation | [[Syphilis]] (congenital/acquired), [[Tuberculosis]], [[Cogan syndrome]], [[Lyme disease]] |

### By Anatomical Depth

- **Superficial keratitis** – Involvement limited to the [[corneal epithelium]] (e.g., [[superficial punctate keratitis]], epithelial [[dendrite]])
- **Stromal keratitis** – Inflammation within the [[corneal stroma]] (e.g., [[herpes stromal keratitis]], [[interstitial keratitis]], deep fungal infiltrate)
- **Endothelial keratitis** – Inflammation targeting the [[corneal endothelium]] (e.g., [[CMV endotheliitis]], [[herpes simplex endotheliitis]])

### By Location

- **Central keratitis** – Involving the visual axis; carries the highest risk of visual morbidity
- **Paracentral keratitis** – Adjacent to the visual axis
- **Peripheral keratitis** – Located in the peripheral cornea; may be associated with systemic autoimmune conditions such as [[rheumatoid arthritis]], [[granulomatosis with polyangiitis]], or [[systemic lupus erythematosus]]

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of keratitis is multifactorial and varies according to the causative agent, but several unifying principles govern the initiation and progression of corneal inflammation.

### Breach of the Epithelial Barrier

Under normal physiological conditions, the intact [[corneal epithelium]], supported by the [[tear film]] and its antimicrobial constituents, provides a formidable barrier against microbial invasion. Keratitis typically requires a predisposing breach in this barrier, which may result from:

- **Mechanical trauma** (corneal abrasion, foreign body, contact lens–induced microtrauma)
- **Contact lens wear** (particularly extended/overnight wear, which causes [[hypoxia]], epithelial thinning, and altered tear film dynamics)
- **[[Dry eye disease]]** ([[keratoconjunctivitis sicca]]) leading to epithelial desiccation and breakdown
- **[[Neurotrophic keratopathy]]** (reduced corneal sensation from [[trigeminal nerve]] damage, post-[[LASIK]], [[diabetes mellitus]])
- **Eyelid abnormalities** ([[lagophthalmos]], [[entropion]], [[trichiasis]])
- **Topical medication toxicity** (preservatives such as [[benzalkonium chloride]])
- **Prior ocular surgery** (e.g., [[penetrating keratoplasty]], [[refractive surgery]])

### Microbial Adherence and Invasion

Once the epithelial barrier is compromised, microbial organisms adhere to exposed [[basement membrane]] components and stromal [[glycosaminoglycans]]. Bacterial adhesins, fungal hyphae, and Acanthamoeba trophozoites exploit specific receptor-ligand interactions:

- **Bacterial keratitis**: *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* utilizes [[pili]], [[flagella]], and [[lipopolysaccharide]] (LPS) to adhere to injured epithelium. It produces [[exotoxin A]], [[elastase]], [[alkaline protease]], and [[phospholipase C]], which cause rapid tissue necrosis. Gram-positive organisms such as *Staphylococcus aureus* produce [[alpha-toxin]], [[protein A]], and various [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs).
- **Fungal keratitis**: Filamentous fungi penetrate the stroma via hyphal extension, often crossing intact [[Descemet's membrane]]. They produce [[mycotoxins]] and [[proteases]] that facilitate deep tissue invasion. The inflammatory response is often more indolent than bacterial keratitis.
- **Viral keratitis (HSV)**: [[Herpes simplex virus]] establishes latency in the [[trigeminal ganglion]] after primary infection. Reactivation leads to anterograde axonal transport to the cornea, causing epithelial (dendritic/geographic ulcer), stromal (immune-mediated [[necrotizing stromal keratitis]] or non-necrotizing [[immune stromal keratitis]]), or endothelial disease. Stromal disease is primarily driven by [[CD4+ T-cell]]–mediated [[delayed-type hypersensitivity]] rather than direct viral cytopathic effect.
- **Acanthamoeba keratitis**: Trophozoites adhere to [[mannose]]-binding protein on the corneal surface, release [[serine proteases]] and [[metalloproteinases]], and cause progressive stromal necrosis. Encystation into highly resistant double-walled cysts makes eradication extremely challenging.

### Inflammatory Cascade

Microbial invasion triggers a robust innate immune response:

1. **Pattern recognition**: [[Toll-like receptors]] (TLRs) on corneal epithelial cells and resident [[keratocytes]] recognize [[pathogen-associated molecular patterns]] (PAMPs), including bacterial LPS (TLR4), peptidoglycan (TLR2), flagellin (TLR5), and viral double-stranded RNA (TLR3).
2. **Cytokine and chemokine release**: Activation of [[NF-κB]] and [[MAPK]] signaling pathways leads to production of [[interleukin-1]] (IL-1), [[interleukin-6]] (IL-6), [[interleukin-8]] (IL-8/CXCL8), [[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] (TNF-α), and [[macrophage inflammatory protein-2]] (MIP-2).
3. **Neutrophil recruitment**: Chemokines attract [[polymorphonuclear leukocytes]] (PMNs) from the [[limbal vasculature]] into the avascular cornea. While essential for microbial killing, neutrophil-derived [[reactive oxygen species]] (ROS), [[myeloperoxidase]], and [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (particularly [[MMP-9]] and [[MMP-2]]) cause collateral stromal destruction, [[collagen]] degradation, and corneal thinning—a process termed **sterile stromal melting**.
4. **Adaptive immunity**: In chronic or recurrent keratitis (particularly HSV stromal keratitis), [[CD4+ T helper cells]], [[regulatory T cells]], and [[antigen-presenting cells]] (including [[Langerhans cells]] that migrate from the limbus) orchestrate a sustained immune response that can perpetuate tissue damage even after pathogen clearance.

### Corneal Neovascularization and Scarring

Prolonged inflammation stimulates the release of pro-angiogenic factors, particularly [[vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF) and [[basic fibroblast growth factor]] (bFGF), leading to [[corneal neovascularization]] from the [[limbal arcade]]. Activated keratocytes transform into [[myofibroblasts]], depositing disorganized collagen and producing [[corneal scarring]] ([[corneal opacity]]) that permanently reduces visual acuity.

## Risk Factors

| **Risk Factor** | **Mechanism / Association** |
|---|---|
| [[Contact lens]] wear (especially extended wear, poor hygiene) | Epithelial hypoxia, microtrauma, biofilm formation, contaminated lens cases |
| Ocular surface disease ([[dry eye]], [[blepharitis]]) | Compromised tear film defense, epithelial breakdown |
| Ocular trauma (agricultural, industrial) | Direct epithelial breach; vegetable matter predisposes to fungal keratitis |
| Prior ocular surgery ([[LASIK]], [[PRK]], [[corneal transplant]]) | Epithelial defects, reduced corneal sensation, suture-related infection |
| [[Immunosuppression]] (topical [[corticosteroids]], systemic immunosuppressants, [[HIV/AIDS]]) | Impaired immune surveillance, reactivation of latent viruses |
| [[Diabetes mellitus]] | [[Neurotrophic keratopathy]], impaired wound healing, altered tear composition |
| [[Vitamin A deficiency]] ([[xerophthalmia]]) | Epithelial keratinization, tear film instability |
| Eyelid abnormalities ([[lagophthalmos]], [[Bell's palsy]], [[ectropion]]) | [[Exposure keratopathy]], incomplete lid closure |
| Contaminated water exposure (hot tubs, swimming pools) | *Acanthamoeba*, *Pseudomonas* exposure |
| Chronic topical medication use | [[Preservative toxicity]], epithelial disruption |

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

Patients with keratitis typically present with an acute or subacute onset of:

- **Ocular pain** – Often severe and disproportionate to clinical findings, particularly in Acanthamoeba keratitis; related to corneal nerve stimulation
- **[[Photophobia]]** – Due to ciliary spasm and anterior segment inflammation
- **Tearing** ([[epiphora]]) – Reflex lacrimation from corneal nerve stimulation
- **Blurred vision** – From corneal edema, infiltrate in the visual axis, or associated [[anterior uveitis]]
- **Foreign body sensation** – Especially with epithelial defects
- **Redness** ([[conjunctival injection]], [[ciliary flush]]) – Particularly perilimbal (ciliary) injection indicating deeper inflammation
- **Mucopurulent discharge** – More prominent in bacterial keratitis; may be minimal in viral or Acanthamoeba keratitis
- **Blepharospasm** – Involuntary eyelid closure secondary to pain

### Signs

Clinical signs vary by etiology but commonly include:

| **Sign** | **Description** | **Etiological Significance** |
|---|---|---|
| Corneal infiltrate | Focal or multifocal area of stromal opacification composed of inflammatory cells and necrotic debris | Present in all forms of infectious keratitis |
| [[Corneal ulcer]] (epithelial defect overlying infiltrate) | Stains with [[fluorescein]] under [[cobalt blue light]] | Indicates active infection with tissue loss |
| [[Hypopyon]] | Layered collection of inflammatory cells in the [[anterior chamber]] | Suggests severe infection; common in bacterial and fungal keratitis |
| [[Anterior chamber reaction]] (cells and flare) | Inflammatory spillover into the aqueous humor | Indicates associated [[anterior uveitis]] |
| [[Corneal edema]] | Stromal and/or epithelial swelling | Endothelial dysfunction or stromal inflammation |
| [[Descemet's folds]] | Stress lines in Descemet's membrane | Stromal edema, inflammation |
| [[Corneal neovascularization]] | Ingrowth of blood vessels from the limbus | Chronic or recurrent keratitis |
| Satellite lesions | Small infiltrates surrounding the main lesion | Highly suggestive of **fungal keratitis** |
| Feathery/serrated margins | Irregular, branching borders of the infiltrate | Characteristic of **filamentous fungal keratitis** |
| [[Dendrite]] (true dendritic ulcer) | Branching epithelial ulcer with terminal bulbs, staining with fluorescein and [[rose bengal]] | Pathognomonic for **HSV epithelial keratitis** |
| [[Ring infiltrate]] (Wessely ring) | Ring-shaped stromal infiltrate | **Acanthamoeba keratitis** (also immune ring in HSV) |
| [[Radial keratoneuritis]] ([[perineural infiltrates]]) | Linear, radial infiltrates along corneal nerves | Highly characteristic of **Acanthamoeba keratitis** |
| [[Lid edema]] and [[conjunctival chemosis]] | Periocular swelling | Severe infection, associated inflammation |

### Etiology-Specific Clinical Features

#### Bacterial Keratitis
- Rapid onset (hours to 1–2 days)
- Well-demarcated, round/oval, suppurative stromal infiltrate
- Significant mucopurulent discharge
- Marked anterior chamber reaction; hypopyon common
- *Pseudomonas* keratitis: rapidly progressive, "ring abscess," greenish discharge, stromal melting
- *Staphylococcal* keratitis: well-circumscribed, white-yellow infiltrate
- *Streptococcal* keratitis: aggressive, may cause crystalline keratopathy (in immunocompromised)

#### Fungal Keratitis (Keratomycosis)
- Insidious onset (days to weeks)
- Dry, raised, gray-white infiltrate with feathery/irregular margins
- Satellite lesions (pathognomonic)
- Endothelial plaque (Descemet's membrane deposits)
- Hypopyon may be present but often immobile (thick, organized)
- History of trauma with vegetable matter or organic material
- Worsening despite antibacterial therapy

#### Herpes Simplex Keratitis
- **Epithelial keratitis**: Classic [[dendritic ulcer]] with terminal bulbs; geographic ulcer in advanced cases
- **Stromal keratitis (non-necrotizing/immune)**: Disc-shaped stromal edema ([[disciform keratitis]]), intact epithelium, [[keratic precipitates]] on endothelium, Wessely immune ring
- **Stromal keratitis (necrotizing)**: Dense stromal infiltration with overlying epithelial defect, severe inflammation, high risk of perforation
- **Endotheliitis**: Linear or sectoral [[keratic precipitates]], overlying stromal and epithelial edema, mild anterior chamber reaction
- Reduced [[corneal sensation]] ([[corneal hypoesthesia]]) is a hallmark finding

#### Acanthamoeba Keratitis
- Early: [[Pseudodendrite]] (may mimic HSV), diffuse punctate epitheliopathy
- Intermediate: Radial keratoneuritis (perineural infiltrates), ring infiltrate
- Late: Dense ring abscess, severe stromal necrosis, [[scleritis]]
- Severe, disproportionate pain (out of proportion to clinical signs)
- Strong association with contact lens wear and water exposure
- Often initially misdiagnosed as HSV or bacterial keratitis

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

A systematic ophthalmic examination is essential:

1. **Visual acuity** ([[Snellen chart]] or [[LogMAR]])
2. **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]** – The cornerstone of diagnosis; allows detailed evaluation of:
   - Size, shape, depth, and location of the infiltrate/ulcer
   - Epithelial defect (with [[fluorescein]] staining under cobalt blue filter)
   - Stromal thinning or melting
   - Anterior chamber reaction (cells, flare, hypopyon)
   - Endothelial deposits (keratic precipitates, plaques)
3. **[[Corneal sensation testing]]** – Using a cotton wisp or [[Cochet-Bonnet esthesiometer]]; reduced sensation suggests HSV keratitis or neurotrophic etiology
4. **[[Eyelid examination]]** – Evaluate for [[blepharitis]], [[meibomian gland dysfunction]], [[lagophthalmos]], [[entropion]], [[trichiasis]]
5. **[[Intraocular pressure]]** measurement – May be elevated (trabeculitis in HSV) or low (ciliary body shutdown in severe inflammation)

### Microbiological Investigation

Corneal scraping for microbiological analysis is indicated for:
- Moderate to severe infiltrates (> 2 mm, central, deep)
- Infiltrates unresponsive to empirical therapy
- Atypical clinical features
- Suspected fungal, Acanthamoeba, or mycobacterial infection

#### Corneal Scraping Technique
- Performed under [[slit-lamp]] magnification using a sterile [[Kimura spatula]], #15 Bard-Parker blade, or 25-gauge needle
- Multiple samples obtained from the leading edge and base of the ulcer
- Specimens inoculated directly onto culture media and glass slides

#### Laboratory Investigations

| **Test** | **Purpose** | **Details** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Gram stain]] | Rapid identification of bacteria (Gram-positive vs. Gram-negative), fungi | Results available within 1 hour |
| [[Giemsa stain]] | Identification of Acanthamoeba cysts/trophozoites, fungi, intracellular organisms | Highlights cellular morphology |
| [[KOH wet mount]] (10% potassium hydroxide) | Rapid detection of fungal hyphae | Dissolves cellular debris, highlights fungal elements |
| [[Calcofluor white]] stain | Fluorescent staining of fungal cell walls and Acanthamoeba cysts | Requires fluorescence microscopy; binds chitin and cellulose |
| [[Lactophenol cotton blue]] | Identification of fungal species morphology | Used on culture isolates |
| [[Acridine orange]] stain | Detection of bacteria, fungi, Acanthamoeba | Fluorescent nucleic acid stain |
| **Culture media** | | |
| [[Blood agar]] | General bacterial culture | Supports most aerobic bacteria |
| [[Chocolate agar]] | Fastidious organisms (*Haemophilus*, *Neisseria*, *Moraxella*) | Enriched medium |
| [[Sabouraud dextrose agar]] | Fungal culture | Incubated at 25–30°C for up to 3 weeks |
| [[Brain-heart infusion broth]] | Enrichment for bacteria and fungi | Liquid medium |
| [[Non-nutrient agar with *E. coli* overlay]] | Acanthamoeba culture | Gold standard for Acanthamoeba isolation |
| [[Thioglycolate broth]] | Anaerobic and microaerophilic organisms | Liquid enrichment medium |
| [[Löwenstein-Jensen medium]] | Mycobacteria (atypical mycobacterial keratitis) | Incubated for 6–8 weeks |

#### Advanced Diagnostic Modalities

- **[[Polymerase chain reaction]] (PCR)**: Highly sensitive and specific for HSV, VZV, CMV, Acanthamoeba, fungi, and atypical mycobacteria. Particularly valuable when cultures are negative or when small tissue samples are available. Multiplex PCR panels can simultaneously detect multiple pathogens.
- **[[Confocal microscopy]] (in vivo)**: Non-invasive, real-time imaging of the cornea at the cellular level. Invaluable for detecting Acanthamoeba cysts (bright, round, double-walled structures 15–25 µm) and fungal hyphae (hyper-reflective, branching filaments). Sensitivity for Acanthamoeba ranges from 80–95% in experienced hands.
- **[[Anterior segment optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT)**: Provides cross-sectional imaging of the cornea, allowing assessment of infiltrate depth, stromal thinning, and Descemet's membrane integrity. Useful for surgical planning and monitoring treatment response.
- **[[Corneal biopsy]]**: Reserved for cases unresponsive to therapy with negative cultures and smears. A partial-thickness or full-thickness corneal biopsy is obtained and submitted for histopathology, special stains (PAS, GMS, AFB), and culture.

## Differential Diagnosis

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| [[Sterile corneal infiltrate]] (contact lens–related) | Small (< 1–2 mm), peripheral, minimal anterior chamber reaction, no epithelial defect or mild, often bilateral |
| [[Corneal foreign body]] | History of trauma, visible foreign body, rust ring |
| [[Corneal abrasion]] | Epithelial defect without infiltrate, clear history of trauma |
| [[Phlyctenular keratoconjunctivitis]] | Nodular lesion at limbus, associated with [[tuberculosis]] or [[staphylococcal hypersensitivity]] |
| [[Thygeson's superficial punctate keratitis]] | Bilateral, coarse, elevated epithelial lesions, no stromal involvement, chronic relapsing course |
| [[Salzmann's nodular degeneration]] | Elevated, bluish-white subepithelial nodules, non-inflammatory |
| [[Corneal dystrophy]] (e.g., [[epithelial basement membrane dystrophy]]) | Bilateral, non-inflammatory, map-dot-fingerprint pattern |

## Management

### General Principles

- **Urgent referral**: All suspected infectious keratitis cases warrant same-day or next-day evaluation by an eye care professional experienced in corneal disease.
- **Contact lens discontinuation**: Immediate cessation of contact lens wear; the lens and case should be submitted for culture.
- **Frequent monitoring**: Daily follow-up initially for moderate-to-severe cases until clinical improvement is documented.
- **Documentation**: Detailed slit-lamp photography and measurement of infiltrate/ulcer size (in mm) at each visit to track progression or resolution.

### Pharmacological Treatment

#### Bacterial Keratitis

**Empirical therapy** is initiated immediately after corneal scraping (or empirically if scraping is deferred for mild cases) and adjusted based on culture and sensitivity results.

| **Severity** | **Recommended Empirical Regimen** |
|---|---|
| Mild (< 2 mm, peripheral, non–sight-threatening) | [[Fluoroquinolone]] monotherapy: [[moxifloxacin]] 0.5% or [[gatifloxacin]] 0.3% q1h while awake |
| Moderate to severe (> 2 mm, central, deep, or with hypopyon) | **Fortified antibiotics** (dual therapy): [[Fortified cefazolin]] (50 mg/mL) or [[fortified vancomycin]] (25–50 mg/mL) alternating with [[fortified tobramycin]] (14 mg/mL) or [[fortified gentamicin]] (14 mg/mL), each q1h around the clock initially |

- **Loading dose**: One drop every 5 minutes for 5 doses, then q1h
- **Tapering**: Gradual reduction in frequency based on clinical response (typically after 48–72 hours of improvement)
- **Adjunctive [[cycloplegic]] agents**: [[Atropine]] 1% or [[cyclopentolate]] 1% BID-TID to reduce ciliary spasm, pain, and prevent [[posterior synechiae]]
- **Avoid topical [[corticosteroids]]** in the initial phase; may be considered cautiously after 48–72 hours of appropriate antimicrobial therapy with documented clinical improvement and confirmed bacterial etiology (controversial; guided by the Steroids for Corneal Ulcers Trial [SCUT] data)

#### Fungal Keratitis

Fungal keratitis requires prolonged, aggressive antifungal therapy due to the organism's ability to penetrate deep into the stroma and across Descemet's membrane.

| **Agent** | **Concentration** | **Route** | **Spectrum** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Natamycin]] 5% suspension | 5% (50 mg/mL) | Topical | First-line for **filamentous fungi** (*Fusarium*, *Aspergillus*) |
| [[Voriconazole]] | 1% topical; 200 mg PO BID; intrastromal (50 µg/0.1 mL) | Topical, oral, intrastromal injection | Broad-spectrum; filamentous and yeast |
| [[Amphotericin B]] | 0.15–0.25% topical; intrastromal (5–10 µg/0.1 mL) | Topical, intrastromal injection | First-line for **yeast** (*Candida*); also covers some filamentous fungi |
| [[Fluconazole]] | 0.2% topical; 200–400 mg PO daily | Topical, oral | Primarily *Candida* spp. |
| [[Itraconazole]] | 200 mg PO BID | Oral | Adjunctive for filamentous fungi |

- Treatment duration: Typically **6–8 weeks minimum**, often longer
- Epithelial debridement may enhance antifungal penetration
- **Avoid topical corticosteroids** – Corticosteroids are generally **contraindicated** in fungal keratitis as they worsen infection
- Intrastromal injection of voriconazole or amphotericin B is increasingly used for deep or refractory cases

#### Herpes Simplex Keratitis

| **Type** | **Treatment** |
|---|---|
| **Epithelial keratitis** (dendritic/geographic) | Topical [[ganciclovir]] 0.15% gel 5×/day OR oral [[acyclovir]] 400 mg 5×/day OR oral [[valacyclovir]] 500 mg TID for 7–14 days. Epithelial debridement may be adjunctive. **Avoid topical corticosteroids.** |
| **Stromal keratitis (immune/non-necrotizing)** | Topical [[prednisolone acetate]] 1% (tapered slowly over weeks to months) WITH concurrent antiviral prophylaxis: oral acyclovir 400 mg BID or valacyclovir 500 mg BID |
| **Necrotizing stromal keratitis** | Aggressive topical corticosteroids with full-dose oral antiviral (acyclovir 400–800 mg 5×/day or valacyclovir 500 mg–1 g TID) |
| **Endotheliitis** | Topical corticosteroids with oral antiviral coverage |
| **Prophylaxis against recurrence** | Long-term oral acyclovir 400 mg BID or valacyclovir 500 mg QD (per the [[Herpetic Eye Disease Study]] [HEDS]) reduces recurrence by ~50% |

#### Acanthamoeba Keratitis

Treatment is prolonged (months) and requires dual anti-amoebic therapy targeting both trophozoites and cysts:

- **[[Polyhexamethylene biguanide]] (PHMB) 0.02%** – First-line cysticidal agent; q1h initially, tapered over months
- **[[Chlorhexidine]] 0.02%** – Alternative or adjunctive biguanide
- **[[Propamidine isethionate]] 0.1% (Brolene)** – Diamidine; used in combination with biguanide
- **[[Hexamidine]] 0.1%** – Alternative diamidine
- Oral [[voriconazole]] or [[miltefosine]] may be considered in refractory cases
- **Topical corticosteroids**: Controversial; may be used cautiously in the later stages to control inflammation, but risk promoting cyst survival
- Treatment duration: **3–6 months minimum**, often 12 months or longer
- Pain management is critical (oral NSAIDs, cycloplegics)

### Surgical Management

Surgical intervention is indicated when medical therapy fails or complications arise:

| **Procedure** | **Indication** |
|---|---|
| **[[Therapeutic penetrating keratoplasty]]** (TPK) | Corneal perforation, impending perforation, progressive infection unresponsive to maximal medical therapy |
| **[[Corneal gluing]]** (cyanoacrylate tissue adhesive) | Small perforations (< 2–3 mm), descemetocele |
| **[[Amniotic membrane transplantation]]** | Persistent epithelial defect, adjunctive to medical therapy, promotes healing |
| **[[Conjunctival flap]]** (Gundersen flap) | Chronic non-healing ulcer, pain management in eyes with poor visual prognosis |
| **[[Corneal cross-linking]]** (CXL) – PACK-CXL | Adjunctive antimicrobial effect via photoactivated riboflavin/UVA; emerging evidence for bacterial and fungal keratitis |
| **[[Intrastromal injection]]** | Delivery of antifungals (voriconazole, amphotericin B) or antibiotics directly into the stroma for deep infections |
| **[[Optical penetrating keratoplasty]]** or [[Deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]] (DALK) | Visual rehabilitation after resolution of infection, for residual corneal scarring |
| **[[Evisceration]] or [[enucleation]]** | End-stage, painful, blind eye with uncontrollable infection or endophthalmitis |

## Complications

Keratitis, particularly when diagnosis or treatment is delayed, can lead to numerous serious complications:

- **[[Corneal scarring]]** ([[corneal opacity]], [[leukoma]]) – The most common sequela; may require [[keratoplasty]] for visual rehabilitation
- **[[Corneal thinning]] and [[descemetocele]]** – Stromal melting with herniation of Descemet's membrane
- **[[Corneal perforation]]** – Ophthalmic emergency requiring urgent surgical intervention
- **[[Secondary glaucoma]]** – From trabecular meshwork obstruction by inflammatory debris, peripheral anterior synechiae, or steroid response
- **[[Anterior uveitis]]** and [[posterior synechiae]] – Inflammatory spillover into the anterior chamber
- **[[Endophthalmitis]]** – Intraocular spread of infection; devastating visual prognosis
- **[[Corneal neovascularization]]** – Compromises future graft survival
- **[[Irregular astigmatism]]** – From corneal scarring and surface irregularity
- **[[Phthisis bulbi]]** – End-stage globe shrinkage from chronic inflammation

## Prognosis

Prognosis depends on the causative organism, severity at presentation, location of the infiltrate relative to the visual axis, time to initiation of appropriate therapy, and host factors.

- **Bacterial keratitis**: Generally favorable with prompt, appropriate therapy. Central ulcers and *Pseudomonas* infections carry worse visual prognosis. The SCUT trial demonstrated that adjunctive topical corticosteroids may improve outcomes in certain subgroups (central ulcers, non-*Nocardia* infections).
- **Fungal keratitis**: Poorer prognosis than bacterial keratitis overall; higher rates of treatment failure, perforation, and need for surgical intervention. *Fusarium* keratitis tends to respond better to natamycin than *Aspergillus*.
- **HSV keratitis**: Epithelial disease generally resolves well; stromal disease carries risk of recurrence and progressive scarring with each episode. Long-term antiviral prophylaxis significantly reduces recurrence.
- **Acanthamoeba keratitis**: Prognosis is highly dependent on early diagnosis. Early-stage disease (before ring infiltrate formation) has significantly better outcomes. Late-stage disease often requires keratoplasty.

---

## Prevention

- **Contact lens hygiene education**: Proper hand washing, daily lens case cleaning and replacement, avoidance of water exposure while wearing lenses, adherence to recommended replacement schedules, avoidance of overnight wear
- **Protective eyewear**: In agricultural and industrial settings to prevent traumatic keratitis
- **Management of ocular surface disease**: Aggressive treatment of [[dry eye disease]], [[blepharitis]], and [[meibomian gland dysfunction]]
- **Antiviral prophylaxis**: Long-term oral acyclovir or valacyclovir for patients with recurrent HSV keratitis
- **Patient education**: Prompt presentation for any symptoms of pain, redness, photophobia, or vision change in contact lens wearers or at-risk individuals
