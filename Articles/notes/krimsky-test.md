# Krimsky Test

---

The **Krimsky Test** (also referred to as the **Krimsky Prism Reflex Test** or **Modified Krimsky Test**) is a clinical method of measuring [[ocular alignment]] by neutralizing the displacement of the [[corneal light reflex]] using handheld [[prisms]]. Originally described by the American ophthalmologist **Dr. Emanuel Krimsky** in 1943, this test is a cornerstone objective technique in the assessment of [[strabismus]], particularly in patients who are unable to cooperate with subjective tests such as the [[cover test]] or the [[prism and alternate cover test]]. The Krimsky Test is especially valuable in the evaluation of infants, young children, patients with [[nystagmus]], patients with poor [[visual acuity]], and individuals with limited [[fixation]] ability. It belongs to the broader family of [[corneal light reflex tests]], which also includes the [[Hirschberg test]] and the [[Brückner test]].

---

## Historical Background

The Krimsky Test was introduced by **Emanuel Krimsky, MD**, who published his seminal work on the quantification of strabismus using prisms placed before the eye to center the corneal light reflex. Prior to Krimsky's contribution, clinicians relied primarily on the [[Hirschberg test]], which estimates the angle of deviation based on the observed displacement of the corneal reflex without the use of prisms. While the Hirschberg test provides a useful estimation (approximately 22 [[prism diopters]] per millimeter of decentration), it is inherently limited in precision. Krimsky refined this approach by introducing prisms of increasing power until the corneal reflex was symmetrically centered in both eyes, thereby providing a more quantitative and reproducible measurement. Over the decades, the Krimsky Test has become an indispensable tool in pediatric ophthalmology and strabismology, and it remains widely taught in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] training programs worldwide.

---

## Pathophysiology of Strabismus Relevant to the Krimsky Test

Understanding the pathophysiology of [[strabismus]] is essential for appreciating the clinical utility of the Krimsky Test. Strabismus refers to any misalignment of the [[visual axes]] of the two eyes, resulting in a deviation that may be horizontal ([[esotropia]], [[exotropia]]), vertical ([[hypertropia]], [[hypotropia]]), or torsional ([[cyclotropia]]). The underlying pathophysiology may involve:

- **Innervational abnormalities**: Disruption of the neural signals from the [[cranial nerves]] (particularly [[cranial nerve III]], [[cranial nerve IV]], and [[cranial nerve VI]]) to the [[extraocular muscles]] can produce paretic or paralytic strabismus.
- **Mechanical restrictions**: Conditions such as [[thyroid eye disease]], [[orbital fractures]], or congenital fibrosis of the [[extraocular muscles]] may physically restrict ocular motility.
- **Sensory deprivation**: Reduced visual input from one eye (e.g., due to [[cataract]], [[corneal opacity]], or [[anisometropia]]) can lead to [[sensory strabismus]], where the brain fails to maintain binocular alignment.
- **Accommodative factors**: Excessive [[accommodation]] relative to [[convergence]] (high [[AC/A ratio]]) can produce [[accommodative esotropia]].
- **Supranuclear and internuclear pathology**: Lesions affecting the [[medial longitudinal fasciculus]], [[paramedian pontine reticular formation]], or other brainstem structures can produce complex patterns of misalignment.

In all of these scenarios, the corneal light reflex will be displaced from its expected centered position in the misaligned eye. The Krimsky Test exploits this displacement by using prisms to optically redirect the light reflex back to the centered position, thereby quantifying the magnitude of the deviation.

### The Corneal Light Reflex and Angle Kappa

A critical concept in interpreting the Krimsky Test is [[angle kappa]] (sometimes referred to as [[angle lambda]]). Angle kappa is the angle between the [[visual axis]] and the [[pupillary axis]]. In most individuals, the [[fovea]] is located slightly temporal to the posterior pole of the eye, resulting in a positive angle kappa where the corneal reflex appears slightly nasal to the center of the [[pupil]]. A large positive angle kappa can simulate [[exotropia]] (termed [[pseudoexotropia]]), while a negative angle kappa can simulate [[esotropia]] (termed [[pseudoesotropia]]). The clinician must account for angle kappa when interpreting the symmetry of corneal reflexes during the Krimsky Test to avoid misdiagnosis.

## Clinical Indications

The Krimsky Test is indicated in a variety of clinical scenarios where objective measurement of ocular deviation is required and subjective cooperation is limited or impossible. Key indications include:

| **Indication** | **Clinical Rationale** |
|---|---|
| Infants and preverbal children | Unable to cooperate with [[cover test]] or subjective prism measurements |
| Patients with [[nystagmus]] | Constant eye movement makes cover testing unreliable |
| Patients with very poor visual acuity | Inability to fixate on a target for cover testing |
| Patients with dense [[amblyopia]] | May have eccentric fixation, making cover test interpretation difficult |
| Patients with [[sensory strabismus]] | Monocular vision loss (e.g., from [[phthisis bulbi]], [[optic atrophy]], or dense media opacity) |
| Uncooperative or cognitively impaired patients | Limited ability to follow instructions for subjective testing |
| Confirmation of [[Hirschberg test]] findings | Provides a more precise quantification of the estimated deviation |
| Intraoperative strabismus measurement | Used during [[strabismus surgery]] to assess alignment under anesthesia |

## Symptoms and Clinical Presentation Prompting the Krimsky Test

Patients who undergo the Krimsky Test typically present with signs and symptoms suggestive of [[ocular misalignment]]. While the Krimsky Test itself is a diagnostic tool rather than a condition, the clinical presentations that prompt its use include:

- **Visible ocular deviation**: Parents or caregivers may notice that one eye appears to turn inward ([[esotropia]]), outward ([[exotropia]]), upward ([[hypertropia]]), or downward ([[hypotropia]]).
- **[[Diplopia]]**: In older, verbal patients with acquired strabismus, double vision may be the chief complaint.
- **[[Amblyopia]]**: Reduced vision in one eye secondary to chronic misalignment.
- **Abnormal [[head posture]]**: Patients may adopt a compensatory head tilt, turn, or chin elevation/depression to minimize diplopia or to use a field of gaze where alignment is better.
- **[[Asthenopia]]**: Eye strain, headaches, and visual fatigue, particularly in patients with intermittent deviations.
- **Photophobia or squinting**: Some patients with intermittent [[exotropia]] may close one eye in bright light.
- **Cosmetic concern**: Even in the absence of visual symptoms, the appearance of misaligned eyes may prompt evaluation.

## Diagnostic Technique: Performing the Krimsky Test

### Equipment Required

- A penlight or transilluminator (point light source)
- A set of handheld [[prism bars]] or loose [[prisms]] (ranging from 1Δ to 45Δ or higher)
- A fixation target (optional, for cooperative patients)

### Standard Krimsky Test Procedure

The standard Krimsky Test is performed as follows:

1. **Patient positioning**: The patient is seated comfortably, and the examiner is positioned directly in front of the patient at a distance of approximately **33 cm (near) or 6 meters (distance)**, depending on the clinical question.

2. **Light presentation**: A penlight is held at the testing distance, aligned with the examiner's line of sight, and directed toward the patient's eyes. The patient is instructed to look at the light (or, in the case of infants, the light is used to attract fixation).

3. **Observation of corneal reflexes**: The examiner observes the position of the [[corneal light reflex]] (also known as the **first Purkinje image**) in each eye. In a patient with orthophoria (normal alignment), the reflexes should appear symmetrically positioned in both pupils, typically slightly nasal to the pupil center due to the positive [[angle kappa]].

4. **Identification of the deviating eye**: If the reflexes are asymmetric, the eye with the displaced reflex is identified as the deviating eye.

5. **Prism placement**: A prism is placed **before the fixating eye** (in the standard Krimsky technique) with the **base oriented in the direction of the deviation** of the non-fixating eye:
   - For **[[esotropia]]** (inward deviation): Place the prism **base-out** before the fixating eye.
   - For **[[exotropia]]** (outward deviation): Place the prism **base-in** before the fixating eye.
   - For **[[hypertropia]]** (upward deviation): Place the prism **base-down** before the fixating eye.
   - For **[[hypotropia]]** (downward deviation): Place the prism **base-up** before the fixating eye.

6. **Incremental adjustment**: The prism power is gradually increased until the corneal light reflex in the deviating eye appears centered and symmetric with the reflex in the fixating eye.

7. **Recording the measurement**: The prism power required to center the reflex is recorded as the magnitude of the deviation in [[prism diopters]] (Δ).

### Modified Krimsky Test

In the **Modified Krimsky Test**, the prism is placed **before the deviating eye** rather than the fixating eye. This modification is particularly useful in patients with:

- **[[Sensory strabismus]]**: Where one eye has very poor vision and cannot take up fixation.
- **Monocular patients**: Where only one eye has useful vision.
- **Eccentric fixation**: Where the deviating eye does not fixate centrally.

The procedure is otherwise identical: the prism power is increased until the corneal reflex in the deviating eye is centered.

| **Test Variant** | **Prism Placement** | **Primary Use** |
|---|---|---|
| Standard Krimsky Test | Before the **fixating** eye | General strabismus measurement |
| Modified Krimsky Test | Before the **deviating** eye | Sensory strabismus, monocular vision, eccentric fixation |

### Measurement in Multiple Gaze Positions

The Krimsky Test can be performed in the **nine diagnostic positions of gaze** to map the pattern of deviation across different fields. This is particularly useful in:

- **[[Incomitant strabismus]]**: Where the deviation varies with gaze direction (e.g., [[cranial nerve palsy]]).
- **[[A-pattern]]** and **[[V-pattern]]** strabismus: Where the horizontal deviation changes with upgaze and downgaze.
- **Restrictive strabismus**: Where mechanical limitation produces gaze-dependent variation.

## Interpretation and Clinical Significance

### Quantification of Deviation

The Krimsky Test provides a direct measurement of the angle of strabismus in [[prism diopters]]. The following table summarizes the approximate relationship between corneal reflex displacement and prism diopter measurement, which also correlates with the [[Hirschberg test]] estimation:

| **Corneal Reflex Position** | **Approximate Deviation** |
|---|---|
| Centered (symmetric) | [[Orthophoria]] (0Δ) |
| At the pupillary margin (nasal) | ~15Δ [[esotropia]] |
| At the pupillary margin (temporal) | ~15Δ [[exotropia]] |
| Midway between pupil center and limbus | ~30Δ |
| At the [[limbus]] | ~45Δ |

### Accuracy and Reliability

The Krimsky Test is considered less precise than the [[prism and alternate cover test]] (PACT), which is the gold standard for measuring strabismus in cooperative patients. Studies have shown that the Krimsky Test may have a variability of approximately **±5 to 10 prism diopters** compared to PACT measurements. However, in populations where PACT cannot be performed, the Krimsky Test provides the most reliable objective measurement available.

Factors affecting accuracy include:

- **Examiner experience**: Skilled clinicians achieve more consistent results.
- **Patient cooperation**: Even minimal fixation improves reliability.
- **Angle kappa variability**: Asymmetric angle kappa between the two eyes can introduce measurement error.
- **Eccentric fixation**: In patients with [[eccentric fixation]], the corneal reflex may not correspond to the true visual axis, potentially leading to under- or overestimation of the deviation.
- **Pupil size**: Mydriasis or miosis can affect the perceived position of the corneal reflex relative to the pupil.

### Differentiating True Strabismus from Pseudostrabismus

The Krimsky Test is valuable in distinguishing true strabismus from [[pseudostrabismus]]. In pseudostrabismus, the corneal light reflexes are symmetric despite the cosmetic appearance of misalignment (often caused by a wide [[nasal bridge]], prominent [[epicanthal folds]], or a large angle kappa). When the Krimsky Test reveals symmetric reflexes without the need for prism correction, the diagnosis of pseudostrabismus is supported.

## Comparison with Other Strabismus Measurement Techniques

| **Test** | **Type** | **Prism Used** | **Patient Cooperation Required** | **Precision** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Krimsky Test** | Objective | Yes | Minimal (fixation on light) | Moderate (±5–10Δ) |
| **[[Hirschberg Test]]** | Objective | No | Minimal | Low (estimation only) |
| **[[Cover Test]]** | Objective/Subjective | No | Moderate | Moderate |
| **[[Prism and Alternate Cover Test]]** | Subjective | Yes | High | High (gold standard) |
| **[[Maddox Rod Test]]** | Subjective | Yes | High | High |
| **[[Synoptophore]]** | Subjective | No (built-in) | High | High |

## Role of the Krimsky Test in Management and Treatment Planning

While the Krimsky Test is fundamentally a diagnostic tool, its measurements directly inform treatment decisions for patients with strabismus. The quantification of the angle of deviation is essential for:

### 1. Optical Correction

- **[[Spectacle correction]]**: Full cycloplegic refraction and appropriate spectacle prescription may reduce or eliminate accommodative components of strabismus. The Krimsky Test can be repeated with spectacles in place to assess the residual deviation.
- **[[Prism correction]]**: In patients with small, stable deviations causing diplopia, [[Fresnel prisms]] or ground-in prisms may be prescribed. The Krimsky measurement guides the prism power selection.

### 2. Pharmacological Interventions

- **[[Atropine penalization]]**: Used in the management of [[amblyopia]] associated with strabismus. The Krimsky Test helps monitor alignment changes during treatment.
- **[[Botulinum toxin]] injection**: [[Botulinum toxin type A]] (Botox) can be injected into an overacting [[extraocular muscle]] to temporarily weaken it and improve alignment. Pre- and post-injection Krimsky measurements are used to assess treatment efficacy.
- **[[Cycloplegic agents]]**: Cycloplegic refraction (using [[cyclopentolate]] or [[atropine]]) may be performed in conjunction with the Krimsky Test to determine the accommodative component of an esotropia.

### 3. Surgical Planning

The Krimsky Test measurement is one of the key inputs in planning [[strabismus surgery]]. Surgical dosing tables (which specify the amount of muscle recession, resection, or plication required) are based on the measured angle of deviation in prism diopters. For example:

| **Deviation (Prism Diopters)** | **Typical Surgical Approach (Esotropia Example)** |
|---|---|
| 15–20Δ | Unilateral [[medial rectus]] recession |
| 20–35Δ | Bilateral medial rectus recession |
| 35–50Δ | Bilateral medial rectus recession + unilateral [[lateral rectus]] resection |
| >50Δ | Three- or four-muscle surgery |

The Krimsky Test is also used **intraoperatively** to assess alignment while the patient is under [[general anesthesia]], as the cover test cannot be performed in an unconscious patient.

### 4. Orthoptic and Vision Therapy

In patients with intermittent deviations or convergence disorders, the Krimsky Test can be used to monitor changes in alignment over the course of [[vision therapy]] or [[orthoptic exercises]]. Serial measurements help track treatment progress.

### 5. Monitoring and Follow-Up

Serial Krimsky Test measurements over time are essential for:

- Tracking the natural history of infantile strabismus
- Assessing the stability of surgical outcomes
- Detecting recurrence or consecutive deviations after surgery
- Monitoring the effect of spectacle or prism wear on alignment

## Special Considerations

### Krimsky Test in Nystagmus

Patients with [[nystagmus]] present a unique challenge for strabismus measurement. The constant oscillatory eye movements make it difficult to perform a reliable cover test. The Krimsky Test is particularly advantageous in this population because:

- It requires only a brief observation of the corneal reflex position.
- The reflex can be assessed during the **null point** (the gaze position where nystagmus intensity is minimal).
- It does not require the patient to maintain steady fixation for an extended period.

### Krimsky Test in Eccentric Fixation

In patients with [[eccentric fixation]] (where the fovea is not used for fixation, often due to dense [[amblyopia]]), the corneal reflex may not accurately represent the visual axis. In such cases, the Modified Krimsky Test (prism before the deviating eye) is preferred, and the clinician should be aware that the measured deviation may differ from the true angle of anomaly.

### Krimsky Test in Sensory Strabismus

[[Sensory strabismus]] occurs when one eye has significantly reduced vision (e.g., from [[retinal detachment]], [[optic nerve hypoplasia]], [[corneal scarring]], or [[phthisis bulbi]]). Since the affected eye cannot take up fixation, the standard cover test is unreliable. The Modified Krimsky Test is the preferred method for quantifying the deviation in these patients.

### Krimsky Test at Near vs. Distance

The Krimsky Test can be performed at both **near (33 cm)** and **distance (6 meters)** fixation distances. Comparing near and distance measurements is clinically important for:

- Identifying a significant **near-distance disparity**, which may suggest a high [[AC/A ratio]] or [[convergence excess]].
- Differentiating [[basic esotropia]] (similar deviation at near and distance) from [[divergence insufficiency]] (greater esotropia at distance) or [[convergence excess]] (greater esotropia at near).

## Limitations of the Krimsky Test

Despite its clinical utility, the Krimsky Test has several recognized limitations:

1. **Lower precision** compared to the prism and alternate cover test (PACT).
2. **Subjectivity** in determining when the corneal reflex is "centered" — this is an examiner-dependent judgment.
3. **Angle kappa variability** can confound interpretation, particularly in patients with high [[hyperopia]] or [[myopia]].
4. **Does not differentiate** between [[tropia]] (manifest deviation) and [[phoria]] (latent deviation) — it measures only the manifest component.
5. **Limited utility** in very small deviations (<5Δ), where the corneal reflex displacement may be imperceptible.
6. **Torsional deviations** ([[cyclotropia]]) cannot be measured with the Krimsky Test.
7. **Requires a visible corneal reflex** — patients with significant [[corneal irregularity]], [[ptosis]] obscuring the pupil, or [[prosthetic eyes]] may not be testable.

## Documentation and Recording

The results of the Krimsky Test should be documented clearly in the clinical record. A standard notation format includes:

- **Test name**: Krimsky or Modified Krimsky
- **Fixation distance**: Near (33 cm) or Distance (6 m)
- **Fixating eye**: Right (OD) or Left (OS)
- **Deviation type and magnitude**: e.g., 30Δ ET (esotropia), 20Δ XT (exotropia), 10Δ RHT (right hypertropia)
- **Gaze position**: Primary, upgaze, downgaze, right gaze, left gaze, etc.

**Example documentation:**

> **Krimsky Test (near, 33 cm):** OD fixating — 25Δ ET, 5Δ LHT  
> **Krimsky Test (distance, 6 m):** OD fixating — 20Δ ET, 5Δ LHT  
> **Modified Krimsky (near):** Prism before OS — 30Δ ET

---

## Summary

The Krimsky Test remains an essential objective technique in the clinical assessment of [[strabismus]]. Its ability to quantify ocular misalignment in patients who cannot cooperate with subjective testing methods makes it irreplaceable in pediatric ophthalmology, neuro-ophthalmology, and general optometric practice. While it is less precise than the prism and alternate cover test, its ease of administration, minimal equipment requirements, and applicability to challenging patient populations ensure its continued relevance in modern eye care. Mastery of the Krimsky Test technique, including an understanding of its limitations and the influence of factors such as [[angle kappa]] and [[eccentric fixation]], is a fundamental competency for all eye care professionals.
