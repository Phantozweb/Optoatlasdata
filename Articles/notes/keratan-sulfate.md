

# Keratan Sulfate

---

**Keratan sulfate (KS)**, also known as **keratosulfate**, is a critically important [[glycosaminoglycan]] (GAG) found abundantly in the [[cornea]], [[cartilage]], and various other connective tissues. Within the field of [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]], keratan sulfate holds paramount significance due to its essential role in maintaining [[corneal transparency]], structural integrity of the [[corneal stroma]], and its involvement in the pathophysiology of numerous ocular diseases, including the [[macular corneal dystrophy|macular corneal dystrophies]], [[keratoconus]], and various [[mucopolysaccharidoses]]. Keratan sulfate was first isolated from bovine cornea by Gruber and colleagues in 1955, making it the last of the major glycosaminoglycans to be characterized. Its unique biochemical properties—particularly its lack of uronic acid residues, distinguishing it from all other GAGs—render it indispensable to the precise organization of [[collagen fibrils]] within the corneal stroma, a prerequisite for optical clarity.

---

## Biochemistry and Molecular Structure

### General Structure

Keratan sulfate is a linear, unbranched [[polysaccharide]] composed of repeating disaccharide units of **[[galactose]] (Gal)** and **[[N-acetylglucosamine]] (GlcNAc)**, linked by alternating β-1,4 and β-1,3 glycosidic bonds. The general repeating unit can be represented as:

> **[→3Galβ1→4GlcNAcβ1→]ₙ**

Both the galactose and N-acetylglucosamine residues can be sulfated at the C-6 position, and the degree of sulfation is highly variable and tissue-specific. This sulfation pattern is critical for the biological activity of keratan sulfate, particularly in the [[cornea]], where high sulfation is essential for maintaining proper hydration and transparency.

### Classification: KS-I, KS-II, and KS-III

Keratan sulfate is classified into three subtypes based on the nature of its linkage to the core [[proteoglycan]] protein:

| **Type** | **Linkage** | **Primary Tissue** | **Core Protein Examples** |
|----------|-------------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| **KS-I** (Corneal KS) | N-linked to [[asparagine]] (Asn) via a complex-type oligosaccharide | [[Cornea]] | [[Lumican]], [[Keratocan]], [[Mimecan]] (osteoglycin) |
| **KS-II** (Skeletal KS) | O-linked to [[serine]] (Ser) or [[threonine]] (Thr) via GalNAc | [[Cartilage]], [[bone]] | [[Aggrecan]], [[fibromodulin]] |
| **KS-III** | O-linked to Ser/Thr via [[mannose]] | [[Brain]], [[nervous tissue]] | Various neural proteoglycans |

**KS-I** is the predominant form in the cornea and is the most relevant to ophthalmic practice. It is characterized by its N-linked attachment to asparagine residues on small leucine-rich proteoglycans ([[SLRPs]]), particularly [[lumican]], [[keratocan]], and [[mimecan]]. The corneal KS chains are typically longer (approximately 10–25 kDa) and more highly sulfated than those found in other tissues.

### Biosynthesis

The biosynthesis of keratan sulfate occurs in the [[Golgi apparatus]] and involves a series of [[glycosyltransferases]] and [[sulfotransferases]]. Key enzymes include:

- **β-1,4-galactosyltransferase (β4GalT)**: Adds galactose residues
- **β-1,3-N-acetylglucosaminyltransferase (β3GnT)**: Adds GlcNAc residues
- **Corneal N-acetylglucosamine-6-O-sulfotransferase ([[CHST6]])**: Sulfates GlcNAc at C-6 position; mutations in this gene are directly responsible for [[macular corneal dystrophy]]
- **Keratan sulfate galactose-6-O-sulfotransferase ([[CHST1]])**: Sulfates galactose at C-6 position

The enzyme **CHST6** (carbohydrate sulfotransferase 6) is of particular clinical importance. It is predominantly expressed in the cornea and is responsible for the high degree of sulfation characteristic of corneal keratan sulfate. Loss-of-function mutations in the *CHST6* gene lead to the synthesis of unsulfated or poorly sulfated keratan sulfate, which is the molecular basis of **[[macular corneal dystrophy]] (MCD)**.

### Degradation

Keratan sulfate is degraded by a series of lysosomal enzymes, including:

- **[[β-galactosidase]]** (deficient in [[Morquio B syndrome]] / [[GM1 gangliosidosis]])
- **[[N-acetylgalactosamine-6-sulfatase]]** (GALNS; deficient in [[Morquio A syndrome]] / [[MPS IVA]])
- **[[β-N-acetylhexosaminidase]]**

Deficiency in any of these catabolic enzymes results in the lysosomal accumulation of keratan sulfate fragments, leading to the clinical manifestations of specific [[mucopolysaccharidoses]], some of which have significant ocular involvement.

## Role in Corneal Physiology

### Corneal Transparency

The [[cornea]] is the most optically transparent tissue in the human body, and keratan sulfate proteoglycans (KSPGs) are fundamental to this property. The corneal [[stroma]], which constitutes approximately 90% of corneal thickness, is composed of approximately 200–250 lamellae of [[type I collagen]] fibrils embedded in an extracellular matrix rich in proteoglycans, primarily KSPGs and [[dermatan sulfate]] proteoglycans ([[DSPGs]]).

The mechanism by which KSPGs maintain transparency involves several interrelated processes:

1. **Regulation of Collagen Fibril Spacing**: KSPGs, particularly [[lumican]] and [[keratocan]], bind to the surface of collagen fibrils and regulate the interfibrillar spacing to a remarkably uniform distance of approximately **30–35 nm**. This uniform spacing is less than half the wavelength of visible light, which, according to the **[[Maurice lattice theory]]** and the **[[Goldman-Benedek theory]]** of corneal transparency, results in destructive interference of scattered light in all directions except the forward direction, thereby permitting transparency.

2. **Hydration Regulation**: The highly sulfated KS chains are polyanionic and attract water molecules through osmotic forces. The balance between the swelling pressure generated by KSPGs and the dehydrating activity of the [[corneal endothelium]] pump (primarily [[Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase]]) maintains corneal hydration at approximately **78%**, which is optimal for transparency.

3. **Collagen Fibril Diameter Regulation**: KSPGs help maintain a uniform collagen fibril diameter of approximately **25–30 nm** in the corneal stroma, which is significantly smaller and more uniform than collagen fibrils in the opaque [[sclera]] (which range from 25–230 nm).

### Distribution Within the Cornea

Keratan sulfate distribution within the cornea is not uniform:

| **Region** | **KS Concentration** | **Clinical Significance** |
|------------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| Central cornea | Highest | Correlates with greatest transparency requirement |
| Peripheral cornea | Lower | Gradual transition to limbal/scleral composition |
| Anterior stroma | Moderate | — |
| Posterior stroma | Highest | Greater hydration regulation needed |
| [[Bowman's layer]] | Present | Structural role |
| [[Descemet's membrane]] | Minimal | — |

The concentration gradient of keratan sulfate—highest centrally and posteriorly—correlates with the regions of the cornea that require the most precise regulation of hydration and fibril spacing for optimal optical performance.

### Corneal Keratan Sulfate Proteoglycans

The three principal KSPGs in the cornea are members of the [[small leucine-rich proteoglycan]] (SLRP) family:

- **[[Lumican]]**: The most abundant KSPG in the cornea. It carries up to four KS chains and is critical for collagen fibril organization. Lumican-null mice develop corneal opacification due to disorganized collagen fibrils.
- **[[Keratocan]]**: Predominantly expressed in the cornea; carries three KS chains. Its expression is considered a marker of the keratocyte phenotype. Keratocan expression is downregulated when [[keratocytes]] transform into [[fibroblasts]] or [[myofibroblasts]] during wound healing.
- **[[Mimecan]]** (Osteoglycin): Carries one or two KS chains. Plays a role in collagen fibrillogenesis.

## Pathophysiology: Keratan Sulfate in Ocular Disease

### Macular Corneal Dystrophy (MCD)

**[[Macular corneal dystrophy]]** (MCD; OMIM #217800) is the most clinically significant ocular disease directly caused by abnormalities in keratan sulfate metabolism. It is an [[autosomal recessive]] [[corneal dystrophy]] classified under the **IC3D Category 1** dystrophies, with a well-defined genetic basis.

#### Molecular Pathogenesis

MCD is caused by **loss-of-function mutations in the *CHST6* gene** (chromosome 16q23.1), which encodes corneal N-acetylglucosamine-6-O-sulfotransferase. Over 150 different mutations have been identified, including missense, nonsense, frameshift, and deletion mutations. The resulting enzyme deficiency leads to the production of **unsulfated or poorly sulfated keratan sulfate** in the cornea.

Unsulfated KS cannot properly regulate collagen fibril spacing and hydration, leading to:
- Accumulation of abnormal proteoglycans within [[keratocytes]] and in the extracellular stroma
- Disruption of the regular collagen lattice arrangement
- Progressive corneal opacification

#### Immunophenotypic Subtypes

MCD is further classified based on serum and corneal keratan sulfate levels, as detected by immunoassay using the monoclonal antibody **5D4** (which recognizes sulfated KS epitopes):

| **Subtype** | **Serum KS (5D4)** | **Corneal KS (5D4)** | **Mechanism** |
|-------------|--------------------|-----------------------|---------------|
| **MCD Type I** | Absent/undetectable | Absent | Complete absence of sulfated KS synthesis |
| **MCD Type IA** | Absent/undetectable | Present in keratocytes only | Residual intracellular sulfated KS |
| **MCD Type II** | Normal or low-normal | Present (reduced) | Partial enzyme activity; some sulfated KS produced |

MCD Type I is the most common subtype worldwide. The distinction between subtypes has implications for understanding the genotype-phenotype correlation but does not significantly alter clinical management.

#### Clinical Presentation

MCD typically presents in the **first to second decade of life** with:

- **Bilateral, symmetric corneal opacification** beginning in the superficial central stroma
- **Irregular, poorly defined, grayish-white stromal opacities** (the "macular" deposits) that extend from limbus to limbus, involving the full thickness of the stroma
- **Diffuse, ground-glass stromal haze** between the focal opacities, distinguishing MCD from [[granular corneal dystrophy]] and [[lattice corneal dystrophy]]
- **Progressive decrease in [[visual acuity]]**
- **Corneal thinning** (unlike most other stromal dystrophies, which may cause normal or increased thickness)
- **Photophobia** and **recurrent corneal erosions** (less common than in [[epithelial basement membrane dystrophy]])

MCD is generally the **most visually debilitating** of the classic stromal corneal dystrophies and often requires surgical intervention at a younger age than granular or lattice dystrophies.

#### Histopathology

Histological examination of MCD corneas reveals:
- **Intracellular accumulations** of glycosaminoglycan material within keratocytes, [[corneal endothelial cells]], and occasionally [[corneal epithelial cells]]
- **Extracellular deposits** of abnormal proteoglycan material throughout the stroma
- Deposits stain positively with **[[Alcian blue]]**, **[[colloidal iron]]**, and **[[periodic acid-Schiff]] (PAS)** stains
- Electron microscopy reveals **electron-lucent vacuoles** within keratocytes containing fibrillogranular material

### Keratoconus

**[[Keratoconus]]** is a progressive, non-inflammatory ectatic disorder of the cornea characterized by progressive thinning, steepening, and conical protrusion. Alterations in keratan sulfate metabolism have been implicated in its pathogenesis:

- Studies have demonstrated **decreased total keratan sulfate content** in keratoconic corneas compared to normal corneas
- **Reduced expression of lumican and keratocan** has been documented in keratoconic tissue
- The loss of KSPGs may contribute to the **disorganization of collagen lamellae** and **reduced biomechanical strength** observed in keratoconus
- Serum levels of keratan sulfate have been investigated as a potential biomarker, though results remain inconsistent

The precise role of KS deficiency in keratoconus pathogenesis—whether it is a primary causative factor or a secondary consequence of keratocyte apoptosis and stromal degradation—remains an area of active investigation.

### Mucopolysaccharidoses (MPS)

Several [[mucopolysaccharidoses]] involve impaired degradation of keratan sulfate, leading to its lysosomal accumulation and significant ocular manifestations:

| **MPS Type** | **Enzyme Deficiency** | **Accumulated GAGs** | **Ocular Manifestations** |
|-------------|----------------------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| **[[MPS IVA]]** (Morquio A) | [[N-acetylgalactosamine-6-sulfatase]] (GALNS) | KS, [[chondroitin-6-sulfate]] | Corneal clouding, fine corneal deposits |
| **[[MPS IVB]]** (Morquio B) | [[β-galactosidase]] | KS | Corneal clouding (milder) |
| **[[MPS VII]]** (Sly syndrome) | [[β-glucuronidase]] | KS, DS, HS, CS | Corneal clouding |

In **MPS IVA (Morquio A syndrome)**, corneal clouding is a prominent feature and results from the accumulation of undegraded keratan sulfate within corneal keratocytes and the extracellular matrix. The corneal opacification is typically diffuse, progressive, and can significantly impair vision, sometimes necessitating [[penetrating keratoplasty]].

### Corneal Wound Healing and Scarring

During [[corneal wound healing]], there is a well-documented shift in proteoglycan expression:

- **Keratocyte apoptosis** occurs immediately following injury
- Surviving keratocytes transform into **[[fibroblasts]]** and **[[myofibroblasts]]**
- These activated cells **downregulate keratan sulfate proteoglycan synthesis** (particularly keratocan and lumican with KS chains) and **upregulate [[dermatan sulfate]] and [[chondroitin sulfate]] proteoglycan synthesis**
- The resulting alteration in GAG composition disrupts the regular collagen fibril spacing, leading to **[[corneal scarring]]** and loss of transparency

This understanding has significant implications for therapeutic strategies aimed at reducing corneal scarring, including the potential for modulating KS expression during wound healing.

## Symptoms

Symptoms attributable to keratan sulfate abnormalities are primarily those of the associated ocular conditions:

### In Macular Corneal Dystrophy
- **Progressive bilateral visual impairment** (typically beginning in adolescence or early adulthood)
- **Glare** and **photophobia** due to light scattering by stromal deposits
- **Reduced contrast sensitivity**
- **Foreign body sensation** (if epithelial involvement or erosions occur)
- **Tearing** (secondary to surface irregularity)

### In Mucopolysaccharidoses
- **Progressive corneal clouding** (often noted in infancy or early childhood)
- **Decreased visual acuity**
- **Photophobia**
- **Nystagmus** (in severe cases with early-onset visual deprivation)
- Systemic symptoms including skeletal dysplasia, cardiac valve disease, and hepatosplenomegaly (depending on MPS type)

### In Keratoconus (KS-related component)
- **Progressive myopia** and **irregular astigmatism**
- **Monocular diplopia** or **ghosting**
- **Frequent changes in spectacle prescription**
- **Reduced best-corrected visual acuity**

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: The cornerstone of diagnosing KS-related corneal pathology. In MCD, slit-lamp examination reveals characteristic grayish-white, irregular, poorly demarcated stromal opacities with intervening diffuse stromal haze. The opacities extend to the limbus and involve the full stromal thickness. Corneal thinning is typically noted.

- **[[Pachymetry]]**: Corneal thickness measurement reveals **thinning** in MCD (in contrast to the normal or increased thickness seen in other stromal dystrophies). Central corneal thickness in MCD is often **less than 500 μm**.

### Imaging

- **[[Anterior segment optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT)**: Provides high-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the cornea, demonstrating the depth and distribution of stromal deposits. In MCD, hyperreflective deposits are seen throughout the full thickness of the stroma.

- **[[Corneal topography]] and [[tomography]]**: Essential for detecting keratoconus and monitoring corneal shape changes. [[Pentacam]] and [[Orbscan]] systems can detect early ectatic changes.

- **[[Confocal microscopy]] (e.g., [[Heidelberg Retina Tomograph]] with Rostock Corneal Module)**: Allows in vivo cellular-level imaging of the cornea. In MCD, confocal microscopy reveals **hyperreflective intracellular deposits within keratocytes** and **extracellular deposits** in the stroma. This modality can help differentiate MCD from other stromal dystrophies.

### Laboratory and Genetic Testing

| **Test** | **Purpose** | **Findings in KS-Related Disease** |
|----------|-------------|-------------------------------------|
| **Serum KS assay (5D4 ELISA)** | Quantify circulating sulfated KS | Absent/low in MCD Type I; elevated in MPS IVA/IVB |
| **Urine GAG analysis** | Screen for mucopolysaccharidoses | Elevated KS in MPS IVA and IVB |
| ***CHST6* gene sequencing** | Confirm MCD diagnosis; identify mutations | Pathogenic variants in MCD |
| **GALNS enzyme assay** | Diagnose MPS IVA | Deficient enzyme activity |
| **β-galactosidase enzyme assay** | Diagnose MPS IVB | Deficient enzyme activity |
| **Histopathology (post-keratoplasty)** | Confirm diagnosis | Alcian blue-positive deposits in keratocytes and stroma |

### Differential Diagnosis

When evaluating corneal opacities potentially related to keratan sulfate abnormalities, the following conditions must be considered:

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|--------------|----------------------------|
| **[[Granular corneal dystrophy]]** (GCD) | Discrete, well-demarcated, breadcrumb-like deposits; clear intervening stroma; [[Masson trichrome]]-positive |
| **[[Lattice corneal dystrophy]]** (LCD) | Refractile branching lines; [[Congo red]]-positive amyloid deposits |
| **[[Schnyder corneal dystrophy]]** | Central crystalline deposits; arcus lipoides; cholesterol/lipid deposits |
| **[[Fuchs endothelial dystrophy]]** | Guttae on Descemet's membrane; endothelial dysfunction; stromal edema |
| **MPS corneal clouding** | Systemic features; elevated urine GAGs; enzyme deficiency |

## Treatment and Management

### Conservative Management

- **[[Spectacle correction]]** and **[[contact lens]] fitting**: In early stages of MCD or keratoconus with KS abnormalities, optimal refractive correction can maintain functional vision. [[Rigid gas-permeable]] (RGP) contact lenses, [[scleral lenses]], and [[hybrid lenses]] are particularly useful in keratoconus for masking irregular astigmatism.

- **Lubricating eye drops**: [[Artificial tears]] and [[ocular lubricants]] may provide symptomatic relief for surface irregularity and foreign body sensation.

- **Photophobia management**: Tinted lenses or photochromic spectacles can help manage glare symptoms.

### Surgical Management

#### Penetrating Keratoplasty (PKP)

**[[Penetrating keratoplasty]]** has historically been the definitive treatment for visually significant MCD. Key considerations include:

- MCD is one of the most common indications for keratoplasty in certain populations (e.g., Iceland, Saudi Arabia, India)
- **Graft survival rates** are generally excellent (>90% at 5 years) due to the avascular nature of the cornea and the relatively young age of patients
- **Recurrence of MCD in the graft** has been documented, typically occurring **5–20 years** after transplantation. Recurrence is thought to result from migration of host keratocytes into the graft or from the host endothelium (which also carries the metabolic defect)
- Full-thickness replacement ensures removal of all affected tissue

#### Deep Anterior Lamellar Keratoplasty (DALK)

**[[Deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]]** (DALK) is increasingly favored over PKP for MCD because:

- It preserves the host [[Descemet's membrane]] and [[endothelium]], reducing the risk of [[endothelial rejection]]
- Lower long-term risk of graft failure
- However, since MCD affects the endothelium (intracellular deposits are found in endothelial cells), there is a theoretical concern that retained host endothelium may serve as a source of recurrence
- The **big-bubble technique** is commonly employed to achieve separation at the level of Descemet's membrane

#### Phototherapeutic Keratectomy (PTK)

**[[Phototherapeutic keratectomy]]** using the [[excimer laser]] may be considered for superficial corneal opacities but has **limited utility in MCD** because:

- MCD deposits extend through the full thickness of the stroma
- PTK can only treat superficial deposits (typically up to 100–150 μm depth)
- May provide temporary improvement in select cases with predominantly anterior disease

### Emerging and Investigational Therapies

#### Gene Therapy

Given that MCD is caused by well-characterized mutations in a single gene (*CHST6*), it represents an attractive target for [[gene therapy]]:

- Preclinical studies have explored viral vector-mediated delivery of functional *CHST6* to corneal keratocytes
- [[Adeno-associated virus]] (AAV) vectors show promise for corneal gene delivery
- Challenges include achieving sustained transgene expression in the slowly dividing keratocyte population and ensuring adequate sulfotransferase activity to restore normal KS sulfation

#### Enzyme Replacement Therapy (ERT)

For mucopolysaccharidoses involving KS accumulation:

- **[[Elosulfase alfa]]** (Vimizim®) is an FDA-approved enzyme replacement therapy for **MPS IVA** (Morquio A syndrome). It provides recombinant human GALNS enzyme
- While ERT addresses systemic manifestations, its efficacy for corneal clouding is **limited** because the cornea is avascular and systemic enzyme delivery does not adequately penetrate corneal tissue
- Intrastromal or topical enzyme delivery strategies are under investigation

#### Hematopoietic Stem Cell Transplantation (HSCT)

**[[Hematopoietic stem cell transplantation]]** has been explored for various MPS types:

- Donor-derived cells can provide a source of functional enzyme
- Corneal improvement has been reported in some MPS types following HSCT, though results for KS-related MPS are variable
- The mechanism of corneal clearing may involve donor-derived monocytes/macrophages that migrate into the cornea and provide cross-correction of enzyme-deficient keratocytes

#### Pharmacological Chaperone Therapy

For specific missense mutations in *CHST6* that result in protein misfolding rather than complete loss of function, **[[pharmacological chaperone therapy]]** represents a potential approach:

- Small molecules that stabilize the mutant enzyme's native conformation could restore partial sulfotransferase activity
- This approach is mutation-specific and would require detailed structural characterization of each mutant enzyme

## Keratan Sulfate as a Biomarker

Keratan sulfate has been investigated as a clinical biomarker in several contexts:

| **Application** | **Specimen** | **Clinical Utility** |
|----------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **MPS IVA/IVB screening** | Blood, urine | Elevated KS levels support diagnosis; used for newborn screening |
| **MPS IVA treatment monitoring** | Blood, urine | KS levels decrease with effective ERT |
| **MCD subtyping** | Serum | 5D4-reactive KS absent in Type I, present in Type II |
| **Osteoarthritis** | Serum, synovial fluid | Elevated KS reflects cartilage degradation (non-ocular) |
| **Corneal wound healing research** | Tear fluid, corneal tissue | KS/DS ratio reflects healing status |

Quantification of keratan sulfate is typically performed using **ELISA** with the monoclonal antibody **5D4**, which specifically recognizes highly sulfated KS epitopes. Liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry ([[LC-MS/MS]]) methods have also been developed for more precise quantification, particularly in newborn screening programs for MPS IVA.

## Keratan Sulfate in Aging and Other Ocular Conditions

### Age-Related Changes

Corneal keratan sulfate undergoes significant changes with aging:

- **Total KS content increases** with age in the human cornea
- **KS chain length increases** progressively
- **Sulfation patterns** may change, potentially affecting hydration dynamics
- These changes may contribute to age-related alterations in corneal biomechanics and the increased susceptibility to [[corneal edema]] in elderly patients

### Diabetic Keratopathy

Alterations in keratan sulfate metabolism have been observed in **[[diabetic keratopathy]]**:

- [[Advanced glycation end-products]] (AGEs) can modify KSPGs, potentially altering their function
- Changes in KSPG expression may contribute to the impaired corneal wound healing observed in diabetic patients
- Altered KS sulfation patterns have been reported in diabetic corneas

### Pterygium

Studies have demonstrated altered proteoglycan expression, including changes in keratan sulfate content, in **[[pterygium]]** tissue compared to normal conjunctiva, suggesting a role for KS in the pathogenesis of this common ocular surface disorder.
