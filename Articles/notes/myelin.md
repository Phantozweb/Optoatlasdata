

# Myelin and the Visual System

---

**Myelin** is a lipid-rich, multilamellar membrane sheath produced by glial cells that ensheathes and insulates [[axon]]s throughout the [[central nervous system]] (CNS) and [[peripheral nervous system]] (PNS). In the context of optometry and ophthalmology, myelin is of paramount clinical significance due to its critical role in the rapid and efficient transmission of [[action potential]]s along the [[optic nerve]], [[optic tract]], [[optic radiations]], and other visual pathway structures. Disorders of myelin—whether developmental, inflammatory, degenerative, or neoplastic—can produce profound visual dysfunction and are frequently first detected during a comprehensive eye examination. The most clinically encountered myelin-related findings in eye care include [[myelinated retinal nerve fibers]] (MRNF), [[optic neuritis]] secondary to [[demyelinating disease]], and the neuro-ophthalmic manifestations of [[multiple sclerosis]] (MS).

---

## Biochemistry and Ultrastructure of Myelin

### Composition

Myelin is unique among biological membranes in its exceptionally high lipid-to-protein ratio, approximately **70–80% lipid and 20–30% protein** by dry weight. The principal lipid constituents include:

| Lipid Component | Approximate Proportion | Function |
|---|---|---|
| [[Cholesterol]] | ~28% of total lipid | Membrane rigidity and compaction |
| [[Galactocerebroside]] | ~24% | Structural integrity, cell signaling |
| [[Ethanolamine plasmalogen]] | ~14% | Membrane fluidity, antioxidant |
| [[Phosphatidylcholine]] | ~11% | Bilayer formation |
| [[Sphingomyelin]] | ~7% | Signal transduction, structural |
| [[Sulfatide]] | ~4% | Adhesion, ion channel clustering |

Key myelin-specific proteins differ between the CNS and PNS:

| Protein | Location | Function |
|---|---|---|
| [[Myelin basic protein]] (MBP) | CNS & PNS | Compaction of cytoplasmic leaflets |
| [[Proteolipid protein]] (PLP) | CNS | Major structural protein; compaction of extracellular leaflets |
| [[Myelin-associated glycoprotein]] (MAG) | CNS & PNS | Axon-glia signaling, periaxonal adhesion |
| [[Myelin oligodendrocyte glycoprotein]] (MOG) | CNS (outermost lamellae) | Immune target; structural role |
| [[Protein zero]] (P0/MPZ) | PNS | Compaction of Schwann cell myelin |

### Myelinating Cells

In the CNS—including the [[optic nerve]], [[optic chiasm]], [[optic tract]], and [[optic radiations]]—myelin is produced by **[[oligodendrocyte]]s**. A single oligodendrocyte can myelinate segments of up to 40–60 axons simultaneously, wrapping its plasma membrane concentrically around axonal segments to form the characteristic multilamellar sheath. In the PNS, **[[Schwann cell]]s** perform the analogous function, but each Schwann cell myelinates only a single internodal segment of one axon.

The [[optic nerve]] is classified as a CNS tract (not a true peripheral nerve), and therefore its axons are myelinated by oligodendrocytes. This distinction is clinically critical because CNS myelin is the target in diseases such as [[multiple sclerosis]] and [[neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder]] (NMOSD).

### Nodes of Ranvier and Saltatory Conduction

Myelin is not continuous along the axon. Gaps between adjacent myelin segments, termed **[[Nodes of Ranvier]]**, are approximately 1 μm in length and are enriched with voltage-gated [[sodium channel]]s (particularly Nav1.6). The myelinated internodal segments, typically 0.2–2 mm in length, have very high electrical resistance and low capacitance, preventing ion leakage. This arrangement enables **[[saltatory conduction]]**, whereby the [[action potential]] effectively "jumps" from node to node, dramatically increasing conduction velocity (up to **150 m/s** in large myelinated fibers compared to ~1 m/s in unmyelinated fibers of similar diameter). In the optic nerve, conduction velocities of approximately **20–50 m/s** are typical, reflecting the relatively small caliber (0.5–5 μm) of [[retinal ganglion cell]] (RGC) axons.

## Myelin in the Visual Pathway: Normal Anatomy

### Retina and Optic Nerve Head

Under normal physiological conditions, the axons of [[retinal ganglion cell]]s within the [[retina]] are **unmyelinated**. Myelination of RGC axons commences at the **[[lamina cribrosa]]**, the collagenous sieve-like structure at the posterior aspect of the [[optic nerve head]] (ONH). Posterior to the lamina cribrosa, oligodendrocytes ensheathe the axons, and the optic nerve proper is fully myelinated. The absence of myelin within the retina is believed to be essential for maintaining retinal transparency and optimal light transmission to the [[photoreceptor]]s.

### Retrochiasmal Pathway

Beyond the optic nerve, myelinated axons continue through the [[optic chiasm]] (where nasal fibers decussate), into the [[optic tract]], synapsing at the [[lateral geniculate nucleus]] (LGN) of the [[thalamus]]. Second-order neurons project via the myelinated [[optic radiations]] (geniculocalcarine tract) to the [[primary visual cortex]] (V1, Brodmann area 17) in the [[occipital lobe]]. Myelin integrity throughout this entire pathway is essential for normal visual function, including [[visual acuity]], [[visual field]] integrity, [[color vision]], and [[contrast sensitivity]].

## Pathophysiology of Myelin Disorders Affecting Vision

### Mechanisms of Demyelination

Demyelination refers to the pathological loss or destruction of the myelin sheath with relative preservation of the underlying axon. The principal pathophysiological mechanisms relevant to the visual system include:

1. **Autoimmune-mediated demyelination**: The most common mechanism in clinical practice. In [[multiple sclerosis]], autoreactive [[T-cell]]s and [[B-cell]]s breach the [[blood-brain barrier]], initiating an inflammatory cascade that targets oligodendrocytes and myelin. [[Macrophage]]s and [[microglia]] strip myelin lamellae from axons. Key autoantigens include [[myelin basic protein]], [[proteolipid protein]], and [[myelin oligodendrocyte glycoprotein]]. In [[neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder]], [[aquaporin-4]] antibodies (AQP4-IgG) target [[astrocyte]]s, leading to secondary demyelination and often more severe, necrotizing lesions.

2. **Antibody-mediated demyelination**: [[MOG antibody-associated disease]] (MOGAD) involves IgG antibodies directed against MOG on the outermost myelin lamellae, causing complement-dependent and cell-mediated demyelination. This entity frequently presents with [[optic neuritis]], particularly bilateral and recurrent forms.

3. **Ischemic demyelination**: Vascular insufficiency to the optic nerve or white matter tracts can produce demyelination. This is relevant in conditions such as [[anterior ischemic optic neuropathy]] (AION) and small vessel disease affecting the optic radiations.

4. **Toxic/metabolic demyelination**: Nutritional deficiencies (e.g., [[vitamin B12 deficiency]], [[copper deficiency]]), toxins (e.g., [[ethambutol]], [[methanol]]), and metabolic derangements (e.g., [[central pontine myelinolysis]]) can damage myelin.

5. **Hereditary dysmyelination**: Genetic disorders such as [[Pelizaeus-Merzbacher disease]] (PLP1 mutations), [[adrenoleukodystrophy]], and [[Krabbe disease]] involve defective myelin formation or maintenance, often presenting with visual impairment in childhood.

### Consequences of Demyelination on Visual Function

When myelin is lost, the exposed axonal membrane has insufficient sodium channels for continuous conduction, and the decreased resistance/increased capacitance leads to:

- **Conduction block**: Complete failure of action potential propagation, producing acute visual loss.
- **Conduction slowing**: Reduced velocity manifesting as delayed [[visual evoked potential]] (VEP) latencies.
- **Temporal dispersion**: Desynchronization of impulses, degrading spatial and temporal resolution.
- **Temperature sensitivity ([[Uhthoff phenomenon]])**: Elevated body temperature further impairs conduction in demyelinated fibers, causing transient visual blurring.
- **Ephaptic transmission**: Abnormal cross-talk between adjacent demyelinated axons, potentially contributing to positive visual phenomena such as [[phosphene]]s (light flashes with eye movement, known as **[[movement phosphenes]]** or **Lhermitte's phenomenon of the eye**).

If demyelination is prolonged or severe, secondary **axonal degeneration** ensues, leading to irreversible [[retinal ganglion cell]] loss, [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL) thinning, and permanent visual deficit.

## Clinical Presentations of Myelin-Related Conditions in Eye Care

### 1. Myelinated (Medullated) Retinal Nerve Fibers (MRNF)

#### Overview

[[Myelinated retinal nerve fibers]] represent a developmental anomaly in which oligodendrocytes aberrantly extend anterior to the [[lamina cribrosa]], myelinating the [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] within the retina itself. This is the most common myelin-related finding encountered during routine optometric examination.

#### Epidemiology

- Prevalence: approximately **0.3–0.98%** of the general population
- Typically unilateral (~67–80% of cases); bilateral in 7.7–20%
- Usually discovered incidentally during [[dilated fundus examination]]
- No sex predilection; present from birth (congenital) in most cases
- Rare acquired forms have been reported following [[papilledema]], [[optic disc drusen]] resolution, or in association with [[multiple sclerosis]]

#### Clinical Appearance

MRNF appear as **white-to-yellowish-white, feathery, striated patches** with irregular, flame-shaped borders that follow the arcuate distribution of the [[retinal nerve fiber layer]]. They are most commonly located:

- Contiguous with the [[optic disc]] margin (most frequent)
- Along the arcuate bundles superiorly and inferiorly
- Rarely, in the peripheral retina discontinuous from the disc

The characteristic features include:

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Color | Bright white to yellowish-white |
| Borders | Feathery, striated, flame-shaped |
| Distribution | Follows RNFL arcuate pattern |
| Retinal vessels | Partially or completely obscured |
| Underlying retina | Obscured; may appear as a relative scotoma |
| Laterality | Usually unilateral |

#### Associated Findings

In most cases, MRNF are an isolated, benign finding. However, extensive or bilateral MRNF may be associated with:

- **[[Amblyopia]]** (particularly if extensive and unilateral, causing relative scotoma and image degradation)
- **[[Myopia]]** (ipsilateral high myopia, possibly due to axial elongation stimulus from image blur)
- **[[Strabismus]]**
- **Enlarged [[optic disc]]** (pseudo-macrodisc appearance)
- **Rare associations**: [[Neurofibromatosis type 1]] (NF1), [[Gorlin syndrome]], [[Down syndrome]], craniosynostosis syndromes

#### Visual Field Correlation

MRNF produce corresponding **[[visual field]] defects** that are absolute or relative scotomas, typically enlarging the [[blind spot]] or producing arcuate-type defects. These are non-progressive and correspond precisely to the area of myelination. It is essential for the clinician to document these baseline field defects to avoid misdiagnosis of [[glaucoma]] or other optic neuropathies.

#### Impact on Diagnostic Testing

MRNF can significantly confound several diagnostic modalities:

| Diagnostic Test | Effect of MRNF |
|---|---|
| [[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT) RNFL | Artifactually **increased** RNFL thickness in affected sectors |
| [[OCT]] ganglion cell analysis | May be normal or show thinning if amblyopia present |
| [[Visual field]] testing | Scotomas corresponding to myelinated areas |
| [[Fundus photography]] | White patches obscuring underlying retinal detail |
| [[Fluorescein angiography]] | Blocked fluorescence in areas of MRNF |

Clinicians must exercise caution when interpreting [[OCT]] RNFL thickness maps in patients with MRNF, as the thickened myelin sheath produces **falsely elevated RNFL values** that may mask concurrent [[glaucoma]]tous thinning in adjacent sectors.

### 2. Optic Neuritis and Demyelinating Disease

#### Optic Neuritis: The Hallmark Myelin Disorder in Eye Care

[[Optic neuritis]] (ON) is an inflammatory demyelinating condition of the [[optic nerve]] and represents the single most important myelin-related disorder encountered in clinical optometry and ophthalmology. It is frequently the **initial presenting manifestation** of [[multiple sclerosis]], occurring as the first clinical event in approximately 20–25% of MS patients.

#### Epidemiology

- Annual incidence: approximately **1–5 per 100,000** population
- Peak age of onset: **20–45 years**
- Female predominance: **3:1 female-to-male ratio**
- Higher prevalence in Northern European and North American populations
- Associated with [[HLA-DR2]] (HLA-DRB1*1501) haplotype

#### Pathophysiology

In typical demyelinating optic neuritis, the inflammatory process involves:

1. Breakdown of the [[blood-brain barrier]] within the optic nerve
2. Infiltration of autoreactive T-lymphocytes (CD4+ and CD8+), B-lymphocytes, and macrophages
3. Release of pro-inflammatory cytokines ([[TNF-α]], [[interferon-γ]], [[interleukin-17]])
4. Direct attack on oligodendrocytes and myelin sheaths
5. Complement activation and antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity
6. Resultant segmental demyelination with conduction block
7. Potential secondary axonal injury and [[Wallerian degeneration]]

#### Symptoms

The classic symptom triad of acute demyelinating optic neuritis includes:

| Symptom | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **Visual loss** | Subacute onset over hours to days; typically unilateral; ranges from mild blurring to no light perception (NLP); central or cecocentral scotoma most common |
| **Periocular pain** | Present in ~92% of cases; worsened by eye movement; may precede visual loss by 1–2 days; due to inflammation of the optic nerve sheath |
| **Dyschromatopsia** | Color vision loss, often disproportionate to visual acuity loss ("red desaturation"); detected with [[Ishihara plates]], [[Hardy-Rand-Rittler plates]], or [[Farnsworth-Munsell 100 Hue test]] |

Additional symptoms include:

- **[[Uhthoff phenomenon]]**: Transient worsening of vision with exercise, hot baths, or fever (present in ~50% of patients during recovery)
- **[[Pulfrich phenomenon]]**: Illusory perception of a pendulum swinging in an elliptical path due to asymmetric conduction velocity between the two optic nerves
- **[[Movement phosphenes]]**: Brief flashes of light triggered by eye movements in a darkened room, caused by mechanical stimulation of demyelinated axons

#### Clinical Signs

| Sign | Details |
|---|---|
| **[[Visual acuity]]** | Variable; 20/20 to NLP; median ~20/60 at nadir |
| **[[Relative afferent pupillary defect]] (RAPD)** | Present in unilateral or asymmetric cases; detected with [[swinging flashlight test]] |
| **[[Color vision]]** | Reduced; red desaturation is a sensitive early indicator |
| **[[Visual field]]** | Central, cecocentral, altitudinal, or diffuse depression; any pattern possible |
| **[[Contrast sensitivity]]** | Markedly reduced; often more affected than Snellen acuity; tested with [[Pelli-Robson chart]] or [[Mars letter chart]] |
| **[[Optic disc]] appearance** | Normal in ~65% (retrobulbar neuritis); swollen in ~35% (papillitis); pallor develops 4–6 weeks after acute episode |
| **[[Retinal venous sheathing]]** (periphlebitis) | Present in ~5–10%; highly suggestive of MS |

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

[[Optical coherence tomography]] has revolutionized the assessment of myelin-related optic nerve damage by providing objective, quantitative measurement of:

- **[[Retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL) thickness**: Acutely swollen in papillitis; progressive thinning (average loss of 10–20 μm) develops over 3–6 months following optic neuritis, reflecting axonal loss secondary to demyelination
- **[[Ganglion cell-inner plexiform layer]] (GCIPL) thickness**: Thinning detectable as early as 1–3 months post-ON; may be a more specific marker of neuronal loss than RNFL
- **[[Optic nerve head]] parameters**: Disc edema quantification acutely; pallor assessment chronically

**OCT normative data changes in optic neuritis:**

| OCT Parameter | Acute Phase | Chronic Phase (>6 months) |
|---|---|---|
| Peripapillary RNFL | Increased (edema) or normal | Decreased (mean ~80 μm vs. ~100 μm normal) |
| Temporal RNFL | May be normal | Most significantly thinned sector |
| GCIPL | Normal or early thinning | Decreased (mean loss ~8–15 μm) |
| Total macular volume | Normal or increased | Decreased |

### Visual Evoked Potentials (VEP)

The **[[visual evoked potential]]** is the electrophysiological gold standard for detecting demyelination of the optic nerve. Pattern-reversal VEP (PR-VEP) measures the cortical response to a reversing checkerboard stimulus.

| VEP Finding | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| **Prolonged P100 latency** (>115 ms) | Hallmark of demyelination; indicates slowed conduction |
| **Reduced P100 amplitude** | Indicates axonal loss or conduction block |
| **Absent response** | Severe conduction block or extensive axonal loss |
| **Persistent latency delay** | Characteristic of prior demyelination; may persist indefinitely even after clinical recovery |

VEP is **highly sensitive** (>95%) for detecting prior optic neuritis, even in subclinical cases, and the prolonged latency is a direct electrophysiological consequence of myelin loss.

### Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

[[Magnetic resonance imaging]] is essential for:

1. **Confirming optic nerve inflammation**: T2-weighted and STIR (short tau inversion recovery) sequences show hyperintense signal within the optic nerve; gadolinium enhancement on T1-weighted images indicates active inflammation and blood-brain barrier breakdown
2. **Prognostication for MS**: The presence of **white matter lesions** (demyelinating plaques) on brain MRI is the strongest predictor of MS development after a first episode of optic neuritis
   - **0 lesions**: ~25% risk of MS at 15 years
   - **≥1 lesion**: ~72% risk of MS at 15 years (Optic Neuritis Treatment Trial data)
3. **Differentiating etiologies**: Longitudinally extensive optic nerve lesions (>50% of nerve length) suggest [[NMOSD]] or [[MOGAD]] rather than MS

### Additional Diagnostic Studies

| Test | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **[[Serum AQP4-IgG antibodies]]** | Diagnose [[neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder]] |
| **[[Serum MOG-IgG antibodies]]** | Diagnose [[MOG antibody-associated disease]] |
| **[[Cerebrospinal fluid]] analysis** | Oligoclonal bands (present in >90% of MS); cell count; protein |
| **[[Blood work]]** | CBC, ESR, CRP, ANA, ACE level, RPR/FTA-ABS, vitamin B12, folate (to exclude mimics) |
| **[[Humphrey visual field]]** | Document and monitor visual field defects |
| **[[Ishihara color plates]]** | Quantify color vision deficit |
| **[[Low-contrast letter acuity]]** (e.g., 2.5% and 1.25% Sloan charts) | Sensitive measure of visual dysfunction in demyelinating disease; used in MS clinical trials |

## Management and Treatment

### Acute Optic Neuritis

The management of acute demyelinating optic neuritis is guided primarily by the landmark **[[Optic Neuritis Treatment Trial]]** (ONTT) and subsequent studies:

#### Pharmacological Interventions

| Treatment | Protocol | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Intravenous methylprednisolone** | 1 g/day IV for 3 days, followed by oral prednisone taper (1 mg/kg/day for 11 days) | Accelerates visual recovery; does NOT improve final visual outcome; reduces short-term risk of MS development (ONTT) |
| **Oral prednisone alone** | 1 mg/kg/day for 14 days | **CONTRAINDICATED** as monotherapy—associated with increased recurrence rate (ONTT) |
| **Plasma exchange (PLEX)** | 5–7 exchanges over 10–14 days | Reserved for severe, steroid-refractory cases; may benefit NMO-related ON |
| **Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG)** | Variable dosing | Limited evidence; sometimes used in steroid-refractory or MOGAD-related ON |

#### Prognosis

- **Visual recovery**: ~95% of patients recover to 20/40 or better within 12 months (ONTT data)
- **Residual deficits**: Subtle abnormalities in [[contrast sensitivity]], [[color vision]], [[stereopsis]], and [[visual field]] often persist despite good Snellen acuity recovery
- **Recurrence**: ~28% risk of recurrence in the same eye; ~35% in the fellow eye over 10 years
- **MS conversion**: ~50% at 15 years overall; higher with baseline MRI lesions

### Disease-Modifying Therapies (DMTs) for Multiple Sclerosis

When optic neuritis occurs in the context of MS or as a clinically isolated syndrome (CIS) with high-risk MRI features, early initiation of **[[disease-modifying therapy]]** is recommended to reduce relapse rate, slow disability progression, and protect myelin:

| DMT Category | Examples | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Injectable** | [[Interferon beta-1a]], [[interferon beta-1b]], [[glatiramer acetate]] | Immunomodulation; reduce inflammatory demyelination |
| **Oral** | [[Fingolimod]], [[dimethyl fumarate]], [[teriflunomide]], [[siponimod]], [[cladribine]], [[ozanimod]], [[ponesimod]] | Various: S1P receptor modulation, lymphocyte sequestration, Nrf2 pathway activation |
| **Infusion** | [[Natalizumab]], [[ocrelizumab]], [[alemtuzumab]], [[ofatumumab]] | Anti-integrin (natalizumab), anti-CD20 B-cell depletion (ocrelizumab, ofatumumab), anti-CD52 (alemtuzumab) |

### Management of NMOSD and MOGAD-Related Optic Neuritis

These conditions require distinct treatment approaches from MS:

- **Acute**: High-dose IV corticosteroids ± plasma exchange
- **Maintenance (NMOSD)**: [[Eculizumab]] (anti-C5 complement), [[inebilizumab]] (anti-CD19), [[satralizumab]] (anti-IL-6R), rituximab, azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil
- **Maintenance (MOGAD)**: IVIG, rituximab, azathioprine, mycophenolate (evidence evolving)

### Management of Myelinated Retinal Nerve Fibers

MRNF are generally a benign finding requiring **no treatment**. Management considerations include:

1. **Documentation**: Thorough fundus photography and OCT baseline documentation
2. **Amblyopia screening**: Essential in pediatric patients with extensive unilateral MRNF; early detection and treatment of [[amblyopia]] with [[occlusion therapy]] or [[atropine penalization]]
3. **Refractive correction**: Address associated [[myopia]] or [[anisometropia]]
4. **Monitoring**: Stable finding; any change should prompt investigation for alternative pathology
5. **Patient education**: Reassurance regarding benign nature; explanation of visual field defects if present

### Neuroprotection and Remyelination: Emerging Therapies

A major frontier in myelin research involves strategies to promote **remyelination** and protect axons from secondary degeneration:

| Approach | Agent/Strategy | Mechanism | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Anti-LINGO-1** | [[Opicinumab]] (BIIB033) | Blocks LINGO-1, a negative regulator of oligodendrocyte differentiation and myelination | Phase II trials in ON and MS; mixed results |
| **Clemastine fumarate** | [[Clemastine]] | Antihistamine with pro-remyelinating properties; promotes oligodendrocyte precursor cell differentiation | Phase II ReBUILD trial showed VEP latency improvement in chronic MS |
| **Biotin (high-dose)** | [[MD1003]] | Enhances myelin synthesis via fatty acid pathways and mitochondrial energy production | Phase III trials in progressive MS; inconclusive |
| **Stem cell therapy** | Mesenchymal stem cells, oligodendrocyte precursor transplantation | Replace lost oligodendrocytes; provide trophic support | Early clinical trials |
| **Sodium channel blockers** | [[Phenytoin]], [[lamotrigine]], [[amiloride]] | Neuroprotection by reducing sodium influx into demyelinated axons, preventing calcium-mediated injury | Phase II trials; phenytoin showed RNFL preservation in acute ON |

## Myelin-Related Conditions: Differential Diagnosis in Eye Care

The following table summarizes key myelin-related conditions that may present to the eye care provider:

| Condition | Myelin Pathology | Key Ocular Features | Diagnostic Clues |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Multiple sclerosis]]** | Autoimmune CNS demyelination | Optic neuritis, INO, nystagmus, uveitis | MRI white matter lesions, oligoclonal bands, VEP delay |
| **[[Neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder]]** | AQP4-Ab mediated astrocytopathy → demyelination | Severe/bilateral ON, poor recovery | AQP4-IgG+, longitudinally extensive ON on MRI |
| **[[MOG antibody-associated disease]]** | MOG-Ab mediated demyelination | ON (often bilateral, disc edema prominent), recurrent | MOG-IgG+, perineural enhancement on MRI |
| **[[Acute disseminated encephalomyelitis]]** (ADEM) | Post-infectious/post-vaccinal demyelination | Bilateral ON, papillitis | Children; monophasic; large, poorly-defined MRI lesions |
| **[[Myelinated retinal nerve fibers]]** | Ectopic myelination within retina | White feathery retinal patches, scotomas | Congenital, stable, characteristic appearance |
| **[[Pelizaeus-Merzbacher disease]]** | Dysmyelination (PLP1 mutation) | Nystagmus, optic atrophy | X-linked; infantile onset; MRI shows diffuse hypomyelination |
| **[[Adrenoleukodystrophy]]** | Peroxisomal disorder → demyelination | Visual field loss, optic atrophy, cortical blindness | Elevated VLCFA; posterior-predominant MRI lesions |
| **[[Krabbe disease]]** | Galactocerebroside accumulation → demyelination | Optic atrophy, cortical visual impairment | Galactocerebrosidase deficiency; infantile onset |

## Role of the Optometrist in Myelin-Related Disease

Optometrists serve a critical role in the detection, monitoring, and co-management of myelin-related visual disorders:

1. **Detection of MRNF**: Identification during routine examination; appropriate documentation, patient education, and amblyopia management
2. **Recognition of optic neuritis**: History of acute unilateral visual loss with pain; identification of RAPD, dyschromatopsia, and visual field defects; urgent referral for neurological evaluation and MRI
3. **Monitoring MS patients**: Serial OCT (RNFL and GCIPL), visual field testing, low-contrast acuity, and color vision assessment to track subclinical disease progression and treatment efficacy
4. **Detection of subclinical demyelination**: Identification of subtle signs (e.g., mild RAPD, reduced contrast sensitivity, VEP abnormalities) that may indicate early or subclinical demyelinating disease
5. **Collaborative care**: Communication with [[neurology]], [[neuro-ophthalmology]], and primary care regarding disease activity, treatment response, and visual rehabilitation needs
6. **Low vision rehabilitation**: For patients with permanent visual loss from severe or recurrent demyelinating optic neuritis

---

## Summary

Myelin is a fundamental biological structure whose integrity is essential for normal visual function. From the benign developmental variant of myelinated retinal nerve fibers to the potentially devastating inflammatory demyelination of optic neuritis and multiple sclerosis, myelin-related conditions span a broad clinical spectrum encountered regularly in optometric practice. A thorough understanding of myelin biochemistry, the pathophysiology of demyelination, and the clinical manifestations of myelin disorders enables the eye care professional to accurately diagnose, appropriately manage, and effectively monitor these conditions, ultimately preserving visual function and improving patient outcomes.
