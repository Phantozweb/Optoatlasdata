

# Slit Lamp Examination

---

**Slit Lamp Examination** (also known as **biomicroscopy**) is a fundamental, indispensable diagnostic procedure in [[Optometry]] and [[Ophthalmology]] that utilizes a high-intensity light source combined with a [[biomicroscope]] to provide a magnified, three-dimensional, stereoscopic view of the anterior and posterior structures of the [[eye]]. The slit lamp is considered the single most essential instrument in the ophthalmic clinical setting, enabling the clinician to examine, in exquisite detail, the [[eyelids]], [[eyelashes]], [[conjunctiva]], [[sclera]], [[cornea]], [[anterior chamber]], [[iris]], [[crystalline lens]], [[anterior vitreous]], and—with the aid of supplementary lenses—the [[retina]], [[optic nerve head]], and [[macula]]. First conceptualized in the early 20th century and refined over decades of optical engineering, the slit lamp examination remains the cornerstone of virtually every comprehensive eye examination performed worldwide.

---

## History and Development

The origins of slit lamp biomicroscopy trace back to the pioneering work of Swedish ophthalmologist **Allvar Gullstrand**, who introduced the first slit lamp in **1911** and was awarded the [[Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine]] for his contributions to the understanding of the dioptrics of the eye. Gullstrand's original instrument combined a [[Nernst glower]] as a light source with a simple microscope. In **1919**, **Vogt** significantly improved the instrument by coupling a more powerful light source with a corneal microscope, enabling detailed examination of the living eye's optical media. The modern binocular slit lamp was further refined by **Goldmann** in the 1930s and 1940s, who introduced the Haag-Streit model that incorporated a binocular microscope with a rotating illumination arm, a design philosophy that persists in contemporary instruments. Today, slit lamps are manufactured by numerous companies (e.g., [[Haag-Streit]], [[Zeiss]], [[Topcon]], [[Nikon]], [[Keeler]]) and may incorporate digital imaging, video capture, and integration with [[electronic health records]] (EHR).

## Instrumentation and Optical Principles

### Components of the Slit Lamp

The slit lamp biomicroscope consists of two primary optical systems mounted on a common axis or pivot:

| Component | Description |
|---|---|
| **Illumination System** | A high-intensity light source (historically [[halogen]]; increasingly [[LED]]) projected through a condensing lens, a variable-width slit aperture, and various filters. The slit can be adjusted in width (0–14 mm), height, angle, and orientation. |
| **Observation System (Biomicroscope)** | A compound binocular microscope providing stereoscopic (3D) viewing at variable magnifications, typically ranging from **6× to 40×** (some instruments up to 50×). Magnification is changed via a rotating drum, stepped turret, or zoom mechanism. |
| **Mechanical System** | A joystick-controlled base allowing smooth X-Y-Z translational movement. The illumination arm and microscope arm share a common pivot (isocentric or parfocal design), ensuring that the focal point of the light and the focal point of the microscope coincide at the same point in space (the **confocal principle**). |
| **Chin Rest and Forehead Bar** | Patient positioning apparatus with adjustable height to align the patient's eye with the instrument's optical axis. An alignment marker or fixation target is often present. |
| **Filters** | Cobalt blue filter (for [[fluorescein]] examination), red-free (green) filter (for vascular assessment and [[nerve fiber layer]] evaluation), neutral density filter, diffuser, and occasionally a yellow barrier (Wratten) filter. |

### Optical Principles

The slit lamp operates on the **Scheimpflug principle** and the concept of an **optical section**. When a narrow beam of light is directed at an angle into a transparent or semi-transparent medium (such as the [[cornea]] or [[crystalline lens]]), the illuminated cross-section becomes visible against the dark, unilluminated background. This optical section allows the clinician to localize the depth and layer of any opacity, deposit, or pathological change within the ocular media. The stereoscopic binocular viewing system provides depth perception, enabling precise three-dimensional localization of findings.

The **confocal (parfocal/isocentric) design** ensures that the slit beam focus and the microscope focus converge at the same point, so that when the illumination is sharply focused on a structure, the microscope is simultaneously in focus on that same structure. This is achieved by having both the illumination arm and the observation arm rotate around a common pivot point.

## Illumination Techniques

Mastery of slit lamp examination requires proficiency in multiple illumination techniques, each designed to highlight different structural features and pathologies. The following table summarizes the principal methods:

| Illumination Technique | Description | Primary Clinical Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Diffuse Illumination** | Wide, unfocused beam with the diffuser in place; provides general overview. | Gross survey of [[eyelids]], [[conjunctiva]], [[sclera]], and [[cornea]]; initial assessment. |
| **Direct Focal Illumination (Parallelepiped)** | Medium-width slit beam (2–4 mm) directed at the structure of interest; microscope and illumination focused on the same point. Creates a "parallelepiped" (block of light) through the cornea. | Examination of [[corneal]] layers, stromal opacities, [[keratic precipitates]], [[corneal edema]], [[Descemet's folds]]. |
| **Optical Section (Thin Slit)** | Very narrow slit beam (<1 mm) creating a thin cross-sectional "slice" through transparent media. | Precise depth localization of corneal foreign bodies, [[corneal dystrophies]], lens opacities ([[cataract]] grading), and anterior chamber depth estimation. |
| **Specular Reflection** | Angle of incidence equals angle of reflection; the reflected light from a smooth surface (e.g., [[corneal endothelium]]) is observed. The microscope is positioned at the mirror-image angle of the illumination arm. | Assessment of [[corneal endothelium]] (cell morphology, [[guttata]], [[Fuchs' endothelial dystrophy]]), contact lens surface quality, and tear film lipid layer. |
| **Retroillumination (Direct)** | Light is focused on a structure behind the area of interest (e.g., the [[iris]] or [[retina]]), and the reflected light illuminates the structure from behind. | Detection of subtle corneal opacities, [[corneal scars]], [[epithelial basement membrane dystrophy]], [[vacuoles]], [[iris transillumination defects]], and posterior capsular opacities. |
| **Retroillumination (Indirect)** | Similar to direct retroillumination, but the area of interest is viewed against the dark background adjacent to the reflex rather than directly against it. | Enhanced contrast for subtle corneal or lenticular changes. |
| **Sclerotic Scatter** | The slit beam is directed at the [[limbus]]; light undergoes [[total internal reflection]] within the cornea, illuminating the entire cornea from within. The cornea acts as a fiber-optic light guide. | Detection of faint corneal edema, [[corneal infiltrates]], subtle [[corneal scars]], and early [[keratitis]]. |
| **Indirect Illumination** | The slit beam is focused adjacent to (but not directly on) the area of interest; the area is viewed by light scattered from the adjacent illuminated zone. | Examination of subtle [[iris]] lesions, [[iris nevi]], [[iris atrophy]], and microcystic corneal edema. |
| **Oscillatory Illumination** | The illumination arm is gently oscillated back and forth during observation. | Detection of subtle aqueous [[flare]] and [[cells]] in the [[anterior chamber]] (indicators of [[uveitis]] or [[anterior chamber inflammation]]). |
| **Tangential Illumination** | Light directed from the side at a steep angle to the surface being examined. | Evaluation of surface irregularities of the [[iris]], elevated lesions, and [[iris nodules]] (e.g., [[Busacca nodules]], [[Koeppe nodules]]). |

## Clinical Procedure and Systematic Examination Protocol

### Patient Preparation

1. **Informed consent**: Explain the procedure to the patient; it is non-invasive and painless.
2. **Positioning**: The patient is seated comfortably with chin on the chin rest and forehead firmly against the forehead bar. The lateral [[canthus]] should be aligned with the alignment marker on the instrument.
3. **Room illumination**: Dim ambient lighting to maximize pupil dilation and enhance contrast.
4. **Instillation of diagnostic agents** (as indicated): [[Fluorescein sodium]] (with [[cobalt blue filter]]) for corneal staining, [[Rose Bengal]] or [[Lissamine Green]] for [[conjunctival]] and corneal devitalized cell staining, or [[topical anesthetic]] if [[applanation tonometry]] is to be performed.

### Systematic Examination Sequence

A thorough slit lamp examination follows an **anterior-to-posterior** (outside-in) systematic approach to ensure no structure is overlooked:

1. **[[Eyelids]] and [[Eyelashes]]**: Evaluate for [[blepharitis]], [[meibomian gland dysfunction]] (MGD), [[chalazion]], [[hordeolum]], [[trichiasis]], [[distichiasis]], [[entropion]], [[ectropion]], lid margin irregularities, [[collarettes]], and lid lesions. Evert the upper lid to examine the [[tarsal conjunctiva]] for [[papillae]], [[follicles]], [[giant papillary conjunctivitis]], and [[foreign bodies]].
2. **[[Conjunctiva]] and [[Sclera]]**: Assess for [[injection]] (ciliary vs. conjunctival), [[chemosis]], [[subconjunctival hemorrhage]], [[pinguecula]], [[pterygium]], [[conjunctival cysts]], [[follicles]], [[papillae]], [[symblepharon]], and [[scleral thinning]] or [[scleritis]].
3. **[[Tear Film]]**: Evaluate [[tear meniscus height]], [[tear break-up time]] (TBUT) using [[fluorescein]] and the [[cobalt blue filter]], and the presence of debris or mucus strands indicative of [[dry eye disease]] (DED).
4. **[[Cornea]]**: This is arguably the most critical component of the slit lamp examination. Evaluate all five layers systematically:
   - **[[Epithelium]]**: Staining defects ([[punctate epithelial erosions]], [[corneal abrasion]], [[dendritic ulcer]]), [[microcysts]], [[epithelial basement membrane dystrophy]] (EBMD/map-dot-fingerprint dystrophy).
   - **[[Bowman's layer]]**: Breaks, scarring.
   - **[[Stroma]]**: Opacities, [[infiltrates]], [[corneal neovascularization]], [[keratoconus]] (Vogt's striae, [[Fleischer ring]], [[Munson's sign]]), [[corneal dystrophies]] (e.g., [[granular dystrophy]], [[lattice dystrophy]], [[macular dystrophy]]), [[interstitial keratitis]], [[corneal edema]], and [[Descemet's folds]].
   - **[[Descemet's membrane]]**: Folds, breaks ([[Haab's striae]] in [[congenital glaucoma]], [[hydrops]] in [[keratoconus]]).
   - **[[Endothelium]]**: [[Guttata]] (via specular reflection), [[keratic precipitates]] (KPs)—fine (stellate, as in [[Fuchs heterochromic iridocyclitis]]) or mutton-fat (granulomatous, as in [[sarcoidosis]] or [[tuberculosis]]-related [[uveitis]]).
5. **[[Anterior Chamber]]**: Assess depth (Van Herick technique for [[angle closure]] screening), clarity (presence of [[cells]] and [[flare]] graded on the Standardization of Uveitis Nomenclature [SUN] scale), [[hypopyon]], [[hyphema]], and [[fibrin]].

   **SUN Grading Scale for Anterior Chamber Cells:**

   | Grade | Cells per Field (1 mm × 1 mm slit beam) |
   |---|---|
   | 0 | < 1 |
   | 0.5+ | 1–5 |
   | 1+ | 6–15 |
   | 2+ | 16–25 |
   | 3+ | 26–50 |
   | 4+ | > 50 |

   **SUN Grading Scale for Anterior Chamber Flare:**

   | Grade | Description |
   |---|---|
   | 0 | None |
   | 1+ | Faint (barely detectable) |
   | 2+ | Moderate (iris and lens details clear) |
   | 3+ | Marked (iris and lens details hazy) |
   | 4+ | Intense (fibrin or plastic aqueous) |

6. **[[Iris]]**: Evaluate for [[synechiae]] (anterior and posterior), [[iris atrophy]], [[heterochromia]], [[iris nevi]], [[iris nodules]] ([[Koeppe nodules]] at the pupillary margin, [[Busacca nodules]] on the iris stroma), [[rubeosis iridis]] ([[neovascularization of the iris]]), [[iris coloboma]], and [[iridodonesis]] (tremulousness suggesting [[lens subluxation]] or [[aphakia]]).
7. **[[Crystalline Lens]]**: Assess for [[cataract]] type and severity using the [[Lens Opacities Classification System III]] (LOCS III) or similar grading:
   - **[[Nuclear sclerosis]]** (NS): Graded 1–4+ based on opalescence and brunescence.
   - **[[Cortical cataract]]**: Spoke-like opacities best seen with retroillumination.
   - **[[Posterior subcapsular cataract]]** (PSC): Central posterior opacity best seen with retroillumination; associated with [[corticosteroid]] use and [[diabetes mellitus]].
   - **[[Anterior subcapsular cataract]]**: May be associated with [[atopic dermatitis]] or trauma.
   - Also evaluate for [[pseudoexfoliation syndrome]] (PXF), [[phacodonesis]], and [[lens subluxation]] (as in [[Marfan syndrome]] or [[homocystinuria]]).
8. **[[Anterior Vitreous]]**: With high magnification, assess for [[vitreous cells]], [[pigment]] (Shafer's sign/[[tobacco dust]], suggestive of [[retinal detachment]] or [[retinal tear]]), and [[vitreous opacities]].

## Supplementary Techniques and Accessory Lenses

The slit lamp's diagnostic capability is vastly extended through the use of accessory lenses and attachments:

| Accessory | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **[[Goldmann applanation tonometer]] (GAT)** | Measurement of [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP); the gold standard for [[tonometry]]. Mounted on the slit lamp. |
| **[[Goldmann three-mirror lens]]** | [[Gonioscopy]] (examination of the [[iridocorneal angle]]) and examination of the peripheral [[retina]], [[ora serrata]], and [[pars plana]]. Requires a coupling agent (e.g., [[methylcellulose]]). |
| **[[Volk]] / [[78D]] / [[90D]] / [[SuperField]] condensing lenses** | Non-contact fundoscopy through the slit lamp; provides a wide, stereoscopic, inverted view of the [[posterior pole]], [[optic disc]], [[macula]], and [[retinal vasculature]]. |
| **[[Hruby lens]]** | A plano-concave lens mounted on the slit lamp for direct (non-inverted) viewing of the posterior vitreous and central fundus. |
| **[[Gonioscopy lenses]]** (e.g., [[Zeiss four-mirror]], [[Posner lens]], [[Sussman lens]]) | Examination of the [[drainage angle]] for [[angle-closure glaucoma]] assessment, [[Shaffer grading]], and identification of [[peripheral anterior synechiae]] (PAS), [[angle recession]], [[neovascularization of the angle]], and [[pigment dispersion]]. |
| **[[Pachymeter]] attachment** | Optical measurement of [[central corneal thickness]] (CCT), relevant for [[glaucoma]] risk assessment and [[refractive surgery]] planning. |
| **Digital imaging systems** | Slit lamp cameras (e.g., integrated CCD/CMOS sensors) for photodocumentation, telemedicine, and longitudinal monitoring. |

## Van Herick Angle Assessment Technique

The **Van Herick technique** is a rapid, non-invasive method performed at the slit lamp to estimate the depth of the [[anterior chamber angle]] and screen for risk of [[angle-closure glaucoma]]:

1. A thin, bright slit beam is directed at the **temporal limbus** at a 60° angle.
2. The clinician compares the width of the dark gap (the anterior chamber depth at the periphery) to the width of the adjacent corneal optical section.

| Grade | AC Depth : Corneal Thickness Ratio | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **Grade 4** | ≥ 1:1 | Wide open angle; no risk of closure |
| **Grade 3** | 1/4 to 1/2 : 1 | Open angle; low risk |
| **Grade 2** | 1/4 : 1 | Narrow angle; moderate risk; [[gonioscopy]] recommended |
| **Grade 1** | < 1/4 : 1 | Very narrow angle; high risk of closure; [[gonioscopy]] mandatory |
| **Slit** | Slit-like or no visible gap | Angle likely closed or occludable; urgent [[gonioscopy]] and possible intervention required |

## Pathophysiology Relevance

While the slit lamp examination itself does not have a "pathophysiology" in the traditional sense (it is a diagnostic tool, not a disease), its clinical value lies in its ability to detect and characterize the pathophysiology of a vast array of ocular conditions. The optical sectioning capability allows clinicians to:

- **Localize pathology by depth**: Distinguish between [[epithelial]], [[stromal]], and [[endothelial]] corneal disease.
- **Characterize inflammatory responses**: Grade [[anterior chamber]] cells and flare to determine the severity and type of [[uveitis]] (granulomatous vs. non-granulomatous), monitor treatment response, and detect complications such as [[posterior synechiae]] or [[cataract]] formation.
- **Identify degenerative changes**: Detect early [[cataract]] formation, [[pseudoexfoliation]], and [[pigment dispersion syndrome]].
- **Assess vascular pathology**: Identify [[neovascularization]] of the [[iris]] ([[rubeosis iridis]]) or [[cornea]], [[conjunctival injection]] patterns, and [[episcleral]] vessel engorgement.
- **Evaluate wound healing and surgical outcomes**: Post-operative assessment following [[cataract surgery]], [[corneal transplantation]] ([[penetrating keratoplasty]], [[DSAEK]], [[DMEK]]), [[refractive surgery]] ([[LASIK]], [[PRK]]), and [[glaucoma filtering surgery]] ([[trabeculectomy]] bleb assessment).
- **Detect and monitor [[glaucoma]]-related findings**: Including [[optic disc]] evaluation (with condensing lenses), [[IOP]] measurement (with [[Goldmann applanation tonometer]]), and angle assessment (with [[gonioscopy]] lenses).

## Indications

The slit lamp examination is indicated in virtually every comprehensive ophthalmic and optometric evaluation. Specific indications include but are not limited to:

- Routine comprehensive eye examination
- Evaluation of [[red eye]] and ocular surface disease
- [[Contact lens]] fitting, follow-up, and complication assessment
- Assessment of [[ocular trauma]] (including [[corneal foreign body]] localization and removal)
- Diagnosis and monitoring of [[anterior uveitis]], [[intermediate uveitis]], and [[panuveitis]]
- [[Cataract]] evaluation and grading
- [[Glaucoma]] screening and monitoring (IOP measurement, angle assessment, optic disc evaluation)
- Pre-operative and post-operative assessment for [[cataract surgery]], [[refractive surgery]], [[corneal transplantation]], and [[glaucoma surgery]]
- Evaluation of [[dry eye disease]]
- Screening for [[diabetic eye disease]] (with fundus lenses)
- Assessment of [[eyelid]] and [[adnexal]] pathology
- Monitoring of [[corneal dystrophies]] and [[corneal ectasias]]
- Evaluation of [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) position and integrity following [[cataract surgery]]

## Contraindications and Limitations

The slit lamp examination is an exceptionally safe, non-invasive procedure with **no absolute contraindications**. Relative considerations include:

- **Patient inability to cooperate**: Young children, patients with severe [[dementia]], or those with significant physical disabilities may be unable to maintain the required positioning. Portable or handheld slit lamps (e.g., [[Kowa SL-15]], [[Keeler PSL]]) can be used for bedside or pediatric examinations.
- **Severe ocular trauma with globe rupture**: Caution must be exercised to avoid applying pressure to the globe. [[Applanation tonometry]] and contact lens-based fundoscopy are contraindicated in suspected [[open globe injury]].
- **Photophobia**: Patients with severe [[photophobia]] (e.g., acute [[iritis]]) may have difficulty tolerating the bright illumination; reducing light intensity and using shorter examination times can mitigate discomfort.

**Limitations** of the standard slit lamp include:

- Limited view of the peripheral [[retina]] without supplementary lenses.
- Inability to image structures posterior to dense media opacities (e.g., mature [[cataract]]).
- Subjective grading systems (e.g., for cells, flare, and cataract) may introduce inter-observer variability.
- Standard slit lamps do not provide quantitative measurements of structures (though attachments such as pachymeters and imaging systems can supplement this).

## Documentation and Grading Systems

Accurate documentation of slit lamp findings is essential for clinical communication, medicolegal purposes, and longitudinal monitoring. Findings are typically recorded using:

- **Standardized grading scales**: SUN criteria for [[anterior chamber]] cells and flare; LOCS III for [[cataract]] grading; Efron grading scales for [[contact lens]]-related complications.
- **Drawings**: Annotated diagrams of the [[cornea]], [[anterior segment]], and [[lens]] indicating the location, size, and depth of findings.
- **Photography**: Slit lamp photodocumentation using integrated or external camera systems provides objective, reproducible records. Anterior segment [[optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT) may supplement slit lamp findings.
- **Descriptive terminology**: Clinicians should use precise, standardized terminology (e.g., "2+ fine, non-granulomatous [[keratic precipitates]] on the inferior corneal endothelium" rather than vague descriptions).

## Advances and Emerging Technologies

Modern slit lamp biomicroscopy continues to evolve:

- **Digital slit lamp imaging**: High-resolution CCD/CMOS cameras integrated into slit lamps allow instant photodocumentation, telemedicine consultation, and AI-assisted analysis.
- **Anterior segment [[optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT)**: Provides cross-sectional imaging of the [[cornea]], [[anterior chamber]], [[iridocorneal angle]], and [[crystalline lens]] with micron-level resolution, complementing slit lamp findings.
- **[[Confocal microscopy]]**: In vivo confocal microscopy (e.g., Heidelberg Retina Tomograph with Rostock Cornea Module) allows cellular-level imaging of the [[cornea]], including [[endothelial cell]] counts, [[nerve fiber]] assessment, and identification of infectious organisms (e.g., [[Acanthamoeba]] cysts).
- **Artificial intelligence (AI)**: Machine learning algorithms are being developed to automatically detect and grade anterior segment pathology from slit lamp images, including [[cataract]], [[pterygium]], and [[corneal opacities]].
- **Portable and smartphone-adapted slit lamps**: Devices such as the [[EyeGo adapter]] and various smartphone-based slit lamp systems are expanding access to biomicroscopy in resource-limited settings and telemedicine applications.

## Clinical Significance in Diagnosis and Treatment Monitoring

The slit lamp examination is not merely a diagnostic tool—it is integral to **treatment planning and monitoring**:

- **[[Anterior uveitis]] management**: Serial slit lamp examinations to grade [[anterior chamber]] cells and flare guide the tapering of [[topical corticosteroids]] (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate 1%]]) and [[cycloplegic agents]] (e.g., [[cyclopentolate]], [[atropine]]).
- **[[Dry eye disease]] management**: TBUT, corneal staining patterns, and [[meibomian gland]] expression findings guide the selection of [[artificial tears]], [[anti-inflammatory agents]] (e.g., [[cyclosporine A]], [[lifitegrast]]), [[punctal plugs]], or [[intense pulsed light]] (IPL) therapy.
- **[[Contact lens]] complications**: Slit lamp findings of [[corneal neovascularization]], [[giant papillary conjunctivitis]], [[microbial keratitis]], or [[corneal infiltrative events]] dictate lens discontinuation, refitting, or pharmacological treatment.
- **Post-surgical monitoring**: Following [[cataract surgery]], the slit lamp is used to assess [[wound integrity]], [[anterior chamber]] depth and inflammation, [[IOL]] centration, and [[posterior capsular opacification]] (PCO). Following [[corneal transplantation]], it monitors graft clarity, [[endothelial rejection]] lines ([[Khodadoust line]]), and [[epithelial rejection]].
- **[[Glaucoma]] management**: [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]] performed at the slit lamp provides [[IOP]] measurements critical for treatment titration. Gonioscopy performed at the slit lamp guides decisions regarding [[laser peripheral iridotomy]] (LPI), [[selective laser trabeculoplasty]] (SLT), or surgical intervention.

---

## Patient Education

Patients should be informed that:

- The slit lamp examination is **painless and non-invasive**.
- Bright light will be directed into the eye, which may cause temporary discomfort or [[afterimages]].
- Diagnostic drops (e.g., [[fluorescein]], [[topical anesthetic]], [[mydriatic agents]]) may be instilled depending on the clinical indication.
- The examination typically takes **5–15 minutes** depending on the complexity of findings.
- Findings will be explained and documented, and any necessary follow-up or treatment will be discussed.

---

## Summary

The **slit lamp examination** remains the most versatile, widely used, and clinically essential diagnostic instrument in [[Optometry]] and [[Ophthalmology]]. Its ability to provide magnified, stereoscopic, optically sectioned views of virtually every anterior segment structure—and, with accessory lenses, the posterior segment—makes it irreplaceable in the detection, diagnosis, grading, and monitoring of an extraordinarily broad spectrum of ocular conditions. Mastery of slit lamp techniques, illumination methods, and supplementary lens use is a foundational competency for every eye care professional.
