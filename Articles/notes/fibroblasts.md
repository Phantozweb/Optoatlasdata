# Fibroblasts in Ocular Biology and Eye Care

---

## Overview

**Fibroblasts** are mesenchymal-derived, spindle-shaped cells that constitute the principal cellular component of [[connective tissue]] throughout the body, including virtually every tissue of the [[eye]] and its adnexa. In the context of [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]], fibroblasts are of paramount clinical importance because they are the primary effector cells responsible for the synthesis, deposition, remodeling, and degradation of the [[extracellular matrix]] (ECM). Their activity directly governs processes such as [[wound healing]], [[fibrosis]], [[scarring]], and tissue homeostasis in ocular structures including the [[cornea]], [[conjunctiva]], [[sclera]], [[Tenon's capsule]], [[trabecular meshwork]], [[lamina cribrosa]], [[orbital tissues]], and the [[retina]].

Understanding fibroblast biology is essential for the eye care professional because dysregulated fibroblast activity underlies the pathophysiology of numerous sight-threatening conditions—from [[corneal scarring]] and [[pterygium]] to [[proliferative vitreoretinopathy]] (PVR), [[glaucoma]] filtration surgery failure, and [[thyroid eye disease]]. This article provides a comprehensive clinical review of fibroblast biology as it pertains to ocular health, disease, diagnosis, and therapeutic management.

---

## Cell Biology and Characterization

### Origin and Differentiation

Fibroblasts originate from [[mesenchymal stem cells]] (MSCs) during embryonic development. In the eye, many fibroblast populations—particularly those of the [[corneal stroma]] (known as [[keratocytes]])—are derived from the [[neural crest]], distinguishing them from fibroblasts elsewhere in the body. This neural crest origin confers unique phenotypic and functional properties, including the capacity to maintain corneal transparency through the production of [[crystallins]] and highly organized [[collagen]] fibrils.

Fibroblasts exist along a phenotypic continuum:

| **Phenotype** | **Characteristics** | **Ocular Relevance** |
|---|---|---|
| **Quiescent fibroblast (keratocyte)** | Low metabolic activity, dendritic morphology, produces [[keratan sulfate]] proteoglycans, maintains ECM homeostasis | Normal [[corneal stroma]] transparency |
| **Activated fibroblast** | Increased proliferation, migration, and ECM synthesis; loss of crystallin expression | Early [[corneal wound healing]], conjunctival inflammation |
| **[[Myofibroblast]]** | Expresses [[alpha-smooth muscle actin]] (α-SMA), generates contractile force, excessive ECM deposition | [[Corneal haze]], [[scarring]], [[fibrosis]], filtration bleb failure, [[PVR]] |
| **Fibrocyte** | Circulating bone marrow–derived precursor that can differentiate into fibroblast/myofibroblast | [[Thyroid eye disease]], orbital fibrosis |

The transition from quiescent fibroblast to myofibroblast is a critical event in ocular pathology and is primarily driven by [[transforming growth factor-beta]] ([[TGF-β]]), particularly the TGF-β1 and TGF-β2 isoforms. Other mediators include [[platelet-derived growth factor]] ([[PDGF]]), [[connective tissue growth factor]] ([[CTGF]]/CCN2), [[interleukin-1]] ([[IL-1]]), [[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] ([[TNF-α]]), and mechanical stress.

### Key Molecular Markers

Fibroblasts are identified and characterized using a panel of molecular markers:

| **Marker** | **Significance** |
|---|---|
| [[Vimentin]] | Intermediate filament protein; general mesenchymal/fibroblast marker |
| [[Alpha-smooth muscle actin]] (α-SMA) | Hallmark of myofibroblast differentiation |
| [[Fibronectin]] (EDA-fibronectin) | Splice variant associated with fibroblast activation and fibrosis |
| [[CD34]] | Expressed by quiescent stromal fibroblasts; lost upon activation |
| [[Thy-1]] (CD90) | Surface glycoprotein; subpopulation marker influencing fibrogenic potential |
| [[ALDH3A1]] / [[Transketolase]] | Corneal crystallins expressed by quiescent keratocytes; lost in activated fibroblasts |
| [[Lumican]], [[Keratocan]] | [[Keratan sulfate]] proteoglycans specific to corneal keratocytes |

## Ocular Distribution and Tissue-Specific Roles

### Corneal Keratocytes / Corneal Fibroblasts

The [[corneal stroma]] comprises approximately 90% of corneal thickness and is populated by a network of quiescent fibroblasts known as **[[keratocytes]]**. These cells are responsible for maintaining the highly organized lamellar arrangement of [[type I collagen]] and [[type V collagen]] fibrils, along with the production of [[proteoglycans]] such as [[lumican]], [[keratocan]], and [[decorin]], which regulate interfibrillar spacing and are essential for [[corneal transparency]].

Upon injury—whether from trauma, infection ([[keratitis]]), surgery ([[photorefractive keratectomy|PRK]], [[LASIK]]), or chemical burn—keratocytes undergo [[apoptosis]] in the immediate wound vicinity, followed by activation, proliferation, and migration of surrounding keratocytes into the wound bed. Under the influence of [[TGF-β]] released from the [[corneal epithelium]] and [[tear film]], these activated fibroblasts may further differentiate into [[myofibroblasts]], leading to disorganized ECM deposition, loss of corneal crystallins, and clinically significant [[corneal haze]] or [[corneal scarring]].

### Conjunctival Fibroblasts

[[Conjunctival]] fibroblasts reside within the [[substantia propria]] of the conjunctiva and play a central role in conjunctival wound healing, inflammation, and fibrosis. They are critically important in the context of:

- **[[Glaucoma]] filtration surgery**: Following [[trabeculectomy]] or implantation of [[glaucoma drainage devices]], conjunctival and sub-Tenon's fibroblast proliferation and collagen deposition are the primary causes of bleb fibrosis and surgical failure.
- **[[Pterygium]]**: Pterygium-derived fibroblasts exhibit an abnormally activated, proliferative, and fibrogenic phenotype, with elevated expression of [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs), growth factors, and inflammatory cytokines.
- **[[Ocular cicatricial pemphigoid]]** and **[[Stevens-Johnson syndrome]]**: Subconjunctival fibrosis driven by chronic fibroblast activation leads to [[symblepharon]], [[fornix foreshortening]], and [[ocular surface disease]].

### Tenon's Capsule Fibroblasts

[[Tenon's capsule]] fibroblasts are of particular surgical significance. Their proliferative and fibrogenic response is the principal determinant of outcome following [[trabeculectomy]]. Modulation of these fibroblasts with antifibrotic agents such as [[mitomycin C]] (MMC) and [[5-fluorouracil]] (5-FU) is standard practice in glaucoma surgery.

### Trabecular Meshwork Cells

While the cells of the [[trabecular meshwork]] (TM) share fibroblast-like characteristics, they are a specialized cell type. However, fibrotic changes within the TM—including increased ECM deposition, cross-linking, and stiffening driven by TGF-β2 (which is elevated in the [[aqueous humor]] of patients with [[primary open-angle glaucoma]])—contribute to increased [[outflow resistance]] and elevated [[intraocular pressure]] ([[IOP]]).

### Lamina Cribrosa Fibroblasts

Fibroblasts within the [[lamina cribrosa]] (LC) of the [[optic nerve head]] are implicated in the pathogenesis of [[glaucomatous optic neuropathy]]. Under conditions of elevated IOP or altered biomechanical stress, LC fibroblasts become activated, increase ECM production (particularly [[collagen type I]], [[collagen type IV]], and [[elastin]]), and may differentiate into myofibroblasts, contributing to structural remodeling of the LC and subsequent [[retinal ganglion cell]] axon damage.

### Orbital Fibroblasts

[[Orbital fibroblasts]] are the key effector cells in **[[thyroid eye disease]]** (TED, also known as [[Graves' orbitopathy]]). These fibroblasts express the [[thyrotropin receptor]] (TSHR) and [[insulin-like growth factor-1 receptor]] (IGF-1R), making them targets of autoantibodies. Upon stimulation, orbital fibroblasts:

- Proliferate and differentiate into [[adipocytes]] (contributing to orbital fat expansion)
- Produce excessive [[hyaluronan]] (glycosaminoglycan), leading to tissue edema
- Secrete pro-inflammatory cytokines ([[IL-6]], [[IL-8]], [[RANTES]])
- Differentiate into myofibroblasts, contributing to [[extraocular muscle]] fibrosis and restrictive [[strabismus]]

### Retinal and Vitreal Fibroblasts

In **[[proliferative vitreoretinopathy]]** (PVR), fibroblasts—along with [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE) cells, [[glial cells]], and [[macrophages]]—form contractile epiretinal and subretinal membranes. These fibroblasts/myofibroblasts generate tractional forces that cause [[retinal detachment]] recurrence following [[vitreoretinal surgery]]. The fibroblast-to-myofibroblast transition, driven by [[TGF-β2]] in the [[vitreous humor]], is a central pathogenic mechanism.

## Pathophysiology of Fibroblast-Mediated Ocular Disease

The pathophysiology of fibroblast-related ocular conditions follows a common cascade:

1. **Tissue injury or inflammatory stimulus** → Release of cytokines and growth factors (TGF-β, PDGF, FGF, IL-1, TNF-α) from damaged epithelium, inflammatory cells, and platelets.
2. **Fibroblast activation** → Quiescent fibroblasts/keratocytes lose their resting phenotype, upregulate proliferation, migration, and ECM synthesis.
3. **Myofibroblast differentiation** → Under sustained TGF-β signaling and mechanical tension, fibroblasts express α-SMA and acquire contractile properties.
4. **Excessive ECM deposition** → Disorganized collagen (types I, III), fibronectin, and tenascin accumulate, disrupting tissue architecture.
5. **Tissue contraction and remodeling** → Myofibroblast-mediated contraction leads to scarring, distortion, and loss of function (e.g., corneal opacity, bleb failure, epiretinal membrane contraction).
6. **Failure of resolution** → In pathological fibrosis, myofibroblasts resist [[apoptosis]] and persist, perpetuating the fibrotic cycle.

### Key Signaling Pathways

| **Pathway** | **Role in Fibroblast Biology** | **Ocular Relevance** |
|---|---|---|
| [[TGF-β]]/[[Smad]] signaling | Master regulator of fibroblast-to-myofibroblast transition; induces α-SMA, collagen, fibronectin | Corneal haze, bleb fibrosis, PVR, TED |
| [[Wnt]]/β-catenin | Promotes fibroblast proliferation and fibrogenesis | Corneal and conjunctival fibrosis |
| [[Rho]]/[[ROCK]] | Regulates cytoskeletal dynamics, contractility, and mechanotransduction | Trabecular meshwork stiffening, myofibroblast contraction |
| [[MAPK]]/[[ERK]] | Mediates proliferative responses to growth factors | Pterygium fibroblast proliferation |
| [[PI3K]]/[[Akt]]/[[mTOR]] | Cell survival, proliferation, and resistance to apoptosis | Myofibroblast persistence in fibrosis |
| [[Hippo]]/[[YAP]]/[[TAZ]] | Mechanosensitive transcriptional regulators of fibroblast activation | Emerging target in ocular fibrosis |
| [[NF-κB]] | Pro-inflammatory signaling in fibroblasts | Orbital fibroblast activation in TED |

## Clinical Presentation (Symptoms and Signs)

Fibroblasts themselves do not produce "symptoms" in isolation; rather, their dysregulated activity manifests through the clinical features of the conditions they drive. The following table summarizes fibroblast-mediated clinical presentations relevant to eye care:

| **Condition** | **Clinical Signs** | **Symptoms** | **Fibroblast Role** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Corneal haze]] / [[corneal scarring]] | Stromal opacity, irregular [[astigmatism]], reduced [[visual acuity]] | Blurred vision, glare, photophobia | Myofibroblast persistence, disorganized collagen |
| Post-[[PRK]] haze | Subepithelial haze (grade 0.5–4), regression | Decreased vision, halos | Anterior stromal myofibroblast formation |
| [[Pterygium]] | Fibrovascular growth over [[limbus]] onto cornea | Irritation, redness, visual disturbance if axis involved | Activated fibroblasts with MMP overexpression |
| [[Trabeculectomy]] bleb failure | Flat, vascularized, fibrosed bleb; elevated IOP | Asymptomatic or symptoms of elevated IOP | Tenon's/conjunctival fibroblast proliferation and scarring |
| [[Proliferative vitreoretinopathy]] | Epiretinal/subretinal membranes, fixed retinal folds, tractional detachment | Vision loss, visual field defects | Fibroblast/myofibroblast membrane contraction |
| [[Thyroid eye disease]] | [[Proptosis]], [[lid retraction]], [[diplopia]], [[optic neuropathy]], periorbital edema | Pain, pressure, double vision, vision loss | Orbital fibroblast hyaluronan production, adipogenesis, fibrosis |
| [[Ocular cicatricial pemphigoid]] | Progressive conjunctival scarring, symblepharon, [[entropion]], [[trichiasis]] | Chronic irritation, tearing, pain, vision loss | Subconjunctival fibroblast-driven fibrosis |
| [[Corneal dystrophies]] (certain types) | Stromal opacities, recurrent erosions | Pain, photophobia, decreased vision | Keratocyte dysfunction in ECM production |

## Diagnostic Modalities

Assessment of fibroblast-mediated pathology in clinical practice relies on a combination of clinical examination, imaging, and, in research or specialized settings, histopathological and molecular analysis.

### Clinical Examination

- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: Essential for evaluating corneal haze (graded 0–4 on the Fantes scale), corneal scarring depth and density, pterygium extent, conjunctival fibrosis, and bleb morphology (using the [[Indiana Bleb Appearance Grading Scale]] or [[Moorfields Bleb Grading System]]).
- **[[Visual acuity]] testing**: Quantifies functional impact of fibroblast-mediated corneal opacity or surface irregularity.
- **[[Tonometry]]**: Monitors [[IOP]] in the context of trabecular meshwork fibrosis or post-trabeculectomy bleb failure.
- **[[Exophthalmometry]]**: Hertel exophthalmometry quantifies [[proptosis]] in [[thyroid eye disease]].

### Imaging

| **Modality** | **Application** |
|---|---|
| **[[Anterior segment OCT]] (AS-OCT)** | High-resolution cross-sectional imaging of corneal haze depth, stromal scarring, bleb wall thickness and morphology |
| **[[Confocal microscopy]] (in vivo)** | Visualization of activated keratocytes/fibroblasts, myofibroblasts (highly reflective cells), nerve plexus changes in the corneal stroma |
| **[[Corneal topography]] / [[tomography]]** | Assessment of irregular astigmatism secondary to scarring |
| **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | Evaluation of bleb internal structure, scleral flap patency |
| **[[Orbital CT]] / [[MRI]]** | Assessment of extraocular muscle enlargement, orbital fat expansion in TED |
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (retinal OCT)** | Detection and monitoring of epiretinal membranes, PVR membranes |
| **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** | Evaluation of vitreous membranes and tractional retinal detachment |

### Histopathology and Immunohistochemistry

In excised tissue specimens (e.g., pterygium, epiretinal membranes, failed bleb tissue), fibroblasts and myofibroblasts are identified by:

- **Hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining**: Spindle-shaped cells within collagenous stroma
- **Immunostaining for α-SMA**: Confirms myofibroblast phenotype
- **Immunostaining for [[vimentin]]**: Confirms mesenchymal origin
- **[[Masson's trichrome stain]]**: Highlights collagen deposition (fibrosis)
- **Immunostaining for [[Ki-67]]**: Assesses proliferative index

### Molecular and Research-Based Diagnostics

- **[[Flow cytometry]]**: Phenotyping of fibroblast subpopulations (CD34, Thy-1, α-SMA)
- **[[Gene expression profiling]]** (RT-PCR, RNA sequencing): Quantification of fibrogenic gene expression (COL1A1, ACTA2, FN1, CTGF)
- **[[ELISA]]**: Measurement of TGF-β, CTGF, and other cytokines in tear fluid, aqueous humor, or vitreous
- **[[Cell culture]]**: Primary fibroblast cultures from corneal, conjunctival, Tenon's, or orbital tissue for in vitro drug testing and mechanistic studies

## Treatment and Management

Therapeutic strategies targeting fibroblasts in ocular disease aim to: (1) prevent excessive fibroblast activation and myofibroblast differentiation, (2) reduce ECM overproduction, (3) promote organized tissue remodeling, and (4) induce myofibroblast apoptosis. Approaches span pharmacological, surgical, and emerging biological therapies.

### Pharmacological Interventions

#### Antimetabolites

| **Agent** | **Mechanism** | **Clinical Use** | **Route** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Mitomycin C]] (MMC)** | Alkylating agent; inhibits fibroblast proliferation and induces apoptosis by cross-linking DNA | Intraoperative application during [[trabeculectomy]], [[pterygium]] excision, [[PRK]] (0.02% for 12–120 seconds) | Topical (sponge application) |
| **[[5-Fluorouracil]] (5-FU)** | Antimetabolite; inhibits thymidylate synthase, blocking DNA synthesis in proliferating fibroblasts | Post-trabeculectomy subconjunctival injection (5 mg/0.1 mL) to rescue failing blebs | Subconjunctival injection |

#### Corticosteroids

[[Corticosteroids]] suppress fibroblast proliferation, reduce inflammatory cytokine production, and inhibit TGF-β–mediated myofibroblast differentiation. They are a mainstay in managing:

- Post-surgical inflammation and fibrosis prevention (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate]], [[dexamethasone]])
- [[Thyroid eye disease]] (intravenous [[methylprednisolone]] pulse therapy)
- Post-PRK haze prophylaxis

#### Anti-TGF-β Strategies

Given the central role of [[TGF-β]] in fibroblast activation:

- **[[Pirfenidone]]**: An antifibrotic agent that inhibits TGF-β signaling, reduces fibroblast proliferation, and decreases collagen synthesis. Under investigation for ocular fibrosis (topical formulations).
- **[[Decorin]]**: A naturally occurring proteoglycan that sequesters TGF-β. Recombinant decorin has shown promise in reducing corneal scarring in preclinical models.
- **Anti-TGF-β antibodies** (e.g., [[lerdelimumab]], [[fresolimumab]]): Investigated in clinical trials for conjunctival scarring post-glaucoma surgery with mixed results.

#### ROCK Inhibitors

[[Rho-associated kinase]] ([[ROCK]]) inhibitors such as [[netarsudil]] and [[ripasudil]] reduce fibroblast contractility, inhibit myofibroblast differentiation, and decrease ECM stiffness. While primarily used for IOP reduction in [[glaucoma]], their antifibrotic properties on trabecular meshwork and potentially on conjunctival fibroblasts are of growing interest.

#### Anti-VEGF Agents

[[Bevacizumab]] and [[ranibizumab]] have been used as adjuncts in pterygium surgery and to modulate bleb vascularity, indirectly affecting fibroblast activity by reducing angiogenesis-associated growth factor signaling.

#### Immunomodulatory Agents for Thyroid Eye Disease

- **[[Teprotumumab]]**: A monoclonal antibody targeting [[IGF-1R]] on orbital fibroblasts. FDA-approved for TED, it dramatically reduces proptosis and diplopia by inhibiting orbital fibroblast activation, hyaluronan production, and adipogenesis. This represents a landmark in fibroblast-targeted therapy.
- **[[Tocilizumab]]**: An [[IL-6 receptor]] antagonist that reduces orbital fibroblast inflammatory signaling.
- **[[Mycophenolate mofetil]]**: Inhibits lymphocyte and fibroblast proliferation; used as a steroid-sparing agent in TED.

### Surgical Approaches

#### Corneal Surgery

- **[[Phototherapeutic keratectomy]] (PTK)**: Excimer laser ablation of superficial corneal scars. Post-PTK, MMC application (0.02%) is used to prevent recurrent fibroblast-mediated haze.
- **[[Corneal transplantation]]** ([[penetrating keratoplasty]], [[deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]]): For dense, visually significant corneal scars unresponsive to conservative management. Fibroblast activity in the graft-host interface influences graft clarity.
- **[[Corneal cross-linking]] (CXL)**: While primarily for [[keratoconus]], CXL modulates keratocyte/fibroblast behavior and ECM cross-linking.

#### Glaucoma Surgery

- **[[Trabeculectomy]]** with intraoperative MMC or 5-FU: Antifibrotic agents are applied to modulate Tenon's capsule and conjunctival fibroblast response.
- **[[Glaucoma drainage devices]]**: Fibroblast encapsulation of the plate determines bleb function; antifibrotic strategies are sometimes employed.
- **Bleb needling with 5-FU**: Mechanical disruption of fibrotic tissue combined with antimetabolite injection to rescue failing blebs.
- **[[Minimally invasive glaucoma surgery]] (MIGS)**: Procedures such as [[iStent]], [[Hydrus]], and [[Xen gel stent]] aim to minimize conjunctival fibroblast stimulation compared to traditional surgery.

#### Vitreoretinal Surgery

- **[[Pars plana vitrectomy]]** with membrane peeling: Removal of fibroblast-containing epiretinal and subretinal membranes in PVR.
- Adjunctive pharmacotherapy (e.g., intravitreal [[daunorubicin]], [[5-FU]], [[low-molecular-weight heparin]]) has been investigated to reduce fibroblast proliferation in PVR, though clinical adoption remains limited.

#### Pterygium Surgery

- Excision with [[conjunctival autograft]] or [[amniotic membrane transplantation]] (AMT): [[Amniotic membrane]] has inherent anti-inflammatory and antifibrotic properties, suppressing fibroblast activation and TGF-β signaling.
- Adjunctive MMC (intraoperative) or [[beta-irradiation]] to reduce recurrence driven by fibroblast proliferation.

### Emerging and Investigational Therapies

| **Therapy** | **Mechanism** | **Status** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Suberoylanilide hydroxamic acid]] (SAHA/Vorinostat)** | Histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor; suppresses myofibroblast differentiation | Preclinical (corneal fibrosis models) |
| **[[Losartan]]** (topical) | Angiotensin II receptor blocker; reduces TGF-β signaling | Preclinical/early clinical (corneal scarring) |
| **Gene therapy (TGF-β decoy receptors)** | Blocks TGF-β at the receptor level | Preclinical |
| **[[siRNA]] targeting CTGF or TGF-β** | RNA interference to silence profibrotic gene expression | Preclinical |
| **[[Nintedanib]]** | Tyrosine kinase inhibitor (PDGFR, FGFR, VEGFR); antifibrotic | Approved for pulmonary fibrosis; under investigation for ocular fibrosis |
| **Stem cell–based therapies** | Replacement of scarred keratocytes with corneal stromal stem cells | Early clinical trials |
| **[[Rosiglitazone]]** (PPARγ agonist) | Inhibits orbital fibroblast differentiation to myofibroblasts | Preclinical (TED models) |
| **Anti-[[LOXL2]] antibodies** | Inhibit collagen cross-linking enzyme; reduce fibrosis | Preclinical |

## Fibroblast Heterogeneity and Clinical Implications

Recent single-cell RNA sequencing studies have revealed significant **heterogeneity** among ocular fibroblast populations. Not all fibroblasts are equally fibrogenic; subpopulations defined by markers such as [[Thy-1]] (CD90), [[CD34]], [[FAP]] (fibroblast activation protein), and [[PDPN]] (podoplanin) exhibit distinct functional profiles. For example:

- **Thy-1–positive** corneal fibroblasts show greater myofibroblast differentiation potential than Thy-1–negative cells.
- **CD34-positive** stromal fibroblasts represent a quiescent, anti-fibrotic population that is lost during tissue injury.
- Understanding this heterogeneity may enable **precision antifibrotic therapy**, targeting only the pathogenic fibroblast subpopulation while preserving beneficial wound healing functions.

## Fibroblasts in Ocular Surface Disease and Dry Eye

Chronic [[ocular surface inflammation]], as seen in severe [[dry eye disease]], [[meibomian gland dysfunction]], and [[allergic eye disease]], can lead to subconjunctival fibrosis through sustained fibroblast activation. Fibroblasts in the [[lacrimal gland]] also contribute to gland fibrosis in conditions such as [[Sjögren's syndrome]], ultimately reducing [[aqueous tear production]] and exacerbating the dry eye cycle.

---

## Fibroblasts and Ocular Oncology

Fibroblasts within the tumor microenvironment—termed **cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs)**—play a role in the progression of ocular and periocular malignancies, including [[uveal melanoma]], [[conjunctival squamous cell carcinoma]], and [[orbital lymphoma]]. CAFs promote tumor growth, angiogenesis, immune evasion, and metastasis through paracrine signaling. This is an emerging area of research with potential therapeutic implications.

## Key Clinical Pearls for the Eye Care Professional

1. **Corneal haze after surface ablation** (PRK) is directly caused by anterior stromal myofibroblast formation; prophylactic MMC application significantly reduces this risk.
2. **Bleb survival** after trabeculectomy is fundamentally a battle against Tenon's capsule fibroblast proliferation—antimetabolite use is evidence-based standard of care.
3. **Teprotumumab** represents the first fibroblast-targeted biologic therapy in ophthalmology, revolutionizing the management of thyroid eye disease.
4. **In vivo confocal microscopy** is the only clinical tool that allows direct, non-invasive visualization of activated fibroblasts and myofibroblasts in the living cornea.
5. **Amniotic membrane** is a powerful biological antifibrotic agent, suppressing TGF-β signaling and myofibroblast differentiation across multiple ocular surface applications.
6. The **TGF-β/Smad pathway** is the single most important therapeutic target in ocular fibrosis research.
