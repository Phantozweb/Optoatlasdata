

# Miosis

**Miosis** (from the Greek *meiōsis*, meaning "lessening") refers to the constriction of the [[pupil]] to a diameter of less than 2 millimeters (mm), resulting from contraction of the [[iris sphincter muscle]] (musculus sphincter pupillae). While physiological miosis occurs as a normal response to bright light and near-vision accommodation, pathological miosis arises from a diverse array of neurological, pharmacological, inflammatory, and systemic etiologies. Understanding the mechanisms, differential diagnosis, and clinical management of miosis is essential for every [[optometrist]] and [[ophthalmologist]], as pupillary abnormalities frequently serve as critical diagnostic indicators of both ocular and systemic disease.

Miosis is the functional opposite of [[mydriasis]] (pupillary dilation). The term should not be confused with **meiosis**, the process of cell division in reproductive biology, nor with **myosis**, an archaic and now-discouraged spelling variant.

## Anatomy and Physiology of Pupillary Constriction

### Relevant Ocular Anatomy

The [[pupil]] is the central aperture of the [[iris]], a pigmented, contractile diaphragm situated between the [[anterior chamber]] and the [[posterior chamber]] of the eye. Two smooth muscles embedded within the iris stroma govern pupil size:

| Muscle | Innervation | Action | Fiber Orientation |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Iris sphincter muscle]] (sphincter pupillae) | [[Parasympathetic nervous system]] via [[cranial nerve III]] ([[oculomotor nerve]]) | Constriction (miosis) | Circular (concentric around the pupil margin) |
| [[Iris dilator muscle]] (dilator pupillae) | [[Sympathetic nervous system]] via long [[ciliary nerves]] | Dilation (mydriasis) | Radial (extending from the pupil margin to the iris root) |

The balance between the tonic activity of these two muscles determines resting pupil diameter under any given set of conditions.

### The Parasympathetic Pathway (Miosis Pathway)

The efferent parasympathetic pathway responsible for miosis follows a well-defined two-neuron arc:

1. **Preganglionic neuron**: Cell bodies reside in the [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]] (accessory oculomotor nucleus) in the dorsal [[midbrain]]. Preganglionic parasympathetic fibers travel within the [[oculomotor nerve]] (CN III), coursing along its inferior division, to synapse in the [[ciliary ganglion]] located in the posterior [[orbit]].

2. **Postganglionic neuron**: Short [[postganglionic fibers]] exit the ciliary ganglion as the [[short ciliary nerves]] (approximately 6–10 in number), which penetrate the [[sclera]] near the [[optic nerve]] and travel anteriorly within the [[suprachoroidal space]] to innervate both the [[iris sphincter muscle]] and the [[ciliary muscle]].

The neurotransmitter at both the preganglionic synapse (in the ciliary ganglion) and the postganglionic neuroeffector junction (at the iris sphincter) is [[acetylcholine]] (ACh), acting on [[nicotinic receptors]] at the ganglion and [[muscarinic receptors]] (specifically the M3 subtype) at the effector muscle.

### The Pupillary Light Reflex

The [[pupillary light reflex]] is the most clinically significant reflex arc producing miosis:

1. **Afferent limb**: Light stimulates [[photoreceptors]] ([[rods]] and [[cones]]) in the [[retina]]. Signals are transmitted via [[retinal ganglion cells]]—specifically, the intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells ([[ipRGCs]]) containing [[melanopsin]]—through the [[optic nerve]] (CN II), [[optic chiasm]], and [[optic tract]] to the [[pretectal olivary nucleus]] in the [[midbrain]].

2. **Interneuron**: From the pretectal nucleus, fibers project bilaterally to both the ipsilateral and contralateral [[Edinger-Westphal nuclei]]. This bilateral projection accounts for the **consensual light reflex**—illumination of one eye produces miosis in both eyes.

3. **Efferent limb**: As described above, parasympathetic fibers travel via CN III → ciliary ganglion → short ciliary nerves → iris sphincter muscle → **miosis**.

### The Near Response (Accommodation Reflex)

Miosis also occurs as one component of the [[near triad]] (also called the [[accommodation reflex]]), which comprises:

- **Accommodation** (increased [[crystalline lens]] convexity via [[ciliary muscle]] contraction)
- **Convergence** (medial rectus–mediated adduction of both eyes)
- **Miosis** (pupillary constriction to increase depth of focus)

The supranuclear pathway for the near response is distinct from the light reflex pathway, originating in the [[occipital cortex]] and [[frontal eye fields]] rather than the pretectal nucleus. This anatomical distinction is clinically significant in conditions such as [[Argyll Robertson pupil]], where the light reflex is abolished but the near response is preserved ("light-near dissociation").

## Pathophysiology

Miosis can be broadly categorized based on its underlying mechanism:

### 1. Physiological Miosis

- **Light reflex**: Bright ambient illumination produces bilateral pupillary constriction.
- **Near response**: Focusing on near objects triggers miosis as part of the near triad.
- **Sleep**: Pupils constrict during sleep due to reduced sympathetic tone and unopposed parasympathetic activity.
- **Age-related miosis (senile miosis)**: Progressive reduction in pupil size with advancing age, attributed to [[iris atrophy]], increased rigidity of the iris stroma, and decreased sympathetic tone. Resting pupil diameter may decrease from approximately 4–5 mm in young adults to 2–3 mm in elderly individuals. This phenomenon is sometimes termed **"senile miosis"** and can reduce [[retinal illuminance]], contributing to difficulty with scotopic (low-light) vision in the elderly.

### 2. Pharmacological Miosis

Numerous pharmacological agents produce miosis through either direct parasympathomimetic stimulation of the iris sphincter or indirect inhibition of sympathetic pupillary dilation:

| Drug Class | Mechanism | Examples | Clinical Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Direct-acting parasympathomimetics (miotics)** | Directly stimulate muscarinic (M3) receptors on the iris sphincter | [[Pilocarpine]], [[carbachol]], [[acetylcholine]] (intraocular) | [[Glaucoma]] treatment, intraoperative miosis, reversal of mydriasis |
| **Indirect-acting parasympathomimetics (anticholinesterases)** | Inhibit [[acetylcholinesterase]], increasing ACh concentration at the neuroeffector junction | [[Physostigmine]], [[echothiophate iodide]], [[demecarium bromide]] | Glaucoma (historically), accommodative [[esotropia]] |
| **Opioids (systemic)** | Stimulate the [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]] centrally, increasing parasympathetic outflow | [[Morphine]], [[heroin]], [[fentanyl]], [[codeine]], [[methadone]] | Analgesic (miosis is a side effect); "pinpoint pupils" are a hallmark of opioid intoxication |
| **Alpha-adrenergic antagonists** | Block sympathetic α1-receptors on the iris dilator muscle | [[Thymoxamine]] (moxisylyte), [[dapiprazole]] | Reversal of diagnostic mydriasis |
| **Sympatholytic agents (central)** | Reduce central sympathetic outflow | [[Clonidine]], [[guanfacine]], [[methyldopa]] | Antihypertensive (miosis is a side effect) |
| **Cholinergic agonists (systemic)** | Generalized parasympathetic stimulation | [[Bethanechol]], [[pilocarpine]] (oral) | Sjögren syndrome, xerostomia (miosis is a side effect) |
| **Organophosphate compounds** | Irreversible acetylcholinesterase inhibition | Nerve agents ([[sarin]], [[VX]]), pesticides ([[malathion]], [[parathion]]) | Toxicological emergency; miosis is a cardinal sign of organophosphate poisoning |
| **Antipsychotics / phenothiazines** | Alpha-adrenergic blockade and variable cholinergic effects | [[Chlorpromazine]], [[thioridazine]] | Psychiatric treatment (miosis is a side effect) |
| **Prostaglandin analogs** | Mild miotic effect via iris sphincter stimulation (clinically minimal) | [[Latanoprost]], [[travoprost]] | Glaucoma treatment |

### 3. Neurological Miosis

#### a. Horner Syndrome ([[Horner syndrome]])

[[Horner syndrome]] (oculosympathetic paresis) is the most clinically important neurological cause of miosis. It results from interruption of the [[sympathetic pathway]] at any point along its three-neuron arc:

- **First-order (central) neuron**: Hypothalamus → ciliospinal center of Budge (C8–T2 in the [[spinal cord]]). Lesions: stroke ([[lateral medullary syndrome]]/[[Wallenberg syndrome]]), demyelination, tumors, syringomyelia.
- **Second-order (preganglionic) neuron**: Ciliospinal center → [[superior cervical ganglion]]. Lesions: [[Pancoast tumor]] (lung apex), cervical rib, thoracic surgery, trauma, neuroblastoma (in children).
- **Third-order (postganglionic) neuron**: Superior cervical ganglion → iris dilator muscle (via the [[internal carotid artery]] plexus and [[long ciliary nerves]]). Lesions: [[carotid artery dissection]], cavernous sinus lesion, cluster headache, otitis media.

The classic triad of Horner syndrome includes:
1. **Miosis** (due to loss of sympathetic innervation to the dilator pupillae)
2. **Ptosis** (due to loss of sympathetic innervation to [[Müller's muscle]])
3. **Anhidrosis** (ipsilateral facial anhidrosis, variable depending on lesion level)

The miosis in Horner syndrome is typically mild (1–2 mm of [[anisocoria]]) and is characteristically more apparent in dim illumination (**dilation lag**), because the affected pupil fails to dilate normally in the dark.

#### b. Pontine Lesions

Bilateral pinpoint pupils are a hallmark of acute [[pontine hemorrhage]] or infarction. The mechanism involves disruption of descending sympathetic fibers traversing the [[pons]], resulting in unopposed parasympathetic tone. This finding, combined with coma and quadriplegia, constitutes a neurosurgical emergency.

#### c. Argyll Robertson Pupil ([[Argyll Robertson pupil]])

Classically associated with [[neurosyphilis]] (tertiary [[syphilis]]), the Argyll Robertson pupil is characterized by:
- Bilateral small, irregular pupils
- Absent or markedly diminished [[pupillary light reflex]]
- Preserved [[accommodation reflex]] (**light-near dissociation**)
- Poor dilation with [[mydriatic]] agents

The lesion is believed to involve the [[pretectal area]] of the dorsal midbrain, selectively disrupting the light reflex pathway while sparing the more ventral near-response pathway.

### 4. Inflammatory Miosis

[[Anterior uveitis]] ([[iritis]]) is a common ocular cause of miosis. Inflammation of the [[iris]] and [[ciliary body]] releases [[prostaglandins]] and other inflammatory mediators (including [[substance P]] and [[cytokines]]) that directly stimulate the iris sphincter muscle, producing reflex miosis. This inflammatory miosis is often accompanied by:

- [[Photophobia]]
- Ocular pain and redness ([[ciliary flush]])
- [[Aqueous flare]] and [[cells]] in the [[anterior chamber]]
- Risk of [[posterior synechiae]] (adhesions between the iris and the [[anterior lens capsule]]), which can permanently distort the pupil

### 5. Traumatic Miosis

Blunt [[ocular trauma]] can produce transient miosis through several mechanisms:
- Direct irritation of the iris sphincter
- Release of inflammatory mediators (traumatic iritis)
- Parasympathetic nerve stimulation from concussive forces

Conversely, severe trauma may cause [[traumatic mydriasis]] (sphincter rupture), so the pupillary response to trauma is variable and must be carefully assessed.

### 6. Congenital Miosis (Microcoria)

[[Microcoria]] is a rare congenital condition characterized by bilateral small pupils (typically <2 mm) that dilate poorly. It may occur as an isolated finding or in association with other developmental anomalies such as [[megalocornea]], [[ectopia lentis]], or [[myopia]]. The condition is attributed to hypoplasia or absence of the [[iris dilator muscle]].

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Miosis itself is a physical sign rather than a symptom. However, patients with pathological miosis may present with a variety of associated complaints:

| Symptom/Sign | Mechanism | Clinical Context |
|---|---|---|
| **Decreased scotopic (dim-light) vision** | Reduced pupil aperture limits retinal illuminance | Senile miosis, pharmacological miosis, Horner syndrome |
| **Blurred near vision** (if miotic agent also affects accommodation) | Ciliary muscle spasm ([[accommodative spasm]]) | Pilocarpine use, organophosphate exposure |
| **Brow ache / headache** | Ciliary spasm and sustained accommodation | Parasympathomimetic drug use |
| **Photophobia and ocular pain** | Iris sphincter spasm secondary to inflammation | [[Anterior uveitis]] |
| **Anisocoria** (unequal pupil size) | Unilateral miosis or contralateral mydriasis | Horner syndrome, unilateral uveitis, pharmacological exposure |
| **Ptosis** | Loss of sympathetic innervation to Müller's muscle | Horner syndrome |
| **Induced myopia** | Forward displacement of the lens-iris diaphragm or ciliary spasm | Pilocarpine, echothiophate |
| **Visual field constriction** | Extreme miosis limits peripheral light entry | Chronic miotic therapy |

In the context of systemic toxicity (e.g., [[organophosphate poisoning]]), miosis is accompanied by the classic **SLUDGE/DUMBELS** mnemonic of cholinergic excess:

- **S**alivation, **L**acrimation, **U**rination, **D**efecation, **G**astrointestinal distress, **E**mesis
- **D**efecation, **U**rination, **M**iosis, **B**ronchospasm/**B**radycardia, **E**mesis, **L**acrimation, **S**alivation

## Diagnosis

### History

A thorough clinical history is paramount and should include:

- **Onset and duration**: Acute vs. chronic; unilateral vs. bilateral
- **Medication history**: Topical ophthalmic drops (especially [[miotics]]), systemic medications (opioids, antihypertensives, antipsychotics)
- **Substance use**: Opioid use or exposure to organophosphate/carbamate compounds
- **Trauma history**: Recent ocular or head trauma
- **Neurological symptoms**: Headache, facial pain, anhidrosis, ptosis, diplopia, weakness
- **Ocular symptoms**: Pain, redness, photophobia, visual changes
- **Past medical history**: Syphilis, diabetes, hypertension, malignancy, autoimmune disease

### Clinical Examination

#### Pupillary Assessment

Systematic pupillary examination is the cornerstone of miosis evaluation:

1. **Measure pupil size** in both bright and dim illumination using a pupil gauge or [[infrared pupillometry]]. Document size in millimeters.
2. **Assess anisocoria**: Determine whether the anisocoria is greater in light or dark:
   - **Greater in dark** → the smaller pupil is the abnormal one (failure to dilate) → suggests **Horner syndrome** or iris sphincter pathology
   - **Greater in light** → the larger pupil is the abnormal one (failure to constrict) → suggests [[mydriasis]] from CN III palsy, pharmacological mydriasis, or iris sphincter damage
3. **Test the direct and consensual light reflexes** using a bright focal light source (e.g., [[transilluminator]] or [[slit lamp]] beam).
4. **Test the near response**: Have the patient fixate on a near target and observe for pupillary constriction. Compare with the light reflex to detect **light-near dissociation**.
5. **Swinging flashlight test**: Evaluate for a [[relative afferent pupillary defect]] (RAPD/[[Marcus Gunn pupil]]), which indicates asymmetric optic nerve or retinal disease. Note: an RAPD does not cause miosis per se but is essential in the differential diagnosis of pupillary abnormalities.

#### Pharmacological Testing

Pharmacological testing is critical for localizing the cause of miosis, particularly in suspected [[Horner syndrome]]:

| Test | Agent | Mechanism | Expected Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cocaine test (4–10%)** | [[Cocaine]] ophthalmic solution | Blocks norepinephrine reuptake at the sympathetic nerve terminal; requires intact sympathetic pathway to dilate the pupil | **Normal eye**: dilates. **Horner syndrome**: fails to dilate (confirms diagnosis). Anisocoria ≥0.8 mm post-cocaine is diagnostic. |
| **Apraclonidine test (0.5–1%)** | [[Apraclonidine]] (iopidine) | Weak α1-agonist; in Horner syndrome, denervation supersensitivity of the dilator muscle causes exaggerated dilation | **Horner eye**: dilates (reversal of anisocoria). **Normal eye**: minimal change or slight miosis (due to α2-mediated inhibition of sympathetic outflow). |
| **Hydroxyamphetamine test (1%)** | [[Hydroxyamphetamine]] (Paredrine) | Releases stored norepinephrine from intact postganglionic nerve terminals | **Preganglionic (1st or 2nd order) Horner**: pupil dilates (postganglionic neuron intact). **Postganglionic (3rd order) Horner**: pupil fails to dilate (depleted norepinephrine stores). |
| **Pilocarpine (dilute, 0.0625–0.125%)** | [[Pilocarpine]] | Direct muscarinic agonist; denervated sphincter shows supersensitivity | Used to confirm cholinergic denervation supersensitivity (e.g., in [[Adie tonic pupil]]—though Adie presents with mydriasis, not miosis). |

> **Note**: The cocaine test has become increasingly difficult to obtain in clinical practice. [[Apraclonidine]] testing has largely replaced it as the first-line pharmacological test for Horner syndrome due to its availability and ease of use.

#### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

[[Slit-lamp examination]] is essential to evaluate:

- **Iris architecture**: Posterior synechiae, iris atrophy, iris sphincter tears, iris nodules
- **Anterior chamber**: Cells and flare (indicating [[anterior uveitis]])
- **Lens**: Posterior subcapsular cataracts (associated with chronic miotic therapy or uveitis)
- **Angle assessment**: [[Gonioscopy]] may be indicated if miotic therapy is being used for [[angle-closure glaucoma]]

#### Neuroimaging

When neurological miosis is suspected (particularly Horner syndrome), appropriate imaging is critical:

- **MRI/MRA of the brain and neck**: To evaluate for carotid dissection (postganglionic Horner), brainstem lesion (first-order Horner), or Pancoast tumor (second-order Horner)
- **CT chest**: For apical lung masses in second-order Horner syndrome
- **CT/MRI orbit and cavernous sinus**: For postganglionic lesions

In pediatric Horner syndrome, urgent evaluation for [[neuroblastoma]] is mandatory, including urine catecholamines ([[vanillylmandelic acid]], [[homovanillic acid]]) and chest/abdominal imaging.

## Differential Diagnosis of Miosis

| Category | Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Physiological** | Light reflex, near response, sleep, aging | Bilateral, symmetric, appropriate to stimulus |
| **Pharmacological** | Pilocarpine, opioids, organophosphates | History of drug exposure; bilateral if systemic, unilateral if topical |
| **Sympathetic denervation** | [[Horner syndrome]] | Ipsilateral miosis + ptosis ± anhidrosis; dilation lag in dark; positive cocaine/apraclonidine test |
| **Parasympathetic irritation** | [[Anterior uveitis]], [[iritis]] | Pain, photophobia, ciliary flush, AC cells/flare |
| **Central neurological** | Pontine hemorrhage, [[Argyll Robertson pupil]] | Bilateral pinpoint pupils (pontine); light-near dissociation (Argyll Robertson) |
| **Congenital** | [[Microcoria]] | Bilateral, present from birth, poor dilation |
| **Traumatic** | Blunt ocular trauma | History of trauma; may have associated hyphema, angle recession |

## Management and Treatment

The management of miosis depends entirely on the underlying etiology. Miosis itself may not require treatment if it is physiological or clinically insignificant. However, when miosis is pathological, treatment is directed at the causative condition.

### 1. Pharmacological Miosis (Iatrogenic)

- **Pilocarpine-induced miosis**: If miosis from [[pilocarpine]] or other miotics causes intolerable side effects (brow ache, induced myopia, dim vision), the clinician may consider alternative [[glaucoma]] therapies such as [[prostaglandin analogs]], [[beta-blockers]], [[alpha-agonists]], or [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]].
- **Opioid-induced miosis**: Miosis from opioid use typically does not require specific ophthalmic treatment. In opioid overdose, administration of [[naloxone]] (an opioid antagonist) reverses systemic toxicity and miosis.
- **Organophosphate-induced miosis**: Emergency treatment includes [[atropine]] (a muscarinic antagonist) to counteract cholinergic excess, and [[pralidoxime]] (2-PAM) to reactivate acetylcholinesterase. Ocular decontamination with copious irrigation is also indicated.

### 2. Horner Syndrome

Treatment of Horner syndrome is directed at the underlying cause:
- **Carotid dissection**: Anticoagulation or antiplatelet therapy; neurosurgical/interventional consultation
- **Pancoast tumor**: Oncological management (surgery, radiation, chemotherapy)
- **Brainstem stroke**: Acute stroke management per neurological protocols
- **Idiopathic**: Observation; the miosis and ptosis are typically mild and may not require intervention

For cosmetic or functional concerns related to anisocoria, low-dose [[phenylephrine]] (2.5%) drops may be used to dilate the miotic pupil, though this is rarely necessary.

### 3. Inflammatory Miosis (Anterior Uveitis)

Management of miosis secondary to [[anterior uveitis]] is a critical component of uveitis treatment:

- **[[Cycloplegic]] and [[mydriatic]] agents**: [[Cyclopentolate]] (1%), [[homatropine]] (2–5%), or [[atropine]] (1%) are used to:
  - Relieve ciliary spasm and pain
  - Prevent and break [[posterior synechiae]]
  - Stabilize the [[blood-aqueous barrier]]
- **Topical [[corticosteroids]]**: [[Prednisolone acetate]] (1%) or [[difluprednate]] (0.05%) to suppress inflammation
- **Systemic anti-inflammatory therapy**: In severe or recurrent cases, systemic corticosteroids or [[immunomodulatory therapy]] may be required

### 4. Traumatic Miosis

- Typically self-limited; resolves as traumatic inflammation subsides
- Cycloplegic agents (e.g., [[cyclopentolate]], [[homatropine]]) may be used for comfort and to prevent synechiae if traumatic iritis is present
- Monitor for associated injuries: [[hyphema]], [[angle recession]], [[lens subluxation]], [[retinal detachment]]

### 5. Senile Miosis

- Generally does not require treatment
- Patients should be counseled about increased lighting needs for reading and other near tasks
- Consider the impact on [[fundoscopy]] and [[retinal imaging]]—pharmacological dilation may be necessary for adequate examination

### 6. Congenital Microcoria

- No definitive treatment exists for the underlying iris dilator hypoplasia
- Pharmacological dilation with [[phenylephrine]] and/or [[tropicamide]] may be attempted for examination purposes but is often limited in efficacy
- Genetic counseling may be appropriate if associated with systemic syndromes

## Complications of Chronic Miosis

Prolonged or chronic miosis, whether pharmacological or pathological, can lead to several clinically significant complications:

| Complication | Mechanism | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Posterior synechiae** | Prolonged iris-lens contact in the setting of inflammation | Can lead to [[iris bombé]], [[pupillary block]], and secondary [[angle-closure glaucoma]] |
| **Reduced retinal illuminance** | Decreased pupillary aperture limits light reaching the retina | Impaired scotopic vision; difficulty driving at night |
| **Induced myopia** | Ciliary spasm from parasympathomimetic agents | Blurred distance vision; may require spectacle adjustment |
| **Posterior subcapsular cataract** | Chronic miotic therapy (especially [[echothiophate iodide]]) | Progressive visual impairment |
| **Retinal detachment** | Chronic miotic therapy may cause vitreous traction (controversial) | Historically reported with long-term echothiophate use |
| **Cystoid macular edema** | Disruption of the blood-aqueous barrier by prostaglandin release | Reported with chronic pilocarpine use, especially post-cataract surgery |

## Special Considerations

### Miosis in the Context of Cataract Surgery

Intraoperative miosis during [[cataract surgery]] ([[phacoemulsification]]) is a significant surgical challenge. Causes include:
- Surgical iris manipulation
- Release of [[prostaglandins]] from iris trauma
- Use of [[nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs]] (NSAIDs) preoperatively is standard to prevent intraoperative miosis
- Patients on systemic [[alpha-1 adrenergic antagonists]] (e.g., [[tamsulosin]] for benign prostatic hyperplasia) are at risk for [[intraoperative floppy iris syndrome]] (IFIS), which includes progressive intraoperative miosis, iris billowing, and iris prolapse

Preoperative protocols to maintain mydriasis typically include:
- Topical [[cyclopentolate]], [[tropicamide]], and [[phenylephrine]]
- Topical NSAIDs (e.g., [[ketorolac]], [[nepafenac]], [[flurbiprofen]])
- Intracameral [[epinephrine]] or [[phenylephrine]] if needed intraoperatively
- Mechanical pupil expansion devices (e.g., [[Malyugin ring]], [[iris hooks]]) for refractory miosis

### Miosis and Pupil-Dependent Refractive Procedures

Miosis affects the optical performance of multifocal [[intraocular lenses]] (IOLs) and certain refractive surgical outcomes. Patients with chronic miosis may experience suboptimal near vision with diffractive multifocal IOLs, which rely on adequate pupil size for their optical design. Preoperative pupillometry is therefore essential in refractive surgical planning.

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis of miosis depends on its etiology:

- **Physiological and age-related miosis**: Benign; no impact on life expectancy or long-term visual function beyond mild scotopic impairment
- **Pharmacological miosis**: Fully reversible upon discontinuation of the causative agent (in most cases)
- **Horner syndrome**: Prognosis depends on the underlying cause; miosis itself is benign, but the underlying pathology (e.g., carotid dissection, malignancy) may carry significant morbidity and mortality
- **Inflammatory miosis**: Resolves with appropriate treatment of the underlying uveitis; risk of permanent pupillary distortion from synechiae if inadequately treated
- **Congenital microcoria**: Stable; lifelong condition with generally good visual prognosis unless associated with other ocular anomalies

---

## Summary

Miosis—constriction of the pupil—is a multifaceted clinical sign with implications spanning routine optometric practice to neurological and toxicological emergencies. A systematic approach to the evaluation of miosis, incorporating careful history, pupillary examination, pharmacological testing, and appropriate neuroimaging, enables the clinician to accurately identify the underlying etiology and institute targeted management. For the eye care professional, mastery of the anatomy, physiology, and pathology of pupillary constriction is indispensable for comprehensive patient care.
