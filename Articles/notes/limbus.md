

# Limbus (Ocular Anatomy)

---

The **limbus** (Latin: *limbus corneae*, meaning "border" or "edge") is the annular transitional zone situated at the junction between the [[cornea]] and the [[sclera]], representing one of the most anatomically and physiologically significant structures of the [[anterior segment]] of the eye. Spanning approximately 1.5 to 2.0 mm in width, the limbus serves as a critical anatomical landmark in clinical ophthalmology and optometry, housing the [[limbal stem cells]] (LSCs) that are essential for corneal epithelial homeostasis and regeneration, providing the primary drainage pathway for [[aqueous humor]] via the [[trabecular meshwork]] and [[Schlemm's canal]], and serving as a vascular transition zone between the avascular cornea and the vascularized sclera and [[conjunctiva]].

The limbus is not merely a passive boundary; it is a biologically active niche whose dysfunction underlies a spectrum of sight-threatening conditions including [[limbal stem cell deficiency]] (LSCD), [[pterygium]], [[corneal neovascularization]], and various forms of [[glaucoma]]. A thorough understanding of limbal anatomy, physiology, and pathology is therefore indispensable for every eye care practitioner.

---

## Gross and Surgical Anatomy

### Topographic Boundaries

The limbus does not possess a single, sharply demarcated boundary. Rather, it is defined differently depending on the tissue plane under consideration. Several anatomical landmarks define its borders:

| **Landmark** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Anterior limbal border** | The point where [[Bowman's layer]] terminates and the corneal epithelium transitions to conjunctival epithelium |
| **Posterior limbal border** | Marked by the [[scleral spur]] internally and the insertion of the [[Tenon's capsule]] externally |
| **Surgical limbus** | A blue-gray zone visible under the operating microscope, approximately 1 mm wide, used as a reference for incisional surgery |
| **Anatomical limbus** | The full 1.5–2.0 mm transitional zone encompassing all tissue layers |

The limbus is not uniform in width around the circumference of the eye. It is typically wider superiorly and inferiorly (approximately 2.0 mm) and narrower nasally and temporally (approximately 1.5 mm). This asymmetry has clinical implications for surgical planning, particularly in procedures such as [[trabeculectomy]], [[limbal relaxing incisions]], and [[corneal transplantation]].

### Histological Architecture

The limbus is a complex, multilayered structure. Each tissue layer of the cornea and sclera undergoes a distinct transition within the limbal zone:

#### Epithelium

The [[corneal epithelium]]—a non-keratinized, stratified squamous epithelium typically 5–7 cell layers thick—transitions at the limbus into the [[conjunctival epithelium]], which is a stratified columnar epithelium containing [[goblet cells]]. The limbal epithelium itself is unique: it is 10–15 cell layers thick and is arranged in undulating ridges known as the **Palisades of Vogt**. These palisades are fibrovascular ridges of [[limbal stroma]] that project into the epithelium, creating a series of rete pegs that dramatically increase the surface area of the basal epithelial layer. The Palisades of Vogt are most prominent in the superior and inferior limbus and are more easily visualized in darkly pigmented individuals.

The deepest cells within the Palisades of Vogt constitute the **[[limbal stem cell]] niche**—the reservoir of slow-cycling, undifferentiated progenitor cells responsible for the continuous renewal of the corneal epithelium throughout life.

#### Stroma

The highly organized, transparent [[corneal stroma]] (composed of regularly spaced [[collagen]] fibrils, predominantly type I and type V) transitions at the limbus into the opaque [[scleral stroma]], where collagen fibrils are larger, more variable in diameter, and irregularly arranged. The limbal stroma is more vascularized and contains a richer population of [[fibroblasts]], [[dendritic cells]], [[melanocytes]], and immune cells compared to the corneal stroma.

#### Descemet's Membrane and Endothelium

[[Descemet's membrane]] terminates at the limbus, where it transitions into the trabecular beams of the [[trabecular meshwork]]. The [[corneal endothelium]] similarly transitions into the endothelial lining of the trabecular meshwork and [[Schlemm's canal]]. This transition zone is of paramount importance for aqueous humor outflow and [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) regulation.

### Vascular Supply

The limbus represents the terminal vascular arcade of the anterior segment. It receives its blood supply from two principal arterial systems:

- **[[Anterior ciliary arteries]]**: Branches of the muscular arteries supplying the [[extraocular muscles]], these vessels penetrate the sclera near the limbus and contribute to the **major arterial circle of the iris** as well as the limbal arcade.
- **[[Conjunctival arteries]]**: Superficial branches that form the **limbal vascular arcade**, a continuous loop of vessels at the peripheral corneal margin.

The limbal vasculature is critical for providing nutrients and oxygen to the peripheral cornea, supporting the metabolic demands of the limbal stem cell niche, and facilitating immune surveillance. The transition from vascularized limbus to avascular cornea is maintained by a delicate balance of [[angiogenic]] and [[anti-angiogenic factors]], including [[vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF) and soluble VEGF receptor-1 (sVEGFR-1), [[thrombospondin-1]], and [[angiostatin]].

### Innervation

The limbus is richly innervated by the [[long posterior ciliary nerves]] and the [[anterior ciliary nerves]], both branches of the [[ophthalmic division]] (V1) of the [[trigeminal nerve]]. Nerve fibers form a dense **limbal nerve plexus** before entering the cornea radially. This innervation is essential for corneal sensitivity, the blink reflex, and trophic support of the corneal epithelium.

## The Limbal Stem Cell Niche

### Biology of Limbal Stem Cells

The concept of the limbus as the repository of corneal epithelial stem cells was first proposed by Davanger and Evensen in 1971 and has since been substantiated by extensive experimental and clinical evidence. [[Limbal stem cells]] (LSCs) are characterized by:

- **Slow cycling (quiescence)**: LSCs divide infrequently under normal conditions, reducing the accumulation of DNA replication errors.
- **High proliferative potential**: When activated, LSCs can undergo extensive self-renewal and generate large numbers of [[transient amplifying cells]] (TACs), which migrate centripetally across the corneal surface.
- **Undifferentiated phenotype**: LSCs express putative stem cell markers including [[p63]] (particularly the ΔNp63α isoform), [[ABCG2]], [[C/EBPδ]], [[Bmi-1]], and [[integrin α9]], while lacking differentiation markers such as [[cytokeratin 3]] (CK3) and [[cytokeratin 12]] (CK12).
- **Niche dependence**: LSCs reside within a specialized microenvironment—the **limbal stem cell niche**—composed of stromal cells (including [[limbal niche cells]] and [[melanocytes]]), extracellular matrix components ([[collagen IV]], [[laminin]], [[fibronectin]]), soluble factors ([[Wnt]], [[Notch]], [[BMP]], [[SHH]] signaling molecules), and the unique vascular and neural architecture of the Palisades of Vogt.

### The X, Y, Z Hypothesis of Corneal Epithelial Maintenance

The maintenance of the corneal epithelium is described by the **X, Y, Z hypothesis** proposed by Thoft and Friend (1983):

- **X** = Proliferation of basal epithelial cells
- **Y** = Centripetal migration of cells from the limbus
- **Z** = Desquamation of superficial epithelial cells from the corneal surface

For corneal epithelial homeostasis: **X + Y = Z**

Disruption of any component of this equation—particularly the limbal contribution (Y)—leads to corneal epithelial failure.

## Pathophysiology of Limbal Disorders

### Limbal Stem Cell Deficiency (LSCD)

[[Limbal stem cell deficiency]] is the prototypical disorder of the limbus and results from the destruction, dysfunction, or deficiency of the limbal stem cell population. LSCD may be classified as:

| **Classification** | **Etiology** | **Examples** |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary (Hereditary)** | Congenital absence or dysfunction of LSCs | [[Aniridia]]-associated keratopathy (PAX6 mutation), [[ectodermal dysplasia]], [[keratitis-ichthyosis-deafness syndrome]] |
| **Secondary (Acquired)** | Destruction of LSCs by external insult | [[Chemical burns]] (alkali > acid), [[thermal burns]], [[Stevens-Johnson syndrome]], [[ocular cicatricial pemphigoid]], chronic [[contact lens]] wear, multiple limbal surgeries, [[radiation keratopathy]], severe microbial [[keratitis]] |

**Pathophysiological cascade of LSCD:**

1. Loss of the limbal stem cell barrier → failure of the limbal "fence" function
2. Invasion of [[conjunctival epithelium]] onto the corneal surface (**conjunctivalization**)
3. Ingrowth of [[blood vessels]] and [[lymphatic vessels]] into the cornea (**[[corneal neovascularization]]**)
4. Chronic [[inflammation]], [[corneal scarring]], and [[corneal opacification]]
5. Persistent [[epithelial defects]], [[corneal ulceration]], and potential [[corneal perforation]]
6. Progressive, often irreversible, visual loss

### Pterygium and Pinguecula

[[Pterygium]] is a fibrovascular growth of degenerative conjunctival tissue that encroaches onto the cornea, typically from the nasal side. While traditionally considered a degenerative condition related to [[ultraviolet radiation]] exposure, contemporary evidence suggests that pterygium may involve focal limbal stem cell dysfunction, allowing conjunctival tissue to breach the limbal barrier. The pathogenesis involves:

- UV-induced damage to limbal stem cells and the limbal niche
- Upregulation of [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs), particularly MMP-1, MMP-2, and MMP-9
- Overexpression of [[VEGF]] and other pro-angiogenic factors
- Altered expression of [[p53]] and proliferation markers

[[Pinguecula]], a yellowish subconjunctival deposit of elastotically degenerated collagen near the limbus, represents a related but less invasive limbal-adjacent pathology.

### Limbal Dermoid

[[Limbal dermoid]] is a [[choristoma]]—a congenital, benign growth of histologically normal tissue in an abnormal location—that typically straddles the limbus. It contains keratinized epithelium, hair follicles, sebaceous glands, and adipose tissue. Limbal dermoids may be isolated or associated with [[Goldenhar syndrome]] (oculoauriculovertebral dysplasia). They can cause [[astigmatism]], [[amblyopia]], and cosmetic disfigurement.

### Limbal Involvement in Glaucoma

The limbus houses the conventional aqueous outflow pathway: the [[trabecular meshwork]], [[Schlemm's canal]], [[collector channels]], and [[aqueous veins]]. Dysfunction of these structures at the limbus is central to the pathophysiology of [[primary open-angle glaucoma]] (POAG) and other forms of glaucoma. Increased resistance to aqueous outflow at the juxtacanalicular tissue of the trabecular meshwork leads to elevated [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP), the primary modifiable risk factor for glaucomatous [[optic neuropathy]].

### Limbal Vernal Keratoconjunctivitis

In [[vernal keratoconjunctivitis]] (VKC), the limbal form is characterized by the formation of gelatinous, papillary hypertrophy at the limbus known as **Horner-Trantas dots**—collections of degenerated [[eosinophils]] and [[epithelial cells]] at the limbal margin. This limbal inflammation can damage the limbal stem cell niche and, if chronic, may contribute to secondary LSCD.

### Limbal Tumors

The limbus is a predilection site for [[ocular surface squamous neoplasia]] (OSSN), which encompasses a spectrum from [[conjunctival intraepithelial neoplasia]] (CIN) to invasive [[squamous cell carcinoma]]. The limbus is vulnerable because it is a site of active cell proliferation (stem cell activity) and is exposed to UV radiation. Other tumors that may arise at the limbus include [[melanoma]], [[lymphoma]], and [[papilloma]].

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Limbal pathology presents with a range of signs and symptoms depending on the specific condition:

| **Condition** | **Symptoms** | **Clinical Signs** |
|---|---|---|
| **LSCD (early)** | Mild irritation, [[photophobia]], tearing | Loss of Palisades of Vogt, stippled [[fluorescein]] staining, whorl-like epitheliopathy |
| **LSCD (advanced)** | Severe pain, marked visual loss, chronic redness | Conjunctivalization, [[corneal neovascularization]], persistent epithelial defects, corneal opacification |
| **Pterygium** | Foreign body sensation, cosmetic concern, visual disturbance if axis involved | Triangular fibrovascular tissue crossing the limbus onto the cornea |
| **Limbal dermoid** | Usually asymptomatic; may cause reduced vision | Dome-shaped, white-yellow mass at the limbus, often inferotemporal |
| **Limbal VKC** | Intense itching, mucous discharge, photophobia | Horner-Trantas dots, limbal papillae, limbal thickening |
| **OSSN** | Often asymptomatic; may cause irritation | Gelatinous or leukoplakic mass at the limbus, feeder vessels, intrinsic vascularity |

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] is the primary tool for evaluating the limbus. Key observations include:

- **Palisades of Vogt**: Best visualized with narrow slit illumination or retroillumination. Their absence suggests limbal stem cell loss.
- **Limbal vasculature**: Assessment of limbal vessel architecture, neovascularization, and vessel ingrowth patterns.
- **Fluorescein staining**: Late or stippled staining of the corneal surface in a whorl pattern (indicating conjunctivalized epithelium) is characteristic of LSCD.
- **Rose Bengal or [[Lissamine green]] staining**: Highlights devitalized or conjunctivalized epithelium on the corneal surface.

### Impression Cytology

[[Impression cytology]] involves applying a cellulose acetate filter or nitrocellulose membrane to the ocular surface to collect superficial epithelial cells for histological or immunohistochemical analysis. In LSCD, impression cytology reveals:

- Presence of [[goblet cells]] on the corneal surface (pathognomonic of conjunctivalization)
- Expression of conjunctival cytokeratins ([[CK13]], [[CK19]]) rather than corneal cytokeratins ([[CK3]], [[CK12]])
- Loss of corneal-specific markers

### In Vivo Confocal Microscopy (IVCM)

[[In vivo confocal microscopy]] (e.g., Heidelberg Retina Tomograph with Rostock Cornea Module) provides cellular-level imaging of the limbus in vivo. It can demonstrate:

- Palisades of Vogt architecture and integrity
- Limbal stem cell niche morphology
- Presence of dendritic cells, inflammatory cells, and blood vessels
- Corneal epithelial cell morphology (conjunctivalized vs. normal)

IVCM is increasingly used as a non-invasive adjunct for diagnosing LSCD and monitoring treatment response.

### Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography (AS-OCT)

[[Anterior segment OCT]] provides high-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the limbus and can demonstrate:

- Limbal epithelial thickness and architecture
- Loss of the normal limbal transition zone
- Subepithelial fibrosis or scarring
- Tumor depth and extent in OSSN or limbal dermoid

### Gonioscopy

[[Gonioscopy]] is the gold-standard technique for evaluating the internal limbal structures, specifically the [[iridocorneal angle]], [[trabecular meshwork]], [[Schlemm's canal]], and [[scleral spur]]. It is essential for:

- Classification of [[glaucoma]] (open-angle vs. angle-closure)
- Detection of angle abnormalities (e.g., [[peripheral anterior synechiae]], [[angle recession]], [[neovascularization of the angle]])
- Surgical planning for glaucoma procedures

### Ultrasound Biomicroscopy (UBM)

[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (typically 35–50 MHz) provides high-resolution imaging of the limbus and angle structures, particularly useful when optical media are opaque. It can delineate:

- Limbal and angle anatomy
- Ciliary body and angle pathology
- Tumor extent in limbal masses

## Staging of Limbal Stem Cell Deficiency

Several staging systems have been proposed for LSCD. A commonly used clinical classification is:

| **Stage** | **Clinical Features** | **Visual Impact** |
|---|---|---|
| **Stage I (Mild)** | Partial loss of Palisades of Vogt; sectoral conjunctivalization not involving the visual axis | Minimal or no visual impairment |
| **Stage II (Moderate)** | Conjunctivalization involving >50% of the corneal surface but sparing the central cornea; superficial neovascularization | Moderate visual impairment |
| **Stage III (Severe/Total)** | 360° loss of limbal stem cells; total conjunctivalization of the corneal surface; dense neovascularization and scarring; persistent epithelial defects | Severe visual impairment to legal blindness |

LSCD may also be classified as **unilateral** or **bilateral**, which has critical implications for treatment planning (see below).

## Management and Treatment

### Medical (Conservative) Management

Conservative measures are appropriate for mild LSCD and as adjunctive therapy:

- **[[Preservative-free artificial tears]]** and **[[lubricating ointments]]**: To optimize the ocular surface environment.
- **[[Autologous serum tears]]**: Contain growth factors ([[epidermal growth factor]], [[nerve growth factor]], [[fibronectin]]) that support epithelial healing and limbal stem cell function. Typically prepared at 20% concentration.
- **[[Bandage contact lenses]]**: Protect the fragile epithelium and reduce mechanical trauma from blinking.
- **Anti-inflammatory therapy**: [[Topical corticosteroids]] (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate]], [[fluorometholone]]) or [[topical cyclosporine A]] to control surface inflammation and create a more favorable environment for stem cell survival.
- **[[Amniotic membrane]] as a biological bandage**: Cryopreserved or dehydrated amniotic membrane (e.g., ProKera®) can be applied to the ocular surface to reduce inflammation, promote epithelial healing, and support limbal stem cell function.

### Surgical Management

Surgical intervention is indicated for moderate to severe LSCD and for other limbal pathologies:

#### Limbal Stem Cell Transplantation

| **Technique** | **Description** | **Indication** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Conjunctival-limbal autograft]] (CLAU)** | Limbal tissue harvested from the healthy fellow eye and transplanted to the affected eye | Unilateral LSCD with a healthy contralateral eye |
| **[[Keratolimbal allograft]] (KLAL)** | Limbal tissue from a cadaveric donor | Bilateral LSCD; requires systemic [[immunosuppression]] |
| **[[Living-related conjunctival-limbal allograft]] (lr-CLAL)** | Limbal tissue from a living related donor (e.g., sibling, parent) | Bilateral LSCD; requires systemic immunosuppression |
| **[[Simple limbal epithelial transplantation]] (SLET)** | Small limbal biopsy from the fellow eye, divided into fragments, and distributed over an amniotic membrane on the recipient cornea | Unilateral LSCD; minimizes donor eye risk |
| **[[Cultivated limbal epithelial transplantation]] (CLET)** | Limbal biopsy expanded ex vivo on a substrate (amniotic membrane or fibrin) and transplanted | Unilateral or bilateral LSCD; requires laboratory infrastructure |
| **[[Cultivated oral mucosal epithelial transplantation]] (COMET)** | Autologous oral mucosal epithelial cells expanded ex vivo and transplanted to the cornea | Bilateral LSCD (no healthy limbal tissue available); avoids immunosuppression |

**SLET**, first described by Sangwan et al. in 2012, has emerged as a particularly promising technique due to its simplicity, cost-effectiveness, and favorable outcomes, especially in resource-limited settings.

#### Pterygium Surgery

Surgical excision of [[pterygium]] with adjunctive measures to reduce recurrence:

- **Bare sclera technique**: High recurrence rate (30–80%); largely abandoned.
- **Conjunctival autograft (CAG)**: Excision with free conjunctival autograft; recurrence rate 5–15%.
- **Amniotic membrane transplantation (AMT)**: Excision with amniotic membrane graft; recurrence rate 10–25%.
- **Adjunctive [[mitomycin C]] (MMC)**: Intraoperative application (0.02–0.04% for 1–3 minutes) to inhibit fibroblast proliferation; reduces recurrence.
- **Adjunctive anti-VEGF**: Subconjunctival [[bevacizumab]] has been investigated to reduce recurrence and neovascularization.

#### Limbal Dermoid Excision

Surgical excision of [[limbal dermoid]] may be performed for cosmetic or optical reasons. Techniques include:

- Simple excision with lamellar keratectomy
- Excision with [[lamellar keratoplasty]] or amniotic membrane grafting for deeper lesions
- In cases with significant corneal involvement, [[penetrating keratoplasty]] may be required

#### Glaucoma Surgery at the Limbus

Many glaucoma surgical procedures are performed at or through the limbus:

- **[[Trabeculectomy]]**: A limbal-based or fornix-based conjunctival flap is created, and a partial-thickness scleral flap is fashioned at the limbus to create a fistula into the anterior chamber.
- **[[Minimally invasive glaucoma surgery]] (MIGS)**: Devices such as the [[iStent]], [[Hydrus Microstent]], and [[Kahook Dual Blade]] are inserted through the limbus to enhance trabecular outflow.
- **[[Goniotomy]] and [[trabeculotomy]]**: Ab interno or ab externo approaches to open the trabecular meshwork at the limbus.

#### Management of Limbal OSSN

- **Topical chemotherapy**: [[5-fluorouracil]] (1% four times daily, pulsed cycles), [[mitomycin C]] (0.04% four times daily for 1–2 weeks with rest periods), or [[interferon alpha-2b]] (1 million IU/mL four times daily) as primary or adjunctive treatment.
- **Surgical excision**: Wide local excision with 3–4 mm margins, cryotherapy to the limbal bed, and adjunctive topical chemotherapy.
- **"No-touch" technique**: Minimizes tumor seeding during excision.

## Pharmacological Considerations Specific to the Limbus

| **Agent** | **Mechanism** | **Limbal Application** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Cyclosporine A]]** (topical) | Calcineurin inhibitor; suppresses T-cell activation | Reduces limbal inflammation in LSCD, VKC, and post-transplant |
| **[[Tacrolimus]]** (topical) | Calcineurin inhibitor | Alternative to cyclosporine for limbal inflammation |
| **[[Mitomycin C]]** | Alkylating agent; inhibits fibroblast proliferation | Adjunct in pterygium surgery, trabeculectomy |
| **[[Bevacizumab]]** (subconjunctival) | Anti-VEGF monoclonal antibody | Reduces corneal neovascularization in LSCD and pterygium |
| **[[Autologous serum]]** | Contains growth factors and anti-inflammatory mediators | Supports limbal stem cell function and epithelial healing |
| **[[Interferon alpha-2b]]** | Antiproliferative, antiviral, immunomodulatory | Treatment of limbal OSSN |
| **[[5-Fluorouracil]]** | Antimetabolite; inhibits thymidylate synthase | Treatment of limbal OSSN; adjunct in trabeculectomy |

## Limbal Anatomy in Contact Lens Practice

The limbus is of particular relevance in [[contact lens]] fitting:

- **Limbal clearance**: In [[scleral contact lens]] fitting, adequate limbal clearance (vault) is essential to avoid compression of the limbal vasculature and stem cell niche. Inadequate clearance can cause limbal blanching, [[limbal stem cell]] damage, and [[neovascularization]].
- **Limbal bearing**: Excessive bearing of rigid gas-permeable (RGP) lenses on the limbus can cause chronic mechanical trauma, [[limbal hyperemia]], and long-term LSCD.
- **[[Superior limbic keratoconjunctivitis]] (SLK)**: A condition involving inflammation of the superior limbal and tarsal conjunctiva, sometimes associated with contact lens wear, that can affect the superior limbal stem cells.
- **Contact lens-induced limbal stem cell deficiency**: Chronic soft contact lens wear, particularly with low-oxygen-transmissibility lenses, has been associated with loss of Palisades of Vogt, limbal hyperemia, and subclinical LSCD.

## Emerging Research and Future Directions

The limbus remains an area of intense research interest:

- **Induced pluripotent stem cell (iPSC)-derived limbal stem cells**: Researchers are investigating the generation of LSCs from [[iPSCs]] to provide an unlimited autologous cell source for transplantation in bilateral LSCD.
- **Gene therapy for aniridia-associated keratopathy**: Targeted correction of [[PAX6]] mutations to restore limbal stem cell function.
- **Bioengineered limbal niches**: Development of synthetic scaffolds that mimic the Palisades of Vogt architecture to support ex vivo expansion and in vivo engraftment of LSCs.
- **Advanced imaging**: Development of non-invasive imaging biomarkers (e.g., OCT angiography of the limbal vasculature, AI-assisted IVCM analysis) for early detection of limbal stem cell loss.
- **Anti-angiogenic therapies**: Novel agents targeting [[corneal neovascularization]] to preserve or restore the limbal barrier.

---

## Key Clinical Pearls

1. **Always examine the limbus**: Systematic evaluation of the limbus with slit-lamp biomicroscopy should be part of every comprehensive eye examination.
2. **Palisades of Vogt are a clinical biomarker**: Their presence indicates a healthy limbal stem cell population; their absence should raise suspicion for LSCD.
3. **Chemical burns are the most common cause of acquired LSCD**: Immediate, copious irrigation followed by long-term ocular surface rehabilitation is critical.
4. **SLET has revolutionized LSCD management**: It offers excellent outcomes with minimal donor site morbidity and is feasible in resource-limited settings.
5. **Limbal pathology is often bilateral in hereditary conditions**: Bilateral involvement necessitates allogeneic or alternative cell sources for transplantation.
6. **Contact lens practitioners must respect the limbus**: Proper lens fitting, adequate oxygen transmissibility, and regular monitoring of limbal health are essential to prevent iatrogenic LSCD.
