# Ora Serrata

The **ora serrata** is the anatomically and clinically significant scalloped boundary that demarcates the transition between the [[retina]] (specifically, the sensory [[neurosensory retina]]) and the [[ciliary body]] of the [[uveal tract]]. It represents the most anterior extent of the retina and is a critical landmark in [[ophthalmic anatomy]], [[vitreoretinal surgery]], [[scleral depression]], and the pathogenesis of numerous peripheral retinal disorders. The term "ora serrata" derives from the Latin *ora* (border, edge) and *serrata* (saw-toothed), reflecting its characteristic serrated or scalloped morphology when viewed from the interior of the globe.

Understanding the ora serrata is essential for every eye care professional—from the [[optometrist]] performing [[dilated fundus examination]] with [[scleral indentation]] to the [[vitreoretinal surgeon]] planning [[pars plana vitrectomy]]—because this region is the site of origin or involvement of a wide spectrum of clinical entities including [[retinal tears]], [[retinal detachment]], [[peripheral retinal degenerations]], [[pars planitis]], and [[vitreous base]] pathology.

---

## Embryology and Development

The ora serrata develops from the [[optic cup]], a derivative of the [[neuroectoderm]]. During embryogenesis, the inner layer of the optic cup differentiates into the neurosensory retina posteriorly and the non-pigmented [[ciliary epithelium]] anteriorly, while the outer layer gives rise to the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE) posteriorly and the pigmented ciliary epithelium anteriorly. The ora serrata is the precise junction where this developmental transition occurs.

At birth, the ora serrata is relatively smooth and poorly defined. The characteristic dentate (tooth-like) processes and intervening bays become more pronounced during postnatal maturation and are fully developed by early childhood. The differential growth of the nasal and temporal portions of the globe accounts for the well-recognized asymmetry of the ora serrata between the two sides.

The [[vitreous base]], a band of particularly firm [[vitreoretinal adhesion]], straddles the ora serrata and is established during development. This adhesion is so robust that it is virtually impossible to separate surgically without causing tissue disruption, a fact of enormous clinical significance in the pathogenesis of [[retinal breaks]] associated with [[posterior vitreous detachment]] (PVD).

## Gross and Microscopic Anatomy

### Topographic Anatomy

The ora serrata is located approximately:

| Parameter | Temporal | Nasal |
|---|---|---|
| **Distance from the [[limbus]]** | ~7.0–8.0 mm | ~5.5–6.5 mm |
| **Distance from the [[equator]]** | ~5.0–6.0 mm | ~4.0–5.0 mm |
| **Width of the ora serrata zone** | ~2.1 mm | ~0.7–0.8 mm |
| **Number of dentate processes (teeth)** | Fewer, more rounded | More numerous, more pointed |
| **Number of oral bays** | Fewer | More numerous |

The ora serrata is not a sharp line but rather a transitional zone. On the temporal side, the transition is broader and more gradual, with the dentate processes being blunt and widely spaced. On the nasal side, the transition is narrower and more abrupt, with the teeth being sharper and more closely packed. This asymmetry is clinically relevant because the nasal ora serrata is closer to the limbus, which influences the safe placement of [[sclerotomy]] ports during [[pars plana vitrectomy]] and intravitreal injections.

The total number of dentate processes (teeth) ranges from approximately 16 to 36 (average ~30), with the nasal half typically containing more teeth than the temporal half.

### Dentate Processes and Oral Bays

- **Dentate processes (teeth):** These are forward extensions of the retina into the [[pars plana]] region of the ciliary body. They represent the most anterior points of retinal tissue.
- **Oral bays (bays of the ora serrata):** These are the recesses between the dentate processes where the pars plana extends posteriorly toward the retina. Oral bays are clinically significant because they are common sites for:
  - [[Enclosed oral bays]] (a normal anatomic variant where a dentate process bridges over a bay)
  - [[Meridional folds]]
  - [[Peripheral cystoid degeneration]]
  - [[Retinal tears]] and [[retinal holes]]

### Histological Architecture

At the histological level, the ora serrata marks the abrupt transition from the complex, multilayered neurosensory retina to the simpler, bilayered epithelium of the [[pars plana]]:

| Layer | Retinal Side (Posterior to Ora) | Ciliary Side (Anterior to Ora) |
|---|---|---|
| **Inner layer (neuroectodermal)** | Neurosensory retina (9–10 layers including [[photoreceptors]], [[bipolar cells]], [[ganglion cells]]) | Non-pigmented ciliary epithelium (single layer of columnar cells) |
| **Outer layer (neuroectodermal)** | [[Retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE) | Pigmented ciliary epithelium |
| **Photoreceptors** | Present (predominantly [[rods]] at the periphery) | Absent |
| **Thickness** | ~0.10–0.15 mm at the extreme periphery (thinnest retina) | ~0.04–0.10 mm (pars plana epithelium) |

At the ora serrata itself, the retina undergoes progressive thinning. The [[ganglion cell layer]] becomes discontinuous, the [[inner nuclear layer]] and [[outer nuclear layer]] merge, and the photoreceptor layer terminates. The [[internal limiting membrane]] (ILM) of the retina continues anteriorly as the inner limiting membrane of the ciliary body and eventually contributes to the [[zonular fibers]] and the [[anterior hyaloid face]].

### The Vitreous Base

The [[vitreous base]] is a three-dimensional band approximately 3–6 mm wide that straddles the ora serrata, extending approximately 1.5–2.0 mm posterior to the ora (over the peripheral retina) and 1–3 mm anterior to the ora (over the pars plana). The vitreous base is the site of the strongest [[vitreoretinal adhesion]] in the eye. The [[collagen fibrils]] of the [[vitreous body]] insert perpendicularly into the [[internal limiting membrane]] and the basement membrane of the non-pigmented ciliary epithelium in this region.

Key clinical implications of the vitreous base:

- During [[posterior vitreous detachment]] (PVD), the vitreous separates from the posterior retina but remains firmly attached at the vitreous base. Traction at the posterior border of the vitreous base can produce [[retinal tears]], particularly [[horseshoe tears]] (flap tears).
- In [[trauma]], avulsion of the vitreous base can occur, producing a characteristic ring of tissue visible on [[fundoscopy]] or during [[vitrectomy]].
- The vitreous base widens with age, extending further posteriorly, which increases the zone of firm vitreoretinal adhesion and may alter the pattern of traction-related pathology.

## Physiology and Functional Significance

The ora serrata itself does not have a discrete physiological function analogous to the [[macula]] or [[optic nerve head]]. However, its functional significance is derived from its role as:

1. **The anterior boundary of the functional retina:** The ora serrata defines the limit of the light-sensitive retina. The extreme peripheral retina adjacent to the ora contributes to the far peripheral [[visual field]] (approximately 90–100 degrees temporally). Pathology at the ora serrata can therefore produce peripheral visual field defects, although these are often asymptomatic due to the low spatial resolution of the far periphery.

2. **A structural transition zone:** The ora serrata is a mechanically vulnerable region because it represents the junction between two tissues of different thickness, rigidity, and adhesion properties. This makes it susceptible to traction-related pathology.

3. **A landmark for surgical access:** The [[pars plana]], immediately anterior to the ora serrata, is the preferred site for surgical entry into the [[vitreous cavity]] (pars plana vitrectomy, [[intravitreal injections]]) because it is avascular and devoid of retinal tissue. Knowledge of the ora serrata's position relative to the [[limbus]] is therefore critical for safe surgical planning.

4. **A site of aqueous humor dynamics:** The [[ciliary epithelium]] immediately anterior to the ora serrata is responsible for [[aqueous humor]] production. While the ora serrata itself does not produce aqueous, inflammatory conditions affecting this region (e.g., [[pars planitis]]) can alter the local microenvironment and influence aqueous dynamics.

## Pathophysiology

The ora serrata region is involved in a broad spectrum of pathological processes. The pathophysiology of ora serrata–related conditions can be categorized as follows:

### 1. Degenerative Changes

The peripheral retina adjacent to the ora serrata is the most common site for age-related degenerative changes:

- **[[Peripheral cystoid degeneration]]:** This is a virtually universal finding in adults, characterized by the formation of microcystoid spaces within the outer plexiform and inner nuclear layers of the peripheral retina. It begins at the ora serrata and extends posteriorly with age. It is considered a normal aging change and is typically non-progressive and asymptomatic.

- **[[Typical degenerative retinoschisis]]:** This represents a coalescence and expansion of the cystoid spaces in peripheral cystoid degeneration, resulting in a splitting of the retina (usually at the outer plexiform layer). It occurs most commonly in the inferotemporal quadrant near the ora serrata. While usually benign, progressive retinoschisis can rarely lead to [[retinal detachment]] if both the inner and outer layers develop breaks.

- **[[Paving stone degeneration]] (cobblestone degeneration):** These are discrete areas of chorioretinal atrophy located between the ora serrata and the equator, most commonly inferotemporally. They result from focal ischemia of the [[choriocapillaris]] and are benign.

- **[[Meridional folds]]:** These are radially oriented folds of redundant retinal tissue aligned along the meridians of the dentate processes. They are developmental variants and are usually clinically insignificant, although they may occasionally be associated with [[meridional complexes]] that include small retinal tears.

### 2. Tractional Pathology

The firm vitreoretinal adhesion at the [[vitreous base]] makes the ora serrata region particularly susceptible to tractional forces:

- **[[Retinal tears]]:** During PVD, dynamic vitreoretinal traction at the posterior border of the vitreous base can produce horseshoe (flap) tears. These are the most common precursors to [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]].

- **[[Retinal dialysis]]:** A retinal dialysis is a circumferential tear at or near the ora serrata, representing a disinsertion of the retina from the pars plana. It is the most common type of retinal break in young patients and is frequently associated with [[ocular trauma]] (particularly blunt trauma). The inferotemporal quadrant is most commonly affected. Non-traumatic (idiopathic) dialyses tend to occur supranasally.

- **[[Giant retinal tear]] (GRT):** A giant retinal tear extends 90 degrees or more of the circumference and typically occurs at or near the ora serrata. GRTs are associated with [[high myopia]], [[Marfan syndrome]], [[Stickler syndrome]], and trauma.

- **[[Vitreous base avulsion]]:** In severe blunt trauma, the vitreous base can be torn away from its attachment at the ora serrata, producing a visible ring or band of avulsed tissue. This is pathognomonic of significant ocular trauma.

### 3. Inflammatory Conditions

- **[[Pars planitis]] (intermediate [[uveitis]]):** This is a chronic inflammatory condition that preferentially affects the pars plana and the peripheral retina adjacent to the ora serrata. It is characterized by the formation of "snowbanks" (white exudative material over the inferior pars plana and ora serrata) and "snowball" opacities in the inferior vitreous. The pathophysiology involves immune-mediated inflammation of the [[vitreous]], pars plana, and peripheral retina, with potential complications including [[cystoid macular edema]] (CME), [[epiretinal membrane]] formation, [[neovascularization]], and [[cataract]].

### 4. Neoplastic Conditions

- **[[Retinoblastoma]]** and [[medulloepithelioma]] can arise from or extend to the ora serrata region, although these are rare.
- **[[Ciliary body melanoma]]** arising from the ciliary body immediately anterior to the ora serrata may extend posteriorly to involve the peripheral retina at the ora.

### 5. Congenital and Developmental Anomalies

- **[[Enclosed oral bays]]:** A developmental variant where a dentate process bridges over an oral bay, creating a small pocket. These are usually benign but may be confused with retinal holes.
- **[[Meridional complexes]]:** Combinations of meridional folds, dentate processes, and associated small retinal breaks.
- **[[Retinal dysplasia]]** and [[persistent fetal vasculature]] (PFV) can involve the ora serrata region.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Pathology at the ora serrata may be entirely asymptomatic or may present with a range of symptoms depending on the specific condition:

| Condition | Typical Symptoms |
|---|---|
| **Peripheral cystoid degeneration** | Asymptomatic |
| **Typical degenerative retinoschisis** | Usually asymptomatic; rarely, peripheral visual field loss |
| **Paving stone degeneration** | Asymptomatic |
| **Retinal tears (at ora serrata)** | [[Photopsia]] (flashing lights), [[floaters]], peripheral visual field loss (if associated with subretinal fluid or detachment) |
| **Retinal dialysis** | May be asymptomatic initially; progressive peripheral visual field loss; may present with [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]] |
| **Giant retinal tear** | Acute photopsia, floaters, visual field loss, decreased [[visual acuity]] |
| **Vitreous base avulsion** | Floaters, photopsia, may be associated with [[vitreous hemorrhage]] |
| **Pars planitis** | Floaters, blurred vision (especially if [[CME]] develops), mild pain or redness (uncommon) |

It is critical to note that many ora serrata pathologies are discovered incidentally during routine [[dilated fundus examination]] or during examination for unrelated complaints. The far peripheral location of the ora serrata means that even significant pathology may not produce symptoms until complications (e.g., retinal detachment) develop.

### Symptoms Warranting Urgent Evaluation

Patients presenting with the following symptoms should undergo urgent dilated examination with careful inspection of the ora serrata and peripheral retina:

- New onset of [[floaters]] (especially a sudden shower of floaters)
- [[Photopsia]] (flashing lights), particularly in the temporal visual field
- A "curtain" or "shadow" in the peripheral visual field
- Sudden decrease in [[visual acuity]]

## Diagnostic Modalities

### 1. Dilated Fundus Examination with Scleral Indentation

The gold standard for clinical evaluation of the ora serrata is **[[binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy]] (BIO)** combined with **[[scleral indentation]]** (scleral depression). This technique allows the examiner to:

- Visualize the ora serrata and the extreme peripheral retina, which are otherwise hidden behind the [[iris]] and [[ciliary body]]
- Elevate the ora serrata into the examiner's line of sight
- Create a dynamic view that allows assessment of vitreoretinal traction, retinal mobility, and the relationship between retinal breaks and the vitreous base
- Differentiate between retinal tears, holes, and degenerative changes

**Technique:** The examiner uses a [[scleral depressor]] (a thimble-type or cotton-tipped applicator) to gently indent the sclera over the pars plana while viewing through the indirect ophthalmoscope with a condensing lens (typically [[20D lens]] or [[28D lens]]). The indentation pushes the ora serrata and peripheral retina into view.

**Contraindications to scleral indentation:**
- Recent intraocular surgery (risk of wound dehiscence)
- Suspected open globe injury
- Extreme patient discomfort or inability to cooperate
- Hyphema or significant anterior segment inflammation (relative)

### 2. Three-Mirror Goldmann Lens Examination

The **[[Goldmann three-mirror lens]]** is a contact lens used at the [[slit lamp]] that provides a detailed, magnified view of the peripheral retina and ora serrata. The most peripheral mirror (the bullet-shaped or D-shaped mirror) is specifically designed for visualization of the ora serrata. This technique provides higher magnification than BIO and is excellent for detailed assessment of peripheral retinal lesions, but it has a smaller field of view and requires patient cooperation.

### 3. Ultrasound Biomicroscopy (UBM)

**[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]]** (UBM) uses high-frequency ultrasound (35–100 MHz) to produce high-resolution cross-sectional images of the anterior segment and the ora serrata region. UBM is particularly useful for:

- Visualizing the ora serrata in eyes with opaque media (e.g., dense [[cataract]], [[vitreous hemorrhage]])
- Assessing the relationship between the ora serrata and [[ciliary body tumors]]
- Evaluating the [[vitreous base]] and its attachments
- Diagnosing [[cyclodialysis cleft]] or other trauma-related pathology near the ora serrata

### 4. Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

While standard [[spectral-domain OCT]] (SD-OCT) is primarily designed for posterior segment imaging, advances in **[[swept-source OCT]]** (SS-OCT) and **[[wide-field OCT]]** have extended imaging capabilities to the peripheral retina and ora serrata. OCT can provide cross-sectional images of:

- Peripheral cystoid degeneration
- Retinoschisis
- Retinal tears and their relationship to the vitreous
- Pars planitis snowbanks

### 5. Ultra-Widefield Imaging

**[[Ultra-widefield fundus photography]]** (e.g., [[Optos]] imaging) and **[[ultra-widefield fluorescein angiography]]** can capture images of the peripheral retina extending to or near the ora serrata in a single frame. These modalities are valuable for:

- Documentation of peripheral retinal pathology
- Screening for retinal tears and detachments
- Monitoring pars planitis and peripheral retinal neovascularization
- Detecting peripheral retinal nonperfusion

### 6. B-Scan Ultrasonography

**[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** (10 MHz) is essential for evaluating the ora serrata region when direct visualization is not possible due to media opacities. It can detect:

- Retinal detachment extending to the ora serrata
- Vitreous base pathology
- Ciliary body masses
- Vitreous hemorrhage

## Management and Treatment

Management of ora serrata–related conditions depends entirely on the specific pathology identified. The following outlines the approach to the most clinically significant conditions:

### 1. Benign Degenerative Changes (No Treatment Required)

The following conditions are considered benign and require no treatment, only documentation and patient education:

- [[Peripheral cystoid degeneration]]
- [[Paving stone degeneration]]
- [[Meridional folds]] (without associated breaks)
- [[Enclosed oral bays]]
- Stable, asymptomatic [[typical degenerative retinoschisis]]

Patients with these findings should be counseled about the symptoms of retinal detachment (new floaters, photopsia, visual field loss) and advised to seek prompt evaluation if such symptoms occur.

### 2. Retinal Tears at the Ora Serrata

Symptomatic retinal tears (i.e., tears associated with acute PVD symptoms such as photopsia and floaters) carry a significant risk of progression to [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]] and require prompt treatment:

- **[[Laser photocoagulation]] (laser retinopexy):** Confluent laser burns are applied around the tear to create a chorioretinal adhesion that prevents subretinal fluid from extending through the break. This is typically performed using an [[indirect laser ophthalmoscope]] with scleral indentation for tears at the ora serrata.

- **[[Cryopexy]] (cryoretinopexy):** A [[cryoprobe]] is applied to the sclera overlying the tear, freezing the retina and RPE to create an adhesion. Cryopexy is often preferred for tears at the ora serrata because:
  - The extreme anterior location may be difficult to reach with laser
  - Cryopexy can be applied through the sclera without requiring direct visualization
  - It is effective even in the presence of mild vitreous hemorrhage

### 3. Retinal Dialysis

- **Small, asymptomatic dialyses without subretinal fluid:** May be observed closely or treated prophylactically with laser or cryopexy.
- **Dialyses with subretinal fluid or retinal detachment:** Require surgical repair, typically with:
  - **[[Scleral buckle]]:** This is often the preferred surgical approach for retinal dialysis, particularly in young patients. A silicone band or sponge is sutured to the sclera overlying the dialysis to indent the wall of the eye and close the break.
  - **[[Pars plana vitrectomy]]** (PPV): May be used alone or in combination with scleral buckling, particularly for complex cases.

### 4. Giant Retinal Tear

Giant retinal tears are surgical emergencies requiring:

- **[[Pars plana vitrectomy]]** with removal of vitreous traction
- **[[Perfluorocarbon liquid]]** (PFCL) to unfold and flatten the retina
- **[[Endolaser photocoagulation]]** to the edges of the tear
- **[[Silicone oil]] or [[sulfur hexafluoride]] (SF6) / [[perfluoropropane]] (C3F8) gas tamponade**
- Possible scleral buckle

### 5. Pars Planitis

Management of [[pars planitis]] depends on severity and the presence of complications:

| Severity | Management |
|---|---|
| **Mild (no CME, VA ≥ 20/40)** | Observation; monitor for progression |
| **Moderate (CME present, VA 20/40–20/80)** | [[Periocular corticosteroid]] injection (e.g., sub-Tenon [[triamcinolone acetonide]]); topical [[NSAID]]s; systemic [[corticosteroids]] |
| **Severe/Refractory** | [[Immunomodulatory therapy]] (e.g., [[methotrexate]], [[mycophenolate mofetil]], [[azathioprine]]); [[biologic agents]] (e.g., [[adalimumab]]); [[intravitreal corticosteroid implants]] (e.g., [[dexamethasone intravitreal implant]] [Ozurdex], [[fluocinolone acetonide implant]] [Retisert]) |
| **Complications (neovascularization, vitreous hemorrhage, tractional detachment)** | [[Pars plana vitrectomy]]; [[panretinal photocoagulation]] for neovascularization; [[anti-VEGF]] agents |
| **Peripheral retinal cryopexy** | Historically used for snowbanking; less commonly employed in the era of modern immunosuppression |

### 6. Vitreous Base Avulsion

Treatment is directed at any associated retinal breaks or detachment. The avulsed vitreous base tissue itself does not require specific treatment but should be documented and the peripheral retina carefully examined for associated tears.

## Surgical Considerations Related to the Ora Serrata

### Pars Plana Vitrectomy (PPV) Port Placement

The ora serrata is the most critical anatomical landmark for safe port placement during [[pars plana vitrectomy]]. Sclerotomy sites must be placed in the [[pars plana]], anterior to the ora serrata, to avoid damaging the retina, but posterior to the [[pars plicata]] to avoid the [[ciliary processes]] and the [[lens]].

| Patient Population | Recommended Distance from Limbus |
|---|---|
| **Phakic adult** | 3.5–4.0 mm |
| **Pseudophakic/Aphakic adult** | 3.0–3.5 mm |
| **Pediatric (infant)** | 2.0–3.0 mm (ora serrata is more anterior) |

These measurements account for the variable distance of the ora serrata from the limbus and are adjusted based on the patient's lens status and age.

### Intravitreal Injections

[[Intravitreal injections]] of [[anti-VEGF]] agents (e.g., [[ranibizumab]], [[aflibercept]], [[bevacizumab]], [[faricimab]]), [[corticosteroids]], or [[antibiotics]] are administered through the pars plana at a measured distance from the limbus (typically 3.5–4.0 mm in phakic eyes, 3.0–3.5 mm in pseudophakic eyes) to ensure the needle enters the vitreous cavity without contacting the ora serrata or the retina.

### Scleral Buckling

During [[scleral buckle]] surgery for retinal detachment, the buckle element must be positioned to support the retinal break. For breaks at or near the ora serrata (e.g., retinal dialysis), the buckle is placed anteriorly, and the surgeon must have precise knowledge of the ora serrata's location to achieve optimal break closure.

## Differential Diagnosis of Ora Serrata Lesions

When evaluating lesions at or near the ora serrata, the clinician must differentiate between:

| Finding | Key Features | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Retinal tear** | Horseshoe or flap shape; may have associated subretinal fluid, hemorrhage, or vitreous pigment ([[Shafer sign]]) | High risk of retinal detachment; requires treatment |
| **Retinal hole (atrophic)** | Round, full-thickness break without a flap; no vitreous traction | Low risk of detachment; usually observed |
| **Retinal dialysis** | Circumferential tear at the ora serrata | Moderate-to-high risk of detachment; often requires surgery |
| **Enclosed oral bay** | Bridging dentate process over an oral bay; appears as a small "hole" | Normal variant; no treatment |
| **Meridional fold** | Radial fold of retinal tissue at a dentate process | Normal variant; no treatment unless associated with a break |
| **Peripheral cystoid degeneration** | Microcystoid changes in the peripheral retina at the ora | Universal aging change; benign |
| **Retinoschisis** | Smooth, dome-shaped elevation of the inner retinal layer; no [[tobacco dust]] in vitreous | Usually benign; differentiate from retinal detachment |
| **Pars planitis snowbank** | White exudative material over the inferior pars plana/ora serrata | Inflammatory; requires monitoring and possible treatment |

## Prognosis

The prognosis for ora serrata–related conditions varies widely:

- **Benign degenerative changes:** Excellent prognosis; no visual consequences in the vast majority of cases.
- **Retinal tears treated promptly:** Excellent prognosis with laser or cryopexy; risk of retinal detachment is reduced from ~30–50% (untreated symptomatic tears) to <5%.
- **Retinal dialysis with detachment:** Good prognosis with scleral buckle surgery, particularly in young patients; anatomic success rates exceed 90%.
- **Giant retinal tear:** Guarded prognosis; anatomic success rates with modern vitrectomy techniques are 70–90%, but visual outcomes depend on macular involvement and [[proliferative vitreoretinopathy]] (PVR).
- **Pars planitis:** Variable; many patients have a chronic, relapsing course. Visual prognosis depends on the development and management of complications, particularly CME.

---

## Summary

The ora serrata is a fundamental anatomical landmark of the eye that serves as the anterior boundary of the retina and the junction with the ciliary body. Its unique scalloped morphology, the firm vitreoretinal adhesion of the overlying vitreous base, and its position as a structural transition zone make it a site of significant clinical importance. Pathology at the ora serrata ranges from benign age-related degenerations to sight-threatening conditions such as retinal tears, retinal dialysis, giant retinal tears, and pars planitis. Thorough examination of the ora serrata using binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy with scleral indentation remains the cornerstone of clinical evaluation, supplemented by modern imaging modalities including ultra-widefield photography, OCT, and ultrasonography. Management is tailored to the specific condition and ranges from observation and patient education to laser photocoagulation, cryopexy, scleral buckling, and pars plana vitrectomy.
