

# Descemet's Membrane

---

**Descemet's membrane** (DM), also known as the **posterior limiting lamina** or **lamina limitans posterior**, is a specialized, acellular [[basement membrane]] situated between the posterior [[corneal stroma]] and the [[corneal endothelium]]. It serves as the basement membrane of the [[corneal endothelial cells]] and plays a critical structural and functional role in maintaining [[corneal transparency]], biomechanical integrity, and fluid homeostasis. First described by the French physician **Jean Descemet** in 1770, this membrane has become a structure of paramount clinical significance in modern [[corneal surgery]], particularly with the advent of endothelial [[keratoplasty]] techniques such as [[Descemet's Stripping Automated Endothelial Keratoplasty]] (DSAEK) and [[Descemet's Membrane Endothelial Keratoplasty]] (DMEK).

Descemet's membrane is unique among corneal layers in that it continues to thicken throughout life, is remarkably resilient to enzymatic degradation, and possesses distinct ultrastructural zones that reflect its prenatal and postnatal developmental origins. Pathological alterations of Descemet's membrane underlie several visually significant conditions, including [[Fuchs' endothelial corneal dystrophy]], [[posterior polymorphous corneal dystrophy]], [[congenital hereditary endothelial dystrophy]], and [[corneal hydrops]] in advanced [[keratoconus]]. Understanding the anatomy, physiology, and pathology of Descemet's membrane is essential for every [[optometrist]] and [[ophthalmologist]] involved in the diagnosis and management of corneal disease.

---

## Anatomy and Ultrastructure

### Gross Anatomy

Descemet's membrane is a thin, transparent, highly elastic sheet that lines the posterior surface of the [[cornea]], immediately anterior to the single-cell-layered [[corneal endothelium]] and posterior to the deepest [[corneal stromal]] [[keratocytes]] and [[collagen]] lamellae. It extends from limbus to limbus and terminates peripherally at the [[Schwalbe's line]], which marks the transition to the [[trabecular meshwork]] of the [[anterior chamber angle]]. This anatomical landmark is of critical importance in [[gonioscopy]] and in the pathogenesis of conditions such as [[Axenfeld-Rieger syndrome]], where a prominent, anteriorly displaced Schwalbe's line (known as [[posterior embryotoxon]]) is observed.

### Thickness

At birth, Descemet's membrane measures approximately **3–4 micrometers (µm)** in thickness. Because [[corneal endothelial cells]] continuously secrete extracellular matrix material onto its posterior surface throughout life, the membrane progressively thickens, reaching approximately **10–15 µm** by the eighth decade of life. In certain pathological states, such as [[Fuchs' endothelial corneal dystrophy]], the membrane may become considerably thicker due to the deposition of abnormal [[collagen]] and the formation of excrescences known as [[guttae]].

### Ultrastructural Zones

Transmission [[electron microscopy]] (TEM) has revealed that Descemet's membrane is composed of two distinct ultrastructural zones:

| **Zone** | **Name** | **Origin** | **Thickness** | **Ultrastructural Appearance** | **Characteristics** |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Anterior Banded Zone (ABZ)** | Fetal layer | Produced in utero (by ~4th month of gestation) | ~3 µm (remains constant throughout life) | Regular, banded pattern with a periodicity of approximately 110 nm | Composed of wide-spaced [[type VIII collagen]] arranged in a hexagonal lattice; does not change after birth |
| **Posterior Non-Banded Zone (PNBZ)** | Postnatal layer | Secreted by endothelial cells throughout postnatal life | Increases from ~0 µm at birth to ~10 µm or more in elderly individuals | Homogeneous, amorphous, non-banded, granular or finely filamentous material | Composed primarily of [[type IV collagen]], [[type VIII collagen]], [[laminin]], [[fibronectin]], and [[perlecan]]; thickens continuously |

In pathological conditions, a **third posterior banded layer (PBL)** may be observed posterior to the PNBZ. This layer is characteristically seen in [[Fuchs' endothelial corneal dystrophy]] and represents abnormal secretory products of dysfunctional endothelial cells. The PBL has a banded appearance similar to the ABZ but is irregular and is associated with the formation of [[corneal guttae]].

### Molecular Composition

Descemet's membrane is a complex extracellular matrix composed of multiple molecular constituents:

- **[[Type IV Collagen]]**: The predominant structural component, forming a meshwork that provides tensile strength and serves as a scaffold for other matrix molecules. Both α1(IV) and α2(IV) chains are present.
- **[[Type VIII Collagen]]**: A short-chain, non-fibrillar collagen that forms the characteristic hexagonal lattice of the anterior banded zone. It is considered a hallmark component of Descemet's membrane.
- **[[Laminin]]**: A major glycoprotein of basement membranes, contributing to cell adhesion and signaling between the endothelium and its substrate.
- **[[Fibronectin]]**: Involved in cell-matrix adhesion and wound healing responses.
- **[[Perlecan]] (Heparan Sulfate Proteoglycan 2)**: A large proteoglycan that contributes to the structural organization and filtration properties of the membrane.
- **[[Nidogen]]** (Entactin): A bridging molecule linking laminin and type IV collagen networks.

The unique combination of these molecules endows Descemet's membrane with its remarkable elasticity, resistance to proteolytic degradation, and ability to serve as a selective barrier.

## Embryology and Development

Descemet's membrane is derived from [[neural crest]] cells, which also give rise to the [[corneal endothelium]], [[corneal stroma]] (keratocytes), and much of the anterior segment of the eye. During embryonic development, the corneal endothelial cells begin secreting the anterior banded zone of Descemet's membrane by approximately the **fourth month of gestation**. This fetal layer is fully formed at birth and does not change in thickness or structure thereafter.

After birth, the endothelial cells continue to deposit extracellular matrix material onto the posterior surface of the membrane, forming the posterior non-banded zone. This process continues throughout the individual's lifetime, accounting for the progressive thickening of Descemet's membrane with age.

The developmental origin of Descemet's membrane from neural crest cells is clinically relevant because many congenital anomalies affecting the membrane—such as [[posterior polymorphous corneal dystrophy]] (PPCD), [[congenital hereditary endothelial dystrophy]] (CHED), and [[Peters anomaly]]—are classified as [[neurocristopathies]], reflecting aberrant neural crest cell migration, differentiation, or function.

## Physiology and Function

Descemet's membrane serves several critical physiological functions:

### 1. Structural Support for the Corneal Endothelium

As the basement membrane of the [[corneal endothelial cells]], Descemet's membrane provides a stable substrate for endothelial cell attachment, migration, and function. The endothelial cells adhere to Descemet's membrane via [[hemidesmosomes]] and [[integrin]]-mediated focal adhesions. This attachment is essential for maintaining the monolayer architecture of the endothelium.

### 2. Contribution to Corneal Transparency

By supporting the endothelial pump-and-barrier function, Descemet's membrane indirectly contributes to the maintenance of [[corneal transparency]]. The endothelial cells actively pump fluid from the [[corneal stroma]] into the [[anterior chamber]] via [[Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase]] pumps and allow passive fluid movement through [[tight junctions]] (zonulae occludentes). Any disruption of Descemet's membrane that compromises endothelial cell integrity can lead to stromal edema and loss of transparency.

### 3. Biomechanical Resilience

Despite its thinness, Descemet's membrane is remarkably strong and elastic. It can withstand significant [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) even when the overlying stroma is severely compromised. This property is dramatically demonstrated in the clinical entity of **[[descemetocele]]** (keratocele), in which the stroma is completely eroded (e.g., by infection or [[corneal ulceration]]), leaving only Descemet's membrane bulging forward under the force of IOP. The membrane's ability to resist perforation in this setting is a testament to its extraordinary tensile strength.

### 4. Selective Permeability Barrier

Descemet's membrane acts as a semi-permeable barrier, allowing the passage of nutrients and metabolic waste products between the [[aqueous humor]] and the corneal stroma while restricting the movement of larger molecules and cells. This filtration function is important for corneal nutrition and metabolic homeostasis.

### 5. Wound Healing and Regeneration

Following injury, [[corneal endothelial cells]] can secrete new Descemet's membrane material to repair defects. However, because human corneal endothelial cells have extremely limited proliferative capacity in vivo (they are arrested in the [[G1 phase]] of the [[cell cycle]]), the regenerative response primarily involves cell migration and enlargement ([[polymegethism]]) rather than cell division. The newly secreted membrane material may differ in composition and ultrastructure from the native membrane, sometimes forming a **retrocorneal fibrous membrane** in pathological healing responses.

## Pathophysiology

Pathological alterations of Descemet's membrane can be broadly categorized into **dystrophic**, **degenerative**, **traumatic**, **inflammatory**, and **ectatic** processes.

### Fuchs' Endothelial Corneal Dystrophy (FECD)

[[Fuchs' endothelial corneal dystrophy]] is the most common and clinically significant condition affecting Descemet's membrane. It is a bilateral, progressive, often asymmetric disorder characterized by:

- **Formation of [[corneal guttae]]**: Focal, wart-like excrescences of abnormal [[collagen]] deposited on the posterior surface of Descemet's membrane by dysfunctional endothelial cells. Guttae appear as dark spots on [[specular microscopy]] and as beaten-metal reflex on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]].
- **Thickening of Descemet's membrane**: Due to the deposition of a posterior banded layer and abnormal extracellular matrix.
- **Progressive endothelial cell loss**: Leading to corneal decompensation, [[stromal edema]], [[epithelial bullae]] ([[bullous keratopathy]]), and ultimately significant visual impairment.
- **Genetic basis**: Both early-onset (rare, associated with mutations in the [[COL8A2]] gene encoding type VIII collagen α2 chain) and late-onset (common, strongly associated with trinucleotide repeat expansion—CTG—in the [[TCF4]] gene) forms have been identified.

### Posterior Polymorphous Corneal Dystrophy (PPCD)

[[Posterior polymorphous corneal dystrophy]] is an autosomal dominant disorder characterized by abnormal, epithelial-like transformation of the corneal endothelial cells. The endothelial cells in PPCD express [[cytokeratins]] and exhibit multilayering, microvilli, and desmosomal junctions—features normally associated with [[epithelial cells]] rather than endothelium. Descemet's membrane in PPCD shows characteristic multilaminar thickening with abnormal collagenous layers. Mutations in [[ZEB1]] (also known as TCF8), [[OVOL2]], and [[GRHL2]] genes have been implicated. Slit-lamp findings include vesicular lesions, band-like lesions, and diffuse opacification at the level of Descemet's membrane.

### Congenital Hereditary Endothelial Dystrophy (CHED)

[[Congenital hereditary endothelial dystrophy]] presents at birth or early infancy with bilateral corneal edema and clouding. It is caused by mutations in the [[SLC4A11]] gene, which encodes a bicarbonate transporter critical for endothelial pump function. Descemet's membrane in CHED is markedly thickened, and the endothelium is attenuated with sparse, dysfunctional cells.

### Descemet's Membrane Detachment

Detachment or separation of Descemet's membrane from the overlying stroma is a recognized complication of:

- **[[Cataract surgery]]** (most common cause): Inadvertent stripping or hydraulic dissection during [[phacoemulsification]] can cause localized or extensive DM detachment.
- **[[Intraocular surgery]]**: Including [[glaucoma]] filtering procedures, [[anterior chamber]] [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) implantation, and [[vitrectomy]].
- **[[Trauma]]**: Blunt or penetrating ocular injuries.
- **Forceps delivery**: Birth trauma can cause vertical or oblique [[Haab's striae]] (breaks in Descemet's membrane) in [[congenital glaucoma]], or horizontal tears in forceps-related injury.

Detached Descemet's membrane scrolls upon itself due to its inherent elasticity, with the endothelial surface rolling inward. Small, peripheral detachments may spontaneously reattach, but larger or visually significant detachments require surgical intervention, typically with intracameral air or [[sulfur hexafluoride]] (SF₆) gas tamponade.

### Descemetocele

A **[[descemetocele]]** occurs when progressive corneal stromal thinning (from infection, inflammation, or [[Mooren's ulcer]]) results in complete loss of the stroma, leaving only Descemet's membrane and the endothelium as the remaining barrier between the anterior chamber and the external environment. Because Descemet's membrane does not stain with [[fluorescein]] (unlike exposed stroma), the Seidel test will be negative unless perforation has occurred. A descemetocele is an **ophthalmic emergency** requiring urgent intervention (e.g., [[tissue adhesive]], [[corneal transplantation]], or [[amniotic membrane]] grafting) to prevent perforation.

### Breaks in Descemet's Membrane

Breaks or ruptures in Descemet's membrane occur in several clinical contexts:

| **Condition** | **Orientation of Breaks** | **Mechanism** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Congenital glaucoma]] ([[Buphthalmos]])** | Horizontal or concentric ([[Haab's striae]]) | Elevated [[IOP]] stretching the cornea in infancy | Pathognomonic sign; causes corneal edema and amblyopia |
| **[[Keratoconus]] (acute [[corneal hydrops]])** | Irregular, often central | Progressive stromal thinning and ectasia leading to spontaneous DM rupture | Sudden corneal edema, pain, decreased vision; usually self-limiting over weeks to months |
| **Forceps birth injury** | Vertical or oblique | Mechanical compression during delivery | Unilateral; may cause amblyopia if not recognized |
| **Traumatic** | Variable | Blunt or penetrating trauma | May cause localized edema; requires careful assessment |

In [[keratoconus]], acute [[corneal hydrops]] results from a sudden break in Descemet's membrane, allowing [[aqueous humor]] to flood into the [[corneal stroma]], producing dramatic stromal and epithelial edema. The endothelial cells gradually migrate to cover the defect, and the edema typically resolves over 6–12 weeks, often leaving a central scar that may paradoxically improve contact lens fitting in some patients.

### Inflammatory and Infectious Conditions

Descemet's membrane may be affected in severe [[keratitis]] (bacterial, fungal, viral, or [[Acanthamoeba]]). In [[herpes simplex virus]] (HSV) [[disciform keratitis]], immune-mediated inflammation targets the endothelium and Descemet's membrane, producing characteristic [[keratic precipitates]] (KPs) on the posterior surface of the membrane. [[Descemet's folds]] (stria) are visible on slit-lamp examination as fine lines in the deep cornea, indicating stromal and/or endothelial edema from any cause.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Symptoms related to Descemet's membrane pathology are primarily a consequence of endothelial dysfunction and resultant corneal edema:

- **Blurred vision**: Progressive or acute, depending on the underlying condition. In FECD, vision is characteristically worse upon waking (due to overnight corneal hydration from closed-eye conditions reducing evaporation) and improves throughout the day.
- **Halos and glare**: Caused by light scattering from corneal edema and guttae.
- **Ocular pain and foreign body sensation**: Particularly when epithelial bullae form and rupture in advanced [[bullous keratopathy]].
- **Photophobia**: From corneal edema and surface irregularity.
- **Tearing**: Reflex [[lacrimation]] secondary to epithelial disruption.
- **Decreased contrast sensitivity**: Even before significant visual acuity loss, patients with early FECD may report difficulty with contrast and low-light conditions.

In conditions involving Descemet's membrane detachment (e.g., post-surgical), patients may present with sudden-onset corneal edema localized to the area of detachment, with corresponding visual impairment.

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] is the primary clinical tool for evaluating Descemet's membrane. Key findings include:

- **Corneal guttae**: Appear as a beaten-metal or golden-brown reflex on retroillumination and as dark excrescences on direct focal illumination of the endothelium.
- **Descemet's folds (striae)**: Fine, parallel lines in the deep stroma/Descemet's membrane indicating edema.
- **Descemetocele**: A clear, bulging area in a thinned cornea that does not stain with fluorescein.
- **Descemet's membrane detachment**: A translucent, scrolled membrane visible in the anterior chamber, often with overlying localized corneal edema.
- **[[Haab's striae]]**: Curvilinear breaks visible in the deep cornea in congenital glaucoma.
- **Keratic precipitates**: Inflammatory deposits on the endothelial surface of Descemet's membrane.

### Specular Microscopy

[[Specular microscopy]] provides non-invasive, high-magnification imaging of the corneal endothelium and the posterior surface of Descemet's membrane. It allows quantitative assessment of:

- **Endothelial cell density (ECD)**: Normal adult values range from approximately 2,000–3,000 cells/mm². Values below 500–800 cells/mm² are associated with risk of corneal decompensation.
- **[[Polymegethism]]**: Variation in cell size (coefficient of variation >30% is abnormal).
- **[[Pleomorphism]]**: Variation in cell shape (percentage of hexagonal cells <60% is abnormal).
- **Guttae**: Appear as dark, non-reflective areas within the endothelial mosaic.

### Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography (AS-OCT)

[[Anterior segment OCT]] (AS-OCT) provides high-resolution, cross-sectional imaging of the cornea and can directly visualize:

- Descemet's membrane detachment (extent, location, and configuration of the detached membrane)
- Corneal edema and its distribution
- Descemetocele
- Thickening of Descemet's membrane in dystrophic conditions
- Interface abnormalities following [[keratoplasty]]

AS-OCT is invaluable in the preoperative planning and postoperative monitoring of endothelial keratoplasty procedures.

### Confocal Microscopy

[[In vivo confocal microscopy]] (IVCM) provides cellular-level imaging of all corneal layers, including Descemet's membrane and the endothelium. It can identify:

- Guttae morphology and density
- Endothelial cell morphology
- Inflammatory cells on Descemet's membrane
- Abnormal deposits or structural changes

### Pachymetry

[[Corneal pachymetry]] (ultrasonic or optical) measures corneal thickness, which serves as an indirect indicator of endothelial/Descemet's membrane function. Central corneal thickness (CCT) greater than approximately 600 µm may suggest endothelial compromise, though normal CCT varies among individuals.

### Ultrasound Biomicroscopy (UBM)

[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] can be useful in evaluating Descemet's membrane detachments, particularly when corneal edema is too dense to permit adequate optical imaging.

## Management and Treatment

### Conservative and Medical Management

- **Hypertonic saline solutions and ointments** (e.g., [[sodium chloride 5%]] drops and ointment): Used to create an osmotic gradient that draws fluid from the edematous cornea, providing temporary symptomatic relief in conditions such as FECD. Drops are used during the day and ointment at bedtime.
- **Hair dryer technique**: Patients with early FECD may be instructed to use a hair dryer held at arm's length on a low, warm setting directed at the eyes upon waking to accelerate corneal dehydration and improve morning vision.
- **Bandage [[contact lens]]**: Applied in cases of painful epithelial bullae to protect exposed nerve endings and promote epithelial healing.
- **Topical [[corticosteroids]]**: Used to manage inflammation in conditions such as [[corneal graft rejection]], disciform keratitis, or inflammatory Descemet's membrane detachment.
- **Intraocular pressure management**: In cases of Descemet's membrane breaks associated with [[congenital glaucoma]], IOP-lowering therapy is the primary treatment.

### Surgical Management

#### Descemet's Membrane Detachment Repair

Small, non-visually significant Descemet's membrane detachments may be observed. Larger or visually significant detachments are treated with:

- **Intracameral air injection**: Air bubble tamponade to press the detached membrane against the overlying stroma, facilitating reattachment. The patient is positioned (usually supine) to optimize bubble contact with the detachment.
- **[[Sulfur hexafluoride]] (SF₆) gas or [[perfluoropropane]] (C₃F₈) gas**: Longer-acting gas tamponade agents used for persistent or recurrent detachments.
- **Suture fixation**: Rarely, transcorneal sutures may be used to secure a detached membrane.

#### Endothelial Keratoplasty

The development of selective endothelial keratoplasty techniques has revolutionized the surgical management of Descemet's membrane and endothelial pathology:

| **Procedure** | **Description** | **Indications** | **Advantages** | **Limitations** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **[[DSAEK]] (Descemet's Stripping Automated Endothelial Keratoplasty)** | Replacement of diseased Descemet's membrane and endothelium with a donor graft consisting of posterior stroma, Descemet's membrane, and endothelium | FECD, [[bullous keratopathy]], failed prior grafts | Faster visual recovery than [[penetrating keratoplasty]] (PK); sutureless; maintains globe integrity | Donor stroma-host interface may limit best-corrected visual acuity; risk of graft dislocation |
| **[[DMEK]] (Descemet's Membrane Endothelial Keratoplasty)** | Selective transplantation of donor Descemet's membrane and endothelium only (no stromal tissue) | FECD, bullous keratopathy | Superior visual outcomes (often 20/20 or better); lower rejection rates; fastest visual recovery among keratoplasty techniques | Technically demanding; higher rebubbling rate; donor tissue preparation is challenging |
| **[[DALK]] (Deep Anterior Lamellar Keratoplasty)** | Replacement of the corneal stroma while preserving the host's Descemet's membrane and endothelium | [[Keratoconus]], stromal scars, stromal dystrophies | Eliminates endothelial rejection; preserves host endothelium | Technically challenging; risk of intraoperative DM perforation (big-bubble technique) |
| **[[PK]] (Penetrating Keratoplasty)** | Full-thickness corneal transplantation | Advanced disease, combined stromal and endothelial pathology, failed prior grafts | Addresses all corneal layers | Longer visual recovery; higher rejection risk; suture-related complications |

##### Descemet's Membrane Endothelial Keratoplasty (DMEK) — Detailed Considerations

DMEK has emerged as the gold standard for the surgical treatment of endothelial dysfunction. Key technical aspects include:

- **Donor tissue preparation**: Descemet's membrane with its endothelium is carefully peeled from the donor cornea. The tissue naturally scrolls with the endothelium on the outside (due to the inherent elasticity of DM).
- **Recipient preparation**: The host's diseased Descemet's membrane is stripped ([[descemetorhexis]]) under air in the anterior chamber.
- **Graft insertion**: The donor DM scroll is inserted into the anterior chamber through a small incision using an injector or a pull-through technique.
- **Unscrolling and positioning**: The graft is carefully unscrolled and centered on the posterior corneal surface.
- **Air tamponade**: An air bubble is injected to press the graft against the posterior stroma, facilitating adhesion.
- **Postoperative positioning**: The patient remains supine for several hours to maintain air-graft-stroma contact.

Complications of DMEK include graft detachment (requiring rebubbling in approximately 20–30% of cases in early series, though rates have decreased with surgical experience), primary graft failure, [[graft rejection]], and iatrogenic [[glaucoma]] from air/gas bubble-induced pupillary block.

#### Descemetocele Management

- **[[Cyanoacrylate tissue adhesive]]**: Applied to seal or reinforce the thinned area.
- **[[Amniotic membrane transplantation]]**: Provides biological scaffolding and anti-inflammatory properties.
- **Emergency [[tectonic keratoplasty]]** or [[corneal transplantation]]: Required for large descemetoceles or impending/actual perforation.

#### Emerging Therapies

- **[[Rho-kinase inhibitors]] (e.g., [[ripasudil]], [[netarsudil]])**: Investigated for their ability to promote corneal endothelial cell proliferation and migration, potentially enhancing endothelial recovery after procedures such as Descemet's stripping only (DSO) without transplantation.
- **[[Descemet's Stripping Only]] (DSO)**: A novel approach for central FECD in which the diseased central Descemet's membrane is stripped without transplantation, relying on peripheral endothelial cell migration to repopulate the denuded area. Often combined with topical Rho-kinase inhibitors. Early results are promising but long-term data are still being accumulated.
- **[[Cultured endothelial cell injection]]**: Injection of cultured human corneal endothelial cells into the anterior chamber combined with Rho-kinase inhibitor application and prone positioning. A landmark clinical trial by Kinoshita et al. (2018) demonstrated efficacy, and this approach may eventually reduce dependence on donor corneal tissue.

## Descemet's Membrane in Specific Clinical Contexts

### Keratoconus and Acute Corneal Hydrops

In advanced [[keratoconus]], progressive stromal thinning and ectasia can lead to spontaneous rupture of Descemet's membrane, a condition known as **acute [[corneal hydrops]]**. Aqueous humor rushes through the break into the stroma, causing sudden, dramatic corneal edema. Management is typically conservative (hypertonic saline, cycloplegics, and observation), as the endothelial cells gradually cover the defect and the edema resolves over weeks to months. Intracameral air or SF₆ injection has been used to hasten resolution. The resulting central scar may paradoxically flatten the cone and improve contact lens fitting.

### Iridocorneal Endothelial (ICE) Syndrome

[[Iridocorneal endothelial syndrome]] encompasses a spectrum of conditions ([[Chandler syndrome]], [[essential iris atrophy]], [[Cogan-Reese syndrome]]) characterized by abnormal proliferation of corneal endothelial cells that migrate across the anterior chamber angle and onto the iris surface. The endothelial cells in ICE syndrome produce an abnormal, glass-like Descemet's membrane that extends beyond its normal boundaries. This ectopic membrane can obstruct the [[trabecular meshwork]], causing secondary [[glaucoma]], and can contract, causing [[corectopia]], [[iris atrophy]], and [[polycoria]].

### Post-Surgical Considerations

Descemet's membrane integrity is a critical consideration in virtually all anterior segment surgeries. During [[phacoemulsification]], instruments and fluid dynamics within the anterior chamber can inadvertently strip or detach Descemet's membrane. Careful surgical technique, including adequate [[viscoelastic]] protection, proper wound construction, and gentle instrument manipulation, is essential to minimize this risk.

## Histopathology

On light microscopy, Descemet's membrane appears as a homogeneous, eosinophilic, PAS-positive (periodic acid-Schiff) band at the posterior cornea. It stains positively for [[type IV collagen]] and [[laminin]] on immunohistochemistry. Electron microscopy reveals the characteristic anterior banded and posterior non-banded zones described above.

In FECD, histopathology demonstrates:
- Thickened Descemet's membrane with a posterior banded layer
- Guttae (mushroom-shaped or anvil-shaped excrescences)
- Attenuated, sparse endothelial cells with degenerative changes
- Subepithelial fibrosis in advanced cases

## Prognosis

The prognosis for conditions affecting Descemet's membrane varies widely depending on the underlying etiology and the availability of appropriate treatment. With modern endothelial keratoplasty techniques, particularly DMEK, patients with FECD and other endothelial disorders can achieve excellent visual outcomes, with the majority attaining best-corrected visual acuity of 20/25 or better. Graft survival rates for DMEK exceed 95% at 5 years in experienced centers. Emerging cell-based therapies and pharmacological approaches (Rho-kinase inhibitors, cultured endothelial cell injection) hold promise for further improving outcomes and reducing dependence on donor tissue.
