

# Tear Film

---

The **tear film** is a complex, highly organized, multi-layered fluid structure that coats the exposed [[ocular surface]], including the [[corneal epithelium]] and [[conjunctival epithelium]]. It is essential for maintaining optical clarity, providing lubrication, delivering nutrients and oxygen to the avascular cornea, and serving as the first line of immunological defense against microbial pathogens. The tear film is approximately 3–10 μm in thickness and possesses a volume of roughly 6–7 μL within the [[conjunctival sac]] at any given time, with a basal turnover rate of approximately 1.2 μL/min. Disruption of tear film homeostasis is the central pathophysiological event in [[dry eye disease]] (DED), one of the most prevalent conditions encountered in clinical [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] practice worldwide.

---

## Anatomy and Structural Composition

### Classical Three-Layer Model

Historically, the tear film was described as a trilaminar structure consisting of three distinct layers:

| Layer | Thickness | Primary Source | Key Components |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lipid layer** | 0.02–0.10 μm | [[Meibomian glands]] (with contributions from [[glands of Zeis]] and [[glands of Moll]]) | Polar and nonpolar lipids, wax esters, cholesterol esters, phospholipids |
| **Aqueous layer** | 3–7 μm | [[Lacrimal gland]] (main and accessory glands of [[Krause]] and [[Wolfring]]) | Water, electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻), proteins (lysozyme, lactoferrin, lipocalin, IgA), glucose, urea, growth factors |
| **Mucin layer** | 0.02–0.05 μm (gel-forming); transmembrane mucins extend 200–500 nm | [[Conjunctival goblet cells]], corneal and conjunctival epithelial cells | Gel-forming mucins (MUC5AC), transmembrane mucins (MUC1, MUC4, MUC16), soluble mucins |

### Modern Integrated Model (Gel-Sol Concept)

Contemporary understanding has evolved beyond the rigid three-layer paradigm. The tear film is now recognized as a **two-phase structure**: an outer lipid layer overlying a **mucoaqueous phase** in which soluble mucins form a concentration gradient, being most dense at the epithelial surface (glycocalyx) and progressively less concentrated toward the lipid-aqueous interface. This integrated model, championed by researchers such as Bron, Tiffany, and Govindarajan, better explains the biophysical behavior of the tear film, including its stability, wettability, and resistance to evaporation.

#### Lipid Layer

The **lipid layer** is the outermost component of the tear film and is primarily secreted by the [[Meibomian glands]], which are large sebaceous glands embedded within the [[tarsal plates]] of the upper and lower [[eyelids]]. There are approximately 25–40 glands in the upper lid and 20–30 in the lower lid. The lipid layer is organized into:

- **Polar lipid sublayer** (inner): Composed of phospholipids and O-acyl-ω-hydroxy fatty acids (OAHFAs), this sublayer interfaces with the aqueous-mucin phase and is critical for spreading and stability.
- **Nonpolar lipid sublayer** (outer): Composed of wax esters and cholesterol esters, this sublayer provides the primary barrier against evaporation.

The lipid layer retards evaporation of the aqueous phase by up to 90–95%, maintains a smooth optical surface, and prevents tear overflow (epiphora) by lowering surface tension at the lid margins. Dysfunction of the Meibomian glands ([[Meibomian gland dysfunction]], MGD) is the leading cause of [[evaporative dry eye]].

#### Aqueous-Mucin Phase

The **aqueous component** constitutes the bulk of the tear film volume and is produced by the main [[lacrimal gland]] (located in the superotemporal orbit within the [[lacrimal fossa]]) and the accessory lacrimal glands of [[Krause]] (located in the conjunctival fornices) and [[Wolfring]] (located near the superior tarsal border). The aqueous phase contains:

- **Electrolytes**: Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, and phosphate, maintaining an osmolarity of approximately **296–302 mOsm/L** under normal conditions.
- **Proteins**: Over 1,500 proteins have been identified in the tear proteome. Key proteins include:
  - **[[Lysozyme]]**: Bacteriolytic enzyme (~1.4 mg/mL), cleaves peptidoglycan in bacterial cell walls.
  - **[[Lactoferrin]]**: Iron-binding glycoprotein (~1.6 mg/mL), bacteriostatic and anti-inflammatory.
  - **[[Lipocalin]]** (tear-specific prealbumin): Binds lipids and scavenges potentially harmful hydrophobic molecules.
  - **Secretory [[IgA]]**: Primary immunoglobulin in tears, prevents microbial adhesion.
  - **[[Epidermal growth factor]] (EGF)**: Promotes corneal epithelial wound healing.
  - **[[Transforming growth factor-beta]] (TGF-β)**: Immunomodulatory cytokine.
- **Glucose**: Approximately 3.6–6.6 mg/dL, essential for corneal epithelial metabolism.
- **Dissolved oxygen**: Supplied from the atmosphere during eye opening and from the palpebral conjunctival vasculature during sleep.

The **mucin component** is produced by [[conjunctival goblet cells]] (secreting gel-forming mucin [[MUC5AC]]) and by the corneal and conjunctival epithelial cells (producing transmembrane mucins [[MUC1]], [[MUC4]], and [[MUC16]]). The transmembrane mucins form the **[[glycocalyx]]**, a dense carbohydrate-rich layer that converts the hydrophobic corneal epithelial surface into a hydrophilic one, enabling the aqueous tear film to spread uniformly. Soluble mucins such as MUC7 and cleaved ectodomains of transmembrane mucins contribute to tear viscosity and lubrication.

## Physiology of the Tear Film

### Tear Production

Tear secretion is regulated by the **[[lacrimal functional unit]]** (LFU), a concept introduced by Stern et al. (1998), which encompasses the lacrimal glands, ocular surface (cornea and conjunctiva), eyelids (including Meibomian glands), and the sensory and motor nerves that connect them. The LFU operates as an integrated feedback loop:

1. **Afferent limb**: Sensory nerve endings (primarily from the [[ophthalmic division]] (V₁) of the [[trigeminal nerve]]) in the cornea and conjunctiva detect environmental stimuli (dryness, temperature, osmolarity changes, mechanical irritation).
2. **Central integration**: Signals are processed in the [[trigeminal nucleus]] and relayed to the [[superior salivatory nucleus]] and [[lacrimal nucleus]] in the [[pons]].
3. **Efferent limb**: Parasympathetic fibers (via the [[facial nerve]], CN VII, through the [[greater petrosal nerve]] and [[pterygopalatine ganglion]]) stimulate lacrimal gland secretion. Sympathetic fibers (from the [[superior cervical ganglion]]) modulate protein and electrolyte secretion.

Basal tear secretion maintains the tear film between blinks, while **reflex tearing** is triggered by ocular surface irritation, bright light, emotional stimuli, or nasal mucosal stimulation (nasolacrimatory reflex). The distinction between "basal" and "reflex" tearing has been debated; some investigators argue that all tearing is reflexive to some degree, driven by continuous low-level sensory input.

### Tear Distribution and the Blink Mechanism

The [[blink reflex]] is essential for tear film distribution, renewal, and stability. The average spontaneous blink rate is 15–20 blinks per minute, with each blink cycle lasting approximately 300–400 milliseconds. During a blink:

1. The upper lid descends, compressing the tear film and expressing Meibomian gland secretions from the gland orifices.
2. The tear film is redistributed across the ocular surface in a superior-to-inferior and temporal-to-nasal pattern.
3. Excess tears are directed toward the [[puncta]] (upper and lower) at the medial [[canthus]].
4. The lipid layer is re-spread over the aqueous-mucin phase, restoring the anti-evaporative barrier.

Incomplete blinks or reduced blink rates (e.g., during prolonged digital screen use, a phenomenon termed **[[digital eye strain]]** or computer vision syndrome) lead to increased tear film evaporation and instability, particularly in the inferior corneal zone.

### Tear Drainage

Tears drain from the ocular surface through the **[[lacrimal drainage system]]**:

- **[[Puncta]]** (superior and inferior) → **[[Canaliculi]]** → **[[Lacrimal sac]]** (within the [[lacrimal fossa]] of the [[lacrimal bone]]) → **[[Nasolacrimal duct]]** → **[[Inferior meatus]]** of the nasal cavity.

The **lacrimal pump mechanism** (Rosengren-Doane mechanism) is driven by the action of the [[orbicularis oculi muscle]] during blinking, which creates negative pressure within the lacrimal sac, drawing tears through the canaliculi. Approximately 10–25% of tear volume is lost to evaporation, with the remainder draining through the nasolacrimal system or being absorbed by the conjunctiva.

### Tear Film Stability and Breakup

Between blinks, the tear film must remain stable and optically smooth. **Tear film breakup** refers to the formation of dry spots on the corneal surface after the last complete blink. The time from the last blink to the first appearance of a dry spot is the **[[tear breakup time]]** (TBUT), a critical clinical measurement. Normal TBUT is generally considered ≥10 seconds. Breakup occurs due to:

- Evaporation of the aqueous phase
- Lipid layer deficiency or contamination
- Mucin layer abnormalities reducing wettability
- Localized thinning from surface irregularities

The **Navier-Stokes equations** and **Marangoni flow** models have been applied to describe the hydrodynamic behavior of the tear film, including the role of surface tension gradients in driving tear film thinning and breakup.

## Pathophysiology of Tear Film Dysfunction

Tear film dysfunction is the hallmark of [[dry eye disease]] (DED), defined by the Tear Film and Ocular Surface Society Dry Eye Workshop II ([[TFOS DEWS II]], 2017) as:

> *"A multifactorial disease of the ocular surface characterized by a loss of homeostasis of the tear film, and accompanied by ocular symptoms, in which tear film instability and hyperosmolarity, ocular surface inflammation and damage, and neurosensory abnormalities play etiological roles."*

### Core Mechanisms

The pathophysiology of tear film disruption revolves around two interrelated core mechanisms:

#### 1. Tear Film Instability

Instability may arise from deficiencies in any tear film component:

- **Lipid layer deficiency** (most commonly from [[Meibomian gland dysfunction]]): Leads to increased evaporation.
- **Aqueous deficiency** (from lacrimal gland dysfunction, as in [[Sjögren syndrome]] or age-related lacrimal gland atrophy): Reduces tear volume.
- **Mucin deficiency** (from goblet cell loss, as in [[Stevens-Johnson syndrome]], [[ocular cicatricial pemphigoid]], or [[vitamin A deficiency]]): Impairs wettability.

#### 2. Tear Hyperosmolarity

Regardless of the initiating cause, tear film instability leads to localized or generalized **[[tear hyperosmolarity]]** (>308 mOsm/L), which is considered the central unifying mechanism in DED pathophysiology. Hyperosmolarity triggers:

- Activation of **[[mitogen-activated protein kinase]] (MAPK)** and **[[nuclear factor-kappa B]] (NF-κB)** signaling cascades in corneal and conjunctival epithelial cells.
- Release of pro-inflammatory cytokines: **[[interleukin-1β]] (IL-1β)**, **[[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] (TNF-α)**, **[[interleukin-6]] (IL-6)**, and **[[matrix metalloproteinase-9]] (MMP-9)**.
- Apoptosis of surface epithelial cells and goblet cells.
- Recruitment of [[T-helper 1]] (Th1) and [[T-helper 17]] (Th17) lymphocytes to the ocular surface and lacrimal gland, perpetuating a **self-amplifying inflammatory cycle** (the "vicious circle" of DED).

### Classification of Tear Film Dysfunction

| Classification | Subtype | Primary Deficiency | Common Etiologies |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Aqueous-deficient dry eye (ADDE)** | Sjögren syndrome ADDE | Aqueous | [[Sjögren syndrome]] (primary or secondary) |
| | Non-Sjögren ADDE | Aqueous | Age-related lacrimal gland dysfunction, [[lacrimal gland infiltration]], [[sarcoidosis]], [[graft-versus-host disease]], post-[[LASIK]] neurotrophic deficiency |
| **Evaporative dry eye (EDE)** | Intrinsic | Lipid | [[Meibomian gland dysfunction]], lid aperture disorders, low blink rate, [[contact lens]] wear |
| | Extrinsic | Lipid/Mucin | [[Vitamin A deficiency]], topical drug preservative toxicity ([[benzalkonium chloride]]), [[contact lens]] wear, [[allergic conjunctivitis]] |
| **Mixed mechanism** | Combined ADDE + EDE | Aqueous + Lipid | Most common clinical presentation; aging, systemic medications ([[antihistamines]], [[anticholinergics]], [[antidepressants]]) |

### Neurosensory Abnormalities

The TFOS DEWS II report recognized **neurosensory abnormalities** as a distinct etiological component. Damage to corneal nerves (e.g., post-[[refractive surgery]], [[herpes simplex keratitis]], [[diabetes mellitus]]-related neuropathy) can lead to:

- **Reduced corneal sensitivity** ([[neurotrophic keratopathy]]): Decreased reflex tearing and blinking, leading to tear film instability.
- **Neuropathic pain**: Patients experience significant ocular discomfort disproportionate to clinical signs, possibly due to peripheral nerve sensitization or central pain processing abnormalities.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Patients with tear film dysfunction present with a constellation of symptoms that may fluctuate in severity throughout the day and are often exacerbated by environmental factors (low humidity, wind, air conditioning, prolonged screen use).

### Common Symptoms

| Symptom | Description |
|---|---|
| **Dryness** | Sensation of ocular dryness, grittiness, or sandiness |
| **Foreign body sensation** | Feeling of something in the eye |
| **Burning/stinging** | Often worsened by environmental triggers |
| **Photophobia** | Light sensitivity due to corneal surface irregularity |
| **Blurred vision** | Fluctuating, often improves with blinking; caused by tear film optical irregularity |
| **Tearing (paradoxical)** | Reflex tearing in response to ocular surface irritation (especially in evaporative dry eye) |
| **Ocular fatigue** | Difficulty sustaining visual tasks |
| **Contact lens intolerance** | Reduced wearing time, discomfort |
| **Mucous discharge** | Stringy or filamentous mucus, especially in aqueous-deficient states |
| **Redness** | [[Conjunctival hyperemia]] from chronic inflammation |

### Symptom Assessment Tools

Standardized questionnaires are essential for quantifying symptom severity and monitoring treatment response:

- **[[Ocular Surface Disease Index]] (OSDI)**: 12-item questionnaire; scores 0–100; ≥13 indicates dry eye symptoms.
- **[[Dry Eye Questionnaire-5]] (DEQ-5)**: 5-item screening tool; score ≥6 suggests dry eye.
- **[[Standard Patient Evaluation of Eye Dryness]] (SPEED)**: Assesses frequency and severity of symptoms.
- **[[Symptom Assessment in Dry Eye]] (SANDE)**: Visual analog scale for frequency and severity.
- **[[National Eye Institute Visual Function Questionnaire]] (NEI-VFQ-25)**: Broader quality-of-life assessment.

## Diagnostic Modalities

A systematic, stepwise approach to tear film evaluation is recommended. The TFOS DEWS II Diagnostic Methodology report (2017) proposed a structured diagnostic battery.

### Screening and History

- Detailed symptom history using validated questionnaires (OSDI, DEQ-5)
- Medication review (systemic and topical)
- Environmental and occupational history
- Contact lens wear history
- Systemic disease history ([[rheumatoid arthritis]], [[systemic lupus erythematosus]], [[Sjögren syndrome]], [[thyroid disease]], [[diabetes]])
- Surgical history (refractive surgery, eyelid surgery)

### Clinical Tests

#### 1. Tear Film Stability

- **[[Fluorescein breakup time]] (FBUT)**: Instill fluorescein sodium, observe tear film under [[cobalt blue filter]] on [[slit-lamp biomicroscope]]. Time from last complete blink to first dark spot. **Normal: ≥10 seconds; Abnormal: <10 seconds; Diagnostic for DED: <5 seconds (per TFOS DEWS II).**
- **[[Non-invasive breakup time]] (NIBUT)**: Measured using instruments such as the [[Keratograph 5M]] (Oculus), [[Tearscope]], or [[topographer]]-based systems. Avoids fluorescein instillation artifact. **Normal: ≥10 seconds.**

#### 2. Tear Volume/Production

- **[[Schirmer test]]**:
  - **Schirmer I (without anesthesia)**: Measures reflex + basal secretion. Whatman #41 filter paper strip placed in lower fornix at junction of lateral and middle thirds. Read at 5 minutes. **Normal: ≥10 mm; Abnormal: <5 mm (suggestive of aqueous deficiency).**
  - **Schirmer I with anesthesia (Jones test)**: Measures basal secretion only. **Normal: ≥5 mm.**
  - **Schirmer II (with nasal stimulation)**: Assesses reflex secretion capacity.
- **[[Phenol red thread test]] (Zone-Quick)**: Cotton thread impregnated with pH-sensitive phenol red dye. Placed in lower fornix for 15 seconds. **Normal: ≥10 mm; Abnormal: <10 mm.** Less reflexogenic than Schirmer.
- **[[Tear meniscus height]] (TMH)**: Measured at the lower lid margin using [[anterior segment OCT]] or slit-lamp. **Normal: ≥0.2 mm; Abnormal: <0.2 mm.**

#### 3. Tear Osmolarity

- **[[TearLab Osmolarity System]]**: Collects 50 nL tear sample from the inferior lateral tear meniscus via microchip. **Normal: <308 mOsm/L; Mild/moderate DED: 308–316 mOsm/L; Severe DED: >316 mOsm/L.** An inter-eye difference of >8 mOsm/L is also diagnostic.

#### 4. Ocular Surface Staining

- **[[Fluorescein]] staining**: Highlights corneal epithelial defects under cobalt blue light (enhanced with [[Wratten #12 yellow filter]]). Graded using the **[[Oxford grading scale]]** (0–V) or **[[NEI/Industry grading scale]]**.
- **[[Lissamine green]] staining**: Stains devitalized or dead conjunctival and corneal epithelial cells. Particularly useful for detecting conjunctival damage. Graded using the **[[van Bijsterveld scoring system]]** (0–9).
- **[[Rose bengal]] staining**: Stains cells unprotected by mucin; largely replaced by lissamine green due to significant patient discomfort.

#### 5. Inflammatory Biomarkers

- **[[InflammaDry]]** (Quidel): Point-of-care immunoassay detecting [[MMP-9]] levels in tears. Positive result (≥40 ng/mL) indicates ocular surface inflammation. Useful for guiding anti-inflammatory therapy.

#### 6. Meibomian Gland Assessment

- **Lid margin examination**: Evaluate for telangiectasia, plugging of gland orifices, irregularity, and notching.
- **Meibomian gland expression**: Digital pressure or use of a [[Mastrota paddle]] to assess quality and expressibility of meibum. Graded on a scale:
  - **Quality**: 0 = clear; 1 = cloudy; 2 = granular; 3 = toothpaste-like/inspissated.
  - **Expressibility**: Number of glands yielding secretion per 5 glands assessed.
- **[[Meibography]]**: Infrared imaging of Meibomian gland morphology (e.g., using [[LipiView II]], [[Keratograph 5M]]). Detects gland dropout (atrophy), truncation, and tortuosity. Graded using the **[[Meiboscore]]** (0–3 per lid).

#### 7. Lipid Layer Assessment

- **[[LipiView II]] (Johnson & Johnson Vision)**: Interferometric measurement of lipid layer thickness. **Normal: ≥75 nm (interferometric color units, ICU); Thin: <60 nm.**
- **[[Tearscope Plus]]**: Specular reflection assessment of lipid layer interference patterns.

#### 8. Advanced Imaging

- **[[Anterior segment optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT)**: High-resolution imaging of tear meniscus height, tear film thickness, and corneal epithelial mapping.
- **[[In vivo confocal microscopy]] (IVCM)**: Enables visualization of corneal nerve density and morphology, inflammatory cell infiltration (dendritic cells), and goblet cell density. Particularly valuable in research settings and for evaluating [[neurotrophic keratopathy]].

### Diagnostic Algorithm (TFOS DEWS II)

| Step | Test | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Symptom questionnaire (OSDI/DEQ-5) | Screening |
| 2 | Tear osmolarity | Homeostasis marker |
| 3 | Ocular surface staining (fluorescein, lissamine green) | Ocular surface damage |
| 4 | TBUT (fluorescein or non-invasive) | Tear film stability |
| 5 | Tear volume (Schirmer, TMH) | Aqueous production |
| 6 | Meibography and gland expression | Meibomian gland status |
| 7 | Inflammatory markers (MMP-9) | Inflammation |

A diagnosis of DED requires **symptoms AND at least one positive homeostasis marker** (osmolarity >308 mOsm/L, TBUT <10 s, or ocular surface staining above threshold).

## Management and Treatment

Management of tear film dysfunction follows a **staged, stepwise approach** based on disease severity, as recommended by the TFOS DEWS II Management and Therapy report.

### Step 1: Education, Environmental Modification, and Tear Supplementation

#### Patient Education
- Explanation of the chronic nature of tear film dysfunction
- Identification and modification of exacerbating factors (screen time, environmental humidity, air flow)
- Emphasis on adherence to therapy

#### Environmental Modifications
- Use of **humidifiers** to increase ambient humidity
- Avoidance of direct air flow from fans, heaters, and air conditioning
- Use of **wraparound glasses** or **moisture chamber spectacles** to reduce evaporation
- Implementation of the **20-20-20 rule** for digital device users (every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds)

#### Lid Hygiene
- Warm compresses (40–45°C for 5–10 minutes) to soften inspissated meibum
- Lid massage to express Meibomian gland contents
- Lid scrubs with dilute baby shampoo, commercially available lid wipes, or [[hypochlorous acid]] (HOCl) spray (e.g., Avenova) to reduce bacterial biofilm on lid margins

#### Artificial Tears

Artificial tears are the mainstay of initial therapy. Selection depends on tear film deficiency type and severity.

| Type | Examples | Characteristics | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Low-viscosity aqueous drops** | Refresh Plus, TheraTears, Systane Ultra | Minimal blur, frequent dosing | Mild aqueous deficiency |
| **High-viscosity drops** | Refresh Celluvisc (CMC 1%), GenTeal Gel | Longer retention, transient blur | Moderate aqueous deficiency |
| **Lipid-containing drops** | Systane Balance, Refresh Optive Advanced, Retaine MGD | Supplement lipid layer | Evaporative dry eye, MGD |
| **Gels/Ointments** | Refresh PM, GenTeal Severe, Lacri-Lube | Prolonged retention, significant blur | Nocturnal use, severe dry eye |
| **Preservative-free formulations** | Unit-dose vials (Refresh Plus PF, TheraTears PF), COMOD system (Hylo) | Avoid preservative toxicity | Frequent dosing (>4×/day), [[contact lens]] wearers, moderate-severe DED |
| **Hyaluronic acid (HA)-based** | Hylo-Forte (0.2% HA), Blink Intensive Tears | Viscoelastic, mucoadhesive, promotes epithelial healing | Moderate-severe DED |
| **Trehalose-containing** | Thealoz Duo (trehalose + HA) | Bioprotectant, osmoprotective | Hyperosmolar tear film |

**Preservative considerations**: [[Benzalkonium chloride]] (BAK), the most common ophthalmic preservative, is cytotoxic to corneal and conjunctival epithelial cells and disrupts the tear film lipid layer. Preservative-free formulations or those with vanishing preservatives (e.g., [[Purite]] in Refresh, [[sodium perborate]] in GenTeal) are preferred for patients using drops more than four times daily.

#### Dietary Supplementation

- **[[Omega-3 essential fatty acids]]**: Oral supplementation with omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA from fish oil or flaxseed oil) has been proposed to improve Meibomian gland function and reduce ocular surface inflammation. The **DREAM study** (Dry Eye Assessment and Management, 2018) showed no significant benefit of omega-3 over olive oil placebo in moderate-to-severe DED, though earlier smaller studies suggested benefit. Clinical practice remains variable.

### Step 2: Prescription Pharmacotherapy and Procedures

#### Anti-Inflammatory Agents

| Agent | Mechanism | Dosing | Key Considerations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Cyclosporine A]] 0.05% (Restasis)** | Calcineurin inhibitor; suppresses T-cell activation and IL-2 production | 1 drop BID | Onset of action 3–6 months; stinging on instillation common |
| **[[Cyclosporine A]] 0.09% (Cequa)** | Nanomicellar formulation for enhanced bioavailability | 1 drop BID | Improved corneal penetration |
| **[[Lifitegrast]] 5% (Xiidra)** | [[Lymphocyte function-associated antigen-1]] (LFA-1) antagonist; blocks T-cell adhesion and activation via [[ICAM-1]] | 1 drop BID | Faster onset (2–6 weeks); dysgeusia (altered taste) common |
| **[[Loteprednol etabonate]] 0.25% (Eysuvis)** | Corticosteroid; short-term pulse therapy for DED flares | 1 drop QID × 2 weeks | Retrometabolized ("soft") steroid; lower risk of IOP elevation |
| **Short-term topical corticosteroids** (e.g., [[fluorometholone]] 0.1%, [[prednisolone acetate]] 1%) | Broad anti-inflammatory | Variable; typically 2–4 weeks | Monitor [[intraocular pressure]]; taper required |

#### Secretagogues

- **[[Diquafosol tetrasodium]] 3% (Diquas)**: P2Y2 purinergic receptor agonist; stimulates aqueous and mucin secretion from conjunctival epithelial cells and goblet cells. Available in Japan and several Asian countries; not FDA-approved in the United States.
- **[[Rebamipide]] 2% (Mucosta)**: Quinolinone derivative; increases mucin (MUC5AC and MUC16) secretion and has anti-inflammatory properties. Available in Japan and South Korea.

#### Lacritin and Novel Biologics

- **[[Lacritin]]**: A tear glycoprotein with prosecretory and mitogenic properties, currently under investigation as a topical therapeutic agent for DED.
- **[[KPI-121]] (loteprednol etabonate submicron)**: Nanoparticle formulation for enhanced ocular surface retention.
- **[[Reproxalap]] (Aldeyra Therapeutics)**: Reactive aldehyde species (RASP) inhibitor; targets inflammatory mediators upstream of cytokine release. Phase III trials ongoing.
- **[[Perfluorohexyloctane]] (NOV03/Miebo)**: A semifluorinated alkane eye drop approved by the FDA (2023) for the treatment of signs and symptoms of DED associated with MGD. It stabilizes the lipid layer and reduces evaporation.

#### Punctal Occlusion

- **[[Punctal plugs]]**: Silicone or collagen plugs inserted into the upper and/or lower [[puncta]] to reduce tear drainage and increase tear film residence time. Collagen plugs are temporary (dissolve in 3–14 days) and useful as a diagnostic trial. Silicone plugs are semi-permanent.
- **[[Punctal cautery]]**: Thermal or radiofrequency cautery for permanent punctal occlusion in severe aqueous-deficient DED.
- **Contraindications**: Active ocular surface inflammation should be controlled before punctal occlusion to avoid retention of inflammatory mediators.

#### In-Office Meibomian Gland Treatments

| Device/Procedure | Mechanism | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **[[LipiFlow]] (Johnson & Johnson Vision)** | Vectored thermal pulsation | Applies controlled heat (42.5°C) to inner lid surface with simultaneous pulsatile pressure to outer lid; single 12-minute treatment per eye |
| **[[iLux]] (Alcon)** | LED-based heat with compression | Handheld device; targeted treatment of individual glands |
| **[[TearCare]] (Sight Sciences)** | Open-eye thermal therapy | Adhesive SmartLid devices apply heat; followed by manual expression |
| **[[Intense pulsed light]] (IPL)** (e.g., OptiLight, Lumenis M22) | Broad-spectrum light (500–800 nm); reduces lid margin inflammation, eradicates [[Demodex]], improves meibum quality | Series of 4 treatments at 2–4 week intervals; FDA-cleared (OptiLight, 2022) for DED due to MGD |
| **[[Meibomian gland probing]]** (Maskin probing) | Mechanical opening of obstructed gland orifices with a fine probe | Performed under topical anesthesia; may relieve intraductal obstruction |
| **[[BlephEx]]** | Mechanical micro-exfoliation of lid margins | Removes bacterial biofilm, collarettes, and debris; adjunctive to lid hygiene |

### Step 3: Oral Therapeutics and Advanced Interventions

- **Oral [[tetracyclines]]** (doxycycline 50–100 mg daily or minocycline 50–100 mg daily): Anti-inflammatory properties (inhibit MMP-9, IL-1, TNF-α); improve Meibomian gland function. Used for moderate-to-severe MGD. Contraindicated in pregnancy and children <12 years.
- **Oral [[pilocarpine]]** (Salagen, 5 mg TID) or **[[cevimeline]]** (Evoxac, 30 mg TID): Muscarinic agonists that stimulate lacrimal and salivary gland secretion. Used in [[Sjögren syndrome]]-associated aqueous-deficient DED.
- **[[Autologous serum tears]]** (20–50% concentration): Prepared from the patient's own blood; contain growth factors (EGF, TGF-β, fibronectin, vitamin A) that promote epithelial healing. Used for severe DED refractory to conventional therapy, [[persistent epithelial defects]], and [[neurotrophic keratopathy]].
- **[[Platelet-rich plasma]] (PRP) tears**: Similar to autologous serum but with higher concentrations of growth factors.
- **[[Amniotic membrane]] transplantation**: Cryopreserved or dehydrated amniotic membrane (e.g., [[Prokera]], [[AmbioDisk]]) placed on the ocular surface to promote healing, reduce inflammation, and restore the ocular surface in severe DED with corneal compromise.

### Step 4: Surgical and Advanced Interventions

- **[[Tarsorrhaphy]]**: Partial or complete surgical closure of the eyelid fissure to reduce the exposed ocular surface area and evaporation. Reserved for severe, refractory cases (e.g., [[neurotrophic keratopathy]], severe [[exposure keratopathy]]).
- **[[Salivary gland transplantation]]** (minor salivary gland autotransplantation): Transfer of labial minor salivary glands to the conjunctival fornix to provide a substitute aqueous source. Performed in end-stage aqueous-deficient DED.
- **[[Boston keratoprosthesis]] (KPro)**: In cases of severe ocular surface disease with corneal opacification refractory to all other treatments.

### Staged Management Summary (TFOS DEWS II)

| Severity Stage | Interventions |
|---|---|
| **Stage 1 (Mild)** | Education, environmental modification, lid hygiene, artificial tears, omega-3 (optional), eyelid therapy |
| **Stage 2 (Moderate)** | Preservative-free tears, anti-inflammatory Rx (cyclosporine, lifitegrast, short-term steroids), punctal plugs, in-office MGD treatments, moisture chamber glasses |
| **Stage 3 (Severe)** | Autologous serum tears, oral secretagogues, oral tetracyclines, therapeutic contact lenses ([[scleral lenses]], [[bandage contact lenses]]) |
| **Stage 4 (Refractory)** | Tarsorrhaphy, amniotic membrane, salivary gland transplantation |

## Special Considerations

### Tear Film and Contact Lens Wear

[[Contact lens]] wear significantly impacts tear film dynamics. The lens divides the tear film into a **pre-lens tear film** (anterior to the lens) and a **post-lens tear film** (between the lens and cornea). Contact lens wear is associated with:

- Increased tear film evaporation
- Reduced lipid layer thickness
- Mucin ball formation
- Altered tear protein composition (increased denatured protein deposition)
- Reduced corneal sensitivity (especially with long-term wear)
- [[Contact lens-related dry eye]] (CLDE), affecting up to 50% of contact lens wearers

Management strategies include switching to daily disposable lenses, using lenses with lower modulus and higher water content or silicone hydrogel materials, rewetting drops compatible with contact lenses, and addressing underlying MGD.

### Tear Film and Refractive Surgery

[[LASIK]], [[PRK]], and other corneal refractive procedures sever corneal nerves, leading to transient (and occasionally prolonged) reduction in corneal sensitivity, decreased reflex tearing, and tear film instability. Post-LASIK dry eye is the most common complication, occurring in up to 95% of patients in the early postoperative period, with most cases resolving within 6–12 months. Pre-operative tear film assessment is essential for patient selection and counseling.

### Tear Film in Aging

Tear film function declines with age due to:
- Lacrimal gland acinar atrophy and fibrosis
- Meibomian gland dropout (age-related MGD)
- Decreased goblet cell density
- Reduced corneal nerve density
- Hormonal changes (particularly [[androgen]] deficiency, which affects Meibomian gland function)

### Tear Film and Systemic Disease

Numerous systemic conditions affect the tear film:
- **[[Sjögren syndrome]]**: Autoimmune lymphocytic infiltration of lacrimal and salivary glands
- **[[Rheumatoid arthritis]]**, **[[systemic lupus erythematosus]]**: Associated with secondary Sjögren syndrome
- **[[Diabetes mellitus]]**: Corneal neuropathy, reduced tear secretion, altered tear composition
- **[[Thyroid eye disease]]**: Increased lid aperture, proptosis, lagophthalmos leading to evaporative dry eye
- **[[Graft-versus-host disease]]**: Severe ocular surface inflammation and lacrimal gland destruction
- **[[Rosacea]]**: Strongly associated with MGD and posterior blepharitis

## Emerging Research and Future Directions

- **Tear film biomarkers**: Proteomic, lipidomic, and metabolomic profiling of tears for early diagnosis and personalized therapy.
- **[[Neurostimulation]]**: The **[[TrueTear]]** device (now discontinued) and **[[iTear100]]** (Olympic Ophthalmics) use electrical neurostimulation of the external nasal nerve to stimulate reflex tear production.
- **Gene therapy**: Targeting lacrimal gland aquaporin expression ([[AQP5]]) to restore aqueous secretion.
- **Stem cell therapy**: Conjunctival goblet cell regeneration from [[induced pluripotent stem cells]] (iPSCs).
- **Microbiome research**: Characterization of the ocular surface microbiome and its role in tear film homeostasis and disease.
- **Artificial intelligence**: AI-driven analysis of meibography, tear film interferometry, and ocular surface staining for automated DED diagnosis and severity grading.
