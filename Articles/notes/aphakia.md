

# Aphakia

---

**Aphakia** (from the Greek *a-*, "without," and *phakos*, "lens") is the clinical condition defined by the **complete absence of the [[crystalline lens]]** from the [[eye]]. This absence may result from surgical removal (most commonly during [[cataract extraction]]), traumatic dislocation or expulsion, or, rarely, congenital failure of lens development. Aphakia fundamentally alters the [[optical system]] of the eye, producing a state of extreme [[hyperopia]] (typically +10.00 to +14.00 [[diopters]] in a previously emmetropic eye), loss of [[accommodation]], and significant optical aberrations that must be corrected to restore functional vision. Although the prevalence of aphakia has declined dramatically since the widespread adoption of [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) implantation during [[cataract surgery]], it remains a clinically important entity encountered in pediatric ophthalmology, in cases of surgical complications, in trauma, and in resource-limited settings where IOL implantation may not be available.

---

## Epidemiology

The epidemiology of aphakia has shifted considerably over the past half-century. Prior to the 1970s, [[intracapsular cataract extraction]] (ICCE) without IOL implantation was the standard of care, and aphakia was an exceedingly common post-surgical state. With the advent of [[extracapsular cataract extraction]] (ECCE) and subsequently [[phacoemulsification]] with posterior chamber IOL implantation, the incidence of surgical aphakia in developed nations has fallen to less than 5% of cataract procedures.

However, aphakia remains prevalent in several clinical contexts:

| **Population** | **Estimated Prevalence / Context** |
|---|---|
| Developing nations | Higher rates due to limited access to IOLs; ICCE still performed in some regions |
| Pediatric patients | Congenital [[cataracts]] removed in infancy; IOL implantation may be deferred in children under 1–2 years |
| Traumatic cases | Lens expulsion or subluxation following blunt or penetrating [[ocular trauma]] |
| Complicated surgeries | Posterior capsule rupture, [[vitreous loss]], or inability to support an IOL |
| Congenital aphakia | Extremely rare; associated with primary failure of [[lens vesicle]] development |

The global burden of aphakia is disproportionately borne by populations in sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and other low-resource settings, where it remains a significant cause of preventable visual impairment and [[blindness]].

## Etiology and Classification

Aphakia is classified according to its underlying cause:

### 1. Surgical Aphakia

The most common form. Occurs following removal of the [[crystalline lens]] without placement of an [[intraocular lens]]. Historically associated with ICCE, it now occurs in specific circumstances:

- **Complicated cataract surgery**: Posterior capsule rupture with [[vitreous loss]], inadequate capsular support, or [[zonular dialysis]] precluding safe IOL placement.
- **Pediatric cataract surgery**: In neonates and very young infants (particularly those under 6–12 months), primary IOL implantation may be deferred due to rapid [[axial length]] growth, unpredictable [[refractive]] outcomes, and risk of secondary membrane formation. The Infant Aphakia Treatment Study (IATS) provided landmark evidence on this topic.
- **Secondary IOL explantation**: Removal of a previously implanted IOL due to dislocation, [[uveitis-glaucoma-hyphema (UGH) syndrome]], chronic [[inflammation]], or IOL opacification.

### 2. Traumatic Aphakia

Results from blunt or penetrating [[ocular trauma]] causing:

- **Lens expulsion**: Complete extrusion of the lens through a ruptured [[globe]].
- **Traumatic lens subluxation or dislocation**: Complete [[ectopia lentis]] into the [[vitreous cavity]] or [[anterior chamber]], functionally rendering the visual axis aphakic.
- **Traumatic absorption**: Rare; rupture of the [[lens capsule]] with subsequent resorption of lens material, particularly in young patients.

### 3. Congenital Aphakia

An exceedingly rare developmental anomaly classified into two subtypes:

- **Primary aphakia**: Complete failure of the [[lens placode]] to form during embryogenesis. The eye typically shows severe [[microphthalmia]] and other anterior segment dysgenesis. Associated with mutations in the **FOXE3** gene on chromosome 1p33.
- **Secondary congenital aphakia**: The lens initially forms but is subsequently resorbed *in utero*, often due to intrauterine inflammation or spontaneous capsular rupture.

## Pathophysiology

The [[crystalline lens]] is a biconvex, avascular, transparent structure suspended by [[zonular fibers]] (zonules of Zinn) attached to the [[ciliary body]]. It contributes approximately **+18 to +20 diopters** of the total refractive power of the eye (the total being approximately +60 diopters). The lens is also responsible for [[accommodation]]—the dynamic change in focal power that allows near focus—through contraction of the [[ciliary muscle]] and resultant changes in lens curvature (the [[Helmholtz theory of accommodation]]).

### Optical Consequences of Lens Absence

When the crystalline lens is absent, the following optical and physiological derangements occur:

1. **High hyperopia**: Loss of +18 to +20 D of converging power results in a markedly hyperopic eye. In a previously [[emmetropic]] eye, the residual refractive error is typically **+10.00 to +14.00 D** (less than the full lens power because the removal of the lens also eliminates the posterior lens surface, which had a diverging effect at the lens-vitreous interface, and because the [[aqueous humor]] now fills the space previously occupied by the lens, altering the overall refractive index relationships).

2. **Loss of accommodation**: The aphakic eye has **zero accommodative amplitude**. Patients require separate optical corrections for distance and near, or multifocal/bifocal corrections.

3. **Absence of the UV-filtering function**: The crystalline lens absorbs a significant proportion of [[ultraviolet (UV) radiation]], particularly UV-B (280–315 nm) and UV-A (315–400 nm). In aphakia, unfiltered UV light reaches the [[retina]], increasing the risk of photic [[maculopathy]] and potentially accelerating [[age-related macular degeneration]] (AMD). Aphakic patients may perceive a bluish-violet tint to their vision ([[cyanopsia]]) due to increased retinal exposure to short-wavelength light.

4. **Altered anterior segment anatomy**: Removal of the lens deepens the [[anterior chamber]], alters [[aqueous humor]] dynamics, and removes the physical barrier between the anterior and posterior segments. This can predispose to:
   - **Vitreous prolapse** into the anterior chamber
   - **[[Pupillary block]]** from vitreous or inflammatory membranes
   - **[[Aphakic glaucoma]]**: Particularly significant in pediatric aphakia, where the incidence of secondary [[open-angle glaucoma]] may reach 10–25% or higher over long-term follow-up
   - **[[Cystoid macular edema]] (CME)**: Irvine-Gass syndrome, more common in aphakic than pseudophakic eyes

5. **Optical aberrations with spectacle correction**: Aphakic spectacles introduce significant [[spherical aberration]], [[chromatic aberration]], [[barrel distortion]], a **ring scotoma** (the "jack-in-the-box" phenomenon), and **image magnification of approximately 25–33%**. This magnification renders spectacle correction impractical for unilateral aphakia due to intolerable [[aniseikonia]].

### Vitreous and Retinal Considerations

The absence of the crystalline lens removes the posterior barrier between the anterior and posterior segments. This facilitates:

- **Anterior vitreous displacement**: Vitreous strands may herniate through the [[pupil]], potentially causing vitreo-retinal traction.
- **Increased risk of [[retinal detachment]]**: Aphakic eyes have a well-documented elevated risk of [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]], estimated at 1–3% over the lifetime, compared to approximately 0.01% in the general phakic population. The mechanism involves vitreous destabilization, posterior vitreous detachment, and peripheral retinal traction, particularly at areas of [[lattice degeneration]] or pre-existing retinal thinning.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Symptoms

Patients with aphakia present with a constellation of visual complaints:

| **Symptom** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|
| Severe blurred vision (distance and near) | Loss of +18–20 D of refractive power; high hyperopia |
| Inability to focus at near | Complete loss of [[accommodation]] |
| [[Cyanopsia]] (blue-tinted vision) | Increased short-wavelength light reaching the retina due to absent UV-filtering lens |
| [[Photophobia]] | Increased retinal light exposure; absent lens absorption |
| Monocular [[diplopia]] or image distortion | Optical aberrations, especially with spectacle correction |
| "Jack-in-the-box" phenomenon | Ring scotoma from high-plus spectacle lenses; objects appear to "jump" into view |
| Reduced depth perception | Magnification differences between eyes ([[aniseikonia]]) in unilateral aphakia |
| Glare and halos | Increased light scatter in the absence of the lens |

### Signs on Clinical Examination

The aphakic eye demonstrates several characteristic clinical signs:

- **Deep anterior chamber**: The [[anterior chamber depth]] is markedly increased due to the absence of the lens.
- **[[Iridodonesis]]**: A tremulous, undulating movement of the [[iris]] with eye movements, resulting from loss of posterior support by the lens. This is a hallmark sign of aphakia.
- **Absent Purkinje images III and IV**: On [[Purkinje image]] testing, the reflections from the anterior and posterior lens surfaces (images III and IV) are absent, confirming the absence of the crystalline lens.
- **Black pupillary reflex**: On [[retinoscopy]] or direct observation, the pupil appears jet black without the normal subtle reflections from the lens surfaces.
- **Vitreous in the anterior chamber**: Strands or formed vitreous may be visible in the pupillary area or anterior chamber, particularly after ICCE or complicated surgery.
- **Surgical signs**: Corneal or limbal scars from prior cataract incisions; [[peripheral iridectomy]] (commonly performed during ICCE to prevent pupillary block); absence of an IOL on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]].
- **High hyperopic refraction**: Retinoscopy reveals a markedly hyperopic reflex with a "with" motion requiring high plus lenses to neutralize.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis

The diagnosis of aphakia is primarily clinical, established through:

1. **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: Direct visualization of the anterior segment confirms the absence of the crystalline lens and any IOL. The anterior chamber is deep, the iris may show iridodonesis, and vitreous may be visible behind the pupil.

2. **[[Retinoscopy]] and [[refraction]]**: Reveals high hyperopia, typically in the range of +10.00 to +14.00 D for a previously emmetropic eye. The retinoscopic reflex in aphakia is characteristically bright and fast-moving.

3. **[[Purkinje image]] analysis**: Absence of the third and fourth Purkinje images confirms the absence of the lens.

4. **History**: Surgical history (prior cataract extraction, IOL explantation), trauma history, or known congenital anomaly.

### Ancillary Diagnostic Testing

| **Test** | **Purpose in Aphakia** |
|---|---|
| [[A-scan ultrasonography]] / [[biometry]] | Measurement of [[axial length]] for IOL power calculation if secondary IOL implantation is planned; confirms absence of lens echo |
| [[B-scan ultrasonography]] | Evaluates posterior segment for [[retinal detachment]], vitreous hemorrhage, or dislocated lens/IOL in the vitreous cavity |
| [[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT) | Assessment of [[macula]] for [[cystoid macular edema]], [[epiretinal membrane]], or other macular pathology |
| [[Anterior segment OCT]] (AS-OCT) | Detailed imaging of anterior chamber depth, angle anatomy, and vitreous prolapse |
| [[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM) | Evaluation of [[ciliary sulcus]] dimensions, capsular remnants, and zonular integrity for secondary IOL planning |
| [[Gonioscopy]] | Assessment of [[anterior chamber angle]] for peripheral anterior synechiae, vitreous in the angle, or angle-closure risk |
| [[Specular microscopy]] | [[Corneal endothelial cell]] count and morphology, particularly important before secondary IOL surgery |
| [[IOL Master]] / optical biometry | Precise axial length and keratometry measurements for IOL power calculation using appropriate aphakic formulas (e.g., [[SRK/T]], [[Holladay]], [[Haigis]], [[Barrett Universal II]]) |

### Differential Diagnosis

Conditions that may mimic or be confused with aphakia include:

- **[[Lens subluxation]] / [[ectopia lentis]]**: The lens is present but displaced; may be partially visible on dilated examination. Causes include [[Marfan syndrome]], [[homocystinuria]], [[Weill-Marchesani syndrome]], and trauma.
- **[[Microspherophakia]]**: A small, spherical lens that may be difficult to visualize.
- **[[Posterior lenticonus]] / [[lentiglobus]]**: Lens anomalies that alter refraction but the lens is present.
- **[[Pseudophakia]]**: An IOL is present; must be distinguished from aphakia on slit-lamp examination, particularly if the IOL is subluxated or posteriorly dislocated.

## Management and Treatment

The management of aphakia is centered on **optical rehabilitation** to correct the high hyperopic refractive error and restore functional vision, along with **monitoring and treatment of associated complications**.

### Optical Correction of Aphakia

There are three primary modalities for optical correction of aphakia:

#### 1. Aphakic Spectacles

Historically the most common correction, aphakic spectacles employ **high-plus lenses** (typically +10.00 to +14.00 D) with additional near add power.

**Advantages:**
- Non-invasive
- Readily available
- Appropriate for bilateral aphakia where aniseikonia is not a concern

**Disadvantages:**

| **Optical Problem** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| Image magnification | ~25–33% magnification; intolerable aniseikonia in unilateral aphakia |
| Ring scotoma | Prismatic effect at lens periphery creates an annular blind zone; "jack-in-the-box" phenomenon |
| [[Barrel distortion]] | Pincushion distortion of straight lines |
| Restricted visual field | Effective field of view is significantly narrowed |
| [[Spherical aberration]] | Degraded image quality, especially peripherally |
| [[Chromatic aberration]] | Color fringing at object edges |
| Heavy weight | Thick, heavy lenses; cosmetically unacceptable to many patients |
| Altered spatial perception | Objects appear closer than they are; impaired depth judgment |

Modern **aspheric lenticular designs** (e.g., the Welsh 4-drop aspheric lens) can reduce some of these aberrations but cannot eliminate them entirely. Aphakic spectacles remain most appropriate for **bilateral aphakia** where the magnification is symmetric.

#### 2. Aphakic Contact Lenses

[[Contact lenses]] provide superior optical correction for aphakia compared to spectacles:

**Advantages:**
- Image magnification reduced to approximately **5–8%** (vs. 25–33% with spectacles)
- Wider field of view
- Reduced aberrations (spherical, chromatic, distortion)
- Cosmetically superior
- Can be used for unilateral aphakia (aniseikonia is within tolerable limits for many patients)

**Types of aphakic contact lenses:**

| **Lens Type** | **Indications / Notes** |
|---|---|
| [[Silicone elastomer lenses]] | Preferred for pediatric aphakia (infants); high Dk/t; extended wear possible; e.g., Silsoft (Bausch + Lomb) |
| [[Rigid gas permeable]] (RGP) lenses | Excellent optics; suitable for older children and adults; daily wear |
| [[Soft hydrogel / silicone hydrogel]] lenses | Available in high-plus powers; comfortable; daily or extended wear |
| [[Scleral lenses]] | Useful in irregular corneas or when other lenses are not tolerated |

**Challenges:**
- Handling difficulties, especially in elderly or pediatric patients
- Risk of [[microbial keratitis]], [[corneal neovascularization]], and [[giant papillary conjunctivitis]]
- Compliance issues
- Cost and need for frequent replacement and follow-up
- In infants, parents must be trained in lens insertion, removal, and care

Contact lenses are the **standard of care for unilateral aphakia in infants** when IOL implantation is deferred, as established by the Infant Aphakia Treatment Study (IATS).

#### 3. Intraocular Lens Implantation (Secondary IOL)

The definitive treatment for aphakia in most cases is **secondary [[intraocular lens]] implantation**, which provides the most physiological optical correction:

- Image magnification: approximately **1–2%** (negligible)
- Full visual field restoration
- No handling or compliance issues
- Permanent correction

**Types of secondary IOL implantation:**

| **Technique** | **Description** | **Requirements** |
|---|---|---|
| **Posterior chamber IOL in the [[ciliary sulcus]]** | IOL placed in the sulcus, anterior to the remaining posterior capsule | Adequate capsular remnant for support; sulcus-fixated IOL (larger optic/haptic preferred) |
| **Posterior chamber IOL with [[scleral fixation]]** | IOL haptics sutured to the [[sclera]] or tucked into scleral tunnels (Yamane technique / flanged IOL fixation) | No capsular support; requires surgical expertise |
| **[[Iris-fixated IOL]]** (e.g., Artisan/Verisyse) | IOL clipped to the mid-peripheral iris | Adequate iris tissue; anterior or retropupillary fixation |
| **[[Anterior chamber IOL]] (ACIOL)** | IOL placed in the anterior chamber angle | Open angles; adequate endothelial cell count; angle-supported or iris-claw designs |
| **Intrascleral haptic fixation (Yamane technique)** | Flanged haptics of a 3-piece IOL externalized through scleral tunnels using 30-gauge needles | No capsular support; increasingly popular due to sutureless design |
| **Glued IOL (Agarwal technique)** | IOL haptics tucked into scleral flaps and secured with fibrin glue | No capsular support; alternative to sutured scleral fixation |

**IOL Power Calculation in Aphakia:**

Accurate [[biometry]] is essential. The IOL power for secondary implantation depends on:
- [[Axial length]] (measured by A-scan ultrasound or optical biometry)
- [[Keratometry]] readings
- Anticipated IOL position (anterior chamber depth for ACIOLs vs. sulcus vs. capsular bag)
- Target refraction

For sulcus-placed IOLs, a **reduction of 0.5 to 1.0 D** from the calculated in-the-bag power is typically applied to account for the more anterior effective lens position. Modern formulas such as the [[Barrett Universal II]], [[Holladay 2]], and [[Haigis]] formula are commonly employed.

### Management of Pediatric Aphakia

Pediatric aphakia presents unique challenges:

- **Amblyopia risk**: Unilateral aphakia in infants carries a very high risk of [[deprivation amblyopia]] if not corrected promptly. Optical correction must be initiated **within days to weeks** of surgery.
- **Contact lens correction**: The IATS demonstrated that contact lens correction of aphakia in infants under 7 months of age produced visual outcomes comparable to primary IOL implantation at age 1 year, with fewer adverse events and reoperations in the contact lens group.
- **Aggressive amblyopia therapy**: [[Occlusion therapy]] (patching) of the fellow eye is essential in unilateral cases.
- **Frequent refraction updates**: The rapidly growing infant eye undergoes significant refractive changes; prescriptions must be updated frequently (every 1–3 months in the first year).
- **Secondary IOL implantation**: Typically considered when the child is older (often age 1–2 years or later), when the eye has undergone most of its axial growth and IOL power calculation is more predictable.
- **Monitoring for [[aphakic glaucoma]]**: Lifelong surveillance is required, as secondary glaucoma may develop years or even decades after surgery.

### Pharmacological Considerations

While aphakia itself is not treated pharmacologically, several medications are relevant in its management:

| **Medication** | **Indication in Aphakia** |
|---|---|
| Topical [[corticosteroids]] (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate 1%]]) | Post-surgical inflammation control |
| Topical [[NSAIDs]] (e.g., [[ketorolac]], [[nepafenac]]) | Prophylaxis and treatment of [[cystoid macular edema]] |
| Topical [[cycloplegics]] (e.g., [[atropine]], [[cyclopentolate]]) | Post-operative comfort; stabilization of the blood-aqueous barrier |
| [[Antiglaucoma medications]] | Management of [[aphakic glaucoma]] (e.g., [[timolol]], [[dorzolamide]], [[brimonidine]], [[latanoprost]]) |
| Topical [[antibiotics]] | Post-operative infection prophylaxis |
| UV-blocking ophthalmic coatings/filters | Protection against UV-induced retinal damage in the absence of the natural lens filter |

### Monitoring and Long-Term Follow-Up

Aphakic patients require lifelong ophthalmic surveillance for:

1. **[[Glaucoma]]**: Particularly in pediatric aphakia; [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) monitoring at every visit; [[gonioscopy]] as indicated; [[visual field testing]] and [[optic nerve]] assessment.
2. **[[Retinal detachment]]**: Patient education regarding symptoms (flashes, floaters, curtain/shadow in vision); periodic [[dilated fundus examination]]; [[peripheral retinal]] evaluation.
3. **[[Cystoid macular edema]]**: [[OCT]] monitoring; prompt treatment with topical NSAIDs and/or corticosteroids.
4. **[[Corneal decompensation]]**: Especially in patients with ACIOLs or prolonged contact lens wear; [[specular microscopy]] to monitor [[endothelial cell density]].
5. **[[Amblyopia]]**: In pediatric patients; ongoing visual acuity assessment and amblyopia therapy.
6. **Refractive changes**: Particularly in children; frequent refraction and prescription updates.

## Complications of Aphakia

| **Complication** | **Mechanism / Notes** |
|---|---|
| [[Aphakic glaucoma]] | Open-angle or angle-closure; incidence 10–25%+ in pediatric aphakia; may present years after surgery |
| [[Retinal detachment]] | 1–3% lifetime risk; vitreous destabilization and traction |
| [[Cystoid macular edema]] (Irvine-Gass syndrome) | Disruption of blood-aqueous and blood-retinal barriers |
| [[Vitreous prolapse]] / [[vitreous incarceration]] | Herniation of vitreous into the anterior chamber or wound |
| [[Pupillary block]] | Vitreous or inflammatory membrane occluding the pupil; may cause secondary angle-closure |
| [[Corneal edema]] / [[bullous keratopathy]] | Endothelial cell loss from surgical trauma, vitreous touch, or ACIOL contact |
| [[Posterior capsule opacification]] (if capsule remnant present) | [[Elschnig pearls]] or fibrotic membrane; treated with [[Nd:YAG laser capsulotomy]] |
| [[Endophthalmitis]] | Rare but devastating post-surgical infection |
| [[Amblyopia]] | Critical concern in pediatric unilateral aphakia |
| UV-related retinal damage | Absent UV filtration by the lens; risk of photic maculopathy |

## Prognosis

The visual prognosis in aphakia depends on several factors:

- **Bilateral vs. unilateral**: Bilateral aphakia with appropriate optical correction generally has a good visual prognosis. Unilateral aphakia, particularly in children, carries a guarded prognosis due to amblyopia risk.
- **Age at onset**: Congenital and infantile aphakia have the highest risk of amblyopia and the most guarded visual prognosis without aggressive early intervention.
- **Adequacy of optical correction**: Prompt, appropriate, and consistent optical correction is the single most important determinant of visual outcome.
- **Comorbidities**: Coexisting [[corneal scarring]], [[glaucoma]], [[retinal detachment]], or [[optic nerve hypoplasia]] significantly worsen prognosis.
- **Secondary IOL implantation**: When feasible, secondary IOL implantation provides the best long-term optical rehabilitation and quality of life.

With modern surgical techniques and optical correction strategies, the majority of aphakic patients can achieve functional and often excellent vision.

## Historical Perspective

The history of aphakia is inextricably linked to the history of [[cataract surgery]]. Ancient techniques of "couching" (displacing the cataractous lens into the vitreous cavity with a needle) produced a form of aphakia that was corrected, if at all, with crude magnifying lenses. The development of cataract extraction techniques by [[Jacques Daviel]] in 1747 (the first planned ECCE) and later the refinement of ICCE in the 19th and early 20th centuries made surgical aphakia a common clinical entity.

The revolutionary implantation of the first [[intraocular lens]] by Sir [[Harold Ridley]] on November 29, 1949, at [[St Thomas' Hospital]] in London, marked the beginning of the end of aphakia as a mass clinical problem. Ridley's observation that [[polymethyl methacrylate]] (PMMA) fragments from shattered aircraft canopies were well-tolerated in the eyes of World War II pilots led him to develop the first posterior chamber IOL. Despite initial skepticism and complications, IOL technology advanced rapidly, and by the 1980s and 1990s, IOL implantation became the standard of care during cataract surgery worldwide.

Today, aphakia is increasingly a condition of historical interest in developed nations, though it remains clinically relevant in specific populations and scenarios as described above.

---

## Summary

Aphakia is the absence of the [[crystalline lens]] from the eye, resulting in high [[hyperopia]], loss of [[accommodation]], and significant optical aberrations. It may be surgical, traumatic, or congenital in origin. Diagnosis is clinical, confirmed by [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]], [[retinoscopy]], and ancillary imaging. Management centers on optical rehabilitation through [[aphakic spectacles]], [[contact lenses]], or secondary [[intraocular lens]] implantation, with the latter providing the most physiological correction. Long-term monitoring for [[glaucoma]], [[retinal detachment]], and [[cystoid macular edema]] is essential. In pediatric aphakia, prompt optical correction and aggressive [[amblyopia]] therapy are critical to optimizing visual outcomes.
