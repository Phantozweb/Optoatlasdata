

# Pars Plana Vitrectomy

---

**Pars plana vitrectomy (PPV)** is a highly specialized microsurgical procedure in which the [[vitreous humor]]—the transparent, gel-like substance that fills the posterior segment of the [[eye]]—is removed through small-gauge incisions placed through the [[pars plana]] of the [[ciliary body]]. First introduced by Robert Machemer in 1971, PPV has undergone transformative evolution over the past five decades and now represents one of the most commonly performed posterior segment surgeries worldwide. It serves as the definitive surgical intervention for a broad spectrum of vitreoretinal pathologies, including [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]], [[proliferative diabetic retinopathy]], [[epiretinal membrane]], [[macular hole]], [[vitreous hemorrhage]], [[endophthalmitis]], and retained lens fragments following complicated [[cataract surgery]]. For the optometrist, a thorough understanding of PPV—its indications, techniques, complications, and postoperative management—is essential for appropriate referral, co-management, and long-term patient care.

---

## Historical Background and Evolution

The concept of vitreous surgery was pioneered by **Robert Machemer**, who in 1971 performed the first closed-system pars plana vitrectomy using a 17-gauge instrument known as the VISC (Vitreous Infusion Suction Cutter). This single-port device combined infusion, cutting, and aspiration into one instrument. The procedure was revolutionary because it allowed surgeons to operate within the closed globe, maintaining [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) and minimizing the risk of [[retinal detachment]] and [[choroidal hemorrhage]].

Throughout the 1970s and 1980s, the technique evolved into a **three-port system** (infusion, illumination, and working port), which became the standard configuration. Gauge sizes progressively decreased:

| Era | Gauge Size | Approximate Sclerotomy Diameter | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1970s | 17-gauge | ~1.5 mm | Original Machemer VISC |
| 1970s–1980s | 20-gauge | ~0.9 mm | Standard three-port system; required suturing |
| Early 2000s | 23-gauge | ~0.6 mm | Transconjunctival, sutureless approach introduced by Eckardt (2005) |
| Mid 2000s | 25-gauge | ~0.5 mm | Introduced by Fujii et al. (2002); sutureless, reduced inflammation |
| 2010s | 27-gauge | ~0.4 mm | Ultra-small incision; enhanced patient comfort |

The transition to **small-gauge, sutureless vitrectomy** (23-, 25-, and 27-gauge) has been one of the most significant advances in vitreoretinal surgery. These systems employ trocar-cannula technology that creates self-sealing, oblique (angled or beveled) sclerotomies, reducing operative time, postoperative inflammation, patient discomfort, and recovery periods. Modern high-speed vitrectomy cutters can operate at cut rates exceeding **10,000 cuts per minute (cpm)**, which improves fluidic stability, reduces vitreous traction on the [[retina]], and allows safer dissection near delicate retinal tissue.

## Surgical Anatomy of the Pars Plana

A comprehensive understanding of the [[pars plana]] anatomy is critical for safe port placement and avoidance of iatrogenic damage.

### The Pars Plana

The pars plana is the flat, relatively avascular posterior portion of the [[ciliary body]], situated between the [[pars plicata]] (anteriorly) and the [[ora serrata]] (posteriorly). It extends approximately **4.0 mm in width temporally** and **3.0 mm nasally** in the adult eye. The pars plana is the preferred site for surgical entry into the [[vitreous cavity]] because:

- It is relatively **avascular**, minimizing hemorrhagic risk.
- It is located **posterior to the [[lens]]** and **anterior to the [[retina]]**, reducing the risk of damage to either structure.
- The [[vitreous base]]—the zone of strongest vitreoretinal adhesion—straddles the ora serrata but the pars plana provides a safe corridor of entry.

### Standard Port Placement

Sclerotomies are typically placed:

- **3.5–4.0 mm posterior to the [[limbus]]** in **phakic** eyes (to avoid the [[crystalline lens]])
- **3.0–3.5 mm posterior to the limbus** in **pseudophakic** or **aphakic** eyes

Three ports are established in the standard configuration:

1. **Infusion cannula** – placed inferotemporal; provides continuous balanced salt solution ([[BSS]]) to maintain globe volume and IOP during surgery.
2. **Endoillumination port** – placed superonasal or superotemporal; delivers intraocular light via fiber-optic probes.
3. **Working/instrument port** – placed in the opposite superior quadrant; used for the vitrectomy cutter, forceps, scissors, laser probes, and other instruments.

In modern practice, **wide-angle viewing systems** (e.g., non-contact panoramic systems such as the Resight or BIOM, or contact lens-based systems) provide a broad view of the [[fundus]], facilitating peripheral vitreous removal and retinal examination during surgery.

## Indications

PPV is indicated for a wide range of vitreoretinal conditions. The following table summarizes the major indications:

| Category | Specific Indications |
|---|---|
| **Retinal Detachment** | [[Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]] (especially complex, recurrent, or with [[proliferative vitreoretinopathy]]), [[tractional retinal detachment]] (e.g., from [[proliferative diabetic retinopathy]]), combined tractional-rhegmatogenous detachment |
| **Vitreous Opacities** | Non-clearing [[vitreous hemorrhage]] (diabetic, traumatic, or from retinal vein occlusion), dense [[asteroid hyalosis]] causing visual impairment, symptomatic [[vitreous floaters]] (floaterectomy) |
| **Macular Pathology** | [[Epiretinal membrane]] (ERM) / [[macular pucker]], full-thickness [[macular hole]], [[vitreomacular traction]] (VMT) syndrome, [[macular edema]] unresponsive to pharmacotherapy |
| **Diabetic Eye Disease** | [[Proliferative diabetic retinopathy]] with non-clearing vitreous hemorrhage, tractional retinal detachment threatening or involving the [[macula]], fibrovascular proliferation |
| **Infectious / Inflammatory** | [[Endophthalmitis]] (exogenous or endogenous) – for vitreous biopsy and intravitreal antibiotic delivery, diagnostic vitrectomy for uveitis of unknown etiology, [[cytomegalovirus retinitis]] complications |
| **Trauma** | [[Intraocular foreign body]] (IOFB) removal, traumatic retinal detachment, traumatic vitreous hemorrhage, [[sympathetic ophthalmia]] prevention |
| **Lens-Related** | Retained/dislocated lens fragments after complicated [[phacoemulsification]], dislocated [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) |
| **Miscellaneous** | [[Submacular hemorrhage]] (with or without tissue plasminogen activator [tPA] and pneumatic displacement), [[retinopathy of prematurity]] (stage 4B/5), optic pit-related [[maculopathy]], complications of [[age-related macular degeneration]] (e.g., submacular surgery) |

## Pathophysiology of Conditions Requiring PPV

The pathophysiological basis for PPV relates to the role of the [[vitreous humor]] in disease processes affecting the posterior segment.

### Vitreous as a Pathological Scaffold

The vitreous is composed of approximately **99% water**, with a structural framework of [[type II collagen]] fibrils and [[hyaluronic acid]] (hyaluronan). With aging, the vitreous undergoes **liquefaction (synchysis)** and **collapse (syneresis)**, leading to [[posterior vitreous detachment]] (PVD). While PVD is usually benign, anomalous or incomplete PVD can result in:

- **Vitreoretinal traction**: Persistent vitreous adhesion to the retinal surface can cause tractional forces leading to [[macular hole]] formation, [[vitreomacular traction syndrome]], or [[tractional retinal detachment]].
- **Retinal breaks**: During acute PVD, traction on areas of strong vitreoretinal adhesion (e.g., at the [[vitreous base]], along retinal vessels, or at areas of [[lattice degeneration]]) can produce retinal tears, which may progress to [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]].
- **Scaffold for neovascularization**: In [[proliferative diabetic retinopathy]] and other ischemic retinopathies, the posterior vitreous cortex serves as a scaffold upon which [[neovascularization]] proliferates. These fragile new vessels are prone to hemorrhage, producing [[vitreous hemorrhage]], and their associated fibrous tissue contracts, causing tractional retinal detachment.

### Vitreous as a Reservoir for Pathological Mediators

The vitreous cavity can accumulate:

- **Blood** (vitreous hemorrhage) from ruptured retinal or choroidal vessels.
- **Inflammatory cells and cytokines** in [[endophthalmitis]] or severe [[uveitis]].
- **Vascular endothelial growth factor ([[VEGF]])** and other pro-angiogenic cytokines, which perpetuate neovascularization and macular edema.
- **Foreign material** (e.g., intraocular foreign bodies, retained lens material).

By removing the vitreous, PPV eliminates the pathological scaffold, clears opacities, removes inflammatory and angiogenic mediators, relieves traction, and provides access to the retinal surface for membrane peeling, laser photocoagulation, and tamponade agent placement.

## Preoperative Evaluation and Diagnosis

### Clinical Assessment

A thorough preoperative evaluation is essential and includes:

- **Best-corrected visual acuity ([[BCVA]])** – establishes baseline and helps guide surgical decision-making and prognosis.
- **[[Intraocular pressure]] (IOP)** measurement – identifies pre-existing [[glaucoma]] or [[ocular hypertension]].
- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]** – evaluates the [[anterior segment]], including the [[cornea]] (clarity for surgical visualization), [[anterior chamber]] (cells, flare), [[iris]] (neovascularization / [[neovascular glaucoma]]), [[lens]] status (phakic, pseudophakic, aphakic; presence of [[cataract]]).
- **Dilated [[fundoscopy]]** – with [[binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy]] (BIO) and slit-lamp [[fundus]] examination using 78D, 90D, or similar lenses. Evaluates the [[vitreous]], [[retina]], [[optic nerve]], and [[macula]].
- **[[Gonioscopy]]** – if neovascularization of the angle is suspected.

### Diagnostic Imaging

| Modality | Role in PPV Planning |
|---|---|
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)** | Gold standard for evaluating macular pathology: [[epiretinal membrane]], [[macular hole]] (staging), [[vitreomacular traction]], [[macular edema]], subretinal fluid. Guides surgical decision-making and timing. |
| **[[OCT angiography]] (OCTA)** | Non-invasive assessment of retinal and choroidal vasculature; identifies areas of [[ischemia]], [[neovascularization]], and perfusion deficits. |
| **[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA)** | Identifies retinal [[ischemia]], [[neovascularization]], vascular leakage, and guides [[panretinal photocoagulation]] (PRP) planning in [[proliferative diabetic retinopathy]]. |
| **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** | Essential when media opacities (e.g., dense vitreous hemorrhage, [[cataract]]) preclude fundus visualization. Evaluates retinal detachment status, vitreous hemorrhage density, presence of intraocular foreign bodies, and posterior segment masses. |
| **[[A-scan biometry]] / [[IOL Master]]** | If combined PPV and cataract surgery (phaco-vitrectomy) is planned, accurate [[axial length]] measurement and IOL power calculation are required. |
| **[[Computed tomography]] (CT)** | Indicated in trauma cases to localize and characterize [[intraocular foreign bodies]] (especially metallic). |

### Systemic Evaluation

- **Anticoagulation status**: Patients on [[warfarin]], [[aspirin]], [[clopidogrel]], or direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) require careful risk-benefit analysis. In many cases, anticoagulants are continued for PPV, but this decision is individualized.
- **Glycemic control**: In diabetic patients, [[hemoglobin A1c]] (HbA1c) levels should be optimized preoperatively, as poor glycemic control is associated with increased surgical complications and poorer visual outcomes.
- **Blood pressure control**: Uncontrolled [[hypertension]] increases the risk of intraoperative and postoperative hemorrhage.

## Surgical Technique

### Anesthesia

PPV can be performed under:

- **Retrobulbar block** or **peribulbar block** – most common; provides akinesia and anesthesia.
- **Sub-Tenon's anesthesia** – increasingly popular; effective with a favorable safety profile.
- **Topical anesthesia with intracameral supplementation** – used in select cases, particularly with small-gauge systems.
- **General anesthesia** – reserved for pediatric patients, patients unable to cooperate, or prolonged/complex cases.

### Core Surgical Steps

1. **Conjunctival displacement and trocar-cannula insertion**: In sutureless surgery, the conjunctiva is displaced to create a valve-like wound architecture. Trocar-cannulas are inserted through the [[sclera]] at the pars plana in an oblique (angled) fashion to promote self-sealing.

2. **Infusion line confirmation**: The infusion cannula (inferotemporal port) is confirmed to be within the vitreous cavity and not subretinal or suprachoroidal before fluid infusion is initiated.

3. **Core vitrectomy**: The central vitreous gel is removed using the high-speed vitrectomy cutter. Modern cutters operate in a **duty cycle**-controlled manner, with port-biased or flow-biased cutting to optimize aspiration efficiency and minimize traction.

4. **Peripheral vitreous removal**: Using scleral indentation (performed by an assistant) and wide-angle viewing, the peripheral vitreous and vitreous base are shaved. This step is critical in retinal detachment surgery to relieve all vitreoretinal traction.

5. **Posterior hyaloid removal**: If a [[posterior vitreous detachment]] has not already occurred, the surgeon may induce PVD using active aspiration over the [[optic disc]] or with the assistance of [[triamcinolone acetonide]] (which stains residual vitreous cortex, making it visible).

6. **Membrane peeling**: For conditions such as [[epiretinal membrane]] or [[internal limiting membrane]] (ILM) peeling in [[macular hole]] surgery, vital dyes are used to enhance visualization:
   - **[[Indocyanine green]] (ICG)** – stains the [[internal limiting membrane]] (ILM)
   - **[[Brilliant blue G]] (BBG)** – stains the ILM with a favorable safety profile
   - **[[Trypan blue]]** – stains epiretinal membranes
   - **[[Triamcinolone acetonide]]** – stains residual vitreous cortex

7. **Endolaser photocoagulation**: [[Laser photocoagulation]] is applied as needed—[[panretinal photocoagulation]] for proliferative diabetic retinopathy, barrier laser around retinal breaks, or retinopexy for retinal detachment.

8. **Fluid-air exchange**: Intraocular fluid is exchanged for air to flatten the retina and prepare for tamponade agent injection.

9. **Tamponade agent placement**: Depending on the pathology, a tamponade agent may be injected:

| Tamponade Agent | Properties | Common Indications | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Air** | Short-acting gas | Simple retinal breaks, macular hole | Resorbs in 5–7 days |
| **[[Sulfur hexafluoride]] (SF₆)** | Non-expansile concentration: 20%; expands ~2× | Macular hole, uncomplicated retinal detachment | 10–14 days |
| **[[Perfluoropropane]] (C₃F₈)** | Non-expansile concentration: 14%; expands ~4× | Complex retinal detachment, giant retinal tears, PVR | 55–65 days |
| **[[Silicone oil]]** (1000 or 5000 cSt) | Long-acting, non-absorbable liquid tamponade | Complex/recurrent retinal detachment, PVR, inferior breaks, pediatric cases, patients unable to position | Indefinite; requires second surgery for removal |
| **Heavy silicone oil (Densiron/Oxane HD)** | Higher specific gravity than water | Inferior retinal detachments | Requires removal |

10. **Port removal and wound closure**: Trocar-cannulas are removed. In sutureless surgery, the oblique sclerotomies self-seal. If leakage is observed, absorbable sutures (e.g., 7-0 [[Vicryl]]) are placed.

### Combined Procedures

PPV is frequently combined with other procedures:

- **Phaco-vitrectomy**: Combined [[phacoemulsification]] cataract extraction with IOL implantation and PPV. Increasingly common, as cataract progression is expected after PPV (especially with gas tamponade), and combined surgery avoids a second procedure.
- **Scleral buckle with PPV**: For complex retinal detachments, particularly those with significant [[proliferative vitreoretinopathy]] (PVR) or anterior traction.
- **Endoscopy-assisted vitrectomy**: Uses an intraocular endoscope for visualization when corneal opacities or other anterior segment pathology limits the surgical view.

## Postoperative Management

### Immediate Postoperative Care

- **Topical medications**: Patients are typically prescribed:
  - **Topical [[corticosteroids]]** (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate]] 1%) – tapered over 4–6 weeks to control inflammation.
  - **Topical [[antibiotics]]** (e.g., [[moxifloxacin]] 0.5% or [[ofloxacin]] 0.3%) – for infection prophylaxis, typically for 1–2 weeks.
  - **Topical [[cycloplegics]]** (e.g., [[atropine]] 1% or [[cyclopentolate]] 1%) – for comfort and to prevent posterior [[synechiae]].
  - **Topical IOP-lowering agents** – if postoperative [[ocular hypertension]] develops.

- **Positioning**: If gas or silicone oil tamponade is used, specific **face-down (prone) positioning** may be required to ensure the tamponade agent apposes the area of pathology (e.g., macular hole, posterior retinal break). Positioning requirements vary:
  - **Macular hole**: Traditionally face-down for 1–2 weeks, though recent evidence supports shorter positioning durations or even no positioning for smaller holes.
  - **Retinal detachment with superior breaks**: Upright positioning may suffice.
  - **Retinal detachment with inferior breaks**: Prone or specific lateral positioning.

- **Activity restrictions**: Patients must **avoid air travel** and **nitrous oxide anesthesia** while intraocular gas is present, as altitude changes and nitrous oxide cause gas expansion, leading to dangerous IOP elevation and potential [[central retinal artery occlusion]].

### Follow-Up Schedule

| Timepoint | Key Assessments |
|---|---|
| **Day 1 postoperative** | IOP measurement, anterior segment evaluation, gas fill assessment, rule out endophthalmitis, retinal status (if visible) |
| **Week 1** | BCVA, IOP, anterior segment inflammation, gas fill level, retinal attachment status |
| **Weeks 2–4** | Progressive gas resorption, retinal status, macular OCT (when media clears), IOP |
| **Months 1–3** | BCVA, OCT assessment of macular anatomy, IOP, cataract progression (in phakic eyes), retinal status |
| **Months 3–12** | Long-term visual acuity, OCT, monitoring for recurrent pathology, cataract development, IOP |

## Complications

As with any intraocular surgery, PPV carries risks of both intraoperative and postoperative complications.

### Intraoperative Complications

| Complication | Description |
|---|---|
| **Iatrogenic retinal breaks** | Caused by instrument contact or vitreous traction during surgery; incidence 5–25% depending on complexity. Treated with endolaser and tamponade. |
| **Hemorrhage** | Intraoperative bleeding from retinal or choroidal vessels; may require endodiathermy, raising IOP temporarily, or use of perfluorocarbon liquids. |
| **Lens touch** | Inadvertent contact with the [[crystalline lens]] in phakic eyes, causing localized [[cataract]]. |
| **Suprachoroidal hemorrhage** | Rare but serious; hemorrhage in the [[suprachoroidal space]] causing expulsive or appositional choroidal detachment. |
| **Retinal incarceration** | Retinal tissue trapped in a sclerotomy site. |

### Postoperative Complications

| Complication | Incidence | Management |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Cataract]] progression** | 60–80% of phakic eyes within 2 years (especially with gas tamponade) | [[Cataract surgery]] (phacoemulsification with IOL) |
| **Elevated IOP / [[Ocular hypertension]]** | 20–30% (especially with gas or silicone oil tamponade) | Topical IOP-lowering agents; may require gas/oil removal or [[glaucoma]] surgery |
| **Recurrent [[retinal detachment]]** | 5–15% depending on initial pathology and PVR status | Repeat PPV with tamponade, scleral buckle |
| **[[Proliferative vitreoretinopathy]] (PVR)** | 5–10% in retinal detachment cases | Repeat PPV with membrane peeling, relaxing retinotomies, silicone oil tamponade |
| **[[Endophthalmitis]]** | 0.03–0.05% (similar to cataract surgery) | Vitreous tap/biopsy, intravitreal antibiotics ([[vancomycin]] + [[ceftazidime]] or [[amikacin]]) |
| **[[Cystoid macular edema]] (CME)** | Variable; more common in diabetic eyes | Topical NSAIDs, corticosteroids, intravitreal [[anti-VEGF]] or [[corticosteroid]] injections |
| **Hypotony** | Especially with sutureless wounds in early postoperative period | Observation (often self-resolving), wound suturing if persistent |
| **Sclerotomy-related retinal detachment** | Retinal break at sclerotomy site | Laser retinopexy, repeat surgery if detachment occurs |
| **Silicone oil complications** | Emulsification, band keratopathy, glaucoma, cataract | Silicone oil removal surgery |
| **Sympathetic ophthalmia** | Extremely rare bilateral granulomatous [[uveitis]] | Systemic immunosuppression |
| **Phthisis bulbi** | End-stage complication from chronic hypotony or inflammation | Enucleation or evisceration in severe cases |

## Visual Outcomes and Prognosis

Visual outcomes following PPV are highly dependent on the underlying pathology, duration of disease, and preoperative visual acuity.

| Condition | Expected Visual Outcome |
|---|---|
| **Epiretinal membrane** | 70–90% achieve ≥2-line improvement in BCVA; final VA often 20/40 or better |
| **Macular hole (Stage 2–3)** | Anatomical closure rate >90% with ILM peeling; significant VA improvement in majority |
| **Macular hole (Stage 4 / chronic)** | Lower closure rates; more modest visual improvement |
| **Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment (macula-on)** | Excellent prognosis; >90% maintain good VA if macula remains attached |
| **Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment (macula-off)** | Variable; VA recovery depends on duration of macular detachment. Better outcomes if surgery within 7–10 days |
| **Vitreous hemorrhage (diabetic)** | VA improvement correlates with underlying retinal status; good outcomes if no significant macular ischemia or traction |
| **Endophthalmitis** | Highly variable; depends on virulence of organism and time to treatment. EVS showed 53% achieved ≥20/40 with tap + inject for less virulent organisms |

## Role of the Optometrist

Optometrists play a critical role in the PPV care continuum:

1. **Identification and referral**: Recognizing conditions requiring PPV (e.g., new retinal detachment, non-clearing vitreous hemorrhage, symptomatic ERM/macular hole) and facilitating timely referral to a vitreoretinal specialist.

2. **Preoperative counseling**: Educating patients about the procedure, expected recovery, positioning requirements, and activity restrictions.

3. **Postoperative co-management**: Monitoring visual acuity recovery, IOP, anterior segment inflammation, gas fill resorption, retinal status, and cataract progression. Adjusting topical medications as directed by the surgeon.

4. **Long-term monitoring**: Surveillance for recurrent retinal detachment, PVR, cataract development, [[glaucoma]], and [[cystoid macular edema]]. Performing serial [[OCT]] and [[visual field]] testing as indicated.

5. **Refractive management**: Addressing refractive changes following PPV (especially after combined phaco-vitrectomy or silicone oil placement/removal) and prescribing appropriate spectacle or contact lens corrections.

## Emerging Technologies and Future Directions

The field of vitreoretinal surgery continues to evolve rapidly:

- **3D digital visualization systems** (e.g., Alcon NGENUITY, TrueVision): Heads-up, three-dimensional surgical displays that replace the traditional binocular microscope, offering enhanced depth perception, digital image filtering, and reduced phototoxicity to the retina.
- **Intraoperative OCT (iOCT)**: Real-time, microscope-integrated OCT imaging during surgery allows the surgeon to visualize retinal microanatomy, confirm membrane peeling completeness, assess subretinal fluid drainage, and verify macular hole closure intraoperatively.
- **Robotic-assisted vitreoretinal surgery**: Platforms such as the Preceyes Surgical System are being investigated to enhance surgical precision, reduce tremor, and enable sub-100-micron maneuvers that exceed human dexterity.
- **Sustained-release drug delivery**: Intravitreal implants (e.g., [[dexamethasone intravitreal implant]] [Ozurdex], [[fluocinolone acetonide]] implant [Iluvien, Retisert]) may be placed during or after PPV to provide prolonged anti-inflammatory or anti-edema therapy.
- **Gene therapy delivery**: PPV provides surgical access for subretinal injection of gene therapy vectors (e.g., [[voretigene neparvovec-rzyl]] [Luxturna] for [[RPE65]]-associated [[retinal dystrophy]]), representing a paradigm shift in the treatment of inherited retinal diseases.
- **Suprachoroidal drug delivery**: Emerging techniques for suprachoroidal injection of pharmacologic agents during or after PPV.

---

## Summary

Pars plana vitrectomy is a cornerstone of modern vitreoretinal surgery, offering definitive treatment for a vast array of posterior segment pathologies. The evolution from large-gauge, sutured approaches to small-gauge, sutureless, high-speed systems has dramatically improved surgical safety, patient comfort, and visual outcomes. Optometrists serve as essential partners in the identification of surgical candidates, timely referral, postoperative co-management, and long-term surveillance. A comprehensive understanding of PPV—including its indications, surgical principles, tamponade agents, complications, and emerging technologies—empowers the optometric clinician to deliver optimal, evidence-based care to patients undergoing this transformative procedure.
