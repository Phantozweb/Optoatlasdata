

# Conjunctiva

---

The **conjunctiva** is a thin, translucent, highly vascularized [[mucous membrane]] that lines the inner surface of the [[eyelids]] and reflects onto the anterior surface of the [[globe]], terminating at the [[limbus]] where it merges with the [[corneal epithelium]]. It plays a critical role in [[ocular surface]] homeostasis, immune defense, tear film stability, and lubrication of the eye. A thorough understanding of conjunctival anatomy, histology, physiology, and pathology is foundational for every [[optometrist]] and [[ophthalmologist]], as the conjunctiva is one of the most frequently examined and clinically significant tissues in eye care practice.

---

## Gross Anatomy

The conjunctiva is conventionally divided into three continuous anatomical zones:

### Palpebral (Tarsal) Conjunctiva

The **[[palpebral conjunctiva]]** lines the inner surface of the [[eyelids]]. It is firmly adherent to the underlying [[tarsal plate]] and cannot be easily separated from it. This portion is further subdivided into:

- **Marginal conjunctiva**: Begins at the [[mucocutaneous junction]] (the [[lid margin]]) and extends approximately 2 mm onto the inner lid surface.
- **Tarsal conjunctiva**: Overlies the [[tarsus]] directly; it is smooth, highly vascular, and the site most commonly examined during [[eversion of the eyelid]] for detection of [[papillae]], [[follicles]], and [[foreign bodies]].
- **Orbital conjunctiva**: Extends from the peripheral edge of the tarsus to the [[fornix]].

### Forniceal Conjunctiva

The **[[fornix]]** (plural: fornices) represents the cul-de-sac or transitional fold where the palpebral conjunctiva reflects upon itself to become the bulbar conjunctiva. The superior fornix is deeper than the inferior fornix. The forniceal conjunctiva is loosely attached to underlying [[Tenon's capsule]] and [[orbital septum]], allowing free movement of the globe. The [[lacrimal gland]] ducts open into the superolateral fornix. Accessory [[lacrimal glands of Krause]] and [[glands of Wolfring]] are concentrated in the fornices, contributing to basal [[tear secretion]].

### Bulbar Conjunctiva

The **[[bulbar conjunctiva]]** covers the anterior [[sclera]] from the fornix to the [[limbus]]. It is loosely attached to the underlying [[Tenon's capsule]] (episcleral tissue) over most of its extent, except at the limbus where it is firmly adherent. This loose attachment permits the tissue to be easily moved over the globe—a feature exploited during [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] and surgical procedures. The final 3 mm zone approaching the limbus is termed the **[[limbal conjunctiva]]** and is of particular clinical importance as it harbors [[limbal stem cells]] critical for [[corneal epithelial]] renewal.

### Plica Semilunaris and Caruncle

The **[[plica semilunaris]]** is a crescent-shaped fold of conjunctiva located at the medial [[canthus]], considered a vestigial remnant of the [[nictitating membrane]] found in other species. The **[[caruncle]]** is a small, fleshy, ovoid mound of modified skin located medial to the plica, containing [[sebaceous glands]], [[sweat glands]], and fine hairs ([[vellus hair]]). Both structures can be sites of pathology including [[cysts]], [[nevi]], and [[neoplasms]].

## Histology and Microanatomy

The conjunctiva is composed of two principal layers:

### Epithelium

The conjunctival [[epithelium]] is a **non-keratinized, stratified columnar epithelium** that varies in thickness and cell type depending on the region:

| Region | Epithelial Type | Layers |
|---|---|---|
| Marginal (lid margin) | Stratified squamous (non-keratinized) | 5–6 |
| Tarsal | Stratified columnar with [[goblet cells]] | 2–3 |
| Forniceal | Stratified columnar, richest in goblet cells | 3–5 |
| Bulbar | Stratified columnar transitioning to stratified squamous near limbus | 3–6 |
| Limbal | Stratified squamous (non-keratinized) | 10–12 |

**[[Goblet cells]]** are unicellular mucin-secreting glands interspersed among the epithelial cells. They are most abundant in the [[inferior fornix]] and the inferonasal bulbar conjunctiva. Goblet cells produce **[[MUC5AC]]**, the gel-forming [[mucin]] that constitutes the innermost mucin layer of the [[tear film]], essential for tear film stability and wettability of the [[ocular surface]]. Loss of goblet cells is a hallmark of conditions such as [[dry eye disease]], [[ocular cicatricial pemphigoid]], [[Stevens-Johnson syndrome]], and [[vitamin A deficiency]] ([[xerophthalmia]]).

Other specialized cells within the epithelium include:

- **[[Langerhans cells]]**: Antigen-presenting cells of the [[immune system]], concentrated near the limbus.
- **[[Melanocytes]]**: Present in the basal epithelial layer; their proliferation can give rise to [[conjunctival nevi]], [[primary acquired melanosis]], and [[conjunctival melanoma]].
- **[[Lymphocytes]]** (intraepithelial): Predominantly [[T cells]], contributing to mucosal immunity.

### Substantia Propria (Stroma)

The **[[substantia propria]]** is a layer of loose [[connective tissue]] beneath the epithelium, divided into:

- **Adenoid (lymphoid) layer**: The superficial layer, containing [[lymphoid tissue]], including [[lymphocytes]], [[plasma cells]], [[mast cells]], and [[neutrophils]]. This layer is not present at birth and develops within the first 2–3 months of life in response to antigenic exposure—a fact that explains why [[follicular conjunctivitis]] does not occur in neonates. Organized lymphoid aggregates known as **[[conjunctiva-associated lymphoid tissue]] (CALT)** are part of the broader [[mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue]] (MALT) system and play a central role in [[ocular surface]] immune surveillance.
- **Fibrous layer**: The deeper layer, composed of collagen and elastic fibers, containing blood vessels, lymphatics, and nerves. It merges with Tenon's capsule posteriorly.

## Blood Supply, Lymphatic Drainage, and Innervation

### Vascular Supply

The conjunctiva receives a dual blood supply:

- **[[Palpebral arterial arcades]]** (derived from the [[medial palpebral artery]] and [[lateral palpebral artery]], branches of the [[ophthalmic artery]] and [[lacrimal artery]]): Supply the palpebral and forniceal conjunctiva.
- **[[Anterior ciliary arteries]]** (branches of the [[muscular arteries]] supplying the [[rectus muscles]]): Supply the bulbar conjunctiva near the limbus.

Venous drainage follows a corresponding pattern, with the palpebral veins draining into the [[ophthalmic veins]] and the anterior conjunctival veins draining into the [[episcleral venous plexus]].

Clinically, distinguishing **conjunctival injection** (superficial, bright red, mobile vessels that blanch with [[phenylephrine]] 2.5%) from **[[ciliary injection]]** (deep, violaceous, perilimbal vessels that do not blanch) is essential for differentiating conjunctival disease from deeper [[anterior segment]] pathology such as [[anterior uveitis]], [[acute angle-closure glaucoma]], or [[keratitis]].

### Lymphatic Drainage

Lymphatic vessels in the conjunctiva drain to the **[[preauricular lymph nodes]]** (lateral conjunctiva) and **[[submandibular lymph nodes]]** (medial conjunctiva). Palpation of these nodes is a critical clinical maneuver in the evaluation of [[conjunctivitis]]—preauricular [[lymphadenopathy]] is a hallmark of [[viral conjunctivitis]] (especially [[adenoviral keratoconjunctivitis]]) and [[chlamydial conjunctivitis]].

### Innervation

Sensory innervation is provided by branches of the [[ophthalmic division (V1)]] and [[maxillary division (V2)]] of the [[trigeminal nerve]]:

- **[[Supraorbital nerve]]**, **[[supratrochlear nerve]]**, **[[infratrochlear nerve]]**, and **[[lacrimal nerve]]** (V1): Superior and lateral conjunctiva.
- **[[Infraorbital nerve]]** (V2): Inferior conjunctiva.

## Physiology and Functions

The conjunctiva serves multiple indispensable functions in maintaining [[ocular surface]] health:

| Function | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Lubrication** | Goblet cell mucin (MUC5AC) and accessory lacrimal gland aqueous secretion maintain the [[tear film]] |
| **Immune defense** | CALT, Langerhans cells, [[IgA]] secretion, and resident immune cells provide mucosal immunity |
| **Barrier function** | Stratified epithelium prevents microbial invasion |
| **Mechanical protection** | Smooth surface reduces friction during [[blinking]] and eye movements |
| **Wound healing** | Conjunctival epithelium can migrate to cover corneal defects in emergencies (conjunctivalization) |
| **Drug absorption** | Serves as a route for topical [[ophthalmic medication]] absorption |

## Pathophysiology of Conjunctival Disease

Conjunctival pathology arises through several fundamental mechanisms:

### Inflammatory Pathways

**[[Conjunctivitis]]**—inflammation of the conjunctiva—is the most common conjunctival pathology and the most frequent cause of a "[[red eye]]" in primary care. The inflammatory cascade may be triggered by:

- **Infectious agents**: [[Bacteria]] (e.g., *[[Staphylococcus aureus]]*, *[[Streptococcus pneumoniae]]*, *[[Haemophilus influenzae]]*, *[[Neisseria gonorrhoeae]]*, *[[Chlamydia trachomatis]]*), [[viruses]] (e.g., [[adenovirus]], [[herpes simplex virus]], [[herpes zoster virus]]), [[fungi]], and [[parasites]] (e.g., *[[Onchocerca volvulus]]*).
- **Allergic/hypersensitivity reactions**: [[Type I hypersensitivity]] (IgE-mediated [[mast cell]] degranulation) in [[seasonal allergic conjunctivitis]] and [[perennial allergic conjunctivitis]]; [[Type IV hypersensitivity]] (T-cell mediated) in [[contact dermatoconjunctivitis]] and [[giant papillary conjunctivitis]]; combined mechanisms in [[vernal keratoconjunctivitis]] and [[atopic keratoconjunctivitis]].
- **Toxic/chemical exposure**: Preservatives (e.g., [[benzalkonium chloride]]), topical medications, chemical burns ([[alkali burns]], [[acid burns]]).
- **Autoimmune/cicatrizing processes**: [[Ocular cicatricial pemphigoid]] (mucous membrane pemphigoid), [[Stevens-Johnson syndrome]]/[[toxic epidermal necrolysis]], [[graft-versus-host disease]], [[linear IgA disease]].

### Degenerative Processes

Chronic ultraviolet (UV) exposure, aging, and environmental irritation lead to degenerative conjunctival changes:

- **[[Pinguecula]]**: Yellowish, elevated deposits of elastotically degenerated collagen ([[elastotic degeneration]]) in the interpalpebral bulbar conjunctiva, typically nasal.
- **[[Pterygium]]**: A wing-shaped fibrovascular growth of conjunctival tissue that encroaches onto the [[cornea]], associated with UV exposure, dry/dusty environments, and potentially involving [[limbal stem cell]] dysfunction and altered [[matrix metalloproteinase]] (MMP) activity.
- **[[Conjunctivochalasis]]**: Redundant, loose folds of bulbar conjunctiva, typically inferior, that can interfere with tear meniscus dynamics and cause [[epiphora]], irritation, and [[tear film instability]].

### Neoplastic Transformation

The conjunctiva can give rise to a spectrum of benign and malignant neoplasms:

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Benign epithelial** | [[Conjunctival papilloma]] (often [[HPV]]-associated), [[keratoacanthoma]] |
| **Pre-malignant/malignant epithelial** | [[Conjunctival intraepithelial neoplasia]] (CIN) / [[ocular surface squamous neoplasia]] (OSSN), [[squamous cell carcinoma]] |
| **Melanocytic** | [[Conjunctival nevus]], [[primary acquired melanosis]] (PAM), [[conjunctival melanoma]] |
| **Lymphoid** | [[Benign reactive lymphoid hyperplasia]], [[conjunctival lymphoma]] (often [[MALT lymphoma]]) |
| **Vascular** | [[Pyogenic granuloma]], [[Kaposi sarcoma]] (in [[HIV/AIDS]]) |
| **Choristomatous** | [[Dermoid]], [[dermolipoma]], [[episcleral osseous choristoma]] |

Risk factors for [[OSSN]] include UV exposure, [[human papillomavirus]] (HPV) infection, [[HIV/AIDS]], [[xeroderma pigmentosum]], and immunosuppression.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Conjunctival disease presents with a constellation of symptoms and signs that vary by etiology:

### Symptoms

| Symptom | Common Associations |
|---|---|
| **Redness** ([[hyperemia]]) | All forms of conjunctivitis, [[subconjunctival hemorrhage]], pterygium |
| **Discharge** | Bacterial (mucopurulent), viral (watery/serous), allergic (mucoid/stringy) |
| **Itching** ([[pruritus]]) | Hallmark of [[allergic conjunctivitis]] |
| **Foreign body sensation** | Pinguecula, pterygium, conjunctivochalasis, papillae, follicles |
| **Tearing** ([[epiphora]]) | Viral conjunctivitis, allergic conjunctivitis, conjunctivochalasis |
| **Burning/stinging** | Toxic conjunctivitis, dry eye-related conjunctival staining |
| **Photophobia** | Suggests corneal involvement ([[keratoconjunctivitis]]) or [[anterior uveitis]] |
| **Blurred vision** | Mucous discharge, corneal involvement, large pterygium inducing [[astigmatism]] |
| **Lid swelling** ([[chemosis]]/[[edema]]) | Severe allergic reactions, viral conjunctivitis, [[orbital cellulitis]] |

### Signs on Examination

Key clinical signs observed during [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] include:

- **[[Conjunctival injection]]**: Diffuse or sectoral dilation of conjunctival blood vessels.
- **[[Chemosis]]**: Edema of the conjunctiva, causing a translucent, balloon-like swelling; severe in allergic reactions and [[adenoviral]] infections.
- **[[Papillae]]**: Small, polygonal elevations with a central vascular core, seen on the tarsal conjunctiva; associated with [[allergic conjunctivitis]], [[bacterial conjunctivitis]], [[contact lens]]-related irritation, and [[floppy eyelid syndrome]]. Giant papillae (>1 mm) are characteristic of [[vernal keratoconjunctivitis]] and [[giant papillary conjunctivitis]].
- **[[Follicles]]**: Small, round, avascular lymphoid aggregates with vessels coursing over (not through) them; characteristic of [[viral conjunctivitis]], [[chlamydial conjunctivitis]], [[toxic conjunctivitis]] (e.g., from [[brimonidine]], [[dipivefrin]]), and [[Parinaud oculoglandular syndrome]].
- **[[Membranes]] and [[pseudomembranes]]**: True membranes (firmly adherent, bleed on removal) are seen in severe [[adenoviral keratoconjunctivitis]], [[gonococcal conjunctivitis]], [[Stevens-Johnson syndrome]], [[ligneous conjunctivitis]], and [[diphtheria]]. Pseudomembranes (easily peeled without bleeding) occur in milder adenoviral infections and some bacterial conjunctivitides.
- **[[Cicatrization]] (scarring)**: [[Subconjunctival fibrosis]], [[symblepharon]] (adhesion of palpebral to bulbar conjunctiva), [[ankyloblepharon]], and [[fornix foreshortening]] are seen in cicatrizing diseases such as [[ocular cicatricial pemphigoid]], [[trachoma]], [[Stevens-Johnson syndrome]], and chemical burns.
- **[[Subconjunctival hemorrhage]]**: Flat, bright red blood beneath the conjunctiva; usually benign and self-limiting but may indicate [[hypertension]], [[coagulopathy]], or [[Valsalva maneuver]].
- **[[Conjunctival concretions]]** (lithiasis): Small, yellowish-white deposits of calcium and cellular debris within the substantia propria, common in elderly patients and those with chronic inflammation.
- **[[Conjunctival cysts]]**: Thin-walled, fluid-filled epithelial inclusion cysts, often post-traumatic or post-surgical.

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical Examination

- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: The cornerstone of conjunctival evaluation. Allows detailed assessment of injection patterns, papillae, follicles, membranes, scarring, masses, and foreign bodies. Use of [[cobalt blue filter]] with [[fluorescein]] staining reveals conjunctival epithelial defects and [[punctate staining]]. [[Rose bengal]] and [[lissamine green]] stain devitalized and [[mucin]]-deficient epithelial cells, useful in [[dry eye]] assessment and detection of early [[OSSN]].
- **[[Eversion of the upper eyelid]]**: Essential for examining the superior tarsal conjunctiva for papillae, follicles, foreign bodies, and concretions.
- **[[Double eversion]]** (with a [[Desmarres retractor]]): Allows inspection of the superior fornix.

### Laboratory Investigations

| Test | Indication |
|---|---|
| **Conjunctival cultures and sensitivity** | Severe, recurrent, or treatment-resistant [[bacterial conjunctivitis]]; [[neonatal conjunctivitis]] ([[ophthalmia neonatorum]]) |
| **Conjunctival swab for [[PCR]]** | [[Adenovirus]], [[herpes simplex virus]], [[Chlamydia trachomatis]], [[Neisseria gonorrhoeae]] |
| **Giemsa staining of conjunctival scrapings** | Identifies [[inclusion bodies]] (chlamydia), [[eosinophils]] (allergic), [[multinucleated giant cells]] (viral), bacterial morphology |
| **[[Impression cytology]]** | Evaluates goblet cell density, [[squamous metaplasia]], and epithelial changes in [[dry eye]], [[vitamin A deficiency]], and [[OSSN]] |
| **[[Incisional or excisional biopsy]]** | Definitive diagnosis of conjunctival masses, [[OSSN]], [[melanoma]], [[lymphoma]], and cicatrizing diseases |
| **[[Immunofluorescence]] / [[immunohistochemistry]]** | Detection of [[immunoglobulin]] and [[complement]] deposits in [[ocular cicatricial pemphigoid]]; tumor markers in neoplasms |
| **[[Anterior segment OCT]] (AS-OCT)** | Non-invasive imaging of conjunctival and corneal lesion thickness, depth of invasion in [[OSSN]], and pterygium morphology |
| **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | Assessment of deep conjunctival/scleral lesions, cysts, and tumors |
| **[[In vivo confocal microscopy]]** | Cellular-level imaging of conjunctival epithelium, goblet cells, inflammatory cells, and early neoplastic changes |

### Point-of-Care Testing

- **[[AdenoPlus]]** (RPS Detector): Rapid immunoassay for adenoviral antigen detection from conjunctival swabs; sensitivity ~90%, specificity ~96%.
- **[[InflammaDry]]**: Detects elevated [[MMP-9]] levels in tears, indicating ocular surface inflammation associated with [[dry eye disease]] and conjunctival compromise.

## Classification of Conjunctivitis

Conjunctivitis is classified by etiology, chronicity, and morphological pattern:

### By Etiology

| Type | Key Features |
|---|---|
| **[[Bacterial conjunctivitis]]** | Mucopurulent discharge, papillary reaction, matting of lids; common organisms: *S. aureus*, *S. pneumoniae*, *H. influenzae* |
| **[[Hyperacute bacterial conjunctivitis]]** | Copious purulent discharge, severe chemosis, rapid onset; *N. gonorrhoeae* (risk of [[corneal perforation]]) |
| **[[Chlamydial conjunctivitis]]** | Chronic follicular reaction, superior tarsal pannus, [[Herbert's pits]] (in [[trachoma]]); *C. trachomatis* serotypes A–C (trachoma), D–K (inclusion conjunctivitis) |
| **[[Viral conjunctivitis]]** | Watery discharge, follicular reaction, preauricular lymphadenopathy; [[adenovirus]] most common |
| **[[Allergic conjunctivitis]]** | Itching, chemosis, papillary reaction, mucoid discharge; seasonal/perennial, vernal, atopic |
| **[[Toxic/medicamentosa conjunctivitis]]** | Chronic follicular reaction, inferior fornix predominance; caused by topical medications or preservatives |
| **[[Autoimmune/cicatrizing conjunctivitis]]** | Progressive scarring, symblepharon, fornix foreshortening; [[OCP]], [[SJS]], [[trachoma]] |

### By Chronicity

- **Acute**: Duration < 4 weeks
- **Chronic**: Duration > 4 weeks
- **Recurrent**: Repeated episodes with intervening asymptomatic periods

### By Morphological Pattern

- **Papillary**: Allergic, bacterial, contact lens-related, [[floppy eyelid syndrome]]
- **Follicular**: Viral, chlamydial, toxic/medicamentosa, [[Moraxella]]
- **Cicatrizing**: Trachoma, OCP, SJS, chemical burns, [[radiation]], chronic medication toxicity
- **Granulomatous**: [[Sarcoidosis]], [[foreign body granuloma]], [[Parinaud oculoglandular syndrome]] (caused by *[[Bartonella henselae]]*, [[tularemia]], [[sporotrichosis]])

## Management and Treatment

### General Principles

Management of conjunctival disease is guided by the underlying etiology, severity, and chronicity. Key principles include:

1. **Accurate diagnosis** before initiating treatment
2. **Avoidance of unnecessary antibiotics** in viral conjunctivitis
3. **Hygiene measures** to prevent transmission of infectious conjunctivitis
4. **Allergen avoidance** in allergic disease
5. **Discontinuation of offending agents** in toxic/medicamentosa conjunctivitis
6. **Systemic therapy** when indicated (chlamydial, gonococcal, autoimmune disease)
7. **Referral** for biopsy of suspicious lesions

### Pharmacological Interventions

| Condition | Treatment |
|---|---|
| **Mild bacterial conjunctivitis** | Topical [[fluoroquinolone]] (e.g., [[moxifloxacin]] 0.5%, [[ofloxacin]] 0.3%), [[erythromycin]] ointment, [[polymyxin B/trimethoprim]] drops |
| **Hyperacute (gonococcal)** | Systemic [[ceftriaxone]] 1g IM single dose + topical [[fluoroquinolone]]; urgent referral; saline lavage |
| **Chlamydial conjunctivitis** | Oral [[azithromycin]] 1g single dose or [[doxycycline]] 100mg BID × 7 days; treat sexual partners |
| **Trachoma** | [[SAFE strategy]] (Surgery, Antibiotics, Facial cleanliness, Environmental improvement); oral azithromycin |
| **Viral (adenoviral)** | Supportive: cool compresses, [[artificial tears]], hygiene; topical [[povidone-iodine]] under investigation; avoid [[corticosteroids]] unless significant [[subepithelial infiltrates]] |
| **Herpes simplex conjunctivitis** | Topical [[ganciclovir]] 0.15% gel or oral [[acyclovir]]/[[valacyclovir]]; **avoid corticosteroids** without antiviral coverage |
| **Seasonal/perennial allergic conjunctivitis** | Topical [[antihistamine]]/[[mast cell stabilizer]] combinations (e.g., [[olopatadine]] 0.1%–0.7%, [[ketotifen]] 0.025%, [[alcaftadine]] 0.25%); cold compresses; oral [[antihistamines]] |
| **Vernal keratoconjunctivitis** | Topical mast cell stabilizers, short-pulse topical [[corticosteroids]] (e.g., [[loteprednol]] 0.5%), topical [[cyclosporine A]] 0.05%–2%, [[tacrolimus]] 0.03% ointment |
| **Atopic keratoconjunctivitis** | Topical [[cyclosporine A]], [[tacrolimus]], mast cell stabilizers; systemic [[immunomodulators]] in severe cases; lid hygiene |
| **Giant papillary conjunctivitis** | Discontinue or change [[contact lens]] type/material; topical mast cell stabilizers; improve lens hygiene |
| **Toxic/medicamentosa** | Discontinue offending agent; switch to [[preservative-free]] formulations; [[artificial tears]] |
| **Ocular cicatricial pemphigoid** | Systemic [[immunosuppression]]: [[dapsone]], [[mycophenolate mofetil]], [[cyclophosphamide]], [[rituximab]]; topical lubrication; surgical management of complications |
| **Dry eye with conjunctival involvement** | [[Artificial tears]], [[cyclosporine A]] 0.05% ([[Restasis]]), [[lifitegrast]] 5% ([[Xiidra]]), [[punctal plugs]], [[autologous serum tears]] |

### Surgical and Procedural Interventions

| Procedure | Indication |
|---|---|
| **[[Pterygium excision]]** with [[conjunctival autograft]] or [[amniotic membrane transplantation]]** | Visually significant or progressive [[pterygium]]; adjunctive [[mitomycin C]] (0.02%) to reduce recurrence |
| **[[Conjunctival biopsy]]** | Suspicious masses, OSSN, melanocytic lesions, cicatrizing disease diagnosis |
| **Excisional biopsy with [[cryotherapy]]** | [[Conjunctival melanoma]], [[PAM with atypia]], [[OSSN]] |
| **Topical [[chemotherapy]]** | [[OSSN]]: topical [[5-fluorouracil]] (1%) or [[mitomycin C]] (0.02–0.04%) or [[interferon alpha-2b]] (1 million IU/mL) |
| **[[Symblepharon lysis]]** and [[fornix reconstruction]]** | Cicatrizing conjunctivitis with restricted motility or [[entropion]] |
| **[[Amniotic membrane transplantation]]** | Ocular surface reconstruction in chemical burns, SJS, OCP, persistent epithelial defects |
| **[[Conjunctival flap]]** (e.g., [[Gundersen flap]])** | Chronic non-healing [[corneal ulcers]], corneal perforations (temporizing measure) |
| **[[Mucous membrane grafting]]** (buccal mucosa) | Severe fornix foreshortening, socket reconstruction |
| **Removal of [[conjunctival concretions]]** | Symptomatic concretions causing foreign body sensation |
| **Drainage/excision of [[conjunctival cysts]]** | Symptomatic retention or inclusion cysts |

## Special Considerations

### Neonatal Conjunctivitis ([[Ophthalmia Neonatorum]])

Conjunctivitis occurring within the first 28 days of life is a medical emergency requiring prompt identification of the causative organism:

| Timing (Post-Birth) | Likely Etiology | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Day 1 | Chemical (silver nitrate prophylaxis) | Self-limiting; observation |
| Days 2–5 | *[[Neisseria gonorrhoeae]]* | Systemic [[ceftriaxone]]; saline irrigation; hospitalization |
| Days 5–14 | *[[Chlamydia trachomatis]]* | Oral [[erythromycin]] × 14 days |
| Days 5–28 | Other bacteria (*S. aureus*, *H. influenzae*) | Topical antibiotics based on culture |
| Days 6–14 | [[Herpes simplex virus]] | Systemic [[acyclovir]]; topical antivirals |

### Conjunctival Involvement in Systemic Disease

The conjunctiva may provide diagnostic clues to systemic conditions:

- **[[Sarcoidosis]]**: Conjunctival granulomas (biopsy may confirm diagnosis)
- **[[Vitamin A deficiency]]**: [[Bitot's spots]] (foamy, keratinized patches on bulbar conjunctiva), [[xerosis]], goblet cell loss
- **[[Sickle cell disease]]**: Comma-shaped conjunctival vessels, vascular sludging
- **[[Jaundice]]**: [[Icterus]] (yellowing of the conjunctiva/sclera)
- **[[Anemia]]**: Pallor of the palpebral conjunctiva
- **[[Atopy]]/[[eczema]]**: Chronic allergic conjunctivitis, atopic keratoconjunctivitis
- **[[Kawasaki disease]]**: Bilateral non-exudative conjunctival injection
- **[[Reactive arthritis]] (Reiter syndrome)**: Mucopurulent conjunctivitis
- **[[HIV/AIDS]]**: Increased risk of [[OSSN]], [[Kaposi sarcoma]], [[microsporidial keratoconjunctivitis]]

### Contact Lens-Related Conjunctival Changes

[[Contact lens]] wear can induce several conjunctival alterations:

- **[[Giant papillary conjunctivitis]]**: Immune-mediated papillary hypertrophy of the superior tarsal conjunctiva
- **[[Contact lens-induced papillary conjunctivitis]]** (CLPC)
- **[[Conjunctival staining]]**: Lid-parallel conjunctival folds (LIPCOF), conjunctival indentation from lens edge
- **Reduced [[goblet cell]] density**: Chronic lens wear may decrease mucin production
- **[[Superior limbic keratoconjunctivitis]]** (SLK)-like changes: Thimerosal sensitivity (historically)

## Prognosis and Complications

Most forms of acute conjunctivitis are self-limiting or respond well to appropriate therapy. However, complications can arise:

- **Corneal involvement**: [[Keratitis]], [[marginal infiltrates]], [[subepithelial infiltrates]] (post-adenoviral), [[corneal ulceration]] (gonococcal), [[pannus]] (trachoma, vernal)
- **Scarring**: [[Symblepharon]], [[trichiasis]], [[entropion]], [[corneal opacification]] in cicatrizing diseases
- **Vision loss**: From corneal scarring, [[limbal stem cell deficiency]], or secondary [[glaucoma]]
- **Chronic discomfort**: Persistent [[dry eye]], [[ocular surface disease]]
- **Malignant transformation**: PAM with atypia → [[conjunctival melanoma]]; CIN → [[invasive squamous cell carcinoma]]

---

## Prevention

- **Hand hygiene**: Critical for preventing transmission of infectious conjunctivitis
- **UV protection**: Sunglasses with UV-A/UV-B blocking reduce risk of [[pterygium]], [[pinguecula]], and [[OSSN]]
- **[[Crede prophylaxis]]** / [[erythromycin ointment]]: Neonatal prophylaxis against ophthalmia neonatorum
- **[[Trachoma]] control**: WHO SAFE strategy in endemic regions
- **Contact lens hygiene**: Proper cleaning, replacement schedules, and avoidance of overnight wear reduce conjunctival complications
- **Allergen avoidance and environmental control**: For allergic conjunctivitis
- **Regular ophthalmic screening**: In immunosuppressed patients and those with known risk factors for [[OSSN]] or [[melanoma]]

---

## Summary

The conjunctiva is a deceptively complex tissue whose health is integral to overall [[ocular surface]] function and visual well-being. Its accessibility to clinical examination makes it one of the most frequently evaluated structures in eye care, yet the breadth of pathology it can manifest—from common infectious and allergic conjunctivitis to sight-threatening cicatrizing diseases and life-threatening malignancies—demands a thorough and systematic approach to diagnosis and management. Mastery of conjunctival anatomy, physiology, and disease is essential for every eye care professional.
