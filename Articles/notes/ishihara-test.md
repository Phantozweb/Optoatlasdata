# Ishihara Test

---

The **Ishihara Test** (also known as the **Ishihara Color Vision Test** or **Ishihara Pseudoisochromatic Plate Test**) is the most widely recognized and clinically utilized screening instrument for the detection of [[color vision deficiency]] (CVD), particularly [[red-green color deficiency]]. Developed by Japanese [[ophthalmologist]] Dr. Shinobu Ishihara and first published in 1917 at the University of Tokyo, the test employs a series of **pseudoisochromatic plates** (PIP) composed of colored dots arranged in patterns that reveal numerals or pathways to individuals with normal [[trichromatic vision]], while remaining indistinguishable or presenting alternative figures to those with specific [[color vision]] anomalies. The Ishihara Test remains a cornerstone of clinical [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] practice, occupying a central role in routine vision screening, occupational health assessments, and the preliminary evaluation of [[congenital color vision deficiency]] and, to a lesser extent, [[acquired color vision deficiency]].

---

## History and Development

Dr. Shinobu Ishihara (1879–1963) developed the test while serving as a military surgeon and professor at the University of Tokyo. Influenced by the earlier work of Professor Jakob Stilling, who had introduced the concept of pseudoisochromatic plates in the late 19th century, Ishihara refined the methodology to create a more reliable and standardized screening tool. The first edition of the test was published in 1917 and contained a set of hand-painted plates. Over the subsequent decades, the test underwent numerous revisions, with improvements in printing technology allowing for greater color accuracy and reproducibility. The current standard edition comprises **38 plates**, although abbreviated versions containing **24 plates** and **14 plates** are also commercially available and widely used in clinical practice.

The enduring clinical utility of the Ishihara Test is attributable to its high [[sensitivity]] for detecting [[protan]] and [[deutan]] defects (collectively termed red-green color vision deficiencies), its ease of administration, its rapid execution time (typically under five minutes), and its minimal requirement for patient literacy or verbal communication, as certain plates utilize pathways rather than numerals.

## Principles of Color Vision and Pathophysiology

### Normal Color Vision

Human [[color vision]] is mediated by three classes of [[cone photoreceptors]] located in the [[retina]], each containing a distinct [[photopigment]] ([[opsin]]) with peak spectral sensitivity at different wavelengths of visible light:

| Cone Type | Photopigment | Peak Sensitivity (λ max) | Color Perception |
|---|---|---|---|
| **L-cones** (Long-wavelength) | [[Erythrolabe]] | ~564 nm | Red |
| **M-cones** (Medium-wavelength) | [[Chlorolabe]] | ~534 nm | Green |
| **S-cones** (Short-wavelength) | [[Cyanolabe]] | ~420 nm | Blue |

Normal [[trichromatic vision]] (trichromacy) requires the presence and proper function of all three cone types. Color discrimination is achieved through opponent processing in the [[retinal ganglion cells]], [[lateral geniculate nucleus]] (LGN), and [[visual cortex]] (area [[V4]]), where signals from the three cone types are compared along red-green, blue-yellow, and luminance channels.

### Pathophysiology of Color Vision Deficiency Detected by the Ishihara Test

The Ishihara Test is specifically designed to detect deficiencies along the **red-green axis** of color vision. These deficiencies arise from genetic mutations affecting the [[opsin]] genes located on the **X chromosome** (Xq28 region), which encode the L-cone and M-cone photopigments. Because of [[X-linked recessive inheritance]], red-green color vision deficiency is significantly more prevalent in males (approximately 8% of males of Northern European descent) than in females (approximately 0.5%).

The pathophysiological basis of red-green CVD involves either the **absence** of a cone photopigment (dichromacy) or the **alteration** of its spectral sensitivity (anomalous trichromacy):

| Classification | Subtype | Defective Cone | Mechanism | Prevalence (Males) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Protanopia** | [[Dichromacy]] | L-cone (Red) | Absence of L-cone opsin | ~1.0% |
| **Protanomaly** | [[Anomalous trichromacy]] | L-cone (Red) | Shifted L-cone spectral sensitivity toward M-cone | ~1.0% |
| **Deuteranopia** | [[Dichromacy]] | M-cone (Green) | Absence of M-cone opsin | ~1.5% |
| **Deuteranomaly** | [[Anomalous trichromacy]] | M-cone (Green) | Shifted M-cone spectral sensitivity toward L-cone | ~5.0% |

In **protanopia**, the L-cone photopigment is entirely absent, and the individual relies solely on M-cones and S-cones for color discrimination. This results in a **confusion axis** along which reds, oranges, and certain greens become indistinguishable. Critically, protanopes also experience a **luminance deficit** in the long-wavelength (red) portion of the spectrum, meaning red objects appear darker than they do to color-normal observers—a phenomenon with significant safety implications (e.g., perception of red traffic signals, brake lights).

In **deuteranopia**, the M-cone photopigment is absent, and the individual relies on L-cones and S-cones. The confusion axis is similar to that of protanopia but without the accompanying luminance loss in the red spectrum.

In **anomalous trichromacy** (protanomaly and deuteranomaly), all three cone types are present, but one photopigment has an abnormal spectral absorption curve shifted toward the peak of an adjacent cone type. This results in reduced, but not absent, color discrimination along the red-green axis. The severity of the deficiency is variable and depends on the degree of spectral overlap between the normal and anomalous photopigments.

The Ishihara Test exploits these **confusion lines** (also known as **isochromatic lines** or **pseudoisochromatic confusion loci**) in [[CIE chromaticity space]]. The colored dots forming the figure and the background are selected such that they differ in chromaticity along axes that are discriminable by normal trichromats but fall along the confusion lines of protan or deutan observers. Thus, the figure "disappears" into the background for affected individuals, or an alternative figure—visible only along the deficient confusion axis—emerges.

### Acquired Color Vision Deficiency

While the Ishihara Test is primarily designed for [[congenital color vision deficiency]], it may also detect acquired red-green defects resulting from:

- [[Optic neuritis]] (particularly associated with [[multiple sclerosis]])
- [[Optic neuropathy]] (toxic, nutritional, compressive, or ischemic)
- [[Macular degeneration]] or [[macular edema]]
- [[Glaucoma]] (advanced stages)
- [[Retinal detachment]] involving the [[macula]]
- Pharmacological toxicity (e.g., [[ethambutol]], [[hydroxychloroquine]], [[digitalis]])
- [[Diabetic retinopathy]]

However, acquired CVD frequently involves the **blue-yellow (tritan) axis**, for which the Ishihara Test has very limited sensitivity. Therefore, the Ishihara Test should **not** be used as the sole instrument for evaluating acquired color vision loss; supplementary tests such as the [[Farnsworth-Munsell 100 Hue Test]], [[Farnsworth D-15 Test]], or [[Hardy-Rand-Rittler (HRR) plates]] are recommended in such clinical contexts.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Patients with [[congenital color vision deficiency]] detected by the Ishihara Test typically present with the following characteristics:

### Subjective Symptoms

- **Difficulty distinguishing red from green**, or confusing colors that contain red or green components (e.g., brown vs. green, orange vs. yellow, pink vs. gray)
- **Difficulty identifying colored objects** in everyday life, such as traffic signals, color-coded maps, electronic displays, or ripening fruit
- **Academic difficulties** in childhood, particularly in tasks involving color identification, color matching, or color-coded educational materials
- **Occupational limitations**, as many professions require normal color vision (e.g., aviation, maritime, electrical engineering, military service, law enforcement, certain medical specialties)
- Patients with **mild anomalous trichromacy** may be entirely unaware of their deficiency until formal testing is performed

### Objective Signs

- **No structural abnormality** of the [[anterior segment]], [[lens]], [[vitreous]], or [[retina]] is present in congenital CVD; the [[fundus examination]] is entirely normal
- [[Visual acuity]] is **unaffected** in congenital red-green CVD
- [[Visual field]] testing is **normal**
- [[Pupillary reflexes]] are **normal**
- The deficiency is **bilateral**, **symmetric**, and **non-progressive** in congenital cases

> **Clinical Pearl:** If a patient demonstrates asymmetric color vision loss, progressive deterioration, or associated visual acuity loss, an **acquired** etiology must be suspected, and further investigation including [[optical coherence tomography]] (OCT), [[visual evoked potential]] (VEP), [[fundus photography]], and [[neuroimaging]] should be pursued.

## Diagnostic Methodology: The Ishihara Test in Detail

### Test Composition and Plate Design

The standard 38-plate edition of the Ishihara Test contains several categories of plates, each serving a distinct diagnostic purpose:

| Plate Category | Plate Numbers (38-plate edition) | Purpose | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Demonstration Plate** | Plate 1 | Control / Introduction | Displays the numeral "12" visible to all observers, including those with CVD. Used to confirm the patient understands the task and to verify adequate illumination. |
| **Transformation Plates** | Plates 2–9 | Screening for CVD | Display a numeral visible to normal trichromats; a **different** numeral is seen by color-deficient observers. |
| **Vanishing Plates** | Plates 10–17 | Screening for CVD | Display a numeral visible to normal trichromats; **no numeral** is seen by color-deficient observers. |
| **Hidden Digit Plates** | Plates 18–21 | Confirmation of CVD | Display a numeral visible **only** to color-deficient observers; normal trichromats see no numeral. |
| **Diagnostic Plates (Classification)** | Plates 22–25 | Differentiation of protan vs. deutan | Display two numerals; color-deficient observers can read only one, depending on whether the defect is protan or deutan. |
| **Tracing Plates (Pathway Plates)** | Plates 26–38 | For illiterate or pediatric patients | Display winding colored pathways instead of numerals; the patient traces the path with a finger or brush. |

### Administration Protocol

Proper administration of the Ishihara Test is essential for valid results. The following standardized protocol should be observed:

1. **Illumination:** The test must be performed under **standardized illumination** approximating [[CIE Standard Illuminant C]] (simulating average daylight with a [[correlated color temperature]] of approximately 6,774 K). The recommended illuminance at the plate surface is **200–750 lux**. Fluorescent lighting with a high [[Color Rendering Index]] (CRI ≥ 90) or dedicated daylight-simulation lamps (e.g., Macbeth Easel Lamp) are preferred. Incandescent tungsten lighting is **not acceptable** due to its warm spectral bias, which can alter the chromaticity of the plates and produce erroneous results.

2. **Viewing Distance:** The plates should be held at a distance of **75 cm (approximately 30 inches)** from the patient, perpendicular to the line of sight.

3. **Viewing Time:** The patient should be allowed **approximately 3–4 seconds** per plate to provide a response. Prolonged scrutiny may allow color-deficient individuals to use luminance cues or edge-detection strategies to identify figures, reducing test sensitivity.

4. **Monocular vs. Binocular Testing:** For routine screening, binocular testing is acceptable. However, when acquired CVD is suspected, **monocular testing** of each eye is mandatory to detect asymmetric defects.

5. **Corrective Lenses:** Patients should wear their habitual [[refractive correction]] ([[spectacles]] or [[contact lenses]]) during testing. However, **tinted lenses**, [[photochromic lenses]], or [[color-filtering lenses]] must be removed, as they can alter the spectral composition of light reaching the retina and invalidate results.

6. **Patient Instructions:** The patient is asked to identify the numeral or trace the pathway on each plate. Responses are recorded and compared to the scoring key provided with the test booklet.

### Scoring and Interpretation

The interpretation of the Ishihara Test follows a systematic approach:

| Result | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| **All plates read correctly (or ≤ 2 errors on plates 2–17)** | Normal color vision |
| **≥ 3 errors on plates 2–17** | Color vision deficiency present |
| **Errors on diagnostic plates 22–25** | Classification as [[protan]] or [[deutan]] defect |
| **Plate 1 not read correctly** | Suspect malingering, severe visual impairment, or improper test conditions |

The test provides a **qualitative** screening result (pass/fail) and a **semi-quantitative** indication of severity based on the number of errors. It can also **classify** the defect as protan or deutan using the diagnostic plates. However, the Ishihara Test **cannot**:

- Quantify the precise **severity** of the deficiency (mild, moderate, severe)
- Reliably detect **tritan** (blue-yellow) defects
- Distinguish between **dichromacy** and **anomalous trichromacy** within the same axis
- Serve as a definitive diagnostic tool for **acquired** color vision loss

For these purposes, supplementary testing with instruments such as the [[anomaloscope]] (Nagel anomaloscope, considered the **gold standard** for classification and grading of red-green CVD), the [[Farnsworth-Munsell 100 Hue Test]], the [[Farnsworth D-15 Test]], the [[Hardy-Rand-Rittler (HRR) plates]], the [[Lanthony Desaturated D-15 Test]], or computerized color vision assessments (e.g., [[Cambridge Colour Test]], [[Colour Assessment and Diagnosis (CAD) test]]) is indicated.

## Psychometric Properties

The Ishihara Test has been extensively validated and demonstrates excellent psychometric properties for its intended purpose of screening red-green CVD:

| Property | Performance |
|---|---|
| **Sensitivity** (red-green CVD) | 91–100% (depending on edition and study) |
| **Specificity** (red-green CVD) | 95–100% |
| **Positive Predictive Value** | High in populations with expected prevalence |
| **Negative Predictive Value** | Very high |
| **Sensitivity** (tritan defects) | Very low (~0–15%); **not clinically useful** |
| **Test-Retest Reliability** | High (κ > 0.90) |

These values establish the Ishihara Test as one of the most sensitive and specific screening instruments available for congenital red-green CVD. Its primary limitation remains its inability to detect [[tritan defects]] and its limited capacity for grading severity.

## Differential Diagnosis

When a patient fails the Ishihara Test, the clinician must consider the following differential diagnoses:

- **[[Congenital red-green color vision deficiency]]** (protanopia, protanomaly, deuteranopia, deuteranomaly) — by far the most common cause
- **[[Acquired optic neuropathy]]** — toxic (e.g., [[ethambutol]], [[methanol]]), nutritional (e.g., [[vitamin B12 deficiency]]), compressive, ischemic, or inflammatory
- **[[Optic neuritis]]** — particularly in the context of [[multiple sclerosis]] or [[neuromyelitis optica]]
- **[[Macular disease]]** — including [[age-related macular degeneration]], [[central serous chorioretinopathy]], [[macular edema]], or [[macular dystrophy]]
- **[[Retinal disease]]** — including [[cone dystrophy]], [[cone-rod dystrophy]], or [[achromatopsia]] (though the latter typically involves all color axes)
- **[[Cerebral achromatopsia]]** — acquired loss of color perception due to lesions in area [[V4]] of the [[visual cortex]]
- **Malingering or simulation** — the demonstration plate (Plate 1) and hidden digit plates help identify deliberate misrepresentation
- **Improper test conditions** — inadequate illumination, tinted lenses, or faded/damaged plates

## Management and Counseling

### General Principles

There is currently **no cure** for [[congenital color vision deficiency]]. The condition is genetically determined, non-progressive, and does not respond to pharmacological or surgical intervention. Management is therefore centered on **education**, **counseling**, **adaptive strategies**, and **occupational guidance**.

### Patient Education and Counseling

- **Explanation of the condition:** Patients (and parents of affected children) should be informed that congenital CVD is a common, benign, inherited trait—not a disease—and that it does not worsen over time or lead to blindness.
- **Genetic counseling:** The [[X-linked recessive]] inheritance pattern should be explained. An affected male inherits the defective gene from his carrier mother. All daughters of an affected male will be obligate carriers. Genetic counseling is particularly relevant for family planning.
- **Academic accommodations:** Teachers and school administrators should be informed so that color-coded educational materials can be supplemented with alternative cues (labels, patterns, textures).
- **Occupational counseling:** Patients should be advised regarding occupations that require normal color vision and may be restricted. These include:
  - Commercial aviation (pilot, air traffic controller)
  - Maritime navigation
  - Military service (certain roles)
  - Law enforcement
  - Electrical and electronic engineering
  - Railway operations
  - Certain medical specialties (pathology, dermatology, ophthalmology)

### Optical Aids and Assistive Technology

- **[[Color-filtering lenses]]** (e.g., EnChroma, Pilestone, ColorMax): These spectacle-mounted filters selectively attenuate wavelengths in the region of spectral overlap between L-cone and M-cone sensitivity curves, theoretically enhancing chromatic contrast for anomalous trichromats. While many users report subjective improvement in color perception and quality of life, **peer-reviewed evidence** regarding their efficacy in improving performance on standardized color vision tests (including the Ishihara Test) is **mixed and limited**. These lenses do **not** restore normal trichromacy and are **not** accepted as a corrective measure for occupational color vision requirements.
- **Smartphone applications and digital tools:** Various applications can assist color-deficient individuals by identifying colors in real-time using the device camera, applying color-transforming filters to digital displays, or providing color labels.
- **Environmental modifications:** Use of high-contrast, non-color-dependent labeling systems in the workplace and home.

### Emerging Therapeutic Research

- **[[Gene therapy]]:** Preclinical studies in animal models (notably squirrel monkeys) have demonstrated that viral vector-mediated delivery of the missing cone opsin gene to the [[retina]] can restore trichromatic color vision. While these results are promising, human clinical trials for congenital CVD gene therapy have not yet been conducted, and significant challenges remain regarding safety, efficacy, and the capacity of the adult [[visual cortex]] to integrate novel chromatic information.

### Management of Acquired Color Vision Deficiency

When the Ishihara Test reveals a new or asymmetric color vision defect suggestive of acquired pathology, management is directed at the **underlying etiology**:

- **[[Optic neuritis]]:** Treatment with intravenous [[corticosteroids]] (e.g., [[methylprednisolone]]) per the Optic Neuritis Treatment Trial (ONTT) protocol; evaluation for [[multiple sclerosis]]
- **Toxic optic neuropathy:** Discontinuation of the offending agent (e.g., [[ethambutol]], [[methanol]]); nutritional supplementation if indicated
- **Compressive optic neuropathy:** [[Neuroimaging]] ([[MRI]] of the brain and orbits with gadolinium contrast) and neurosurgical consultation
- **Macular disease:** Appropriate retinal evaluation and management (e.g., [[anti-VEGF therapy]] for [[neovascular AMD]], [[laser photocoagulation]], [[intravitreal injections]])

## Special Populations and Considerations

### Pediatric Testing

The Ishihara Test can be administered to children as young as **4–6 years of age**, provided they can reliably identify numerals. For younger or pre-literate children, the **tracing (pathway) plates** (Plates 26–38) are invaluable, as they require only the ability to follow a colored line with a finger. Alternative pediatric color vision tests include the [[Colour Vision Testing Made Easy (CVTME)]] plates, which use shapes (circle, square, star) instead of numerals.

Early identification of CVD in children is important for:
- Avoiding misattribution of academic difficulties to learning disabilities
- Implementing appropriate classroom accommodations
- Providing anticipatory guidance regarding future occupational choices

### Screening Recommendations

- The [[American Academy of Ophthalmology]] (AAO) and the [[American Optometric Association]] (AOA) recommend color vision screening as part of comprehensive pediatric eye examinations.
- Many countries mandate color vision testing for specific occupational certifications (e.g., aviation medical examinations per [[International Civil Aviation Organization]] (ICAO) standards, maritime certifications per [[International Maritime Organization]] (IMO) standards).
- The Ishihara Test is the most commonly specified screening instrument in occupational health regulations worldwide.

### Limitations and Caveats

| Limitation | Clinical Implication |
|---|---|
| Cannot detect tritan (blue-yellow) defects | Supplementary testing required for suspected acquired CVD |
| Cannot grade severity precisely | Anomaloscope or arrangement tests needed for quantification |
| Cannot distinguish dichromacy from anomalous trichromacy | Anomaloscope required for definitive classification |
| Plate degradation over time | Plates must be replaced periodically; faded or damaged plates yield unreliable results |
| Illumination-dependent | Results invalid under non-standardized lighting |
| Digital/electronic versions may lack calibration | Monitor-based versions require strict color calibration and are generally not accepted for occupational certification |

## Medicolegal and Occupational Considerations

The Ishihara Test carries significant medicolegal weight in occupational health settings. Failure on the Ishihara Test may disqualify individuals from certain professions or require further evaluation with more definitive instruments (e.g., the [[Nagel anomaloscope]] or the [[CAD test]]). Clinicians administering the test must ensure:

- Standardized test conditions (illumination, viewing distance, timing)
- Use of a current, undamaged edition of the plates
- Accurate recording and reporting of results
- Appropriate referral for confirmatory testing when occupational decisions depend on the outcome
- Awareness that some jurisdictions accept alternative or supplementary tests for occupational certification

---

## Summary

The **Ishihara Test** remains the preeminent screening tool for [[red-green color vision deficiency]] in clinical [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]]. Its elegant design, rooted in the principles of [[pseudoisochromatic]] confusion, provides a rapid, reliable, and highly sensitive method for detecting [[protan]] and [[deutan]] defects. While it cannot replace more comprehensive instruments such as the [[Nagel anomaloscope]] for definitive classification and severity grading, and while it is insensitive to [[tritan]] defects, its unparalleled combination of sensitivity, specificity, ease of use, and widespread availability ensures its continued centrality in color vision assessment for the foreseeable future. Clinicians must be mindful of its limitations, adhere to standardized administration protocols, and employ supplementary testing when clinical circumstances demand a more detailed evaluation of color vision function.
