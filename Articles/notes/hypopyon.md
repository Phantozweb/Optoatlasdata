

# Hypopyon

---

**Hypopyon** (from the Greek *hypo*, meaning "beneath," and *pyon*, meaning "pus") is a clinical sign characterized by the visible accumulation of purulent or inflammatory exudate—predominantly composed of [[leukocytes]], [[fibrin]], and cellular debris—in the [[anterior chamber]] of the eye, settling inferiorly due to gravity. This layered collection of inflammatory material forms a whitish-yellow, horizontally demarcated fluid level behind the [[cornea]] and anterior to the [[iris]], and is readily observable on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] or, in severe cases, with the unaided eye.

Hypopyon is not a disease entity in itself but rather a significant clinical sign that indicates intense [[anterior segment]] inflammation. It may arise from infectious, non-infectious, or neoplastic etiologies and demands urgent clinical evaluation to determine the underlying cause and initiate appropriate management. The presence of a hypopyon universally signals a severe disruption of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]] and carries implications for visual prognosis if not promptly and correctly addressed.

---

## Epidemiology

The incidence of hypopyon varies considerably depending on the underlying etiology and the population studied. In the context of infectious [[keratitis]], hypopyon has been reported in approximately 10–30% of cases of bacterial [[corneal ulcer]], with higher rates observed in infections caused by virulent organisms such as *[[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]]* and *[[Staphylococcus aureus]]*. In [[endophthalmitis]]—both exogenous (post-surgical or post-traumatic) and endogenous—hypopyon is a hallmark finding, occurring in up to 75–86% of acute postoperative cases.

Non-infectious causes, including [[Behçet disease]], [[HLA-B27]]-associated [[anterior uveitis]], and drug-induced hypopyon (notably with [[rifabutin]] therapy), account for a smaller but clinically important proportion of cases. Masquerade syndromes, particularly [[retinoblastoma]] in children and [[lymphoma]] or [[leukemia]] in adults, represent rare but critical differential diagnoses where the "pseudohypopyon" consists of neoplastic cells rather than inflammatory exudate.

| **Etiology Category** | **Common Causes** | **Approximate Frequency of Hypopyon** |
|---|---|---|
| Infectious Keratitis | *Pseudomonas*, *Staphylococcus*, *Streptococcus*, Fungal, *Acanthamoeba* | 10–30% of bacterial ulcers; higher in fungal |
| Endophthalmitis (Exogenous) | Post-[[cataract surgery]], post-intravitreal injection, post-trauma | 75–86% of acute cases |
| Endophthalmitis (Endogenous) | Candidemia, bacteremia, IV drug use | 50–70% |
| Non-infectious Uveitis | [[Behçet disease]], [[HLA-B27]] uveitis, [[Sarcoidosis]] | Variable; ~30% in Behçet |
| Drug-Induced | [[Rifabutin]], [[Cidofovir]], [[Methotrexate]] (intracameral) | Dose-dependent |
| Masquerade Syndromes | [[Retinoblastoma]], [[Lymphoma]], [[Leukemia]] | Rare; pseudohypopyon |

## Anatomy and Relevant Structures

Understanding the anatomy of the [[anterior segment]] is essential for comprehending the pathogenesis and clinical significance of hypopyon.

### Anterior Chamber

The [[anterior chamber]] is the fluid-filled space bounded anteriorly by the posterior surface of the [[cornea]] (specifically the [[corneal endothelium]]) and posteriorly by the anterior surface of the [[iris]] and the [[pupil]]. It is filled with [[aqueous humor]], a clear, optically transparent fluid produced by the [[ciliary body]] epithelium. The average depth of the anterior chamber is approximately 3.0–3.5 mm centrally, and its volume is roughly 0.25 mL. The inferior angle of the anterior chamber, formed by the junction of the [[cornea]] and [[iris]], is where gravitationally dependent material—including inflammatory cells—tends to accumulate, forming the characteristic layered appearance of hypopyon.

### Blood-Aqueous Barrier

The [[blood-aqueous barrier]] (BAB) is a physiological barrier that maintains the immune privilege of the anterior chamber by restricting the passage of plasma proteins and blood cells into the [[aqueous humor]]. It consists of two components:

1. **The [[iris]] vascular endothelium** — tight junctions between endothelial cells of iris capillaries prevent paracellular leakage.
2. **The non-pigmented [[ciliary epithelium]]** — tight junctions between these cells restrict passage of molecules from the ciliary stroma into the posterior chamber.

Disruption of the BAB is the fundamental prerequisite for hypopyon formation. When inflammatory mediators, infectious agents, or neoplastic processes compromise these tight junctions, proteins, fibrin, and inflammatory cells flood into the aqueous humor, eventually settling inferiorly.

### Trabecular Meshwork

The [[trabecular meshwork]], located at the [[iridocorneal angle]], is the primary site of [[aqueous humor]] outflow. Inflammatory debris, fibrin, and cellular material associated with hypopyon can obstruct the trabecular meshwork, leading to secondary [[open-angle glaucoma]] or, in cases with extensive [[posterior synechiae]] and pupillary block, secondary [[angle-closure glaucoma]]. This mechanism of [[secondary glaucoma]] is a critical complication of hypopyon-associated inflammation.

## Pathophysiology

The formation of hypopyon involves a complex cascade of immunological and inflammatory events that culminate in the visible accumulation of leukocytic material in the anterior chamber.

### Mechanism of Formation

1. **Initiating Stimulus**: An infectious agent (bacterial, fungal, viral, or parasitic), autoimmune process, or neoplastic infiltration triggers an inflammatory response in the anterior segment or, in cases of endogenous endophthalmitis, seeds the eye hematogenously.

2. **Blood-Aqueous Barrier Breakdown**: Inflammatory mediators—including [[prostaglandins]] (particularly PGE₂), [[interleukins]] (IL-1, IL-6, IL-8), [[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] (TNF-α), and [[vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF)—cause vasodilation and increased vascular permeability in the iris and ciliary body vasculature. This leads to disruption of the tight junctions constituting the [[blood-aqueous barrier]].

3. **Cellular Influx**: [[Polymorphonuclear leukocytes]] (PMNs), predominantly [[neutrophils]], migrate from the iris and ciliary body vasculature into the aqueous humor via [[chemotaxis]]. In chronic or granulomatous inflammation, [[macrophages]], [[lymphocytes]], and [[plasma cells]] may also be present. In masquerade syndromes, the cellular component consists of neoplastic cells.

4. **Protein and Fibrin Exudation**: Plasma proteins, including [[fibrinogen]], leak into the anterior chamber and are converted to [[fibrin]] by tissue thromboplastin. Fibrin strands may form membranes across the pupil or within the anterior chamber, contributing to the structural organization of the hypopyon.

5. **Gravitational Settling**: The cellular and proteinaceous exudate, being denser than normal aqueous humor, settles inferiorly under the influence of gravity, forming the characteristic horizontally layered, meniscus-shaped collection visible behind the inferior cornea.

### Sterile vs. Infectious Hypopyon

A critical distinction in the pathophysiology of hypopyon is whether the exudate is **sterile** or **infectious**:

- **Sterile Hypopyon**: Occurs when the inflammatory exudate does not contain viable organisms. This is seen in:
  - Reactive hypopyon secondary to severe [[corneal ulcer]] (where organisms remain confined to the cornea and the anterior chamber reaction is a sterile immune response)
  - [[Behçet disease]] and other autoimmune uveitides
  - Drug-induced hypopyon (e.g., [[rifabutin]])
  - [[Toxic anterior segment syndrome]] (TASS)
  - Lens-induced ([[phacoantigenic]]) uveitis

- **Infectious Hypopyon**: Contains viable microorganisms within the anterior chamber. This is characteristic of:
  - [[Endophthalmitis]] (bacterial or fungal)
  - Direct corneal perforation with microbial invasion
  - Endogenous seeding from systemic infection

The distinction between sterile and infectious hypopyon has profound therapeutic implications, as infectious hypopyon typically requires intravitreal or intracameral antimicrobial therapy, whereas sterile hypopyon may respond to anti-inflammatory treatment alone.

### Molecular and Cellular Mediators

| **Mediator** | **Source** | **Role in Hypopyon Formation** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Prostaglandin E2]] (PGE₂) | [[Cyclooxygenase]] pathway in iris/ciliary body | Vasodilation, BAB breakdown |
| [[Interleukin-1]] (IL-1) | Macrophages, corneal epithelium | Pro-inflammatory cytokine, neutrophil recruitment |
| [[Interleukin-8]] (IL-8) | Endothelial cells, macrophages | Potent neutrophil chemoattractant |
| [[TNF-α]] | Macrophages, T-cells | Amplifies inflammatory cascade, BAB disruption |
| [[Complement]] (C3a, C5a) | Serum proteins in aqueous | Opsonization, chemotaxis, mast cell degranulation |
| [[Fibrinogen]] / [[Fibrin]] | Plasma leakage through disrupted BAB | Structural matrix of hypopyon, membrane formation |
| [[Matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs) | Neutrophils, corneal stromal cells | Tissue destruction, corneal melting |

## Etiology

### Infectious Causes

#### Bacterial Keratitis

Bacterial [[corneal ulcer]] is one of the most common causes of hypopyon in clinical practice. Organisms most frequently associated with hypopyon-forming keratitis include:

- ***[[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]]*** — Particularly common in [[contact lens]] wearers; produces aggressive, rapidly progressive ulcers with copious mucopurulent discharge and frequent hypopyon formation.
- ***[[Staphylococcus aureus]]*** — Including methicillin-resistant strains ([[MRSA]]); common in compromised corneas.
- ***[[Streptococcus pneumoniae]]*** — Classic cause of central, deep stromal ulcers with hypopyon (historically termed "ulcus serpens").
- ***[[Moraxella]]* species** — Typically indolent, oval-shaped inferior corneal ulcers in debilitated or immunocompromised patients.

The hypopyon in bacterial keratitis is typically **sterile**, representing a reactive anterior chamber inflammation rather than direct bacterial invasion of the aqueous humor, unless corneal perforation has occurred.

#### Fungal Keratitis

Fungal [[corneal ulcer]] is an important cause of hypopyon, particularly in tropical and agricultural settings. Filamentous fungi (***[[Fusarium]]*, *[[Aspergillus]]***) and yeasts (***[[Candida]]***) are the most common pathogens. Fungal keratitis characteristically presents with:

- Feathery, satellite lesions with irregular borders
- An immune ring ([[Wessely ring]])
- A hypopyon that may be disproportionately large relative to the size of the corneal infiltrate
- An **endothelial plaque** — a collection of inflammatory material adherent to the corneal endothelium, which is relatively specific for fungal infection
- Slow, insidious progression

Importantly, in fungal keratitis, the hypopyon may occasionally be **infectious** (containing viable fungal elements), particularly with *Candida* species, which can penetrate an intact [[Descemet membrane]].

#### Endophthalmitis

[[Endophthalmitis]] is an intraocular infection involving the [[vitreous humor]] and/or [[aqueous humor]], and hypopyon is one of its cardinal signs.

- **Acute postoperative endophthalmitis**: Most commonly follows [[cataract surgery]] (incidence ~0.03–0.2%). *[[Staphylococcus epidermidis]]* and other [[coagulase-negative staphylococci]] are the most frequent causative organisms. Hypopyon is present in approximately 75–86% of cases.
- **Chronic (delayed-onset) postoperative endophthalmitis**: Caused by low-virulence organisms such as *[[Cutibacterium acnes]]* (formerly *Propionibacterium acnes*) or fungi. Hypopyon may be intermittent or low-grade.
- **Post-intravitreal injection endophthalmitis**: Increasing in incidence with the widespread use of anti-[[VEGF]] agents. Organisms include streptococcal species and coagulase-negative staphylococci.
- **Post-traumatic endophthalmitis**: Follows penetrating or perforating ocular trauma. *[[Bacillus cereus]]* is a particularly devastating cause with rapid progression and poor visual prognosis.
- **Endogenous endophthalmitis**: Results from hematogenous seeding of the eye from a distant focus of infection. *[[Candida]]* species (in immunocompromised patients, IV drug users, or those with indwelling catheters) and *[[Klebsiella pneumoniae]]* (particularly in East Asian populations with hepatic abscess) are common causes.

#### Acanthamoeba Keratitis

*[[Acanthamoeba]]* keratitis, most commonly associated with [[contact lens]] wear and exposure to contaminated water, can produce a hypopyon, particularly in advanced cases. The characteristic [[ring infiltrate]] and severe [[radial keratoneuritis]] (perineural infiltration causing intense pain disproportionate to clinical findings) help distinguish this entity.

#### Viral Keratitis

Severe [[herpes simplex virus]] (HSV) or [[varicella-zoster virus]] (VZV) [[keratouveitis]] can occasionally produce a small hypopyon, though this is less common than with bacterial or fungal infections.

### Non-Infectious Causes

#### Behçet Disease

[[Behçet disease]] is a systemic vasculitis characterized by recurrent oral and genital ulcers, skin lesions, and ocular inflammation. Ocular involvement occurs in 50–70% of patients and classically manifests as a **shifting, transient hypopyon** associated with explosive, non-granulomatous [[anterior uveitis]]. The hypopyon in Behçet disease is sterile and characteristically mobile—it may shift with changes in head position, reflecting its relatively low fibrin content compared to infectious hypopyon. Behçet-associated hypopyon uveitis carries a significant risk of visual loss due to concurrent [[retinal vasculitis]], [[macular edema]], and [[optic atrophy]].

#### HLA-B27-Associated Anterior Uveitis

Severe, recurrent [[HLA-B27]]-associated [[anterior uveitis]] can produce a hypopyon, particularly during acute flares. This is a non-granulomatous uveitis with a fibrinous anterior chamber reaction, and the hypopyon is sterile. Associated systemic conditions include [[ankylosing spondylitis]], [[reactive arthritis]] (formerly Reiter syndrome), [[inflammatory bowel disease]], and [[psoriatic arthritis]].

#### Drug-Induced Hypopyon

Several medications have been associated with hypopyon formation:

- **[[Rifabutin]]**: Used in the prophylaxis and treatment of *Mycobacterium avium* complex (MAC) infection in HIV/AIDS patients. Rifabutin-associated uveitis with hypopyon is dose-dependent and typically occurs at higher doses or when co-administered with drugs that inhibit its metabolism (e.g., [[clarithromycin]], [[fluconazole]]).
- **[[Cidofovir]]**: An antiviral agent used for [[cytomegalovirus]] (CMV) retinitis; can cause anterior uveitis with hypopyon.
- **Intracameral agents**: [[Toxic anterior segment syndrome]] (TASS) following inadvertent introduction of toxic substances into the anterior chamber during surgery can produce a sterile hypopyon.

#### Lens-Induced Uveitis

[[Phacoantigenic uveitis]] (also known as lens-induced granulomatous uveitis) occurs when lens proteins are released into the anterior chamber following trauma or surgery, inciting an immune response. A hypopyon may form in severe cases, and the exudate may contain lens material and macrophages laden with phagocytosed lens particles ([[phacolytic]] cells).

### Masquerade Syndromes (Pseudohypopyon)

A **pseudohypopyon** refers to the accumulation of non-inflammatory material in the anterior chamber that mimics the appearance of a true hypopyon. Critical masquerade syndromes include:

- **[[Retinoblastoma]]**: The most common intraocular malignancy of childhood. Tumor cells may seed the anterior chamber, producing a pseudohypopyon. This diagnosis must be considered in any child presenting with hypopyon and no clear infectious or inflammatory etiology.
- **[[Intraocular lymphoma]]** (primary vitreoretinal lymphoma): Typically a [[diffuse large B-cell lymphoma]]; may present with anterior chamber cells and pseudohypopyon in adults.
- **[[Leukemia]]**: Leukemic infiltration of the anterior chamber can produce a pseudohypopyon, sometimes with a characteristic greenish hue (chloroma) in myeloid leukemias.
- **[[Juvenile xanthogranuloma]] (JXG)**: A rare cause of spontaneous [[hyphema]] and pseudohypopyon in children due to iris infiltration.
- **Anterior segment [[melanoma]]**: Pigmented tumor cells may settle inferiorly, producing a dark-colored pseudohypopyon.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

Patients with hypopyon typically present with a constellation of symptoms reflecting severe anterior segment inflammation:

| **Symptom** | **Description** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|---|
| **Pain** | Moderate to severe, deep, aching ocular pain | Reflects [[ciliary spasm]] and inflammation; may be disproportionate in *Acanthamoeba* keratitis |
| **Photophobia** | Sensitivity to light | Due to [[ciliary body]] inflammation and [[iris sphincter]] irritation |
| **Decreased vision** | Blurred or reduced [[visual acuity]] | Due to corneal edema, aqueous turbidity, and inflammatory debris |
| **Redness** | [[Ciliary flush]] (circumcorneal injection) and/or diffuse [[conjunctival injection]] | Indicates anterior segment inflammation |
| **Tearing** | [[Epiphora]] (excessive lacrimation) | Reflex tearing from corneal and uveal irritation |
| **Discharge** | Mucopurulent discharge (in infectious keratitis) | Suggests bacterial etiology |
| **Visible white layer** | Patient or observer may notice a white or yellowish layer in the lower part of the eye | Corresponds to the hypopyon itself |

### Signs

On [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]], the following signs are characteristically observed:

- **Hypopyon**: A horizontally layered, whitish-yellow collection of exudate in the inferior anterior chamber. The height of the hypopyon is measured in millimeters and documented serially to monitor progression or resolution. A hypopyon less than 1 mm may be subtle and require careful slit-lamp examination.

- **Anterior chamber cells and flare**: Graded on the Standardization of Uveitis Nomenclature (SUN) Working Group scale (0 to 4+). Cells represent inflammatory cells suspended in the aqueous, while [[flare]] represents protein leakage (Tyndall effect).

- **Fibrin**: Strands or membranes of fibrin may be visible in the anterior chamber, sometimes forming a pupillary membrane that can lead to [[seclusio pupillae]] (complete posterior synechiae with pupillary membrane) and [[iris bombé]].

- **Corneal findings** (if keratitis is the cause): [[Corneal infiltrate]], [[corneal ulcer]], [[corneal edema]], [[Descemet folds]], [[keratic precipitates]] (KPs). In fungal keratitis, satellite lesions and an endothelial plaque may be present.

- **Conjunctival injection**: Diffuse or sectoral, with or without [[chemosis]].

- **[[Posterior synechiae]]**: Adhesions between the posterior iris surface and the anterior [[lens capsule]], indicating significant inflammation.

- **Elevated or decreased [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP)**: IOP may be elevated due to trabecular meshwork obstruction by inflammatory debris, or decreased due to ciliary body shutdown ([[ciliary body]] hypotony).

- **Vitritis** (in endophthalmitis): Vitreous haze or opacification visible on [[dilated fundus examination]] or [[B-scan ultrasonography]].

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

The diagnosis of hypopyon is clinical, made by direct observation of the layered exudate in the anterior chamber. However, determining the **underlying etiology** requires a systematic diagnostic approach.

#### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] is the primary diagnostic tool. Key observations include:

- Height and color of the hypopyon (measured in mm)
- Presence of corneal pathology (infiltrate, ulcer, perforation)
- Character of keratic precipitates (fine/non-granulomatous vs. mutton-fat/granulomatous)
- Anterior chamber depth and fibrin formation
- Iris abnormalities (nodules, neovascularization, heterochromia)
- Lens status (pseudophakia, aphakia, lens opacity)
- Vitreous involvement (anterior vitreous cells)

#### Intraocular Pressure Measurement

[[Goldmann applanation tonometry]] or non-contact tonometry should be performed, with caution in cases of corneal ulceration or perforation. IOP may be elevated (inflammatory or steroid-induced glaucoma) or low (ciliary body inflammation).

#### Dilated Fundus Examination

When media clarity permits, [[dilated fundus examination]] with [[indirect ophthalmoscopy]] is essential to evaluate for:
- [[Vitritis]] (endophthalmitis)
- [[Retinal vasculitis]] (Behçet disease)
- Retinal or choroidal lesions (retinoblastoma, lymphoma, metastatic endophthalmitis)
- [[Retinal detachment]]

#### B-Scan Ultrasonography

[[B-scan ultrasonography]] is indispensable when posterior segment visualization is obscured by anterior segment inflammation, corneal opacity, or dense hypopyon. It can reveal:
- Vitreous opacities and membranes (endophthalmitis)
- Retinal detachment
- Intraocular masses (retinoblastoma, melanoma)
- Choroidal thickening or effusion

### Laboratory Investigations

#### Corneal Scraping and Culture

In cases of suspected infectious [[keratitis]], corneal scraping is performed for:

| **Investigation** | **Purpose** | **Details** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Gram stain]] | Rapid identification of bacteria | Differentiates gram-positive from gram-negative organisms |
| [[Giemsa stain]] | Morphological assessment | Useful for fungi, *Acanthamoeba* cysts, and inflammatory cells |
| [[KOH preparation]] (10% KOH) | Fungal identification | Dissolves cellular debris, revealing fungal hyphae |
| [[Calcofluor white]] stain | Fungal and *Acanthamoeba* detection | Fluorescent stain binding to chitin and cellulose |
| Culture (blood agar, chocolate agar, Sabouraud dextrose agar, brain-heart infusion broth) | Organism identification and sensitivity | Gold standard for microbiological diagnosis |
| [[Polymerase chain reaction]] (PCR) | Molecular identification | Highly sensitive; useful for atypical organisms, viruses, and *Acanthamoeba* |

#### Aqueous and Vitreous Sampling

In suspected [[endophthalmitis]], **anterior chamber paracentesis** (aqueous tap) and/or **vitreous tap or biopsy** are performed for:
- Gram stain and culture
- Fungal stain and culture
- PCR (bacterial 16S rRNA, fungal 18S/28S rRNA, viral DNA)
- Cytology (in suspected masquerade syndromes)

The **Endophthalmitis Vitrectomy Study (EVS)** established that vitreous cultures have a higher yield (approximately 70%) compared to aqueous cultures (approximately 40%) in postoperative endophthalmitis.

#### Systemic Workup

When a non-infectious or systemic etiology is suspected, the following investigations may be warranted:

| **Suspected Etiology** | **Investigations** |
|---|---|
| [[Behçet disease]] | [[Pathergy test]], [[HLA-B51]], ESR, CRP; clinical criteria (International Study Group) |
| [[HLA-B27]] uveitis | [[HLA-B27]] typing, sacroiliac joint radiography, ESR, CRP |
| [[Sarcoidosis]] | Chest X-ray, serum [[ACE]] level, serum [[lysozyme]], chest CT, biopsy |
| Endogenous endophthalmitis | Blood cultures, echocardiography, abdominal imaging, urinalysis |
| Masquerade syndrome | Aqueous/vitreous cytology, flow cytometry, [[IL-10]]/[[IL-6]] ratio, systemic imaging |
| [[Tuberculosis]] | [[Tuberculin skin test]] (Mantoux), [[QuantiFERON-TB Gold]], chest X-ray |

### Anterior Chamber Paracentesis Technique

[[Anterior chamber paracentesis]] is a critical diagnostic (and occasionally therapeutic) procedure in the evaluation of hypopyon. The technique involves:

1. Topical anesthesia with [[proparacaine]] or [[tetracaine]]
2. Aseptic preparation with [[povidone-iodine]] 5%
3. Insertion of a 27- or 30-gauge needle on a tuberculin syringe through the peripheral cornea (limbal approach)
4. Aspiration of 0.1–0.2 mL of aqueous humor
5. Immediate inoculation onto culture media and preparation of smears

This procedure carries a small risk of complications including [[hyphema]], lens damage, and [[corneal decompensation]], and should be performed by experienced clinicians.

## Differential Diagnosis

The differential diagnosis of hypopyon includes all conditions that can produce a layered collection of material in the anterior chamber:

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| Infectious keratitis with reactive hypopyon | Corneal infiltrate/ulcer present; hypopyon typically sterile |
| [[Endophthalmitis]] | Vitritis, pain, decreased red reflex; recent surgery or injection |
| [[Behçet disease]] | Shifting hypopyon, oral/genital ulcers, retinal vasculitis |
| [[HLA-B27]] uveitis | Recurrent, unilateral, fibrinous; associated spondyloarthropathy |
| [[Retinoblastoma]] (pseudohypopyon) | Child; leukocoria, strabismus; calcification on imaging |
| [[Intraocular lymphoma]] (pseudohypopyon) | Older adult; vitreous cells, subretinal infiltrates |
| [[Hyphema]] | Red blood cells (not white); layered blood in anterior chamber |
| [[Toxic anterior segment syndrome]] (TASS) | Onset within 24 hours of surgery; diffuse corneal edema; sterile |
| [[Phacoantigenic uveitis]] | History of trauma or surgery with lens disruption |

## Grading and Classification

Hypopyon is typically graded by its **height** in millimeters, measured at the slit lamp:

| **Grade** | **Height** | **Clinical Implication** |
|---|---|---|
| Trace | < 0.5 mm | Mild; may be difficult to detect without careful examination |
| Mild | 0.5–1.0 mm | Moderate inflammation; close monitoring required |
| Moderate | 1.0–2.0 mm | Significant inflammation; aggressive treatment indicated |
| Severe | > 2.0 mm | Severe inflammation; consider infectious etiology or endophthalmitis |
| Total (filling) | Fills entire anterior chamber | Emergent; may indicate endophthalmitis or severe keratitis |

Serial measurement of hypopyon height is essential for monitoring treatment response.

## Management

### General Principles

Management of hypopyon is directed at:

1. **Identifying and treating the underlying cause** (infectious vs. non-infectious vs. masquerade)
2. **Controlling inflammation** to prevent structural damage
3. **Preventing complications** such as [[posterior synechiae]], [[secondary glaucoma]], [[corneal decompensation]], and [[phthisis bulbi]]
4. **Preserving or restoring visual function**

### Pharmacological Treatment

#### Antimicrobial Therapy (for Infectious Etiologies)

**Bacterial Keratitis with Hypopyon:**

| **Agent** | **Concentration** | **Regimen** | **Coverage** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Fortified cefazolin]] (or vancomycin) | 50 mg/mL (or 25–50 mg/mL) | Every 30–60 minutes initially | Gram-positive organisms |
| [[Fortified tobramycin]] (or gentamicin) | 14 mg/mL (or 9–14 mg/mL) | Every 30–60 minutes initially | Gram-negative organisms |
| [[Fluoroquinolone]] (moxifloxacin, gatifloxacin) | 0.5% | Every 30–60 minutes initially (monotherapy option) | Broad-spectrum |

Fortified topical antibiotics are the mainstay of treatment for bacterial keratitis with hypopyon. Dual therapy with a fortified [[cephalosporin]] (or [[glycopeptide]]) and a fortified [[aminoglycoside]] provides broad-spectrum coverage pending culture results. Fourth-generation [[fluoroquinolones]] ([[moxifloxacin]], [[gatifloxacin]]) may be used as monotherapy for less severe cases.

**Fungal Keratitis with Hypopyon:**

| **Agent** | **Route** | **Coverage** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Natamycin]] 5% | Topical (every 1–2 hours) | Filamentous fungi (first-line) |
| [[Voriconazole]] 1% | Topical (every 1–2 hours) | Broad-spectrum antifungal |
| [[Amphotericin B]] 0.15–0.25% | Topical | Yeasts (*Candida*); some filamentous fungi |
| [[Oral voriconazole]] | Systemic (200–400 mg BID) | Adjunctive for deep or severe infections |
| Intrastromal/intracameral voriconazole | Injection | Refractory cases |

**Endophthalmitis:**

Based on the landmark **[[Endophthalmitis Vitrectomy Study]] (EVS)** and subsequent evidence:

| **Agent** | **Route** | **Dose** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Vancomycin]] | Intravitreal | 1.0 mg / 0.1 mL |
| [[Ceftazidime]] | Intravitreal | 2.25 mg / 0.1 mL |
| [[Amphotericin B]] (if fungal suspected) | Intravitreal | 5–10 µg / 0.1 mL |
| [[Voriconazole]] (if fungal suspected) | Intravitreal | 100 µg / 0.1 mL |
| Fortified topical antibiotics | Topical | As above |
| Systemic antibiotics | Oral/IV | Case-dependent (e.g., oral moxifloxacin, IV vancomycin) |

The EVS demonstrated that immediate [[pars plana vitrectomy]] (PPV) was beneficial for patients presenting with light perception (LP) only vision, while tap-and-inject was equivalent to vitrectomy for patients with better than LP vision in acute postoperative endophthalmitis.

#### Anti-Inflammatory Therapy

**[[Corticosteroids]]:**

Topical corticosteroids are a cornerstone of managing the inflammatory component of hypopyon, but their use must be carefully timed and monitored:

- **[[Prednisolone acetate]] 1%**: The most commonly used topical corticosteroid; dosed every 1–2 hours initially, with gradual taper based on clinical response.
- **[[Dexamethasone]] 0.1%**: Alternative topical steroid.
- **Periocular corticosteroids** (sub-Tenon [[triamcinolone acetonide]] 40 mg): For severe, non-infectious uveitis with hypopyon.
- **Systemic corticosteroids** (oral [[prednisone]] 1 mg/kg/day): For severe non-infectious uveitis (e.g., Behçet disease) or as adjunctive therapy in endophthalmitis (controversial).
- **Intravitreal [[dexamethasone]]** (400 µg / 0.1 mL): May be used adjunctively in endophthalmitis (evidence from post-EVS studies is mixed).

> **CRITICAL CAUTION**: Corticosteroids must **never** be initiated before ruling out an infectious etiology or before appropriate antimicrobial coverage is established. Premature or inappropriate corticosteroid use in infectious hypopyon can lead to catastrophic worsening of infection, corneal perforation, and loss of the eye.

**[[Cycloplegic]] / [[Mydriatic]] Agents:**

| **Agent** | **Concentration** | **Duration of Action** | **Purpose** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Atropine sulfate]] | 1% | 7–14 days | Cycloplegia, pain relief, prevention of posterior synechiae |
| [[Homatropine]] | 2–5% | 1–3 days | Moderate cycloplegia |
| [[Cyclopentolate]] | 1% | 24 hours | Short-acting cycloplegia |

[[Cycloplegic agents]] serve multiple purposes in hypopyon management:
- **Pain relief**: By paralyzing the [[ciliary muscle]] and relieving ciliary spasm
- **Prevention of [[posterior synechiae]]**: By dilating the pupil and keeping the iris mobile
- **Stabilization of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]]**: By reducing vascular permeability

Atropine 1% is the preferred agent in severe inflammation due to its prolonged duration of action.

#### Immunomodulatory Therapy (for Non-Infectious Etiologies)

For recurrent or refractory non-infectious hypopyon (e.g., Behçet disease):

- **[[Azathioprine]]**: First-line steroid-sparing agent for Behçet uveitis
- **[[Cyclosporine A]]**: Calcineurin inhibitor; effective in Behçet disease
- **[[Mycophenolate mofetil]]**: Alternative immunosuppressant
- **[[Infliximab]]** or **[[Adalimumab]]**: Anti-TNF-α biologic agents; highly effective for refractory Behçet uveitis with hypopyon. Adalimumab is FDA-approved for non-infectious intermediate, posterior, and panuveitis.
- **[[Interferon-alpha]]**: Used in some centers for Behçet disease

#### IOP-Lowering Agents

If [[secondary glaucoma]] develops due to trabecular meshwork obstruction or pupillary block:

- **Topical [[beta-blockers]]** (e.g., [[timolol]] 0.5%)
- **Topical [[alpha-agonists]]** (e.g., [[brimonidine]] 0.2%)
- **Oral or IV [[acetazolamide]]** (for acute IOP elevation)
- **Topical [[dorzolamide]]** or oral [[methazolamide]]
- **Avoid [[prostaglandin analogs]]** in active uveitis (may exacerbate inflammation)
- **Avoid [[pilocarpine]]** (may worsen posterior synechiae and increase inflammation)

### Surgical Management

#### Anterior Chamber Washout

In cases of severe, non-resolving hypopyon with organized fibrin membranes, pupillary block, or secondary glaucoma unresponsive to medical therapy, **anterior chamber washout** may be necessary. This involves irrigating the anterior chamber with balanced salt solution ([[BSS]]) through paracentesis ports to mechanically remove the inflammatory debris and fibrin.

#### Pars Plana Vitrectomy (PPV)

[[Pars plana vitrectomy]] is indicated in:
- Endophthalmitis with LP-only vision (per EVS guidelines)
- Worsening endophthalmitis despite tap-and-inject
- Fungal endophthalmitis (lower threshold for vitrectomy due to poor response to intravitreal antifungals alone)
- Diagnostic vitrectomy for suspected masquerade syndromes
- Retained intraocular foreign body with endophthalmitis

#### Therapeutic Keratoplasty

**Therapeutic [[penetrating keratoplasty]]** may be required in cases of:
- Corneal perforation secondary to infectious keratitis with hypopyon
- Progressive corneal melting unresponsive to maximal medical therapy
- Impending perforation with descemetocele formation

This procedure serves both a structural (tectonic) and therapeutic purpose by removing the infected corneal tissue and replacing it with donor tissue.

#### Evisceration or Enucleation

In devastating cases where the eye is blind, painful, and non-salvageable (e.g., panophthalmitis), **[[evisceration]]** (removal of intraocular contents while preserving the scleral shell) or **[[enucleation]]** (removal of the entire globe) may be necessary as a last resort. Enucleation is preferred when an intraocular malignancy (e.g., retinoblastoma) cannot be excluded.

## Complications

Hypopyon, if inadequately treated, can lead to numerous sight-threatening and globe-threatening complications:

| **Complication** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|
| [[Posterior synechiae]] | Adhesion of iris to lens capsule due to fibrin and inflammation |
| [[Seclusio pupillae]] | 360° posterior synechiae with pupillary membrane |
| [[Iris bombé]] | Aqueous trapped behind iris due to seclusio; secondary angle closure |
| [[Secondary glaucoma]] | Trabecular obstruction, pupillary block, or peripheral anterior synechiae |
| [[Corneal decompensation]] | Endothelial cell loss from inflammation and hypopyon contact |
| [[Band keratopathy]] | Chronic inflammation leading to calcium deposition in Bowman layer |
| [[Cataract]] (complicated) | Chronic inflammation and/or corticosteroid use |
| [[Cystoid macular edema]] | Chronic inflammation affecting the macula |
| [[Phthisis bulbi]] | End-stage globe shrinkage from chronic inflammation and ciliary body destruction |
| [[Corneal perforation]] | Progressive stromal melting in infectious keratitis |
| [[Retinal detachment]] | Tractional or exudative, particularly in endophthalmitis |

---

## Prognosis

The visual prognosis of hypopyon depends critically on the underlying etiology, the timeliness of diagnosis, and the adequacy of treatment:

- **Bacterial keratitis with sterile hypopyon**: Generally favorable with prompt, appropriate antibiotic therapy; visual outcome depends on the location and extent of corneal scarring.
- **Fungal keratitis with hypopyon**: Poorer prognosis than bacterial keratitis due to delayed diagnosis, limited antifungal penetration, and higher rates of perforation.
- **Acute postoperative endophthalmitis**: Visual outcomes range widely; the EVS reported that approximately 53% of patients achieved 20/40 or better, and 15% had worse than 5/200.
- **Endogenous endophthalmitis**: Generally poor prognosis, particularly with virulent organisms (*Bacillus*, *Aspergillus*).
- **Behçet disease**: Prognosis has improved significantly with biologic therapy, but recurrent episodes can lead to cumulative damage.
- **Masquerade syndromes**: Prognosis depends on the underlying malignancy and systemic disease status.

---

## Special Considerations in Pediatric Patients

Hypopyon in children warrants particular vigilance due to the possibility of [[retinoblastoma]] presenting as a pseudohypopyon. Any child presenting with anterior chamber inflammation and hypopyon without a clear infectious or traumatic etiology should undergo urgent [[dilated fundus examination]], [[B-scan ultrasonography]], and, if indicated, examination under anesthesia (EUA) with imaging to rule out intraocular malignancy. Other pediatric considerations include [[juvenile idiopathic arthritis]] (JIA)-associated uveitis (though hypopyon is uncommon in JIA) and [[endogenous endophthalmitis]] in neonates with sepsis.

---

## Summary

Hypopyon is a critical clinical sign in ophthalmology and optometry that demands immediate and thorough evaluation. Its presence indicates severe disruption of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]] and may herald sight-threatening or life-threatening conditions ranging from infectious keratitis and endophthalmitis to systemic vasculitis and intraocular malignancy. A systematic approach to diagnosis—incorporating detailed history, comprehensive slit-lamp examination, appropriate microbiological sampling, and targeted systemic investigation—is essential. Management is etiology-specific and may involve intensive topical and intravitreal antimicrobials, anti-inflammatory agents, immunomodulatory therapy, and surgical intervention. Early recognition and aggressive, targeted treatment are the cornerstones of preserving visual function in patients presenting with hypopyon.
