

# Retinal Detachment

**Retinal Detachment** (RD) is a sight-threatening ocular emergency characterized by the separation of the [[neurosensory retina]] from the underlying [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE). This separation disrupts the metabolic support provided by the RPE and [[choroidal vasculature]] to the photoreceptors, leading to progressive photoreceptor degeneration and, if untreated, permanent vision loss. Retinal detachment constitutes one of the most urgent conditions encountered in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] practice, demanding prompt recognition, accurate classification, and timely referral for definitive management.

The annual incidence of rhegmatogenous retinal detachment (the most common subtype) is approximately 6.3–17.9 per 100,000 population, with a lifetime risk estimated at roughly 1 in 300. Understanding the pathophysiology, risk factors, clinical presentation, diagnostic modalities, and treatment strategies for retinal detachment is essential for every eye care practitioner.

---

## Anatomy and Physiology of the Retina Relevant to Detachment

A thorough understanding of retinal detachment requires appreciation of the anatomical relationships that maintain retinal attachment under physiological conditions.

The [[retina]] is a multilayered neurosensory tissue lining the inner posterior segment of the eye. It is embryologically derived from the [[optic cup]], which gives rise to two layers: the inner [[neurosensory retina]] (comprising nine layers from the [[internal limiting membrane]] to the photoreceptor layer) and the outer [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE). Between these two structures lies a potential space—the **subretinal space**—which is a remnant of the embryonic [[optic vesicle]] cavity.

Under normal conditions, retinal attachment is maintained by several mechanisms:

| **Mechanism** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Interphotoreceptor matrix (IPM)** | A glycosaminoglycan-rich extracellular matrix that provides adhesive bonding between photoreceptor outer segments and RPE microvilli |
| **RPE metabolic pump** | Active transport of fluid from the subretinal space to the [[choriocapillaris]] via RPE ion channels, maintaining a relative dehydration of the subretinal space |
| **Intraocular pressure (IOP)** | The positive [[intraocular pressure]] exerts a tamponade force pressing the neurosensory retina against the RPE |
| **Interdigitation of outer segments** | Physical interdigitation of photoreceptor outer segments with RPE apical processes |
| **Vitreous body** | The intact [[vitreous humor]] provides structural support to the retina, particularly at the [[vitreous base]] |

The retina is firmly attached at only two locations: the [[ora serrata]] (via the vitreous base) and the [[optic disc]] margin. Elsewhere, the neurosensory retina is held in apposition to the RPE primarily by the mechanisms listed above, making it inherently vulnerable to detachment when these forces are disrupted.

## Classification

Retinal detachment is classified into three principal types based on the underlying pathogenic mechanism:

### 1. Rhegmatogenous Retinal Detachment (RRD)

**Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment** (from the Greek *rhegma*, meaning "break" or "rent") is the most common form, accounting for approximately 80–90% of all retinal detachments. It occurs when a full-thickness break in the neurosensory retina allows liquefied [[vitreous humor]] to pass through the defect and accumulate in the subretinal space, thereby separating the neurosensory retina from the RPE.

The pathogenesis of RRD requires three elements:
1. **A retinal break** (tear, hole, or dialysis)
2. **Liquefied vitreous** capable of passing through the break
3. **Tractional or gravitational forces** that promote fluid entry into the subretinal space

### 2. Tractional Retinal Detachment (TRD)

**Tractional retinal detachment** results from mechanical pulling on the retina by fibrovascular or fibrocellular membranes in the [[vitreous cavity]] or on the retinal surface. Unlike RRD, there is no primary retinal break (although a combined tractional-rhegmatogenous detachment can occur). TRD is most commonly associated with:

- [[Proliferative diabetic retinopathy]] (PDR) — the most common cause
- [[Retinopathy of prematurity]] (ROP)
- [[Proliferative vitreoretinopathy]] (PVR)
- Penetrating ocular trauma
- [[Sickle cell retinopathy]]

### 3. Exudative (Serous) Retinal Detachment

**Exudative retinal detachment** occurs when fluid accumulates in the subretinal space due to hydrostatic, osmotic, or inflammatory mechanisms, without a retinal break or vitreoretinal traction. Causes include:

- [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]] (VKH)
- [[Central serous chorioretinopathy]] (CSCR)
- [[Posterior scleritis]]
- Choroidal tumors (e.g., [[choroidal melanoma]], [[choroidal metastasis]], [[choroidal hemangioma]])
- Severe [[pre-eclampsia]] / [[eclampsia]]
- [[Coats disease]]
- [[Uveal effusion syndrome]]

| **Feature** | **Rhegmatogenous** | **Tractional** | **Exudative** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Retinal break present** | Yes | No (unless combined) | No |
| **Vitreoretinal traction** | May be present (initiating break) | Primary mechanism | Absent |
| **Subretinal fluid source** | Liquefied vitreous | Transudation | Exudation / transudation |
| **Retinal surface** | Corrugated, mobile | Concave, taut, immobile | Smooth, convex, shifting |
| **Shifting fluid** | Uncommon | No | Yes (gravity-dependent) |
| **Most common cause** | PVD with retinal tear | Proliferative diabetic retinopathy | Choroidal tumors, inflammatory disease |

## Pathophysiology

### Rhegmatogenous Retinal Detachment

The pathophysiology of RRD is intimately linked to the aging process of the [[vitreous body]]. The vitreous is a transparent gel composed of approximately 98% water, [[type II collagen]] fibrils, and [[hyaluronic acid]] (hyaluronan). With age, the vitreous undergoes two concurrent degenerative processes:

1. **Liquefaction (synchysis):** Enzymatic and oxidative degradation of hyaluronic acid and collagen cross-links leads to the formation of fluid-filled lacunae within the vitreous gel.
2. **Syneresis and collapse:** Aggregation of collagen fibrils and progressive liquefaction cause the vitreous gel to contract and collapse.

These processes culminate in **[[posterior vitreous detachment]]** (PVD), in which the posterior [[vitreous cortex]] separates from the [[internal limiting membrane]] (ILM) of the retina. PVD is a normal age-related event occurring in approximately 65% of individuals over age 65. However, when abnormal vitreoretinal adhesions exist—particularly at areas of [[lattice degeneration]], retinal tufts, or the vitreous base—the separating vitreous exerts dynamic traction on the retina, potentially creating a full-thickness retinal tear.

Once a retinal break is formed, the following sequence ensues:

1. Liquefied vitreous gains access to the subretinal space through the break.
2. The RPE metabolic pump is overwhelmed by the rate of fluid influx.
3. The neurosensory retina progressively separates from the RPE.
4. Photoreceptor outer segments, deprived of their RPE-mediated metabolic support (phagocytosis of shed outer segment discs, retinoid recycling via the [[visual cycle]], nutrient transport), begin to degenerate.
5. If the [[macula]] detaches, central visual acuity is severely compromised.
6. Prolonged detachment leads to photoreceptor apoptosis, [[outer nuclear layer]] thinning, [[gliosis]], and ultimately irreversible structural and functional damage.

#### Types of Retinal Breaks

| **Type of Break** | **Description** | **Common Location** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Horseshoe (flap) tear** | U-shaped tear with an anterior flap of retina pulled by vitreous traction | Superotemporal quadrant (most common) | Dynamic vitreoretinal traction during acute PVD |
| **Operculated hole** | Round hole with a free-floating operculum (avulsed retinal plug) in the vitreous | Variable | Vitreous traction avulses a piece of retina; traction is relieved |
| **Atrophic round hole** | Small round hole without an operculum, typically in areas of lattice degeneration | Peripheral retina, often inferotemporal | Chronic atrophy of thinned retina |
| **Retinal dialysis** | Circumferential tear at or near the [[ora serrata]] | Inferotemporal (spontaneous); supernasal (traumatic) | Trauma or idiopathic; common in young patients |
| **Giant retinal tear** | Tear extending ≥90° (3 clock hours) of the retinal circumference | Variable | Trauma, high myopia, hereditary vitreoretinopathies |

### Tractional Retinal Detachment

In TRD, the proliferation of fibrovascular or fibrocellular tissue on the retinal surface or within the vitreous cavity generates tangential or anteroposterior traction that mechanically pulls the retina away from the RPE. In [[proliferative diabetic retinopathy]], retinal ischemia triggers upregulation of [[vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF) and other cytokines, promoting [[neovascularization]] at the [[optic disc]] (NVD) or elsewhere (NVE). These new vessels grow along the posterior hyaloid face and become scaffolded by fibrous tissue. Contraction of this fibrovascular complex exerts traction on the underlying retina, leading to TRD. The detachment typically begins in areas of strongest vitreoretinal adhesion and may be localized or extensive.

### Exudative Retinal Detachment

Exudative RD results from the accumulation of subretinal fluid due to disruption of the [[blood-retinal barrier]] (either inner or outer), increased hydrostatic pressure in the choroidal vasculature, or decreased oncotic pressure. There is no retinal break and no significant vitreoretinal traction. The subretinal fluid is often protein-rich and may shift with changes in patient position (shifting fluid), a hallmark clinical feature.

## Risk Factors

### Risk Factors for Rhegmatogenous Retinal Detachment

| **Risk Factor** | **Relative Risk / Details** |
|---|---|
| **[[Posterior vitreous detachment]]** | Most important precipitating event; ~10–15% of acute symptomatic PVDs are associated with retinal tears |
| **[[Myopia]] (especially high myopia > −6.00 D)** | 4–10× increased risk; elongated axial length, thinner retina, increased prevalence of lattice degeneration |
| **[[Lattice degeneration]]** | Present in 6–8% of the general population; found in ~30–40% of eyes with RRD |
| **Previous retinal detachment in the fellow eye** | 10–15% lifetime risk of RD in the fellow eye |
| **Family history of retinal detachment** | Increased risk, particularly in families with connective tissue disorders |
| **Previous [[cataract surgery]] (aphakia/pseudophakia)** | Aphakic eyes: ~2–3% risk; pseudophakic eyes: ~0.5–1.5% risk; risk increases with complicated surgery, posterior capsule rupture, vitreous loss |
| **Ocular trauma** | Blunt or penetrating trauma can cause retinal dialysis, giant retinal tears, or direct retinal breaks |
| **Age** | Peak incidence in the 6th–7th decades (coinciding with PVD) and in the 2nd–3rd decades (myopia-related) |
| **Male sex** | Males have a slightly higher incidence than females |
| **[[Marfan syndrome]], [[Stickler syndrome]], [[Ehlers-Danlos syndrome]]** | Hereditary connective tissue disorders associated with vitreous abnormalities and increased RD risk |
| **[[Retinal detachment]] in the fellow eye** | Significant risk factor for bilateral involvement |
| **Nd:YAG [[laser capsulotomy]]** | Slightly increased risk of RD following posterior capsulotomy |

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

The symptomatology of retinal detachment varies depending on the type, extent, and location of the detachment, as well as whether the [[macula]] is involved.

#### Prodromal Symptoms (Associated with Acute PVD)

- **[[Photopsia]]** (flashes of light): Caused by mechanical stimulation of the retina by vitreous traction. Typically described as brief, arc-like flashes in the temporal visual field, more noticeable in dim illumination. Photopsias may persist for weeks after PVD onset.
- **[[Floaters]]**: Sudden onset of new floaters, often described as a "shower of spots," "cobwebs," or a "swarm of gnats." These may represent:
  - Condensed vitreous collagen (benign)
  - [[Vitreous hemorrhage]] (Shafer's sign / "tobacco dust" — pigmented cells in the anterior vitreous, pathognomonic for a retinal tear)
  - Avulsed retinal tissue (operculum)

#### Symptoms of Retinal Detachment

- **Visual field defect**: Patients classically describe a "curtain," "veil," or "shadow" encroaching on their peripheral vision. The location of the field defect corresponds to the area of detached retina (e.g., a superior detachment produces an inferior field defect).
- **Decreased visual acuity**: Occurs when the detachment involves or threatens the [[macula]]. Macula-on (mac-on) detachments may preserve central acuity initially, whereas macula-off (mac-off) detachments present with significant central vision loss.
- **Metamorphopsia**: Distortion of central vision may occur with macular involvement or macular edema.
- **Relative afferent pupillary defect ([[RAPD]])**: May be present in extensive retinal detachments involving a large area of the retina.

#### Asymptomatic Retinal Detachment

Chronic, slowly progressive retinal detachments (particularly inferior RRDs from atrophic holes or exudative detachments) may be asymptomatic and discovered incidentally during routine [[dilated fundus examination]].

### Signs

#### Anterior Segment Findings

- **Decreased [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP):** The affected eye often has an IOP 2–5 mmHg lower than the fellow eye due to increased uveoscleral outflow and decreased aqueous production from associated low-grade inflammation.
- **Anterior chamber cells and flare:** Mild [[anterior uveitis]] may be present, particularly in longstanding detachments.
- **[[Shafer's sign]] (tobacco dust):** Pigmented cells (melanin granules from disrupted RPE) visible in the anterior vitreous on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]. This finding is highly suggestive of a retinal tear and has a positive predictive value of approximately 70–95% for retinal break.

#### Posterior Segment Findings

- **Elevated, undulating retina:** In RRD, the detached retina appears as a translucent, corrugated, mobile membrane that undulates with eye movements. The subretinal fluid is typically clear.
- **Retinal break(s):** Identification of the causative break(s) is critical. Horseshoe tears appear as red, U-shaped defects with a flap; round holes appear as small, well-defined red spots; dialyses appear as circumferential breaks at the ora serrata.
- **Vitreous hemorrhage:** May obscure the fundus view; associated with tearing of retinal blood vessels during break formation.
- **Demarcation line (high-water mark):** A pigmented line at the posterior border of a chronic retinal detachment, representing RPE hyperplasia at the junction of attached and detached retina. Indicates chronicity (typically present after ≥3 months).
- **Intraretinal cysts / retinal thinning:** Seen in chronic detachments.
- **[[Proliferative vitreoretinopathy]] (PVR):** The most common cause of failure of retinal detachment surgery. Characterized by the proliferation and contraction of cellular membranes on both surfaces of the detached retina and on the posterior vitreous face. PVR is graded using the updated Retina Society classification (Grades A, B, C [anterior and posterior]).

| **PVR Grade** | **Clinical Features** |
|---|---|
| **Grade A** | Vitreous haze, vitreous pigment clumps, pigment clusters on inferior retina |
| **Grade B** | Wrinkling of inner retinal surface, retinal stiffness, vessel tortuosity, rolled edges of retinal break, decreased vitreous mobility |
| **Grade C (CP/CA)** | Full-thickness fixed retinal folds; CP = posterior to equator; CA = anterior to equator. Subclassified by number of clock hours involved |

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical Examination

- **[[Dilated fundus examination]]:** The cornerstone of retinal detachment diagnosis. Performed using [[binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy]] (BIO) with a condensing lens (typically +20 D or +28 D) and [[scleral indentation]] to visualize the peripheral retina, ora serrata, and pars plana. Scleral depression is essential for identifying small peripheral breaks, lattice degeneration, and dialyses.
- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] with fundus contact or non-contact lenses:** A +78 D, +90 D, or [[Goldmann three-mirror contact lens]] provides high-magnification stereoscopic views of the posterior pole and peripheral retina. The Goldmann lens is particularly valuable for examining the retinal periphery and identifying retinal breaks.
- **[[Visual acuity]] testing:** Establishes baseline acuity and helps determine macular status (mac-on vs. mac-off).
- **[[Pupillary examination]]:** Assessment for RAPD.
- **[[Intraocular pressure]] measurement:** Typically reduced in the affected eye.

### Imaging

#### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT) provides high-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the retina and is invaluable in the evaluation of retinal detachment:

- **Macular OCT:** Determines macular attachment status (mac-on vs. mac-off), identifies subclinical subretinal fluid, and detects associated macular pathology (e.g., [[epiretinal membrane]], [[macular hole]], [[cystoid macular edema]]).
- **Peripheral OCT / Wide-field OCT:** Newer swept-source OCT platforms can image the peripheral retina and may help identify shallow peripheral detachments or retinoschisis.
- **Postoperative monitoring:** OCT is essential for monitoring anatomical outcomes after retinal detachment repair, including the resolution of subretinal fluid, restoration of the [[ellipsoid zone]] (photoreceptor integrity line), and detection of persistent or recurrent detachment.

#### B-Scan Ultrasonography

[[B-scan ultrasonography]] is critical when the fundus view is obscured by media opacities (e.g., dense [[vitreous hemorrhage]], [[cataract]], [[corneal opacity]]):

- The detached retina appears as a highly reflective, mobile membrane attached at the [[optic disc]] and [[ora serrata]].
- Differentiates retinal detachment from [[posterior vitreous detachment]], [[vitreous hemorrhage]], and [[choroidal detachment]].
- Can identify associated findings such as choroidal tumors underlying an exudative detachment.
- **Kinetic (dynamic) B-scan:** Assesses retinal mobility; a stiff, immobile retina suggests PVR or tractional detachment.

#### Fundus Photography and Ultra-Widefield Imaging

- **[[Ultra-widefield fundus imaging]]** (e.g., Optos): Captures up to 200° of the retina in a single image, providing excellent documentation of the extent of detachment, location of breaks, and areas of lattice degeneration. Useful for monitoring and patient education.
- **[[Fundus autofluorescence]] (FAF):** May reveal RPE changes associated with chronic detachment (hypo- or hyperautofluorescence).

#### Fluorescein Angiography (FA) and Indocyanine Green Angiography (ICGA)

- **[[Fluorescein angiography]]:** Not routinely used for RRD but may be helpful in differentiating exudative from rhegmatogenous detachment, identifying choroidal tumors, or evaluating for conditions such as [[central serous chorioretinopathy]] or [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]].
- **[[Indocyanine green angiography]]:** Useful for evaluating choroidal pathology underlying exudative detachments.

#### Visual Field Testing

- **[[Goldmann perimetry]] or automated perimetry:** Can document the extent of visual field loss corresponding to the area of detachment. Useful for medicolegal documentation and monitoring recovery.

## Differential Diagnosis

Several conditions may mimic retinal detachment and must be considered in the differential diagnosis:

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| **[[Retinoschisis]]** | Splitting of retinal layers (typically outer plexiform layer in degenerative retinoschisis); smooth, dome-shaped elevation; absolute scotoma on visual field testing; thin, immobile inner wall; no pigmented cells in vitreous |
| **[[Choroidal detachment]]** | Smooth, dome-shaped, dark brown elevations; do not extend to the optic disc; transillumination may be positive; associated with hypotony, inflammation, or surgery |
| **[[Choroidal melanoma]] / tumor** | Solid, dome-shaped or mushroom-shaped mass; intrinsic vasculature on FA/ICGA; characteristic ultrasonographic features (low-medium internal reflectivity, acoustic hollowing) |
| **[[Posterior vitreous detachment]]** | Weiss ring visible; no retinal elevation; vitreous membrane visible on B-scan but not attached at disc and ora |
| **[[Vitreous hemorrhage]]** | Obscured fundus view; no retinal elevation on B-scan (unless concurrent RD) |

## Management

### Urgency and Triage

The urgency of retinal detachment management depends critically on macular status:

| **Macular Status** | **Urgency** | **Rationale** |
|---|---|---|
| **Macula-on (mac-on) RD** | **Surgical emergency** — repair within 24–72 hours (ideally within 24 hours) | Preventing macular detachment preserves central visual acuity; visual prognosis is significantly better if the macula remains attached |
| **Macula-off (mac-off) RD — recent (≤7 days)** | **Urgent** — repair within 7–10 days | Earlier repair associated with better visual outcomes; photoreceptor degeneration is progressive but partially reversible in the early period |
| **Macula-off RD — chronic (>7 days)** | **Semi-urgent** — repair within 1–2 weeks | Visual prognosis is guarded; structural damage may be irreversible, but anatomical reattachment is still beneficial |

**Pre-operative positioning:** For mac-on detachments with superior breaks, patients may be instructed to position themselves so that the break is in the most dependent position (e.g., upright positioning for inferior breaks) to slow the progression of subretinal fluid toward the macula while awaiting surgery.

### Prophylactic Treatment of Retinal Breaks

Not all retinal breaks require treatment. The decision to treat is based on the type of break, symptoms, and risk factors:

- **Symptomatic horseshoe tears:** High risk of progression to RRD (~30–50%); **treatment is strongly recommended** with [[laser photocoagulation]] (retinopexy) or [[cryopexy]].
- **Symptomatic operculated holes:** Lower risk (~2–5%); treatment is generally recommended, especially if associated with vitreous hemorrhage or subclinical subretinal fluid.
- **Asymptomatic horseshoe tears:** Moderate risk; treatment is often recommended, particularly in phakic eyes, fellow eyes of RD patients, or eyes scheduled for cataract surgery.
- **Asymptomatic atrophic round holes:** Low risk (~0.05–0.3% per year); observation is generally appropriate unless associated with subclinical subretinal fluid or other high-risk features.
- **Asymptomatic lattice degeneration without holes:** Very low risk of RD; prophylactic treatment is **not** routinely recommended in the general population but may be considered in high-risk patients (e.g., fellow eye of RD, high myopia with family history).

### Surgical Treatment of Rhegmatogenous Retinal Detachment

#### 1. Pneumatic Retinopexy (PR)

**Pneumatic retinopexy** is an office-based procedure suitable for selected uncomplicated RRDs:

**Indications:**
- Single break or group of breaks within 1 clock hour
- Break(s) located in the superior 8 clock hours (4 o'clock to 8 o'clock)
- No significant PVR
- Phakic or pseudophakic eye
- Cooperative patient capable of maintaining postoperative positioning

**Technique:**
1. [[Cryopexy]] or [[laser retinopexy]] is applied around the retinal break(s).
2. An expansile gas bubble (typically **[[sulfur hexafluoride]]** [SF₆] 0.3–0.5 mL or **[[perfluoropropane]]** [C₃F₈] 0.3 mL) is injected into the vitreous cavity via pars plana.
3. The patient is positioned so that the gas bubble tamponades the retinal break, allowing the RPE pump to reabsorb subretinal fluid.
4. Strict postoperative positioning is maintained for 5–7 days.

**Success rate:** Single-operation anatomical success rate of approximately 60–80%; with additional procedures, the final success rate approaches 95–99%.

**Advantages:** Office-based, less invasive, faster visual recovery, lower cost.

**Disadvantages:** Strict positioning requirements, higher re-detachment rate than scleral buckle or vitrectomy, risk of new or missed breaks, not suitable for complex detachments.

#### 2. Scleral Buckle (SB)

**[[Scleral buckle]]** surgery has been the gold standard for RRD repair since its introduction by Charles Schepens in the 1950s.

**Technique:**
1. A [[conjunctival peritomy]] is performed, and the [[rectus muscles]] are isolated.
2. The retinal break(s) are localized using indirect ophthalmoscopy and scleral indentation.
3. [[Cryopexy]] is applied to the area surrounding the break(s) to create a chorioretinal adhesion.
4. A silicone sponge or solid silicone band (encircling or segmental) is sutured to the [[sclera]] to create an indentation (buckle effect) that:
   - Relieves vitreoretinal traction at the break
   - Apposes the RPE to the neurosensory retina
   - Reduces the volume of the vitreous cavity
5. Subretinal fluid may be drained externally (external drainage) or allowed to reabsorb spontaneously (non-drain technique).

**Success rate:** Single-operation anatomical success rate of approximately 85–95%.

**Advantages:** Does not require entry into the vitreous cavity (external approach), preserves the natural lens, effective for young patients with clear vitreous, excellent for retinal dialysis and inferior breaks.

**Disadvantages:** Refractive change (induced [[myopia]], [[astigmatism]]), risk of scleral perforation, infection (buckle extrusion/infection), diplopia (extraocular muscle disturbance), anterior segment ischemia (rare).

#### 3. Pars Plana Vitrectomy (PPV)

**[[Pars plana vitrectomy]]** (PPV) has become the most commonly performed procedure for RRD repair in many centers, particularly for complex detachments.

**Indications:**
- RRD with PVR
- Giant retinal tears
- Posterior retinal breaks
- Vitreous hemorrhage obscuring the view
- Combined tractional-rhegmatogenous detachment
- Failed scleral buckle or pneumatic retinopexy
- Pseudophakic RRD (increasingly preferred as primary procedure)

**Technique:**
1. Three-port (or occasionally two-port) [[pars plana]] sclerotomies are created (typically 23-gauge, 25-gauge, or 27-gauge systems).
2. Core and peripheral [[vitrectomy]] is performed, relieving all vitreoretinal traction.
3. Retinal breaks are identified and treated with [[endolaser photocoagulation]] or cryopexy.
4. Subretinal fluid is drained (internally via the break or via a posterior retinotomy).
5. A tamponade agent is placed in the vitreous cavity:
   - **Gas tamponade:** SF₆ (20% concentration, lasts ~2 weeks) or C₃F₈ (14% concentration, lasts ~6–8 weeks)
   - **[[Silicone oil]] tamponade:** Used for complex detachments, PVR, or patients unable to position; requires a second surgery for removal
6. Postoperative face-down or specific positioning is required to tamponade the break(s).

**Success rate:** Single-operation anatomical success rate of approximately 85–95%; with additional surgery, >95%.

**Advantages:** Direct visualization and treatment of breaks, relief of traction, effective for complex cases, can be combined with other procedures (e.g., [[phacoemulsification]], membrane peeling).

**Disadvantages:** Accelerated [[cataract]] formation in phakic eyes (nearly universal within 1–2 years), risk of iatrogenic retinal breaks, endophthalmitis, elevated IOP from gas expansion, need for postoperative positioning, air travel restrictions with gas tamponade.

#### 4. Combined Scleral Buckle and Vitrectomy

A combined approach may be employed for complex detachments, particularly those with:
- Significant PVR
- Inferior retinal breaks with inadequate tamponade from gas alone
- Young patients with strong vitreoretinal adhesion
- Giant retinal tears

### Surgical Treatment of Tractional Retinal Detachment

- **Pars plana vitrectomy** with membrane segmentation and/or delamination is the primary surgical approach.
- **Anti-VEGF agents** (e.g., [[bevacizumab]], [[ranibizumab]], [[aflibercept]]) may be administered as a preoperative adjunct (typically 3–7 days before surgery) to reduce neovascularization and intraoperative hemorrhage in proliferative diabetic retinopathy.
- Silicone oil tamponade may be required for severe cases with extensive PVR.

### Treatment of Exudative Retinal Detachment

Management is directed at the underlying cause:
- **Inflammatory:** Systemic corticosteroids, immunosuppressive agents (e.g., for VKH)
- **Neoplastic:** Radiation therapy, chemotherapy, enucleation (for choroidal melanoma)
- **Vascular:** Treatment of underlying hypertension, pre-eclampsia
- **CSCR:** Observation, [[photodynamic therapy]] (PDT), mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists

## Pharmacological Considerations

| **Agent** | **Role in Retinal Detachment** |
|---|---|
| **Topical [[cycloplegic]] agents (e.g., [[atropine]] 1%)** | Reduce ciliary spasm and pain; stabilize the blood-aqueous barrier preoperatively |
| **Topical [[corticosteroids]] (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate]] 1%)** | Control postoperative inflammation |
| **Topical [[antibiotics]]** | Prophylaxis against postoperative [[endophthalmitis]] |
| **Topical/oral [[IOP-lowering agents]]** | Manage postoperative IOP elevation (common with gas tamponade or silicone oil) |
| **Intravitreal [[anti-VEGF]] agents** | Preoperative adjunct in TRD secondary to PDR; reduce neovascularization |
| **Systemic [[corticosteroids]]** | Treatment of exudative RD secondary to inflammatory conditions (e.g., VKH) |
| **Intravitreal [[corticosteroids]] (e.g., [[triamcinolone acetonide]])** | Adjunctive use in PVR to reduce inflammation and cellular proliferation (investigational) |
| **Anti-proliferative agents (e.g., [[5-fluorouracil]], [[daunorubicin]])** | Investigated as adjuncts to prevent PVR; limited clinical adoption due to toxicity concerns |

## Prognosis and Visual Outcomes

The visual prognosis following retinal detachment repair depends on several critical factors:

| **Factor** | **Impact on Prognosis** |
|---|---|
| **Macular status at time of surgery** | **Most important prognostic factor.** Mac-on RD: ~85–90% achieve ≥20/40 acuity. Mac-off RD: ~40–60% achieve ≥20/40; outcomes decline with increasing duration of macular detachment |
| **Duration of macular detachment** | Shorter duration correlates with better visual recovery; detachments of <7 days duration have significantly better outcomes than those >7 days |
| **Extent of detachment** | More extensive detachments have worse prognosis |
| **Presence of PVR** | PVR is the leading cause of surgical failure; associated with worse anatomical and visual outcomes |
| **Number of surgical procedures** | Multiple surgeries are associated with worse visual outcomes due to cumulative retinal damage |
| **Preoperative visual acuity** | Better preoperative acuity generally predicts better postoperative acuity |
| **Patient age** | Younger patients may have better photoreceptor recovery potential |
| **Height of macular detachment** | Greater macular elevation is associated with worse visual recovery |

### Complications of Retinal Detachment Surgery

| **Complication** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| **Re-detachment** | Occurs in 5–15% of cases; most commonly due to PVR or new/missed breaks |
| **[[Proliferative vitreoretinopathy]] (PVR)** | Most common cause of surgical failure; occurs in ~5–10% of primary RRD repairs |
| **[[Cataract]]** | Nearly universal after vitrectomy with gas tamponade in phakic patients (within 1–2 years) |
| **Elevated [[IOP]] / [[glaucoma]]** | Due to gas expansion, silicone oil, or steroid response |
| **[[Endophthalmitis]]** | Rare but devastating; incidence ~0.05–0.1% |
| **[[Cystoid macular edema]]** | May limit visual recovery despite anatomical success |
| **[[Epiretinal membrane]]** | Common after vitrectomy; may require secondary membrane peeling |
| **Diplopia** | After scleral buckle surgery due to extraocular muscle disturbance |
| **Refractive change** | Myopic shift after scleral buckle; hyperopic shift after silicone oil removal |
| **Sympathetic ophthalmia** | Extremely rare; bilateral granulomatous [[panuveitis]] following surgical trauma |

## Role of the Optometrist

[[Optometrists]] play a critical role in every stage of retinal detachment management:

1. **Prevention and risk assessment:** Identifying patients at high risk (high myopes, family history, lattice degeneration, fellow eye RD, post-cataract surgery) and counseling them about warning symptoms.
2. **Early detection:** Performing thorough [[dilated fundus examinations]] with [[binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy]] and [[scleral indentation]], particularly in patients presenting with acute-onset floaters, photopsia, or visual field defects.
3. **Urgent referral:** Recognizing the signs of retinal detachment and facilitating emergent referral to a [[vitreoretinal surgeon]].
4. **Postoperative co-management:** Monitoring patients after retinal detachment surgery, including assessment of visual acuity, IOP, anterior segment inflammation, retinal reattachment status, and detection of complications such as PVR, cataract, or re-detachment.
5. **Patient education:** Instructing patients about postoperative positioning requirements, activity restrictions, air travel restrictions (with intraocular gas), and symptoms that warrant immediate re-evaluation.

## Special Considerations

### Retinal Detachment in Pediatric Patients

Retinal detachment in children is relatively rare but presents unique challenges:
- Often diagnosed late due to inability to report symptoms
- Higher incidence of traumatic RD and RD associated with [[retinopathy of prematurity]], [[Stickler syndrome]], [[Marfan syndrome]], and [[familial exudative vitreoretinopathy]] (FEVR)
- Higher rates of PVR
- Surgical outcomes are generally worse than in adults

### Retinal Detachment in High Myopia

Highly myopic eyes (axial length >26 mm or refractive error > −6.00 D) have:
- Thinner retina with increased prevalence of lattice degeneration and atrophic holes
- Posterior staphyloma, which may predispose to macular holes and foveoschisis
- Higher risk of RRD, particularly after cataract surgery or Nd:YAG capsulotomy
- [[Myopic traction maculopathy]] may mimic or coexist with retinal detachment

### Bilateral Retinal Detachment

Bilateral simultaneous retinal detachment is uncommon in RRD but should raise suspicion for:
- Exudative causes (VKH, bilateral choroidal tumors, pre-eclampsia)
- Hereditary vitreoretinopathies (Stickler syndrome)
- Bilateral high myopia with lattice degeneration

---

## Summary

Retinal detachment remains one of the most important ophthalmic emergencies, requiring a high index of clinical suspicion, thorough examination technique, and prompt management to preserve vision. The optometrist serves as a frontline provider in the detection and triage of retinal detachment, and a thorough understanding of its pathophysiology, classification, clinical features, and management options is indispensable for optimal patient outcomes.
