# Phenol Red Thread Test

---

The **Phenol Red Thread Test** (PRT test), also known as the **Zone-Quick test** or **cotton thread test**, is a minimally invasive clinical diagnostic procedure used in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] to quantitatively assess [[aqueous tear production]]. Developed by Kurihashi and colleagues in 1977 in Japan, the test employs a fine cotton thread impregnated with the pH-sensitive indicator [[phenol red]] (phenolsulfonphthalein) to measure the volume of [[tear fluid]] present in the [[inferior conjunctival fornix]] and produced reflexively over a brief 15-second collection period. The test serves as a rapid, comfortable, and clinically practical alternative to the traditional [[Schirmer test]] for the evaluation of [[tear film]] adequacy and the diagnosis of [[dry eye disease]] (DED), also known as [[keratoconjunctivitis sicca]].

The PRT test has gained widespread clinical adoption due to its speed, patient comfort, reduced reflex tearing artifact, and ease of interpretation. It is considered particularly valuable in screening for [[aqueous-deficient dry eye]] (ADDE) and is a standard component of the comprehensive [[dry eye]] diagnostic workup in modern clinical practice.

---

## History and Development

The Phenol Red Thread Test was first introduced by **Kurihashi, Yanagihara, and Honda** in 1977 as a novel method for tear volume assessment. The developers sought to address several well-recognized limitations of the [[Schirmer test]], which had been the gold standard for tear secretion measurement since its introduction by Otto Schirmer in 1903. The primary criticisms of the Schirmer test included its lengthy 5-minute testing duration, significant patient discomfort due to the relatively large and stiff [[Whatman filter paper]] strip, and the substantial degree of [[reflex tearing]] provoked by the paper strip's irritation of the [[palpebral conjunctiva]] and [[corneal surface]], which confounded accurate measurement of basal tear secretion.

The PRT test was designed to overcome these limitations by utilizing a much finer, softer cotton thread that would cause minimal mechanical irritation to the ocular surface, thereby reducing reflex tearing and providing a more accurate reflection of residual and basal [[tear volume]]. The incorporation of [[phenol red]] as a pH indicator allowed for easy visualization of the wetted length, as the dye changes color from yellow to red upon contact with the slightly alkaline [[tear fluid]] (normal tear pH approximately 7.0–7.4).

The test was subsequently commercialized and distributed under the trade name **Zone-Quick** (Showa Yakuhin Kako Co., Ltd., Tokyo, Japan; later distributed by Menicon Co., Ltd. and other international distributors). It has since been validated in numerous clinical studies and is recognized by the [[Tear Film and Ocular Surface Society]] (TFOS) [[Dry Eye Workshop]] (DEWS) reports as a clinically useful measure of tear volume.

## Principles and Pathophysiology

### Physiological Basis

The PRT test fundamentally measures the **volume of tear fluid** available in the [[lower conjunctival sac]] and the rate of tear production over a defined short interval. Understanding the test requires knowledge of the physiology of the [[tear film]] and [[lacrimal functional unit]].

The [[tear film]] is a complex, multi-layered fluid structure that coats the [[ocular surface]], comprising:

| Layer | Thickness | Source | Function |
|-------|-----------|--------|----------|
| **[[Lipid layer]]** | ~0.1 µm | [[Meibomian glands]] | Retards evaporation, stabilizes tear film |
| **[[Aqueous layer]]** | ~3–4 µm | [[Main lacrimal gland]], [[accessory lacrimal glands]] ([[Glands of Krause]], [[Glands of Wolfring]]) | Provides hydration, nutrients, antimicrobial factors |
| **[[Mucin layer]]** | ~0.02–0.05 µm (gel-forming), transmembrane mucins | [[Goblet cells]], [[corneal epithelium]], [[conjunctival epithelium]] | Promotes wettability, anchors tear film to epithelium |

The PRT test primarily evaluates the **aqueous component** of the tear film. The aqueous layer is produced by the [[main lacrimal gland]] (responsible for reflex and emotional tearing) and the [[accessory lacrimal glands of Krause and Wolfring]] (believed to contribute significantly to basal tear secretion). The total tear volume in the normal eye is estimated at approximately 6–9 µL, with a tear turnover rate of approximately 1.0–1.2 µL/min under basal (non-stimulated) conditions.

### Mechanism of the PRT Test

The PRT test thread, when placed in the [[inferior conjunctival fornix]], absorbs tear fluid through **capillary action** along the cotton fibers. As the alkaline tear fluid (pH ~7.0–7.4) contacts the [[phenol red]] dye impregnated in the thread, the indicator undergoes a **colorimetric pH-dependent transition**:

- At **pH < 6.8**: Phenol red appears **yellow** (its acidic form)
- At **pH 6.8–8.2** (transition range): Phenol red shifts from yellow through **orange** to **red**
- At **pH > 8.2**: Phenol red appears **bright red/fuchsia** (its alkaline form)

Since normal human tears have a pH of approximately **7.0 to 7.4**, the thread changes from its original yellow color to a distinct **orange-red** or **red** color upon wetting, providing a clearly visible demarcation between the wetted and unwetted portions of the thread. This color change greatly facilitates accurate measurement of the wetted length.

### Relationship to Tear Dynamics

The length of thread wetted during the 15-second test period reflects a combination of:

1. **Residual tear volume**: The pre-existing tear fluid pooled in the [[tear meniscus]] and [[inferior conjunctival fornix]] at the time of thread placement.
2. **Basal tear secretion**: The ongoing production of tears by the [[accessory lacrimal glands]] during the test period.
3. **Minimal reflex tearing**: Although the PRT test is designed to minimize reflex stimulation, a small degree of reflex secretion from the [[main lacrimal gland]] may still occur, particularly in sensitive patients.

Because the thread is extremely fine (approximately 0.5 mm in diameter) and soft, it causes significantly less mechanical stimulation of the [[conjunctiva]] and [[cornea]] than the Schirmer strip, and therefore the PRT result is considered to more closely approximate **basal and residual tear volume** rather than reflex tear secretion.

## Clinical Indications

The PRT test is indicated in the following clinical scenarios:

- **Screening and diagnosis of [[dry eye disease]]** (particularly [[aqueous-deficient dry eye]])
- **Evaluation of tear production** in patients presenting with symptoms of [[ocular surface disease]]
- **Monitoring treatment response** in patients undergoing therapy for [[dry eye syndrome]], including [[artificial tears]], [[cyclosporine A]] ([[Restasis]]), [[lifitegrast]] ([[Xiidra]]), or [[punctal occlusion]]
- **Pre-operative assessment** of tear function prior to [[refractive surgery]] (e.g., [[LASIK]], [[PRK]]), [[cataract surgery]], or [[contact lens]] fitting
- **Assessment of tear function** in patients with [[Sjögren syndrome]], [[rheumatoid arthritis]], [[systemic lupus erythematosus]], [[graft-versus-host disease]], and other systemic conditions associated with [[lacrimal gland]] dysfunction
- **Pediatric tear assessment**: Due to its short duration and minimal discomfort, the PRT test is particularly well-suited for use in children and uncooperative patients
- **Contact lens practice**: Evaluation of tear adequacy in [[contact lens]] wearers experiencing [[contact lens-related dry eye]]
- **Comparative studies**: Used alongside other diagnostic tests such as the [[Schirmer test]], [[tear break-up time]] (TBUT), [[tear meniscus height]] measurement, and [[tear osmolarity]] testing

## Test Materials and Equipment

### Thread Specifications

The standard PRT test thread has the following characteristics:

| Parameter | Specification |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Material** | Fine-grade cotton thread |
| **Dye** | [[Phenol red]] (phenolsulfonphthalein) |
| **Thread diameter** | ~0.5 mm |
| **Total thread length** | 70–75 mm |
| **Color (dry/unwetted)** | Yellow |
| **Color (wetted by tears)** | Orange-red to red |
| **Folded notch** | 3 mm from one end (for hooking over the lower lid) |
| **Graduated scale** | Millimeter markings along the thread length |
| **Packaging** | Individually sealed sterile packets |

### Additional Equipment

- **Timer**: A precise timer or stopwatch capable of measuring 15 seconds
- **Millimeter ruler or scale**: For measuring the wetted length (if not pre-graduated on the thread)
- **Good illumination**: Adequate lighting to visualize the color change boundary
- **Slit lamp biomicroscope** (optional): May be used for precise measurement of the wetted length under magnification

## Test Procedure

### Step-by-Step Protocol

1. **Patient preparation**: The patient should be seated comfortably in the examination chair. The procedure should be explained to the patient to minimize anxiety. **No topical anesthetic is instilled** prior to the test, as the PRT test is designed to measure tear production under near-basal conditions, and anesthesia would suppress reflex tearing and alter the result. The patient should not have instilled any [[eye drops]] for at least 2 hours prior to testing if possible.

2. **Thread preparation**: Remove the PRT thread from its sterile packaging, handling it by the longer end to avoid contamination of the folded tip. Identify the **3 mm folded notch** at one end of the thread.

3. **Thread placement**: Instruct the patient to look straight ahead or slightly upward. Gently pull down the [[lower eyelid]] to expose the [[inferior conjunctival fornix]]. Hook the **3 mm folded end** of the thread over the **temporal one-third** of the lower eyelid margin, allowing the folded portion to rest within the [[inferior conjunctival sac]]. The remaining length of the thread hangs freely over the lower lid and cheek.

4. **Timing**: Start the timer immediately upon thread placement. The patient is instructed to **blink normally** during the test. The standard test duration is **15 seconds**.

5. **Thread removal**: After exactly 15 seconds, gently remove the thread from the lower eyelid.

6. **Measurement**: Immediately measure the length of the **red/orange-red colored (wetted) portion** of the thread in millimeters, starting from the folded tip. The color change from yellow to red provides a clear demarcation line. Record the measurement for each eye separately.

7. **Documentation**: Record the PRT result in millimeters for the right eye (OD) and left eye (OS), along with the date, time, and any relevant clinical notes.

### Testing Considerations

- **Bilateral testing**: Both eyes may be tested simultaneously to save time, or sequentially. Simultaneous testing is commonly performed in clinical practice.
- **Environmental factors**: Testing should ideally be performed in a room with controlled humidity and temperature, as extreme environmental conditions can affect tear evaporation rates.
- **Time of day**: Tear production exhibits [[diurnal variation]], with lower values often observed in the morning. Consistency in testing time is recommended for longitudinal monitoring.
- **Repeat testing**: If results are equivocal, the test may be repeated after a brief interval (at least 5–10 minutes) to allow tear film recovery.

## Interpretation of Results

### Normal and Abnormal Values

| PRT Result (mm/15 sec) | Interpretation |
|------------------------|----------------|
| **≥ 20 mm** | **Normal** tear production |
| **10–19 mm** | **Borderline / Mild deficiency** — may warrant further investigation |
| **< 10 mm** | **Abnormal** — suggestive of [[aqueous-deficient dry eye]] |
| **< 5 mm** | **Severely reduced** — strongly suggestive of significant aqueous tear deficiency |

> **Note**: The most widely cited diagnostic cutoff value for dry eye using the PRT test is **< 10 mm/15 seconds**, though some studies have proposed alternative cutoffs ranging from 9 to 12 mm depending on the population studied and the diagnostic criteria employed.

### Normative Data

Studies have established the following approximate normative values:

| Population | Mean PRT Value (mm/15 sec) | Standard Deviation |
|------------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| **Normal adults (20–40 years)** | 20–25 mm | ±5–8 mm |
| **Normal adults (40–60 years)** | 17–22 mm | ±5–7 mm |
| **Normal adults (>60 years)** | 14–19 mm | ±5–7 mm |
| **Dry eye patients** | 5–12 mm | ±3–5 mm |
| **Sjögren syndrome patients** | 2–8 mm | ±2–4 mm |

Tear production generally **decreases with age**, and PRT values tend to be slightly lower in older adults. Some studies have reported slightly higher values in females compared to males in younger age groups, though this finding is not consistent across all studies. [[Hormonal factors]], particularly [[androgen]] and [[estrogen]] levels, are known to influence [[lacrimal gland]] function and may contribute to sex-related differences.

### Factors Affecting Results

Several factors may influence PRT test results and should be considered during interpretation:

| Factor | Effect on PRT Result |
|--------|---------------------|
| **Topical anesthetics** | Decrease (suppresses reflex tearing) — should NOT be used |
| **Recent eye drop instillation** | May artificially increase result |
| **Environmental humidity** | Low humidity may decrease; high humidity may increase |
| **Wind/air currents** | May increase evaporation, decreasing result |
| **Patient anxiety/discomfort** | May increase reflex tearing, increasing result |
| **Eyelid laxity** | May affect thread placement and tear pooling |
| **[[Conjunctivochalasis]]** | Redundant conjunctival folds may trap tears, affecting measurement |
| **Recent [[contact lens]] wear** | May alter tear dynamics |
| **Systemic medications** (e.g., [[antihistamines]], [[anticholinergics]], [[beta-blockers]], [[diuretics]]) | May decrease tear production |
| **Systemic conditions** (e.g., [[Sjögren syndrome]], [[diabetes mellitus]], [[thyroid disease]]) | May decrease tear production |
| **Time of day** | [[Diurnal variation]] in tear production |

## Comparison with the Schirmer Test

The PRT test is most frequently compared with the [[Schirmer test]], as both are designed to assess tear production volume. The following table summarizes the key differences:

| Parameter | **Phenol Red Thread Test** | **[[Schirmer Test]]** |
|-----------|---------------------------|----------------------|
| **Test duration** | 15 seconds | 5 minutes |
| **Strip/thread material** | Fine cotton thread (~0.5 mm) | Whatman No. 41 filter paper (5 mm × 35 mm) |
| **Indicator** | [[Phenol red]] (pH-sensitive dye) | None (wetting front measured directly) |
| **Color change** | Yellow → Red upon wetting | No color change (white paper becomes translucent) |
| **Patient comfort** | Minimal discomfort; well-tolerated | Moderate discomfort; may cause significant irritation |
| **Reflex tearing** | Minimal (due to fine, soft thread) | Significant (due to large, stiff paper strip) |
| **What is measured** | Primarily residual tear volume + basal secretion | Basal + reflex secretion (Schirmer I without anesthesia); Basal secretion (Schirmer I with anesthesia / Schirmer II) |
| **Normal cutoff** | ≥ 10 mm/15 sec | ≥ 10 mm/5 min (Schirmer I without anesthesia); ≥ 5 mm/5 min (with anesthesia) |
| **Dry eye cutoff** | < 10 mm/15 sec | < 10 mm/5 min (without anesthesia); < 5 mm/5 min (with anesthesia) |
| **Sensitivity for dry eye** | Moderate (56–85%, varies by study) | Moderate (42–83%, varies by study) |
| **Specificity for dry eye** | Moderate (60–90%, varies by study) | Moderate (50–90%, varies by study) |
| **Pediatric suitability** | Excellent (short duration, minimal discomfort) | Poor (long duration, significant discomfort) |
| **Repeatability** | Good | Moderate (high variability due to reflex tearing) |
| **Use of anesthesia** | Not required | Optional (Schirmer I with/without; Schirmer II with nasal stimulation) |

### Correlation Between PRT and Schirmer Tests

Multiple studies have demonstrated a **moderate positive correlation** between PRT and Schirmer test results (correlation coefficients typically ranging from r = 0.40 to r = 0.70). However, the two tests are not interchangeable, as they measure somewhat different aspects of tear dynamics. The PRT test is more reflective of residual tear volume and basal secretion, while the Schirmer test (without anesthesia) includes a larger component of reflex tearing. Clinicians should interpret results in the context of the specific test performed and consider using both tests in combination for a more comprehensive assessment.

## Diagnostic Accuracy and Clinical Validation

### Sensitivity and Specificity

The diagnostic performance of the PRT test for [[dry eye disease]] has been evaluated in numerous clinical studies. Reported values vary depending on the diagnostic gold standard used, the cutoff value selected, and the study population:

| Study / Source | Cutoff (mm/15 sec) | Sensitivity (%) | Specificity (%) |
|---------------|--------------------:|----------------:|----------------:|
| Sakamoto et al. (1993) | < 10 | 56 | 69 |
| Patel et al. (1998) | < 10 | 71 | 67 |
| Tomlinson et al. (2001) | < 10 | 76 | 68 |
| Saleh et al. (2006) | < 9 | 86 | 83 |
| Labetoulle et al. (2002) | < 10 | 80 | 73 |
| Hamano et al. (1982) | < 10 | 68 | 75 |

> **Clinical Note**: No single diagnostic test for [[dry eye disease]] has both high sensitivity and high specificity. The TFOS DEWS II Diagnostic Methodology Report (2017) recommends a **battery of tests** approach, combining subjective symptom questionnaires (e.g., [[OSDI]], [[DEQ-5]], [[SPEED]]) with objective tests including [[tear break-up time]], [[tear osmolarity]], [[ocular surface staining]] (e.g., [[fluorescein staining]], [[lissamine green staining]], [[rose bengal staining]]), [[tear meniscus height]], [[meibography]], and tear volume assessment (PRT or Schirmer test) for comprehensive dry eye diagnosis and classification.

### Reproducibility and Reliability

The PRT test has demonstrated **good intra-session and inter-session reproducibility** in multiple studies, with coefficients of repeatability generally superior to those reported for the Schirmer test. This improved reproducibility is attributed to the reduced reflex tearing artifact associated with the finer, less irritating thread. However, some degree of variability remains inherent due to biological fluctuations in tear production.

## Role in Dry Eye Disease Classification

The PRT test plays a specific role in the classification of [[dry eye disease]] according to the [[TFOS DEWS II]] framework:

### Aqueous-Deficient Dry Eye (ADDE)

A reduced PRT value (< 10 mm/15 sec) is a hallmark finding in [[aqueous-deficient dry eye]], which results from inadequate tear production by the [[lacrimal glands]]. ADDE is further subclassified into:

- **[[Sjögren syndrome]]-associated ADDE**: Autoimmune destruction of the [[lacrimal gland]] and [[salivary glands]], often presenting with severely reduced PRT values (frequently < 5 mm/15 sec)
- **Non-Sjögren ADDE**: Lacrimal gland dysfunction due to age-related changes, [[lacrimal gland inflammation]], [[lacrimal gland obstruction]], reflex sensory block (e.g., post-[[LASIK]]), or systemic medications

### Evaporative Dry Eye (EDE)

In [[evaporative dry eye]], which is primarily caused by [[meibomian gland dysfunction]] (MGD) and lipid layer deficiency, PRT values may be **normal or near-normal**, as the aqueous production is not primarily affected. However, in advanced or mixed cases, secondary aqueous deficiency may develop, leading to reduced PRT values.

### Mixed Mechanism Dry Eye

Many patients with [[dry eye disease]] have a **mixed mechanism** involving both aqueous deficiency and increased evaporation. In these cases, PRT values may be variably reduced, and the test should be interpreted alongside [[tear break-up time]], [[meibography]], and [[lipid layer assessment]] (e.g., [[interferometry]]).

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Patients

The PRT test is considered the **preferred tear volume test in children** due to its extremely short duration (15 seconds vs. 5 minutes for Schirmer) and minimal discomfort. Studies in pediatric populations have established age-appropriate normative values, which tend to be slightly higher than adult values due to robust lacrimal gland function in youth.

### Contact Lens Wearers

[[Contact lens]]-related dry eye is a common clinical complaint. The PRT test can be performed with contact lenses removed to assess baseline tear production. Some studies have also evaluated PRT values with lenses in situ, though this is not standard practice. Reduced PRT values in contact lens wearers may indicate inadequate tear support for successful lens wear and may guide decisions regarding lens material, replacement schedule, or supplemental [[artificial tears]].

### Post-Refractive Surgery Patients

Patients who have undergone [[LASIK]], [[PRK]], or other [[corneal refractive surgery]] procedures frequently experience [[dry eye]] symptoms due to disruption of [[corneal nerves]] and the afferent limb of the [[lacrimal reflex arc]]. PRT testing is valuable in the pre-operative assessment of tear function and in monitoring post-operative recovery of tear production.

### Sjögren Syndrome and Autoimmune Disease

In patients with suspected [[Sjögren syndrome]], the PRT test serves as a rapid screening tool for [[aqueous tear deficiency]]. Severely reduced values (< 5 mm/15 sec) in the context of [[xerostomia]] (dry mouth), positive [[anti-SSA/Ro]] and [[anti-SSB/La]] antibodies, and characteristic [[salivary gland biopsy]] findings support the diagnosis. The PRT test is included in some classification criteria for Sjögren syndrome as an objective measure of ocular dryness.

## Advantages and Limitations

### Advantages

- **Rapid**: Only 15 seconds, compared to 5 minutes for the Schirmer test
- **Comfortable**: Minimal patient discomfort due to the fine, soft thread
- **Reduced reflex tearing**: More accurate reflection of basal/residual tear volume
- **Easy to interpret**: Clear color change (yellow to red) facilitates measurement
- **No anesthesia required**: Simplifies the testing protocol
- **Good reproducibility**: Superior repeatability compared to the Schirmer test
- **Pediatric-friendly**: Well-suited for children and uncooperative patients
- **Portable**: Can be performed in any clinical setting without specialized equipment
- **Cost-effective**: Individually packaged threads are inexpensive

### Limitations

- **Does not differentiate tear film layers**: Measures aqueous volume only; does not assess [[lipid layer]] or [[mucin layer]] function
- **Moderate sensitivity and specificity**: Not sufficient as a standalone diagnostic test for dry eye
- **Affected by residual tear volume**: Measures a combination of residual and newly secreted tears, making it difficult to isolate basal secretion rate
- **Environmental sensitivity**: Results may be influenced by ambient humidity, temperature, and air currents
- **Thread placement variability**: Slight differences in thread position (temporal vs. central lower lid) may affect results
- **Not universally available**: In some regions, PRT threads may be less readily available than Schirmer strips
- **Cannot assess reflex tearing capacity**: Unlike the Schirmer test with nasal stimulation (Schirmer II), the PRT test does not evaluate the reflex secretory pathway
- **Color interpretation subjectivity**: In some cases, the boundary between wetted and unwetted portions may be indistinct, particularly with very low tear volumes

## Management Implications

While the PRT test is a **diagnostic tool** rather than a treatment, its results directly inform clinical management decisions for patients with [[dry eye disease]]:

### Based on PRT Results

| PRT Result | Suggested Clinical Action |
|------------|--------------------------|
| **≥ 20 mm** (Normal) | Reassurance; address symptoms with other diagnostic findings if present |
| **10–19 mm** (Borderline) | Further evaluation with [[TBUT]], [[osmolarity]], [[ocular surface staining]]; consider [[artificial tears]] PRN; address environmental and lifestyle factors |
| **< 10 mm** (Aqueous deficiency) | Initiate treatment: [[preservative-free artificial tears]], [[gel lubricants]], consider [[cyclosporine A]] ([[Restasis]]) or [[lifitegrast]] ([[Xiidra]]), [[punctal plugs]], [[moisture chamber glasses]], [[omega-3 fatty acid]] supplementation |
| **< 5 mm** (Severe deficiency) | Aggressive treatment: [[autologous serum tears]], [[punctal occlusion]] (plugs or [[punctal cautery]]), [[scleral contact lenses]] for ocular surface protection, systemic evaluation for [[Sjögren syndrome]] or other autoimmune conditions, consider [[secretagogues]] (e.g., oral [[pilocarpine]], [[cevimeline]]) |

### Integration into the Dry Eye Diagnostic Algorithm

The [[TFOS DEWS II]] diagnostic algorithm recommends the following stepwise approach, in which the PRT test plays a defined role:

1. **Symptom screening**: [[OSDI]], [[DEQ-5]], or other validated questionnaire
2. **Homeostasis markers**: [[Non-invasive tear break-up time]] (NIBUT), [[tear osmolarity]], [[ocular surface staining]]
3. **Subtype classification**: [[Tear volume assessment]] (PRT or Schirmer test), [[meibography]], [[lipid layer assessment]], [[blink rate analysis]]

The PRT test is positioned in **Step 3** as a subtype classification tool, helping to determine whether the patient's dry eye is primarily **aqueous-deficient**, **evaporative**, or **mixed**.

## Pharmacological and Therapeutic Context

Although the PRT test itself is not a therapeutic intervention, understanding its role in guiding pharmacological and procedural management is essential:

### Pharmacological Interventions Guided by PRT Results

| Therapy | Mechanism | Indication Based on PRT |
|---------|-----------|------------------------|
| **[[Artificial tears]]** (e.g., [[carboxymethylcellulose]], [[hyaluronic acid]], [[polyethylene glycol]]) | Tear supplementation | PRT < 10–20 mm; first-line therapy |
| **[[Cyclosporine A]] 0.05% ([[Restasis]])** | Anti-inflammatory; increases tear production via T-cell inhibition | PRT < 10 mm; chronic aqueous deficiency |
| **[[Lifitegrast]] 5% ([[Xiidra]])** | [[LFA-1]]/[[ICAM-1]] antagonist; anti-inflammatory | PRT < 10 mm with inflammatory component |
| **[[Varenicline]] nasal spray ([[Tyrvaya]])** | [[Nicotinic acetylcholine receptor]] agonist; stimulates tear production via trigeminal pathway | Reduced PRT values; aqueous deficiency |
| **[[Punctal plugs]]** ([[collagen]] or [[silicone]]) | Reduces tear drainage; conserves existing tears | PRT < 10 mm; inadequate response to drops alone |
| **Oral [[secretagogues]]** ([[pilocarpine]], [[cevimeline]]) | [[Muscarinic receptor]] agonists; stimulate lacrimal and salivary secretion | PRT < 5 mm; [[Sjögren syndrome]]-associated ADDE |
| **[[Autologous serum tears]]** | Provides growth factors, fibronectin, and nutrients | PRT < 5 mm; severe refractory dry eye |
| **[[Scleral contact lenses]]** | Creates fluid reservoir over cornea | PRT < 5 mm; severe ocular surface disease |

## Quality Control and Standardization

To ensure reliable and reproducible PRT results, clinicians should adhere to the following quality control measures:

1. **Standardized thread source**: Use commercially manufactured, quality-controlled PRT threads from reputable suppliers
2. **Proper storage**: Store threads in a cool, dry environment; avoid exposure to moisture or extreme temperatures that could prematurely activate the dye
3. **Consistent technique**: Place the thread at the **temporal one-third** of the lower lid consistently; avoid touching the [[cornea]] during placement
4. **Precise timing**: Use a reliable timer; the 15-second duration must be strictly observed
5. **Immediate measurement**: Read the wetted length immediately after removal, as the color front may continue to advance slightly due to capillary wicking
6. **Controlled environment**: Minimize drafts and maintain consistent room humidity when possible
7. **Avoid confounders**: Ensure no topical medications have been instilled recently; note any systemic medications that may affect tear production
8. **Documentation**: Record results in millimeters with notation of the eye tested, time of day, and any relevant clinical context

## Emerging Research and Future Directions

Ongoing research continues to refine the clinical utility of the PRT test:

- **Automated measurement systems**: Development of digital imaging and software-based measurement of the wetted thread length to reduce subjective measurement error
- **Modified thread compositions**: Investigation of alternative pH indicators and thread materials to improve sensitivity and color contrast
- **Correlation with advanced tear diagnostics**: Studies comparing PRT results with [[tear osmolarity]] (e.g., [[TearLab]]), [[tear meniscus height]] measured by [[anterior segment OCT]], [[interferometry]]-based lipid layer thickness, and [[MMP-9]] point-of-care testing ([[InflammaDry]])
- **Normative database expansion**: Establishment of age-, sex-, and ethnicity-specific normative databases for more accurate interpretation
- **Integration with artificial intelligence**: AI-assisted interpretation of PRT results in the context of multimodal dry eye diagnostic data
- **Longitudinal monitoring**: Use of serial PRT measurements to track treatment response and disease progression over time

---

## Summary

The **Phenol Red Thread Test** is a rapid, minimally invasive, and clinically valuable diagnostic tool for the quantitative assessment of [[tear production]] and [[tear volume]]. Its 15-second testing duration, excellent patient comfort, reduced reflex tearing artifact, and clear colorimetric endpoint make it a practical and reliable alternative to the [[Schirmer test]] in the evaluation of [[aqueous-deficient dry eye]]. While no single test is sufficient for the comprehensive diagnosis of [[dry eye disease]], the PRT test occupies an important role in the multimodal diagnostic algorithm recommended by the [[TFOS DEWS II]] guidelines. Clinicians should interpret PRT results in conjunction with symptom questionnaires, [[tear break-up time]], [[tear osmolarity]], [[ocular surface staining]], and [[meibomian gland]] assessment to achieve accurate diagnosis, appropriate subtype classification, and optimal management of patients with [[dry eye disease]].
