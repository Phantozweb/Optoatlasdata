# Deep Anterior Lamellar Keratoplasty (DALK)

---

## Definition

**Deep Anterior Lamellar Keratoplasty (DALK)** is a selective, partial-thickness [[corneal transplantation]] technique in which the host [[corneal epithelium]], [[Bowman's layer]], and [[corneal stroma]] are removed while preserving the recipient's native [[Descemet's membrane]] (DM) and [[corneal endothelium]]. A full-thickness donor corneal button, from which the endothelium and Descemet's membrane have been stripped, is then sutured onto the recipient bed. DALK has emerged as the preferred surgical alternative to [[penetrating keratoplasty]] (PK) for a wide range of corneal stromal pathologies in which the endothelium remains healthy and functional. By retaining the host endothelium, DALK virtually eliminates the risk of [[endothelial graft rejection]], the most visually devastating complication of full-thickness corneal transplantation, and confers superior long-term graft survival.

---

## Historical Background

The concept of lamellar keratoplasty dates to the early 19th century, but modern DALK owes its development to several pivotal innovations. In 1984, Archila described manual deep stromal dissection techniques. The landmark advancement came in 2002 when Mohamed Anwar and Khalil Teichmann published the **"big-bubble" technique**, in which a large-bore needle is used to inject air into the deep stroma, creating a pneumatic separation between the posterior stroma and [[Descemet's membrane]]. This technique dramatically improved the consistency of achieving a bare Descemet's membrane interface ("type-1 big bubble") and transformed DALK from a technically unpredictable procedure into a reproducible surgical approach. Subsequent refinements—including the Melles technique of manual viscodissection, the Sarnicola modification, and the use of [[femtosecond laser]]-assisted trephination—have further expanded the surgeon's armamentarium.

## Anatomy and Pathophysiology

### Relevant Corneal Anatomy

A thorough understanding of [[corneal anatomy]] is essential for appreciating the rationale and execution of DALK.

| Layer | Approximate Thickness | Composition | Relevance to DALK |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Corneal epithelium]] | 50–55 µm | Stratified squamous non-keratinized epithelium | Removed during surgery; regenerates from host [[limbal stem cells]] |
| [[Bowman's layer]] | 8–14 µm | Acellular condensation of anterior stroma | Removed with the diseased stroma |
| [[Corneal stroma]] | ~500 µm (90% of total thickness) | Organized [[collagen]] lamellae (predominantly type I), [[keratocytes]], and extracellular matrix | Primary target of excision; site of pathology in most DALK indications |
| [[Dua's layer]] (pre-Descemet's layer) | 10–15 µm | Acellular, strong collagenous layer | Described by Harminder Dua in 2013; may be encountered as a separate plane during big-bubble dissection ("type-2 bubble") |
| [[Descemet's membrane]] | 10–15 µm (increases with age) | Basement membrane of the endothelium; type IV and VIII collagen | **Preserved** in DALK; serves as the recipient bed |
| [[Corneal endothelium]] | ~5 µm (monolayer) | Non-regenerative hexagonal cell monolayer; maintains corneal deturgescence via Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump | **Preserved** in DALK; elimination of endothelial rejection risk is the primary advantage |

### Pathophysiological Basis for DALK

DALK is indicated when corneal pathology is confined to the epithelium and stroma, leaving the endothelium functionally intact. The pathophysiology varies by indication:

- **[[Keratoconus]]**: Progressive, non-inflammatory [[ectasia]] characterized by enzymatic degradation of stromal collagen (increased [[matrix metalloproteinases]], decreased [[tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases]]), apoptosis of [[keratocytes]], breaks in [[Bowman's layer]], and resultant corneal thinning and irregular [[astigmatism]]. The endothelium remains healthy in the vast majority of cases, making keratoconus the single most common indication for DALK worldwide.
- **[[Corneal scarring]]**: Post-infectious (e.g., healed [[herpes simplex keratitis]], [[bacterial keratitis]], [[fungal keratitis]]) or post-traumatic stromal opacification with an intact endothelium.
- **[[Corneal dystrophies]]**: Stromal dystrophies such as [[granular dystrophy]], [[lattice dystrophy]], and [[macular corneal dystrophy]] involve progressive deposition of abnormal material within the stroma. Because the endothelium is unaffected, DALK is an ideal therapeutic approach and may reduce the rate of dystrophy recurrence compared to PK (since the host Descemet's membrane acts as a barrier to recurrence in some dystrophies).
- **[[Corneal ectasia]]**: Post-[[LASIK]] or post-[[photorefractive keratectomy]] (PRK) ectasia with stromal thinning and irregular astigmatism.
- **[[Pellucid marginal degeneration]]**: Inferior peripheral corneal thinning with intact endothelium.
- **Corneal [[dermoid]]**: Superficial or deep stromal dermoids amenable to lamellar excision.

In all these conditions, the fundamental pathophysiological rationale is the same: the disease process disrupts the structural and optical integrity of the stroma (and sometimes Bowman's layer and epithelium), while the endothelial pump function and Descemet's membrane integrity are maintained. DALK selectively replaces the diseased tissue while preserving the patient's own immunologically privileged endothelial layer.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Patients presenting as candidates for DALK typically exhibit symptoms and signs referable to their underlying corneal stromal disease. These include:

### Symptoms

- **Decreased [[visual acuity]]**: Progressive, often not correctable with [[spectacles]] or [[contact lenses]] (especially in advanced keratoconus or dense scarring).
- **[[Irregular astigmatism]]**: Distortion, monocular [[diplopia]], ghosting of images.
- **[[Photophobia]]**: Particularly with anterior stromal scarring or dystrophic deposits.
- **[[Glare]] and halos**: Due to irregular corneal surface and stromal opacities.
- **Contact lens intolerance**: Inability to achieve adequate fit or comfort, especially in keratoconus.
- **Ocular surface discomfort**: Foreign body sensation in cases with epithelial irregularity overlying stromal pathology.

### Signs

- Central or paracentral [[corneal opacity]] or scarring
- Corneal thinning and [[ectasia]] (e.g., Munson's sign, Vogt's striae, [[Fleischer ring]] in keratoconus)
- Stromal deposits (crystalline, granular, lattice, or macular patterns in dystrophies)
- Irregular corneal topography on [[corneal topography|topographic]] and [[corneal tomography|tomographic]] imaging
- Preserved corneal endothelial cell density and morphology on [[specular microscopy]]

## Diagnostic Evaluation

A comprehensive preoperative assessment is critical to confirm the indication for DALK, rule out endothelial dysfunction, and plan the surgical approach.

### Diagnostic Modalities

| Modality | Purpose in DALK Evaluation |
|---|---|
| [[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] | Assess depth and extent of stromal pathology; evaluate for Descemet's folds, guttae, or endothelial abnormalities |
| [[Corneal topography]] (e.g., [[Placido disc]]-based) | Quantify surface irregularity, keratoconus indices (e.g., [[KISA%]], [[I-S value]]) |
| [[Corneal tomography]] (e.g., [[Pentacam]], [[Orbscan]], [[Galilei]]) | Full pachymetric mapping; anterior and posterior elevation maps; assess thinnest point; detect ectasia |
| [[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT) — [[anterior segment OCT]] (AS-OCT) | High-resolution cross-sectional imaging of corneal layers; determine depth of stromal pathology; assess Descemet's membrane integrity; measure residual stromal bed thickness |
| [[Specular microscopy]] | **Essential**: Confirm adequate [[endothelial cell density]] (ECD) and normal [[endothelial cell morphology]] ([[polymegathism]], [[pleomorphism]]). An ECD >1,500–2,000 cells/mm² is generally required for DALK candidacy |
| [[Confocal microscopy]] (e.g., [[Heidelberg Retina Tomograph]] with Rostock Cornea Module) | In vivo cellular-level assessment of keratocyte density, nerve plexus, and depth of pathology |
| [[Corneal pachymetry]] | Central and peripheral thickness measurements; critical for surgical planning |
| [[Visual acuity]] testing (BCVA, with spectacles and contact lenses) | Document baseline acuity; confirm that visual impairment warrants surgical intervention |
| [[Keratometry]] / [[Corneal power]] assessment | Baseline measurements for postoperative comparison and [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) calculation if combined surgery is planned |
| [[Endothelial cell count]] of donor tissue | Ensure donor cornea quality (though donor endothelium is removed in DALK, overall tissue quality matters) |

### Indications for DALK Over Penetrating Keratoplasty

DALK is preferred over [[penetrating keratoplasty]] when:

1. The corneal endothelium is healthy and functional (ECD >1,500 cells/mm²).
2. Pathology is limited to the anterior and/or stromal layers.
3. There is no concurrent endothelial disease (e.g., [[Fuchs' endothelial dystrophy]], [[posterior polymorphous dystrophy]]).
4. The patient is young and long-term graft survival is a priority (reduced rejection risk).
5. The patient is at high risk for traumatic wound dehiscence (e.g., children, athletes, monocular patients) — the closed-globe nature of DALK provides superior structural integrity.

### Contraindications

- **Absolute**: Concurrent endothelial dysfunction; full-thickness corneal pathology involving Descemet's membrane and endothelium (e.g., combined stromal and endothelial disease).
- **Relative**: Extensive Descemet's membrane scarring or breaks (e.g., advanced [[hydrops]] in keratoconus with Descemet's rupture); previous failed DALK with Descemet's membrane damage; very deep stromal [[neovascularization]] extending to Descemet's membrane.

## Surgical Technique

### Preoperative Preparation

- Informed consent discussing risks, benefits, and alternatives (including PK).
- Donor corneal tissue procurement from an [[eye bank]]; tissue is evaluated for endothelial quality (though the donor endothelium will be removed, overall tissue health affects stromal clarity).
- Preoperative topical [[antibiotic]] prophylaxis (e.g., [[moxifloxacin]] or [[gatifloxacin]]).
- General or [[retrobulbar anesthesia]] / [[peribulbar anesthesia]]; occasionally [[topical anesthesia]] with sedation.

### Key Surgical Steps

#### 1. Trephination

A partial-thickness [[trephination]] of the host cornea is performed to approximately 60–80% depth using a vacuum trephine (e.g., Hessburg-Barron, Hanna), a [[femtosecond laser]], or a manual trephine. The trephine diameter is typically 7.5–8.5 mm, depending on the extent of pathology and corneal diameter.

#### 2. Stromal Dissection and Removal

Several techniques exist for deep stromal removal:

**a. Big-Bubble Technique (Anwar & Teichmann)**

This is the most widely adopted method:

1. After partial trephination, a 27- or 30-gauge needle is inserted into the deep stroma, directed toward the center of the cornea, at a depth of approximately 80% of corneal thickness.
2. Air (or, in some modifications, [[viscoelastic]]) is injected steadily.
3. A **type-1 big bubble** forms when air separates the posterior stroma from Descemet's membrane, creating a large, well-defined dome. This is the ideal outcome, yielding a smooth, bare Descemet's membrane surface.
4. A **type-2 big bubble** occurs when air dissects between Descemet's membrane and [[Dua's layer]] (pre-Descemet's layer), producing a smaller, more opaque bubble. This plane is also acceptable but requires more careful dissection.
5. A **mixed bubble** (type-1 and type-2 coexisting) may also occur.
6. Once the bubble is formed, the anterior stroma is excised layer by layer using a crescent blade or spatula, and the deep stromal lamella is carefully peeled away, exposing the bare Descemet's membrane.

**b. Manual Layer-by-Layer Dissection**

If the big-bubble technique fails (reported in 10–30% of cases depending on surgeon experience and corneal pathology), the surgeon proceeds with careful manual dissection using a Melles spatula or similar instrument, removing stroma in successive layers until a thin residual stromal bed overlying Descemet's membrane is achieved. This "pre-Descemetic" DALK leaves a thin layer of posterior stroma (typically <20 µm) over Descemet's membrane.

**c. Viscoelastic Dissection (Melles Technique)**

Injection of a [[viscoelastic]] substance (e.g., [[sodium hyaluronate]]) into the deep stroma to achieve hydrodissection of the stromal-Descemet's interface.

**d. Femtosecond Laser-Assisted DALK**

The [[femtosecond laser]] can be used for precise trephination and lamellar cuts at predetermined depths, potentially improving the reproducibility of the dissection plane.

#### 3. Donor Preparation

The donor corneal button is trephined (typically 0.25–0.50 mm larger than the recipient bed to reduce postoperative [[myopia]] and improve wound apposition). The donor [[Descemet's membrane]] and [[endothelium]] are carefully peeled off, leaving a full-thickness stromal-Bowman's-epithelial donor disc.

#### 4. Graft Placement and Suturing

The donor tissue is placed onto the recipient bed (bare Descemet's membrane or pre-Descemetic stroma) and secured with sutures. Suturing techniques include:

- **Interrupted 10-0 [[nylon]] sutures** (16 sutures is typical)
- **Single continuous 10-0 nylon suture**
- **Combined interrupted and continuous sutures**

Suture tension is adjusted to minimize postoperative [[astigmatism]]. Air or [[balanced salt solution]] (BSS) may be injected into the [[anterior chamber]] to tamponade Descemet's membrane against the donor tissue and prevent interface fluid accumulation.

#### 5. Intraoperative Complications and Conversion

The most significant intraoperative complication is **Descemet's membrane perforation (microperforation or macroperforation)**:

| Perforation Type | Size | Management |
|---|---|---|
| Microperforation | <1–2 mm | Inject air into the anterior chamber; proceed with DALK. Usually seals spontaneously. |
| Macroperforation | >2 mm | May require conversion to [[penetrating keratoplasty]] if the tear is large and cannot be managed. Small-to-moderate tears may be managed with air tamponade and careful suturing. |

Reported Descemet's membrane perforation rates range from **4% to 39%** in published series, with lower rates in experienced surgeons and higher rates in eyes with prior hydrops, deep scarring, or corneal thinning.

## Postoperative Management

### Pharmacological Interventions

| Medication Class | Examples | Purpose | Typical Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Topical [[corticosteroids]] | [[Prednisolone acetate]] 1%, [[dexamethasone]] 0.1% | Suppress inflammation and reduce stromal rejection risk | Tapered over 6–12 months (much shorter taper than PK) |
| Topical [[antibiotics]] | [[Moxifloxacin]] 0.5%, [[ofloxacin]] 0.3% | Prevent [[infectious keratitis]] | 2–4 weeks postoperatively |
| Topical [[lubricants]] | Preservative-free [[artificial tears]], [[hyaluronic acid]]-based drops | Promote epithelial healing; manage [[dry eye]] | As needed; often long-term |
| [[Cycloplegic]] agents | [[Atropine]] 1%, [[cyclopentolate]] 1% | Reduce ciliary spasm and pain; stabilize the [[blood-aqueous barrier]] | 1–2 weeks |
| Oral [[analgesics]] | [[Acetaminophen]], [[NSAIDs]] | Pain management | As needed in early postoperative period |

**Note**: Unlike [[penetrating keratoplasty]], DALK does **not** require long-term systemic [[immunosuppression]] and typically requires a shorter course of topical corticosteroids because the risk of endothelial rejection is eliminated. Stromal rejection can still occur but is far less common and less visually threatening.

### Suture Management

- Selective suture removal begins at **3–6 months** postoperatively, guided by [[corneal topography]] to reduce astigmatism.
- All sutures are typically removed by **12–18 months**.
- [[Refraction]] and [[spectacle]] or [[contact lens]] fitting are optimized after suture removal.

### Postoperative Monitoring Schedule

| Time Point | Key Assessments |
|---|---|
| Day 1 | Graft clarity, epithelial integrity, anterior chamber depth, [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP), Descemet's membrane apposition |
| Week 1 | Epithelial healing, graft edema, interface fluid, IOP |
| Month 1 | Visual acuity, graft clarity, suture integrity, IOP |
| Months 3–6 | Topography-guided selective suture removal; refraction |
| Months 6–12 | Continued suture management; monitor for rejection |
| Annually | Long-term graft clarity, endothelial cell count, refraction, IOP |

## Complications

### Early Complications

- **Descemet's membrane detachment / interface fluid**: Managed with air or [[sulfur hexafluoride]] (SF₆) gas injection into the anterior chamber.
- **Double anterior chamber (DM detachment)**: Occurs when fluid or air accumulates between the donor stroma and the recipient Descemet's membrane. Requires re-bubbling with air or gas.
- **Epithelial defect**: Usually heals within 5–7 days; managed with lubricants and [[bandage contact lens]].
- **Elevated [[intraocular pressure]]**: May result from retained air or steroid response; managed with [[anti-glaucoma medications]].
- **[[Infectious keratitis]]**: Rare; requires aggressive topical and potentially systemic antimicrobial therapy.
- **Microperforation-related aqueous leak**: Usually self-limited.

### Late Complications

- **[[Astigmatism]]**: The most common refractive complication; managed with selective suture removal, [[rigid gas permeable]] (RGP) contact lenses, [[scleral lenses]], or secondary refractive procedures (e.g., [[photorefractive keratectomy]], [[LASIK]], [[arcuate keratotomy]]).
- **Interface haze/opacity**: More common when a pre-Descemetic dissection (residual stroma over DM) is performed rather than a bare Descemet's membrane dissection. May limit best-corrected visual acuity.
- **Stromal graft rejection**: Presents as stromal haze, [[keratic precipitates]] on the donor stroma (not on the endothelium), and decreased vision. Treated with intensive topical corticosteroids. Importantly, **endothelial rejection does not occur** in DALK (unless there was an unrecognized Descemet's membrane perforation with donor endothelial tissue transplanted).
- **Graft failure**: Rare; may result from persistent interface opacity, recurrence of stromal dystrophy, or secondary endothelial decompensation (uncommon).
- **Recurrence of primary disease**: Stromal dystrophies (especially [[lattice dystrophy]] and [[granular dystrophy]]) may recur in the donor tissue over years to decades.
- **[[Ectasia]] recurrence**: Rare; reported in keratoconus if the disease process affects the residual host tissue.
- **Wound dehiscence**: Less common and less catastrophic than in PK because the globe remains "closed" (Descemet's membrane and endothelium are intact). This is a major safety advantage of DALK, particularly in trauma-prone populations.

## Visual Outcomes

Visual outcomes after DALK have improved significantly with the adoption of the big-bubble technique, which achieves a bare Descemet's membrane interface:

| Parameter | Bare DM (Big-Bubble) DALK | Pre-Descemetic DALK | Penetrating Keratoplasty |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mean postoperative BCVA | 20/25 – 20/40 | 20/30 – 20/50 | 20/25 – 20/40 |
| Interface haze | Minimal to none | Mild to moderate | N/A (full thickness) |
| Endothelial rejection rate | **0%** | **0%** | 5–30% (lifetime) |
| Endothelial cell loss (5 years) | 2–10% (physiological) | 2–10% | 30–50% |
| Graft survival (10 years) | >90% | >85% | 60–80% |
| Astigmatism | Comparable to PK | Comparable to PK | Comparable |

Studies consistently demonstrate that when a bare Descemet's membrane interface is achieved, DALK provides visual acuity outcomes comparable to PK, with the added benefits of dramatically reduced rejection risk and superior long-term endothelial cell preservation.

## DALK Versus Penetrating Keratoplasty: Comparative Summary

| Feature | DALK | [[Penetrating Keratoplasty]] |
|---|---|---|
| Globe integrity during surgery | Closed (DM intact) | Open-sky |
| Risk of expulsive [[choroidal hemorrhage]] | Virtually eliminated | Present |
| Endothelial rejection | **Eliminated** | Major risk (leading cause of graft failure) |
| Stromal rejection | Possible (rare, treatable) | Possible |
| Long-term endothelial cell loss | Minimal | Significant |
| Steroid duration | Shorter taper | Prolonged (often >1 year) |
| Wound strength | Superior | Inferior (full-thickness wound) |
| Surgical complexity | Higher (learning curve) | Moderate |
| Conversion to PK | Possible if DM perforation | N/A |
| Visual acuity (bare DM) | Comparable to PK | Reference standard |
| Graft survival (long-term) | Superior | Good but limited by rejection |

## Special Considerations

### DALK in Pediatric Patients

DALK is increasingly favored in children with keratoconus or stromal dystrophies because:
- Children are at higher risk of traumatic wound dehiscence (active lifestyles).
- Long-term graft survival is paramount given their life expectancy.
- Elimination of endothelial rejection reduces the need for prolonged immunosuppression and compliance-dependent steroid regimens.

### DALK After Acute [[Corneal Hydrops]]

Acute hydrops in keratoconus involves a break in Descemet's membrane with aqueous influx into the stroma. After resolution (typically 2–3 months), Descemet's membrane heals with scarring. DALK can still be performed, but the big-bubble technique may be more difficult due to Descemet's membrane irregularity and scarring. Conversion rates to PK are higher in post-hydrops eyes.

### DALK and [[Corneal Cross-Linking]] (CXL)

In early-to-moderate keratoconus, [[corneal cross-linking]] may halt progression and delay or obviate the need for DALK. In cases where DALK is performed, some surgeons perform simultaneous CXL of the donor tissue ("DALK-Xtra") to theoretically reduce the risk of ectasia recurrence, though evidence for this practice remains limited.

### Repeat DALK

DALK can be repeated if the initial graft fails (e.g., due to interface opacity or dystrophy recurrence), provided the host endothelium remains healthy. This is a significant advantage over PK, where repeat surgery carries progressively higher rejection risk.

## Prognosis

The prognosis following DALK is excellent, particularly for keratoconus:

- **Graft survival rates** exceed **90–95% at 5 years** and **>90% at 10 years** in most published series, significantly superior to PK.
- **Visual rehabilitation** is achieved in the majority of patients, with most attaining a [[best-corrected visual acuity]] (BCVA) of 20/40 or better.
- **Endothelial cell density** remains stable over time, as the host endothelium is not subject to allograft-mediated immune attack.
- **Quality of life** improvements are substantial, with patients reporting reduced glare, improved contrast sensitivity, and decreased dependence on contact lenses.

The primary limitation remains the **surgical learning curve**: the big-bubble technique requires significant experience, and Descemet's membrane perforation rates decrease substantially with surgeon experience (from >30% in early cases to <5% in expert hands).

---

## Summary

Deep Anterior Lamellar Keratoplasty represents a paradigm shift in the surgical management of corneal stromal disease. By preserving the host [[Descemet's membrane]] and [[corneal endothelium]], DALK eliminates the risk of [[endothelial graft rejection]], provides superior long-term graft survival, maintains endothelial cell density, and offers enhanced globe integrity compared to [[penetrating keratoplasty]]. With the refinement of the big-bubble technique and advances in surgical instrumentation, DALK has become the procedure of choice for [[keratoconus]], [[corneal dystrophies]], and other stromal pathologies with intact endothelial function. Optometrists play a critical role in the preoperative evaluation, postoperative co-management, and long-term monitoring of DALK patients, including refractive rehabilitation, suture management guidance, and surveillance for complications.
