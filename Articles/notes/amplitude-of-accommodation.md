

# Amplitude of Accommodation

**Amplitude of Accommodation (AA)** is a fundamental measurement in clinical [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] that quantifies the maximum increase in [[dioptric power]] that the [[crystalline lens]] of the eye can achieve when shifting focus from a distant object to the nearest point at which an object can be held in clear focus. It represents the total accommodative capacity of the eye and is expressed in [[diopters]] (D). The amplitude of accommodation is one of the most clinically significant parameters in the assessment of the [[accommodative system]], as its decline is a universal, age-related physiological process that ultimately manifests as [[presbyopia]]. Accurate measurement and interpretation of the amplitude of accommodation are essential for the diagnosis of accommodative dysfunctions, the prescription of appropriate [[refractive correction]], and the management of a wide range of near-vision complaints encountered in clinical practice.

---

## Definition and Fundamental Concepts

The amplitude of accommodation is defined as the difference in [[refractive power]] of the eye between its fully relaxed state (focused at the [[far point]]) and its maximally accommodated state (focused at the [[near point]]). Mathematically, it is expressed as:

> **AA = 1/Near Point (in meters) − 1/Far Point (in meters)**

For an [[emmetropic]] eye with a far point at optical infinity, the formula simplifies to:

> **AA = 1/Near Point (in meters)**

For example, if the nearest point of clear vision is 10 cm (0.1 m), the amplitude of accommodation is 10.00 D. In a [[myopic]] or [[hyperopic]] individual, the far point must be accounted for in the calculation.

Key terminology associated with amplitude of accommodation includes:

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **[[Near Point of Accommodation]] (NPA)** | The closest point at which an object can be seen clearly with maximum accommodative effort |
| **[[Far Point]]** | The farthest point at which an object can be seen clearly without accommodative effort |
| **[[Punctum Proximum]]** | Latin term for the near point of accommodation |
| **[[Punctum Remotum]]** | Latin term for the far point |
| **[[Accommodative Demand]]** | The amount of accommodation required to focus at a given working distance |
| **[[Accommodative Response]]** | The actual amount of accommodation exerted by the eye in response to a stimulus |
| **[[Lag of Accommodation]]** | The difference between accommodative demand and accommodative response, typically 0.25–0.75 D |

The amplitude of accommodation is a monocular measurement, though it can also be assessed binocularly. The binocular amplitude of accommodation is typically approximately 0.50 D greater than the monocular measurement due to the synergistic effect of [[convergence]]-driven accommodation (the [[accommodative convergence]] relationship).

## Anatomy and Physiology of Accommodation

### The Accommodative Apparatus

The process of [[accommodation]] is mediated by a complex anatomical and neurophysiological system. The principal structures involved include:

- **[[Crystalline Lens]]**: A biconvex, transparent, avascular structure located posterior to the [[iris]] and anterior to the [[vitreous humor]]. The lens is composed of an outer [[lens capsule]], an underlying [[lens epithelium]], and a core of [[lens fibers]]. The elasticity of the lens and its capsule is the primary determinant of accommodative ability.
- **[[Ciliary Body]]**: A ring-shaped muscular structure that encircles the lens. The [[ciliary muscle]], composed of longitudinal (Brücke's), radial, and circular (Müller's) fibers, is the effector muscle of accommodation.
- **[[Zonular Fibers]] (Zonules of Zinn)**: Suspensory ligaments that connect the [[ciliary body]] to the equator of the [[crystalline lens]]. These fibers transmit the mechanical forces generated by ciliary muscle contraction to the lens.
- **[[Parasympathetic Nervous System]]**: Accommodation is primarily driven by parasympathetic innervation via the [[oculomotor nerve]] (cranial nerve III). Preganglionic fibers originate in the [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]] and synapse in the [[ciliary ganglion]]. Postganglionic fibers travel via the [[short ciliary nerves]] to innervate the ciliary muscle.

### The Helmholtz Mechanism of Accommodation

The most widely accepted theory of accommodation is the **[[Helmholtz theory]]**, proposed by [[Hermann von Helmholtz]] in 1855. According to this model:

1. **Unaccommodated (Distant Vision) State**: The [[ciliary muscle]] is relaxed. The [[zonular fibers]] are under tension, pulling the equator of the [[crystalline lens]] radially outward. This flattens the lens, reducing its dioptric power, and the eye is focused for distance.

2. **Accommodated (Near Vision) State**: The [[ciliary muscle]] contracts, primarily the circular (Müller's) fibers. This contraction moves the ciliary body anteriorly and inward, releasing tension on the [[zonular fibers]]. The elastic [[lens capsule]] then molds the lens into a more spherical shape, increasing its anterior and posterior surface curvatures, increasing its axial thickness, and thereby increasing its dioptric power. The eye is now focused for near objects.

The amplitude of accommodation thus reflects the maximum change in lens power achievable through this mechanism. The [[Schachar theory]] offers an alternative explanation, proposing that equatorial zonular tension increases during accommodation, but this model has not gained widespread acceptance in the clinical literature.

### The Near Triad

Accommodation does not occur in isolation. It is part of the **[[near triad]]** (also called the near reflex or accommodative triad), which consists of three synkinetic responses:

1. **[[Accommodation]]** – Increase in lens power for near focus
2. **[[Convergence]]** – Simultaneous inward rotation of both eyes to maintain [[binocular fixation]] on a near target
3. **[[Pupillary Miosis]]** – Constriction of the [[pupil]] to increase depth of focus and reduce [[spherical aberration]]

The interrelationship between accommodation and convergence is quantified by the **[[AC/A ratio]]** (accommodative convergence to accommodation ratio), a critical parameter in the evaluation of [[binocular vision]] disorders.

## Pathophysiology

### Age-Related Decline: Presbyopia

The amplitude of accommodation undergoes a predictable, progressive decline throughout life. This decline is the pathophysiological basis of **[[presbyopia]]**, the most common accommodative disorder worldwide. The mechanisms underlying this decline are multifactorial:

1. **Lenticular Sclerosis**: With aging, the [[crystalline lens]] undergoes progressive compaction and hardening (sclerosis) of the [[lens nucleus]]. New lens fibers are continuously deposited throughout life, but older fibers in the nucleus become increasingly rigid and less deformable. This reduces the ability of the lens to change shape in response to ciliary muscle contraction.

2. **Loss of Lens Capsule Elasticity**: The [[lens capsule]] also loses elasticity with age, diminishing its ability to mold the lens into a more convex shape during accommodation.

3. **Changes in Lens Geometry**: The lens increases in equatorial diameter and axial thickness with age, altering the geometric relationship between the lens, [[zonular fibers]], and [[ciliary body]].

4. **Ciliary Muscle Changes**: Although the [[ciliary muscle]] retains contractile ability well into old age, age-related changes in its configuration and the posterior attachment of the [[zonular fibers]] may reduce the efficiency of force transmission to the lens.

5. **Vitreous and Posterior Segment Changes**: Some researchers have proposed that age-related changes in the [[vitreous body]] and posterior lens support may contribute to the decline in accommodation, though this remains an area of active investigation.

The classic data compiled by **Duane (1912)** and later by **Hofstetter** established normative values for the expected amplitude of accommodation at various ages. Hofstetter's formulas remain the most widely used clinical reference:

| Formula | Equation |
|---|---|
| **Maximum expected AA** | AA = 25 − 0.4 × age |
| **Average expected AA** | AA = 18.5 − 0.3 × age |
| **Minimum expected AA** | AA = 15 − 0.25 × age |

These formulas predict, for example, that a 40-year-old patient would have a minimum expected amplitude of 5.00 D, an average of 6.50 D, and a maximum of 9.00 D. By age 50–55, the amplitude typically falls below 2.00 D, rendering unaided near vision at typical working distances functionally inadequate.

### Accommodative Insufficiency

**[[Accommodative insufficiency]]** is a non-presbyopic condition in which the amplitude of accommodation is significantly lower than the age-expected minimum (typically at least 2.00 D below the Hofstetter minimum). It is the most common accommodative dysfunction in children and young adults and must be differentiated from [[presbyopia]], which is an age-appropriate decline. Etiologies include:

- Prolonged near work and [[visual fatigue]]
- Systemic illness (e.g., [[diabetes mellitus]], [[anemia]], [[multiple sclerosis]])
- Head trauma or [[traumatic brain injury]] (TBI)
- Pharmacological agents (e.g., [[anticholinergic drugs]], [[sympathomimetic agents]], [[antihistamines]], [[antidepressants]])
- [[Adie's tonic pupil]] (parasympathetic denervation)
- Chronic fatigue and general debility
- Poorly corrected [[refractive error]], particularly [[hyperopia]]

### Other Accommodative Dysfunctions Related to Amplitude

While the amplitude of accommodation is the primary metric for accommodative insufficiency and presbyopia, it is also relevant in the differential diagnosis of other accommodative disorders:

- **[[Accommodative Excess]] (Spasm of Accommodation)**: A condition in which the accommodative system is over-active, sometimes producing a falsely high or variable amplitude measurement. Patients may present with [[pseudomyopia]].
- **[[Accommodative Infacility]]**: Difficulty in rapidly changing accommodative focus between near and far targets. The amplitude may be normal, but the dynamics of accommodation are impaired.
- **[[Ill-Sustained Accommodation]]**: The initial amplitude of accommodation is normal, but the patient cannot maintain accommodative effort over time, leading to progressive blur during sustained near tasks.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Patients with a reduced amplitude of accommodation—whether due to [[presbyopia]], [[accommodative insufficiency]], or other causes—typically present with a characteristic constellation of symptoms related to near-vision tasks:

- **Blurred near vision**: The hallmark symptom. Patients report difficulty reading, using digital devices, or performing other close-up tasks. Text may appear blurred or may initially be clear but become progressively blurred with sustained effort ([[ill-sustained accommodation]]).
- **[[Asthenopia]]**: Eye strain, discomfort, or a sensation of "pulling" or "aching" around the eyes, particularly after prolonged near work.
- **Headaches**: Frontal or periorbital headaches, often exacerbated by reading or near tasks.
- **Difficulty with focus changes**: Patients may report that vision is slow to clear when shifting gaze from near to far or vice versa.
- **Holding reading material at arm's length**: A classic behavioral adaptation in early [[presbyopia]], as increasing the working distance reduces the accommodative demand.
- **Need for increased illumination**: Patients may seek brighter lighting for near tasks, as increased illumination induces [[pupillary miosis]], which increases [[depth of focus]] and partially compensates for reduced accommodation.
- **Avoidance of near tasks**: In children and young adults with [[accommodative insufficiency]], avoidance of reading or homework may be misinterpreted as a behavioral or attention issue.
- **[[Diplopia]]**: Occasionally, reduced accommodation may disrupt the [[accommodation-convergence]] relationship, leading to intermittent [[binocular vision]] difficulties.

In pediatric populations, symptoms may be less clearly articulated. Clinicians should maintain a high index of suspicion for accommodative dysfunction in children presenting with reading difficulties, poor academic performance, or nonspecific visual complaints.

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical Measurement Techniques

Several standardized methods exist for measuring the amplitude of accommodation. The choice of technique may influence the measured value, and clinicians should be aware of the relative advantages and limitations of each.

#### 1. Push-Up Method (Donders' Method)

The **push-up method** is the most commonly used clinical technique for measuring the amplitude of accommodation. It was originally described by **[[Franciscus Donders]]**.

**Procedure:**
1. The patient wears their best-corrected distance [[refractive correction]].
2. One eye is occluded for monocular testing.
3. A near-point card with an appropriate acuity target (typically one line above the patient's best near [[visual acuity]]) is held at arm's length and slowly moved toward the patient along a ruler or [[RAF rule]] (Royal Air Force near-point rule).
4. The patient is instructed to report the first sustained blur of the target.
5. The distance from the [[spectacle plane]] (or [[corneal plane]]) to the target at the point of first sustained blur is recorded.
6. The amplitude of accommodation is calculated as the reciprocal of this distance in meters (AA = 1/NPA in meters).

**Advantages:** Simple, rapid, requires minimal equipment.

**Limitations:** Tends to overestimate the true amplitude of accommodation by approximately 1.00–2.00 D due to the increasing angular subtense of the target as it approaches the eye (proximal cue effect) and the contribution of [[depth of focus]].

#### 2. Push-Down (Pull-Away) Method

The **push-down method** is the reverse of the push-up technique.

**Procedure:**
1. The target is initially held very close to the eye (within the blur zone).
2. The target is slowly moved away from the patient until the first point of sustained clarity is reported.
3. The amplitude is calculated as the reciprocal of this distance.

**Advantages:** May provide a more conservative and potentially more accurate estimate of the amplitude, as it reduces the influence of depth of focus.

**Limitations:** Less commonly used; some patients find it more difficult to identify the point of "first clarity" compared to "first blur."

#### 3. Minus Lens Method (Sheard's Method)

The **minus lens method** uses progressively increasing [[minus lenses]] to stimulate accommodation while the patient views a fixed near target.

**Procedure:**
1. The patient wears their best-corrected distance correction and fixates a near target at a standard distance (typically 40 cm or 33 cm).
2. Minus lenses are introduced in −0.25 D steps (using a [[phoropter]] or trial lens set) over the distance correction.
3. The patient reports when the target first becomes and remains blurred.
4. The amplitude of accommodation is calculated as the sum of the accommodative demand at the test distance plus the total minus lens power added before sustained blur.

For example, at 40 cm (2.50 D demand), if the patient accepts −4.00 D of additional minus before blur, the amplitude is 2.50 + 4.00 = 6.50 D.

**Advantages:** Eliminates the proximal cue artifact inherent in the push-up method; generally considered more accurate.

**Limitations:** Tends to yield lower values (approximately 1.00–2.00 D less) than the push-up method; more time-consuming; requires a phoropter or trial lens set.

#### 4. Dynamic Retinoscopy

**[[Dynamic retinoscopy]]** (e.g., [[Monocular Estimation Method]] (MEM) retinoscopy, [[Nott retinoscopy]]) provides an objective assessment of the accommodative response at near. While not a direct measure of the amplitude of accommodation, it is invaluable in assessing the accuracy of the accommodative response (i.e., [[lag of accommodation]] or [[lead of accommodation]]) and can provide indirect information about accommodative function.

#### 5. Autorefraction and Aberrometry

Modern [[autorefractors]] and [[wavefront aberrometers]] with dynamic accommodation measurement capabilities can objectively quantify the accommodative response and, in some instruments, estimate the amplitude of accommodation. These instruments are particularly useful in research settings and in patients who cannot provide reliable subjective responses (e.g., young children, cognitively impaired individuals).

### Comparison of Measurement Methods

| Method | Type | Typical AA Value Relative to Push-Up | Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Push-Up (Donders') | Subjective | Reference standard (highest values) | Simple, fast, minimal equipment | Overestimates AA due to proximal cues and depth of focus |
| Push-Down | Subjective | Slightly lower than push-up | Reduces depth of focus artifact | Less intuitive for patients |
| Minus Lens (Sheard's) | Subjective | ~1.00–2.00 D lower than push-up | Eliminates proximal cue; more accurate | More time-consuming; requires lens set |
| Dynamic Retinoscopy | Objective | Measures response, not amplitude directly | Objective; useful in children | Does not directly measure AA |
| Autorefraction | Objective | Variable | Objective; repeatable | Instrument-dependent; expensive |

### Normative Data and Clinical Interpretation

When interpreting the measured amplitude of accommodation, the clinician should compare the result to age-expected norms using **Hofstetter's formulas** (see Pathophysiology section). A measured amplitude that falls at or below the minimum expected value for the patient's age is considered clinically significant and warrants further investigation and management.

**Clinical Rule of Thumb:** Patients should have an amplitude of accommodation at least **twice** the habitual near working demand to maintain comfortable, sustained near vision. For a standard near working distance of 40 cm (2.50 D demand), the patient should ideally have at least 5.00 D of amplitude. This principle is sometimes referred to as **Percival's criterion** for comfortable accommodation, though it is more commonly attributed to general clinical guidelines.

## Differential Diagnosis

When a patient presents with a reduced amplitude of accommodation, the clinician must consider a broad differential diagnosis:

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| **[[Presbyopia]]** | Age-appropriate decline (typically >40 years); bilateral; progressive |
| **[[Accommodative Insufficiency]]** | Below age-expected minimum; often in children/young adults; may be unilateral or bilateral |
| **[[Ill-Sustained Accommodation]]** | Initial amplitude normal; declines with sustained effort |
| **[[Accommodative Excess]]** | Variable or falsely elevated amplitude; associated with [[pseudomyopia]] |
| **[[Accommodative Infacility]]** | Amplitude may be normal; difficulty with rapid focus changes |
| **Pharmacologically induced** | History of [[anticholinergic]], [[sympathomimetic]], or other drug use |
| **Neurological causes** | [[Cranial nerve III palsy]], [[Adie's tonic pupil]], [[traumatic brain injury]], [[demyelinating disease]] |
| **Systemic disease** | [[Diabetes mellitus]], [[Graves' disease]], [[myasthenia gravis]], [[anemia]] |
| **Uncorrected [[hyperopia]]** | Latent hyperopia consumes accommodative reserve; correcting the refractive error may restore normal amplitude |

## Management and Treatment

### Optical Correction

The primary management of reduced amplitude of accommodation—particularly in [[presbyopia]]—is the prescription of appropriate optical correction to supplement the deficient accommodative system.

#### Single Vision Reading Lenses

**[[Single vision]] reading glasses** or near-addition lenses provide a fixed amount of plus power to reduce the accommodative demand at the desired working distance. The required addition is calculated based on the patient's amplitude of accommodation, habitual working distance, and the principle that only approximately **one-half to two-thirds** of the total amplitude should be used for sustained comfortable near work.

**Add Calculation Example:**
- Patient age: 48 years
- Measured AA: 3.50 D (push-up)
- Desired working distance: 40 cm (2.50 D demand)
- Comfortable sustained accommodation: 3.50 × 0.50 = 1.75 D
- Required add: 2.50 − 1.75 = +0.75 D

#### Bifocal and Multifocal Lenses

**[[Bifocal lenses]]** provide two distinct optical zones: one for distance and one for near. **[[Progressive addition lenses]] (PALs)** offer a continuous gradient of increasing plus power from the distance zone to the near zone, providing clear vision at all intermediate distances. These are the most commonly prescribed lens designs for presbyopic patients.

#### Contact Lens Options

- **[[Multifocal contact lenses]]**: Available in simultaneous vision (concentric or aspheric) and alternating (translating) designs.
- **[[Monovision]]**: One eye is corrected for distance and the other for near. This approach sacrifices some [[stereoacuity]] but is well-tolerated by many patients.
- **Modified monovision**: Combines a single-vision distance lens in one eye with a multifocal lens in the other.

### Vision Therapy / Accommodative Training

For non-presbyopic patients with [[accommodative insufficiency]], [[ill-sustained accommodation]], or [[accommodative infacility]], **[[vision therapy]]** (also known as orthoptic exercises or accommodative training) is a well-established, evidence-based treatment approach. The goal is to improve the amplitude, accuracy, flexibility, and sustainability of the accommodative response.

Common vision therapy techniques include:

- **Push-up accommodative training**: The patient practices focusing on a near target as it is moved progressively closer, attempting to maintain clarity at shorter distances over successive sessions.
- **Minus lens flippers**: The patient alternates between viewing through minus lenses (stimulating accommodation) and plus lenses (relaxing accommodation) to improve [[accommodative facility]]. Standard flipper powers are ±2.00 D.
- **Hart chart exercises**: The patient alternates fixation between a near Hart chart and a distant Hart chart, reading letters at each distance to train accommodative flexibility.
- **Computer-based accommodative training programs**: Software programs that provide controlled accommodative stimuli and track patient performance.

A typical vision therapy program for accommodative insufficiency may span **12–24 sessions** (in-office, typically once or twice weekly) supplemented by daily home exercises. Studies have demonstrated significant improvements in amplitude of accommodation and symptom resolution with structured vision therapy programs.

### Pharmacological Interventions

Pharmacological agents play a limited but important role in the management of conditions affecting the amplitude of accommodation:

- **[[Pilocarpine]]**: A [[parasympathomimetic]] (muscarinic agonist) that stimulates ciliary muscle contraction. Low-dose pilocarpine (e.g., 1.25%) has been investigated and approved (as **Vuity™**, pilocarpine HCl ophthalmic solution 1.25%) for the pharmacological treatment of [[presbyopia]]. By inducing [[miosis]], it increases [[depth of focus]] and can temporarily improve near vision. It does not restore true accommodative amplitude but provides a functional improvement.
- **Avoidance of accommodative-suppressing drugs**: In patients with pharmacologically induced accommodative insufficiency, discontinuation or substitution of offending agents (e.g., [[anticholinergics]], [[topiramate]], [[selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors]]) may restore normal accommodative function.
- **[[Cycloplegic agents]]** (e.g., [[atropine]], [[cyclopentolate]], [[tropicamide]]): These are used diagnostically to paralyze the ciliary muscle and reveal the full [[cycloplegic refraction]], particularly in children with suspected [[latent hyperopia]] or [[accommodative spasm]]. They are not therapeutic for reduced amplitude but are essential diagnostic tools.

### Surgical and Emerging Approaches

Several surgical and device-based approaches have been developed or are under investigation for the restoration of accommodation or the treatment of [[presbyopia]]:

- **[[Refractive surgery]] for presbyopia**: Techniques such as [[monovision LASIK]], [[presbyLASIK]] (multifocal corneal ablation profiles), and [[corneal inlays]] (e.g., [[KAMRA inlay]], [[Raindrop inlay]]) aim to improve near vision through modification of the [[corneal]] optics rather than restoration of true accommodation.
- **[[Accommodating intraocular lenses]] (AIOLs)**: Implanted during [[cataract surgery]], these lenses are designed to move or change shape within the eye in response to ciliary muscle contraction, theoretically restoring some degree of accommodation. Examples include the **[[Crystalens]]** and newer designs under development. Clinical results have been variable, with most current AIOLs providing limited true accommodative amplitude (typically 1.00–2.00 D).
- **[[Multifocal intraocular lenses]]**: These IOLs (e.g., [[diffractive multifocal IOLs]], [[extended depth of focus (EDOF) IOLs]]) provide simultaneous distance and near vision through optical design rather than true accommodation.
- **Lens refilling procedures (Phaco-Ersatz)**: Experimental approaches involving removal of the sclerosed lens contents and refilling the [[lens capsule]] with a flexible polymer to restore lens deformability. This remains investigational.
- **Scleral expansion procedures**: Based on the [[Schachar theory]], these procedures (e.g., [[scleral expansion bands]]) aim to restore accommodation by expanding the [[sclera]] overlying the [[ciliary body]]. Clinical evidence for efficacy is limited, and these procedures are not widely performed.
- **Femtosecond laser lentotomy**: Experimental use of [[femtosecond laser]] to create microincisions within the [[crystalline lens]] to increase its flexibility. This approach is in early-stage research.

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Considerations

In children, the amplitude of accommodation is typically very high (12–16 D or more), and accommodative dysfunction may be overlooked. [[Accommodative insufficiency]] in children can mimic or exacerbate [[attention deficit disorder]], [[learning disabilities]], and reading difficulties. A comprehensive [[binocular vision]] assessment, including measurement of the amplitude of accommodation, [[accommodative facility]], and [[vergence]] function, should be part of every pediatric eye examination.

### Traumatic Brain Injury

Patients with [[traumatic brain injury]] (TBI), including [[concussion]], frequently exhibit accommodative dysfunction, with reduced amplitude of accommodation being one of the most common findings. Post-TBI accommodative insufficiency may be unilateral or bilateral and often coexists with [[convergence insufficiency]], [[oculomotor dysfunction]], and [[saccadic]] or [[pursuit]] deficits. Management typically involves a combination of plus lens additions for near work and structured [[vision therapy]].

### Systemic Disease

Clinicians should be aware that a reduced amplitude of accommodation in a young patient may be an early sign of systemic disease, including [[diabetes mellitus]] (which can cause transient accommodative changes due to osmotic lens swelling), [[multiple sclerosis]], [[myasthenia gravis]], or [[Wilson's disease]]. A thorough case history and, when indicated, referral for systemic evaluation are essential.

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis for patients with reduced amplitude of accommodation depends on the underlying etiology:

- **[[Presbyopia]]**: The decline is irreversible and progressive, but is effectively managed with optical correction. Virtually all patients achieve satisfactory near vision with appropriate lenses.
- **[[Accommodative insufficiency]] (non-presbyopic)**: With appropriate vision therapy and/or optical correction, the prognosis is excellent, particularly in children and young adults. Studies report success rates of 80–95% with structured therapy programs.
- **Pharmacologically induced**: Prognosis is excellent upon discontinuation of the offending agent.
- **Neurological or systemic causes**: Prognosis depends on the underlying condition and its treatability.

---

## Summary

The amplitude of accommodation is a cornerstone measurement in clinical eye care, reflecting the dynamic focusing capacity of the [[crystalline lens]] and the [[accommodative system]]. Its systematic decline with age underlies the universal condition of [[presbyopia]], while premature or excessive reduction signals accommodative dysfunction requiring targeted diagnosis and management. Clinicians must be proficient in the accurate measurement and interpretation of the amplitude of accommodation, familiar with the full spectrum of etiologies for its reduction, and knowledgeable about the range of optical, therapeutic, pharmacological, and surgical interventions available to restore comfortable and functional near vision.
