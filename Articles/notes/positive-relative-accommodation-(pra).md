

# Positive Relative Accommodation (PRA)

---

**Positive Relative Accommodation (PRA)** is a clinical measurement in [[optometry]] that quantifies the maximum amount of [[accommodation]] a patient can stimulate while maintaining clear, single [[binocular vision]] at a fixed near working distance, without altering [[vergence]] demand. It is expressed in [[diopters]] (D) and represents the ability of the [[ciliary muscle]] to increase its contractile effort beyond the tonic accommodative level required for a given near target. PRA is a fundamental component of the comprehensive [[binocular vision]] assessment and plays a critical role in determining the flexibility and reserve of the accommodative system, guiding [[refractive correction]] decisions, evaluating [[accommodative dysfunction]], and predicting patient comfort with prescribed lenses—particularly [[progressive addition lenses]] (PALs), [[bifocals]], and near-vision corrections.

PRA is tested in conjunction with its counterpart, [[Negative Relative Accommodation]] (NRA), and together these values define the total range of relative accommodation available to the patient. While NRA measures the ability to relax accommodation, PRA specifically isolates the capacity to *increase* accommodation. A reduced PRA value is clinically significant and may indicate [[accommodative insufficiency]], latent [[hyperopia]], over-plussed refractive corrections, or impending [[presbyopia]]. Conversely, an abnormally high PRA may suggest excessive accommodative tone or [[accommodative spasm]].

---

## Definition and Fundamental Concepts

Positive Relative Accommodation is formally defined as the maximum amount of accommodation that can be stimulated while [[convergence]] is held constant at a fixed near distance. The term "relative" denotes that accommodation is being measured *relative to* a fixed vergence posture—that is, the eyes remain converged on a near target at a set distance (typically 40 cm), and only the accommodative component is manipulated through the introduction of progressively stronger minus (concave) lenses.

### Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **[[Accommodation]]** | The dynamic process by which the [[crystalline lens]] increases its refractive power to focus on near objects, mediated by contraction of the [[ciliary muscle]] and relaxation of the [[zonules of Zinn]]. |
| **[[Relative Accommodation]]** | The range of accommodation that can be exerted while maintaining a fixed vergence angle. Divided into PRA (stimulation) and [[NRA]] (relaxation). |
| **PRA** | The maximum accommodative effort that can be stimulated beyond the baseline demand at a given near distance, measured by adding minus lenses binocularly. |
| **[[NRA]]** | The maximum amount of accommodation that can be relaxed from the baseline demand at a given near distance, measured by adding plus lenses binocularly. |
| **[[Vergence]]** | The simultaneous movement of both eyes in opposite directions to maintain [[binocular fixation]] on a target at varying distances. |
| **[[Accommodative Convergence/Accommodation (AC/A) Ratio]]** | The amount of convergence (in [[prism diopters]]) stimulated per diopter of accommodation. This ratio is intrinsically linked to PRA performance. |

The concept of PRA is rooted in [[Donders']] classical work on accommodation and the later refinements by [[Duane]] and [[Fincham]], who elucidated the synkinetic relationship between accommodation and convergence—the [[near triad]] (accommodation, convergence, and [[miosis]]). PRA testing effectively isolates the accommodative limb of this triad by constraining the vergence limb.

## Pathophysiology and Physiological Basis

### The Accommodative Mechanism

Understanding PRA requires a thorough appreciation of the [[accommodative apparatus]]. Accommodation is primarily mediated by the following anatomical structures:

1. **[[Ciliary Muscle]]**: A ring of smooth muscle within the [[ciliary body]], innervated by [[parasympathetic]] fibers from the [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]] via the [[oculomotor nerve]] (CN III) and the [[short ciliary nerves]]. Contraction of the ciliary muscle reduces the diameter of the ciliary ring.
2. **[[Zonules of Zinn]] (Zonular Fibers)**: Suspensory ligaments connecting the ciliary body to the equator of the [[crystalline lens]]. When the ciliary muscle contracts, zonular tension decreases.
3. **[[Crystalline Lens]]**: A biconvex, transparent, avascular structure that increases its curvature (particularly the anterior surface) and axial thickness when zonular tension is released, thereby increasing its [[refractive power]].
4. **[[Lens Capsule]]**: The elastic capsule surrounding the lens provides the restoring force that molds the lens into a more spherical shape when zonular tension is reduced.

During PRA testing, the introduction of minus lenses creates an artificial increase in the accommodative demand. The [[retinal image]] is rendered optically hyperopic (blurred), stimulating the [[accommodative reflex arc]]. The ciliary muscle must contract further to compensate for the additional minus power, increasing the curvature of the crystalline lens to restore a clear retinal image. This process continues until the patient reaches the maximum limit of accommodative effort—the endpoint of PRA.

### Neural Control and the Accommodative Reflex

The accommodative response is governed by a complex [[neural feedback loop]]:

- **Afferent Limb**: Blur detected at the [[retina]] (specifically the [[fovea]]) is processed by the [[visual cortex]] (area [[V1]]) and higher-order cortical areas.
- **Central Processing**: The [[accommodative controller]], believed to reside in the [[midbrain]] and [[cerebral cortex]], computes the required change in lens power.
- **Efferent Limb**: Signals travel via the [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]] → [[ciliary ganglion]] → [[short ciliary nerves]] → [[ciliary muscle]], producing contraction and increased accommodation.

PRA is limited by:

- The **maximum contractile capacity** of the ciliary muscle.
- The **elasticity of the crystalline lens** and its capsule (which diminishes with age, as in [[presbyopia]]).
- The **interaction between accommodation and convergence** via the [[AC/A ratio]]—excessive accommodative effort may drive convergence beyond the fusional vergence range, causing [[diplopia]] before the true accommodative limit is reached.
- **[[Depth of focus]]**—the inherent tolerance of the visual system to small amounts of defocus, which can mask small changes in accommodation.

### Relationship to Vergence

The synkinetic link between accommodation and convergence is central to understanding PRA. When accommodation is stimulated (as during PRA testing), accommodative convergence is also stimulated proportionally, as dictated by the [[AC/A ratio]]. To maintain single binocular vision on the fixed near target, the patient must exert [[fusional divergence]] (negative fusional vergence) to counteract the excess convergence driven by the increased accommodation. Therefore, PRA is limited not only by the accommodative system but also by the patient's **[[negative fusional vergence]] (NFV)** reserves.

This interplay means that:

- A patient with a **high AC/A ratio** will experience greater convergence demand per diopter of accommodation stimulated, exhausting fusional divergence reserves sooner and yielding a **lower PRA**.
- A patient with **low negative fusional vergence** reserves will similarly demonstrate a reduced PRA, as they cannot compensate for the accommodative convergence driven by the minus lenses.
- A patient with [[convergence excess]] may show a characteristically reduced PRA.

## Clinical Significance

PRA is clinically significant in multiple domains of optometric practice:

### 1. Assessment of Accommodative Function

PRA directly evaluates the **stimulatory reserve** of the accommodative system. A reduced PRA (less negative than expected) may indicate:

- **[[Accommodative Insufficiency]]**: Inability to exert adequate accommodation for near tasks, often presenting with [[asthenopia]], blurred near vision, and difficulty sustaining near work.
- **[[Accommodative Fatigue]]**: A condition where the accommodative system tires with sustained near effort, sometimes preceding frank insufficiency.
- **Early [[Presbyopia]]**: As the crystalline lens loses elasticity with age, the total amplitude of accommodation decreases, and PRA values decline correspondingly.
- **[[Latent Hyperopia]]**: Uncorrected or under-corrected [[hyperopia]] consumes accommodative reserve for distance, leaving less available for near stimulation.

### 2. Lens Prescription Verification

PRA and NRA are used together to verify the appropriateness of a tentative [[refraction]]:

| Scenario | PRA | NRA | Interpretation |
|----------|-----|-----|----------------|
| Balanced correction | ≈ −2.50 D | ≈ +2.50 D | Appropriate refractive balance |
| Over-plussed (too much plus power) | Reduced (less negative) | Increased (more positive) | Patient is over-corrected with plus; reduce plus power |
| Under-plussed (insufficient plus power) | Increased (more negative) | Reduced (less positive) | Patient may need more plus power |
| Equal and low | Low | Low | Reduced accommodative amplitude or binocular dysfunction |

The **binocular cross-cylinder test** and the **balance between PRA and NRA** serve as critical endpoints in the [[subjective refraction]] process. Ideally, PRA and NRA should be approximately equal in magnitude, indicating that the refractive correction places the patient's accommodative resting point symmetrically within their range.

### 3. Near Addition Determination

In [[presbyopic]] patients, PRA and NRA values help determine the optimal [[near addition]] (add power). The add is adjusted so that PRA and NRA are balanced, ensuring the patient has equal accommodative reserve in both directions. An add that is too strong will reduce PRA (the patient cannot stimulate further accommodation through the strong plus), while an add that is too weak will reduce NRA.

### 4. Binocular Vision Analysis

PRA is an integral component of comprehensive [[binocular vision]] analysis systems, including:

- **[[Morgan's Expected Values]]**: Normative data for PRA and other binocular vision parameters.
- **[[Sheard's Criterion]]**: While primarily applied to vergence, the principle of maintaining adequate reserves applies to accommodative testing.
- **[[Graphical Analysis]]** (Zone of Clear Single Binocular Vision, or [[ZCSBV]]): PRA and NRA define the accommodative boundaries of the zone.
- **[[OEP (Optometric Extension Program) Analysis]]**: Integrates PRA into a systematic evaluation of visual function.

## Diagnosis: Clinical Measurement Technique

### Standard Protocol

PRA is measured as part of the standard [[binocular refraction]] sequence, typically performed after the distance subjective refraction has been finalized and the patient is wearing their best distance correction (or tentative refraction) in a [[phoropter]] or [[trial frame]].

#### Step-by-Step Procedure

1. **Patient Setup**: The patient is seated comfortably behind the [[phoropter]] with the distance subjective refraction in place. Both eyes are open (binocular conditions).
2. **Near Target Placement**: A near-point card with a line of best [[visual acuity]] letters (typically one to two lines above threshold, e.g., 20/25 or 20/30 equivalent at near) is placed at the standard near working distance of **40 cm (16 inches)**, corresponding to a 2.50 D accommodative demand.
3. **Instruction to Patient**: The patient is instructed to keep the letters as clear as possible and to report when the letters first become blurred and cannot be cleared (the **first sustained blur** point).
4. **Lens Addition**: **Minus (concave) lenses** are added binocularly in **−0.25 D steps** at a steady rate (approximately one step every 2–3 seconds, allowing the patient time to attempt to clear the target).
5. **Endpoint**: The test is terminated when the patient reports **sustained blur** that cannot be cleared. The total amount of minus lens power added is recorded as the PRA value.
6. **Recording**: PRA is recorded as a **negative value** (e.g., −2.50 D), reflecting the minus lens power added.

### Expected Normative Values

| Parameter | Expected Value | Standard Deviation |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| **PRA** | **−2.37 D** | ±1.00 D |
| **NRA** | **+2.00 D** | ±0.50 D |

*Values based on [[Morgan's norms]] for young adult patients with normal binocular vision.*

| Age Group | Expected PRA Range |
|-----------|--------------------|
| Children (6–12 years) | −2.50 to −4.00 D |
| Young adults (18–30 years) | −2.00 to −3.00 D |
| Pre-presbyopes (35–45 years) | −1.50 to −2.50 D |
| Early presbyopes (45–55 years) | −0.50 to −1.50 D |
| Advanced presbyopes (>55 years) | 0.00 to −0.50 D |

*Note: PRA values decline with age in parallel with the decline in [[amplitude of accommodation]].*

### Factors Affecting PRA Measurement

| Factor | Effect on PRA |
|--------|--------------|
| **Age** | PRA decreases with age due to [[presbyopia]] and reduced lens elasticity |
| **Uncorrected [[refractive error]]** | [[Hyperopia]] reduces PRA; [[myopia]] may artificially increase it |
| **Over-plussed correction** | Reduces PRA (accommodative starting point is shifted toward relaxation) |
| **Under-plussed correction** | May increase PRA (more accommodative range available for stimulation) |
| **[[Accommodative insufficiency]]** | Directly reduces PRA |
| **[[Accommodative spasm]]** | May paradoxically reduce PRA (system already at high tone) or produce variable results |
| **High [[AC/A ratio]]** | Reduces PRA due to excessive convergence demand |
| **Low [[negative fusional vergence]]** | Reduces PRA (cannot compensate for accommodative convergence) |
| **Fatigue / illness** | May reduce PRA |
| **Medications** (e.g., [[parasympatholytics]], [[anticholinergics]]) | Reduce PRA by impairing ciliary muscle contraction |
| **[[Cycloplegia]]** | Eliminates PRA (ciliary muscle paralyzed) |
| **Target luminance and contrast** | Low luminance or contrast may reduce measurable PRA |
| **Patient attention and effort** | Reduced cooperation yields artificially low PRA |

## Symptoms Associated with Abnormal PRA

Patients with reduced PRA may present with a constellation of symptoms related to inadequate accommodative reserve for near tasks. These symptoms are not unique to PRA abnormalities but, when correlated with reduced PRA findings, strongly suggest accommodative system dysfunction.

### Common Symptoms

- **[[Asthenopia]]** (eye strain): Particularly with prolonged near work such as reading, computer use, or mobile device use.
- **Blurred near vision**: Intermittent or progressive blur during sustained near tasks, often worsening toward the end of the day.
- **Headaches**: Typically [[frontal headache]] or [[periorbital]] discomfort, exacerbated by near work.
- **Difficulty with sustained near focus**: Inability to maintain clear vision during prolonged reading or study.
- **Print "swimming" or moving**: A subjective sensation of text instability.
- **Avoidance of near tasks**: Particularly in children, who may be misdiagnosed with attention or learning difficulties.
- **Pulling away from near work**: Increasing the working distance to reduce accommodative demand.
- **Slow refocusing**: Difficulty shifting focus between near and distance (may overlap with [[accommodative infacility]]).
- **[[Diplopia]]**: If the accommodative effort drives convergence beyond fusional limits (related to the AC/A interaction).

### Symptom Correlation Table

| Symptom | Likely Mechanism | Related Finding |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Near blur | Insufficient accommodative amplitude | Low PRA, low [[amplitude of accommodation]] |
| Frontal headache | Sustained ciliary muscle effort near maximum capacity | Low PRA, low accommodative facility |
| Intermittent diplopia at near | Accommodative convergence exceeds fusional divergence | Low PRA with high AC/A ratio |
| Reading avoidance (children) | Discomfort and blur with near tasks | Low PRA, [[accommodative insufficiency]] |
| Distance blur after near work | [[Accommodative spasm]] or hysteresis | Variable PRA, [[pseudomyopia]] |

## Differential Diagnosis

When PRA is found to be abnormal, the clinician must consider the following differential diagnoses:

| Condition | PRA Finding | Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|------------|------------------------|
| **[[Accommodative Insufficiency]]** | Reduced PRA | Low [[amplitude of accommodation]] (below [[Hofstetter's minimum]]), reduced [[accommodative facility]] with minus lenses |
| **[[Accommodative Infacility]]** | May have normal PRA but slow response | Reduced [[binocular accommodative facility]] (BAF) with ±2.00 D flippers |
| **[[Accommodative Excess]] / [[Accommodative Spasm]]** | Variable; may be reduced or normal | Elevated lead of accommodation on [[MEM retinoscopy]], variable refraction, [[pseudomyopia]] |
| **[[Convergence Excess]]** | Reduced PRA due to high AC/A | High [[esophoria]] at near, high AC/A ratio, reduced NFV at near |
| **[[Presbyopia]]** | Reduced PRA proportional to age | Age-appropriate reduction in amplitude of accommodation |
| **Latent [[Hyperopia]]** | Reduced PRA | Higher plus accepted on [[cycloplegic refraction]] than manifest refraction |
| **Over-plussed correction** | Reduced PRA, increased NRA | PRA/NRA imbalance; improvement with reduction of plus power |

## Relationship to Other Clinical Tests

PRA does not exist in isolation. It must be interpreted within the context of a comprehensive binocular vision evaluation. The following tests are closely related:

### Amplitude of Accommodation

The [[amplitude of accommodation]] (AA), measured by the push-up, push-down, or minus lens method, represents the **total** accommodative range. PRA represents only the **stimulatory portion** of relative accommodation at a fixed vergence. A patient may have a normal AA but reduced PRA if vergence limitations (e.g., low NFV) constrain the relative measurement.

### Accommodative Facility

[[Accommodative facility]] testing (using ±2.00 D flipper lenses) evaluates the **speed and sustainability** of accommodative changes. A patient with normal PRA but reduced facility may have difficulty with dynamic accommodative tasks despite adequate static range.

### MEM Retinoscopy

[[Monocular Estimate Method (MEM) retinoscopy]] provides an objective measure of the [[accommodative response]] at near. A high lag of accommodation (more plus than expected) may correlate with reduced PRA, suggesting the accommodative system is under-responding.

### Binocular Cross-Cylinder (BCC) Test

The [[binocular cross-cylinder]] test (also known as the fused cross-cylinder or FCC test) determines the accommodative posture at near and is used to establish the tentative near add. PRA and NRA are then used to refine and verify this add.

### Fusional Vergence Ranges

[[Negative fusional vergence]] (NFV) and [[positive fusional vergence]] (PFV) at near directly influence PRA and NRA, respectively. Limited NFV constrains PRA because the patient cannot diverge sufficiently to compensate for accommodative convergence.

### Near Phoria

The [[near phoria]] (measured by [[von Graefe]], [[Maddox rod]], or [[cover test]]) provides context for PRA interpretation. An [[esophoric]] patient at near may have reduced PRA due to the additional convergence demand, while an [[exophoric]] patient may have relatively higher PRA.

## Management and Treatment

Management of abnormal PRA depends on the underlying etiology. The following approaches are organized by clinical scenario:

### 1. Refractive Correction Optimization

If PRA/NRA imbalance suggests an inappropriate refractive correction:

- **Over-plussed correction** (low PRA, high NRA): Reduce plus power (or increase minus power) in 0.25 D steps until PRA and NRA are balanced.
- **Under-plussed correction** (high PRA, low NRA): Increase plus power in 0.25 D steps.
- The goal is to achieve **symmetry** between PRA and NRA values, placing the patient's accommodative resting point at the midpoint of their relative accommodative range.

### 2. Near Addition Adjustment (Presbyopia)

For [[presbyopic]] patients:

- Adjust the [[near addition]] so that PRA and NRA are approximately equal.
- If PRA is disproportionately low relative to NRA with a given add, the add may be too strong.
- If PRA is disproportionately high relative to NRA, the add may be too weak.
- Target: PRA ≈ NRA in absolute value, or at minimum, ensure adequate PRA for comfortable sustained near work.

### 3. Vision Therapy for Accommodative Dysfunction

When reduced PRA is attributable to [[accommodative insufficiency]] or [[accommodative infacility]], [[vision therapy]] (VT) / [[orthoptics]] may be indicated:

#### Accommodative Therapy Techniques

| Technique | Description | Target |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| **Minus lens stimulation** | Patient reads through progressively stronger minus lenses to build accommodative stamina | Increase PRA and accommodative amplitude |
| **[[Hart chart]] (near-far rock)** | Alternating fixation between near and distance letter charts | Improve accommodative facility and amplitude |
| **[[Accommodative flippers]]** (±2.00 D) | Rapid alternation between plus and minus lenses while reading | Improve accommodative facility |
| **[[Brock string]]** with accommodative demand | Combining convergence and accommodative exercises | Integrate accommodation and vergence |
| **Computer-based accommodative training** | Software programs (e.g., [[HTS]], [[VividVision]]) that systematically challenge accommodation | Progressive accommodative training |
| **Push-up accommodative training** | Bringing a near target progressively closer until blur, then attempting to clear | Increase amplitude of accommodation |
| **Lens sorting** | Identifying lens powers by their effect on clarity—requires active accommodation | Improve accommodative awareness and control |

#### Therapy Protocol Considerations

- **Frequency**: Typically 1–2 in-office sessions per week, supplemented by daily home exercises (15–20 minutes).
- **Duration**: 8–24 weeks depending on severity and compliance.
- **Monitoring**: PRA should be re-measured periodically (every 4–6 weeks) to track progress.
- **Endpoint**: Normalization of PRA (within 1 SD of age-expected norms), resolution of symptoms, and improved accommodative facility.

### 4. Pharmacological Interventions

Pharmacological management is rarely the primary treatment for reduced PRA but may be considered in specific scenarios:

| Agent | Mechanism | Indication |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| **[[Pilocarpine]]** (low-dose, e.g., 0.01–0.1%) | [[Parasympathomimetic]]; stimulates ciliary muscle contraction | Presbyopia management (e.g., [[Vuity]]); may transiently improve PRA in presbyopes |
| **[[Brimonidine]]-pilocarpine combinations** | Miosis + accommodation stimulation | Investigational presbyopia drops |

*Note: Pharmacological agents that impair accommodation ([[cycloplegics]] such as [[atropine]], [[cyclopentolate]], [[tropicamide]]) will reduce or eliminate PRA and should be avoided when accommodative function is being assessed or treated.*

### 5. Prismatic Correction

In cases where reduced PRA is secondary to vergence dysfunction (e.g., [[convergence excess]] with high AC/A ratio):

- **[[Base-out prism]]** at near may reduce the convergence demand, freeing accommodative capacity and improving PRA.
- **[[Prism]] in spectacles** or [[Fresnel prism]] may be prescribed as a temporary or permanent measure.

### 6. Ergonomic and Behavioral Modifications

- **[[20-20-20 rule]]**: Every 20 minutes, look at a target 20 feet away for 20 seconds to relax accommodation.
- **Optimal working distance**: Maintain 40 cm (16 inches) for near tasks; avoid excessively close working distances that increase accommodative demand.
- **Adequate lighting**: Ensure proper illumination to maximize contrast and reduce accommodative effort.
- **Digital device management**: Reduce screen time or use appropriate near corrections for prolonged digital device use.

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Patients

Children typically have high amplitudes of accommodation and correspondingly robust PRA values. Reduced PRA in a child is a significant finding and may indicate:

- [[Accommodative insufficiency]] (which may mimic or coexist with [[attention deficit disorder]] or [[learning disabilities]])
- Uncorrected [[hyperopia]]
- Early [[accommodative dysfunction]] secondary to systemic illness, head trauma, or medication effects

PRA testing in children requires careful instruction and patience, as young patients may not reliably report blur endpoints.

### Presbyopic Patients

In [[presbyopia]], PRA declines predictably with age as the [[crystalline lens]] loses elasticity and the [[ciliary muscle]] undergoes age-related changes. PRA testing in presbyopes is primarily used to:

- Verify and balance the near addition
- Ensure the patient has adequate accommodative reserve for comfortable near work through their prescribed add

### Post-Surgical Patients

Patients who have undergone [[cataract surgery]] with [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) implantation typically have minimal or no measurable accommodation (and thus minimal PRA) unless an [[accommodating IOL]] (e.g., [[Crystalens]]) or other presbyopia-correcting technology is employed. [[Pseudophakic]] patients with monofocal IOLs will have PRA values near zero.

## Graphical Analysis and the Zone of Clear Single Binocular Vision

PRA and NRA define the **accommodative limits** of the [[Zone of Clear Single Binocular Vision]] (ZCSBV), also known as [[Percival's zone]] or the area of comfort. In graphical analysis:

- The **PRA line** represents the maximum accommodative stimulation boundary.
- The **NRA line** represents the maximum accommodative relaxation boundary.
- The **demand line** represents the accommodative stimulus at various working distances.
- The **fusional vergence ranges** define the horizontal (vergence) boundaries.

A patient with reduced PRA will have a ZCSBV that is truncated on the accommodative stimulation side, meaning they have less tolerance for tasks requiring increased accommodation (e.g., reading at closer distances, or reading through under-corrected lenses).

## Summary of Key Clinical Points

| Clinical Point | Detail |
|---------------|--------|
| **What PRA measures** | Maximum stimulation of accommodation at a fixed vergence demand |
| **How it is measured** | Addition of minus lenses binocularly at 40 cm until sustained blur |
| **Normal value** | Approximately −2.37 D (±1.00 D) in young adults |
| **Low PRA indicates** | Accommodative insufficiency, over-plussed Rx, presbyopia, high AC/A ratio, low NFV |
| **High PRA indicates** | Under-plussed Rx, accommodative excess, or normal robust accommodation |
| **Primary clinical uses** | Rx verification, add determination, accommodative dysfunction diagnosis, binocular vision analysis |
| **Treatment of low PRA** | Rx adjustment, vision therapy, ergonomic modifications, rarely pharmacological |
