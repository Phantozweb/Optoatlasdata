

# Proteoglycans

**Proteoglycans** are a class of heavily [[glycosylated]] macromolecules consisting of a core protein covalently attached to one or more [[glycosaminoglycan]] (GAG) chains. In the context of [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]], proteoglycans are indispensable structural and regulatory components of virtually every ocular tissue, most critically the [[cornea]], [[vitreous humor]], [[trabecular meshwork]], [[sclera]], and [[retina]]. Their unique physicochemical properties—including the capacity to bind water, regulate [[collagen]] fibril spacing, modulate growth factor signaling, and maintain tissue transparency—render them central to both normal ocular physiology and the pathophysiology of numerous sight-threatening conditions. Disruptions in proteoglycan metabolism, expression, or structure are implicated in [[corneal dystrophies]], [[macular corneal dystrophy]], [[glaucoma]], [[myopia]], [[diabetic retinopathy]], and [[keratoconus]], among other disorders.

This article provides a comprehensive clinical overview of proteoglycans as they pertain to the eye, encompassing their biochemistry, distribution, physiological roles, pathophysiology, clinical manifestations, diagnostic modalities, and therapeutic considerations.

## Biochemistry and Classification

### General Structure

A proteoglycan molecule consists of two fundamental components:

1. **Core protein**: A polypeptide backbone that varies in size from ~10 kDa (e.g., [[decorin]]) to >500 kDa (e.g., [[versican]]). The core protein determines tissue localization, receptor interactions, and intracellular signaling capacity.
2. **Glycosaminoglycan (GAG) side chains**: Long, unbranched polysaccharide chains composed of repeating disaccharide units. GAGs are highly negatively charged due to abundant sulfate and carboxyl groups, enabling them to attract water and cations, thereby generating significant osmotic swelling pressure.

The major classes of GAGs found in ocular proteoglycans include:

| GAG Type | Repeating Disaccharide | Sulfation Pattern | Primary Ocular Location |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Keratan sulfate]] (KS) | Gal–GlcNAc | Variable 6-O-sulfation | [[Corneal stroma]], posterior stroma predominantly |
| [[Chondroitin sulfate]] (CS) | GlcA–GalNAc | 4-O or 6-O-sulfation | [[Sclera]], [[corneal stroma]], [[vitreous]] |
| [[Dermatan sulfate]] (DS) | IdoA–GalNAc | 4-O-sulfation | [[Corneal stroma]], anterior stroma predominantly |
| [[Heparan sulfate]] (HS) | GlcA/IdoA–GlcNAc | Complex, variable | [[Basement membranes]], [[retina]], [[lens capsule]], [[trabecular meshwork]] |
| [[Hyaluronan]] (HA) | GlcA–GlcNAc | Non-sulfated | [[Vitreous humor]], [[trabecular meshwork]] |

> **Note:** [[Hyaluronan]] is technically a GAG but not a proteoglycan, as it is not covalently attached to a core protein. However, it interacts extensively with proteoglycans (e.g., [[aggrecan]], [[versican]]) through link proteins and is therefore discussed in this context.

### Classification of Ocular Proteoglycans

Proteoglycans are classified by their GAG composition, core protein family, and tissue localization. The most clinically relevant ocular proteoglycans are:

#### Small Leucine-Rich Proteoglycans (SLRPs)

The [[SLRP]] family is the most critical group of proteoglycans in the [[cornea]]. SLRPs are characterized by a relatively small core protein (~36–42 kDa) containing leucine-rich repeat (LRR) domains that mediate protein–protein interactions, particularly with [[collagen]] fibrils.

| SLRP | GAG Chain(s) | Corneal Location | Primary Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Decorin]] | Dermatan sulfate / Chondroitin sulfate | Anterior and mid-stroma | Collagen fibril spacing, TGF-β regulation |
| [[Lumican]] | Keratan sulfate | Throughout stroma, enriched posteriorly | Collagen fibril spacing, corneal transparency |
| [[Keratocan]] | Keratan sulfate | Corneal stroma (cornea-specific) | Corneal transparency, fibril diameter regulation |
| [[Mimecan]] (Osteoglycin) | Keratan sulfate | Corneal stroma | Collagen fibril organization |
| [[Biglycan]] | Dermatan sulfate / Chondroitin sulfate | Corneal stroma, sclera | Collagen organization, growth factor modulation |
| [[Fibromodulin]] | Keratan sulfate | Corneal stroma, sclera | Collagen cross-linking regulation |

#### Large Aggregating Proteoglycans (Hyalectans)

| Proteoglycan | GAG Chain(s) | Ocular Location | Primary Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Versican]] | Chondroitin sulfate | [[Vitreous]], [[trabecular meshwork]], [[sclera]] | Viscoelasticity, aqueous outflow regulation |
| [[Aggrecan]] | Chondroitin sulfate, Keratan sulfate | [[Vitreous]] (minor) | Water retention, structural support |

#### Basement Membrane Proteoglycans

| Proteoglycan | GAG Chain(s) | Ocular Location | Primary Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Perlecan]] | Heparan sulfate | [[Lens capsule]], [[Descemet's membrane]], [[ILM]], vascular basement membranes | Filtration barrier, growth factor reservoir |
| [[Agrin]] | Heparan sulfate | [[Retinal vasculature]], [[neuromuscular junction]] | Blood-retinal barrier integrity |
| [[Collagen XVIII]] | Heparan sulfate | [[Retinal pigment epithelium]], vascular basement membranes | Anti-angiogenic (endostatin release) |

#### Cell-Surface Proteoglycans

| Proteoglycan | GAG Chain(s) | Ocular Location | Primary Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Syndecan-1 through -4]] | Heparan sulfate, Chondroitin sulfate | [[Corneal epithelium]], [[conjunctiva]], [[retina]] | Cell adhesion, wound healing, growth factor co-receptor |
| [[Glypican-1 through -6]] | Heparan sulfate | [[Retina]], [[ciliary body]] | Hedgehog/Wnt signaling modulation |
| [[CD44]] | Chondroitin sulfate (variable) | [[Trabecular meshwork]], [[corneal epithelium]] | Hyaluronan receptor, cell migration |

## Physiological Roles in Ocular Tissues

### Corneal Transparency

The [[cornea]] is the most proteoglycan-dependent tissue in the eye with respect to its primary optical function: transparency. Corneal transparency is fundamentally dependent on the precise, uniform spacing of [[collagen]] type I fibrils within the [[corneal stroma]]—a spacing of approximately 55–60 nm center-to-center. This lattice-like arrangement produces destructive interference of scattered light (as described by the [[Maurice lattice theory]]) and is maintained almost entirely by the interaction of SLRPs with collagen fibrils.

**Mechanism of transparency maintenance:**

1. **[[Lumican]]** and **[[keratocan]]** bind to the surface of collagen fibrils via their LRR core protein domains, while their [[keratan sulfate]] GAG chains extend into the interfibrillar space.
2. The highly hydrophilic, negatively charged KS chains generate electrostatic repulsion between adjacent fibrils, maintaining uniform spacing.
3. **[[Decorin]]** and **[[biglycan]]**, bearing [[dermatan sulfate]] chains, similarly regulate fibril diameter and prevent fibril fusion.
4. The combined action of these SLRPs ensures that collagen fibrils remain uniformly thin (~25–30 nm diameter in the human cornea) and evenly spaced.

**Evidence from knockout models:**
- *Lumican*-null mice develop bilateral corneal opacification with disorganized, thickened collagen fibrils and loss of uniform interfibrillar spacing.
- *Keratocan*-null mice exhibit thinner corneas with altered fibril architecture.
- *Decorin*-null mice show irregular fibril diameters and skin fragility, with corneal stromal abnormalities.
- *Mimecan*-null mice demonstrate thicker collagen fibrils in the posterior stroma.

These findings unequivocally establish that SLRPs are necessary and sufficient regulators of corneal stromal architecture and transparency.

### Corneal Hydration

Proteoglycans are the primary determinants of corneal [[stromal hydration]]. The GAG chains of corneal SLRPs generate an osmotic swelling pressure of approximately 55 mmHg, which must be counterbalanced by the active dehydration function of the [[corneal endothelium]] (via Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pumps and bicarbonate transport). Normal corneal hydration is approximately 78% water content. Disruption of proteoglycan composition or endothelial pump function leads to stromal edema and loss of transparency.

### Vitreous Humor Structure

The [[vitreous humor]] is a transparent, gel-like structure composed of ~99% water, with its structural integrity maintained by a network of [[collagen]] type II/IX/V/XI fibrils and proteoglycans/GAGs. [[Hyaluronan]] is the predominant GAG, while [[versican]] and type IX collagen (which bears chondroitin sulfate chains and thus functions as a proteoglycan) stabilize the collagen–HA network. Age-related degradation of these proteoglycans contributes to [[vitreous liquefaction]] (synchysis) and [[posterior vitreous detachment]] (PVD).

### Trabecular Meshwork and Aqueous Outflow

The [[trabecular meshwork]] (TM) is rich in proteoglycans, including [[versican]], [[decorin]], [[biglycan]], and various [[heparan sulfate proteoglycans]]. These molecules contribute to the extracellular matrix (ECM) of the TM and regulate resistance to [[aqueous humor]] outflow. The GAG composition of the TM—particularly the balance between [[hyaluronan]], [[chondroitin sulfate]], and [[heparan sulfate]]—directly modulates outflow facility and, consequently, [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP).

### Scleral Biomechanics

The [[sclera]] contains abundant [[dermatan sulfate]] and [[chondroitin sulfate]] proteoglycans (primarily [[decorin]] and [[biglycan]]), which regulate collagen fibril organization and tissue biomechanics. Alterations in scleral proteoglycan content are implicated in the pathogenesis of [[myopia]], where scleral thinning and remodeling occur.

### Retinal and Interphotoreceptor Matrix

The [[interphotoreceptor matrix]] (IPM) surrounding [[photoreceptors]] contains unique proteoglycans, including [[SPACR]] (sialoprotein associated with cones and rods) and [[SPACRCAN]], which bear chondroitin sulfate chains. These proteoglycans facilitate [[retinoid]] transport between [[photoreceptors]] and the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE) and contribute to retinal adhesion.

## Pathophysiology

Proteoglycan dysfunction in the eye can arise from genetic mutations, enzymatic degradation, inflammatory processes, metabolic disorders, or age-related changes. The following subsections detail the major pathophysiological mechanisms.

### Macular Corneal Dystrophy (MCD)

[[Macular corneal dystrophy]] (MCD; OMIM #217800) is the prototypical proteoglycan disorder of the eye and the most clinically significant condition directly caused by defective proteoglycan metabolism. MCD is an [[autosomal recessive]] [[corneal dystrophy]] caused by mutations in the **[[CHST6]]** gene (carbohydrate sulfotransferase 6), located on chromosome 16q23.

**Molecular mechanism:**
- CHST6 encodes corneal [[N-acetylglucosamine-6-O-sulfotransferase]], the enzyme responsible for sulfating [[keratan sulfate]] chains in the cornea.
- Loss-of-function mutations result in the synthesis of **unsulfated or undersulfated keratan sulfate** (sometimes termed "non-sulfated KS" or "KS-like" material).
- Unsulfated KS cannot maintain proper electrostatic repulsion between collagen fibrils, leading to disrupted fibril spacing.
- Additionally, the abnormal KS accumulates within [[keratocytes]] and in the extracellular stroma as electron-dense deposits.

**Immunophenotypic subtypes:**

| Subtype | Serum KS | Corneal KS | Keratocyte Deposits |
|---|---|---|---|
| MCD Type I | Absent/undetectable | Absent | Present (unsulfated GAG) |
| MCD Type IA | Absent/undetectable | Present (low sulfation) | Present |
| MCD Type II | Normal/detectable | Present (low sulfation) | Present |

**Clinical consequence:** Progressive corneal opacification beginning in the first decade of life, with diffuse stromal haze and focal gray-white deposits extending to the limbus, ultimately requiring [[penetrating keratoplasty]] or [[deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]] (DALK).

### Keratoconus

[[Keratoconus]] is associated with significant alterations in corneal proteoglycan expression. Studies have demonstrated:
- Decreased total [[keratan sulfate]] proteoglycan content in keratoconic corneas.
- Reduced expression of [[lumican]] and [[keratocan]].
- Altered [[decorin]] expression and distribution.
- Increased activity of [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs), particularly [[MMP-2]] and [[MMP-9]], which degrade proteoglycan core proteins.
- These changes contribute to collagen fibril disorganization, stromal thinning, and biomechanical weakening characteristic of the disease.

### Glaucoma and Trabecular Meshwork Remodeling

In [[primary open-angle glaucoma]] (POAG), pathological accumulation of [[extracellular matrix]] material in the [[trabecular meshwork]] and [[juxtacanalicular tissue]] increases outflow resistance. Key proteoglycan-related changes include:
- Increased deposition of [[chondroitin sulfate]] proteoglycans in the TM.
- Accumulation of [[versican]] and [[biglycan]] in the juxtacanalicular region.
- Cross-linking and aggregation of proteoglycans with other ECM components.
- Decreased [[hyaluronidase]] activity, leading to HA accumulation.
- These changes collectively reduce aqueous outflow facility and elevate [[intraocular pressure]].

### Myopia and Scleral Remodeling

In the development of [[axial myopia]], the sclera undergoes significant remodeling characterized by:
- Decreased total [[glycosaminoglycan]] content, particularly [[dermatan sulfate]] and [[chondroitin sulfate]].
- Reduced expression of [[decorin]] and [[biglycan]] in the posterior sclera.
- Increased [[MMP]] activity leading to proteoglycan degradation.
- Loss of collagen fibril organization and scleral thinning.
- These proteoglycan changes are believed to be driven by retinal signaling cascades involving [[dopamine]], [[retinoic acid]], and growth factors.

### Diabetic Retinopathy

In [[diabetic retinopathy]], proteoglycan alterations contribute to [[blood-retinal barrier]] breakdown:
- Loss of [[heparan sulfate proteoglycans]] (particularly [[perlecan]] and [[agrin]]) from retinal capillary basement membranes.
- Hyperglycemia-induced non-enzymatic glycation of proteoglycan core proteins.
- Reduced HS chain length and sulfation, diminishing growth factor sequestration capacity.
- Loss of HS-mediated [[VEGF]] regulation, contributing to pathological [[neovascularization]].

### Vitreoretinal Disorders

Age-related changes in vitreous proteoglycans contribute to:
- [[Posterior vitreous detachment]] (PVD): Degradation of [[type IX collagen]] (a proteoglycan) and loss of [[hyaluronan]]–collagen interactions lead to vitreous liquefaction and gel collapse.
- [[Vitreomacular traction]]: Altered proteoglycan composition at the vitreoretinal interface affects adhesion strength.
- [[Proliferative vitreoretinopathy]] (PVR): Aberrant proteoglycan expression by proliferating cells contributes to epiretinal membrane formation.

### Mucopolysaccharidoses (MPS)

The [[mucopolysaccharidoses]] are a group of [[lysosomal storage disorders]] caused by deficiency of enzymes required for GAG degradation. Several MPS subtypes have prominent ocular manifestations due to GAG accumulation:

| MPS Type | Enzyme Deficiency | Accumulated GAG | Ocular Manifestation |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[MPS I]] (Hurler/Scheie) | α-L-iduronidase | DS, HS | Severe corneal clouding |
| [[MPS II]] (Hunter) | Iduronate-2-sulfatase | DS, HS | Mild corneal clouding, retinal degeneration |
| [[MPS IV]] (Morquio) | N-acetylgalactosamine-6-sulfatase / β-galactosidase | KS, CS | Corneal clouding |
| [[MPS VI]] (Maroteaux-Lamy) | N-acetylgalactosamine-4-sulfatase | DS | Corneal clouding, glaucoma |
| [[MPS VII]] (Sly) | β-glucuronidase | DS, HS, CS | Corneal clouding |

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Proteoglycan-related ocular pathology manifests through several clinical signs and symptoms, depending on the tissue affected:

### Corneal Manifestations
- **Progressive corneal opacification**: Diffuse stromal haze, as seen in [[macular corneal dystrophy]] and [[mucopolysaccharidoses]].
- **Decreased visual acuity**: Due to loss of corneal transparency from disrupted collagen fibril spacing.
- **Photophobia and glare**: Resulting from light scatter by disorganized stromal architecture.
- **Recurrent corneal erosions**: In some dystrophies where epithelial-stromal adhesion is compromised.
- **Corneal thinning**: As in [[keratoconus]], where proteoglycan loss contributes to biomechanical failure.

### Anterior Segment Manifestations
- **Elevated intraocular pressure**: From proteoglycan accumulation in the [[trabecular meshwork]].
- **Angle-closure or open-angle [[glaucoma]]**: In MPS and other storage disorders.

### Posterior Segment Manifestations
- **Floaters and photopsia**: From vitreous liquefaction and PVD related to proteoglycan degradation.
- **Retinal detachment**: Secondary to vitreoretinal interface changes.
- **Macular edema**: From blood-retinal barrier breakdown in diabetic proteoglycan loss.
- **Retinal degeneration**: In certain MPS subtypes.

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

[[Slit-lamp examination]] is the primary clinical tool for evaluating corneal proteoglycan disorders. In [[macular corneal dystrophy]], characteristic findings include:
- Focal, gray-white, irregular stromal opacities with indistinct borders.
- Diffuse intervening stromal haze (unlike [[granular corneal dystrophy]], where the intervening stroma is clear).
- Opacities extending to the peripheral cornea and limbus.
- Involvement of all stromal layers, including the area anterior to [[Descemet's membrane]].

### Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography (AS-OCT)

[[AS-OCT]] provides high-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the cornea, enabling:
- Quantification of stromal haze density and distribution.
- Measurement of corneal thickness (often reduced in MCD).
- Assessment of deposit depth for surgical planning.

### Corneal Topography and Tomography

[[Corneal topography]] (e.g., [[Placido disc]]-based systems) and [[corneal tomography]] (e.g., [[Scheimpflug imaging]] with [[Pentacam]]) are essential for:
- Detecting keratoconus-associated proteoglycan changes (corneal thinning, irregular astigmatism, posterior elevation abnormalities).
- Monitoring progression of corneal ectasia.

### Confocal Microscopy

[[In vivo confocal microscopy]] (IVCM) allows cellular-level imaging of the cornea and can demonstrate:
- Hyperreflective intracellular deposits within [[keratocytes]] in MCD.
- Disrupted stromal lamellar architecture.
- Abnormal nerve morphology in proteoglycan-deficient corneas.

### Histopathology and Immunohistochemistry

Definitive diagnosis of proteoglycan disorders often requires tissue analysis:
- **[[Alcian blue]] staining**: Stains GAGs blue; in MCD, deposits stain intensely with Alcian blue.
- **[[Colloidal iron]] staining**: Highlights acidic mucopolysaccharides.
- **[[Periodic acid-Schiff]] (PAS) staining**: May be positive in some deposits.
- **Immunohistochemistry**: Antibodies against specific KS epitopes (e.g., monoclonal antibody 5D4) can distinguish MCD subtypes.
- **[[Electron microscopy]]**: Reveals electron-dense intracytoplasmic inclusions in keratocytes and extracellular fibrillogranular material.

### Genetic Testing

[[Molecular genetic testing]] for **[[CHST6]]** mutations is the gold standard for confirming [[macular corneal dystrophy]]. Sequencing of the CHST6 coding region identifies pathogenic variants in >90% of MCD cases. Genetic testing is also available for MPS enzyme genes.

### Serum Keratan Sulfate Assay

Measurement of serum [[keratan sulfate]] levels (by ELISA using monoclonal antibody 5D4) can help subtype MCD:
- **MCD Type I**: Serum KS undetectable or markedly reduced.
- **MCD Type II**: Serum KS within normal range.

### Enzyme Assays

For suspected [[mucopolysaccharidoses]], specific lysosomal enzyme activity assays in leukocytes or fibroblasts are diagnostic. Urine GAG analysis (quantitative and qualitative) provides supportive evidence.

### Gonioscopy and Tonometry

[[Gonioscopy]] and [[tonometry]] are essential for evaluating proteoglycan-related changes in the [[trabecular meshwork]] and monitoring [[intraocular pressure]] in conditions such as glaucoma associated with MPS or age-related TM remodeling.

## Management and Treatment

### Conservative Management

#### Optical Correction
- [[Spectacles]] and [[contact lenses]] (including [[rigid gas-permeable lenses]]) may provide adequate visual correction in early stages of proteoglycan-related corneal opacification or keratoconus.
- [[Scleral lenses]] are particularly useful for irregular astigmatism secondary to corneal proteoglycan disorders.

#### Lubricants and Ocular Surface Management
- Preservative-free [[artificial tears]] and [[lubricating ointments]] help manage ocular surface discomfort associated with corneal irregularity.
- Some formulations contain [[hyaluronic acid]] (a GAG), which may provide additional benefit through its viscoelastic and water-retaining properties.

### Pharmacological Interventions

#### Enzyme Replacement Therapy (ERT)
- For [[mucopolysaccharidoses]], systemic [[enzyme replacement therapy]] (e.g., [[laronidase]] for MPS I, [[galsulfase]] for MPS VI) can reduce systemic GAG accumulation but has **limited efficacy for corneal clouding** due to poor penetration of the avascular cornea.
- Intrastromal or topical enzyme delivery is under investigation.

#### Anti-Glaucoma Medications
- [[Prostaglandin analogs]] (e.g., [[latanoprost]], [[travoprost]]) increase uveoscleral outflow partly by stimulating [[MMP]]-mediated remodeling of scleral and ciliary body proteoglycans.
- [[Rho kinase inhibitors]] (e.g., [[netarsudil]]) modulate TM ECM, including proteoglycan composition, to enhance conventional outflow.

#### Anti-VEGF Therapy
- In [[diabetic retinopathy]] and other conditions where loss of [[heparan sulfate proteoglycans]] contributes to pathological angiogenesis, [[anti-VEGF agents]] (e.g., [[ranibizumab]], [[aflibercept]], [[bevacizumab]]) counteract the downstream effects of proteoglycan-mediated VEGF dysregulation.

#### Atropine for Myopia Control
- Low-dose [[atropine]] (0.01%–0.05%) for [[myopia control]] may exert some of its effects through modulation of scleral proteoglycan metabolism, although the precise mechanism remains under investigation.

### Surgical Approaches

#### Corneal Transplantation
- **[[Penetrating keratoplasty]] (PKP)**: The traditional definitive treatment for advanced [[macular corneal dystrophy]] with significant visual impairment. Full-thickness corneal replacement addresses all layers of proteoglycan-related opacification.
- **[[Deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]] (DALK)**: Preferred when the [[corneal endothelium]] is healthy, as it replaces the affected stroma while preserving the host's [[Descemet's membrane]] and endothelium, reducing [[endothelial rejection]] risk.
- **Recurrence**: MCD can recur in the graft, typically after 5–30 years, as host keratocytes repopulate the donor stroma and resume production of abnormal proteoglycans.

#### Phototherapeutic Keratectomy (PTK)
- [[Excimer laser]] [[PTK]] may be used for superficial proteoglycan deposits causing visual disturbance, though it is generally less effective for deep stromal involvement as seen in MCD.

#### Corneal Cross-Linking (CXL)
- [[Corneal collagen cross-linking]] with [[riboflavin]] and [[ultraviolet-A]] light is the standard treatment for progressive [[keratoconus]]. While CXL primarily targets collagen, it also affects the proteoglycan matrix by increasing stromal rigidity and potentially altering GAG–collagen interactions.

#### Trabecular Meshwork Surgery
- Procedures such as [[trabeculectomy]], [[goniotomy]], and [[minimally invasive glaucoma surgery]] (MIGS) devices (e.g., [[iStent]], [[Kahook Dual Blade]]) address the increased outflow resistance caused by proteoglycan accumulation in the TM.

### Emerging and Investigational Therapies

#### Gene Therapy
- [[Gene therapy]] for [[CHST6]] mutations in MCD is under preclinical investigation. Adeno-associated virus (AAV)-mediated delivery of functional CHST6 to corneal keratocytes could theoretically restore normal keratan sulfate sulfation and prevent disease progression or recurrence after transplantation.

#### Substrate Reduction Therapy
- For MPS, [[substrate reduction therapy]] (e.g., [[genistein]], which inhibits GAG synthesis) is being explored as an adjunct to ERT, with potential benefits for corneal clouding.

#### Proteoglycan-Based Biomaterials
- Recombinant proteoglycans and GAG-based hydrogels are being developed as corneal substitutes, vitreous replacements, and drug delivery vehicles. [[Lumican]]-functionalized scaffolds have shown promise in promoting organized collagen fibrillogenesis in tissue-engineered corneas.

#### Intrastromal Enzyme Injection
- Direct injection of recombinant sulfatases or glycosidases into the corneal stroma is being explored for MPS-related corneal clouding, aiming to degrade accumulated GAGs locally.

## Proteoglycans in Ocular Wound Healing

Proteoglycans play critical roles in [[corneal wound healing]]:

1. **Acute phase**: Injury triggers degradation of existing KS-proteoglycans ([[lumican]], [[keratocan]]) by activated [[MMPs]] and [[ADAMTS]] enzymes.
2. **Fibrotic phase**: Activated [[keratocytes]] (myofibroblasts) synthesize [[chondroitin sulfate]]/[[dermatan sulfate]] proteoglycans (particularly [[biglycan]] and oversulfated [[decorin]]) instead of the normal KS-proteoglycans.
3. **Consequence**: The shift from KS to CS/DS proteoglycans disrupts normal collagen fibril spacing, producing corneal scarring and opacity.
4. **Resolution**: Gradual restoration of KS-proteoglycan synthesis by quiescent keratocytes is necessary for scar remodeling and partial transparency recovery.

Understanding this proteoglycan switch is critical for developing anti-scarring therapies, including [[decorin]]-based treatments that modulate [[TGF-β]] signaling and reduce fibrosis.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of proteoglycan-related ocular conditions varies significantly:

- **[[Macular corneal dystrophy]]**: Progressive visual loss is expected without surgical intervention. PKP and DALK have excellent short-to-medium-term outcomes, but recurrence in the graft is possible over decades.
- **[[Keratoconus]]**: With early detection and [[CXL]], progression can be halted in most patients. Proteoglycan-related stromal changes are partially stabilized by cross-linking.
- **[[Mucopolysaccharidoses]]**: Corneal clouding may be partially addressed by PKP, but systemic disease determines overall prognosis. ERT improves systemic outcomes but has limited corneal benefit.
- **[[Glaucoma]]**: Proteoglycan-mediated TM changes are addressable with medical and surgical therapy, though the underlying ECM remodeling is chronic and progressive.
- **[[Myopia]]**: Scleral proteoglycan changes associated with axial elongation are currently irreversible, emphasizing the importance of myopia control strategies.
