

# Collarette

---

## Definition

A **collarette** is a distinctive cylindrical or sleeve-like accumulation of fibrinous, waxy, or crusty material that encircles the base of an [[eyelash]] ([[cilia]]) at the level of the [[lid margin]]. Collarettes are pathognomonic clinical signs most strongly associated with **[[Demodex]] infestation** of the [[eyelash follicle]], specifically by the parasitic mite *[[Demodex folliculorum]]*. In clinical optometric and ophthalmologic practice, the identification of collarettes on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] is considered a hallmark finding that strongly suggests the presence of [[Demodex blepharitis]], a subtype of [[anterior blepharitis]] that has gained increasing recognition as a significant and often underdiagnosed contributor to chronic [[ocular surface disease]].

The term "collarette" derives from its morphological resemblance to a small collar or ruff encircling the lash shaft. While superficially similar to the crusting and scaling seen in other forms of [[blepharitis]] (e.g., [[staphylococcal blepharitis]] or [[seborrheic blepharitis]]), collarettes possess unique structural and compositional characteristics that distinguish them as a discrete clinical entity. Their recognition is of paramount importance because it directs the clinician toward targeted anti-parasitic therapy rather than conventional blepharitis management alone.

---

## Historical Context

The association between collarettes and [[Demodex]] mites was first systematically described in the dermatologic and ophthalmic literature in the mid-to-late 20th century, although *Demodex folliculorum* itself was first identified by Simon in 1842 and *[[Demodex brevis]]* was described by Akbulatova in 1963. For decades, the clinical significance of Demodex infestation of the eyelids was debated, with many practitioners considering the mites to be commensal organisms of no pathological consequence. However, landmark studies by Gao, Kheirkhah, and others in the early 2000s established a clear dose-dependent relationship between Demodex mite counts and the severity of [[anterior blepharitis]], with collarettes serving as the most reliable clinical indicator of significant infestation. This body of work fundamentally shifted the understanding of collarettes from a nonspecific sign of lid margin disease to a specific biomarker of Demodex-mediated pathology.

## Anatomy and Relevant Structures

Understanding the formation of collarettes requires familiarity with the microanatomy of the [[eyelid margin]] and the [[eyelash follicle]].

### Eyelash Follicle Complex

Each [[eyelash]] originates from a [[hair follicle]] embedded within the [[orbicularis oculi]] muscle of the [[eyelid]]. The follicle is composed of:

| Structure | Description |
|---|---|
| **[[Hair bulb]]** | The deepest portion of the follicle, containing the [[dermal papilla]] and [[matrix cells]] responsible for lash growth |
| **[[Inner root sheath]]** | A keratinized sheath that surrounds the growing lash shaft within the follicle |
| **[[Outer root sheath]]** | The outermost epithelial layer of the follicle, continuous with the [[epidermis]] |
| **[[Sebaceous gland]] (Gland of Zeis)** | Small holocrine glands that open into the lash follicle, producing [[sebum]] that lubricates the lash |
| **[[Gland of Moll]]** | Modified apocrine [[sweat glands]] that open near the lash base |

*Demodex folliculorum* preferentially inhabits the [[lash follicle]] itself, residing within the space between the lash shaft and the follicular epithelium, typically oriented with its head (gnathosoma) directed toward the follicular base. The mite feeds on [[epithelial cells]], [[sebum]], and follicular contents. The collarette forms at the point where the lash shaft emerges from the follicular ostium at the [[lid margin]] surface.

### Lid Margin Zones

The [[lid margin]] is divided into the **anterior lamella** (skin and [[orbicularis oculi]]) and the **posterior lamella** ([[tarsus]] and [[palpebral conjunctiva]]), separated by the **gray line** ([[mucocutaneous junction]]). Collarettes are found exclusively on the anterior lid margin, at the base of the lashes, and should be distinguished from posterior lid margin findings such as [[meibomian gland]] orifice capping or pouting, which are associated with [[meibomian gland dysfunction]] (MGD) and [[posterior blepharitis]].

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of collarette formation is intimately linked to the life cycle, feeding behavior, and immunogenic properties of *[[Demodex folliculorum]]*.

### Demodex Life Cycle and Follicular Colonization

*Demodex folliculorum* is an obligate ectoparasite of the human [[pilosebaceous unit]]. The mite's life cycle—from egg to larva to protonymph to deutonymph to adult—spans approximately 14 to 18 days and occurs entirely within the lash follicle. Multiple mites (often 3–6 or more) may inhabit a single follicle simultaneously. As the mites feed, mate, and die within the follicle, they generate a substantial amount of biological debris.

### Mechanisms of Collarette Formation

Collarette formation is a multifactorial process involving the following mechanisms:

1. **Mechanical disruption of the follicular epithelium:** The mouthparts (chelicerae) and clawed legs (pedipalps) of *Demodex* physically abrade and consume [[epithelial cells]] lining the follicular wall and the [[inner root sheath]]. This results in reactive [[hyperkeratinization]] of the follicular epithelium—an attempt by the tissue to compensate for ongoing cellular loss. The excess keratinized material is extruded along the lash shaft toward the skin surface, accumulating as the characteristic collarette.

2. **Accumulation of mite waste products:** Demodex mites produce fecal material, shed exoskeletons (exuviae), and upon death, their decomposing bodies contribute proteinaceous and lipid-rich debris to the follicular contents. This material migrates upward along the lash shaft and consolidates at the follicular ostium.

3. **Sebaceous gland dysfunction:** *Demodex folliculorum* consumes [[sebum]] produced by the [[glands of Zeis]], and the physical presence of mites within the follicle can obstruct sebaceous outflow. Altered sebum composition and reduced flow contribute to the waxy, lipid-laden character of the collarette material.

4. **Inflammatory exudate:** The presence of Demodex mites and, critically, their associated endosymbiotic bacterium *[[Bacillus oleronius]]* (now reclassified as *[[Bacillus megaterium]]* in some literature), triggers a localized [[innate immune response]]. [[Toll-like receptor 2]] (TLR-2) activation on follicular epithelial cells and infiltrating [[inflammatory cells]] leads to the release of pro-inflammatory [[cytokines]] including [[interleukin-1β]] (IL-1β), [[interleukin-17]] (IL-17), and [[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] (TNF-α). The resulting inflammatory exudate—composed of [[fibrin]], [[plasma proteins]], [[neutrophils]], and [[lymphocytes]]—contributes to the collarette's composition and the surrounding lid margin [[erythema]] and [[edema]].

5. **Biofilm contribution:** Recent research has demonstrated that Demodex mites facilitate the formation of a polymicrobial [[biofilm]] on the lid margin and within the follicle. This biofilm, composed of bacterial colonies (including *[[Staphylococcus]]* and *[[Streptococcus]]* species), [[extracellular polysaccharides]], and trapped debris, may integrate with and reinforce the collarette structure, making it adherent and resistant to simple mechanical removal.

### Composition of the Collarette

Histochemical and microscopic analysis of collarette material has revealed a complex composition:

| Component | Origin |
|---|---|
| Keratinized epithelial cells | Reactive [[hyperkeratinization]] of follicular epithelium |
| Lipid/sebaceous material | [[Glands of Zeis]], altered sebum |
| Demodex exoskeletons and eggs | Mite life cycle debris |
| Fecal material (guanine) | Mite metabolic waste |
| Fibrin and plasma proteins | Inflammatory exudate |
| Bacterial biofilm matrix | Secondary microbial colonization |
| Inflammatory cell remnants | [[Neutrophils]], [[macrophages]] |

This heterogeneous composition gives collarettes their characteristic translucent-to-opaque, waxy, cylindrical appearance, which is distinct from the brittle, flaky scales of [[seborrheic blepharitis]] or the hard, fibrinous crusts of [[staphylococcal blepharitis]].

## Clinical Presentation

### Morphological Characteristics

On [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]], collarettes present as:

- **Cylindrical, sleeve-like deposits** tightly encircling the lash shaft at its emergence from the lid margin
- **Translucent to semi-opaque** in color, often with a waxy or gelatinous sheen
- **Firmly adherent** to the lash shaft; they do not easily flake off with blinking or gentle manipulation
- Typically located at the **base** of the lash, though they may extend several millimeters along the shaft in severe cases
- Often described as resembling a "collar" or "cuff" around the lash

### Distribution

Collarettes may be found on:

- **Upper and lower eyelids**, though they are frequently more prominent on the upper lid due to the greater number and density of lashes
- **Multiple lashes** simultaneously; widespread collarette formation across many lashes is indicative of heavy Demodex infestation
- Both eyes ([[bilateral]] involvement is the rule rather than the exception)

### Associated Clinical Findings

Collarettes rarely occur in isolation. They are typically accompanied by a constellation of findings that together constitute the clinical picture of [[Demodex blepharitis]]:

| Finding | Description | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Lid margin [[erythema]]** | Redness along the anterior lid margin | Inflammatory response to mite antigens |
| **Lid margin [[telangiectasia]]** | Dilated blood vessels on the lid margin | Chronic inflammation |
| **[[Madarosis]]** | Loss of eyelashes | Follicular destruction by mites |
| **[[Trichiasis]]** | Misdirected lash growth | Follicular scarring and distortion |
| **[[Meibomian gland dysfunction]]** | Inspissation, gland dropout, altered meibum | *[[Demodex brevis]]* infestation of [[meibomian glands]] |
| **[[Conjunctival hyperemia]]** | Redness of the [[bulbar conjunctiva]] or [[palpebral conjunctiva]] | Secondary ocular surface inflammation |
| **[[Blepharoconjunctivitis]]** | Combined lid and conjunctival inflammation | Spread of inflammatory mediators |
| **[[Corneal]] involvement** | [[Superficial punctate keratitis]] (SPK), corneal [[neovascularization]], [[phlyctenule]], marginal [[corneal infiltrates]] | Immune-mediated corneal response to Demodex antigens |
| **[[Chalazion]] (recurrent)** | Chronic [[granulomatous]] inflammation of meibomian gland | Associated with *Demodex brevis* |
| **[[Dry eye disease]]** | [[Tear film instability]], reduced [[tear break-up time]] (TBUT) | Secondary to MGD and ocular surface inflammation |

## Symptoms

Patients with collarettes and underlying [[Demodex blepharitis]] may present with a wide range of symptoms, though it is important to note that some patients—particularly the elderly—may be relatively asymptomatic despite significant mite burden. Common symptoms include:

- **[[Ocular pruritus]]** (itching): Often the most prominent symptom; characteristically worse upon waking, as Demodex mites are more active in darkness
- **Lid margin irritation and burning**
- **Foreign body sensation**
- **[[Epiphora]]** (tearing): Reflex tearing secondary to ocular surface irritation
- **Blurred vision**: Intermittent, often related to [[tear film]] instability
- **Crusting of lashes upon waking**: Patients may describe their lashes being "stuck together" in the morning
- **Eyelash loss**: Patients may notice lashes falling out or thinning
- **Chronic redness of the eyes and lids**
- **Photophobia**: In cases with corneal involvement
- **Recurrent [[stye]] ([[hordeolum]]) or [[chalazion]]**

### Symptom Severity Grading

| Grade | Description |
|---|---|
| **Mild** | Occasional itching, minimal crusting, few collarettes visible |
| **Moderate** | Persistent itching and irritation, multiple collarettes, lid margin erythema, mild SPK |
| **Severe** | Intense pruritus, widespread collarettes, madarosis, corneal involvement, significant visual disturbance |

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

The diagnosis of collarettes is primarily clinical, based on careful [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] of the lid margins.

#### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy Protocol

1. **Magnification:** Begin at low magnification (6–10×) for a general survey of the lid margins, then increase to high magnification (16–25× or higher) for detailed inspection of individual lash bases.
2. **Illumination:** Use direct focal illumination with a moderately narrow beam. Diffuse illumination may also be helpful for an overview.
3. **Systematic inspection:** Examine the upper and lower lid margins of both eyes. Evert the upper lid to inspect the [[superior tarsal conjunctiva]] for associated [[papillary conjunctivitis]].
4. **Identification of collarettes:** Look for the characteristic cylindrical, translucent-to-waxy deposits encircling lash bases. Distinguish from:
   - **Flaky scales** (seborrheic blepharitis): Greasy, loose, easily displaced
   - **Hard crusts/collarettes** (staphylococcal blepharitis): Brittle, fibrinous, often associated with ulceration of the lid margin
   - **Nits (louse eggs)**: Oval, firmly cemented to the lash shaft, associated with [[Phthirus pubis]] infestation ([[phthiriasis palpebrarum]])

#### Differential Diagnosis of Lash Base Deposits

| Finding | Morphology | Associated Condition |
|---|---|---|
| **Collarette** | Cylindrical, waxy, translucent sleeve at lash base | [[Demodex blepharitis]] |
| **Flaky scales** | Greasy, loose, yellowish flakes | [[Seborrheic blepharitis]] |
| **Fibrinous crusts** | Hard, brittle, collarette-like but with ulceration beneath | [[Staphylococcal blepharitis]] |
| **Nits** | Oval, firmly cemented, translucent to brown | [[Phthiriasis palpebrarum]] |
| **Sleeves (follicular casts)** | Thin, transparent sheaths along lash shaft | Nonspecific; may overlap with Demodex |

### Lash Epilation and Microscopic Examination

The gold standard for confirming Demodex infestation associated with collarettes is **microscopic examination of epilated lashes**.

#### Procedure:

1. Epilate 2–4 lashes (with collarettes) from each eyelid using fine forceps under slit-lamp guidance.
2. Place the epilated lashes on a glass microscope slide.
3. Add a drop of **fluorescein sodium** solution, **saline**, or **immersion oil** to the slide and apply a coverslip.
4. Examine under a **light microscope** at 10× and 25–40× magnification.
5. Count the number of adult mites, nymphs, larvae, and eggs visible on and around the lash roots.

#### Interpretation:

| Mite Count (per 4 lashes) | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| 0 | No infestation detected (does not rule out low-level infestation) |
| 1–2 | Low-level infestation; may be commensal |
| 3–5 | Moderate infestation; likely pathogenic |
| >5 | Heavy infestation; strongly pathogenic |

A count of **≥3 mites per 4 epilated lashes** is generally considered clinically significant and consistent with [[Demodex blepharitis]].

### In Vivo Confocal Microscopy (IVCM)

**[[In vivo confocal microscopy]]** (e.g., Heidelberg Retina Tomograph with Rostock Cornea Module) allows non-invasive, real-time visualization of Demodex mites within the lash follicles at the cellular level. Mites appear as elongated, hyper-reflective bodies within the follicular lumen. IVCM can also assess follicular wall integrity, inflammatory cell infiltration, and meibomian gland architecture. While highly sensitive and specific, IVCM is not widely available in routine clinical practice and is primarily used in research settings and specialized clinics.

### Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography (AS-OCT)

Emerging evidence suggests that **[[anterior segment OCT]]** may be capable of detecting Demodex mites within lash follicles as hyper-reflective structures, though this modality is not yet validated for routine diagnostic use in Demodex blepharitis.

### Point-of-Care Testing

Commercial point-of-care diagnostic platforms are under development and in early clinical use for rapid detection of Demodex mites and their associated biomarkers, including [[matrix metalloproteinase-9]] (MMP-9) levels in the [[tear film]], which may be elevated in Demodex-associated [[ocular surface inflammation]].

## Grading and Classification

Several grading systems have been proposed for Demodex blepharitis severity, with collarette burden serving as a key parameter:

### Collarette Grading Scale (Modified from Gao et al.)

| Grade | Description |
|---|---|
| **0** | No collarettes visible |
| **1 (Mild)** | Collarettes on 1–2 lashes per lid |
| **2 (Moderate)** | Collarettes on 3–5 lashes per lid |
| **3 (Severe)** | Collarettes on >5 lashes per lid or involving the majority of lashes |

This grading can be combined with assessments of lid margin erythema, meibomian gland function, and corneal findings to generate a composite severity score for [[Demodex blepharitis]].

## Management and Treatment

The management of collarettes is directed at eradicating or reducing the underlying [[Demodex]] mite population, removing the collarette debris, restoring lid margin hygiene, and addressing secondary [[ocular surface disease]].

### Lid Hygiene

Meticulous [[lid hygiene]] is the cornerstone of collarette management and should be initiated in all patients regardless of severity.

#### Mechanical Debridement

- **In-office debridement:** The clinician can perform gentle mechanical removal of collarettes using a fine spatula, golf club spud, or cotton-tipped applicator moistened with an acaricidal agent (e.g., [[tea tree oil]] solution) under slit-lamp magnification. This provides immediate symptomatic relief and reduces the mite reservoir.
- **At-home lid scrubs:** Patients should be instructed to perform daily lid scrubs using commercially available lid hygiene products. Products containing **[[4-terpineol]]** (a purified component of [[tea tree oil]]) or **[[hypochlorous acid]]** are preferred for their anti-Demodex activity.

### Pharmacological Interventions

#### Tea Tree Oil (TTO) and Terpinen-4-ol (T4O)

**[[Tea tree oil]]** (TTO), derived from *Melaleuca alternifolia*, has been the most extensively studied topical acaricidal agent for Demodex blepharitis. Its primary active component, **[[terpinen-4-ol]]** (T4O), has demonstrated potent in vitro and in vivo mite-killing activity.

| Formulation | Concentration | Application | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| TTO lid scrub | 50% TTO (in-office) | Weekly in-office application to lid margins | Highly effective but may cause irritation; requires clinician application |
| TTO lid wipe | 5–10% TTO | Daily at-home use | Lower concentration for patient self-administration |
| T4O lid cleanser | 1–2.5% T4O | Twice daily at-home use | Better tolerated than full TTO; commercially available (e.g., Cliradex®, now reformulated) |

**Mechanism of action:** T4O disrupts the mite's cell membranes and neuromuscular function, leading to paralysis and death. It also has antibacterial properties against *Bacillus oleronius* and biofilm-forming bacteria.

**Adverse effects:** Ocular surface irritation, stinging, [[contact dermatitis]], and allergic reactions may occur, particularly with higher concentrations of TTO. Patients should be counseled to avoid direct contact with the [[cornea]] and [[conjunctiva]].

#### Lotilaner Ophthalmic Solution 0.25% (TP-03, Xdemvy®)

**[[Lotilaner]]** ophthalmic solution 0.25% (marketed as **Xdemvy®**, Tarsus Pharmaceuticals) received **[[FDA]] approval in July 2023** as the first prescription pharmaceutical specifically indicated for the treatment of [[Demodex blepharitis]]. This represents a landmark development in the management of collarettes and Demodex-associated lid disease.

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Drug class** | [[Isoxazoline]] parasiticide |
| **Mechanism of action** | Inhibits [[GABA]]-gated and [[glutamate]]-gated chloride channels in Demodex mite neurons and muscle cells, causing mite paralysis and death |
| **Dosing** | One drop in each eye twice daily (approximately 12 hours apart) for 6 weeks |
| **Efficacy** | Phase 2b/3 clinical trials (Saturn-1 and Saturn-2) demonstrated statistically significant mite eradication (collarette cure and mite density reduction) compared to vehicle at Day 42 |
| **Safety** | Generally well tolerated; most common adverse effects include instillation site stinging/burning and [[chalazion]]/[[hordeolum]] formation |

Lotilaner's approval has provided clinicians with a standardized, evidence-based, prescription treatment option for collarettes and Demodex blepharitis, addressing a long-standing unmet need.

#### Ivermectin

**[[Ivermectin]]**, a macrocyclic lactone antiparasitic, has been used both topically (1% cream, off-label for periocular use) and orally (200 μg/kg, off-label) for refractory Demodex blepharitis.

- **Oral ivermectin** may be considered in severe or recalcitrant cases, particularly when there is concurrent [[demodicosis]] of the facial skin ([[rosacea]]-associated Demodex dermatitis).
- **Topical ivermectin 1% cream** (Soolantra®) applied to the periocular skin (avoiding direct ocular contact) has shown efficacy in reducing mite counts and collarette burden.

#### Metronidazole

**[[Metronidazole]]** gel (0.75–1%) applied to the lid margins has anti-inflammatory and possible anti-parasitic properties. It is sometimes used as an adjunctive therapy, particularly in patients with concurrent [[ocular rosacea]].

#### Topical Antibiotics

While not directly acaricidal, topical antibiotics such as **[[erythromycin]] ointment** or **[[azithromycin]] ophthalmic solution 1%** may be prescribed to address secondary bacterial infection of the lid margin and to reduce the burden of *Bacillus oleronius* and other pathogenic bacteria associated with Demodex infestation.

#### Anti-Inflammatory Agents

- **[[Corticosteroids]]** (e.g., [[loteprednol etabonate]] 0.5%, [[fluorometholone]] 0.1%): Short-term use may be warranted to control acute inflammatory flares, particularly when corneal involvement (e.g., marginal infiltrates, [[phlyctenulosis]]) is present. Prolonged use should be avoided due to risks of [[steroid-induced glaucoma]] and [[posterior subcapsular cataract]].
- **[[Cyclosporine A]] ophthalmic emulsion** (0.05%, Restasis®; 0.09%, Cequa®): May be used as a steroid-sparing anti-inflammatory agent for chronic Demodex-associated [[ocular surface inflammation]] and secondary [[dry eye disease]].
- **[[Lifitegrast]] ophthalmic solution 5%** (Xiidra®): An [[LFA-1]] antagonist that may address the T-cell-mediated inflammatory component of Demodex blepharitis.

### In-Office Procedures

#### Microblepharoexfoliation (BlephEx®)

**[[BlephEx]]** is a handheld, in-office device that uses a spinning medical-grade micro-sponge to mechanically debride the lid margin, removing collarettes, biofilm, bacterial debris, and mite-related deposits. The procedure is typically performed every 4–6 months as maintenance therapy.

#### Intense Pulsed Light (IPL) Therapy

**[[Intense pulsed light]]** (IPL) therapy, originally developed for dermatologic applications, has been adapted for periocular use in the treatment of [[meibomian gland dysfunction]] and [[Demodex blepharitis]]. IPL delivers broad-spectrum light (500–1200 nm) to the periocular skin, which:

- Causes thermal destruction of Demodex mites
- Reduces lid margin [[telangiectasia]] and inflammation
- Improves meibomian gland function
- Decreases bacterial load

Multiple treatment sessions (typically 3–4, spaced 2–4 weeks apart) are required, with maintenance sessions as needed.

#### Thermal Pulsation (LipiFlow®)

**[[LipiFlow]]** thermal pulsation therapy is primarily indicated for [[meibomian gland dysfunction]] but may be used as an adjunctive treatment in patients with collarettes and concurrent posterior lid disease, addressing the evaporative [[dry eye]] component.

### Treatment Algorithm

| Severity | First-Line | Second-Line | Adjunctive |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Daily T4O lid cleanser; in-office debridement | Lotilaner 0.25% BID × 6 weeks | Warm compresses, artificial tears |
| **Moderate** | Lotilaner 0.25% BID × 6 weeks; T4O lid cleanser | Oral ivermectin (single dose or short course); topical ivermectin cream | Topical azithromycin; BlephEx; IPL |
| **Severe** | Lotilaner 0.25% BID × 6 weeks + oral ivermectin | Combination TTO in-office + T4O at-home + topical antibiotic | Short-course topical steroid (if corneal involvement); cyclosporine A; IPL series |

## Prognosis and Recurrence

Collarettes and Demodex blepharitis are chronic conditions with a high propensity for recurrence. Complete eradication of Demodex mites is difficult to achieve, as reinfection from facial skin reservoirs, bedding, and close personal contacts is common. Patients should be counseled that:

- **Long-term lid hygiene** is essential to prevent recurrence
- **Periodic retreatment** may be necessary
- **Environmental measures** (e.g., washing pillowcases and bed linens in hot water, avoiding sharing towels and cosmetics) may reduce reinfection risk
- **Follow-up examinations** at 4–6 week intervals during active treatment, and every 3–6 months thereafter, are recommended to monitor collarette burden and mite counts

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Patients

Demodex infestation and collarettes are uncommon in children but may occur, particularly in immunocompromised patients or those with recurrent [[chalazia]]. Treatment in pediatric populations requires careful consideration of agent safety and tolerability. Lotilaner 0.25% has not been extensively studied in children under 18 years of age.

### Elderly Patients

The prevalence of Demodex infestation increases with age, with studies reporting near-universal colonization in individuals over 70 years of age. Collarettes may be particularly prevalent in this population, and clinicians should maintain a high index of suspicion during routine examinations. Elderly patients may be relatively asymptomatic despite heavy mite burdens.

### Immunocompromised Patients

Patients with [[HIV/AIDS]], those receiving [[immunosuppressive therapy]], or those with systemic [[autoimmune diseases]] may experience more severe Demodex infestations and more prominent collarette formation. Aggressive and sustained treatment is often required.

### Contact Lens Wearers

[[Contact lens]] wearers with collarettes and Demodex blepharitis may experience increased lens intolerance, [[giant papillary conjunctivitis]], and [[corneal]] complications. Treatment of the underlying Demodex infestation often improves contact lens comfort and reduces complication rates.

## Relationship to Other Ocular and Systemic Conditions

| Condition | Relationship to Collarettes/Demodex |
|---|---|
| **[[Ocular rosacea]]** | Strong association; Demodex infestation is highly prevalent in rosacea patients; collarettes may coexist with rosacea-related lid and corneal findings |
| **[[Meibomian gland dysfunction]]** | *Demodex brevis* infests meibomian glands; collarettes (from *D. folliculorum*) and MGD often coexist |
| **[[Dry eye disease]]** | Secondary to MGD and ocular surface inflammation caused by Demodex |
| **Recurrent [[chalazion]]** | Associated with Demodex-mediated meibomian gland obstruction and inflammation |
| **[[Keratoconus]]** | Some studies suggest a higher prevalence of Demodex infestation in keratoconus patients; the relationship is under investigation |
| **[[Pterygium]]** | Possible association with chronic ocular surface inflammation from Demodex |

---

## Summary

Collarettes are a clinically distinctive and diagnostically significant finding on the eyelid margin, serving as the most reliable clinical biomarker of *[[Demodex folliculorum]]* infestation. Their identification on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] should prompt a thorough evaluation for [[Demodex blepharitis]] and associated [[ocular surface disease]], followed by targeted acaricidal therapy. The approval of [[lotilaner]] ophthalmic solution 0.25% (Xdemvy®) has ushered in a new era of evidence-based, FDA-approved treatment for this condition. Ongoing lid hygiene, patient education, and periodic monitoring are essential to manage the chronic and recurrent nature of Demodex-related lid disease.
