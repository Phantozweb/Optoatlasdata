

# Diplopia

---

**Diplopia**, commonly referred to as **double vision**, is a visual symptom in which a single object is perceived as two separate images. It represents one of the most clinically significant complaints encountered in [[Optometry]] and [[Ophthalmology]] practice, as it may herald benign refractive anomalies or signal life-threatening neurological emergencies such as [[intracranial aneurysm]], [[myasthenia gravis]], or [[brainstem]] pathology. A thorough understanding of the anatomical, neurological, and optical substrates of diplopia is essential for every eye care practitioner to ensure timely diagnosis, appropriate triage, and effective management.

Diplopia may be classified broadly as **monocular** (persisting when one eye is occluded) or **binocular** (resolving upon occlusion of either eye). This fundamental distinction drives the entire diagnostic algorithm and determines whether the etiology is optical/retinal or neuromuscular/orbital in nature.

---

## Classification

### Binocular Diplopia

**Binocular diplopia** is the most clinically consequential form. It occurs only when both eyes are open and resolves immediately when either eye is covered. It results from misalignment of the [[visual axes]], preventing corresponding retinal points from receiving the same image simultaneously. The underlying mechanism involves disruption of normal [[binocular vision]] and [[sensory fusion]].

Binocular diplopia may be further sub-classified by the direction of image separation:

| Sub-type | Description | Common Etiologies |
|---|---|---|
| **Horizontal diplopia** | Images separated side-by-side | [[Sixth nerve palsy]] (lateral rectus paresis), [[convergence insufficiency]], [[internuclear ophthalmoplegia]] |
| **Vertical diplopia** | Images separated one above the other | [[Fourth nerve palsy]] (superior oblique paresis), [[thyroid eye disease]], [[orbital floor fracture]], [[skew deviation]] |
| **Torsional (cyclodiplopia)** | Images tilted or rotated relative to each other | [[Superior oblique palsy]], [[superior oblique myokymia]], cyclotorsional decompensation |
| **Oblique diplopia** | Combined horizontal and vertical separation | Multiple cranial nerve involvement, [[cavernous sinus]] lesions, complex orbital pathology |

### Monocular Diplopia

**Monocular diplopia** persists when the unaffected eye is occluded and is almost always attributable to optical or structural abnormalities within the affected eye itself. It is rarely neurological in origin. Common causes include:

- **[[Cornea]]l irregularity**: [[keratoconus]], [[corneal dystrophy]], [[dry eye disease]], [[pterygium]], corneal scarring
- **[[Lens]] pathology**: [[cataract]] (especially nuclear or posterior subcapsular), [[lens subluxation]], posterior capsular opacification
- **[[Iris]] abnormalities**: [[polycoria]], large [[iridectomy]]
- **[[Retina]]l pathology**: [[epiretinal membrane]], [[macular edema]] causing metamorphopsia perceived as diplopia
- **Refractive error**: uncorrected or improperly corrected high [[astigmatism]]
- **Cerebral polyopia**: rare cortical phenomenon producing multiple images (associated with [[occipital lobe]] lesions)

### Physiological Diplopia

**Physiological diplopia** is a normal phenomenon in which objects located significantly anterior or posterior to the current point of [[fixation]] are seen as double. This occurs because these objects fall on non-corresponding (disparate) retinal points. Physiological diplopia is typically suppressed by the visual system and is not pathological, though it may become noticeable in patients with disrupted suppression mechanisms or heightened visual awareness.

---

## Epidemiology

The incidence of diplopia in the general population has been estimated at approximately **5.9 per 100,000 person-years** for acute-onset binocular diplopia, though this figure varies considerably depending on the population studied and the inclusion criteria used. Diplopia accounts for a significant proportion of urgent referrals to neuro-ophthalmology services.

Key epidemiological observations include:

- **[[Sixth nerve palsy]]** is the most common isolated [[cranial nerve]] palsy causing diplopia, followed by [[fourth nerve palsy]] and [[third nerve palsy]].
- [[Thyroid eye disease]] (Graves' ophthalmopathy) is the most common cause of diplopia in the adult population when all etiologies are considered.
- In the pediatric population, diplopia may be masked by [[suppression]] or [[anomalous retinal correspondence]], making it less commonly reported despite the presence of [[strabismus]].
- The prevalence increases with age, correlating with the rising incidence of [[microvascular ischemia]], [[diabetes mellitus]], [[hypertension]], and [[atherosclerosis]].
- Traumatic diplopia, particularly from [[orbital fractures]], is more common in younger males due to higher rates of facial trauma.

## Anatomy and Pathophysiology

### Neuroanatomical Basis of Binocular Single Vision

Normal [[binocular single vision]] (BSV) requires precise coordination of the [[extraocular muscles]] (EOMs) of both eyes so that images of a fixated object fall on corresponding retinal points—primarily the [[fovea]] of each eye. This process depends on the integrity of:

1. **Afferent visual pathways**: Intact [[retina]], [[optic nerve]], [[optic chiasm]], [[optic tract]], [[lateral geniculate nucleus]], [[optic radiations]], and [[primary visual cortex]] (V1).
2. **Sensory fusion centers**: Higher cortical areas responsible for merging the two monocular images into a single percept with [[stereopsis]].
3. **Motor fusion and vergence systems**: Supranuclear centers including the [[frontal eye fields]], [[parietal eye fields]], and [[cerebellum]] that generate vergence commands.
4. **Ocular motor nuclei and nerves**: The [[oculomotor nerve]] (CN III), [[trochlear nerve]] (CN IV), and [[abducens nerve]] (CN VI) and their respective nuclei in the [[midbrain]] and [[pons]].
5. **Extraocular muscles and orbital structures**: The six EOMs ([[medial rectus]], [[lateral rectus]], [[superior rectus]], [[inferior rectus]], [[superior oblique]], [[inferior oblique]]), their [[tendons]], [[pulleys]], and the surrounding [[orbital fascia]].
6. **Neuromuscular junction**: The synaptic interface between motor neurons and EOM fibers, which is the target in [[myasthenia gravis]].

### Pathophysiological Mechanisms

Diplopia arises when any component of this integrated system is disrupted. The principal pathophysiological mechanisms include:

#### 1. Cranial Nerve Palsies

Damage to CN III, CN IV, or CN VI at any point along their course—from the brainstem nucleus to the orbital insertion—produces characteristic patterns of EOM weakness and resultant diplopia.

- **[[Third nerve palsy]]**: May be complete (involving the [[levator palpebrae superioris]] and [[pupil]]) or partial. The eye is typically "down and out" due to unopposed action of the [[lateral rectus]] and [[superior oblique]]. Pupil involvement (mydriasis) raises suspicion for compressive etiologies such as [[posterior communicating artery aneurysm]]. Pupil-sparing palsies are more commonly ischemic (microvascular).
- **[[Fourth nerve palsy]]**: Produces weakness of the [[superior oblique muscle]], resulting in hypertropia of the affected eye, worsened in contralateral gaze and ipsilateral head tilt ([[Bielschowsky head tilt test]] positive). It is the most common cause of isolated vertical diplopia. Congenital CN IV palsy may decompensate in adulthood.
- **[[Sixth nerve palsy]]**: Produces weakness of the [[lateral rectus muscle]], resulting in an [[esotropia]] that is greatest in ipsilateral gaze. It is the most common isolated cranial nerve palsy due to the long intracranial course of CN VI, making it vulnerable to raised [[intracranial pressure]], trauma, and vascular insults.

#### 2. Neuromuscular Junction Disorders

**[[Myasthenia gravis]]** (MG) is the prototypical neuromuscular junction disorder causing diplopia. Autoantibodies against [[acetylcholine receptors]] (AChR) or, less commonly, [[muscle-specific kinase]] (MuSK) impair neuromuscular transmission. Ocular MG presents with fluctuating, fatigable [[ptosis]] and diplopia that may mimic any cranial nerve palsy pattern. The hallmark is variability—symptoms worsen with sustained effort and improve with rest. Approximately 50–80% of patients with ocular MG will develop generalized MG within two years.

#### 3. Restrictive Orbitopathy

In **[[thyroid eye disease]]** (TED), autoimmune inflammation causes enlargement and fibrosis of the EOMs, most commonly the [[inferior rectus]] and [[medial rectus]]. This restricts ocular motility mechanically rather than through neural deficit. The resulting [[hypotropia]] or [[esotropia]] produces vertical or horizontal diplopia, respectively. Forced duction testing is positive, distinguishing restriction from paresis.

**[[Orbital floor fracture]]** (blowout fracture) may entrap the [[inferior rectus muscle]] or surrounding orbital tissue, mechanically restricting upgaze and producing vertical diplopia.

#### 4. Supranuclear and Internuclear Pathology

- **[[Internuclear ophthalmoplegia]]** (INO): A lesion of the [[medial longitudinal fasciculus]] (MLF) impairs adduction of the ipsilateral eye with nystagmus of the contralateral abducting eye. In younger patients, INO is most commonly caused by [[multiple sclerosis]]; in older patients, by brainstem [[stroke]].
- **[[Skew deviation]]**: A vertical misalignment caused by disruption of otolithic-ocular pathways in the brainstem or cerebellum. It is a supranuclear cause of vertical diplopia and must be distinguished from CN IV palsy.

#### 5. Decompensated Phorias and Vergence Dysfunction

A pre-existing [[phoria]] (latent deviation) may decompensate under conditions of fatigue, illness, medication effects, or aging, producing intermittent binocular diplopia. **[[Convergence insufficiency]]** is a common vergence disorder causing intermittent horizontal diplopia at near, particularly in young adults and patients with [[traumatic brain injury]].

#### 6. Optical and Structural Causes (Monocular Diplopia)

As described above, irregularities in the refractive media—[[cornea]], [[aqueous humor]], [[crystalline lens]], or [[vitreous]]—can produce multiple or ghost images in a single eye. The mechanism involves irregular refraction or diffraction of light, creating two or more focal points on the [[retina]].

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Symptom Characteristics

Patients with diplopia may describe their symptoms in various ways, and careful history-taking is paramount. Key symptom features to elicit include:

| Feature | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|
| **Monocular vs. binocular** | Cover test at bedside immediately differentiates; monocular = optical; binocular = neuromuscular/orbital |
| **Onset** | Sudden onset suggests vascular event, aneurysm, or acute CN palsy; gradual onset suggests TED, tumor, or decompensating phoria |
| **Constancy** | Constant diplopia suggests fixed paresis or restriction; intermittent suggests MG, decompensating phoria, or convergence insufficiency |
| **Direction of separation** | Horizontal, vertical, torsional, or oblique—guides localization |
| **Gaze dependency** | Diplopia worse in a specific gaze direction suggests paretic or restrictive etiology in that field of action |
| **Diurnal variation** | Worsening toward end of day or with fatigue is classic for [[myasthenia gravis]] |
| **Associated symptoms** | [[Ptosis]], [[proptosis]], pain, [[pupil]] abnormality, headache, numbness, weakness—each narrows the differential |
| **Head posture** | Compensatory head tilt or turn suggests longstanding palsy (especially CN IV) |

### Associated Signs

On examination, the clinician may observe:

- **[[Strabismus]]**: Manifest ocular deviation (tropia) in primary position or specific gaze directions
- **[[Ptosis]]**: Suggests CN III palsy or myasthenia gravis
- **[[Pupil]] abnormalities**: Mydriasis with CN III palsy (compressive); normal pupil in microvascular CN III palsy
- **[[Proptosis]]**: Suggests thyroid eye disease, orbital tumor, or [[cavernous sinus]] pathology
- **[[Nystagmus]]**: May accompany INO, cerebellar disease, or vestibular pathology
- **Lid retraction or lid lag**: Characteristic of [[thyroid eye disease]]
- **Fatigable ptosis**: Curtain sign or Cogan lid twitch in myasthenia gravis
- **Pain**: Periorbital or retro-orbital pain may suggest inflammatory, compressive, or ischemic etiology; severe pain with CN III palsy is an emergency (aneurysm until proven otherwise)

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

A systematic approach to the diplopic patient is essential:

#### Step 1: Confirm and Classify

- **Cover-uncover test**: Determines if diplopia is monocular or binocular. If covering either eye eliminates the diplopia, it is binocular.
- **Alternate cover test with prism bar**: Quantifies the magnitude and direction of deviation in multiple gaze positions.

#### Step 2: Characterize the Deviation

- **Versions (ductions and versions)**: Assess the range of motion of each eye in all nine diagnostic positions of gaze. Underaction of a specific muscle suggests paresis; overaction of the antagonist may also be observed.
- **Hirschberg test / Krimsky test**: Estimates deviation using corneal light reflexes.
- **Maddox rod testing**: Dissociates the eyes to measure the subjective angle of deviation and identify torsional components.
- **Lancaster red-green test** or **Hess screen**: Maps the field of action of each EOM, graphically demonstrating patterns of underaction and overaction. These are invaluable for monitoring progression or recovery.
- **[[Bielschowsky head tilt test]]**: Differentiates CN IV palsy from other causes of vertical deviation. Hypertropia increases on tilting the head toward the side of the paretic superior oblique.

#### Step 3: Assess for Restriction vs. Paresis

- **Forced duction test (passive)**: Performed under topical anesthesia; the globe is grasped with forceps and moved in the direction of limited gaze. Resistance indicates mechanical restriction (e.g., TED, orbital fracture entrapment). Free movement suggests paresis.
- **Force generation test (active)**: The patient attempts to look in the direction of limitation while the examiner holds the globe; reduced force confirms paresis.

#### Step 4: Evaluate for Systemic and Neurological Signs

- **Pupil examination**: Assess for [[relative afferent pupillary defect]] (RAPD), anisocoria, and pupillary reactivity. A dilated, fixed pupil with CN III palsy is a neurosurgical emergency.
- **Orbicularis oculi and facial strength**: Assess for [[facial nerve]] involvement.
- **Fatigability testing**: Sustained upgaze for 60 seconds to provoke ptosis; ice pack test (improvement of ptosis after 2 minutes of ice application suggests MG).
- **Neurological examination**: Assess other cranial nerves, cerebellar function, motor and sensory systems, and reflexes.

### Diagnostic Investigations

| Investigation | Indication |
|---|---|
| **[[Magnetic resonance imaging]] (MRI) of brain and orbits** | Suspected intracranial pathology, CN palsy of unclear etiology, INO, skew deviation, orbital mass |
| **[[Magnetic resonance angiography]] (MRA) or [[CT angiography]] (CTA)** | Suspected [[aneurysm]] (especially posterior communicating artery with CN III palsy and pupil involvement) |
| **[[Computed tomography]] (CT) of orbits** | Orbital fracture, metallic foreign body, acute trauma |
| **[[Acetylcholine receptor antibody]] (AChR Ab) testing** | Suspected myasthenia gravis |
| **[[Anti-MuSK antibody]] testing** | Seronegative MG (AChR Ab negative) |
| **[[Edrophonium (Tensilon) test]]** | Bedside test for MG (less commonly used due to cardiac risks; ice pack test preferred) |
| **[[Single-fiber electromyography]] (SFEMG)** | Most sensitive test for neuromuscular junction dysfunction |
| **[[Thyroid function tests]] (TFTs) and [[thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulin]] (TSI)** | Suspected thyroid eye disease |
| **[[Erythrocyte sedimentation rate]] (ESR) and [[C-reactive protein]] (CRP)** | Suspected [[giant cell arteritis]] or inflammatory etiology |
| **[[Lumbar puncture]]** | Suspected raised intracranial pressure, meningeal disease, or demyelination |
| **Blood glucose, [[HbA1c]], blood pressure** | Microvascular risk factor assessment in presumed ischemic CN palsy |
| **[[Chest CT]]** | Screening for [[thymoma]] in confirmed myasthenia gravis |

### Red Flags in Diplopia

The following features mandate urgent investigation and/or referral:

- Acute CN III palsy with **pupil involvement** (aneurysm until proven otherwise)
- Diplopia with **progressive headache**, **papilledema**, or altered consciousness (raised ICP, mass lesion)
- Diplopia with **proptosis and chemosis** (cavernous sinus thrombosis, orbital cellulitis)
- Multiple cranial nerve palsies (cavernous sinus lesion, meningeal carcinomatosis, brainstem pathology)
- Diplopia in a **young patient with INO** (multiple sclerosis)
- Diplopia with **dysphagia, dysarthria, or respiratory difficulty** (generalized myasthenia gravis, brainstem stroke)

## Differential Diagnosis

The differential diagnosis of diplopia is extensive and spans multiple organ systems:

| Category | Conditions |
|---|---|
| **Vascular** | Microvascular ischemic CN palsy ([[diabetes]], [[hypertension]]), [[posterior communicating artery aneurysm]], [[cavernous sinus thrombosis]], brainstem [[stroke]], [[giant cell arteritis]] |
| **Inflammatory/Autoimmune** | [[Myasthenia gravis]], [[thyroid eye disease]], [[orbital inflammatory syndrome]] (orbital pseudotumor), [[multiple sclerosis]], [[sarcoidosis]], [[systemic lupus erythematosus]] |
| **Neoplastic** | [[Pituitary adenoma]], [[meningioma]], [[schwannoma]], [[nasopharyngeal carcinoma]], metastatic disease, [[lymphoma]] |
| **Traumatic** | [[Orbital fracture]] with muscle entrapment, traumatic CN palsy, [[traumatic brain injury]] with convergence insufficiency |
| **Infectious** | [[Orbital cellulitis]], [[mucormycosis]], [[herpes zoster ophthalmicus]], meningitis, [[neurosyphilis]] |
| **Degenerative/Congenital** | Decompensated congenital CN IV palsy, [[progressive supranuclear palsy]], age-related decompensation of phoria |
| **Iatrogenic** | Post-surgical (strabismus surgery, retinal detachment surgery, orbital surgery), post-[[botulinum toxin]] injection |
| **Optical (monocular)** | [[Keratoconus]], [[cataract]], [[corneal scar]], uncorrected [[astigmatism]], [[lens subluxation]] |

## Management and Treatment

Management of diplopia is directed at the underlying etiology, symptomatic relief, and restoration of binocular single vision where possible.

### Acute Management and Triage

- **Emergency referral**: Any diplopia with pupil-involving CN III palsy, signs of raised ICP, cavernous sinus syndrome, or acute neurological deficit requires immediate neuroimaging and neurosurgical/neurological consultation.
- **Observation period**: Isolated, pupil-sparing CN palsies in patients with known microvascular risk factors (diabetes, hypertension) may be observed for spontaneous recovery over **3–6 months** with serial examinations. If no improvement occurs, further investigation is warranted.

### Optical and Orthoptic Management

| Intervention | Description | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| **Occlusion (patching)** | Monocular occlusion eliminates diplopia immediately | Acute diplopia of any cause; temporary measure |
| **[[Fresnel prism]]** | Press-on prism applied to spectacle lens to realign images | Small-to-moderate stable deviations; useful during recovery phase |
| **Ground-in [[prism]]** | Prism incorporated into spectacle prescription | Stable, chronic, small deviations (typically ≤10 prism diopters per lens) |
| **Bangerter foil / translucent occluder** | Partial occlusion that degrades image in one eye without full patching | Cosmetically preferable alternative to patching; useful in children and adults |
| **Sector occlusion** | Opaque tape on a portion of the spectacle lens | Diplopia present only in specific gaze positions (e.g., downgaze in CN IV palsy) |
| **Refractive correction** | Correction of [[astigmatism]], [[keratoconus]] management with [[rigid gas permeable]] lenses or [[scleral lenses]] | Monocular diplopia from optical causes |

### Orthoptic Exercises and Vision Therapy

- **[[Convergence insufficiency]]**: Treated with office-based [[vergence therapy]] and home-based exercises (e.g., pencil push-ups, Brock string, computer-based vergence training). The **Convergence Insufficiency Treatment Trial (CITT)** demonstrated that office-based vergence/accommodative therapy is the most effective treatment.
- **Decompensated phorias**: Fusional vergence exercises may improve fusional reserves and re-establish compensation.

### Pharmacological Interventions

| Agent | Mechanism | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Pyridostigmine]]** (Mestinon) | [[Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor]]; prolongs ACh activity at the neuromuscular junction | [[Myasthenia gravis]]—first-line symptomatic treatment |
| **[[Corticosteroids]]** (oral prednisone, IV methylprednisolone) | Anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive | Thyroid eye disease (active inflammatory phase), orbital inflammatory syndrome, MG exacerbation |
| **[[Azathioprine]], [[mycophenolate mofetil]], [[rituximab]]** | Steroid-sparing immunosuppressive agents | Chronic myasthenia gravis, refractory TED |
| **[[Teprotumumab]]** | Monoclonal antibody against [[insulin-like growth factor 1 receptor]] (IGF-1R) | FDA-approved for active thyroid eye disease; reduces proptosis, diplopia, and inflammation |
| **[[Botulinum toxin]] injection** | Chemodenervation of overacting antagonist muscle | Selected cases of acute CN VI palsy (injection into medial rectus to prevent contracture); chronic strabismus |

### Surgical Management

Surgical intervention is considered when diplopia is chronic, stable, and not amenable to optical or pharmacological management.

#### Strabismus Surgery

**[[Strabismus surgery]]** (extraocular muscle surgery) is the definitive treatment for stable, non-resolving binocular diplopia caused by ocular misalignment. Principles include:

- **Recession**: Weakening a muscle by moving its insertion posteriorly on the globe.
- **Resection**: Strengthening a muscle by removing a segment and reattaching it.
- **Transposition procedures**: Redirecting the action of functioning muscles to compensate for paralyzed muscles (e.g., [[Knapp procedure]] for CN III palsy, [[Jensen procedure]] or full tendon transposition for CN VI palsy).
- **Adjustable sutures**: Allow postoperative fine-tuning of alignment under topical anesthesia; particularly useful in complex or restrictive cases.

Timing: Surgery is generally deferred for at least **6 months** after onset of an acquired palsy to allow for maximum spontaneous recovery. Stable measurements on at least two consecutive visits are required before surgical planning.

#### Orbital Surgery

- **[[Orbital decompression]]**: For thyroid eye disease with compressive optic neuropathy, severe proptosis, or exposure keratopathy. Diplopia may worsen, improve, or develop de novo after decompression, often necessitating subsequent strabismus surgery.
- **Orbital fracture repair**: Surgical repair of blowout fractures with release of entrapped tissue and reconstruction of the orbital floor with implant material. Indications include persistent diplopia in primary or reading position, enophthalmos >2 mm, or large fracture size.

#### Neurosurgical Intervention

- Clipping or coiling of [[intracranial aneurysm]] causing compressive CN III palsy.
- Resection of tumors compressing cranial nerves or brainstem structures.
- [[Thymectomy]] for myasthenia gravis with thymoma or as adjunctive therapy in generalized MG.

### Management of Monocular Diplopia

- **[[Cataract surgery]]**: Removal of the cataractous lens and implantation of an [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) resolves diplopia caused by lenticular changes.
- **[[Corneal transplantation]]** or surface procedures: For corneal scarring or irregularity.
- **[[Rigid gas permeable contact lenses]]** or **[[scleral lenses]]**: Mask corneal irregularity in keratoconus or post-surgical ectasia.
- **Treatment of macular pathology**: [[Anti-VEGF]] injections for macular edema, [[vitrectomy]] with membrane peel for epiretinal membrane.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of diplopia depends entirely on the underlying etiology:

- **Microvascular CN palsies**: Excellent prognosis; approximately **85–90%** resolve spontaneously within 3–6 months.
- **Traumatic CN palsies**: Variable; partial palsies have better recovery than complete palsies.
- **Compressive CN palsies**: Prognosis depends on the nature and treatability of the compressive lesion. Recovery after aneurysm treatment is variable.
- **Myasthenia gravis**: Chronic condition requiring long-term management; ocular symptoms can be well-controlled with medication in most patients.
- **Thyroid eye disease**: Diplopia may persist after the inflammatory phase resolves due to fibrotic changes; surgical correction is often required.
- **Convergence insufficiency**: Excellent prognosis with appropriate vision therapy.
- **Monocular diplopia**: Generally excellent prognosis once the optical cause is corrected.

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Diplopia

Children with [[strabismus]] rarely complain of diplopia because the immature visual system develops [[suppression]] and/or [[anomalous retinal correspondence]] to avoid diplopia. However, acute-onset strabismus in a child (e.g., from CN VI palsy due to posterior fossa tumor) may produce diplopia before suppression develops. Any child presenting with acute diplopia warrants urgent neuroimaging.

### Post-Surgical Diplopia

Diplopia is a recognized complication of various ocular and orbital surgeries, including:

- **[[Cataract surgery]]**: Rare; may result from anesthetic myotoxicity (especially with retrobulbar or peribulbar blocks), or from altered optical state unmasking a pre-existing phoria.
- **[[Refractive surgery]]** ([[LASIK]], [[PRK]]): Monocular diplopia from irregular corneal surface; binocular diplopia from induced anisometropia or disrupted fusion.
- **[[Retinal detachment surgery]]** (scleral buckle): Mechanical restriction from the buckle element may cause diplopia.
- **[[Orbital decompression]]**: As noted above, new-onset diplopia occurs in a significant proportion of patients.

### Diplopia in Traumatic Brain Injury

Patients with [[traumatic brain injury]] (TBI) frequently experience diplopia due to CN palsies, convergence insufficiency, or accommodative dysfunction. A multidisciplinary approach involving [[neuro-optometry]], [[neuro-ophthalmology]], and rehabilitation medicine is essential.

---

## Summary

Diplopia is a multifaceted symptom requiring a systematic, anatomically guided diagnostic approach. The critical first step is distinguishing monocular from binocular diplopia. Binocular diplopia demands careful assessment of ocular motility, cranial nerve function, and systemic associations to identify potentially life-threatening etiologies. Management ranges from simple optical correction and prism therapy to complex surgical intervention, depending on the underlying cause. The optometrist plays a pivotal role in the initial evaluation, triage, and ongoing management of patients with diplopia, often serving as the first point of contact in the diagnostic pathway.
