

# Toric Contact Lenses

---

**Toric contact lenses** are specialized [[contact lens]]es designed to correct [[astigmatism]], a common [[refractive error]] caused by an asymmetric curvature of the [[cornea]] or [[crystalline lens]]. Unlike conventional spherical contact lenses, which possess a single optical power across the entire lens surface, toric lenses incorporate two distinct optical powers oriented at specific meridians perpendicular to one another. This dual-power configuration compensates for the differential refractive states along the principal meridians of the astigmatic eye, thereby enabling clear, focused vision at all distances for which the lens is prescribed. Toric contact lenses are available in [[soft contact lens|soft (hydrogel and silicone hydrogel)]], [[rigid gas permeable]] (RGP), and [[hybrid contact lens|hybrid]] modalities, and they represent one of the most clinically significant innovations in modern [[contact lens]] practice.

---

## Epidemiology and Clinical Significance

[[Astigmatism]] is one of the most prevalent refractive errors worldwide, affecting an estimated 30–60% of the adult population to a clinically significant degree (≥0.75 [[diopter|diopters (D)]]). Studies have demonstrated that approximately one-third of all patients presenting for [[contact lens fitting]] have astigmatism of 0.75 D or greater, a threshold at which uncorrected astigmatism produces meaningful degradation of [[visual acuity]] and [[contrast sensitivity]]. Despite this prevalence, historically a disproportionately low percentage of contact lens wearers were fitted with toric lenses, largely due to earlier limitations in lens design, manufacturing precision, and fitting complexity. However, advances in [[computer-aided design]], [[lathe-cut]] and [[cast-molded]] manufacturing technologies, and stabilization systems have dramatically expanded the availability and reliability of toric contact lenses, making them a first-line option for astigmatic correction in contemporary clinical practice.

The clinical significance of toric contact lenses extends beyond simple refractive correction. Uncorrected or undercorrected astigmatism has been associated with [[asthenopia]] (eye strain), reduced performance in visually demanding tasks, impaired [[stereopsis]], and decreased quality of life. In pediatric populations, significant uncorrected astigmatism may contribute to [[amblyopia|amblyogenic]] risk. Toric contact lenses therefore serve a critical role in optimizing visual function and ocular comfort across diverse patient demographics.

## Pathophysiology of Astigmatism (Rationale for Toric Lens Design)

To understand the clinical necessity and optical design of toric contact lenses, a thorough understanding of the [[pathophysiology]] of [[astigmatism]] is essential.

### Optical Basis of Astigmatism

Astigmatism arises when the refractive system of the eye does not possess rotational symmetry. In a perfectly [[emmetropic]] eye, the [[cornea]] and [[crystalline lens]] refract incoming light rays to a single focal point on the [[retina]]. In an astigmatic eye, the principal refracting surfaces exhibit different curvatures along different meridians, resulting in two focal lines rather than a single focal point. This optical configuration is described by [[Sturm's conoid]], in which the interval between the two focal lines is termed the **[[circle of least confusion]]**—the plane at which the retinal image is most uniformly blurred but not sharply focused.

### Types of Astigmatism

| **Classification** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **[[Corneal astigmatism]]** | Caused by asymmetric curvature of the anterior corneal surface; the most common form. Measured via [[keratometry]] or [[corneal topography]]. |
| **[[Lenticular astigmatism]]** | Caused by asymmetric curvature, tilt, or decentration of the [[crystalline lens]]. |
| **[[Regular astigmatism]]** | Principal meridians are perpendicular (90° apart); correctable with toric lenses. Subtypes include **with-the-rule** (steep meridian near 90°), **against-the-rule** (steep meridian near 180°), and **oblique** (steep meridian between 120°–150° or 30°–60°). |
| **[[Irregular astigmatism]]** | Principal meridians are not perpendicular; often associated with [[keratoconus]], [[corneal scarring]], [[pellucid marginal degeneration]], or post-surgical changes. May require [[rigid gas permeable]] or [[scleral lens]]es rather than soft toric lenses. |
| **[[Residual astigmatism]]** | Astigmatism remaining after corneal astigmatism is neutralized (e.g., by an RGP lens); attributable to lenticular or posterior corneal contributions. |

### The Tear Lens Effect

When a [[rigid gas permeable]] contact lens is placed on an astigmatic cornea, the [[tear film]] fills the space between the posterior lens surface and the anterior corneal surface, creating a **[[tear lens]]** (also called the **lacrimal lens** or **fluid lens**). Because the RGP lens has a spherical posterior surface and the cornea is toroidal, the tear lens assumes a toric shape that partially or fully neutralizes corneal astigmatism. This is a key distinction: **soft toric lenses correct astigmatism through their built-in optical power**, whereas **RGP lenses correct corneal astigmatism primarily through the tear lens mechanism**. A toric RGP lens is required only when significant [[residual astigmatism]] (typically lenticular in origin) persists after the tear lens has neutralized the corneal component.

## Lens Design and Optical Principles

### Optical Zones and Power Configuration

A toric contact lens incorporates a **spherical component** and a **cylindrical component**. The spherical power corrects the [[myopia]] or [[hyperopia]] present in the least ametropic meridian, while the cylindrical power corrects the additional refractive error in the most ametropic meridian. The axis of the cylinder must be precisely aligned with the patient's astigmatic axis to achieve optimal correction.

The optical zone of a toric lens is designed such that the two principal powers are oriented along meridians 90° apart. Any rotational misalignment of the lens on the eye introduces a **residual refractive error** that degrades visual acuity. The relationship between lens rotation and residual cylinder is governed by the following principle:

> **For every 1° of lens rotation off-axis, approximately 3.5% of the cylinder power remains uncorrected as residual astigmatism.**

More precisely, for a lens rotated by θ degrees from the intended axis:

- Residual cylinder power ≈ 2 × (prescribed cylinder) × sin(θ)
- Residual cylinder axis is oriented 45° from the original axis when rotation is significant

Clinically, rotational misalignment of **≤5°** is generally considered acceptable, as the residual astigmatism induced is typically below the threshold of clinical significance for most cylinder powers. Misalignment of **10° or more** produces visually significant degradation, particularly with higher cylinder powers.

### Stabilization Methods

Maintaining consistent rotational orientation is the central engineering challenge in toric contact lens design. Several stabilization mechanisms have been developed:

| **Stabilization Method** | **Mechanism** | **Advantages** | **Disadvantages** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Prism ballast]]** | Incorporates a base-down prism (typically 1.0–1.5 [[prism diopter]]s) to weight the inferior portion of the lens, using gravity to maintain orientation. | Simple design; effective in many patients. | Increased lens thickness inferiorly; may reduce [[oxygen transmissibility]] ([[Dk/t]]); potential for inferior decentration; less effective in non-upright gaze positions. |
| **[[Peri-ballast]] (Periballast)** | Thickened zones at the lens periphery (typically inferior) rather than incorporating optical prism. | Avoids prismatic effect on vision; improved comfort in some designs. | Similar thickness concerns as prism ballast. |
| **[[Thin zone stabilization]] (Double slab-off / Dynamic stabilization)** | Superior and inferior lens zones are thinned ("slab-off"), creating thicker nasal and temporal zones. Lid forces during blinking interact with the thick-thin profile to maintain orientation. | Excellent rotational stability; thinner overall profile; better oxygen performance; effective across gaze positions. | Requires precise manufacturing; may be less effective with atypical lid anatomy. |
| **[[Accelerated Stabilization Design]] (ASD)** | Proprietary variation of thin-zone stabilization with optimized thickness profiles engineered via computational modeling for rapid settling and consistent orientation. | Fast stabilization; minimal rotation with blinking; high reproducibility. | Proprietary to specific manufacturers. |
| **[[Truncation]]** | The inferior edge of the lens is cut flat (truncated), allowing it to rest on the [[lower eyelid]] margin for rotational stability. Primarily used in RGP toric lenses. | Effective for RGP lenses; simple concept. | Rarely used in soft lenses; may cause discomfort; cosmetic concerns. |
| **[[Back-surface toric]] (Bitoric RGP)** | The posterior surface of an RGP lens is manufactured with a toric curve matching the corneal toricity, providing mechanical alignment with the corneal meridians. | Excellent alignment on highly toric corneas; improved centration and comfort for RGP wearers. | Complex fitting; requires accurate keratometry; limited to RGP modality. |

Modern soft toric lenses predominantly employ **thin-zone (dynamic) stabilization** designs, which leverage the mechanical interaction between the [[eyelid]]s and the lens thickness profile during the [[blink]] cycle to achieve rapid and consistent orientation.

## Lens Materials and Modalities

### Material Categories

| **Material** | **Examples** | **Key Properties** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Hydrogel]]** | [[HEMA]]-based polymers (e.g., etafilcon A, ocufilcon D) | Moderate water content (38–75%); lower [[Dk]] values; good initial comfort; lower cost. |
| **[[Silicone hydrogel]]** | Senofilcon A, comfilcon A, fanfilcon A, samfilcon A, lotrafilcon B, delefilcon A | High [[oxygen transmissibility]] (Dk/t often >100); reduced risk of [[hypoxia]]-related complications; available in daily disposable and reusable modalities. |
| **[[Rigid gas permeable]] (RGP)** | Fluorosilicone acrylate polymers (e.g., Boston XO, Menicon Z) | Very high Dk; excellent optical quality; tear lens effect corrects corneal astigmatism; durable. |
| **[[Hybrid contact lens|Hybrid]]** | RGP center with soft skirt (e.g., SynergEyes Duette) | Combines optical quality of RGP with comfort of soft lens; useful for irregular astigmatism or patients intolerant of full RGP lenses. |

### Replacement Schedules

| **Modality** | **Description** | **Clinical Considerations** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Daily disposable]]** | Single-use; discarded after each wear. | Lowest risk of [[contact lens-related infection]]; ideal for [[allergic conjunctivitis]] patients; reduced [[lens deposit]] accumulation; highest compliance profile. |
| **Bi-weekly (2-week)** | Replaced every 14 days; requires nightly disinfection. | Widely available in toric designs; moderate cost. |
| **Monthly** | Replaced every 30 days; requires nightly disinfection. | Cost-effective; broad parameter availability. |
| **Extended/Continuous wear** | Worn overnight for up to 6–30 nights (material-dependent). | Requires high-Dk silicone hydrogel material; increased risk of [[microbial keratitis]]; careful patient selection required. |
| **Custom/Annual RGP** | Replaced annually or as needed. | Maximum parameter customization; ideal for high or unusual astigmatism. |

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms Indicating Toric Lens Need

Patients who are candidates for toric contact lenses typically present with one or more of the following symptoms attributable to uncorrected or undercorrected [[astigmatism]]:

- **Blurred vision** at distance and/or near, often described as "ghosting," "shadowing," or "smearing" of images
- **[[Asthenopia]]** (eye strain), particularly during prolonged near work, computer use, or driving
- **Headaches**, often frontal or temporal, associated with sustained visual tasks
- **Squinting** or partial eyelid closure to achieve a [[pinhole effect]] and transiently improve acuity
- **Difficulty with night vision**, including increased [[glare]] and [[halos]] around lights (due to pupil dilation unmasking peripheral astigmatic aberrations)
- **Reduced [[contrast sensitivity]]**, impacting performance in low-luminance environments
- **Tilting or turning of the head** to align the visual axis with a clearer meridian (particularly in oblique astigmatism)
- **Dissatisfaction with spherical contact lenses**, reporting that vision is "not as sharp" as with [[spectacle]]s, or experiencing fluctuating vision with blinking

In patients already wearing spherical contact lenses, the presence of uncorrected [[residual astigmatism]] may manifest as persistent visual complaints despite an apparently correct spherical equivalent prescription. [[Over-refraction]] with a sphero-cylindrical trial lens set over the existing contact lens will reveal the residual cylinder.

## Diagnosis and Pre-Fitting Assessment

A comprehensive pre-fitting evaluation is essential to determine candidacy for toric contact lenses and to select the optimal lens parameters.

### Refraction

- **[[Subjective refraction]]**: Full manifest refraction with careful determination of cylinder power and axis using the [[Jackson cross-cylinder]] technique. The cylinder axis must be determined to within ±5° for accurate toric lens prescribing.
- **[[Cycloplegic refraction]]**: Indicated in pediatric patients, young hyperopes, or when accommodative fluctuation confounds subjective findings.
- **[[Autorefraction]]**: Useful as a starting point but must be confirmed subjectively.
- **[[Vertex distance]] correction**: When converting a spectacle prescription to a contact lens prescription, vertex distance correction must be applied to both the sphere and cylinder components, particularly for powers exceeding ±4.00 D.

### Keratometry and Corneal Topography

- **[[Keratometry]]**: Measures the curvature of the central anterior corneal surface along the two principal meridians. Provides the magnitude and axis of [[corneal astigmatism]]. Essential for base curve selection and for differentiating corneal from lenticular astigmatism.
- **[[Corneal topography]]**: Provides a comprehensive map of corneal curvature across the entire corneal surface. Critical for detecting [[irregular astigmatism]], [[keratoconus]], [[pellucid marginal degeneration]], or post-surgical irregularity that may contraindicate soft toric fitting and necessitate RGP or [[scleral lens]] options.
- **[[Corneal tomography]]** (e.g., [[Scheimpflug imaging]], [[Pentacam]]): Evaluates both anterior and posterior corneal surfaces; useful for detecting subclinical [[ectasia]].

### Biomicroscopy

- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: Comprehensive anterior segment examination to assess [[eyelid]] anatomy (position, tension, blink dynamics), [[tear film]] quality and quantity ([[tear break-up time]], [[Schirmer test]], [[tear meniscus height]]), [[conjunctiva]]l health, [[cornea]]l integrity, and any pre-existing conditions that may affect contact lens wear (e.g., [[blepharitis]], [[meibomian gland dysfunction]], [[dry eye disease]], [[pterygium]], [[corneal neovascularization]]).

### Tear Film Assessment

Adequate [[tear film]] quality and quantity are prerequisites for successful contact lens wear. Patients with significant [[dry eye disease]] may experience reduced comfort, visual fluctuation, and increased risk of [[corneal staining]] with toric lenses. Assessment includes:

- **[[Tear break-up time]] (TBUT)**
- **[[Schirmer test]]** (basal and reflex)
- **[[Tear meniscus height]]**
- **[[Meibography]]** for [[meibomian gland dysfunction]]
- **Ocular surface staining** with [[fluorescein]], [[lissamine green]], or [[rose bengal]]

### Pupil Size Assessment

Measurement of [[pupil]] diameter under photopic and scotopic conditions ensures that the optical zone of the toric lens adequately covers the entrance pupil, minimizing [[higher-order aberrations]] and visual disturbances in low-light conditions.

### Lid Anatomy and Dynamics

The interaction between the [[eyelid]]s and the contact lens is the primary mechanism of rotational stabilization in modern toric designs. Assessment of lid tension, [[palpebral aperture]] size, lid position relative to the [[limbus]], and blink completeness is critical for predicting stabilization performance.

## Fitting Procedure

### Empirical vs. Diagnostic Fitting

- **Empirical fitting**: The lens is ordered based on refraction and keratometry data without a diagnostic trial lens. Feasible with modern soft toric lenses due to high manufacturing reproducibility, but a follow-up assessment is mandatory.
- **Diagnostic fitting**: A trial lens from a fitting set is placed on the eye, and the fit is assessed in real-time. Preferred for complex prescriptions, first-time wearers, or when lens behavior on the eye is uncertain.

### Assessment of Toric Lens Fit

Once the toric lens is placed on the eye, the following parameters are evaluated after allowing adequate settling time (typically 10–20 minutes for soft lenses):

#### 1. Centration
The lens should center well over the [[cornea]], with the optical zone covering the [[pupil]] in all gaze positions.

#### 2. Movement
Appropriate lens movement with blinking (typically 0.25–0.50 mm for soft lenses) ensures adequate [[tear exchange]] beneath the lens, which is essential for corneal oxygenation and metabolic waste removal.

#### 3. Rotational Orientation and Stability

This is the **most critical assessment** for toric lenses. Most toric lenses have **laser-etched scribe marks** (typically at the 6 o'clock position) that indicate the lens orientation on the eye.

- **Ideal**: Scribe mark at 6 o'clock (0° rotation).
- **Acceptable**: ≤5° rotation from intended position, with consistent orientation across multiple blinks.
- **Unacceptable**: >10° rotation, inconsistent orientation, or excessive rotation with blinking.

The degree and direction of rotation are documented and used to adjust the prescribed axis using the **LARS rule**:

> **LARS: Left Add, Right Subtract**
>
> - If the lens rotates to the **left** (counterclockwise as viewed by the examiner), **add** the degree of rotation to the spectacle cylinder axis.
> - If the lens rotates to the **right** (clockwise as viewed by the examiner), **subtract** the degree of rotation from the spectacle cylinder axis.

*Example*: Spectacle Rx: −2.00 / −1.25 × 180. Lens rotates 10° to the left. Adjusted contact lens axis = 180 + 10 = 190° (or equivalently, 010°).

#### 4. Over-Refraction

A [[sphero-cylindrical over-refraction]] is performed with the toric lens in situ to verify the accuracy of the correction. Ideally, the over-refraction should be plano (or near-plano) with no significant residual cylinder. Any residual sphere or cylinder is incorporated into the final lens order.

#### 5. Visual Acuity

Best-corrected [[visual acuity]] (BCVA) with the toric lens should approximate the spectacle BCVA. If a significant discrepancy exists, the fit, rotation, and prescription should be re-evaluated.

## Management and Troubleshooting

### Common Fitting Challenges and Solutions

| **Problem** | **Likely Cause** | **Management** |
|---|---|---|
| Blurred vision, stable lens | Incorrect power or axis; residual astigmatism | Perform over-refraction; adjust sphere, cylinder, or axis accordingly. |
| Blurred vision, rotating lens | Inadequate rotational stabilization | Change to a lens design with superior stabilization; consider different base curve or diameter; consider RGP toric. |
| Fluctuating vision with blinks | Excessive lens rotation or decentration with each blink | Evaluate lid-lens interaction; try alternative stabilization design; ensure appropriate base curve and diameter. |
| Lens discomfort | Poor fit (tight or loose); lens edge interaction with lids; dry eye | Adjust base curve/diameter; switch material; address underlying [[dry eye disease]]; consider daily disposable modality. |
| Lens decentration | Base curve too flat or too steep; inappropriate diameter; high corneal toricity | Adjust base curve; increase diameter; consider back-surface toric RGP for highly toric corneas. |
| Persistent rotation >10° | Lid anatomy incompatible with stabilization design; lens too loose | Try alternative stabilization method; increase diameter; consider prism-ballasted design for specific lid configurations. |
| Residual astigmatism with RGP | Lenticular astigmatism not corrected by tear lens | Fit front-surface toric or bitoric RGP lens. |

### Special Populations

#### Presbyopic Astigmats
Patients with both [[astigmatism]] and [[presbyopia]] may benefit from **toric multifocal contact lenses**, which combine astigmatic correction with [[progressive addition lens|progressive]] or concentric near-add zones. These lenses are increasingly available in soft silicone hydrogel materials and represent a growing segment of the toric lens market. Alternatively, a **monovision** approach using toric lenses (one eye corrected for distance, the other for near) may be employed.

#### Keratoconus and Irregular Astigmatism
Soft toric lenses are generally **not appropriate** for [[irregular astigmatism]] because they conform to the irregular corneal surface and fail to create the smooth refracting surface needed for optical correction. In these cases, [[rigid gas permeable]] lenses, [[scleral lens]]es, [[hybrid contact lens]]es, or [[piggyback lens]] systems are preferred. However, mild or early keratoconus with predominantly regular astigmatism may occasionally be managed with specialty soft toric designs.

#### Pediatric Patients
Toric contact lenses may be indicated in children with significant astigmatism contributing to [[amblyopia]] risk or in cases where spectacle correction is impractical (e.g., high [[anisometropia]]). Daily disposable toric lenses are preferred in pediatric populations due to simplified care regimens and reduced infection risk.

#### Post-Surgical Patients
Patients who have undergone [[corneal transplantation]] ([[penetrating keratoplasty]], [[DALK]]), [[radial keratotomy]], or complicated [[refractive surgery]] may develop significant regular or irregular astigmatism. Toric RGP or scleral lenses are often the preferred modality in these cases.

## Pharmacological Considerations

While toric contact lenses are not pharmacological agents, several pharmacological considerations are relevant to their clinical use:

- **[[Preservative]]-free [[artificial tears]]**: Recommended for contact lens wearers experiencing dryness. Preserved drops may interact with lens materials, particularly hydrogel lenses, causing [[lens deposit]]s or [[toxic keratopathy]].
- **[[Topical antihistamines]] and [[mast cell stabilizers]]**: May be used in patients with [[allergic conjunctivitis]] who wear toric lenses. Drops should ideally be instilled before lens insertion or after lens removal.
- **[[Topical antibiotics]]**: Required for treatment of [[contact lens-related infection]]s, including [[microbial keratitis]]. Lens wear must be discontinued during active infection.
- **[[Contact lens solutions]]**: Proper selection of [[multipurpose solution]]s or [[hydrogen peroxide]] disinfection systems is essential. Certain solution-material combinations may cause [[solution-induced corneal staining]] (SICS) or [[corneal infiltrative events]].

## Complications

Complications associated with toric contact lens wear are generally similar to those of all contact lens modalities but may include specific issues related to the toric design:

| **Complication** | **Description** | **Management** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Microbial keratitis]]** | Sight-threatening corneal infection; risk increased with overnight wear, poor hygiene, and contaminated lens cases. | Immediate lens discontinuation; corneal cultures; intensive [[topical antibiotic]] therapy (e.g., fortified [[fluoroquinolone]]). |
| **[[Contact lens-induced acute red eye]] (CLARE)** | Acute inflammatory response, often associated with overnight wear. | Lens discontinuation; topical anti-inflammatory agents if indicated. |
| **[[Giant papillary conjunctivitis]] (GPC)** | Immune-mediated papillary reaction of the upper [[tarsal conjunctiva]]; associated with lens deposits and mechanical irritation. | Switch to daily disposable modality; topical [[mast cell stabilizer]]; improve lens hygiene. |
| **[[Corneal neovascularization]]** | Growth of blood vessels into the normally avascular cornea due to chronic [[hypoxia]]. | Switch to higher-Dk silicone hydrogel material; reduce wearing time; consider RGP lenses. |
| **[[Corneal staining]]** | Punctate epithelial erosions detected with [[fluorescein]] staining; may be mechanical, desiccation-related, or solution-induced. | Evaluate fit; change solution; address dry eye; consider material change. |
| **Rotational instability-related visual complaints** | Fluctuating or suboptimal vision due to inconsistent lens orientation. | Re-evaluate fit; apply LARS rule; change stabilization design; consider RGP toric. |

## Recent Advances and Future Directions

The field of toric contact lens technology continues to evolve rapidly:

- **Expanded parameter ranges**: Modern toric lenses are increasingly available in higher cylinder powers (up to −5.75 D in some soft designs), broader axis increments (1° steps in custom lenses), and wider sphere power ranges, accommodating a greater proportion of astigmatic patients.
- **Customized soft toric lenses**: [[Wavefront-guided]] and [[aberrometry]]-based custom soft toric lenses can correct [[higher-order aberrations]] in addition to sphere and cylinder, potentially improving visual quality beyond conventional toric correction.
- **Advanced stabilization algorithms**: Computational fluid dynamics and finite element modeling are being applied to optimize lens thickness profiles for superior rotational stability across diverse lid anatomies.
- **Smart and drug-eluting toric lenses**: Research is underway on toric lenses incorporating biosensors (e.g., for [[intraocular pressure]] monitoring in [[glaucoma]] patients) or sustained drug delivery systems (e.g., for [[dry eye disease]] or [[allergic conjunctivitis]] management).
- **3D printing and additive manufacturing**: Emerging technologies may enable rapid, on-demand fabrication of custom toric lenses with unprecedented parameter precision.
- **Myopia control toric lenses**: Combination designs incorporating [[peripheral defocus]] modification for [[myopia control]] with toric correction are under development for the growing pediatric myopia population.
- **Improved materials**: Next-generation silicone hydrogel polymers with enhanced wettability, reduced modulus, and higher oxygen transmissibility continue to improve comfort and ocular health outcomes for toric lens wearers.

---

## Summary

Toric contact lenses are an indispensable tool in the modern optometric and ophthalmologic armamentarium for the correction of [[astigmatism]]. Successful fitting requires a thorough understanding of the optical principles of astigmatic correction, meticulous pre-fitting assessment, careful lens selection based on patient anatomy and visual needs, and systematic evaluation of lens performance on the eye. With the continued advancement of lens materials, stabilization designs, and manufacturing technologies, toric contact lenses offer increasingly precise, comfortable, and safe vision correction for the large and diverse population of astigmatic patients.
