

# Hemianopsia

---

**Hemianopsia** (also spelled **hemianopia**) is a neurological visual field defect characterized by the loss of vision in one half of the [[visual field]] in one or both eyes. This condition arises from damage to the [[visual pathway]] posterior to the [[optic chiasm]], including the [[optic tract]], [[lateral geniculate nucleus]] (LGN), [[optic radiations]], or the [[primary visual cortex]] (V1, [[Brodmann area 17]]). Hemianopsia is not a disease of the eye itself but rather a manifestation of central nervous system pathology, making it a critical diagnostic sign in both optometric and neurological practice. It profoundly impacts a patient's functional vision, mobility, reading ability, and overall quality of life.

Hemianopsia is among the most common visual field defects encountered following [[cerebrovascular accident]] (stroke), [[traumatic brain injury]] (TBI), and intracranial neoplasms. Understanding its classification, pathophysiology, clinical presentation, diagnostic evaluation, and management strategies is essential for every eye care practitioner.

---

## Classification

Hemianopsia is classified based on the laterality, congruity, and specific pattern of the visual field loss. Precise classification aids in neuroanatomical localization of the lesion.

### By Laterality and Side

| **Type** | **Description** | **Typical Lesion Location** |
|---|---|---|
| **Homonymous Hemianopsia** | Loss of the same half (right or left) of the visual field in both eyes | Post-chiasmal: [[optic tract]], [[LGN]], [[optic radiations]], or [[occipital cortex]] |
| **Heteronymous Hemianopsia (Bitemporal)** | Loss of the temporal visual field in both eyes | [[Optic chiasm]] (e.g., [[pituitary adenoma]]) |
| **Heteronymous Hemianopsia (Binasal)** | Loss of the nasal visual field in both eyes | Bilateral lateral compression of the chiasm (rare) |

### By Completeness

| **Subtype** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Complete Hemianopsia** | Total loss of one half of the visual field with a sharp vertical demarcation along the midline |
| **Incomplete Hemianopsia** | Partial loss of one half of the visual field; may spare portions of the affected hemifield |
| **Quadrantanopsia (Quadrantanopia)** | Loss of one quarter of the visual field; superior or inferior quadrant |
| **Hemianopsia with Macular Sparing** | Preservation of central (macular) vision within the affected hemifield, typically 2–10° |
| **Hemianopsia with Macular Splitting** | The vertical midline bisects the foveal representation, with no sparing of central vision |

### By Congruity

**Congruity** refers to the degree of similarity of the visual field defect between the two eyes:

- **Congruent Hemianopsia**: The defect is nearly identical in shape, size, and density in both eyes. This is characteristic of lesions in the [[occipital lobe]] or posterior [[optic radiations]], where fibers from corresponding retinal points are closely aligned.
- **Incongruent Hemianopsia**: The defect differs significantly between the two eyes. This pattern is more typical of lesions in the [[optic tract]] or anterior [[optic radiations]] ([[temporal lobe]] or [[parietal lobe]]), where fibers from corresponding retinal points are not yet fully organized.

The general clinical rule is: **the more posterior the lesion, the more congruent the field defect**.

## Epidemiology

Hemianopsia is a relatively common neurological visual deficit. Key epidemiological data include:

- **Stroke** is the leading cause, accounting for approximately 50–70% of all cases of homonymous hemianopsia. An estimated 8–25% of stroke survivors develop some form of homonymous visual field loss.
- **Traumatic brain injury** accounts for approximately 14–20% of cases.
- **Intracranial tumors** (primary and metastatic) account for approximately 10–15%.
- **Other causes** include [[cerebral hemorrhage]], [[arteriovenous malformation]] (AVM), neurosurgical complications, [[multiple sclerosis]], [[cerebral abscess]], and [[posterior cortical atrophy]] associated with [[Alzheimer's disease]].
- The prevalence of homonymous hemianopsia in the general population is estimated at approximately 0.8%, but this rises dramatically in stroke rehabilitation populations.
- Bitemporal hemianopsia is most commonly associated with [[pituitary adenoma]] and other [[sellar]] and [[parasellar]] masses.

## Anatomy of the Visual Pathway and Neuroanatomical Basis

A thorough understanding of the [[visual pathway]] is essential for localizing the lesion responsible for hemianopsia.

### The Retrochiasmal Visual Pathway

1. **[[Retina]]**: [[Photoreceptors]] (rods and cones) transduce light into neural signals. [[Retinal ganglion cells]] (RGCs) transmit these signals via their axons, which form the [[optic nerve]] (cranial nerve II).

2. **[[Optic Nerve]] (CN II)**: Each optic nerve carries fibers from the ipsilateral eye. Lesions here cause monocular visual field loss, not hemianopsia.

3. **[[Optic Chiasm]]**: Located superior to the [[pituitary gland]] and inferior to the [[hypothalamus]]. Here, nasal retinal fibers (carrying temporal visual field information) decussate (cross), while temporal retinal fibers (carrying nasal visual field information) remain ipsilateral. Compression of the chiasm (classically by a [[pituitary adenoma]]) disrupts the crossing nasal fibers, producing **bitemporal hemianopsia**.

4. **[[Optic Tract]]**: Each optic tract carries fibers from the ipsilateral temporal retina and the contralateral nasal retina—thus representing the contralateral visual hemifield. A lesion of the right optic tract produces a **left homonymous hemianopsia** (typically incongruent). An [[afferent pupillary defect]] (relative, or [[RAPD]]) may be present contralateral to the lesion due to asymmetric pupillary fiber involvement.

5. **[[Lateral Geniculate Nucleus]] (LGN)**: The primary thalamic relay station for visual information, located in the [[thalamus]]. It has a laminar structure (six layers) receiving input from both eyes. Lesions here are relatively uncommon but produce contralateral homonymous hemianopsia, sometimes with a characteristic wedge-shaped or sectoranopia pattern due to its dual blood supply from the [[anterior choroidal artery]] and [[lateral posterior choroidal artery]].

6. **[[Optic Radiations]] (Geniculocalcarine Tract)**: These fibers project from the LGN to the [[primary visual cortex]].
   - **[[Meyer's Loop]]** (temporal lobe radiations): Fibers carrying information from the superior visual field (inferior retinal quadrants) loop anteriorly through the [[temporal lobe]] before coursing posteriorly. Lesions here (e.g., temporal lobe tumors, surgery for [[temporal lobe epilepsy]]) produce a **contralateral superior quadrantanopsia** ("pie in the sky" defect).
   - **[[Baum's Loop]]** / Parietal lobe radiations: Fibers carrying information from the inferior visual field (superior retinal quadrants) course through the [[parietal lobe]]. Lesions here produce a **contralateral inferior quadrantanopsia** ("pie on the floor" defect). Parietal lobe lesions may also be associated with [[optokinetic nystagmus]] (OKN) asymmetry and visual [[neglect]]/[[inattention]].

7. **[[Primary Visual Cortex]] (V1, Striate Cortex, [[Brodmann Area 17]])**: Located along the banks of the [[calcarine sulcus]] in the [[occipital lobe]]. The cortex has a precise [[retinotopic map]]:
   - The **superior bank** (cuneus) represents the inferior visual field.
   - The **inferior bank** (lingual gyrus) represents the superior visual field.
   - The **posterior pole** of the occipital cortex represents the [[macula]] (central vision) and has a disproportionately large cortical representation (**cortical magnification**).
   - The **anterior calcarine cortex** represents the peripheral visual field, including the monocular temporal crescent.

   Lesions of the occipital cortex produce **highly congruent** contralateral homonymous hemianopsia. **Macular sparing** is common with occipital lobe lesions, potentially due to:
   - Dual blood supply to the occipital pole from both the [[posterior cerebral artery]] (PCA) and branches of the [[middle cerebral artery]] (MCA).
   - The large cortical representation of the macula, making complete destruction less likely.
   - Bilateral cortical representation of the macula (debated).

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of hemianopsia is directly related to the interruption of neural signal transmission along the [[visual pathway]]. The specific mechanism depends on the underlying etiology:

### Cerebrovascular Causes (Most Common)

- **[[Ischemic stroke]]**: Occlusion of the [[posterior cerebral artery]] (PCA) is the most common vascular cause of homonymous hemianopsia. The PCA supplies the [[occipital lobe]], including the [[primary visual cortex]], as well as portions of the [[optic radiations]] and the [[lateral geniculate nucleus]]. Ischemia leads to neuronal death within the affected territory, resulting in permanent visual field loss. The [[middle cerebral artery]] (MCA) supplies the optic radiations as they course through the temporal and parietal lobes; MCA territory strokes can produce hemianopsia (often quadrantanopsia) in conjunction with other neurological deficits such as [[hemiparesis]], [[aphasia]] (dominant hemisphere), or [[hemispatial neglect]] (non-dominant hemisphere).
- **[[Hemorrhagic stroke]]**: Intracerebral hemorrhage can directly destroy visual pathway structures or cause compression and edema affecting adjacent tissue.

### Neoplastic Causes

- Intracranial tumors (e.g., [[glioblastoma]], [[meningioma]], [[metastatic disease]]) can compress, infiltrate, or destroy visual pathway structures. The visual field defect may develop gradually as the tumor grows, or acutely if hemorrhage occurs within the tumor.
- **[[Pituitary adenoma]]** and other [[sellar]]/[[parasellar]] tumors (e.g., [[craniopharyngioma]], [[meningioma]] of the [[tuberculum sellae]]) classically compress the [[optic chiasm]] from below, producing **bitemporal hemianopsia**. The pattern may begin as a bitemporal superior quadrantanopsia due to the inferior nasal fibers being compressed first.

### Traumatic Causes

- [[Traumatic brain injury]] (TBI) can cause hemianopsia through direct contusion of the [[occipital cortex]] (coup or contrecoup injury), shearing of [[optic radiation]] fibers ([[diffuse axonal injury]]), or secondary effects such as [[cerebral edema]], [[herniation]], or post-traumatic [[hydrocephalus]].

### Other Pathophysiological Mechanisms

- **Demyelination**: [[Multiple sclerosis]] can produce retrochiasmal lesions affecting the optic radiations or occipital cortex, though [[optic neuritis]] (pre-chiasmal) is more common.
- **Neurodegenerative disease**: [[Posterior cortical atrophy]] (a variant of [[Alzheimer's disease]]) progressively destroys the occipital and parieto-occipital cortex, leading to complex visual field defects including hemianopsia.
- **[[Arteriovenous malformation]] (AVM)**: Can cause hemianopsia through hemorrhage, steal phenomenon, or direct compression.
- **Infection/Abscess**: [[Cerebral abscess]], [[toxoplasmosis]] (in immunocompromised patients), and other infections can produce mass lesions affecting the visual pathway.
- **Iatrogenic**: Neurosurgical procedures (e.g., temporal lobectomy for epilepsy) can damage [[Meyer's loop]], producing superior quadrantanopsia.

### Neuroplasticity and Recovery

Following acute injury, some degree of spontaneous recovery of the visual field may occur, particularly within the first 1–3 months. This is attributed to:
- Resolution of [[cerebral edema]] and [[penumbral]] tissue recovery.
- Unmasking of previously suppressed neural pathways.
- Limited neuroplastic reorganization.

Complete spontaneous recovery of a dense homonymous hemianopsia is uncommon. Approximately 17–20% of patients with stroke-related homonymous hemianopsia show some degree of spontaneous improvement, most occurring within the first 3–6 months.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Primary Visual Symptoms

- **Loss of awareness of objects, people, or obstacles on the affected side**: Patients may bump into doorframes, furniture, or other objects on the side of the field loss.
- **Reading difficulties**: Patients with **right homonymous hemianopsia** have particular difficulty because they cannot see upcoming text to the right (the direction of reading in English and most Western languages). This is sometimes called **hemianopic alexia** or **hemianopic dyslexia**. Patients with **left homonymous hemianopsia** have difficulty finding the beginning of the next line of text.
- **Difficulty with navigation and mobility**: Patients may have trouble crossing streets, navigating crowded environments, and driving.
- **Visual hallucinations**: Some patients experience [[visual hallucinations]] in the blind hemifield, a phenomenon known as **[[release hallucinations]]** or **[[Charles Bonnet syndrome]]**-like phenomena. These are typically formed (e.g., people, animals, patterns) and the patient usually retains insight that they are not real.

### Associated Neurological Symptoms (Depending on Lesion Location)

| **Lesion Location** | **Associated Findings** |
|---|---|
| [[Optic tract]] | Contralateral [[RAPD]] (Wernicke hemianopic pupil), incongruent defect, possible [[optic atrophy]] (band/bow-tie pattern) |
| [[Temporal lobe]] | Superior quadrantanopsia ("pie in the sky"), [[seizures]], [[auditory hallucinations]], memory disturbance, [[Wernicke's aphasia]] (dominant hemisphere) |
| [[Parietal lobe]] | Inferior quadrantanopsia ("pie on the floor"), [[hemispatial neglect]] (especially right parietal), [[optokinetic nystagmus]] asymmetry, [[astereognosis]], [[agraphesthesia]] |
| [[Occipital lobe]] | Highly congruent hemianopsia, macular sparing common, may be isolated (no other neurological deficits), [[visual agnosia]], [[prosopagnosia]], [[achromatopsia]] (if ventral occipital involvement) |
| Bilateral [[occipital lobe]] | [[Cortical blindness]], [[Anton syndrome]] (denial of blindness), [[Riddoch phenomenon]] (perception of motion in blind field) |
| [[Optic chiasm]] | Bitemporal hemianopsia, possible [[optic atrophy]], endocrine dysfunction (if [[pituitary]] involvement) |

### Impact on Activities of Daily Living

Hemianopsia significantly impairs:
- **Driving**: Many jurisdictions require a minimum binocular visual field (often 120° horizontal) for licensure. Patients with complete homonymous hemianopsia typically do not meet this requirement.
- **Reading**: Reduced reading speed and accuracy, particularly with right-sided defects.
- **Mobility and safety**: Increased risk of falls and collisions.
- **Psychosocial well-being**: Depression, anxiety, social isolation, and loss of independence are common.

### Hemispatial Neglect vs. Hemianopsia

It is critically important to distinguish **hemianopsia** (a sensory visual field loss) from **[[hemispatial neglect]]** (an attentional-perceptual disorder, most commonly following right [[parietal lobe]] damage). In neglect, the patient fails to attend to stimuli on the contralesional side despite having intact (or partially intact) visual pathways. The two conditions can coexist, particularly after MCA territory strokes, and their co-occurrence worsens functional outcomes. Key differentiating features include:

| **Feature** | **Hemianopsia** | **Hemispatial Neglect** |
|---|---|---|
| Awareness of deficit | Usually aware | Often unaware (anosognosia) |
| Compensatory head/eye turning | Present (toward blind side) | Absent or reduced |
| Response to visual field testing | Consistent field loss | Inconsistent; may miss stimuli even in "intact" field |
| Line bisection test | Relatively accurate | Deviates toward ipsilesional side |
| Extinction on double simultaneous stimulation | Absent (unless coexisting neglect) | Present |

## Diagnosis

### Clinical History

A thorough history is essential and should include:
- **Onset**: Sudden (vascular), gradual (tumor, neurodegeneration), or post-traumatic.
- **Laterality**: Which side of the visual field is affected.
- **Associated symptoms**: Headache, weakness, speech difficulty, seizures, endocrine symptoms.
- **Past medical history**: Vascular risk factors ([[hypertension]], [[diabetes mellitus]], [[atrial fibrillation]], [[hyperlipidemia]]), history of cancer, prior stroke or TBI.

### Visual Field Testing (Perimetry)

[[Perimetry]] is the cornerstone of diagnosis and characterization of hemianopsia.

- **[[Humphrey Visual Field Analyzer]] (HVF)**: Automated static perimetry is the standard of care. The **24-2** and **30-2** test strategies are most commonly used. The **10-2** strategy can further evaluate macular involvement. Key findings include:
  - A vertical step or sharp demarcation along the vertical meridian.
  - Assessment of congruity by comparing the two eyes.
  - Determination of macular sparing vs. splitting.
  - Quantification of mean deviation (MD) and pattern standard deviation (PSD).

- **[[Goldmann Perimetry]]**: Manual kinetic perimetry remains valuable for mapping the full extent of the visual field, particularly the far periphery and the monocular temporal crescent. It is also useful for patients who cannot perform automated perimetry reliably.

- **[[Confrontation Visual Field Testing]]**: A bedside screening technique. Methods include finger counting, hand comparison, and red target comparison in each quadrant. While useful for detecting gross defects, confrontation testing has limited sensitivity (approximately 40–70% for detecting hemianopsia) and should not be relied upon as the sole diagnostic method.

- **[[Frequency Doubling Technology]] (FDT) Perimetry**: Can be used as a screening tool but is less commonly employed for characterizing hemianopsia than standard automated perimetry.

- **[[Esterman Binocular Visual Field Test]]**: A functional binocular visual field test often required for driving assessments. It evaluates the integrated visual field of both eyes and is used to determine whether a patient meets the minimum visual field requirements for driving licensure.

### Neuroimaging

Neuroimaging is **mandatory** in all cases of newly diagnosed hemianopsia to identify the underlying etiology.

- **[[Magnetic Resonance Imaging]] (MRI)** of the brain with and without gadolinium contrast is the preferred modality. It provides superior soft tissue resolution for identifying:
  - Ischemic infarction (acute: [[diffusion-weighted imaging]] [DWI]; chronic: [[FLAIR]] and T2 hyperintensity)
  - Hemorrhage
  - Tumors and mass lesions
  - Demyelinating plaques
  - Structural abnormalities

- **[[Computed Tomography]] (CT)** of the head is often the initial study in the acute setting (e.g., emergency department) due to its speed and availability. It is highly sensitive for acute hemorrhage but less sensitive than MRI for ischemic stroke (especially in the first 6–12 hours) and posterior fossa lesions.

- **[[CT Angiography]] (CTA) or [[MR Angiography]] (MRA)**: May be indicated to evaluate for vascular occlusion, stenosis, or [[arteriovenous malformation]].

### Pupillary Examination

- A **[[relative afferent pupillary defect]] (RAPD)** may be present with optic tract lesions (contralateral to the lesion) due to asymmetric input to the [[pretectal nucleus]]. This is because the optic tract carries more crossed (nasal) fibers than uncrossed (temporal) fibers.
- RAPD is **not** expected with lesions posterior to the LGN (optic radiations, occipital cortex), as the pupillary fibers exit the visual pathway at the level of the [[pretectal nucleus]] in the [[midbrain]].

### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

- **[[Optical Coherence Tomography]]** of the [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL) and [[ganglion cell layer]] (GCL) can demonstrate **retrograde trans-synaptic degeneration** in chronic post-chiasmal lesions. This manifests as:
  - Thinning of the RNFL and GCL in a pattern corresponding to the visual field defect.
  - In homonymous hemianopsia: nasal RNFL thinning in the ipsilateral eye and temporal RNFL thinning in the contralateral eye (corresponding to the affected retinal ganglion cells projecting through the damaged pathway).
  - In bitemporal hemianopsia: nasal RNFL thinning in both eyes (corresponding to the decussating nasal fibers).
- OCT findings develop over weeks to months following the initial insult and can serve as a structural biomarker of retrograde degeneration.

### Additional Diagnostic Considerations

- **[[Optokinetic Nystagmus]] (OKN) Testing**: Asymmetric OKN response (reduced when the stimulus moves toward the side of the lesion) suggests a parietal lobe lesion rather than an occipital lobe lesion.
- **[[Electroencephalography]] (EEG)**: May be indicated if seizures are suspected.
- **Endocrine evaluation**: Indicated in bitemporal hemianopsia to assess for [[pituitary]] dysfunction (e.g., [[prolactin]], [[growth hormone]], [[thyroid-stimulating hormone]], [[cortisol]], [[gonadotropins]]).

## Differential Diagnosis

While the pattern of hemianopsia is usually distinctive, the following conditions should be considered in the differential:

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| [[Hemispatial neglect]] | Attentional deficit, not sensory; inconsistent on testing; anosognosia common |
| [[Retinal detachment]] or large [[retinal]] lesion | Monocular; fundoscopic abnormality visible |
| [[Glaucoma]] (advanced) | Typically arcuate or nasal step defects; elevated [[intraocular pressure]]; [[optic disc]] cupping |
| [[Optic neuropathy]] (e.g., [[ischemic optic neuropathy]]) | Monocular; altitudinal defect common; [[optic disc]] edema or pallor |
| [[Functional (non-organic) visual field loss]] | Inconsistent results; tubular fields; normal neuroimaging |
| [[Migraine]] with aura | Transient (typically 20–60 minutes); scintillating scotoma; positive visual phenomena |

## Management and Treatment

Management of hemianopsia is multifaceted, involving treatment of the underlying cause, visual rehabilitation, optical interventions, and psychosocial support.

### Treatment of the Underlying Etiology

- **Stroke**: Acute management per stroke protocols (e.g., [[thrombolysis]], [[thrombectomy]]); secondary prevention with [[antiplatelet therapy]], [[anticoagulation]] (if [[atrial fibrillation]]), [[statin therapy]], and management of vascular risk factors.
- **Tumor**: Neurosurgical resection, [[radiation therapy]], and/or [[chemotherapy]] as appropriate.
- **Pituitary adenoma**: Transsphenoidal surgical resection; medical management with [[dopamine agonists]] (e.g., [[cabergoline]], [[bromocriptine]]) for [[prolactinoma]]; [[radiation therapy]] for residual or recurrent disease.
- **Hydrocephalus**: [[Cerebrospinal fluid]] diversion (e.g., [[ventriculoperitoneal shunt]]).

### Optical and Prismatic Interventions

Optical strategies aim to expand the functional visual field or shift images from the blind hemifield into the seeing hemifield.

#### Fresnel Prism Segments

- **Peripheral [[Fresnel prism]] segments** (typically 15–40 prism diopters) can be applied to the spectacle lens on the side of the field loss. These prisms shift peripheral images from the blind hemifield into the seeing hemifield, providing an awareness cue. The patient must learn to make a saccadic eye movement into the blind field to fixate on the shifted image.
- **Peli Prism System**: Developed by Dr. Eli Peli at the [[Schepens Eye Research Institute]], this system uses high-power (40Δ) press-on Fresnel prisms placed on the upper and/or lower portions of the spectacle lens on the side of the hemianopsia (avoiding the central visual axis to prevent diplopia in primary gaze). This creates peripheral field expansion of approximately 20° while maintaining clear central vision. Studies have demonstrated improved detection of obstacles and hazards on the blind side.

#### Sector Prisms

- Lower-power prisms (e.g., 8–12Δ) placed across the entire lens on the side of the field loss can provide a modest field shift but may cause adaptation difficulties and cosmetic concerns.

#### Mirror-Based Systems

- Small mirrors mounted on spectacle frames have been explored but are generally impractical and poorly tolerated.

### Visual Rehabilitation and Compensatory Training

#### Compensatory Saccadic Training

- This is the most evidence-based rehabilitation approach. Patients are trained to make systematic, organized saccadic eye movements into the blind hemifield to compensate for the field loss. Training programs include:
  - **Computer-based saccadic training**: Programs such as **NVT (Neuro-Vision Technology)** and various research-based protocols train patients to make rapid, accurate saccades into the blind field in response to visual targets.
  - **Reading-specific training**: Techniques to improve reading efficiency, including using a straight edge or finger to track lines, increasing font size, and practicing systematic return saccades (for left hemianopsia) or anticipatory saccades (for right hemianopsia).
  - **Scanning training**: Structured exercises to improve visual scanning in real-world environments (e.g., crossing streets, navigating stores).

- Studies have demonstrated that compensatory saccadic training can improve visual search performance, reading speed, and functional mobility.

#### Vision Restoration Therapy (VRT)

- **[[Vision Restoration Therapy]]** (e.g., NovaVision VRT) involves repeated stimulation of the border zone (transition area) between the intact and blind visual fields, with the goal of expanding the visual field. The patient fixates centrally while light stimuli are presented at the border of the scotoma.
- **Evidence**: VRT remains controversial. Some studies have reported modest visual field expansion (approximately 2–5° on average), while others have attributed apparent improvements to eye movement artifacts, improved fixation stability, or shifts in the criterion for detection rather than true perceptual recovery. The American Academy of Neurology has stated that the evidence is insufficient to support or refute the efficacy of VRT.

#### Auditory and Multisensory Substitution

- Training patients to use auditory cues and head-turning strategies to compensate for the visual field loss.

### Assistive Technology

- **Smartphone and tablet applications**: Various apps provide visual field training exercises and reading aids.
- **Head-mounted displays**: Emerging technologies using augmented reality to highlight objects in the blind hemifield.
- **Text-to-speech software**: Can assist patients with significant reading difficulties.

### Pharmacological Considerations

There are no pharmacological agents that directly restore lost visual field in hemianopsia. However, pharmacological management may be relevant in the following contexts:

- **Neuroprotection in acute stroke**: Timely administration of [[tissue plasminogen activator]] (tPA) or mechanical [[thrombectomy]] can limit infarct size and potentially preserve visual pathway tissue.
- **Management of visual hallucinations**: If release hallucinations are distressing, low-dose [[atypical antipsychotics]] (e.g., [[quetiapine]]) or [[selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors]] (SSRIs) may be considered, though reassurance and education are often sufficient.
- **Treatment of comorbid depression/anxiety**: [[SSRIs]], [[SNRIs]], or psychological therapy for the significant psychosocial impact of hemianopsia.
- **Dopamine agonists**: For prolactinoma-related bitemporal hemianopsia (e.g., [[cabergoline]]).

### Driving Considerations

- Driving with hemianopsia is a critical safety and medicolegal issue. Regulations vary by jurisdiction:
  - Many countries and states require a minimum binocular horizontal visual field of **120°** (some require 140° or more).
  - Patients with complete homonymous hemianopsia typically have a binocular field of approximately **120°** or less (depending on the extent of macular sparing and the monocular temporal crescent of the seeing side), often failing to meet the minimum requirement.
  - Some jurisdictions allow driving with hemianopsia if the patient can demonstrate adequate compensatory scanning ability, often assessed through specialized driving evaluations.
  - The optometrist plays a key role in documenting the visual field deficit, counseling the patient about driving restrictions, and referring for formal driving assessment when appropriate.

### Low Vision Rehabilitation

- Referral to a [[low vision]] specialist or rehabilitation center is recommended for patients with persistent hemianopsia.
- Services may include:
  - Comprehensive low vision evaluation
  - Prescription of appropriate optical devices
  - Orientation and mobility training
  - Occupational therapy for activities of daily living
  - Psychological support and counseling

## Prognosis

The prognosis for hemianopsia depends on the underlying etiology, the extent and location of the lesion, and the timeliness of intervention:

- **Spontaneous recovery**: Occurs in approximately 17–20% of stroke-related cases, primarily within the first 1–3 months. Recovery is more likely with smaller lesions and incomplete field defects. After 6 months, significant spontaneous improvement is rare.
- **Tumor-related hemianopsia**: May improve following successful surgical resection or tumor shrinkage with medical/radiation therapy, particularly in cases of chiasmal compression where the neural tissue has been compressed but not destroyed.
- **Traumatic hemianopsia**: Variable prognosis; some recovery may occur as cerebral edema resolves.
- **Chronic hemianopsia**: Patients can achieve significant functional improvement through compensatory strategies and rehabilitation, even without recovery of the visual field itself.

## Special Considerations in Optometric Practice

### Role of the Optometrist

The optometrist is often the first clinician to detect hemianopsia, particularly in cases with insidious onset. Key responsibilities include:

1. **Detection**: Performing thorough visual field testing as part of comprehensive eye examinations, especially in patients with risk factors for cerebrovascular disease.
2. **Urgent referral**: Newly detected hemianopsia requires **urgent neuroimaging and neurological evaluation**. Acute onset hemianopsia may represent an evolving stroke, and time-sensitive interventions (e.g., thrombolysis) may be indicated.
3. **Documentation**: Precise characterization of the visual field defect (type, congruity, macular involvement) to aid neuroanatomical localization.
4. **Rehabilitation**: Prescribing prismatic devices, providing compensatory training, and coordinating multidisciplinary rehabilitation.
5. **Monitoring**: Serial visual field testing to track stability, progression, or recovery.
6. **Patient education**: Counseling patients and families about the nature of the condition, safety precautions, driving restrictions, and available rehabilitation options.

### Pediatric Hemianopsia

- Hemianopsia in children may result from [[perinatal stroke]], congenital brain malformations, [[hydrocephalus]], or brain tumors (e.g., [[craniopharyngioma]], [[optic pathway glioma]]).
- Children may not report visual field loss and may present with developmental delays, reading difficulties, or behavioral changes.
- The developing brain has greater neuroplastic potential, and some children may develop more effective compensatory strategies than adults.
- Visual field testing in young children requires specialized techniques (e.g., [[confrontation testing]] with toys, [[arc perimetry]], behavioral methods).

---

## Summary

Hemianopsia is a significant neuro-ophthalmic condition with profound implications for visual function, safety, and quality of life. Its accurate diagnosis requires a systematic approach combining clinical history, visual field testing, pupillary examination, OCT, and neuroimaging. Management is multidisciplinary, encompassing treatment of the underlying cause, optical interventions (particularly Fresnel prism systems), compensatory saccadic training, and comprehensive low vision rehabilitation. The optometrist plays a central role in detection, characterization, referral, rehabilitation, and long-term monitoring of patients with hemianopsia.
