

# Branch Retinal Artery Occlusion (BRAO)

---

**Branch Retinal Artery Occlusion (BRAO)** is an acute, vision-threatening [[retinal vascular occlusion]] characterized by the sudden interruption of blood flow through one or more branches of the [[central retinal artery]] (CRA). This results in ischemic infarction of the inner [[retina]] supplied by the affected arterial branch. BRAO accounts for approximately 38% of all acute retinal artery occlusions and represents a critical ophthalmic emergency that demands prompt clinical evaluation not only for visual rehabilitation but also for the identification of potentially life-threatening systemic vascular disease, particularly [[stroke]] and [[cardiovascular disease]]. The condition is most commonly caused by [[embolism]] from [[carotid artery]] atherosclerotic plaques or cardiac valvular disease and is widely regarded as the ocular equivalent of a [[transient ischemic attack]] (TIA) or cerebrovascular accident.

---

## Epidemiology

BRAO is the second most common form of acute retinal artery occlusion after [[central retinal artery occlusion]] (CRAO). Key epidemiological features include:

| Parameter | Details |
|---|---|
| **Incidence** | Approximately 5 per 100,000 person-years for all retinal artery occlusions; BRAO constitutes ~38% |
| **Mean age at presentation** | 60–65 years |
| **Sex predilection** | Slight male predominance |
| **Laterality** | Unilateral in the vast majority of cases; bilateral BRAO is exceedingly rare and suggests systemic vasculitis or coagulopathy |
| **Most commonly affected branch** | [[Temporal retinal arteries]], particularly the superotemporal branch |
| **Associated systemic mortality** | Significantly elevated cardiovascular and cerebrovascular mortality within 5–10 years of diagnosis |

The prevalence of BRAO increases with age and is strongly associated with traditional cardiovascular risk factors including [[hypertension]], [[diabetes mellitus]], [[hyperlipidemia]], [[carotid artery stenosis]], [[atrial fibrillation]], and tobacco use. Population-based studies have demonstrated that patients with retinal artery occlusion have a markedly increased risk of subsequent [[myocardial infarction]] and [[stroke]], with the highest risk occurring within the first week following the ocular event.

---

## Anatomy and Vascular Supply

A thorough understanding of the retinal arterial anatomy is essential for comprehending the pathophysiology and clinical manifestations of BRAO.

The [[ophthalmic artery]], a branch of the [[internal carotid artery]], gives rise to the [[central retinal artery]], which enters the [[optic nerve]] approximately 10–15 mm posterior to the globe. Upon reaching the [[optic disc]], the CRA bifurcates into superior and inferior divisions, which further divide into superotemporal, inferotemporal, superonasal, and inferonasal branches. These branches are [[end arteries]]—they lack significant anastomotic connections—meaning that occlusion of any single branch results in complete ischemia of the retinal territory it supplies.

The inner retinal layers—including the [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL), [[ganglion cell layer]] (GCL), [[inner plexiform layer]] (IPL), and [[inner nuclear layer]] (INL)—are supplied exclusively by the retinal arterial circulation. The outer retinal layers, including the [[photoreceptor layer]], receive their blood supply from the [[choroidal circulation]] via the [[choriocapillaris]]. This dual blood supply explains why BRAO causes inner retinal ischemia and edema while the outer retina may remain relatively preserved.

In approximately 15–33% of the population, a [[cilioretinal artery]]—derived from the [[posterior ciliary arteries]] and thus the choroidal circulation—supplies a variable portion of the [[macula]]. The presence or absence of a cilioretinal artery can significantly influence the visual outcome in BRAO, particularly when the occluded branch would otherwise supply the [[fovea]].

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of BRAO involves acute cessation of arterial blood flow to a segment of the retina, leading to ischemic injury of the inner retinal layers. The mechanisms can be categorized as follows:

### Embolic Occlusion (Most Common)

The overwhelming majority of BRAO cases (>90%) are caused by embolic material lodging at an arterial bifurcation point. Three principal types of [[retinal emboli]] have been identified:

| Embolus Type | Origin | Fundoscopic Appearance | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cholesterol (Hollenhorst plaque)** | [[Carotid artery]] atherosclerotic plaque | Refractile, yellow-orange, often at bifurcations | Most common type (~74%); indicates significant [[atherosclerosis]] |
| **Fibrin-platelet** | Cardiac valves, carotid thrombus | Dull white, elongated, non-refractile | Often associated with [[cardiac valvular disease]] or [[carotid stenosis]]; may be transient |
| **Calcific** | [[Cardiac valves]] (especially [[aortic valve]]) | White, non-refractile, round | Least common but most visually devastating; tends to lodge proximally |

### Thrombotic Occlusion

In situ [[thrombosis]] may occur at sites of pre-existing arterial wall damage from [[atherosclerosis]], [[vasculitis]], or [[arteriolosclerosis]]. This mechanism is more common in patients with [[giant cell arteritis]] (GCA), [[systemic lupus erythematosus]] (SLE), [[polyarteritis nodosa]], and other inflammatory vasculopathies.

### Vasospasm

Transient vasospasm of retinal arterioles can produce BRAO-like episodes, particularly in younger patients. This has been associated with [[migraine]], [[Raynaud phenomenon]], and use of vasoconstrictive substances such as [[cocaine]] or [[ergot alkaloids]].

### Other Rare Mechanisms

- **[[Hypercoagulable states]]**: [[Antiphospholipid syndrome]], [[Factor V Leiden mutation]], [[protein C deficiency]], [[protein S deficiency]], [[hyperhomocysteinemia]]
- **[[Sickle cell disease]]**: Sickling of erythrocytes within retinal arterioles
- **Iatrogenic**: Following [[retrobulbar injection]], [[cosmetic filler injection]] (particularly periorbital [[hyaluronic acid]] fillers), or ophthalmic surgical procedures
- **[[Susac syndrome]]**: Autoimmune endotheliopathy causing branch retinal artery occlusions, sensorineural hearing loss, and encephalopathy

### Ischemic Cascade

Upon arterial occlusion, the inner retina is deprived of oxygen and glucose. Experimental studies in primate models have demonstrated that:

- **Within 90–100 minutes** of complete retinal arterial occlusion, irreversible damage to the inner retinal neurons begins.
- **By 4 hours**, extensive and permanent inner retinal infarction has occurred.
- The ischemic cascade involves failure of the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump, intracellular calcium accumulation, release of excitotoxic [[glutamate]], generation of reactive oxygen species, and ultimately [[apoptosis]] and [[necrosis]] of retinal ganglion cells and inner retinal neurons.

In BRAO, because only a segment of the retina is affected and because partial or spontaneous reperfusion may occur (emboli may fragment and migrate distally), the degree of ischemic damage is often less severe than in CRAO, and visual prognosis is correspondingly better.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

The hallmark symptom of BRAO is **sudden, painless, partial visual field loss** corresponding to the retinal territory supplied by the occluded arterial branch. Key symptomatic features include:

- **Acute onset**: Patients typically describe an abrupt onset of a visual field defect, often noticed upon waking or during routine activities.
- **Sectoral visual field loss**: The field defect corresponds to the area of retinal ischemia. Because retinal nerve fibers from the superior retina project to the inferior visual field and vice versa, a superotemporal BRAO produces an inferonasal visual field defect.
- **Altitudinal defects**: Superior or inferior altitudinal field loss is common when major superior or inferior branch arteries are occluded.
- **Central visual acuity**: May be preserved if the [[fovea]] is not within the ischemic zone. However, if the occluded branch supplies the papillomacular bundle or foveal region, significant reduction in [[visual acuity]] occurs.
- **Painless**: Unlike [[optic neuritis]] or [[acute angle-closure glaucoma]], BRAO is characteristically painless.
- **Transient vs. permanent**: Some patients experience transient visual field loss lasting minutes to hours ([[amaurosis fugax]]) before a permanent BRAO develops, suggesting recurrent embolization.

### Signs

#### Anterior Segment Examination

The [[anterior segment]] examination is typically unremarkable in BRAO. Unlike CRAO, BRAO does not cause [[neovascular glaucoma]] (NVG) because the ischemic retinal area is insufficient to generate the critical threshold of [[vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF) required to drive anterior segment [[neovascularization]]. However, a thorough [[slit-lamp examination]] should be performed to rule out concurrent anterior segment pathology.

#### Fundoscopic Findings

[[Dilated fundus examination]] reveals the characteristic findings of BRAO:

| Finding | Description |
|---|---|
| **Retinal whitening (edema)** | Sectoral, well-demarcated area of superficial retinal opacification corresponding to the territory of the occluded branch; caused by intracellular edema (cytotoxic edema) of the ischemic inner retinal layers |
| **Visible embolus** | A refractile or white plaque may be visible at the point of arterial occlusion, typically at a bifurcation; present in approximately 60–70% of BRAO cases |
| **Attenuated arteriole** | The affected branch arteriole may appear narrowed or attenuated distal to the occlusion |
| **Segmental "box-carring" or "cattle-trucking"** | Segmentation of the blood column within the affected arteriole, indicating severely reduced or absent flow |
| **Cotton-wool spots** | May be present in the ischemic zone, representing focal infarction of the [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] |
| **Cherry-red spot** | Absent in BRAO (this is a hallmark of CRAO, where the entire macula is ischemic except the foveola supplied by the choroid) |
| **Relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD)** | Typically absent or minimal in BRAO due to the limited area of retinal involvement; may be present if a large retinal area is affected |

Over days to weeks, the retinal whitening gradually resolves as the edematous inner retinal tissue undergoes atrophy. Chronic findings include:

- **Inner retinal atrophy and thinning** in the affected sector
- **Arteriolar sclerosis** and narrowing
- **Optic disc pallor** (sectoral) if the papillomacular bundle was involved
- **Persistent visible emboli** (Hollenhorst plaques may remain visible indefinitely)

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

[[Optical coherence tomography]] is an indispensable tool in the diagnosis and monitoring of BRAO:

- **Acute phase**: OCT demonstrates **hyperreflectivity and thickening of the inner retinal layers** (RNFL, GCL, IPL, INL) in the affected sector, corresponding to intracellular edema. The outer retinal layers remain structurally intact.
- **Chronic phase**: Progressive **thinning and atrophy of the inner retinal layers** is observed, with relative preservation of the outer retina. [[Ganglion cell-inner plexiform layer]] (GC-IPL) thickness maps on spectral-domain OCT (SD-OCT) can precisely delineate the area of ganglion cell loss.
- **OCT can differentiate BRAO from other causes of retinal whitening**, such as [[cotton-wool spots]], [[commotio retinae]], or [[macular edema]] from other etiologies.

### Fundus Fluorescein Angiography (FFA)

[[Fluorescein angiography]] provides definitive confirmation of BRAO and characterizes the hemodynamic consequences:

| Phase | Findings in BRAO |
|---|---|
| **Arterial phase** | Delayed or absent filling of the affected branch arteriole; the dye front may stop abruptly at the site of occlusion |
| **Arteriovenous phase** | Delayed arteriovenous transit time in the affected sector; the corresponding venous branch shows delayed filling |
| **Late phase** | Possible mild staining of the vessel wall at the occlusion site; no significant leakage in uncomplicated cases |
| **Embolic visualization** | The embolus may appear as a filling defect within the arteriole |

FFA is particularly useful for distinguishing BRAO from [[branch retinal vein occlusion]] (BRVO), which presents with retinal hemorrhages, edema, and venous dilation rather than retinal whitening and arterial non-perfusion.

### OCT Angiography (OCTA)

[[OCT angiography]] is a non-invasive imaging modality that provides depth-resolved visualization of retinal and choroidal vasculature without dye injection:

- Demonstrates **decreased or absent flow signal** in the superficial capillary plexus (SCP) and, to a lesser extent, the deep capillary plexus (DCP) in the affected sector.
- Can delineate the precise area of capillary non-perfusion.
- Useful for longitudinal monitoring of reperfusion or persistent ischemia.
- May reveal subtle areas of capillary dropout not apparent on clinical examination.

### Visual Field Testing

[[Automated perimetry]] (e.g., [[Humphrey visual field]] 24-2 or 30-2) documents the scotoma corresponding to the ischemic retinal territory:

- **Sectoral, arcuate, or altitudinal visual field defects** are characteristic.
- The defect respects the horizontal raphe if a superior or inferior branch is occluded.
- Serial visual field testing can monitor for improvement or stability over time.

### Ancillary Ophthalmic Testing

- **[[Relative afferent pupillary defect]] (RAPD) testing**: Usually absent or trace in BRAO; a prominent RAPD should raise suspicion for more extensive ischemia or concurrent optic neuropathy.
- **[[Intraocular pressure]] (IOP) measurement**: Typically normal; NVG is exceedingly rare in isolated BRAO.
- **[[Color vision testing]]**: May be affected if the papillomacular bundle is involved.

## Systemic Workup

BRAO is a **medical emergency** not only for the eye but for the patient's overall vascular health. It is considered a **retinal stroke equivalent**, and current evidence-based guidelines recommend urgent systemic evaluation analogous to that performed for TIA or acute ischemic stroke.

### Recommended Systemic Investigations

| Investigation | Rationale |
|---|---|
| **[[Carotid duplex ultrasonography]]** | Detect ipsilateral [[carotid artery stenosis]] or plaque; present in up to 70% of BRAO patients |
| **[[Echocardiography]]** (transthoracic ± transesophageal) | Identify cardiac sources of embolism: [[valvular heart disease]], [[patent foramen ovale]] (PFO), [[left atrial thrombus]], [[cardiac myxoma]], [[endocarditis]] |
| **[[Electrocardiogram]] (ECG) / [[Holter monitor]]** | Detect [[atrial fibrillation]] or other arrhythmias |
| **[[Magnetic resonance imaging]] (MRI) of the brain** | Identify concurrent or prior cerebral infarction; studies show 20–25% of BRAO patients have evidence of acute cerebral ischemia on diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) |
| **[[Magnetic resonance angiography]] (MRA) or [[CT angiography]] (CTA)** | Evaluate intracranial and extracranial vasculature |
| **Blood pressure measurement** | Screen for undiagnosed or uncontrolled [[hypertension]] |
| **Fasting lipid panel** | Assess for [[hyperlipidemia]] |
| **Fasting blood glucose / [[HbA1c]]** | Screen for [[diabetes mellitus]] |
| **Complete blood count (CBC)** | Evaluate for [[polycythemia]], [[thrombocytosis]], or [[leukocytosis]] |
| **[[Erythrocyte sedimentation rate]] (ESR) and [[C-reactive protein]] (CRP)** | Mandatory in patients ≥50 years to exclude [[giant cell arteritis]] |
| **[[Hypercoagulability panel]]** | In patients <50 years without traditional risk factors: [[antiphospholipid antibodies]], [[lupus anticoagulant]], [[homocysteine]], [[protein C]], [[protein S]], [[antithrombin III]], [[Factor V Leiden]] |

### Stroke Risk and Urgent Referral

Landmark studies, including the work by Hayreh et al. and the population-based analyses from Park et al. (2015), have demonstrated that:

- Patients with retinal artery occlusion have a **2- to 8-fold increased risk of stroke** compared to age-matched controls.
- The risk of stroke is **highest within the first 7 days** following the retinal event.
- Up to **25% of patients with BRAO have concurrent silent cerebral infarcts** on MRI.

Current guidelines from the **American Heart Association/American Stroke Association (AHA/ASA)** classify retinal artery occlusion as an **acute ischemic cerebrovascular event** and recommend emergent evaluation in a stroke center or equivalent facility, ideally within 24 hours of symptom onset.

## Differential Diagnosis

The differential diagnosis of BRAO includes conditions that may present with acute sectoral retinal whitening or sudden visual field loss:

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| **[[Central retinal artery occlusion]] (CRAO)** | Diffuse retinal whitening, cherry-red spot, profound vision loss, RAPD |
| **[[Branch retinal vein occlusion]] (BRVO)** | Flame-shaped hemorrhages, venous dilation, macular edema; no retinal whitening |
| **[[Cotton-wool spot]]** | Smaller, more focal area of retinal whitening; associated with [[hypertension]], [[diabetes]], [[HIV]] |
| **[[Commotio retinae]] (Berlin edema)** | History of ocular trauma; outer retinal disruption on OCT |
| **[[Optic neuritis]]** | Pain with eye movement, central scotoma, RAPD; normal fundus or disc edema |
| **[[Paracentral acute middle maculopathy]] (PAMM)** | Hyperreflective band at the level of the INL on OCT; involves the deep capillary plexus |
| **[[Acute macular neuroretinopathy]] (AMN)** | Wedge-shaped reddish-brown lesions; hyperreflectivity of the outer plexiform/outer nuclear layer on OCT |
| **[[Susac syndrome]]** | Multiple BRAOs, sensorineural hearing loss, encephalopathy; typically in young women |

## Management

### Acute Management

Unlike CRAO, where the entire macular blood supply is compromised and the window for intervention is extremely narrow, BRAO often has a more favorable natural history because:

1. The fovea may be spared.
2. Emboli frequently fragment and migrate distally, allowing partial reperfusion.
3. Collateral circulation may develop over time.

Nevertheless, acute management strategies have been employed, particularly when the fovea is threatened:

#### Conservative / Observation

- Many cases of BRAO are managed conservatively, as spontaneous visual improvement occurs in **approximately 80% of patients** over weeks to months.
- The retinal whitening resolves as the edematous tissue undergoes atrophy, and the visual field defect may partially improve as collateral circulation develops or the embolus migrates.

#### Ocular Massage

- **Digital ocular massage** or massage with a [[Goldmann three-mirror lens]] may be attempted in the acute setting to dislodge the embolus distally.
- Technique: Firm pressure is applied to the globe through the closed eyelid for 5–15 seconds, then suddenly released. This is repeated multiple times. The goal is to create fluctuations in [[intraocular pressure]] that may mechanically dislodge the embolus.
- Efficacy is unproven in controlled trials, but it is a low-risk intervention that may be attempted in the acute setting.

#### IOP-Lowering Therapy

- Acute reduction of IOP may improve the perfusion pressure gradient across the [[lamina cribrosa]] and facilitate distal migration of the embolus.
- Agents include:
  - **[[Timolol]] 0.5%** topical
  - **[[Brimonidine]] 0.2%** topical
  - **[[Acetazolamide]] 500 mg IV or oral** (a [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitor]])
  - **[[Anterior chamber paracentesis]]**: Withdrawal of 0.1–0.2 mL of aqueous humor via a paracentesis needle to rapidly lower IOP. This is typically performed by an ophthalmologist.

#### Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy (HBOT)

- [[Hyperbaric oxygen therapy]] has been proposed to increase oxygen delivery to the ischemic retina via diffusion from the [[choroidal circulation]].
- May be considered in acute BRAO with foveal involvement if available within the therapeutic window (ideally <8–12 hours).
- Evidence is limited and largely based on case series and retrospective studies.

#### Intra-arterial Thrombolysis

- **Selective ophthalmic artery catheterization with local delivery of [[tissue plasminogen activator]] (tPA)** has been investigated for acute retinal artery occlusions.
- The **EAGLE study** (European Assessment Group for Lysis in the Eye, 2015) evaluated intra-arterial fibrinolysis for CRAO and was terminated early due to lack of efficacy and increased adverse events. Data specific to BRAO are limited.
- This intervention is **not routinely recommended** for BRAO given the generally favorable natural history and the procedural risks (stroke, hemorrhage).

#### Systemic Thrombolysis

- Intravenous tPA, as used in acute ischemic stroke, has been reported in case series for acute retinal artery occlusion.
- Given the stroke-equivalent nature of BRAO, some stroke centers may consider IV tPA if the patient presents within the standard thrombolytic window (4.5 hours) and meets criteria.
- This remains controversial and is not standard of care for isolated BRAO.

### Chronic / Long-Term Management

The most critical aspect of BRAO management is **secondary prevention of cardiovascular and cerebrovascular events**:

| Intervention | Details |
|---|---|
| **Antiplatelet therapy** | [[Aspirin]] 81–325 mg daily is the cornerstone of secondary prevention; [[clopidogrel]] may be used in aspirin-intolerant patients |
| **Anticoagulation** | [[Warfarin]] or direct oral anticoagulants ([[DOACs]]) such as [[apixaban]], [[rivaroxaban]], or [[dabigatran]] if [[atrial fibrillation]] or other cardioembolic source is identified |
| **Statin therapy** | [[Atorvastatin]] or [[rosuvastatin]] for [[hyperlipidemia]] and plaque stabilization |
| **Antihypertensive therapy** | Target blood pressure per current guidelines (typically <130/80 mmHg) |
| **Glycemic control** | Optimization of [[HbA1c]] in diabetic patients |
| **Smoking cessation** | Counseling and pharmacotherapy as needed |
| **Carotid endarterectomy / stenting** | Referral to [[vascular surgery]] if ipsilateral [[carotid stenosis]] ≥70% (symptomatic) per NASCET criteria |
| **Cardiac intervention** | PFO closure, valve replacement, or other cardiac procedures as indicated |

### Management of Complications

- **[[Retinal neovascularization]]**: Rare in isolated BRAO but may occur if the ischemic area is extensive. If present, [[panretinal photocoagulation]] (PRP) or [[anti-VEGF]] intravitreal injections (e.g., [[bevacizumab]], [[ranibizumab]], [[aflibercept]]) may be indicated.
- **[[Neovascular glaucoma]]**: Exceedingly rare in BRAO; managed with anti-VEGF injections, PRP, and IOP-lowering agents if it occurs.
- **[[Macular edema]]**: Uncommon in BRAO (more characteristic of BRVO); if present, [[intravitreal corticosteroids]] or anti-VEGF agents may be considered.

## Prognosis

The visual prognosis of BRAO is generally more favorable than that of CRAO:

| Prognostic Factor | Details |
|---|---|
| **Initial visual acuity** | Approximately 74–89% of patients with BRAO present with visual acuity of 20/40 or better |
| **Visual improvement** | ~80% of patients experience some degree of visual improvement over weeks to months |
| **Final visual acuity** | Majority retain 20/40 or better; prognosis is worse if the fovea is involved |
| **Visual field recovery** | Partial improvement is common, but a residual scotoma often persists |
| **Recurrence** | Risk of recurrent BRAO or other retinal vascular events is approximately 1–2% per year |
| **Systemic prognosis** | Significantly increased cardiovascular and cerebrovascular morbidity and mortality; 5-year mortality rate is elevated compared to age-matched controls |

Factors associated with poorer visual prognosis include:

- Foveal involvement
- Calcific emboli (tend to cause more proximal and complete occlusion)
- Delayed presentation
- Absence of a [[cilioretinal artery]]
- Underlying vasculitis (e.g., [[giant cell arteritis]])

## Special Considerations

### Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)

In any patient ≥50 years presenting with BRAO, [[giant cell arteritis]] must be excluded. Although GCA more commonly causes [[anterior ischemic optic neuropathy]] (AION) or CRAO, it can present as BRAO. Key features suggesting GCA include:

- New-onset headache, jaw claudication, scalp tenderness, polymyalgia rheumatica symptoms
- Markedly elevated ESR (>50 mm/hr) and CRP
- Requires urgent [[temporal artery biopsy]] and immediate initiation of high-dose systemic [[corticosteroids]] (e.g., [[methylprednisolone]] 1 g IV daily for 3 days, followed by oral [[prednisone]] 1 mg/kg/day with taper)

### Younger Patients (<50 years)

BRAO in younger patients without traditional cardiovascular risk factors warrants an expanded workup including:

- [[Hypercoagulability panel]]
- [[Antinuclear antibody]] (ANA), [[anti-double-stranded DNA]], [[antiphospholipid antibodies]]
- Evaluation for [[Susac syndrome]], [[sickle cell disease]], [[oral contraceptive]] use, [[substance abuse]] (particularly [[cocaine]])
- [[Transesophageal echocardiography]] to evaluate for [[patent foramen ovale]] with paradoxical embolism

### Iatrogenic BRAO

BRAO has been reported following:

- **Cosmetic filler injections**: Particularly [[hyaluronic acid]] fillers injected in the glabellar, nasal, or periorbital regions. Retrograde arterial flow can carry filler material into the [[ophthalmic artery]] and its branches. Treatment includes immediate injection of [[hyaluronidase]] (if hyaluronic acid filler), aggressive IOP lowering, and emergent ophthalmologic consultation.
- **Retrobulbar/peribulbar anesthesia**: Rare complication.
- **Ophthalmic surgery**: Including [[cataract surgery]] and [[vitreoretinal surgery]].

## Role of the Optometrist

Optometrists play a critical role in the management of BRAO at multiple levels:

1. **Acute recognition**: Identifying the characteristic fundoscopic findings and symptoms of BRAO during comprehensive eye examinations.
2. **Emergent referral**: Facilitating urgent referral to a [[retina specialist]] and/or stroke center for systemic evaluation.
3. **Systemic risk factor identification**: Coordinating with primary care physicians and cardiologists for cardiovascular workup.
4. **Longitudinal monitoring**: Performing serial [[OCT]], [[visual field testing]], and [[dilated fundus examination]] to monitor for resolution, complications (neovascularization), or recurrence.
5. **Patient education**: Counseling patients on the systemic significance of BRAO, the importance of medication compliance, and the need for ongoing cardiovascular risk factor management.
6. **Incidental embolus detection**: Asymptomatic retinal emboli (Hollenhorst plaques) detected during routine eye examinations should prompt cardiovascular referral, as they are associated with increased stroke and cardiovascular mortality.

---

## Summary

Branch Retinal Artery Occlusion is a sight-threatening and systemically significant vascular event that demands prompt ophthalmic diagnosis and urgent systemic evaluation. While the ocular prognosis is generally favorable compared to CRAO, the identification of BRAO should trigger an immediate and comprehensive cardiovascular and cerebrovascular workup, as it serves as a sentinel event for potentially life-threatening systemic vascular disease. The optometrist, as a primary eye care provider, is uniquely positioned to detect BRAO, initiate appropriate referrals, and contribute to the long-term management and monitoring of affected patients.
