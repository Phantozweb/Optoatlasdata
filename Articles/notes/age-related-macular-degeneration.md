

# Age-Related Macular Degeneration

---

**Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)** is a chronic, progressive degenerative disease of the [[macula]], the central region of the [[retina]] responsible for high-acuity and color vision. AMD is the leading cause of irreversible central vision loss among individuals aged 50 years and older in developed nations. The disease is characterized by the accumulation of [[drusen]], progressive dysfunction and loss of the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE), and subsequent degeneration of overlying [[photoreceptors]]. AMD is broadly classified into two clinical forms: **non-neovascular (dry/atrophic) AMD** and **neovascular (wet/exudative) AMD**. While dry AMD accounts for approximately 85–90% of all cases, neovascular AMD is responsible for the majority of severe, rapid vision loss associated with the disease.

---

## Epidemiology

AMD is a major global public health concern. The worldwide prevalence is estimated at approximately 8.7% among individuals aged 45–85 years, with projections suggesting that nearly 288 million people will be affected by 2040. The prevalence increases significantly with advancing age: approximately 2% of individuals aged 50–59 are affected, rising to over 30% in those aged 75 and older.

| **Risk Factor** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| **Age** | Strongest risk factor; prevalence increases exponentially after age 50 |
| **Ethnicity** | Higher prevalence in Caucasian populations; lower in African American and Hispanic populations for late AMD, though early AMD rates may be comparable |
| **Smoking** | Most significant modifiable risk factor; 2–4× increased risk; dose-dependent relationship |
| **Family History / Genetics** | First-degree relatives have 3–6× increased risk; [[CFH]], [[ARMS2/HTRA1]], [[C3]], [[CFB]] gene variants strongly implicated |
| **Sex** | Slightly higher prevalence in females, possibly related to longer life expectancy |
| **Cardiovascular Disease** | [[Hypertension]], [[atherosclerosis]], and elevated [[C-reactive protein]] associated with increased risk |
| **Obesity** | BMI > 30 associated with progression from early to advanced AMD |
| **Diet** | Low dietary intake of [[lutein]], [[zeaxanthin]], [[omega-3 fatty acids]], and antioxidants associated with increased risk |
| **Sunlight Exposure** | Chronic cumulative exposure to blue and ultraviolet light may contribute |

## Anatomy and Physiology of the Macula

Understanding AMD requires a thorough knowledge of macular anatomy. The [[macula lutea]] is an oval-shaped, pigmented area approximately 5.5 mm in diameter centered on the [[fovea]], located temporal to the [[optic disc]]. The fovea (approximately 1.5 mm in diameter) contains the highest density of [[cone photoreceptors]] and is devoid of retinal blood vessels, relying entirely on the underlying [[choriocapillaris]] for its metabolic supply.

The critical layers involved in AMD pathology include:

- **[[Photoreceptor]] outer segments** – Continuously shed and are phagocytosed by the RPE.
- **[[Retinal Pigment Epithelium]] (RPE)** – A monolayer of polarized, pigmented cells that performs essential functions including phagocytosis of photoreceptor outer segments, transport of nutrients and waste, absorption of stray light, secretion of growth factors (including [[VEGF]] and [[PEDF]]), and maintenance of the outer [[blood-retinal barrier]].
- **[[Bruch's membrane]]** – A pentalaminar extracellular matrix structure situated between the RPE and the choriocapillaris, serving as a selective filtration barrier for metabolic exchange.
- **[[Choriocapillaris]]** – The innermost capillary layer of the [[choroid]], providing oxygen and nutrients to the outer retina.

The functional integrity of the RPE–Bruch's membrane–choriocapillaris complex is central to retinal health, and its progressive failure is the hallmark of AMD.

---

## Pathophysiology

The pathogenesis of AMD is multifactorial, involving a complex interplay of aging, genetic susceptibility, oxidative stress, chronic inflammation, and complement dysregulation. The disease process can be conceptualized as a cascade of events affecting the RPE–Bruch's membrane–choriocapillaris complex.

### 1. Aging and Metabolic Dysfunction

With normal aging, the RPE accumulates [[lipofuscin]], a heterogeneous mixture of autofluorescent, non-degradable byproducts of photoreceptor outer segment phagocytosis. A major toxic component of lipofuscin is **[[A2E]]** (N-retinylidene-N-retinylethanolamine), which is phototoxic and inhibits lysosomal function, further impairing RPE waste processing. Concurrently, Bruch's membrane undergoes age-related thickening due to the accumulation of lipid-rich debris, cross-linked collagen, and advanced glycation end-products (AGEs), reducing its hydraulic conductivity and impeding metabolic exchange between the RPE and choriocapillaris.

### 2. Drusen Formation

[[Drusen]] are focal extracellular deposits that accumulate between the basal lamina of the RPE and the inner collagenous layer of Bruch's membrane. They are the clinical hallmark of early AMD. Drusen contain a complex mixture of lipids (including esterified and unesterified [[cholesterol]], phospholipids), proteins (including [[apolipoprotein E]], [[vitronectin]], [[amyloid-beta]], and complement components such as [[C3]], [[C5]], and the [[membrane attack complex]] (MAC)), and cellular debris.

Drusen are classified as:

| **Drusen Type** | **Size** | **Characteristics** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Small (hard) drusen** | < 63 µm | Well-defined borders, round, discrete | Common with aging; low risk of progression alone |
| **Intermediate drusen** | 63–124 µm | Slightly less defined borders | Indicate intermediate AMD; moderate risk |
| **Large (soft) drusen** | ≥ 125 µm | Poorly defined borders, may be confluent | High risk for progression to advanced AMD |
| **Reticular pseudodrusen (subretinal drusenoid deposits)** | Variable | Located subretinally (above RPE), best seen on [[infrared reflectance]] and [[OCT]] | Strongly associated with progression to [[geographic atrophy]] and type 3 [[macular neovascularization]] |

### 3. Oxidative Stress

The macula is particularly vulnerable to [[oxidative stress]] due to its high metabolic rate, intense light exposure, high oxygen tension from the choriocapillaris, and abundance of polyunsaturated fatty acids in photoreceptor outer segment membranes. Reactive oxygen species (ROS) damage RPE cellular components, including mitochondrial DNA, lipids, and proteins. Smoking exacerbates oxidative damage by depleting endogenous antioxidants (e.g., [[glutathione]], [[ascorbic acid]]) and generating additional free radicals.

### 4. Complement Dysregulation and Chronic Inflammation

A pivotal discovery in AMD research was the identification of polymorphisms in the [[complement factor H]] (CFH) gene (particularly the Y402H variant) as a major genetic risk factor. CFH is a key negative regulator of the [[alternative complement pathway]]. Loss-of-function variants lead to unchecked complement activation at the level of Bruch's membrane and the choriocapillaris, resulting in chronic, low-grade inflammation termed **para-inflammation**. This leads to formation of the membrane attack complex (MAC), endothelial cell injury in the choriocapillaris, RPE damage, and recruitment of inflammatory cells including [[macrophages]] and [[microglia]]. Other complement-related genetic variants implicated include [[C3]], [[CFB]], [[CFI]], and [[C2]].

### 5. Choroidal Vascular Insufficiency

Progressive loss of the choriocapillaris, which may precede RPE atrophy in some models, reduces oxygen and nutrient delivery to the outer retina. This ischemic environment is a potent stimulus for the upregulation of [[vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF), which drives the neovascular form of AMD.

### 6. Neovascularization (Wet AMD)

In neovascular AMD, pathological angiogenesis occurs in response to an imbalance between pro-angiogenic factors (primarily [[VEGF-A]], but also [[placental growth factor]] (PlGF), [[PDGF]], and [[angiopoietin-2]]) and anti-angiogenic factors (such as [[PEDF]] and [[thrombospondin-1]]). Abnormal new blood vessels, collectively termed **[[macular neovascularization]] (MNV)**, arise and are classified by their anatomical origin:

| **MNV Type** | **Former Terminology** | **Origin** | **Location** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Type 1 MNV** | Occult [[choroidal neovascularization]] (CNV) | [[Choroid]] | Sub-RPE (beneath the RPE) |
| **Type 2 MNV** | Classic CNV | Choroid | Subretinal (above the RPE, below the neurosensory retina) |
| **Type 3 MNV** | [[Retinal angiomatous proliferation]] (RAP) | Retinal circulation | Intraretinal, extending downward toward the RPE/choroid |

These immature, fenestrated vessels leak fluid, lipid, and blood into the subretinal and sub-RPE spaces, causing acute disruption of macular architecture and rapid visual decline. Without treatment, the neovascular complex eventually undergoes fibrotic scarring, forming a **disciform scar**, which results in permanent, severe central vision loss.

## Classification and Staging

The clinical staging of AMD is most commonly based on the **Age-Related Eye Disease Study (AREDS)** classification and the more refined **Beckman Clinical Classification (2013)**:

| **Category** | **Clinical Findings** | **Risk of Progression to Late AMD (5 years)** |
|---|---|---|
| **No apparent aging changes** | No drusen, no pigmentary abnormalities | Baseline |
| **Normal aging changes** | Small drusen (≤ 63 µm, "drupelets") only | Very low |
| **Early AMD** | Medium drusen (> 63 µm, ≤ 125 µm) without pigmentary abnormalities | ~1.3% |
| **Intermediate AMD** | Large drusen (> 125 µm) and/or any pigmentary abnormalities (hyper- or hypopigmentation) associated with at least medium drusen | ~18% (higher with bilateral large drusen and pigmentary changes, up to ~47%) |
| **Late AMD** | Neovascular AMD (MNV with associated exudation) **and/or** [[Geographic Atrophy]] (GA) of the RPE involving the foveal center | N/A (endpoint) |

**[[Geographic atrophy]]** (GA) represents the advanced form of dry AMD, characterized by well-demarcated areas of RPE loss with exposure of the underlying choroidal vessels, accompanied by overlying photoreceptor degeneration. GA typically begins in the perifoveal region and gradually expands to involve the foveal center, causing progressive, irreversible central scotomas.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Early and Intermediate AMD

Many patients with early AMD are **asymptomatic** or report only subtle visual changes. Symptoms, when present, may include:

- Mild blurring of central vision, particularly under low-luminance conditions
- Difficulty with dark adaptation (prolonged [[dark adaptation]] time, often one of the earliest functional deficits)
- Reduced [[contrast sensitivity]]
- Need for increased illumination for reading
- Mild [[metamorphopsia]] (distortion of straight lines)

### Late AMD – Geographic Atrophy

- Progressive, painless central or paracentral [[scotoma]]
- Difficulty reading, recognizing faces, and performing tasks requiring fine detail
- Gradual onset over months to years
- Relatively preserved peripheral vision

### Late AMD – Neovascular AMD

- **Sudden or rapid onset** of central visual disturbance
- Prominent [[metamorphopsia]] (a hallmark symptom; patients may notice distortion on the [[Amsler grid]])
- Central or paracentral scotoma
- Decreased [[visual acuity]], which may be severe
- Occasionally, perception of a dark spot in central vision
- In cases of subretinal hemorrhage, sudden and profound vision loss

## Diagnosis and Diagnostic Modalities

A comprehensive diagnostic evaluation for AMD involves a combination of clinical examination and advanced multimodal imaging.

### 1. Clinical Examination

- **[[Best-corrected visual acuity]] (BCVA):** Measured using a [[Snellen chart]] or, preferably, an [[ETDRS]] (Early Treatment Diabetic Retinopathy Study) chart for standardized, reproducible measurements.
- **[[Amsler grid]] testing:** A simple, rapid screening tool for detecting metamorphopsia and central/paracentral scotomas. Patients with intermediate AMD should be instructed in home Amsler grid monitoring.
- **[[Dilated fundus examination]]:** Using [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] with a condensing lens (e.g., [[78D lens]], [[90D lens]], or [[Volk lens]]) or [[binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy]]. Findings may include drusen, pigmentary changes, RPE atrophy, subretinal fluid, subretinal hemorrhage, lipid exudates, or disciform scarring.

### 2. Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

**[[Spectral-domain OCT]] (SD-OCT)** and **[[Swept-source OCT]] (SS-OCT)** are indispensable in the diagnosis, classification, and monitoring of AMD. OCT provides high-resolution, cross-sectional images of the retinal layers and enables detection of:

- Drusen (size, volume, and morphology)
- Reticular pseudodrusen (subretinal drusenoid deposits)
- RPE irregularities (elevations, detachments, atrophy)
- [[Pigment epithelial detachment]] (PED) – serous, fibrovascular, or drusenoid
- Subretinal fluid (SRF)
- Intraretinal fluid (IRF) / [[cystoid macular edema]]
- Subretinal hyperreflective material (SHRM), often representing fibrin, blood, or neovascular tissue
- Geographic atrophy (loss of RPE and outer retinal layers, with enhanced choroidal signal due to loss of overlying tissue – "choroidal hypertransmission")
- Outer retinal tubulations (a biomarker of photoreceptor degeneration)

**[[Enhanced depth imaging OCT]] (EDI-OCT)** and SS-OCT allow improved visualization of the [[choroid]], enabling measurement of [[choroidal thickness]], which may be reduced in AMD.

### 3. OCT Angiography (OCTA)

**[[OCT angiography]]** is a non-invasive imaging modality that detects blood flow by comparing sequential OCT B-scans. OCTA can identify and delineate macular neovascularization (MNV) without the need for intravenous dye injection. It is particularly useful for:

- Detection of subclinical (non-exudative) MNV
- Characterization of MNV type and morphology
- Visualization of choriocapillaris flow voids (areas of non-perfusion)
- Monitoring treatment response

### 4. Fundus Fluorescein Angiography (FFA)

**[[Fluorescein angiography]]** remains a gold-standard diagnostic tool for neovascular AMD. After intravenous injection of [[sodium fluorescein]], sequential photographs capture the dye transit through the retinal and choroidal vasculature. Key findings include:

- **Classic CNV:** Well-defined early hyperfluorescence with progressive late leakage
- **Occult CNV:** Fibrovascular PED (stippled hyperfluorescence) or late leakage of undetermined source
- **Staining of drusen** (window defects)
- **Blocked fluorescence** from subretinal hemorrhage
- **RPE window defects** in geographic atrophy

### 5. Indocyanine Green Angiography (ICGA)

**[[Indocyanine green angiography]]** uses a dye that binds extensively to plasma proteins and fluoresces in the near-infrared spectrum, allowing superior visualization of the choroidal vasculature through overlying blood, pigment, and lipid. ICGA is particularly valuable for:

- Identifying Type 1 MNV (occult CNV) that may be poorly defined on FFA
- Detecting [[polypoidal choroidal vasculopathy]] (PCV), now classified as aneurysmal Type 1 MNV
- Evaluating Type 3 MNV (RAP)

### 6. Fundus Autofluorescence (FAF)

**[[Fundus autofluorescence]]** imaging detects the natural fluorescence of [[lipofuscin]] in the RPE. It is invaluable for:

- Mapping geographic atrophy (areas of GA appear as well-demarcated zones of **hypoautofluorescence** due to RPE loss)
- Identifying patterns of hyperautofluorescence at the margins of GA, which predict the rate of atrophy expansion (e.g., banded, diffuse, patchy, or focal patterns)
- Detecting reticular pseudodrusen

### 7. Microperimetry

**[[Microperimetry]]** (fundus-related perimetry) provides a functional map of retinal sensitivity correlated with specific retinal locations. It is useful for quantifying the functional impact of AMD, particularly in geographic atrophy, and for monitoring disease progression in clinical trials.

### 8. Dark Adaptometry

Impaired [[dark adaptation]] is one of the earliest measurable functional deficits in AMD, often preceding clinically visible changes. Instruments such as the **AdaptDx** measure the rod intercept time, which is prolonged in AMD. This may serve as a biomarker for early disease detection and risk stratification.

## Differential Diagnosis

While the clinical presentation of AMD is often characteristic, several conditions may mimic its features and must be considered:

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| [[Central serous chorioretinopathy]] (CSC) | Younger patients, serous PED, focal RPE leak on FFA, often self-limiting |
| [[Polypoidal choroidal vasculopathy]] (PCV) / Aneurysmal Type 1 MNV | Polypoidal vascular lesions on ICGA, serosanguineous PED, more common in Asian and African-descent populations; now considered within the AMD spectrum |
| [[Myopic macular degeneration]] | High myopia, lacquer cracks, posterior staphyloma |
| [[Macular dystrophies]] (e.g., [[Stargardt disease]], [[Best disease]], [[pattern dystrophy]]) | Younger onset, characteristic patterns on FAF and OCT, genetic testing |
| [[Macular telangiectasia type 2]] | Temporal juxtafoveal telangiectasia, crystalline deposits, characteristic OCT and FFA findings |
| [[Choroidal melanoma]] or [[choroidal metastasis]] | Elevated choroidal mass, B-scan ultrasonography findings |

## Management and Treatment

### Non-Neovascular (Dry) AMD

#### Nutritional Supplementation – AREDS and AREDS2

The landmark **[[Age-Related Eye Disease Study]] (AREDS, 2001)** and its successor **[[AREDS2]] (2013)** demonstrated that specific nutritional supplementation can reduce the risk of progression from intermediate to advanced AMD by approximately **25%** over 5 years.

| **Component** | **AREDS Formula** | **AREDS2 Formula (Recommended)** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Vitamin C]] | 500 mg | 500 mg |
| [[Vitamin E]] | 400 IU | 400 IU |
| [[Zinc]] (as zinc oxide) | 80 mg | 80 mg (or 25 mg in low-zinc formulation) |
| [[Copper]] (as cupric oxide) | 2 mg (to prevent zinc-induced copper deficiency) | 2 mg |
| [[Beta-carotene]] | 15 mg | **Removed** (replaced by lutein/zeaxanthin due to increased lung cancer risk in smokers) |
| [[Lutein]] | Not included | 10 mg |
| [[Zeaxanthin]] | Not included | 2 mg |

**Indications:** AREDS2 supplementation is recommended for patients with **intermediate AMD** (bilateral large drusen or pigmentary abnormalities) or **late AMD in one eye**. It is **not** indicated for patients with no AMD or early AMD only, as no benefit was demonstrated in these groups.

#### Lifestyle Modifications

- **Smoking cessation** – The single most impactful modifiable intervention
- **Cardiovascular risk factor management** – Control of [[hypertension]], [[hyperlipidemia]], and maintenance of healthy body weight
- **Dietary optimization** – Increased intake of dark leafy green vegetables (rich in lutein and zeaxanthin), fish (rich in omega-3 fatty acids), nuts, and fruits
- **UV/blue light protection** – Use of sunglasses with UV protection; blue-light filtering [[intraocular lenses]] after [[cataract surgery]] may offer theoretical benefit
- **Regular exercise** – Associated with reduced risk of AMD progression

#### Monitoring

Patients with intermediate AMD should be counseled on **home monitoring** with the [[Amsler grid]] or digital home monitoring devices (e.g., **ForeseeHome** monitoring program, which uses preferential hyperacuity perimetry) to detect early conversion to neovascular AMD. Regular follow-up with [[OCT]] imaging is essential.

#### Emerging Therapies for Geographic Atrophy

Two complement inhibitors have received FDA approval for the treatment of geographic atrophy:

- **[[Pegcetacoplan]]** (Syfovre®, Apellis Pharmaceuticals) – A [[C3]] inhibitor administered via intravitreal injection every 25–60 days. The OAKS and DERBY trials demonstrated a statistically significant reduction in the rate of GA lesion growth over 12–24 months.
- **[[Avacincaptad pegol]]** (Izervay®, Astellas/Iveric Bio) – A [[C5]] inhibitor administered via monthly intravitreal injection. The GATHER1 and GATHER2 trials showed significant reduction in GA growth rate.

These agents slow the rate of GA expansion but do not reverse existing atrophy. Long-term visual function outcomes and the clinical significance of growth rate reduction continue to be studied. Notably, both agents have been associated with a small increased risk of developing neovascular AMD (conversion to wet AMD), necessitating careful monitoring.

### Neovascular (Wet) AMD

#### Anti-VEGF Therapy

**Intravitreal anti-[[VEGF]] injections** represent the standard of care and have revolutionized the treatment of neovascular AMD. These agents inhibit VEGF-A, reducing vascular permeability, suppressing neovascular growth, and promoting regression of MNV.

| **Agent** | **Mechanism** | **Dosing Regimen** | **Key Trials** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Ranibizumab]]** (Lucentis®) | Humanized monoclonal antibody fragment (Fab) binding VEGF-A | 0.5 mg monthly or PRN or treat-and-extend | [[MARINA]], [[ANCHOR]], [[PIER]], [[HARBOR]] |
| **[[Bevacizumab]]** (Avastin®) | Full-length humanized monoclonal antibody binding VEGF-A (off-label) | 1.25 mg monthly or PRN or treat-and-extend | [[CATT]], [[IVAN]] |
| **[[Aflibercept]]** (Eylea®) | Recombinant fusion protein (VEGF trap) binding VEGF-A, VEGF-B, and PlGF | 2 mg monthly × 3, then every 8 weeks or treat-and-extend | [[VIEW 1]], [[VIEW 2]] |
| **[[Aflibercept 8 mg]]** (Eylea HD®) | Higher-dose aflibercept | 8 mg monthly × 3, then every 8–16 weeks | [[PULSAR]] |
| **[[Brolucizumab]]** (Beovu®) | Humanized single-chain antibody fragment (scFv) binding VEGF-A | 6 mg monthly × 3, then every 8–12 weeks | [[HAWK]], [[HARRIER]] |
| **[[Faricimab]]** (Vabysmo®) | Bispecific antibody targeting both VEGF-A and [[angiopoietin-2]] (Ang-2) | 6 mg monthly × 4, then every up to 16 weeks | [[TENAYA]], [[LUCERNE]] |

**Treatment Regimens:**

- **Fixed dosing:** Injections at predetermined intervals (e.g., monthly). Provides consistent suppression but may result in overtreatment.
- **Pro re nata (PRN):** Injections given only when disease activity is detected (fluid on OCT, decreased VA). Risk of undertreatment with reactive approach.
- **Treat-and-extend (T&E):** The most widely adopted regimen. After initial loading doses, the interval between injections is gradually extended if the macula is dry, or shortened if recurrence is detected. This approach aims to individualize treatment, reduce visit burden, and maintain disease control.

**Safety Considerations:**

- Common: Subconjunctival hemorrhage, transient floaters, mild ocular discomfort
- Uncommon but serious: [[Endophthalmitis]] (~0.02–0.05% per injection), [[retinal detachment]], traumatic [[cataract]], sustained [[intraocular pressure]] elevation
- Brolucizumab-specific: Reports of [[retinal vasculitis]] and [[retinal vascular occlusion]], including severe vision loss, have led to cautious use and monitoring protocols

#### Photodynamic Therapy (PDT)

**[[Verteporfin]] photodynamic therapy** (Visudyne®) involves intravenous infusion of the photosensitizer verteporfin, followed by activation with a 689 nm non-thermal diode laser applied to the area of CNV. This generates reactive oxygen species that cause selective thrombosis of the neovascular membrane. PDT was the first approved treatment for subfoveal CNV (TAP and VIP trials) but has largely been supplanted by anti-VEGF therapy. It retains a role as **adjunctive therapy** in:

- [[Polypoidal choroidal vasculopathy]] (PCV/aneurysmal Type 1 MNV), often combined with anti-VEGF (EVEREST II trial)
- Persistent or refractory neovascular AMD
- Cases where anti-VEGF monotherapy is insufficient

#### Thermal Laser Photocoagulation

**[[Laser photocoagulation]]** was historically used for extrafoveal and juxtafoveal classic CNV (Macular Photocoagulation Study, MPS). However, it causes permanent scotomas corresponding to the treatment area and has a high recurrence rate. It is now **rarely used** for AMD, having been replaced by anti-VEGF therapy, but may occasionally be considered for extrafoveal lesions in specific circumstances.

#### Surgical Interventions

Surgical approaches for AMD are limited and generally reserved for specific complications:

- **[[Pneumatic displacement of submacular hemorrhage]]:** Intravitreal injection of expansile gas (e.g., [[SF6]] or [[C3F8]]), often combined with intravitreal or subretinal [[tissue plasminogen activator]] (tPA), to displace large submacular hemorrhages away from the fovea.
- **[[Submacular surgery]]:** Vitrectomy with surgical removal of CNV membranes or submacular hemorrhage. The Submacular Surgery Trials (SST) showed limited visual benefit for most AMD subtypes, and this approach is not routinely performed.
- **[[Macular translocation]]:** Historically attempted to relocate the fovea to a healthier area of RPE; largely abandoned due to complexity and complications.

## Low Vision Rehabilitation

For patients with advanced AMD and significant, irreversible vision loss, **[[low vision rehabilitation]]** is an essential component of comprehensive care. Referral to a [[low vision specialist]] should be made when visual impairment interferes with daily activities despite optimal medical or surgical treatment.

Services and devices include:

- **Optical aids:** High-powered spectacle-mounted magnifiers, hand-held and stand magnifiers, telescopic devices
- **Electronic aids:** Closed-circuit television (CCTV) magnifiers, tablet-based magnification applications, screen readers
- **Non-optical aids:** Large-print materials, high-contrast items, task lighting, talking devices
- **Eccentric viewing training:** Teaching patients to use a preferred retinal locus (PRL) outside the scotomatous area for fixation
- **Occupational therapy and orientation/mobility training**
- **Psychosocial support:** Counseling for depression and anxiety, which are highly prevalent in AMD patients with vision loss
- **Implantable miniature telescope (IMT):** The [[CentraSight]] implantable miniature telescope is an FDA-approved device for end-stage bilateral AMD, implanted during cataract surgery in one eye to project a magnified image onto a larger area of the retina.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of AMD varies significantly by disease stage and type:

- **Early AMD:** Generally good visual prognosis; many patients maintain functional vision for years. Annual progression rate to late AMD is approximately 1–2%.
- **Intermediate AMD:** Variable; risk of progression to late AMD is significant, particularly with bilateral large drusen and pigmentary changes. AREDS2 supplementation reduces this risk.
- **Geographic Atrophy:** Progressive and irreversible. The rate of GA expansion averages approximately 1.5–2.6 mm²/year but is highly variable. Foveal involvement leads to severe central vision loss. New complement inhibitors may slow progression.
- **Neovascular AMD:** Without treatment, the natural history is poor, with most patients progressing to legal blindness within 2 years. With anti-VEGF therapy, approximately 90–95% of patients can maintain vision, and 30–40% achieve significant visual improvement (≥ 15 ETDRS letters gained). Long-term outcomes depend on treatment adherence, baseline visual acuity, lesion characteristics, and the presence of subretinal fibrosis.

## Emerging Therapies and Future Directions

The AMD therapeutic landscape is rapidly evolving. Key areas of active research include:

- **Gene therapy:** Subretinal or intravitreal delivery of genes encoding anti-VEGF proteins (e.g., **[[RGX-314]]**, **[[ADVM-022/ixoberogene soroparvovec]]**) to provide sustained, endogenous anti-VEGF production, potentially reducing or eliminating the need for repeated injections.
- **Port delivery system (PDS):** The **[[Susvimo]]** (ranibizumab port delivery system) is a surgically implanted, refillable intraocular reservoir that continuously releases ranibizumab. The ARCHWAY trial demonstrated non-inferiority to monthly ranibizumab injections with refills every 6 months. (Note: Susvimo was voluntarily recalled by Genentech in 2022 due to device-related issues but represents a proof-of-concept for sustained delivery.)
- **Complement inhibition for GA:** Beyond pegcetacoplan and avacincaptad pegol, additional complement pathway targets are under investigation.
- **Neuroprotection:** Agents aimed at preserving photoreceptor and RPE cell survival.
- **Stem cell and RPE cell replacement therapy:** Transplantation of stem cell-derived RPE cells (e.g., from [[induced pluripotent stem cells]] (iPSCs) or [[embryonic stem cells]]) into areas of geographic atrophy to restore RPE function. Early-phase clinical trials are underway.
- **Artificial intelligence (AI) in AMD:** Deep learning algorithms applied to OCT and fundus imaging for automated detection, grading, and prediction of AMD progression and treatment response. AI-based tools are increasingly being integrated into clinical workflows.
- **Biomarkers:** Identification of imaging biomarkers (e.g., hyperreflective foci on OCT, drusen volume, choriocapillaris flow deficits on OCTA) and systemic biomarkers (e.g., complement activation products) to improve risk stratification and personalize treatment.

## Patient Education and Counseling

Effective patient education is a cornerstone of AMD management. Key counseling points include:

1. **Nature of the disease:** AMD affects central vision but does not cause total blindness; peripheral vision is preserved.
2. **Importance of monitoring:** Regular home Amsler grid testing and prompt reporting of any new distortion, blurring, or dark spots.
3. **Smoking cessation:** Strongly and repeatedly encouraged.
4. **Nutritional supplementation:** AREDS2 vitamins as indicated; dietary counseling.
5. **Treatment adherence:** For neovascular AMD, emphasizing the critical importance of maintaining the anti-VEGF injection schedule to prevent irreversible vision loss.
6. **Driving safety:** Assessment of visual function for driving; referral for driving evaluation if indicated.
7. **Support resources:** Referral to low vision services, support groups (e.g., Macular Disease Foundation, American Macular Degeneration Foundation), and mental health services as needed.

---

## Summary

Age-Related Macular Degeneration is a complex, multifactorial disease that remains the predominant cause of central vision loss in the aging population. Advances in multimodal imaging—particularly [[OCT]], [[OCTA]], and [[fundus autofluorescence]]—have transformed our ability to detect, classify, and monitor the disease with unprecedented precision. The advent of anti-VEGF therapy has dramatically improved outcomes for neovascular AMD, while the recent approval of complement inhibitors for geographic atrophy marks the beginning of a new therapeutic era for dry AMD. Ongoing research into gene therapy, cell-based therapies, sustained drug delivery, and artificial intelligence promises to further refine and personalize the management of this sight-threatening condition. The role of the optometrist is central in early detection, risk stratification, patient education, co-management with retinal specialists, and provision of low vision rehabilitation services.
