

# Orthokeratology

---

**Orthokeratology** (commonly abbreviated as **Ortho-K**, also known as **corneal reshaping therapy (CRT)**, **corneal refractive therapy**, **overnight vision correction**, or **vision shaping treatment (VST)**) is a non-surgical, reversible clinical technique that employs specially designed [[rigid gas-permeable contact lenses]] (RGP lenses) worn primarily during sleep to temporarily reshape the [[corneal]] [[epithelium]] and alter the [[refractive error]] of the eye. The procedure is most commonly prescribed for the correction of [[myopia]] (nearsightedness), although modern lens designs also address mild [[hyperopia]], [[astigmatism]], and [[presbyopia]]. In recent decades, orthokeratology has gained significant clinical prominence as one of the most evidence-based interventions for **[[myopia control]]** in pediatric and adolescent populations, with robust data demonstrating a slowing of [[axial length]] elongation of the [[globe]].

The technique relies on the application of a reverse-geometry lens design that exerts controlled hydraulic forces on the [[tear film]] beneath the lens, inducing a redistribution of [[corneal epithelial cells]] from the central cornea to the mid-peripheral cornea. This results in a temporary flattening of the central corneal curvature, thereby reducing the refractive power of the eye and enabling clear unaided vision during waking hours after lens removal.

---

## History and Evolution

The concept of reshaping the cornea with contact lenses dates back to the 1940s and 1950s, when early practitioners observed that patients wearing flat-fitting [[polymethyl methacrylate]] (PMMA) hard contact lenses experienced temporary reductions in myopia after lens removal. These observations were largely anecdotal and uncontrolled.

In the 1960s, **George Jessen** is widely credited with formalizing the concept, introducing the "orthofocus" technique, which involved fitting a PMMA lens with a base curve flatter than the flattest [[keratometry]] reading (the so-called "Jessen factor"). However, the results were unpredictable, the degree of correction was limited (typically less than 1.00 D), and the non-permeable PMMA material posed significant risks for [[corneal hypoxia]].

The modern era of orthokeratology began in the late 1990s and early 2000s with two pivotal developments:

1. **Reverse-geometry lens designs**: Pioneered by practitioners such as **Tom Reim**, **John Mountford**, and **Richard Wlodyga**, these lenses featured a central optic zone flatter than the cornea, surrounded by a steeper "reverse curve," a peripheral alignment curve, and an edge lift zone. This four- to five-zone design dramatically improved centration, predictability, and the magnitude of achievable correction.
2. **High-[[oxygen permeability]] (Dk) materials**: The advent of hyper-Dk materials such as [[hexafocon A]], [[Boston XO]], and [[Menicon Z]] (with Dk values exceeding 100 × 10⁻¹¹) allowed safe overnight wear by permitting adequate oxygen transmissibility ([[Dk/t]]) to the [[corneal endothelium]] and [[corneal stroma]], dramatically reducing the risk of [[corneal edema]] and [[microbial keratitis]].

The **U.S. [[Food and Drug Administration]] (FDA)** approved the first overnight orthokeratology lens designs in 2002, including the **Paragon CRT** (Paragon Vision Sciences) and **Euclid Emerald** lens systems. Since then, numerous proprietary designs have been developed worldwide, including **BE Free**, **DreamLens**, **iSee**, **Contex OK**, and many others.

## Mechanism of Action and Pathophysiology

### Corneal Reshaping Biomechanics

The fundamental mechanism of orthokeratology involves the **redistribution of corneal epithelial tissue** rather than permanent structural alteration of the [[corneal stroma]]. Understanding this mechanism requires knowledge of the hydraulic forces generated by the reverse-geometry lens design interacting with the post-lens [[tear film]].

#### Reverse-Geometry Lens Zones

A typical modern orthokeratology lens consists of four to five distinct zones:

| Zone | Name | Function |
|------|------|----------|
| **Zone 1** | Base Curve / Treatment Zone (BOZR) | Flatter than central corneal curvature; creates positive pressure (compression) on the central epithelium |
| **Zone 2** | Reverse Curve (RC) | Significantly steeper than the base curve; creates a reservoir of negative pressure (suction) in the mid-peripheral tear film |
| **Zone 3** | Alignment Curve (AC) | Aligns with the mid-peripheral cornea to provide lens stability, centration, and bearing |
| **Zone 4** | Peripheral Curve / Edge Lift (PC) | Provides adequate edge clearance for tear exchange and lens removal |

#### Hydraulic (Squeeze Film) Forces

When the orthokeratology lens is placed on the eye and the eyelids close during sleep, the following biomechanical events occur:

1. **Central compression**: The flat base curve of the lens creates a thin tear film layer (approximately 5–10 μm) over the central cornea. The positive hydraulic pressure generated by this thin tear film compresses the central [[corneal epithelium]], causing a thinning of the central epithelial layer by approximately 5–10 μm. This thinning is achieved through a combination of cellular compaction and lateral migration of epithelial cells.

2. **Mid-peripheral suction**: The steep reverse curve creates a thick tear reservoir (approximately 40–60 μm) in the mid-peripheral zone. The negative hydraulic pressure in this region draws epithelial cells centrifugally, resulting in a **thickening of the mid-peripheral epithelium** by approximately 5–10 μm.

3. **Net effect**: The central epithelial thinning flattens the anterior corneal surface, reducing the [[corneal power]] and thereby correcting myopia. The mid-peripheral epithelial thickening steepens the mid-peripheral cornea.

This process has been confirmed by multiple studies using **[[optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)** and **high-frequency digital [[ultrasound]] [[pachymetry]]**, which demonstrate that the total corneal stromal thickness remains unchanged during orthokeratology treatment. The reshaping is confined entirely to the epithelial layer.

#### Refractive Optics

The central corneal flattening reduces the refractive power of the anterior corneal surface. The relationship between corneal flattening and refractive change can be approximated by the formula:

> **ΔD ≈ 0.50 × ΔK**

Where ΔD is the change in refractive error (in diopters) and ΔK is the change in central keratometry (in diopters). In practice, approximately **0.50 to 0.75 D of myopic correction** is achieved for every **1.00 D of central corneal flattening**.

Modern orthokeratology lens designs can reliably correct:

| Refractive Error | Typical Correction Range |
|---|---|
| [[Myopia]] | Up to −6.00 D (FDA-approved up to −6.00 D; off-label up to −8.00 to −10.00 D with advanced designs) |
| [[Astigmatism]] (with-the-rule) | Up to −1.75 D (standard designs); up to −3.50 D (toric ortho-K designs) |
| [[Hyperopia]] | Up to +3.00 D (limited designs available) |
| [[Presbyopia]] | Multifocal ortho-K designs (emerging) |

### Myopia Control Mechanism

One of the most clinically significant aspects of orthokeratology is its demonstrated ability to slow the progression of myopia in children and adolescents. The proposed mechanism involves the **relative peripheral myopic defocus** created by the mid-peripheral corneal steepening.

In a myopic eye corrected with conventional [[spectacle lenses]] or standard [[contact lenses]], the peripheral retinal image typically falls behind the [[retina]] (peripheral [[hyperopic defocus]]), which is believed to serve as a stimulus for continued [[axial elongation]] of the globe. Orthokeratology, by steepening the mid-peripheral cornea, shifts the peripheral image shell **in front of** the peripheral retina (peripheral myopic defocus), thereby removing the stimulus for axial elongation.

This theory is supported by animal models of emmetropization (studies in [[chick]], [[tree shrew]], and [[primate]] models) and by clinical trials demonstrating that orthokeratology reduces axial length elongation by approximately **40–60%** compared to single-vision spectacles or contact lenses.

## Clinical Indications

Orthokeratology is indicated in the following clinical scenarios:

- **Myopia correction** in patients who desire freedom from daytime spectacles or contact lenses but are not candidates for or do not desire [[refractive surgery]] (e.g., [[LASIK]], [[PRK]], [[SMILE]])
- **Myopia control** in pediatric and adolescent patients with progressive myopia, particularly those with:
  - Family history of high myopia
  - Young age of myopia onset (before age 10)
  - Rapid rate of myopia progression (≥ 0.50 D/year)
  - East Asian ethnicity (higher prevalence and progression rates)
- **Occupational or lifestyle needs**: Athletes, military personnel, pilots, swimmers, and individuals in dusty or dry environments who find daytime contact lens wear impractical
- **Mild to moderate [[astigmatism]]** correction (particularly with-the-rule astigmatism)
- **Anisometropia** management in children who are non-compliant with spectacles
- **Pre-presbyopic and presbyopic patients** using multifocal ortho-K designs (emerging indication)

## Contraindications

### Absolute Contraindications

- Active [[anterior segment]] infection (e.g., [[bacterial keratitis]], [[herpes simplex keratitis]], [[Acanthamoeba keratitis]])
- Active [[corneal inflammation]] or [[uveitis]]
- Severe [[dry eye disease]] unresponsive to treatment
- [[Keratoconus]] or other [[corneal ectasia]]
- Corneal scarring or opacity involving the central visual axis
- Inability to comply with lens care, hygiene, and follow-up requirements
- Corneal [[endothelial cell]] count below safe thresholds

### Relative Contraindications

- High myopia (> −6.00 D) — may achieve partial correction
- High astigmatism (> −1.75 D with standard designs)
- Against-the-rule or oblique astigmatism (more challenging to fit)
- Large [[pupil]] diameter relative to treatment zone (may cause [[halos]] and [[glare]])
- History of recurrent [[corneal erosion]]
- Chronic [[allergic conjunctivitis]] or [[giant papillary conjunctivitis]]
- Systemic conditions affecting wound healing (e.g., uncontrolled [[diabetes mellitus]], [[autoimmune disease]])
- Use of systemic medications that affect corneal physiology (e.g., [[isotretinoin]], [[amiodarone]])

## Patient Selection and Pre-Fitting Assessment

### Comprehensive Eye Examination

A thorough baseline examination is essential before initiating orthokeratology and should include:

| Assessment | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **[[Manifest refraction]]** and **[[cycloplegic refraction]]** | Accurate determination of refractive error; cycloplegic refraction is critical in children to unmask latent hyperopia and determine true myopia |
| **[[Keratometry]] / [[Corneal topography]]** | Measurement of central and peripheral corneal curvature; identification of corneal irregularity, [[astigmatism]] pattern, and [[eccentricity]] (e-value) |
| **[[Corneal topography]] (Placido disc or [[Scheimpflug imaging]])** | Detailed mapping of the anterior corneal surface; essential for lens design selection and post-treatment monitoring |
| **[[Pachymetry]]** (optical or ultrasonic) | Central and peripheral corneal thickness measurement |
| **[[Anterior segment OCT]]** | Epithelial thickness mapping (if available); baseline for monitoring epithelial redistribution |
| **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]** | Assessment of [[eyelid]] anatomy, [[tear film]], [[conjunctiva]], [[cornea]], and [[anterior chamber]] |
| **[[Tear film assessment]]** ([[TBUT]], [[Schirmer test]], [[tear meniscus height]]) | Evaluation of tear film quality and quantity; adequate tear film is essential for lens comfort and corneal health |
| **[[Pupillometry]]** | Measurement of scotopic and mesopic pupil diameter; large pupils relative to the treatment zone may experience visual aberrations |
| **[[Axial length]] measurement** (e.g., [[IOLMaster]], [[Lenstar]]) | Baseline measurement for myopia control monitoring in children |
| **[[Fundus examination]]** | Rule out peripheral retinal pathology, especially in higher myopes |
| **[[Intraocular pressure]] (IOP)** | Baseline measurement; note that corneal reshaping may affect [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]] readings |

### Ideal Candidate Profile

The ideal orthokeratology candidate typically presents with:

- Myopia of −0.50 to −6.00 D
- Corneal astigmatism ≤ 1.50 D (with-the-rule preferred)
- Corneal eccentricity (e-value) of 0.4–0.6 (a more prolate cornea responds more predictably)
- Adequate tear film quality and quantity
- Motivated patient (or parent) with realistic expectations
- Ability to handle, insert, remove, and care for rigid lenses
- Commitment to regular follow-up visits

## Lens Design and Fitting

### Empirical vs. Diagnostic Fitting

Modern orthokeratology fitting can be performed using two approaches:

1. **Empirical fitting**: The practitioner submits corneal topography data, refraction, and other measurements to the lens manufacturer, which uses proprietary software algorithms to calculate the optimal lens parameters. This is the most common approach with modern designs.

2. **Diagnostic (trial) fitting**: The practitioner selects a trial lens from a fitting set based on keratometry and refraction, evaluates the fluorescein pattern, and refines the fit. This approach provides immediate feedback but requires a comprehensive fitting set.

### Key Lens Parameters

| Parameter | Description |
|---|---|
| **Base Curve (BOZR)** | Determines the amount of central corneal flattening; typically set flatter than flat-K by the target correction amount (Jessen factor) |
| **Reverse Curve** | Controls the rate of transition from the flat central zone to the alignment zone; steeper reverse curves create more rapid epithelial redistribution |
| **Alignment Curve** | Matches the mid-peripheral corneal curvature to provide centration and stability |
| **Peripheral Curve / Edge Lift** | Provides adequate tear exchange and facilitates lens removal |
| **Overall Diameter** | Typically 10.0–11.5 mm; must be large enough to encompass the treatment zone and alignment zone |
| **Optic Zone Diameter** | Typically 6.0–8.0 mm; must be adequate to cover the entrance pupil |
| **Lens Material (Dk)** | Must have Dk ≥ 100 × 10⁻¹¹ for safe overnight wear |

### Fluorescein Pattern Assessment

When a diagnostic lens is placed on the eye, the ideal [[fluorescein]] pattern under [[cobalt blue]] illumination with a [[Wratten #12 yellow filter]] should demonstrate:

- **Central touch (bearing)**: Minimal or no fluorescein pooling centrally, indicating the flat base curve is in contact with or very close to the central cornea
- **Mid-peripheral pooling (reverse curve reservoir)**: A bright ring of fluorescein pooling in the reverse curve zone, indicating adequate tear reservoir for the hydraulic mechanism
- **Alignment zone bearing**: A dark ring of contact in the alignment zone, indicating proper lens centration and stability
- **Peripheral edge lift**: A thin ring of fluorescein at the lens edge, indicating adequate tear exchange

### Over-Refraction

A [[spherical over-refraction]] is performed with the diagnostic lens in situ. The ideal over-refraction should be approximately **+0.50 to +0.75 D** (slightly over-plussed), as the Jessen factor accounts for the expected regression of effect during the day.

## Treatment Protocol and Adaptation

### Initial Adaptation Period

The orthokeratology treatment effect develops progressively over the first **7–14 days** of overnight wear:

| Timeframe | Expected Outcome |
|---|---|
| **Night 1** | 50–75% of target correction achieved upon waking |
| **Days 2–3** | 75–90% of target correction |
| **Days 7–10** | 90–100% of target correction; vision may still fluctuate during the day |
| **Days 10–14** | Full correction typically stabilized; minimal diurnal fluctuation |

During the adaptation period, patients may require temporary daytime spectacles or soft contact lenses for residual refractive error.

### Wearing Schedule

- **Minimum overnight wear**: 6–8 hours per night is typically required to achieve and maintain the full treatment effect
- **Consistency**: Nightly wear is recommended for optimal and stable results; skipping nights may result in partial regression of the effect
- **Daytime wear**: The lenses are removed upon waking, and the patient enjoys clear unaided vision throughout the day

### Diurnal Regression

The orthokeratology effect is inherently temporary and reversible. After lens removal in the morning, the cornea gradually returns toward its original shape. The rate of regression depends on:

- Magnitude of the initial correction (higher corrections regress faster)
- Duration of overnight wear
- Individual corneal biomechanical properties
- Tear film dynamics

Most patients experience stable vision for **12–16 hours** after lens removal. If lenses are discontinued entirely, the cornea typically returns to its baseline shape within **72 hours to 2 weeks**, depending on the duration of prior treatment.

## Follow-Up Schedule and Monitoring

A structured follow-up protocol is essential for safety and efficacy:

| Visit | Timing | Key Assessments |
|---|---|---|
| **Visit 1** | 1 day after first overnight wear (morning, with lenses in situ) | Lens centration, movement, fluorescein pattern, over-refraction, corneal topography, slit-lamp examination |
| **Visit 2** | 1 week | Unaided visual acuity, manifest refraction, corneal topography, slit-lamp examination |
| **Visit 3** | 1 month | Unaided VA, refraction, topography, slit-lamp, lens condition assessment |
| **Visit 4** | 3 months | Comprehensive assessment including all of the above |
| **Ongoing** | Every 6 months | Comprehensive assessment; axial length measurement in myopia control patients; lens replacement as needed (typically every 12–18 months) |

### Topographic Assessment of Treatment

[[Corneal topography]] is the single most important tool for monitoring orthokeratology treatment. The ideal post-treatment topographic pattern is a **"bull's-eye" pattern** (also called a "smiley face" or "central island-free" pattern) characterized by:

- **Central flattening**: A well-centered zone of reduced corneal power corresponding to the treatment zone
- **Mid-peripheral steepening**: A concentric ring of increased corneal power surrounding the treatment zone (the "treatment ring" or "red ring")
- **Symmetry**: The treatment zone should be well-centered on the [[visual axis]] or [[corneal vertex]]

Common topographic abnormalities that require lens modification include:

| Topographic Finding | Likely Cause | Corrective Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Decentered treatment zone (smiley face)** | Lens decentration (usually inferior-temporal) | Steepen alignment curve, increase lens diameter, adjust reverse curve |
| **Central island** | Excessive central tear pooling; inadequate central compression | Flatten base curve or steepen reverse curve |
| **Incomplete treatment (residual myopia)** | Insufficient Jessen factor; inadequate wearing time | Flatten base curve; ensure adequate overnight wearing hours |
| **Over-correction (induced hyperopia)** | Excessive central flattening | Steepen base curve; reduce Jessen factor |
| **Asymmetric treatment** | Toric cornea; lens rotation | Consider toric ortho-K design |

## Myopia Control: Evidence Base

Orthokeratology is one of the most extensively studied interventions for myopia control. The following landmark studies and meta-analyses have established its efficacy:

### Key Clinical Studies

| Study | Year | Design | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|---|
| **LORIC (Longitudinal Orthokeratology Research in Children)** | 2005 | Prospective, non-randomized | Ortho-K reduced axial elongation by 46% over 2 years compared to spectacle-wearing controls |
| **CRAYON (Corneal Reshaping and Yearly Observation of Nearsightedness)** | 2012 | Prospective, non-randomized | Ortho-K reduced axial elongation by 56% over 2 years |
| **ROMIO (Retardation of Myopia in Orthokeratology)** | 2012 | Randomized controlled trial | Ortho-K reduced axial elongation by 43% over 2 years |
| **TO-SEE (Toric Orthokeratology – Slowing Eye Elongation)** | 2014 | Randomized controlled trial | Toric ortho-K reduced axial elongation by 52% over 2 years in children with moderate-to-high astigmatism |
| **Charm & Cho meta-analysis** | 2013 | Meta-analysis | Pooled analysis confirmed 40–50% reduction in axial elongation |
| **Sun et al. meta-analysis** | 2015 | Meta-analysis of 7 studies | Confirmed mean reduction in axial elongation of 0.26 mm over 2 years |
| **Bullimore et al.** | 2021 | Systematic review and meta-analysis | Confirmed ortho-K reduces axial elongation by approximately 0.09–0.12 mm/year |

### Combination Therapy

Emerging evidence supports the combination of orthokeratology with low-dose [[atropine]] (0.01%–0.05%) for enhanced myopia control. Studies such as the **AOK (Atropine with Orthokeratology)** study and **Kinoshita et al. (2020)** have demonstrated that combination therapy may provide additive effects, with axial elongation reductions exceeding 60–70% compared to single-vision correction alone.

## Complications and Safety

### Infectious Complications

The most serious potential complication of orthokeratology is **[[microbial keratitis]]**, which is a risk inherent to any form of overnight contact lens wear. The estimated incidence of microbial keratitis in orthokeratology is approximately:

- **7.7 per 10,000 patient-years** (Bullimore et al., 2013)
- Comparable to the risk of overnight wear of silicone hydrogel soft contact lenses

The most commonly implicated organisms include:

| Organism | Notes |
|---|---|
| **[[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]]** | Most common cause of severe bacterial keratitis in contact lens wearers |
| **[[Acanthamoeba]]** | Associated with water contamination of lens cases; higher incidence reported in some Asian studies |
| **[[Staphylococcus aureus]]** | Common commensal; may cause keratitis in compromised corneas |
| **[[Serratia marcescens]]** | Gram-negative rod; associated with contaminated lens care solutions |
| **Fungal organisms** | Rare but reported |

**Risk mitigation strategies** include:

- Strict hand hygiene before lens handling
- Use of approved [[hydrogen peroxide]]-based disinfection systems (e.g., [[ClearCare]], [[AO Sept]]) or multipurpose solutions specifically approved for RGP lenses
- Avoidance of water exposure to lenses and lens cases (no tap water rinsing)
- Regular lens case replacement (every 1–3 months)
- Patient education on symptoms of infection (pain, redness, discharge, photophobia) and the need for immediate presentation
- Regular follow-up visits

### Non-Infectious Complications

| Complication | Description | Management |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Corneal staining]]** (punctate epithelial erosions) | Mild central or mid-peripheral staining; common in early adaptation | Usually self-limiting; may require lens parameter adjustment |
| **[[Corneal iron ring]] (Fleischer-like ring)** | Iron deposition at the junction of the treatment zone and mid-peripheral steepening | Benign; no treatment required; resolves upon discontinuation |
| **Lens binding (adherence)** | Lens adheres to the cornea upon waking; may cause epithelial disruption upon removal | Instill [[preservative-free artificial tears]] before lens removal; adjust lens fit (increase edge lift) |
| **[[Corneal warpage]]** | Irregular corneal topography due to poorly fitting lens | Discontinue wear; refit with corrected parameters |
| **Induced [[higher-order aberrations]]** | Increased [[spherical aberration]], [[coma]], and [[trefoil]] due to the abrupt transition between treatment zone and mid-peripheral steepening | May cause halos, glare, and reduced contrast sensitivity, especially with large pupils; optimize treatment zone centration and diameter |
| **Dimple veiling** | Small, round depressions in the epithelium caused by trapped air bubbles beneath the lens | Usually benign and self-resolving; ensure proper lens application technique |
| **[[Conjunctival]] injection** | Mild redness upon lens removal | Usually transient; evaluate for lens fit issues or solution sensitivity |
| **Reduced [[contrast sensitivity]]** | Particularly in low-light conditions | Related to treatment zone size relative to pupil size; optimize lens design |
| **[[Fibrillary lines]]** | Fine lines in the corneal epithelium | Benign; associated with mechanical effect of the lens |

## Pharmacological Considerations

While orthokeratology is primarily a device-based therapy, several pharmacological agents are relevant to its practice:

| Agent | Role |
|---|---|
| **[[Sodium fluorescein]]** | Essential for fluorescein pattern assessment during diagnostic fitting |
| **[[Topical anesthetic]]** (e.g., [[proparacaine]], [[tetracaine]]) | May be used during initial lens insertion for anxious patients; NOT for routine home use |
| **[[Preservative-free artificial tears]]** | Recommended for instillation before lens removal in the morning to prevent lens binding and epithelial disruption |
| **[[Hydrogen peroxide]] disinfection systems** | Preferred lens care system for overnight ortho-K lenses due to superior disinfection efficacy and lack of preservative-related toxicity |
| **Low-dose [[atropine]]** (0.01%–0.05%) | Used as adjunctive therapy for enhanced myopia control in combination with ortho-K |
| **[[Topical antibiotics]]** (e.g., [[fluoroquinolones]]) | Used in the management of suspected or confirmed microbial keratitis |
| **[[Cycloplegic agents]]** (e.g., [[cyclopentolate]], [[tropicamide]]) | Used during pre-fitting cycloplegic refraction, especially in pediatric patients |

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Patients

Orthokeratology is widely used in children as young as **6–8 years of age** for myopia control. Key considerations include:

- **Parental involvement**: Parents must be educated on lens insertion, removal, care, and recognition of complications
- **Cycloplegic refraction**: Essential for accurate determination of refractive error in children
- **Axial length monitoring**: Serial [[axial length]] measurements (every 6 months) are the gold standard for assessing myopia control efficacy
- **Compliance**: Children are generally excellent overnight lens wearers, as the lenses are worn during sleep and removed by parents in the morning
- **Safety**: Studies have demonstrated that the risk profile in children is comparable to that in adults when proper hygiene and follow-up protocols are maintained

### High Myopia

Patients with myopia exceeding −6.00 D may benefit from **partial reduction orthokeratology**, in which the lenses reduce a portion of the myopia (e.g., −4.00 to −5.00 D), and the residual refractive error is corrected with thin daytime spectacles or low-powered soft contact lenses. This approach may still confer myopia control benefits through peripheral defocus modification.

### Post-Refractive Surgery

Orthokeratology has been reported in cases of **myopic regression** following [[LASIK]] or [[PRK]], although fitting is more complex due to the altered corneal geometry (oblate corneal shape). Specialized post-surgical ortho-K designs are available.

## Comparison with Other Myopia Control Strategies

| Intervention | Mechanism | Approximate Efficacy (Axial Length Reduction) | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Orthokeratology** | Peripheral myopic defocus via corneal reshaping | 40–60% | Freedom from daytime correction; well-studied; reversible | Requires overnight lens wear; risk of microbial keratitis; requires regular follow-up |
| **Low-dose [[atropine]]** (0.01%–0.05%) | Muscarinic receptor antagonism; scleral remodeling | 30–50% (dose-dependent) | Easy to administer (eye drops); minimal side effects at low doses | Rebound effect upon cessation; variable efficacy; requires compounding in some regions |
| **[[Multifocal soft contact lenses]]** (e.g., [[MiSight 1 day]]) | Peripheral myopic defocus | 40–50% | Disposable; easy to fit; FDA-approved for myopia control | Daytime wear required; potential for reduced visual quality |
| **[[Spectacle lenses]] with peripheral defocus** (e.g., [[DIMS]], [[HAL]]) | Peripheral myopic defocus | 50–60% | Non-invasive; easy compliance | Efficacy dependent on wearing time; cosmetic concerns in some patients |
| **Ortho-K + low-dose atropine** | Combined mechanisms | 60–70%+ | Potentially superior efficacy | Increased treatment burden; limited long-term data |

## Regulatory Status

- **United States**: FDA-approved for overnight wear for myopia correction up to −6.00 D and astigmatism up to −1.75 D (Paragon CRT, Euclid Emerald, and others). Myopia control is considered an off-label use in the U.S.
- **European Union**: CE-marked for myopia correction and increasingly recognized for myopia control
- **China, Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Hong Kong, Singapore**: Widely practiced and regulated; orthokeratology is one of the most commonly prescribed myopia control interventions in East Asia
- **Australia and New Zealand**: Regulated by the [[Therapeutic Goods Administration]] (TGA) and [[Medsafe]], respectively

---

## Emerging Technologies and Future Directions

- **Customized wavefront-guided ortho-K designs**: Lenses designed based on individual corneal topography and wavefront aberrometry data to optimize visual quality and reduce higher-order aberrations
- **Dual-focus ortho-K**: Designs incorporating additional zones of myopic defocus for enhanced myopia control
- **Scleral orthokeratology**: Experimental concept using large-diameter scleral lenses for corneal reshaping
- **3D-printed ortho-K lenses**: Emerging manufacturing technology for rapid, customized lens production
- **Extended-range ortho-K**: Advanced designs targeting higher levels of myopia (−8.00 to −10.00 D) and higher astigmatism
- **Artificial intelligence (AI)-assisted fitting**: Machine learning algorithms to predict optimal lens parameters from topographic and biometric data
- **Combination therapies**: Ongoing clinical trials evaluating ortho-K combined with [[red light therapy]], [[outdoor time interventions]], and pharmacological agents

---

## Summary

Orthokeratology represents a mature, evidence-based clinical modality in modern optometric and ophthalmic practice. Its dual role as both a refractive correction technique and a myopia control intervention has positioned it as an indispensable tool in the management of the global myopia epidemic. When prescribed and monitored by a skilled practitioner with appropriate patient selection, lens design, and follow-up protocols, orthokeratology offers a safe, effective, and reversible alternative to daytime optical correction and refractive surgery. The continued evolution of lens materials, designs, and fitting technologies promises to further expand the scope and efficacy of this remarkable clinical technique.
