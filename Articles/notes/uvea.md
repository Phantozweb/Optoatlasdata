

# Uvea

---

The **uvea** (also referred to as the **uveal tract** or **tunica vasculosa oculi**) is the pigmented, highly vascular middle coat of the [[eye]], situated between the outer fibrous tunic (the [[sclera]] and [[cornea]]) and the inner neural tunic (the [[retina]]). It is the principal vascular supply to the intraocular structures and plays indispensable roles in [[aqueous humor]] dynamics, light regulation, [[accommodation]], and ocular immune privilege. The uvea is composed of three anatomically continuous but functionally distinct structures: the [[iris]], the [[ciliary body]], and the [[choroid]]. Pathology of the uvea—collectively termed **uveal disease**—encompasses a broad spectrum of inflammatory, neoplastic, degenerative, and congenital conditions that are among the most clinically significant entities encountered in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]].

---

## Gross and Microscopic Anatomy

### Overview of the Three Components

The uveal tract extends from the [[pupil]] anteriorly to the [[optic nerve]] posteriorly. Its three divisions are continuous with one another and share a common embryological origin from [[neural crest]] cells and [[mesoderm]].

| Component | Location | Primary Functions |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Iris]]** | Most anterior; forms the posterior wall of the [[anterior chamber]] | Regulates pupil size and light entry; contributes to [[aqueous humor]] outflow |
| **[[Ciliary Body]]** | Intermediate; extends from the [[scleral spur]] to the [[ora serrata]] | Produces [[aqueous humor]]; controls [[accommodation]] via the [[zonules of Zinn]]; contributes to [[uveoscleral outflow]] |
| **[[Choroid]]** | Posterior; lines the inner sclera from the ora serrata to the optic nerve | Supplies oxygen and nutrients to the outer [[retina]] and [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE); thermoregulation; light absorption |

### The Iris

The [[iris]] is a thin, contractile diaphragm with a central aperture—the [[pupil]]. It is composed of:

- **Anterior border layer**: A discontinuous layer of [[fibroblasts]] and [[melanocytes]] that determines iris color.
- **Stroma**: Loose [[connective tissue]] containing [[collagen]], [[melanocytes]], [[mast cells]], [[macrophages]], blood vessels (branches of the [[major arterial circle of the iris]]), and nerves.
- **[[Sphincter pupillae]]**: A ring of [[smooth muscle]] innervated by [[parasympathetic]] fibers from the [[oculomotor nerve]] (CN III) via the [[short ciliary nerves]], responsible for [[miosis]].
- **[[Dilator pupillae]]**: A myoepithelial layer innervated by [[sympathetic]] fibers originating from the [[superior cervical ganglion]], responsible for [[mydriasis]].
- **Posterior pigmented epithelium**: Two layers of heavily pigmented [[epithelium]] continuous with the non-pigmented epithelium of the [[ciliary body]].

The iris vasculature is unique in that it lacks an internal elastic lamina, making it particularly susceptible to inflammation-induced vascular leakage—a hallmark of [[anterior uveitis]].

### The Ciliary Body

The [[ciliary body]] is a wedge-shaped structure divided into:

- **Pars plicata (corona ciliaris)**: The anterior, ridged portion containing approximately 70–80 [[ciliary processes]]. These processes are the site of [[aqueous humor]] secretion through active secretion, ultrafiltration, and diffusion. Each process is covered by a bilayer of epithelium: an inner [[non-pigmented ciliary epithelium]] (NPCE) and an outer [[pigmented ciliary epithelium]] (PCE).
- **Pars plana (orbiculus ciliaris)**: The posterior, flat portion extending to the [[ora serrata]]. The pars plana is a relatively avascular zone and is the preferred surgical entry site for [[pars plana vitrectomy]] and intravitreal injections.
- **[[Ciliary muscle]]**: A smooth muscle with longitudinal (Brücke's), radial, and circular (Müller's) fibers. Contraction of the ciliary muscle relaxes the [[zonules of Zinn]], allowing the [[crystalline lens]] to assume a more spherical shape for near focus ([[accommodation]]). The longitudinal fibers also open the [[trabecular meshwork]] and [[Schlemm's canal]], facilitating [[aqueous humor]] outflow.

The ciliary body receives its blood supply from the [[long posterior ciliary arteries]] and the [[anterior ciliary arteries]], which anastomose to form the [[major arterial circle of the iris]].

### The Choroid

The [[choroid]] is the most posterior and largest component of the uvea, approximately 0.1–0.22 mm thick. It is composed of the following layers (from external to internal):

1. **Suprachoroidal lamina (lamina fusca)**: A potential space between the sclera and choroid containing [[melanocytes]], [[fibroblasts]], collagen, and traversing vessels and nerves. This space is clinically significant as the site of [[choroidal effusion]] and [[suprachoroidal hemorrhage]].
2. **Haller's layer (outer vascular layer)**: Contains large-caliber choroidal vessels (arteries and veins).
3. **Sattler's layer (inner vascular layer)**: Contains medium-caliber vessels.
4. **[[Choriocapillaris]]**: A dense, single-layered capillary network with fenestrated endothelium that provides the primary blood supply to the outer retina, including the [[photoreceptors]] and [[retinal pigment epithelium]]. The choriocapillaris has one of the highest blood flow rates per unit tissue weight of any vascular bed in the body.
5. **[[Bruch's membrane]]**: A pentalaminar structure (basement membrane of the RPE, inner collagenous layer, elastic layer, outer collagenous layer, basement membrane of the choriocapillaris) that serves as a selective barrier between the choroid and the RPE. Degeneration of Bruch's membrane is central to the pathogenesis of [[age-related macular degeneration]] (AMD).

The choroid receives approximately 70–80% of the total ocular blood flow, primarily through the [[short posterior ciliary arteries]]. Venous drainage occurs via the [[vortex veins]] (venae vorticosae), typically four in number, which exit the globe posterior to the equator.

## Embryology

The uveal tract has a dual embryological origin:

- **[[Neural crest]] cells**: Give rise to the stromal components—melanocytes, fibroblasts, and connective tissue of the iris, ciliary body, and choroid, as well as the ciliary muscle and the vascular endothelium.
- **[[Neuroectoderm]]** (from the [[optic cup]]): Gives rise to the pigmented and non-pigmented epithelium of the ciliary body, the posterior pigmented epithelium of the iris, the [[sphincter pupillae]], and the [[dilator pupillae]] muscles.

Developmental anomalies of the uvea include [[aniridia]], [[coloboma]] (failure of closure of the [[embryonic fissure]]), [[persistent pupillary membrane]], and [[albinism]] (deficient melanin synthesis in uveal melanocytes).

## Physiology and Function

### Aqueous Humor Production and Dynamics

The [[ciliary body]] is the sole producer of [[aqueous humor]], secreting approximately 2.0–2.5 µL/min in the healthy adult eye. Aqueous humor is produced by the [[non-pigmented ciliary epithelium]] through three mechanisms:

1. **Active secretion** (accounts for ~80–90%): Energy-dependent transport of ions (Na⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻) via [[Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase]] and [[carbonic anhydrase]], creating an osmotic gradient that draws water into the [[posterior chamber]].
2. **Ultrafiltration**: Hydrostatic pressure-driven movement of plasma across the ciliary capillaries.
3. **Diffusion**: Passive movement of lipid-soluble substances along concentration gradients.

Aqueous humor flows from the posterior chamber through the [[pupil]] into the [[anterior chamber]], exiting via the **conventional (trabecular) pathway** (~80–90%) through the [[trabecular meshwork]] and [[Schlemm's canal]], and the **unconventional (uveoscleral) pathway** (~10–20%) through the ciliary muscle interstitial spaces into the [[suprachoroidal space]]. Disruption of these pathways by uveal inflammation or structural changes is a major mechanism of [[secondary glaucoma]].

### Light Regulation

The [[iris]] functions as a dynamic aperture, modulating the amount of light reaching the [[retina]]. The [[pupillary light reflex]] involves [[parasympathetic]] constriction of the [[sphincter pupillae]] in response to light, while the [[sympathetic]] nervous system mediates dilation via the [[dilator pupillae]] in dim conditions or during the fight-or-flight response.

### Accommodation

Contraction of the [[ciliary muscle]] under [[parasympathetic]] control (via [[muscarinic receptors]]) relaxes the [[zonules of Zinn]], allowing the elastic [[crystalline lens]] to increase its curvature and refractive power for near vision. This mechanism diminishes with age ([[presbyopia]]) as the lens loses elasticity.

### Choroidal Blood Flow and Thermoregulation

The [[choroid]] provides metabolic support to the outer retina and dissipates heat generated by light absorption in the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] and [[photoreceptors]]. Choroidal blood flow is regulated by both autonomic innervation and local autoregulatory mechanisms, including [[nitric oxide]] (NO) and [[endothelin-1]].

### Immune Privilege

The uvea plays a critical role in maintaining [[ocular immune privilege]]—a phenomenon whereby the eye tolerates foreign antigens without mounting a destructive inflammatory response. This is mediated by:

- The [[blood-aqueous barrier]] (tight junctions of the non-pigmented ciliary epithelium and iris vascular endothelium)
- The [[blood-retinal barrier]] (tight junctions of the RPE and retinal vascular endothelium)
- Immunosuppressive factors in the aqueous humor (e.g., [[TGF-β]], [[α-melanocyte-stimulating hormone]], [[vasoactive intestinal peptide]])
- **[[Anterior chamber-associated immune deviation]]** (ACAID): A systemic tolerance mechanism triggered by antigens introduced into the anterior chamber

Breakdown of these barriers is the fundamental event in [[uveitis]].

## Pathophysiology of Uveal Disease

### Uveitis (Uveal Inflammation)

[[Uveitis]] is the most clinically important category of uveal pathology, accounting for approximately 10–15% of legal blindness in the developed world. It is classified anatomically by the Standardization of Uveitis Nomenclature (SUN) Working Group:

| SUN Classification | Primary Site of Inflammation | Includes |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Anterior uveitis]]** | [[Anterior chamber]] | [[Iritis]], [[iridocyclitis]], [[anterior cyclitis]] |
| **[[Intermediate uveitis]]** | [[Vitreous]] | [[Pars planitis]], [[posterior cyclitis]], [[hyalitis]] |
| **[[Posterior uveitis]]** | [[Retina]] and/or [[choroid]] | [[Choroiditis]], [[chorioretinitis]], [[retinitis]], [[neuroretinitis]] |
| **[[Panuveitis]]** | Anterior chamber, vitreous, and retina/choroid | [[Behçet disease]], [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]], [[sympathetic ophthalmia]] |

#### Mechanisms of Inflammation

The pathophysiology of uveitis involves a complex interplay of innate and adaptive immune responses:

1. **Breakdown of the blood-ocular barriers**: Inflammatory mediators ([[prostaglandins]], [[leukotrienes]], [[histamine]], [[cytokines]] such as [[TNF-α]], [[IL-1β]], [[IL-6]], [[IL-17]]) increase vascular permeability, allowing proteins and inflammatory cells to enter the aqueous humor and vitreous.
2. **T-cell-mediated autoimmunity**: Many forms of non-infectious uveitis are driven by autoreactive [[CD4+ T helper cells]] (Th1 and Th17 subsets) that recognize uveal or retinal antigens (e.g., [[S-antigen]], [[interphotoreceptor retinoid-binding protein]]).
3. **Molecular mimicry**: Microbial antigens may share structural homology with ocular antigens, triggering cross-reactive immune responses (e.g., [[HLA-B27]]-associated anterior uveitis and enteric bacteria).
4. **Innate immune activation**: [[Toll-like receptors]] (TLRs) on uveal resident cells (macrophages, dendritic cells) recognize pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) and damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), initiating inflammatory cascades.
5. **Granulomatous vs. non-granulomatous inflammation**: Granulomatous uveitis (e.g., [[sarcoidosis]], [[tuberculosis]], [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]]) is characterized by aggregates of [[epithelioid cells]] and [[multinucleated giant cells]], producing large "mutton-fat" [[keratic precipitates]] and [[Busacca nodules]] or [[Koeppe nodules]] on the iris. Non-granulomatous uveitis (e.g., [[HLA-B27]]-associated) features predominantly [[lymphocytes]] and [[plasma cells]] with fine keratic precipitates.

### Uveal Neoplasia

The uvea is the most common site of primary intraocular malignancy in adults. Key neoplasms include:

- **[[Uveal melanoma]]**: The most common primary intraocular malignancy in adults, arising from uveal [[melanocytes]]. It most frequently involves the [[choroid]] (~85%), followed by the [[ciliary body]] (~10%) and [[iris]] (~5%). Genetic alterations include [[GNAQ]]/[[GNA11]] mutations (>80%), [[BAP1]] loss (associated with metastatic risk), and [[monosomy 3]] (poor prognosis). Metastasis occurs hematogenously, most commonly to the [[liver]].
- **[[Choroidal nevus]]**: A benign melanocytic lesion found in approximately 5–10% of the population. Risk factors for malignant transformation to melanoma include thickness >2 mm, subretinal fluid, symptoms, orange pigment ([[lipofuscin]]), margin within 3 mm of the [[optic disc]], ultrasonographic hollowness, and absence of a surrounding halo (mnemonic: "To Find Small Ocular Melanoma Using Helpful Hints" or the TFSOM-UHHD criteria).
- **[[Choroidal hemangioma]]**: A benign vascular tumor, either circumscribed (sporadic) or diffuse (associated with [[Sturge-Weber syndrome]]).
- **[[Choroidal metastasis]]**: The most common intraocular malignancy overall, most frequently from [[breast cancer]] in women and [[lung cancer]] in men.
- **[[Iris melanoma]]**: Generally carries a better prognosis than posterior uveal melanoma due to earlier detection and lower metastatic potential.
- **[[Medulloepithelioma]]**: A rare embryonal tumor of the [[non-pigmented ciliary epithelium]], typically presenting in childhood.

### Uveal Effusion and Detachment

- **[[Choroidal detachment]]**: Separation of the choroid from the sclera, which may be serous (transudative, as in hypotony) or hemorrhagic (rupture of choroidal vessels, often intraoperatively or post-traumatic). Serous choroidal detachments are smooth and dome-shaped, while hemorrhagic detachments are dark and convex, potentially "kissing" in the pupillary space.
- **[[Uveal effusion syndrome]]**: Idiopathic serous detachment of the choroid and [[ciliary body]], often associated with nanophthalmos or abnormal scleral permeability.

### Congenital and Developmental Anomalies

- **[[Coloboma]]**: A defect resulting from incomplete closure of the [[embryonic fissure]], typically located inferonasal. May involve the iris (keyhole pupil), ciliary body, choroid, or optic nerve.
- **[[Aniridia]]**: Absence or near-complete absence of the iris, often associated with [[PAX6]] gene mutations. Associated with [[Wilms tumor]] in the [[WAGR syndrome]] (Wilms tumor, Aniridia, Genitourinary anomalies, intellectual disability [Retardation]).
- **[[Albinism]]**: Deficient melanin in uveal melanocytes leading to iris transillumination, [[foveal hypoplasia]], and [[nystagmus]].
- **[[Persistent pupillary membrane]]**: Remnants of the anterior [[tunica vasculosa lentis]] that may bridge the pupil.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

The symptoms of uveal disease vary significantly depending on the anatomical location and nature of the pathology.

### Anterior Uveal Disease (Anterior Uveitis)

| Symptom | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Pain** | Ciliary spasm ([[ciliary body]] irritation); often deep, aching |
| **Photophobia** | Sphincter and ciliary muscle spasm triggered by light |
| **Lacrimation** | Reflex tearing from trigeminal nerve stimulation |
| **Blurred vision** | Protein and cells in the [[aqueous humor]]; [[posterior synechiae]]; [[cystoid macular edema]] |
| **Redness** | [[Ciliary flush]] (circumcorneal injection) due to dilation of deep episcleral and anterior ciliary vessels |
| **Miosis** | Sphincter spasm from [[prostaglandin]]-mediated irritation |

### Intermediate and Posterior Uveal Disease

| Symptom | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Floaters** | Inflammatory cells and debris in the [[vitreous]] |
| **Blurred or decreased vision** | [[Vitritis]], [[cystoid macular edema]], [[choroidal neovascularization]], [[serous retinal detachment]] |
| **Scotomata** | Chorioretinal lesions affecting the visual field |
| **Metamorphopsia** | Macular involvement (edema, subretinal fluid, or neovascularization) |
| **Typically painless** | Posterior structures lack pain fibers |

### Uveal Neoplasia

- Choroidal melanoma may be asymptomatic or present with blurred vision, visual field defects, photopsia, or metamorphopsia depending on location and size.
- Iris melanoma may present as a visible pigmented lesion, pupil distortion ([[corectopia]]), or [[secondary glaucoma]].

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical Examination

- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: The cornerstone of anterior uveitis diagnosis. Allows visualization of:
  - **[[Keratic precipitates]]** (KPs): Inflammatory cell deposits on the [[corneal endothelium]]. Fine KPs suggest non-granulomatous inflammation; large, greasy "mutton-fat" KPs suggest granulomatous inflammation.
  - **[[Anterior chamber cells and flare]]**: Graded 0 to 4+ per the SUN criteria. Cells represent inflammatory cells; flare represents protein leakage from disrupted blood-aqueous barrier.
  - **[[Posterior synechiae]]**: Adhesions between the iris and [[anterior lens capsule]], which may cause an irregular pupil or [[seclusio pupillae]] (360° synechiae) leading to [[iris bombé]] and [[secondary angle-closure glaucoma]].
  - **[[Koeppe nodules]]** (at the pupillary margin) and **[[Busacca nodules]]** (on the iris stroma): Granulomatous iris nodules.
  - **[[Iris heterochromia]]**: May indicate [[Fuchs heterochromic iridocyclitis]] or other chronic uveal conditions.
  - **[[Hypopyon]]**: Layered white blood cells in the inferior anterior chamber, seen in severe anterior uveitis (e.g., [[HLA-B27]]-associated, [[Behçet disease]], [[endophthalmitis]]).

- **[[Gonioscopy]]**: Examination of the [[anterior chamber angle]] to assess for peripheral anterior synechiae (PAS), angle neovascularization, or angle deposits.

- **[[Dilated fundus examination]]** with [[indirect ophthalmoscopy]] and [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy with condensing lenses]]: Essential for evaluating intermediate and posterior uveitis, choroidal lesions, and vitreous inflammation.

### Imaging

| Modality | Application in Uveal Disease |
|---|---|
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)** | Detection and monitoring of [[cystoid macular edema]], subretinal fluid, choroidal thickening, epiretinal membrane; enhanced depth imaging (EDI-OCT) for choroidal evaluation |
| **[[Fundus fluorescein angiography]] (FFA)** | Identifies vascular leakage, [[cystoid macular edema]], retinal vasculitis, disc hyperfluorescence, capillary non-perfusion |
| **[[Indocyanine green angiography]] (ICGA)** | Superior for imaging choroidal vasculature; identifies choroidal granulomas, stromal vasculitis (e.g., in [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]], [[sarcoidosis]], [[birdshot chorioretinopathy]]) |
| **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** | Evaluates choroidal masses (melanoma vs. metastasis vs. hemangioma), choroidal detachment, and posterior segment pathology when media opacity precludes fundoscopy |
| **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | High-resolution imaging of the [[iris]], [[ciliary body]], and [[anterior chamber angle]]; useful for ciliary body tumors, cyclitic membranes, and plateau iris |
| **[[Fundus autofluorescence]] (FAF)** | Detects [[lipofuscin]] accumulation (e.g., orange pigment over choroidal nevi/melanoma), RPE changes in inflammatory chorioretinopathies |
| **[[OCT angiography]] (OCTA)** | Non-invasive detection of [[choroidal neovascularization]] and choriocapillaris flow alterations |
| **[[MRI]] of the orbit** | Characterization of uveal masses; uveal melanoma typically shows hyperintensity on T1-weighted and hypointensity on T2-weighted images due to melanin content |

### Laboratory Investigations

Laboratory workup is guided by clinical presentation and suspected etiology. Common investigations for uveitis include:

| Test | Associated Condition |
|---|---|
| **[[HLA-B27]]** | [[Ankylosing spondylitis]], [[reactive arthritis]], [[inflammatory bowel disease]], [[psoriatic arthritis]] |
| **[[ACE]] (angiotensin-converting enzyme), [[lysozyme]], chest X-ray/CT** | [[Sarcoidosis]] |
| **[[QuantiFERON-TB Gold]], [[tuberculin skin test]], chest X-ray** | [[Tuberculosis]] |
| **[[FTA-ABS]], [[RPR]]/[[VDRL]]** | [[Syphilis]] |
| **[[Toxoplasma]] IgG/IgM** | [[Toxoplasmosis]] |
| **[[CBC]], [[ESR]], [[CRP]]** | General inflammatory markers |
| **[[ANA]], [[anti-dsDNA]]** | [[Systemic lupus erythematosus]], [[juvenile idiopathic arthritis]] |
| **[[ANCA]]** | [[Granulomatosis with polyangiitis]] |
| **Aqueous/vitreous [[PCR]]** | Viral etiologies ([[herpes simplex virus]], [[varicella-zoster virus]], [[cytomegalovirus]]), [[toxoplasmosis]], [[tuberculosis]] |
| **Aqueous/vitreous [[cytology]]** | [[Intraocular lymphoma]] (masquerade syndrome) |

### Biopsy

- **[[Fine-needle aspiration biopsy]] (FNAB)**: May be performed for indeterminate intraocular masses, particularly to differentiate uveal melanoma from metastasis or lymphoma.
- **[[Vitreous biopsy]]**: Indicated in suspected [[intraocular lymphoma]] or infectious endophthalmitis unresponsive to empiric therapy.
- **Gene expression profiling (e.g., [[DecisionDx-UM]])**: Used for prognostication in uveal melanoma, classifying tumors as Class 1 (low metastatic risk) or Class 2 (high metastatic risk).

## Management and Treatment

### Pharmacological Treatment of Uveitis

#### Anterior Uveitis

1. **[[Cycloplegic agents]]** (e.g., [[cyclopentolate]] 1%, [[atropine]] 1%):
   - Relieve pain by paralyzing the ciliary muscle and sphincter pupillae (reducing spasm)
   - Prevent and break [[posterior synechiae]]
   - Stabilize the [[blood-aqueous barrier]]

2. **Topical [[corticosteroids]]** (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate]] 1%, [[difluprednate]] 0.05%):
   - Mainstay of treatment for non-infectious anterior uveitis
   - Dosing frequency titrated to severity (e.g., hourly in severe cases, tapered gradually)
   - Complications include [[steroid-induced glaucoma]] and [[posterior subcapsular cataract]]

3. **Periocular corticosteroid injections** (e.g., [[sub-Tenon's triamcinolone acetonide]]):
   - Used for unilateral intermediate or posterior uveitis, or anterior uveitis refractory to topical therapy

4. **Intravitreal corticosteroid injections/implants**:
   - [[Triamcinolone acetonide]] (intravitreal)
   - [[Dexamethasone intravitreal implant]] (Ozurdex®): Sustained-release, biodegradable implant lasting approximately 3–6 months
   - [[Fluocinolone acetonide intravitreal implant]] (Retisert®, Yutiq®): Long-acting implants for chronic non-infectious posterior uveitis

5. **Systemic corticosteroids** (e.g., [[prednisone]] 1 mg/kg/day with taper):
   - Indicated for bilateral or severe posterior/panuveitis
   - Long-term use limited by systemic side effects ([[osteoporosis]], [[diabetes mellitus]], [[adrenal suppression]], [[Cushing syndrome]])

#### Immunomodulatory Therapy (Corticosteroid-Sparing Agents)

Indicated when corticosteroids cannot be tapered below a threshold dose (typically ≤7.5 mg/day prednisone equivalent) or when corticosteroid side effects are unacceptable.

| Drug Class | Examples | Mechanism | Key Considerations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Antimetabolites** | [[Methotrexate]], [[mycophenolate mofetil]], [[azathioprine]] | Inhibit purine/pyrimidine synthesis, suppressing lymphocyte proliferation | Methotrexate is often first-line; requires monitoring of hepatic and renal function, CBC |
| **T-cell inhibitors** | [[Cyclosporine A]], [[tacrolimus]] | Inhibit [[calcineurin]], blocking IL-2 production and T-cell activation | Nephrotoxicity, hypertension; monitor renal function and drug levels |
| **Alkylating agents** | [[Cyclophosphamide]], [[chlorambucil]] | Cross-link DNA, suppressing rapidly dividing cells | Reserved for severe, sight-threatening disease; significant toxicity (bone marrow suppression, infertility, malignancy risk) |
| **Biologic agents** | [[Adalimumab]] (anti-[[TNF-α]]), [[infliximab]], [[tocilizumab]] (anti-[[IL-6R]]), [[rituximab]] (anti-[[CD20]]) | Targeted blockade of specific cytokines or cell surface molecules | Adalimumab is FDA-approved for non-infectious intermediate, posterior, and panuveitis; screen for [[tuberculosis]] and [[hepatitis B]] before initiation |
| **Interferons** | [[Interferon-α 2a]] | Immunomodulatory and antiviral | Used in refractory [[Behçet disease]]-associated uveitis |

#### Treatment of Infectious Uveitis

Infectious etiologies require targeted antimicrobial therapy:

| Etiology | Treatment |
|---|---|
| **[[Toxoplasmosis]]** | [[Pyrimethamine]] + [[sulfadiazine]] + [[folinic acid]] ± systemic corticosteroids; alternative: [[trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole]] |
| **[[Tuberculosis]]** | Standard anti-tubercular therapy (RIPE: [[rifampin]], [[isoniazid]], [[pyrazinamide]], [[ethambutol]]) for 6–9 months |
| **[[Syphilis]]** | IV [[penicillin G]] for 10–14 days (neurosyphilis protocol) |
| **[[Herpes simplex]]/[[Varicella-zoster]] virus** | Oral [[valacyclovir]] or IV [[acyclovir]]; topical/systemic corticosteroids as adjunct |
| **[[Cytomegalovirus]]** | Intravitreal/systemic [[ganciclovir]] or [[foscarnet]] |
| **[[Toxocariasis]]** | Systemic corticosteroids ± [[albendazole]]; avoid laser to active larvae |

### Management of Uveal Neoplasia

#### Uveal Melanoma

Treatment depends on tumor size, location, and patient factors:

| Modality | Indication |
|---|---|
| **Observation** | Small, indeterminate choroidal lesions without high-risk features |
| **[[Plaque brachytherapy]]** (e.g., [[iodine-125]], [[ruthenium-106]]) | Medium-sized tumors (most common treatment); radioactive plaque sutured to sclera overlying the tumor |
| **[[Proton beam radiotherapy]]** | Medium to large tumors, particularly those near the [[optic disc]] or [[macula]] |
| **[[Transpupillary thermotherapy]] (TTT)** | Small posterior tumors; often adjunctive to brachytherapy |
| **[[Enucleation]]** | Large tumors, tumors with extrascleral extension, blind painful eyes, or when other modalities are not feasible |
| **[[Local resection]]** (endoresection or exoresection) | Selected cases, particularly ciliary body or iris melanomas |
| **Systemic surveillance** | Liver imaging (ultrasound, MRI, or CT) every 6–12 months for metastatic screening; liver function tests |

#### Choroidal Metastasis

- Treatment of the primary malignancy
- External beam radiation, plaque brachytherapy, or systemic/intravitreal anti-[[VEGF]] therapy
- [[Photodynamic therapy]] for selected cases

### Surgical Management of Uveal Complications

| Complication | Surgical Approach |
|---|---|
| **[[Cataract]]** (secondary to chronic uveitis or corticosteroid use) | [[Phacoemulsification]] with [[IOL]] implantation; ensure quiescence for ≥3 months preoperatively; aggressive perioperative anti-inflammatory therapy |
| **[[Secondary glaucoma]]** | [[Trabeculectomy]] with [[mitomycin C]], [[glaucoma drainage device]] (e.g., [[Ahmed valve]], [[Baerveldt implant]]), or [[cyclodestructive procedures]] |
| **[[Epiretinal membrane]]** | [[Pars plana vitrectomy]] with membrane peeling |
| **[[Vitreous opacities]]** | [[Pars plana vitrectomy]] (diagnostic and/or therapeutic) |
| **[[Band keratopathy]]** | [[EDTA chelation]] of the [[cornea]] |
| **[[Iris bombé]]** | [[Nd:YAG laser peripheral iridotomy]] or surgical iridectomy |

## Complications of Uveal Disease

Chronic or recurrent uveal inflammation can lead to numerous sight-threatening complications:

- **[[Cystoid macular edema]]** (CME): The most common cause of visual loss in uveitis
- **[[Secondary glaucoma]]**: Due to trabecular meshwork obstruction by inflammatory cells/debris, peripheral anterior synechiae, pupillary block from posterior synechiae, or corticosteroid-induced
- **[[Cataract]]**: Posterior subcapsular cataract from chronic inflammation and/or corticosteroid use
- **[[Band keratopathy]]**: Calcium deposition in Bowman's layer, particularly in chronic childhood uveitis (e.g., [[juvenile idiopathic arthritis]]-associated)
- **[[Posterior synechiae]]** and **[[seclusio pupillae]]**
- **[[Choroidal neovascularization]]**
- **[[Retinal detachment]]** (exudative or tractional)
- **[[Optic disc edema]]** and [[optic atrophy]]
- **[[Phthisis bulbi]]**: End-stage, shrunken, non-functional globe from chronic inflammation and ciliary body destruction with resultant [[hypotony]]

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis of uveal disease depends on the etiology, anatomical location, chronicity, and response to treatment:

- **Acute anterior uveitis** (e.g., [[HLA-B27]]-associated): Generally favorable with prompt treatment; recurrences are common but typically responsive to therapy.
- **Chronic anterior uveitis** (e.g., [[juvenile idiopathic arthritis]]-associated): Higher risk of complications due to insidious onset and prolonged inflammation.
- **Intermediate uveitis**: Variable; [[pars planitis]] in young patients often has a favorable long-term prognosis with appropriate management.
- **Posterior and panuveitis**: Prognosis depends on etiology; conditions such as [[Behçet disease]] and [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]] require aggressive immunosuppression to prevent irreversible visual loss.
- **Uveal melanoma**: Five-year mortality from metastatic disease is approximately 30–50% for tumors with monosomy 3/Class 2 gene expression profile; iris melanoma has a significantly better prognosis.

## Special Considerations in Optometric Practice

Optometrists play a critical role in the detection, initial management, and co-management of uveal disease:

1. **Early recognition**: Identifying signs of anterior uveitis (ciliary flush, anterior chamber cells and flare, keratic precipitates, posterior synechiae) during routine examination.
2. **Screening**: Monitoring patients at risk for uveitis (e.g., children with [[juvenile idiopathic arthritis]], patients with [[ankylosing spondylitis]] or other [[HLA-B27]]-associated conditions).
3. **Choroidal lesion surveillance**: Documenting and monitoring [[choroidal nevi]] for signs of malignant transformation using the TFSOM-UHHD criteria.
4. **Intraocular pressure monitoring**: Detecting [[secondary glaucoma]] from uveitis or corticosteroid therapy.
5. **Referral**: Timely referral to [[ophthalmology]] or [[uveitis specialist]] for posterior uveitis, recurrent or refractory anterior uveitis, suspected uveal neoplasia, or cases requiring systemic immunosuppression.
6. **Co-management**: Monitoring treatment response, managing topical medications, and screening for medication side effects.
