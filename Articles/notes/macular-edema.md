

# Macular Edema

**Macular Edema** is a pathological condition characterized by the abnormal accumulation of extracellular fluid within the [[Macula]], the central region of the [[Retina]] responsible for high-acuity central vision. This fluid accumulation results in macular thickening and disruption of the highly organized retinal architecture, leading to significant visual impairment. Macular edema is not a disease entity in itself but rather a common final pathway and clinical manifestation of numerous ocular and systemic conditions, including [[Diabetic Retinopathy]], [[Retinal Vein Occlusion]], post-surgical inflammation ([[Irvine-Gass Syndrome]]), [[Uveitis]], and others. It remains one of the leading causes of central vision loss worldwide and is a critical area of focus for both [[Optometry]] and [[Ophthalmology]] practitioners.

---

## Epidemiology

The prevalence of macular edema varies significantly depending on the underlying etiology. [[Diabetic Macular Edema]] (DME) is the most common form and represents the leading cause of visual impairment in patients with [[Diabetes Mellitus]]. Population-based studies estimate that DME affects approximately 6.8% of all diabetic patients globally, with prevalence increasing with disease duration and severity of [[Diabetic Retinopathy]]. In the Wisconsin Epidemiologic Study of Diabetic Retinopathy (WESDR), the 10-year incidence of DME was approximately 20.1% in patients with type 1 diabetes and 13.9% in insulin-treated type 2 diabetes.

Macular edema secondary to [[Retinal Vein Occlusion]] (RVO) is the second most common cause. [[Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion]] (BRVO) has an estimated prevalence of 0.6–1.1% in the general population, and macular edema develops in a substantial proportion of these cases. [[Central Retinal Vein Occlusion]] (CRVO) is less common but carries a higher risk of severe macular edema and vision loss.

Pseudophakic [[Cystoid Macular Edema]] (CME), also known as [[Irvine-Gass Syndrome]], occurs following [[Cataract Surgery]]. Subclinical CME, detectable only on [[Optical Coherence Tomography]] (OCT) or [[Fluorescein Angiography]] (FA), may occur in up to 20–30% of uncomplicated cataract surgeries, while clinically significant CME with visual symptoms occurs in approximately 1–2% of cases. The incidence increases substantially with surgical complications such as posterior capsule rupture or vitreous loss.

Macular edema associated with [[Uveitis]] is a major cause of visual morbidity in inflammatory eye disease, occurring in approximately 20–35% of uveitis patients, particularly those with intermediate or posterior [[Uveitis]].

## Anatomy and Structural Considerations

Understanding the anatomy of the [[Macula]] is essential for comprehending the pathogenesis of macular edema. The macula is the central area of the retina, approximately 5.5 mm in diameter, centered on the [[Fovea]]. The fovea itself is a depression approximately 1.5 mm in diameter, containing the highest density of [[Cone Photoreceptors]] and lacking inner retinal layers, which allows for maximal visual acuity.

The unique anatomical features of the macula predispose it to fluid accumulation:

- **High metabolic demand**: The macula has the highest metabolic rate of any tissue in the body, making it particularly susceptible to ischemic and inflammatory insults.
- **Henle's fiber layer**: The obliquely oriented axons of the [[Outer Plexiform Layer]] in the macular region (known as [[Henle's Fiber Layer]]) create potential spaces where fluid can accumulate in a characteristic petaloid (flower-petal) pattern.
- **Absence of retinal vasculature at the [[Foveal Avascular Zone]] (FAZ)**: The central fovea is devoid of retinal capillaries and depends entirely on the [[Choriocapillaris]] for its oxygen and nutrient supply, making it vulnerable to disruptions in choroidal perfusion.
- **[[Müller Cells]]**: These specialized glial cells span the entire thickness of the retina and play a critical role in maintaining retinal fluid homeostasis. Dysfunction of Müller cells contributes significantly to macular edema.

Two primary barriers maintain retinal fluid homeostasis:

| Barrier | Location | Components | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Inner [[Blood-Retinal Barrier]] (iBRB)** | Retinal vasculature | [[Tight Junctions]] between retinal vascular [[Endothelial Cells]], [[Pericytes]], [[Astrocytes]], and Müller cell foot processes | Prevents leakage of plasma constituents from retinal capillaries into the neural retina |
| **Outer [[Blood-Retinal Barrier]] (oBRB)** | [[Retinal Pigment Epithelium]] (RPE) | Tight junctions (zonulae occludentes) between adjacent [[RPE]] cells | Regulates transport between the [[Choriocapillaris]] and the subretinal space; actively pumps fluid from the subretinal space toward the choroid |

Disruption of either or both of these barriers is the fundamental mechanism underlying macular edema.

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of macular edema is multifactorial and involves a complex interplay of vascular, inflammatory, and cellular mechanisms. The final common pathway is the breakdown of the [[Blood-Retinal Barrier]] (BRB), leading to increased vascular permeability and/or impaired fluid resorption, resulting in net fluid accumulation within the retinal tissue.

### Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor (VEGF)-Mediated Mechanisms

[[Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor]] (VEGF) is the most extensively studied mediator of macular edema. VEGF-A, in particular, is a potent inducer of vascular permeability—originally identified as "vascular permeability factor." VEGF increases permeability by:

- **Phosphorylation and disruption of tight junction proteins**: VEGF activates intracellular signaling cascades (via [[VEGF Receptor 2]], VEGFR-2) that lead to phosphorylation of [[Occludin]], [[Claudin-5]], and [[VE-Cadherin]], causing disassembly of intercellular junctions.
- **Induction of fenestrations**: VEGF promotes the formation of fenestrae and transcellular pores in retinal endothelial cells.
- **Vesiculovacuolar organelle (VVO) formation**: VEGF stimulates the formation of VVOs, which facilitate transcellular transport of plasma proteins.
- **Leukocyte adhesion and inflammation**: VEGF upregulates [[Intercellular Adhesion Molecule-1]] (ICAM-1) on endothelial cells, promoting leukostasis and further BRB breakdown.

In [[Diabetic Macular Edema]], chronic hyperglycemia leads to increased VEGF expression through multiple pathways, including activation of the [[Polyol Pathway]], accumulation of [[Advanced Glycation End Products]] (AGEs), activation of [[Protein Kinase C]] (PKC), and increased flux through the [[Hexosamine Pathway]].

### Inflammatory Mechanisms

Inflammation plays a central role in many forms of macular edema, particularly in uveitic CME, post-surgical CME, and increasingly recognized in DME:

- **Prostaglandins**: [[Prostaglandins]], particularly PGE2, are released following tissue injury or inflammation and increase vascular permeability. In [[Irvine-Gass Syndrome]], surgical trauma triggers the [[Arachidonic Acid Cascade]], leading to prostaglandin and leukotriene production.
- **Cytokines and chemokines**: [[Interleukin-6]] (IL-6), [[Interleukin-1β]] (IL-1β), [[Tumor Necrosis Factor-alpha]] (TNF-α), and [[Monocyte Chemoattractant Protein-1]] (MCP-1) are elevated in the vitreous of patients with macular edema and contribute to BRB breakdown.
- **Leukostasis**: Adherent leukocytes in retinal capillaries cause endothelial cell damage, capillary non-perfusion, and increased permeability.

### Hydrostatic and Oncotic Mechanisms

According to [[Starling's Law]] of fluid exchange, fluid movement across capillary walls is governed by the balance of hydrostatic and oncotic pressures. In macular edema:

- **Increased intravascular hydrostatic pressure** (e.g., in [[Retinal Vein Occlusion]] or systemic [[Hypertension]]) drives fluid out of vessels.
- **Decreased intravascular oncotic pressure** (e.g., in [[Nephrotic Syndrome]] or severe hypoalbuminemia) reduces the osmotic force retaining fluid within vessels.
- **Increased tissue oncotic pressure** occurs when leaked plasma proteins (particularly [[Albumin]]) accumulate in the extracellular space, drawing additional fluid into the retina.

### Müller Cell Dysfunction

[[Müller Cells]] are critical regulators of retinal water homeostasis. They express [[Aquaporin-4]] (AQP4) water channels and [[Kir4.1]] potassium channels, which work in concert to transport water from the retinal parenchyma into the blood and vitreous. In pathological states such as diabetes, Müller cells undergo reactive gliosis, with downregulation of Kir4.1 channels and redistribution of AQP4, leading to impaired fluid clearance and intracellular (cytotoxic) edema.

### RPE Dysfunction

Dysfunction of the [[Retinal Pigment Epithelium]] disrupts the outer BRB and impairs the active fluid transport (pump function) from the subretinal space to the choroid. This mechanism is particularly relevant in conditions such as [[Central Serous Chorioretinopathy]], [[Age-Related Macular Degeneration]], and following retinal detachment repair.

### Types of Macular Edema by Fluid Location

| Type | Fluid Location | OCT Appearance | Common Etiologies |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Intracellular (Cytotoxic)** | Within retinal cells (primarily Müller cells) | Diffuse retinal thickening without distinct cystic spaces | Early ischemia, acute phase of vein occlusion |
| **Extracellular (Vasogenic)** | Extracellular spaces, particularly outer plexiform and inner nuclear layers | Cystoid spaces (petalloid pattern), sponge-like thickening | DME, RVO, post-surgical CME, uveitis |
| **Subretinal Fluid** | Between neurosensory retina and RPE | Dome-shaped hyporeflective space beneath the retina | DME (in ~15–30% of cases), CRVO, uveitis |
| **Mixed** | Combination of above | Variable | Advanced or chronic macular edema |

## Etiology and Classification

Macular edema can be classified by its underlying etiology:

### Diabetic Macular Edema (DME)

[[Diabetic Macular Edema]] is the most prevalent form and can occur at any stage of [[Diabetic Retinopathy]]. It is classified by the Early Treatment Diabetic Retinopathy Study (ETDRS) as **Clinically Significant Macular Edema (CSME)** when any of the following criteria are met:

1. Retinal thickening at or within 500 μm of the center of the macula
2. Hard exudates at or within 500 μm of the center of the macula, if associated with adjacent retinal thickening
3. A zone of retinal thickening one disc area or larger, any part of which is within one disc diameter of the center of the macula

Modern classification further distinguishes between **center-involving** and **non-center-involving** DME based on OCT findings, which has significant implications for treatment decisions.

### Macular Edema Secondary to Retinal Vein Occlusion

Both [[Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion]] and [[Central Retinal Vein Occlusion]] frequently cause macular edema through a combination of increased hydrostatic pressure, ischemia-driven VEGF upregulation, and inflammatory mediator release.

### Pseudophakic Cystoid Macular Edema (Irvine-Gass Syndrome)

[[Irvine-Gass Syndrome]] develops weeks to months following [[Cataract Surgery]], typically peaking at 4–6 weeks postoperatively. Risk factors include complicated surgery, posterior capsule rupture, vitreous loss, iris trauma, and use of [[Prostaglandin Analog]] glaucoma medications.

### Uveitic Macular Edema

Macular edema is the most common structural complication and leading cause of visual loss in [[Uveitis]], particularly [[Intermediate Uveitis]], [[Posterior Uveitis]], and [[Panuveitis]].

### Other Etiologies

- [[Retinitis Pigmentosa]]-associated CME
- [[Epiretinal Membrane]]-associated macular edema
- Radiation retinopathy
- Drug-induced macular edema (e.g., [[Prostaglandin Analogs]], [[Niacin]], [[Taxanes]], [[Fingolimod]])
- [[Choroidal Neovascularization]]
- Post-[[Retinal Detachment]] repair

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Symptoms

Patients with macular edema typically present with:

- **Decreased visual acuity**: The hallmark symptom, ranging from mild blurring to severe central vision loss. Visual acuity may fluctuate throughout the day.
- **Metamorphopsia**: Distortion of straight lines, caused by displacement of [[Photoreceptors]] by accumulated fluid. Patients may report that door frames, lines of text, or grid patterns appear wavy or bent.
- **Micropsia or macropsia**: Objects may appear smaller or larger than their actual size due to photoreceptor displacement.
- **Central or paracentral scotoma**: A relative or absolute blind spot in the central visual field.
- **Decreased contrast sensitivity**: Difficulty distinguishing objects from their background, particularly in low-contrast situations.
- **Dyschromatopsia**: Altered color perception, particularly affecting blue-yellow discrimination.
- **Difficulty with reading and fine detail tasks**: Due to the combination of reduced acuity, metamorphopsia, and contrast sensitivity loss.

Notably, mild or early macular edema may be entirely **asymptomatic**, underscoring the importance of routine screening examinations, particularly in high-risk populations such as diabetic patients.

### Clinical Signs on Examination

| Examination Method | Findings |
|---|---|
| **[[Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy]] with [[Fundus Lens]]** | Loss of foveal reflex, retinal thickening, cystoid spaces (may appear as a honeycomb pattern), hard exudates (yellowish, waxy deposits representing lipoprotein extravasation), microaneurysms, intraretinal hemorrhages |
| **[[Direct Ophthalmoscopy]]** | Reduced or absent foveal light reflex, subtle retinal elevation (difficult to appreciate without stereoscopic viewing) |
| **[[Amsler Grid]] Testing** | Metamorphopsia, scotomas in the central 10 degrees of visual field |
| **[[Contact Lens Biomicroscopy]]** | Stereoscopic view revealing retinal thickening, cystoid changes, and subretinal fluid |

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

[[Optical Coherence Tomography]] is the gold standard for the diagnosis, quantification, and monitoring of macular edema. Modern [[Spectral-Domain OCT]] (SD-OCT) and [[Swept-Source OCT]] (SS-OCT) provide high-resolution cross-sectional images of the retina with axial resolution of 5–7 μm.

**Key OCT findings in macular edema include:**

- **Increased central subfield thickness (CST)**: Measured as the average retinal thickness within the central 1 mm ETDRS subfield. Normal CST varies by device but is typically 240–280 μm on SD-OCT. Values exceeding 300–320 μm (device-dependent) are generally considered abnormal.
- **Intraretinal cystoid spaces**: Hyporeflective (dark) round or oval spaces within the retinal layers, most commonly in the [[Inner Nuclear Layer]] (INL) and [[Outer Plexiform Layer]] (OPL)/[[Henle's Fiber Layer]].
- **Diffuse retinal thickening**: Sponge-like thickening without well-defined cystoid spaces.
- **Subretinal fluid (SRF)**: A dome-shaped hyporeflective space between the neurosensory retina and the [[RPE]].
- **Hard exudates**: Hyperreflective foci with posterior shadowing.
- **Disruption of the [[Ellipsoid Zone]] (EZ)**: Previously known as the IS/OS junction; disruption correlates with photoreceptor damage and poorer visual prognosis.
- **Disruption of the [[External Limiting Membrane]] (ELM)**: Another biomarker of photoreceptor integrity and visual prognosis.
- **Disorganization of Retinal Inner Layers (DRIL)**: A biomarker in DME associated with poorer visual outcomes.
- **Hyperreflective foci (HRF)**: Small, discrete hyperreflective dots within the retinal layers, thought to represent activated microglia, lipoprotein extravasation, or migrating RPE cells; associated with inflammation and poorer outcomes.

**OCT morphological classification of DME (adapted from various classification systems):**

| Pattern | Description | Prognostic Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Sponge-like diffuse thickening** | Diffuse low-reflectivity areas with increased retinal thickness | Moderate response to anti-VEGF |
| **Cystoid macular edema** | Well-defined cystoid spaces, predominantly in OPL and INL | Variable; large cysts may indicate chronicity |
| **Serous retinal detachment** | Subretinal fluid accumulation | May respond well to anti-VEGF; consider inflammatory component |
| **Tractional** | Vitreomacular traction or epiretinal membrane contributing to edema | May require [[Vitrectomy]] |
| **Mixed** | Combination of above patterns | Treatment guided by predominant component |

### OCT Angiography (OCTA)

[[OCT Angiography]] is a non-invasive imaging modality that provides depth-resolved visualization of retinal and choroidal vasculature without the need for intravenous dye injection. In macular edema, OCTA can:

- Delineate the [[Foveal Avascular Zone]] (FAZ) and detect FAZ enlargement indicating macular ischemia
- Identify microaneurysms and areas of capillary non-perfusion
- Assess the deep capillary plexus, which is often more severely affected in DME
- Monitor treatment response

However, OCTA has limitations in the presence of significant edema, as cystoid spaces can create segmentation artifacts and signal attenuation.

### Fluorescein Angiography (FA)

[[Fluorescein Angiography]] remains an important diagnostic tool, particularly for:

- **Identifying the source and pattern of leakage**: Focal leakage from microaneurysms versus diffuse leakage from dilated capillaries
- **Detecting macular ischemia**: Enlargement of the FAZ and areas of capillary non-perfusion, which have prognostic significance
- **Classic petaloid pattern**: Late-phase hyperfluorescence in a flower-petal configuration centered on the fovea, characteristic of cystoid macular edema
- **Optic disc leakage**: Late staining of the optic disc, commonly seen in Irvine-Gass syndrome and uveitic CME

**Phases of FA relevant to macular edema:**

| Phase | Timing | Findings in Macular Edema |
|---|---|---|
| **Arteriovenous** | 20–30 seconds | Microaneurysms, capillary dilation |
| **Venous** | 30–60 seconds | Progressive leakage from microaneurysms and capillaries |
| **Late/Recirculation** | 5–10 minutes | Pooling of dye in cystoid spaces (petaloid pattern), diffuse leakage, disc staining |

### Fundus Autofluorescence (FAF)

[[Fundus Autofluorescence]] can reveal increased autofluorescence in areas of cystoid macular edema due to accumulation of fluorophores (such as lipofuscin) in displaced photoreceptor outer segments and RPE cells. It is particularly useful in chronic CME.

### B-Scan Ultrasonography

[[B-Scan Ultrasonography]] may be useful when media opacities (e.g., dense cataract, vitreous hemorrhage) preclude adequate OCT or fundoscopic examination, or to evaluate for vitreomacular traction or posterior vitreous detachment.

## Differential Diagnosis

It is essential to differentiate macular edema from other causes of macular thickening or central vision loss:

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| [[Central Serous Chorioretinopathy]] | Subretinal fluid without intraretinal cystic changes; "smokestack" or "inkblot" leakage on FA; typically young males |
| [[Age-Related Macular Degeneration]] (wet) | Subretinal or sub-RPE fluid associated with [[Choroidal Neovascularization]]; drusen; pigmentary changes |
| [[Epiretinal Membrane]] | Retinal thickening and distortion due to tractional membrane; may coexist with CME |
| [[Vitreomacular Traction]] | Incomplete posterior vitreous detachment with traction on the fovea; may cause secondary macular edema |
| [[Macular Hole]] | Full-thickness defect in the fovea; may have surrounding cuff of subretinal fluid |
| [[Nicotinic Acid Maculopathy]] | Cystoid changes without leakage on FA; history of niacin use |

## Management and Treatment

The management of macular edema is guided by the underlying etiology, severity, chronicity, and the presence of center involvement. A multimodal approach is often required.

### Observation and Systemic Optimization

- **Glycemic control**: In DME, intensive glycemic control (HbA1c < 7%) has been shown to reduce the risk of developing macular edema and slow its progression (DCCT/EDIC, UKPDS studies).
- **Blood pressure control**: Systemic [[Hypertension]] exacerbates macular edema; target blood pressure < 130/80 mmHg.
- **Lipid management**: [[Dyslipidemia]] is associated with increased hard exudate deposition; [[Fenofibrate]] has demonstrated benefit in reducing DME progression (FIELD and ACCORD Eye studies).
- **Discontinuation of offending medications**: If drug-induced macular edema is suspected (e.g., [[Prostaglandin Analogs]], [[Fingolimod]]).

### Pharmacological Treatment

#### Anti-VEGF Therapy

[[Anti-VEGF]] intravitreal injections are the first-line treatment for center-involving DME and macular edema secondary to RVO. Available agents include:

| Agent | Mechanism | Dosing | Key Trials |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Ranibizumab]]** (Lucentis®) | Monoclonal antibody fragment binding all VEGF-A isoforms | 0.3 mg or 0.5 mg intravitreal injection | RISE/RIDE, RESTORE, BRAVO, CRUISE |
| **[[Aflibercept]]** (Eylea®) | Fusion protein (VEGF Trap) binding VEGF-A, VEGF-B, and [[Placental Growth Factor]] (PlGF) | 2 mg intravitreal injection | VISTA/VIVID, VIBRANT, COPERNICUS, GALILEO |
| **[[Bevacizumab]]** (Avastin®) | Full-length monoclonal antibody binding all VEGF-A isoforms (off-label) | 1.25 mg intravitreal injection | DRCR.net Protocol T, CATT (for AMD) |
| **[[Brolucizumab]]** (Beovu®) | Single-chain antibody fragment binding VEGF-A | 6 mg intravitreal injection | KESTREL/KITE (DME) |
| **[[Faricimab]]** (Vabysmo®) | Bispecific antibody targeting both VEGF-A and [[Angiopoietin-2]] (Ang-2) | 6 mg intravitreal injection | YOSEMITE/RHINE (DME), BALATON/COMINO (RVO) |

**DRCR.net Protocol T** (a landmark comparative effectiveness trial) demonstrated that for eyes with DME and baseline visual acuity of 20/50 or worse, [[Aflibercept]] provided superior visual acuity gains at one year compared to [[Bevacizumab]] and [[Ranibizumab]]. At two years, aflibercept and ranibizumab were comparable, and both were superior to bevacizumab in this subgroup. For eyes with better baseline acuity (20/32–20/40), all three agents performed similarly.

**Treatment regimens** include:

- **Monthly (PRN - pro re nata)**: Injections given monthly until edema resolves, then as needed based on recurrence
- **Treat-and-extend (TAE)**: After initial loading doses, injection intervals are progressively extended if the macula remains dry, and shortened if edema recurs
- **Fixed dosing**: Regular injections at predetermined intervals (e.g., every 4 or 8 weeks)

#### Intravitreal Corticosteroids

Corticosteroids address the inflammatory component of macular edema and are particularly useful in pseudophakic eyes, uveitic CME, and DME refractory to anti-VEGF therapy.

| Agent | Formulation | Duration of Action | Key Considerations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Dexamethasone Intravitreal Implant]]** (Ozurdex®) | Biodegradable sustained-release implant | ~4–6 months | FDA-approved for DME, RVO-associated ME, and uveitic ME; risk of [[Intraocular Pressure]] elevation and [[Cataract]] progression |
| **[[Fluocinolone Acetonide Intravitreal Implant]]** (Iluvien®) | Non-biodegradable sustained-release implant | Up to 36 months | FDA-approved for DME in patients previously treated with corticosteroids without clinically significant IOP rise; FAME trial |
| **[[Fluocinolone Acetonide Intravitreal Implant]]** (Retisert®) | Surgically implanted non-biodegradable device | Up to 30 months | Primarily for chronic non-infectious posterior uveitis; significant risk of IOP elevation and cataract |
| **[[Triamcinolone Acetonide]]** (intravitreal) | Suspension injection | ~3 months | Off-label; used as adjunct or in refractory cases; significant IOP and cataract risk |

**Adverse effects of intravitreal corticosteroids:**
- Elevated [[Intraocular Pressure]] (IOP): Occurs in approximately 30–40% of patients; may require [[IOP-Lowering Medications]] or, rarely, [[Glaucoma Surgery]]
- [[Cataract]] progression: Occurs in the majority of phakic eyes over time; nearly universal with long-acting implants
- [[Endophthalmitis]]: Rare but serious risk with any intravitreal injection (~0.02–0.05% per injection)

#### Topical and Periocular Therapies

- **Topical [[Nonsteroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs]] (NSAIDs)**: Agents such as [[Ketorolac]], [[Nepafenac]], [[Bromfenac]], and [[Diclofenac]] are widely used for prophylaxis and treatment of pseudophakic CME. They inhibit [[Cyclooxygenase]] (COX) enzymes, reducing prostaglandin synthesis.
- **Topical corticosteroids**: [[Prednisolone Acetate]], [[Difluprednate]], and others are used in conjunction with NSAIDs for post-surgical and uveitic CME.
- **Periocular (sub-Tenon's) corticosteroid injection**: [[Triamcinolone Acetonide]] (40 mg) injected into the sub-Tenon's space can be effective for uveitic CME and as an adjunct in other forms of macular edema, with a lower risk of IOP elevation compared to intravitreal injection.

### Laser Photocoagulation

#### Focal/Grid Laser

[[Focal Laser Photocoagulation]] was the standard of care for DME prior to the anti-VEGF era, based on the ETDRS, which demonstrated that focal/grid laser reduced the risk of moderate visual loss by approximately 50%. Current indications include:

- **Non-center-involving DME** with focal leakage from identifiable microaneurysms
- **Persistent DME** despite anti-VEGF therapy (as adjunctive treatment)
- **Settings with limited access** to anti-VEGF agents

The mechanism involves direct treatment of leaking microaneurysms (focal laser) and/or application of a grid pattern to areas of diffuse leakage and capillary non-perfusion, which is thought to reduce oxygen consumption by the outer retina and improve oxygenation from the choroid.

#### Subthreshold Micropulse Laser

[[Subthreshold Micropulse Laser]] (SML) therapy delivers laser energy in short, repetitive pulses, allowing the RPE to cool between pulses and avoiding thermal damage to the neurosensory retina. This technique stimulates RPE function and cytokine modulation without creating visible burns. It is increasingly used for:

- Center-involving DME (as an alternative or adjunct to anti-VEGF)
- Chronic or recurrent macular edema
- Patients who prefer fewer intravitreal injections

### Surgical Management

#### Pars Plana Vitrectomy (PPV)

[[Pars Plana Vitrectomy]] is indicated for macular edema associated with:

- **Vitreomacular traction (VMT)**: Documented on OCT as anteroposterior traction on the macula by an adherent posterior hyaloid
- **Epiretinal membrane (ERM)**: Tangential traction contributing to macular thickening
- **Refractory macular edema**: In select cases unresponsive to pharmacological therapy, vitrectomy with [[Internal Limiting Membrane]] (ILM) peeling may be beneficial

The proposed mechanisms of benefit include removal of tractional forces, removal of VEGF and inflammatory mediators from the vitreous cavity, and improved oxygenation of the inner retina.

## Prognosis and Visual Outcomes

The prognosis of macular edema depends on multiple factors:

| Factor | Impact on Prognosis |
|---|---|
| **Duration of edema** | Chronic edema (>12 months) is associated with irreversible photoreceptor damage and poorer visual recovery |
| **Baseline visual acuity** | Eyes with better baseline VA tend to maintain better final VA, though eyes with worse baseline VA show greater absolute improvement |
| **Integrity of EZ and ELM on OCT** | Intact ellipsoid zone and external limiting membrane predict better visual outcomes |
| **Macular ischemia** | Enlargement of the FAZ and capillary non-perfusion are associated with poorer visual prognosis |
| **Presence of DRIL** | Disorganization of retinal inner layers correlates with worse visual acuity |
| **Subretinal fluid** | In DME, presence of SRF may be associated with better anti-VEGF response compared to intraretinal fluid alone |
| **Hard exudate deposition** | Subfoveal hard exudates may cause permanent photoreceptor damage even after edema resolution |
| **Underlying etiology** | Pseudophakic CME generally has a favorable prognosis; chronic uveitic CME and ischemic RVO-associated ME carry poorer prognoses |

## Monitoring and Follow-Up

Regular monitoring is essential for optimal management:

- **OCT**: The primary tool for monitoring treatment response; performed at each visit to assess central subfield thickness, cystoid changes, and subretinal fluid
- **Visual acuity testing**: [[Best-Corrected Visual Acuity]] (BCVA) using standardized charts (e.g., [[ETDRS Chart]])
- **Fundus examination**: Dilated [[Fundoscopy]] to assess for hard exudates, hemorrhages, and other retinal pathology
- **FA and OCTA**: Performed periodically to assess perfusion status and guide treatment decisions
- **IOP monitoring**: Essential in patients receiving intravitreal corticosteroids

**Typical follow-up schedule for anti-VEGF treatment of DME:**
- Monthly visits during the loading phase (typically 3–6 monthly injections)
- Individualized intervals during the maintenance phase based on treat-and-extend or PRN protocols
- Consideration of treatment discontinuation after sustained resolution (e.g., 3–6 months of stability without treatment)

## Prevention

Preventive strategies are particularly relevant for DME:

- **Strict glycemic control**: HbA1c < 7% (DCCT/EDIC)
- **Blood pressure management**: Target < 130/80 mmHg
- **Lipid control**: Consider [[Fenofibrate]] in diabetic patients
- **Regular screening**: Annual dilated eye examinations for all diabetic patients; more frequent examinations for those with existing retinopathy
- **Prophylactic topical NSAIDs**: Perioperative use of topical NSAIDs (e.g., [[Nepafenac]], [[Ketorolac]]) reduces the incidence of pseudophakic CME, particularly in high-risk patients (diabetics, those with prior CME, complicated surgery)

## Emerging Therapies and Future Directions

The field of macular edema treatment is rapidly evolving:

- **Port Delivery System (PDS)**: A surgically implanted refillable reservoir (Susvimo®) that continuously delivers [[Ranibizumab]] into the vitreous, potentially reducing treatment burden to refills every 6 months (Archway trial).
- **Gene therapy**: Investigational approaches using [[Adeno-Associated Virus]] (AAV) vectors to deliver genes encoding anti-VEGF proteins (e.g., RGX-314, ADVM-022), potentially providing sustained VEGF suppression with a single treatment.
- **Bispecific and multi-target antibodies**: [[Faricimab]] (targeting VEGF-A and Ang-2) is already approved; additional multi-target agents are in development.
- **Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs)**: Intravitreal sustained-release formulations targeting multiple pathways downstream of VEGF and other growth factor receptors.
- **High-dose aflibercept** (Eylea HD®, 8 mg): Approved for DME and other retinal conditions, offering extended durability with dosing intervals up to 12–16 weeks (PHOTON trial).
- **Sustained-release anti-VEGF implants**: Various biodegradable and non-biodegradable platforms under investigation.
- **Complement inhibitors and neuroprotective agents**: Targeting additional pathways involved in retinal inflammation and degeneration.

---

## Role of the Optometrist

[[Optometrists]] play a critical role in the management of macular edema through:

1. **Screening and early detection**: Routine dilated fundus examinations and OCT screening in at-risk populations
2. **Diagnosis and classification**: Utilizing OCT, fundus photography, and clinical examination to identify and characterize macular edema
3. **Referral**: Timely referral to [[Retina Specialists]] for treatment when indicated
4. **Co-management**: Monitoring treatment response, managing systemic risk factors, and coordinating care with ophthalmologists and primary care providers
5. **Patient education**: Counseling patients on the importance of adherence to treatment, systemic disease management, and regular follow-up
6. **Low vision rehabilitation**: Providing [[Low Vision]] services for patients with permanent visual impairment from chronic macular edema
