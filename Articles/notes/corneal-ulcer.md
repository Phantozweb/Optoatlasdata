

# Corneal Ulcer

---

A **corneal ulcer** (also termed **ulcerative keratitis**) is a sight-threatening, open defect of the [[corneal epithelium]] with underlying [[corneal stroma]]l infiltration and necrosis, most commonly resulting from infectious or inflammatory processes. It represents one of the most urgent conditions encountered in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] practice, demanding rapid diagnosis and aggressive management to preserve visual acuity and structural integrity of the [[globe]]. Corneal ulceration is a leading cause of [[corneal blindness]] worldwide, particularly in developing nations, and is responsible for an estimated 1.5–2 million new cases of monocular blindness annually.

Unlike a superficial [[corneal abrasion]] or [[punctate epithelial erosion]], a true corneal ulcer implies loss of epithelial tissue accompanied by stromal inflammation, excavation, and, in many cases, an identifiable infectious organism. The condition may progress to [[corneal perforation]], [[endophthalmitis]], and permanent vision loss if not treated promptly and appropriately.

---

## Epidemiology

The incidence of corneal ulceration varies significantly by geography, socioeconomic status, and the prevalence of risk factors. In the United States, the annual incidence is estimated at approximately 71,000 cases, with [[contact lens]] wear being the predominant risk factor in industrialized nations. In tropical and agricultural regions of South and Southeast Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, and parts of Central and South America, ocular trauma with vegetative matter is the leading predisposing factor, and fungal etiologies are proportionally more common.

| **Demographic Factor** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| Global incidence | 1.5–2 million cases/year (WHO estimates) |
| U.S. incidence | ~71,000 cases/year |
| Peak age groups | Young adults (contact lens users); elderly (ocular surface disease) |
| Gender predilection | Slight male predominance in agricultural settings; equal in contact lens–related cases |
| Leading risk factor (developed nations) | [[Contact lens]] wear (especially extended/overnight wear) |
| Leading risk factor (developing nations) | Ocular trauma (agricultural, vegetative matter) |

## Anatomy and Relevant Structures

Understanding the anatomy of the [[cornea]] is essential for appreciating the pathophysiology of corneal ulceration. The cornea is an avascular, transparent tissue approximately 540 µm thick centrally and 700 µm peripherally, composed of five distinct layers:

1. **[[Corneal epithelium]]** – A stratified, non-keratinized squamous epithelium (5–7 cell layers thick) that serves as the primary barrier against microbial invasion. Tight junctions between superficial epithelial cells and the [[tear film]] provide the first line of defense.
2. **[[Bowman's layer]]** (Bowman's membrane) – An acellular condensation of the anterior stroma composed of randomly arranged [[collagen]] fibrils. Once disrupted, it does not regenerate, and its loss contributes to scarring.
3. **[[Corneal stroma]]** – Comprising approximately 90% of corneal thickness, the stroma consists of regularly arranged [[type I collagen]] lamellae, [[keratocytes]], and a glycosaminoglycan-rich extracellular matrix. Its organized architecture is responsible for corneal transparency.
4. **[[Descemet's membrane]]** – A thick basement membrane produced by the [[corneal endothelium]]. It is remarkably resistant to enzymatic degradation and infection, and its integrity is a critical determinant of whether a deep ulcer will perforate.
5. **[[Corneal endothelium]]** – A monolayer of hexagonal cells responsible for maintaining corneal deturgescence through active ion transport. Endothelial damage from deep ulceration or associated [[anterior uveitis]] can lead to [[corneal edema]].

The [[limbus]], the transitional zone between the cornea and [[sclera]], houses [[limbal stem cells]] critical for epithelial regeneration and also contains the [[limbal vasculature]] from which inflammatory cells are recruited during the ulcerative process.

## Etiology and Risk Factors

Corneal ulcers may be broadly classified by etiology into **infectious** and **non-infectious (sterile)** categories.

### Infectious Etiologies

| **Category** | **Common Organisms** | **Key Associations** |
|---|---|---|
| **Bacterial** | *[[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]]*, *[[Staphylococcus aureus]]*, *[[Streptococcus pneumoniae]]*, *[[Serratia marcescens]]*, *[[Moraxella]]* spp. | Contact lens wear, trauma, ocular surface disease |
| **Fungal** | *[[Fusarium]]* spp., *[[Aspergillus]]* spp. (filamentous); *[[Candida]]* spp. (yeast) | Vegetative trauma, topical corticosteroid use, tropical climates |
| **Viral** | [[Herpes simplex virus]] (HSV-1, HSV-2), [[Varicella-zoster virus]] (VZV) | Prior herpetic disease, immunosuppression |
| **Protozoal** | *[[Acanthamoeba]]* spp. | Contact lens wear with contaminated water exposure, poor lens hygiene |
| **Parasitic** | *[[Onchocerca volvulus]]*, *[[Microsporidia]]* | Endemic regions (onchocerciasis); immunocompromised patients |

### Non-Infectious (Sterile) Etiologies

- **[[Neurotrophic keratitis]]** – Impaired [[corneal innervation]] (e.g., post-[[herpes zoster ophthalmicus]], post-surgical [[trigeminal nerve]] damage) leading to epithelial breakdown and sterile ulceration.
- **[[Exposure keratopathy]]** – Incomplete lid closure ([[lagophthalmos]]) from [[facial nerve palsy]], [[thyroid eye disease]], or post-surgical causes.
- **[[Autoimmune conditions]]** – [[Rheumatoid arthritis]], [[Wegener's granulomatosis]] ([[granulomatosis with polyangiitis]]), [[systemic lupus erythematosus]], and [[Mooren's ulcer]] (an idiopathic peripheral ulcerative keratitis).
- **[[Vitamin A deficiency]]** ([[xerophthalmia]]) – A major cause of corneal ulceration and blindness in malnourished children in developing countries.
- **Chemical and thermal burns** – Alkali burns are particularly devastating due to deep penetration.
- **Persistent [[epithelial defect]]** – From any cause, including [[dry eye disease]], [[recurrent corneal erosion syndrome]], or post-surgical states.

### Risk Factors

| **Risk Factor** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|
| [[Contact lens]] wear (especially overnight/extended wear) | Epithelial hypoxia, mechanical microtrauma, biofilm formation, altered tear film dynamics |
| Ocular surface disease ([[dry eye]], [[blepharitis]], [[meibomian gland dysfunction]]) | Compromised tear film defense, epithelial desiccation |
| Prior ocular surgery ([[LASIK]], [[PRK]], [[penetrating keratoplasty]]) | Disrupted epithelial barrier, reduced corneal sensation |
| Topical [[corticosteroid]] use | Immunosuppression, facilitation of fungal and herpetic infection |
| [[Diabetes mellitus]] | Impaired wound healing, neurotrophic changes, immune dysfunction |
| [[Immunosuppression]] (HIV/AIDS, chemotherapy, organ transplant) | Reduced host immune response |
| Trauma (especially with organic/vegetative matter) | Direct inoculation of organisms, epithelial disruption |
| [[Trichiasis]], [[entropion]], [[ectropion]] | Chronic mechanical epithelial damage or exposure |

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of corneal ulceration involves a complex interplay between microbial virulence factors, host immune responses, and structural degradation of corneal tissue.

### Stage 1: Epithelial Breach

The intact [[corneal epithelium]], supported by the [[tear film]] (containing [[lysozyme]], [[lactoferrin]], [[secretory IgA]], and [[defensins]]), constitutes a formidable barrier. Ulceration begins when this barrier is compromised—by trauma, contact lens–induced hypoxia, desiccation, or pre-existing epithelial disease—allowing microbial adherence and invasion.

### Stage 2: Microbial Adherence and Colonization

Organisms adhere to exposed [[basement membrane]] components and damaged epithelial cells via specific adhesins. *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, for example, binds to asialo-GM1 glycolipid receptors exposed on injured or hypoxic epithelial cells. Fungal hyphae penetrate between stromal lamellae, and *Acanthamoeba* trophozoites bind to [[mannose]]-containing glycoproteins on the corneal surface.

### Stage 3: Stromal Invasion and Inflammatory Response

Once organisms penetrate the epithelium, they invade the [[corneal stroma]], triggering a robust innate immune response. [[Keratocytes]] undergo [[apoptosis]] and release [[cytokines]] and [[chemokines]] (including [[interleukin-1]] (IL-1), [[interleukin-6]] (IL-6), [[interleukin-8]] (IL-8), and [[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] (TNF-α)), which recruit [[neutrophils]] and [[macrophages]] from the [[limbal vasculature]].

The resulting **stromal infiltrate**—a dense accumulation of inflammatory cells, necrotic debris, and organisms—is the hallmark clinical finding. Neutrophils release [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs), particularly [[MMP-2]], [[MMP-8]], and [[MMP-9]], as well as [[reactive oxygen species]] (ROS) and [[serine proteases]] (e.g., [[elastase]], [[cathepsin G]]), which degrade stromal [[collagen]] and [[proteoglycans]]. This enzymatic degradation, combined with microbial proteases (e.g., *Pseudomonas* elastase and alkaline protease), leads to progressive stromal thinning and tissue necrosis—the ulcer crater.

### Stage 4: Anterior Chamber Reaction

Inflammatory mediators diffuse posteriorly through [[Descemet's membrane]] into the [[anterior chamber]], producing a secondary [[anterior uveitis]] with [[aqueous flare]], [[cells]], and, in severe cases, a [[hypopyon]] (a layered collection of inflammatory cells in the inferior anterior chamber). The hypopyon in infectious keratitis is typically sterile (reactive) but may contain organisms in cases of frank perforation or endophthalmitis.

### Stage 5: Resolution or Progression

With appropriate treatment, the inflammatory response is controlled, organisms are eradicated, and re-epithelialization occurs. Stromal defects are repaired by fibroblastic transformation of keratocytes, which deposit disorganized collagen, resulting in a [[corneal scar]] ([[corneal opacity]], [[leukoma]]). Without treatment, the ulcer may deepen through Descemet's membrane, producing a [[descemetocele]] (a bulging, transparent membrane at the ulcer base), and ultimately [[corneal perforation]] with risk of [[iris prolapse]], [[endophthalmitis]], and loss of the eye.

### Pathophysiology by Organism Type

| **Organism** | **Distinctive Pathophysiological Features** |
|---|---|
| *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* | Rapid liquefactive necrosis; exotoxin A inhibits protein synthesis; elastase and alkaline protease cause extensive stromal lysis; biofilm formation on contact lenses |
| *Staphylococcus aureus* | Alpha-toxin causes epithelial cell lysis; protein A inhibits opsonization; may produce chronic, indolent ulcers |
| *Streptococcus pneumoniae* | Pneumolysin causes direct cytotoxicity; serpiginous (creeping) ulcer pattern; associated with dacryocystitis |
| Filamentous fungi (*Fusarium*, *Aspergillus*) | Hyphae penetrate deep into stroma along lamellar planes; satellite lesions; immune ring (Wessely ring); slow, indolent course |
| *Candida* spp. | Typically in compromised corneas (prior disease, steroid use); dense white infiltrate; less deep penetration than filamentous fungi |
| [[Herpes simplex virus]] | Dendritic or geographic epithelial ulceration (active viral replication); stromal necrotizing keratitis (immune-mediated); corneal hypoesthesia |
| *Acanthamoeba* | Perineural infiltration causing severe [[radial keratoneuritis]]; ring infiltrate; extremely painful; resistant cyst form |

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

Patients with corneal ulcers typically present with an acute or subacute onset of:

- **Pain** – Often severe; disproportionate pain relative to clinical findings is characteristic of *Acanthamoeba* keratitis. Pain may be reduced or absent in [[neurotrophic keratitis]].
- **[[Photophobia]]** – Due to associated [[anterior uveitis]] and [[ciliary spasm]].
- **Tearing** ([[epiphora]]) – Reflex lacrimation from corneal nerve stimulation.
- **[[Blepharospasm]]** – Involuntary eyelid closure secondary to pain and photophobia.
- **Decreased visual acuity** – Proportional to the size, depth, and location of the ulcer (central ulcers cause greater visual impairment).
- **Foreign body sensation** – From epithelial disruption.
- **Mucopurulent or purulent discharge** – Especially in bacterial ulcers.
- **Red eye** ([[conjunctival injection]], [[ciliary flush]]) – Due to inflammation.

### Signs

| **Clinical Sign** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Epithelial defect** | Stains with [[fluorescein]] (viewed under [[cobalt blue light]] or [[slit lamp]] with blue filter); defines the ulcer margins |
| **Stromal infiltrate** | White-yellow, opaque area of inflammatory cell accumulation within the stroma; may be focal or diffuse |
| **Stromal thinning/excavation** | Loss of stromal tissue creating a crater; assessed by [[slit lamp]] optical section |
| **[[Corneal edema]]** | Surrounding the ulcer; indicates endothelial compromise or stromal inflammation |
| **[[Descemet's folds]]** | Stress lines in Descemet's membrane from stromal edema and inflammation |
| **[[Anterior chamber reaction]]** | Cells and flare in the anterior chamber; graded 0 to 4+ |
| **[[Hypopyon]]** | Layered white collection in the inferior anterior chamber; indicates severe inflammation |
| **[[Descemetocele]]** | Bulging, clear area at the ulcer base where only Descemet's membrane remains; indicates imminent perforation |
| **[[Corneal neovascularization]]** | Ingrowth of blood vessels from the limbus; more common in chronic or herpetic ulcers |
| **Satellite lesions** | Small infiltrates surrounding the main lesion; highly suggestive of fungal etiology |
| **Ring infiltrate** | Annular stromal infiltrate; seen in *Acanthamoeba* keratitis and some immune-mediated processes |
| **[[Radial keratoneuritis]]** | Linear, radial perineural infiltrates; pathognomonic for *Acanthamoeba* keratitis |
| **Feathery margins** | Irregular, filamentous borders of the infiltrate; characteristic of fungal keratitis |
| **Dendritic/geographic pattern** | Branching epithelial ulcer with terminal bulbs (dendritic) or larger amoeboid pattern (geographic); pathognomonic for [[herpes simplex keratitis]] |

### Clinical Features by Etiology

| **Etiology** | **Typical Presentation** |
|---|---|
| **Bacterial (Pseudomonas)** | Rapid onset; large, central, suppurative ulcer; mucopurulent discharge; stromal melting; hypopyon common; contact lens history |
| **Bacterial (Gram-positive cocci)** | Well-defined, round/oval, gray-white infiltrate; may be more indolent than Gram-negative; associated with ocular surface disease |
| **Fungal (filamentous)** | Insidious onset; dry, raised, feathery-edged infiltrate; satellite lesions; immune ring; history of vegetative trauma; worsening on steroids |
| **Fungal (yeast)** | Dense, white, well-circumscribed infiltrate; compromised cornea; immunosuppressed patient |
| **Herpes simplex** | Dendritic ulcer with terminal bulbs staining with fluorescein and [[rose bengal]]; reduced corneal sensation; recurrent episodes; may progress to geographic ulcer or stromal keratitis |
| **Acanthamoeba** | Severe pain out of proportion to findings; early: pseudodendrites, epithelial irregularity; late: ring infiltrate, radial keratoneuritis; contact lens wear with water exposure |
| **Sterile/autoimmune** | Peripheral location (often near limbus); associated with systemic autoimmune disease; may show progressive peripheral corneal thinning ([[peripheral ulcerative keratitis]]) |

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

A thorough [[slit lamp biomicroscopy]] examination is the cornerstone of diagnosis. The clinician should systematically evaluate:

1. **Lids and adnexa** – Assess for [[blepharitis]], [[meibomian gland dysfunction]], [[trichiasis]], [[entropion]], [[ectropion]], [[lagophthalmos]], and [[dacryocystitis]].
2. **Conjunctiva** – Note the pattern and severity of injection (diffuse vs. ciliary flush), discharge character, and presence of [[follicles]] or [[papillae]].
3. **Cornea** – Document the ulcer's location (central, paracentral, peripheral), size (measured in millimeters), shape, depth (estimated by optical section), and character of the infiltrate (color, density, margins, satellite lesions). Assess for [[corneal thinning]], [[descemetocele]], and [[perforation]].
4. **[[Corneal sensation]]** – Tested with a cotton wisp or [[Cochet-Bonnet esthesiometer]]; reduced sensation suggests herpetic or *Acanthamoeba* etiology, or [[neurotrophic keratitis]].
5. **Anterior chamber** – Grade cells and flare; note presence and height of [[hypopyon]].
6. **[[Fluorescein staining]]** – Delineates the epithelial defect. [[Rose bengal]] or [[lissamine green]] staining may highlight devitalized epithelial cells at the ulcer margins and is particularly useful in herpetic disease.
7. **[[Seidel test]]** – Application of concentrated fluorescein over a suspected perforation site; a positive test (streaming of aqueous diluting the fluorescein) confirms perforation.

### Microbiological Investigation

Corneal cultures remain the gold standard for identifying the causative organism and guiding targeted therapy. Indications for corneal scraping and culture include:

- Ulcer >2 mm in diameter
- Deep stromal involvement
- Central or visual axis location
- Chronic or non-responsive ulcer
- Suspected fungal, *Acanthamoeba*, or atypical infection
- History of prior topical antibiotic or corticosteroid use
- Presence of hypopyon or descemetocele

#### Scraping Technique

Using a sterile [[Kimura spatula]], #15 Bard-Parker blade, or sterile calcium alginate swab, the base and leading edges of the ulcer are scraped under slit lamp magnification after instillation of preservative-free topical anesthetic (e.g., [[proparacaine]] 0.5%). Material is inoculated directly onto culture media and glass slides.

#### Culture Media

| **Medium** | **Purpose** |
|---|---|
| [[Blood agar]] (5% sheep blood) | General bacterial culture; supports most aerobic bacteria |
| [[Chocolate agar]] | Fastidious organisms (e.g., *[[Haemophilus]]*, *[[Neisseria]]*) |
| [[Sabouraud dextrose agar]] | Fungal culture (incubated at 25–30°C for up to 4 weeks) |
| [[Brain-heart infusion broth]] | Enrichment medium for bacteria and fungi |
| [[Thioglycolate broth]] | Anaerobic and microaerophilic organisms |
| [[Non-nutrient agar with E. coli overlay]] | *Acanthamoeba* culture |
| [[Lowenstein-Jensen medium]] | *[[Mycobacterium]]* spp. (atypical mycobacteria) |

#### Microscopy and Staining

| **Stain** | **Utility** |
|---|---|
| [[Gram stain]] | Rapid identification of bacteria (Gram-positive vs. Gram-negative; cocci vs. rods) |
| [[Giemsa stain]] | Fungi, *Acanthamoeba* cysts and trophozoites, intracellular organisms, inflammatory cells |
| [[KOH wet mount]] (10% potassium hydroxide) | Rapid identification of fungal hyphae |
| [[Calcofluor white]] (with fluorescence microscopy) | Fungal cell walls and *Acanthamoeba* cysts (chitin and cellulose binding) |
| [[Acridine orange]] | Bacteria and fungi (fluorescence) |
| [[Ziehl-Neelsen]] / [[Kinyoun stain]] | Acid-fast organisms (*Mycobacterium*, *Nocardia*) |
| [[Lactophenol cotton blue]] | Fungal morphology identification |

### Advanced Diagnostic Modalities

- **[[Confocal microscopy]]** (e.g., Heidelberg Retina Tomograph with Rostock Corneal Module) – Provides in vivo, high-resolution imaging of the cornea at the cellular level. Particularly valuable for diagnosing *Acanthamoeba* keratitis (double-walled cysts, 15–25 µm) and fungal keratitis (hyphae) without the need for invasive scraping. Sensitivity for *Acanthamoeba* ranges from 80–100% in experienced hands.
- **[[Polymerase chain reaction]] (PCR)** – Highly sensitive and specific molecular technique for detecting microbial DNA. Useful for HSV, VZV, *Acanthamoeba*, fungi, and atypical bacteria. Results available within hours. Limitations include inability to determine organism viability and potential for contamination.
- **[[Anterior segment optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT)** – Non-contact imaging that provides cross-sectional visualization of the cornea, allowing precise measurement of ulcer depth, stromal thinning, and detection of descemetocele. Useful for monitoring treatment response.
- **[[Corneal biopsy]]** – Reserved for culture-negative, non-responsive ulcers. A partial-thickness or full-thickness corneal specimen is obtained (typically 2–3 mm trephine) and submitted for histopathology, special stains, and culture. May be combined with therapeutic [[penetrating keratoplasty]].
- **[[Next-generation sequencing]] (metagenomic deep sequencing)** – Emerging technology capable of identifying all microbial DNA/RNA in a sample without prior knowledge of the pathogen. Promising for culture-negative cases.

## Differential Diagnosis

It is critical to distinguish a true corneal ulcer from other conditions that may mimic its appearance:

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| [[Corneal abrasion]] | Epithelial defect without stromal infiltrate; no anterior chamber reaction |
| [[Sterile corneal infiltrate]] (contact lens–related) | Small (<1.5 mm), peripheral, minimal/no epithelial defect, minimal pain, no anterior chamber reaction; often multiple |
| [[Marginal keratitis]] (staphylococcal hypersensitivity) | Peripheral, crescent-shaped infiltrates with clear interval between infiltrate and limbus; associated with chronic blepharitis |
| [[Phlyctenular keratoconjunctivitis]] | Nodular lesion at limbus; hypersensitivity reaction to microbial antigens |
| [[Corneal foreign body]] with rust ring | History of high-speed particle; visible foreign body or rust ring |
| [[Salzmann's nodular degeneration]] | Elevated, bluish-white nodules; no epithelial defect; chronic |
| [[Terrien's marginal degeneration]] | Peripheral corneal thinning with intact epithelium; lipid deposition; non-inflammatory |
| [[Corneal neoplasia]] ([[ocular surface squamous neoplasia]]) | Gelatinous or papillomatous lesion; typically at limbus; slow-growing |

## Classification and Grading

### By Severity

| **Grade** | **Criteria** |
|---|---|
| **Mild** | Ulcer <2 mm; peripheral location; superficial depth; no anterior chamber reaction; no hypopyon |
| **Moderate** | Ulcer 2–5 mm; paracentral; moderate depth; mild anterior chamber reaction; small or no hypopyon |
| **Severe** | Ulcer >5 mm or involving visual axis; deep stromal involvement; significant anterior chamber reaction; hypopyon; descemetocele; scleral extension; impending perforation |

### By Location

- **Central** – Involving the visual axis; greatest impact on visual acuity; higher risk of visually significant scarring.
- **Paracentral** – Adjacent to the visual axis.
- **Peripheral** – Within 2 mm of the limbus; may be associated with autoimmune [[peripheral ulcerative keratitis]] (PUK).

## Management

### General Principles

1. **Identify and eliminate the inciting cause** (e.g., discontinue contact lens wear, treat lid abnormalities, address systemic disease).
2. **Obtain appropriate microbiological specimens** before initiating therapy when indicated.
3. **Initiate empiric broad-spectrum antimicrobial therapy** immediately; do not wait for culture results.
4. **Tailor therapy** based on culture and sensitivity results.
5. **Monitor closely** with frequent follow-up (daily or more frequently for severe ulcers).
6. **Manage complications** (anterior uveitis, elevated [[intraocular pressure]], impending perforation).

### Pharmacological Treatment

#### Empiric Antibiotic Therapy for Bacterial Corneal Ulcers

| **Severity** | **Recommended Regimen** | **Dosing Frequency** |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | [[Fluoroquinolone]] monotherapy: [[moxifloxacin]] 0.5% or [[gatifloxacin]] 0.5% or [[levofloxacin]] 1.5% | Every 1–2 hours while awake for 24–48 hours, then taper |
| **Moderate to Severe** | **Option 1:** Fortified [[tobramycin]] (14 mg/mL) + fortified [[cefazolin]] (50 mg/mL) or fortified [[vancomycin]] (25–50 mg/mL) — alternating every 30–60 minutes around the clock | Every 30–60 minutes (alternating), including overnight for first 24–48 hours |
| | **Option 2:** Fourth-generation fluoroquinolone monotherapy (moxifloxacin 0.5% or gatifloxacin 0.5%) — acceptable for moderate ulcers; some experts prefer fortified antibiotics for severe cases | Every 30–60 minutes |

**Key pharmacological notes:**
- **Fortified antibiotics** are compounded from parenteral preparations and provide higher corneal tissue concentrations than commercially available drops.
- **[[Tobramycin]]** or **[[gentamicin]]** provides Gram-negative coverage (including *Pseudomonas*).
- **[[Cefazolin]]** or **[[vancomycin]]** provides Gram-positive coverage (including [[MRSA]] when vancomycin is used).
- **Fourth-generation [[fluoroquinolones]]** (moxifloxacin, gatifloxacin) offer broad-spectrum coverage against both Gram-positive and Gram-negative organisms with excellent corneal penetration and are the preferred monotherapy agents.
- **[[Ciprofloxacin]] 0.3%** was previously the standard fluoroquinolone but has higher rates of corneal crystalline deposits and increasing resistance patterns.

#### Antifungal Therapy

| **Agent** | **Spectrum** | **Route** | **Notes** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Natamycin]] 5% suspension | Filamentous fungi (*Fusarium*, *Aspergillus*) | Topical | First-line for filamentous fungal keratitis; only commercially available topical antifungal in many countries |
| [[Voriconazole]] 1% | Broad-spectrum (filamentous and yeast) | Topical (compounded); also oral (200–400 mg BID) and intrastromal injection (50 µg/0.1 mL) | Excellent corneal penetration; increasingly used as adjunct or alternative |
| [[Amphotericin B]] 0.15–0.25% | Yeast (*Candida*); some filamentous fungi | Topical (compounded) | First-line for yeast keratitis; poor penetration for deep infections |
| [[Fluconazole]] 0.2% | Yeast (*Candida*) | Topical; oral (200–400 mg/day) | Alternative for yeast keratitis |
| [[Itraconazole]] | Filamentous fungi | Oral (200 mg BID) | Adjunctive systemic therapy |

**Dosing for fungal keratitis:** Topical antifungals are typically administered every 1 hour around the clock initially, then tapered gradually over weeks to months. Fungal ulcers require prolonged treatment (often 6–12 weeks).

#### Anti-Acanthamoeba Therapy

Dual therapy targeting both trophozoites and cysts is essential:

| **Agent** | **Class** | **Dosing** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Polyhexamethylene biguanide]] (PHMB) 0.02% | Biguanide (cysticidal) | Every 1 hour initially, then taper |
| [[Chlorhexidine]] 0.02% | Biguanide (cysticidal) | Alternative to PHMB; every 1 hour initially |
| [[Propamidine isethionate]] 0.1% (Brolene) | Diamidine (cysticidal and trophozoiticidal) | Every 1 hour initially, combined with a biguanide |
| [[Hexamidine]] 0.1% | Diamidine | Alternative to propamidine |

Treatment duration for *Acanthamoeba* keratitis is typically **3–6 months or longer** due to the extreme resistance of the cyst form.

#### Antiviral Therapy (Herpes Simplex Keratitis)

| **Agent** | **Route** | **Indication** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Ganciclovir]] 0.15% gel | Topical | Epithelial keratitis (dendritic/geographic ulcer) |
| [[Trifluridine]] 1% | Topical | Epithelial keratitis (alternative; more epitheliotoxic) |
| [[Acyclovir]] 3% ointment | Topical | Epithelial keratitis (not available in U.S.) |
| [[Oral acyclovir]] 400 mg 5×/day or [[oral valacyclovir]] 500 mg TID | Systemic | Adjunctive for epithelial keratitis; primary for stromal/endothelial keratitis; long-term prophylaxis (acyclovir 400 mg BID) |

### Adjunctive Therapy

| **Agent/Intervention** | **Purpose** |
|---|---|
| [[Cycloplegic agents]] ([[atropine]] 1%, [[cyclopentolate]] 1%) | Relieve ciliary spasm (pain), prevent posterior [[synechiae]], stabilize blood-aqueous barrier |
| [[Oral analgesics]] (NSAIDs, acetaminophen, opioids for severe pain) | Pain management |
| [[Preservative-free artificial tears]] | Lubrication, dilution of inflammatory mediators |
| [[Oral doxycycline]] (50–100 mg BID) or [[oral tetracycline]] | Anti-collagenase (MMP inhibition); anti-inflammatory; useful in sterile melts and as adjunct |
| [[Topical acetylcysteine]] 10–20% | Collagenase inhibitor; used in progressive stromal melting |
| [[Autologous serum tears]] (20%) | Provide growth factors, fibronectin, and anti-inflammatory components; promote epithelial healing |
| [[Tissue adhesive]] ([[cyanoacrylate glue]]) | Seal small perforations or impending perforations; applied with a [[bandage contact lens]] overlay |
| [[Bandage contact lens]] | Protect healing epithelium; reduce pain; used after acute infection is controlled (NEVER during active infection unless with intensive antimicrobial coverage) |
| [[Amniotic membrane transplantation]] | Promotes epithelial healing; anti-inflammatory; anti-scarring; used for persistent epithelial defects and as adjunct in severe ulcers |

### Corticosteroid Use in Corneal Ulcers

The use of topical [[corticosteroids]] in infectious keratitis remains one of the most debated topics in corneal disease management.

**The Steroids for Corneal Ulcers Trial (SCUT)** – A landmark multicenter, randomized, placebo-controlled trial published in 2012 – evaluated adjunctive topical [[prednisolone phosphate]] 1% in bacterial corneal ulcers. Key findings:

- No statistically significant overall benefit of corticosteroids on best-corrected visual acuity at 3 months.
- Subgroup analyses suggested potential benefit in central ulcers, ulcers with baseline vision of counting fingers or worse, and *Pseudomonas* ulcers.
- No significant increase in adverse events (perforation, need for surgery) with steroid use.

**Current consensus:**
- **Never initiate corticosteroids before 48 hours of appropriate antimicrobial therapy** and evidence of clinical response.
- **Contraindicated** in fungal and *Acanthamoeba* keratitis (may worsen infection dramatically).
- May be considered cautiously in bacterial ulcers with significant inflammation after culture-confirmed organism and demonstrated antibiotic sensitivity.
- **Essential** in herpetic stromal (immune) keratitis, always with concurrent antiviral coverage.

### Surgical Management

Surgical intervention is indicated when medical therapy fails or complications arise:

| **Procedure** | **Indication** |
|---|---|
| **[[Therapeutic penetrating keratoplasty]]** (TPK) | Corneal perforation, impending perforation unresponsive to medical therapy, progressive infection despite maximal medical therapy; provides both tissue replacement and microbiological specimen |
| **[[Patch graft]]** (lamellar or full-thickness) | Small perforations; tectonic support |
| **[[Amniotic membrane transplantation]]** | Persistent epithelial defect; adjunctive anti-inflammatory and pro-healing |
| **[[Conjunctival flap]]** (Gundersen flap) | Chronic, non-healing ulcers; provides vascular supply; sacrifices visual potential |
| **[[Tissue adhesive]] (cyanoacrylate)** | Small perforations (<2–3 mm); temporizing measure |
| **[[Tarsorrhaphy]]** (temporary or permanent) | Exposure keratopathy; neurotrophic ulcers; promotes healing by protecting the corneal surface |
| **[[Evisceration]] or [[enucleation]]** | End-stage: uncontrollable infection with endophthalmitis; painful blind eye |
| **[[Optical penetrating keratoplasty]]** | Performed after infection resolution for visual rehabilitation of dense corneal scarring |
| **[[Deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]] (DALK)** | Visual rehabilitation post-ulcer with intact Descemet's membrane and endothelium; lower rejection risk than full-thickness PK |
| **[[Corneal collagen cross-linking]] (CXL)** | Emerging adjunctive therapy (PACK-CXL: photoactivated chromophore for keratitis); antimicrobial effect of UV-A/riboflavin; may be useful in early, non-responsive bacterial or fungal keratitis |

## Complications

| **Complication** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| [[Corneal scarring]] / [[leukoma]] | Most common sequela; density and location determine visual impact |
| [[Corneal perforation]] | Medical emergency; risk of iris prolapse, endophthalmitis, phthisis |
| [[Descemetocele]] | Impending perforation; requires urgent intervention |
| [[Secondary glaucoma]] | From anterior chamber inflammation, synechiae, or steroid use |
| [[Posterior synechiae]] | Adhesion of iris to lens from anterior uveitis |
| [[Endophthalmitis]] | Intraocular infection following perforation |
| [[Corneal neovascularization]] | Chronic inflammation; may complicate future keratoplasty |
| [[Irregular astigmatism]] | From corneal scarring and thinning |
| [[Phthisis bulbi]] | End-stage globe shrinkage from chronic inflammation |
| [[Cataract]] | From chronic inflammation or prolonged steroid use |

## Prognosis

Prognosis depends on multiple factors:

- **Organism**: Bacterial ulcers (especially *Pseudomonas*) can be rapidly destructive but often respond well to appropriate antibiotics. Fungal and *Acanthamoeba* ulcers carry a worse prognosis due to diagnostic delay and prolonged treatment requirements.
- **Ulcer size and depth**: Larger, deeper ulcers have worse outcomes.
- **Location**: Central ulcers result in greater visual morbidity.
- **Time to treatment**: Early diagnosis and initiation of appropriate therapy are the strongest predictors of favorable outcome.
- **Host factors**: Immunosuppression, diabetes, and ocular surface disease worsen prognosis.
- **Presenting visual acuity**: Worse presenting vision correlates with worse final outcome.

With prompt, appropriate treatment, many bacterial corneal ulcers heal within 2–3 weeks, though residual scarring is common. Fungal ulcers may require 6–12 weeks of treatment. *Acanthamoeba* keratitis may require months of therapy and has a significant rate of requiring surgical intervention.

## Prevention

| **Preventive Measure** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| Proper [[contact lens]] hygiene | Wash hands before handling lenses; use recommended disinfecting solutions; never use tap water; replace cases regularly; avoid overnight wear unless specifically prescribed |
| Protective eyewear | In agricultural, industrial, and sports settings to prevent trauma |
| Prompt treatment of ocular surface disease | Manage [[dry eye]], [[blepharitis]], [[meibomian gland dysfunction]], lid malposition |
| Judicious use of topical corticosteroids | Avoid unsupervised chronic use; monitor for secondary infection |
| Nutritional supplementation | [[Vitamin A]] supplementation in at-risk populations to prevent [[xerophthalmia]] |
| Patient education | Recognize warning signs (pain, redness, photophobia, discharge) and seek immediate care |
| Prophylactic antibiotics | After corneal surgery or significant corneal trauma |

## Special Considerations

### Contact Lens–Related Corneal Ulcers

Contact lens wear is the single most important risk factor in developed nations. The risk is highest with:
- **Extended (overnight) wear** – 10–15× increased risk compared to daily wear
- **Poor hygiene** – Inadequate hand washing, topping off solutions, water exposure
- **Cosmetic/decorative lenses** – Often obtained without prescription; poor fit and hygiene

*Pseudomonas aeruginosa* is the most common organism in contact lens–related ulcers. All contact lens wear must be immediately discontinued upon diagnosis, and the lens and case should be submitted for culture.

### Pediatric Corneal Ulcers

Corneal ulcers in children present unique challenges:
- Often delayed presentation due to poor symptom reporting
- Higher risk of [[amblyopia]] from corneal scarring in the visual axis
- Examination may require sedation or examination under anesthesia
- Trauma and [[vernal keratoconjunctivitis]] are common predisposing factors in children

### Corneal Ulcers in Developing Countries

- Agricultural trauma with vegetative matter is the leading cause
- Fungal keratitis predominates in tropical regions
- Limited access to microbiological facilities and fortified antibiotics
- Traditional eye medicines (herbal remedies) may delay presentation and worsen outcomes
- [[Vitamin A deficiency]] remains a significant cause of corneal ulceration and blindness in children
