

# Glycosaminoglycans

**Glycosaminoglycans (GAGs)** are a family of complex, linear, negatively charged [[polysaccharide]] chains composed of repeating disaccharide units that play indispensable structural and regulatory roles throughout the [[eye]]. Formerly referred to as **mucopolysaccharides**, these macromolecules are critical components of the [[extracellular matrix]] (ECM) of virtually every ocular tissue, including the [[cornea]], [[vitreous humor]], [[trabecular meshwork]], [[sclera]], [[retina]], and [[lens capsule]]. Their unique physicochemical properties—particularly their extraordinary capacity for water retention, their interactions with [[growth factors]] and [[cytokines]], and their contribution to tissue transparency and biomechanics—make them central to normal ocular physiology and to the pathogenesis of numerous ophthalmic diseases.

For the optometrist and ophthalmologist, a thorough understanding of glycosaminoglycans is essential because alterations in GAG metabolism underlie or contribute to conditions ranging from [[corneal dystrophies]] and [[macular corneal dystrophy]] to [[glaucoma]], [[dry eye disease]], [[mucopolysaccharidoses]], and [[diabetic retinopathy]]. Furthermore, GAG-based therapeutics—most notably [[sodium hyaluronate]] eye drops and [[chondroitin sulfate]]-containing viscoelastics—are among the most commonly prescribed and utilized agents in clinical eye care.

---

## Biochemistry and Classification

### Basic Structure

All glycosaminoglycans are unbranched polysaccharide chains constructed from repeating disaccharide units. Each disaccharide consists of:

1. An **amino sugar** — either [[N-acetylglucosamine]] (GlcNAc) or [[N-acetylgalactosamine]] (GalNAc)
2. A **uronic acid** — either [[glucuronic acid]] (GlcA) or [[iduronic acid]] (IdoA), with the exception of [[keratan sulfate]], which contains [[galactose]] instead of a uronic acid

The chains are heavily modified by **sulfation** at various positions (O-sulfation and N-sulfation), which confers a high density of negative charge. This polyanionic character is responsible for the remarkable water-binding capacity of GAGs and their ability to form hydrated gels that resist compressive forces.

### Classification of Ocular GAGs

The major classes of glycosaminoglycans found in ocular tissues are summarized below:

| GAG Class | Repeating Disaccharide | Sulfation | Molecular Weight (kDa) | Primary Ocular Location(s) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Hyaluronic acid]] (Hyaluronan, HA)** | GlcA – GlcNAc | None (unsulfated) | 100 – 10,000+ | [[Vitreous humor]], [[aqueous humor]], [[corneal epithelium]], [[lacrimal gland]] |
| **[[Chondroitin sulfate]] (CS)** | GlcA – GalNAc | 4-O or 6-O sulfation | 5 – 50 | [[Corneal stroma]], [[sclera]], [[trabecular meshwork]] |
| **[[Dermatan sulfate]] (DS)** | IdoA/GlcA – GalNAc | 4-O sulfation | 15 – 40 | [[Corneal stroma]], [[sclera]] |
| **[[Keratan sulfate]] (KS)** | Gal – GlcNAc | 6-O sulfation on both sugars | 4 – 19 | [[Corneal stroma]] (predominant GAG) |
| **[[Heparan sulfate]] (HS)** | GlcA/IdoA – GlcNAc | Variable N-, 2-O, 6-O, 3-O sulfation | 5 – 50 | [[Basement membranes]], [[lens capsule]], [[retinal vasculature]], [[trabecular meshwork]] |
| **[[Heparin]]** | IdoA – GlcNAc | Heavily sulfated | 6 – 30 | [[Mast cells]] of [[conjunctiva]] and [[uvea]] |

With the exception of [[hyaluronic acid]], all GAGs are covalently attached to a core protein to form **[[proteoglycans]]**. The major ocular proteoglycans include [[lumican]], [[keratocan]], [[mimecan]] (osteoglycin), and [[decorin]] in the cornea; [[aggrecan]] and [[versican]] in the vitreous and sclera; and [[perlecan]], [[agrin]], and [[collagen XVIII]] in basement membranes.

## Role of Glycosaminoglycans in Ocular Tissues

### Cornea

The [[corneal stroma]] is the tissue in which GAGs play their most celebrated and best-understood role. The stroma comprises approximately 90% of corneal thickness and consists of regularly arranged [[collagen]] fibrils (predominantly [[type I collagen]] and [[type V collagen]]) embedded in a ground substance rich in proteoglycans.

**[[Keratan sulfate]]** is the predominant GAG of the adult human corneal stroma, accounting for more than 50% of total stromal GAGs. It is primarily carried by the small leucine-rich proteoglycans (SLRPs) [[lumican]] and [[keratocan]]. **[[Chondroitin sulfate]]** and **[[dermatan sulfate]]** are carried mainly by [[decorin]] and [[mimecan]].

The precise spacing of collagen fibrils in the stroma—maintained at approximately 55–60 nm center-to-center—is essential for [[corneal transparency]]. GAG chains extending from SLRPs regulate this interfibrillar spacing through:

- **Electrostatic repulsion** between negatively charged sulfate and carboxylate groups
- **Osmotic swelling pressure** generated by water attraction to the polyanionic GAG chains
- **Steric bridging** between adjacent collagen fibrils

Disruption of GAG composition or distribution leads to loss of the regular fibrillar lattice and consequent **corneal opacification**, as classically demonstrated in [[macular corneal dystrophy]] (MCD), where a deficiency of sulfated keratan sulfate results in corneal clouding.

### Vitreous Humor

The [[vitreous body]] is a transparent, gel-like structure that fills the posterior segment of the eye. Its gel state is maintained by a network of [[type II collagen]] fibrils interspersed with high concentrations of **[[hyaluronic acid]]** (HA). Vitreous HA has an exceptionally high molecular weight (often exceeding 2–4 million Daltons) and occupies a vast hydrodynamic volume, trapping water and maintaining vitreous volume and transparency.

Age-related degradation of HA and collagen leads to **vitreous liquefaction** ([[synchysis]]) and **vitreous collapse** ([[syneresis]]), which are precursors to [[posterior vitreous detachment]] (PVD) and may contribute to [[retinal tears]] and [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]].

### Trabecular Meshwork and Aqueous Outflow

The [[trabecular meshwork]] (TM) and [[Schlemm's canal]] region contain significant quantities of **[[hyaluronic acid]]**, **[[chondroitin sulfate]]**, **[[heparan sulfate]]**, and **[[dermatan sulfate]]**. GAGs in the juxtacanalicular tissue (JCT) contribute to the resistance to [[aqueous humor]] outflow. Accumulation of GAGs—particularly HA and CS—in the TM has been implicated in increased [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) in [[primary open-angle glaucoma]] (POAG).

The enzyme **[[hyaluronidase]]** can degrade HA in the TM and has been shown experimentally to reduce outflow resistance, providing a rationale for enzymatic approaches to IOP lowering.

### Tear Film and Ocular Surface

**[[Hyaluronic acid]]** is a natural component of the [[tear film]] and the [[conjunctival]] and [[corneal epithelial]] surface glycocalyx. It contributes to tear film stability, lubrication, and epithelial wound healing. The [[mucin]] layer of the tear film, produced by [[goblet cells]] and surface epithelial cells, also contains GAG-like domains. Deficiency of surface GAGs is associated with [[dry eye disease]] and [[ocular surface disease]].

### Sclera

The [[sclera]] is rich in **[[chondroitin sulfate]]**, **[[dermatan sulfate]]**, and smaller amounts of **[[heparan sulfate]]**. GAGs contribute to scleral hydration, biomechanics, and the regulation of scleral remodeling. Alterations in scleral GAG content have been implicated in the pathogenesis of [[myopia]], where increased GAG degradation may contribute to scleral thinning and axial elongation.

### Retina and Retinal Vasculature

**[[Heparan sulfate]] proteoglycans** (HSPGs) are critical components of the [[retinal vascular basement membrane]] and the [[internal limiting membrane]] (ILM). They regulate the binding and signaling of [[vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF), [[fibroblast growth factor]] (FGF), and other angiogenic and neurotrophic factors. Loss of HSPG integrity in the retinal vasculature is an early event in [[diabetic retinopathy]] and contributes to increased vascular permeability and [[neovascularization]].

### Lens Capsule

The [[lens capsule]] is a thick basement membrane rich in **[[heparan sulfate]] proteoglycans** (particularly [[perlecan]]) and [[type IV collagen]]. HSPGs in the capsule regulate growth factor availability to the [[lens epithelium]] and play a role in the pathogenesis of [[posterior capsular opacification]] (PCO) following [[cataract surgery]].

## Pathophysiology of GAG-Related Ocular Disease

### Mucopolysaccharidoses (MPS)

The **[[mucopolysaccharidoses]]** are a group of inherited [[lysosomal storage disorders]] caused by deficiency of specific enzymes required for the stepwise degradation of GAGs. Undegraded or partially degraded GAGs accumulate within [[lysosomes]] of cells throughout the body, including ocular tissues, leading to progressive cellular dysfunction and tissue damage.

| MPS Type | Eponym | Deficient Enzyme | Accumulated GAG(s) | Key Ocular Manifestations |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **MPS I-H** | [[Hurler syndrome]] | [[α-L-iduronidase]] | DS, HS | Severe [[corneal clouding]], [[retinal degeneration]], [[optic nerve]] swelling |
| **MPS I-S** | [[Scheie syndrome]] | [[α-L-iduronidase]] | DS, HS | [[Corneal clouding]], [[glaucoma]] |
| **MPS I-H/S** | Hurler-Scheie | [[α-L-iduronidase]] | DS, HS | Moderate corneal clouding |
| **MPS II** | [[Hunter syndrome]] | [[Iduronate-2-sulfatase]] | DS, HS | Mild/no corneal clouding; [[retinal degeneration]], [[papilledema]] |
| **MPS III** (A–D) | [[Sanfilippo syndrome]] | Various (HS degradation) | HS | Minimal ocular involvement; possible [[retinal degeneration]] |
| **MPS IV** (A, B) | [[Morquio syndrome]] | [[Galactose-6-sulfatase]] / [[β-galactosidase]] | KS, CS | [[Corneal clouding]] (fine, diffuse) |
| **MPS VI** | [[Maroteaux-Lamy syndrome]] | [[Arylsulfatase B]] | DS | Severe [[corneal clouding]], [[glaucoma]], [[optic nerve compression]] |
| **MPS VII** | [[Sly syndrome]] | [[β-glucuronidase]] | DS, HS, CS | [[Corneal clouding]] |
| **MPS IX** | Natowicz syndrome | [[Hyaluronidase]] | HA | Periarticular soft tissue masses; ocular involvement rare |

**Corneal clouding** is the hallmark ocular finding in many MPS types and results from the intracellular and extracellular accumulation of GAGs within [[keratocytes]] and the corneal stroma, disrupting the regular collagen fibril arrangement necessary for transparency.

### Macular Corneal Dystrophy (MCD)

**[[Macular corneal dystrophy]]** (MCD) is an [[autosomal recessive]] [[corneal dystrophy]] caused by mutations in the **[[CHST6]]** gene, which encodes corneal [[N-acetylglucosamine-6-O-sulfotransferase]], the enzyme responsible for sulfation of keratan sulfate in the cornea. The resulting deficiency of normally sulfated KS leads to:

- Accumulation of **unsulfated or undersulfated keratan sulfate** (termed "antigenic KS" in MCD type I, where serum KS is undetectable, versus MCD type II, where serum KS is present but corneal KS is abnormal)
- Deposition of abnormal glycoprotein material in [[keratocytes]], [[Descemet's membrane]], and the [[corneal endothelium]]
- Progressive **corneal opacification** beginning in the first decade of life, with irregular, poorly defined gray-white stromal deposits extending to the limbus

MCD is the only corneal dystrophy that extends to the [[limbus]] and involves the full thickness of the stroma, distinguishing it from [[granular corneal dystrophy]] and [[lattice corneal dystrophy]].

### Glaucoma

Alterations in GAG metabolism within the [[trabecular meshwork]] contribute to the pathogenesis of [[primary open-angle glaucoma]]. Specifically:

- Increased deposition of **[[chondroitin sulfate]]** and **[[hyaluronic acid]]** in the JCT increases outflow resistance
- Decreased activity of **matrix metalloproteinases** (MMPs) and GAG-degrading enzymes leads to ECM accumulation
- Changes in **[[heparan sulfate]]** proteoglycan expression alter TGF-β signaling, promoting fibrotic remodeling of the outflow pathway

In **[[pseudoexfoliation syndrome]]** and **[[pseudoexfoliative glaucoma]]**, abnormal fibrillar material containing GAGs (particularly [[chondroitin sulfate]]) accumulates on the [[lens]], [[iris]], [[ciliary body]], and TM, obstructing aqueous outflow.

### Diabetic Retinopathy

In [[diabetes mellitus]], hyperglycemia-induced alterations in GAG metabolism contribute to retinal vascular pathology:

- **Loss of [[heparan sulfate]]** from the retinal capillary basement membrane reduces the charge-selective barrier to plasma protein leakage
- Decreased HS impairs the sequestration and regulation of **[[VEGF]]** and **[[FGF-2]]**, promoting [[neovascularization]]
- Enzymatic degradation of HS by **[[heparanase]]**, which is upregulated in diabetic tissues, further exacerbates vascular permeability

### Myopia

Scleral remodeling in [[myopia]] involves significant changes in GAG composition:

- Decreased total GAG content, particularly **[[chondroitin sulfate]]** and **[[dermatan sulfate]]**
- Increased activity of GAG-degrading enzymes
- Reduced scleral [[proteoglycan]] synthesis
- These changes contribute to scleral thinning, reduced rigidity, and progressive axial elongation

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

The clinical manifestations of GAG-related ocular pathology depend on the specific tissue involved and the nature of the GAG abnormality:

### Corneal Manifestations
- **Progressive bilateral corneal clouding** — characteristic of MPS and MCD
- **Decreased [[visual acuity]]** — due to stromal opacification
- **[[Photophobia]]** and **glare** — from light scatter by disorganized stromal collagen
- **Recurrent [[corneal erosions]]** — in some dystrophies with epithelial basement membrane involvement

### Anterior Segment Manifestations
- **Elevated [[intraocular pressure]]** — from GAG accumulation in the [[trabecular meshwork]]
- **[[Narrow angles]]** or **angle closure** — from thickened iris or ciliary body in MPS
- **[[Pseudoexfoliation]] material** — GAG-containing deposits on anterior segment structures

### Posterior Segment Manifestations
- **[[Floaters]]** and **[[photopsia]]** — from vitreous liquefaction and PVD due to HA degradation
- **[[Retinal degeneration]]** — in MPS types with retinal GAG accumulation
- **[[Optic disc swelling]]** or **[[optic atrophy]]** — from intracranial hypertension or direct optic nerve compression in MPS
- **[[Diabetic macular edema]]** and **[[proliferative diabetic retinopathy]]** — associated with HS loss

### Ocular Surface Manifestations
- **[[Dry eye]] symptoms** — burning, foreign body sensation, tearing, blurred vision
- **[[Punctate epithelial erosions]]** — from inadequate surface GAG protection
- **Reduced [[tear break-up time]]** (TBUT)

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

**[[Slit-lamp examination]]** is the primary tool for detecting corneal GAG-related pathology. In MPS, diffuse corneal haze is observed, often more prominent in the anterior stroma. In MCD, focal gray-white opacities with indistinct borders are seen throughout the stroma, extending to the periphery and involving all stromal layers.

### Corneal Imaging

- **[[Anterior segment optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT):** Provides high-resolution cross-sectional imaging of corneal deposits, stromal thickness, and endothelial changes
- **[[Confocal microscopy]] (in vivo):** Reveals hyperreflective intracellular deposits within [[keratocytes]] in MPS and MCD; allows assessment of keratocyte density and morphology
- **[[Corneal topography]] and [[pachymetry]]:** Detects irregular astigmatism and increased corneal thickness from stromal edema or GAG accumulation
- **[[Specular microscopy]]:** Evaluates [[corneal endothelial]] cell density and morphology, which may be affected in MCD and MPS

### Genetic Testing

- **[[CHST6]] gene sequencing** for suspected [[macular corneal dystrophy]]
- **Enzyme assays** and **genetic panels** for suspected [[mucopolysaccharidoses]]
- Identification of specific mutations guides prognosis and eligibility for [[enzyme replacement therapy]]

### Laboratory Investigations

- **Urine GAG quantification** (total and fractionated) — elevated in MPS; specific GAG patterns help identify the MPS subtype
- **Serum [[keratan sulfate]]** levels — reduced or absent in MCD type I; normal in MCD type II
- **Leukocyte or fibroblast enzyme assays** — definitive diagnosis of specific MPS types
- **[[Electrophoresis]]** of urinary GAGs — distinguishes DS, HS, KS, and CS accumulation patterns

### Tonometry and Gonioscopy

- **[[Goldmann applanation tonometry]]** or **[[non-contact tonometry]]** to assess IOP in patients with suspected GAG-related [[glaucoma]]
- **[[Gonioscopy]]** to evaluate the [[iridocorneal angle]] for GAG deposits, pseudoexfoliation material, or angle narrowing

### Posterior Segment Evaluation

- **[[Fundoscopy]]** and **[[optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)** to assess for retinal degeneration, macular edema, or optic nerve changes
- **[[Electroretinography]] (ERG)** — may reveal reduced scotopic and photopic responses in MPS with retinal involvement
- **[[Fluorescein angiography]]** (FA) and **[[OCT angiography]]** (OCTA) — to evaluate retinal vascular integrity in diabetic retinopathy associated with HS loss

## Management and Treatment

### Topical GAG-Based Therapeutics

#### Sodium Hyaluronate (Hyaluronic Acid) Eye Drops

**[[Sodium hyaluronate]]** is the most widely used GAG-based ophthalmic therapeutic. Available in concentrations ranging from 0.1% to 0.4% (and higher in some formulations), it is the gold standard [[artificial tear]] for [[dry eye disease]] management.

**Mechanism of action:**
- Exceptional water-binding capacity (can bind up to 1,000 times its weight in water)
- Viscoelastic properties provide prolonged ocular surface residence time
- Promotes [[corneal epithelial]] wound healing by interacting with [[CD44]] receptors on epithelial cells
- Anti-inflammatory properties through modulation of [[inflammatory cytokines]]
- Mucoadhesive properties enhance tear film stability

**Clinical indications:**
- [[Dry eye disease]] (aqueous-deficient and evaporative)
- Post-[[refractive surgery]] ocular surface rehabilitation
- [[Contact lens]]-related dryness
- [[Sjögren syndrome]]-associated dry eye
- Post-[[cataract surgery]] ocular surface management
- [[Neurotrophic keratopathy]]

#### Chondroitin Sulfate

**[[Chondroitin sulfate]]** is used in combination with sodium hyaluronate in some artificial tear formulations and is a key component of **[[ophthalmic viscoelastic devices]]** (OVDs) used during intraocular surgery.

**Properties:**
- Provides corneal endothelial protection during surgery
- Contributes to space maintenance in the [[anterior chamber]]
- Synergistic with HA in dispersive OVDs (e.g., [[Viscoat]] — 4% chondroitin sulfate + 3% sodium hyaluronate)

### Ophthalmic Viscoelastic Devices (OVDs)

OVDs are GAG-based surgical adjuncts essential in modern [[cataract surgery]], [[glaucoma surgery]], and [[corneal transplantation]].

| OVD Category | Primary GAG Component | Properties | Example Products |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cohesive** | High-MW [[hyaluronic acid]] | High viscosity, space maintenance, easy removal | [[Healon]], [[Provisc]], [[Amvisc Plus]] |
| **Dispersive** | [[Chondroitin sulfate]] ± low-MW HA | Endothelial protection, coats surfaces, retained in eye | [[Viscoat]], [[DisCoVisc]] |
| **Viscoadaptive** | Very high-MW HA | Adapts behavior to flow conditions | [[Healon5]] |

### Enzyme Replacement Therapy (ERT)

For systemic [[mucopolysaccharidoses]], **[[enzyme replacement therapy]]** has become available for several subtypes:

- **[[Laronidase]]** (Aldurazyme) — for MPS I
- **[[Idursulfase]]** (Elaprase) — for MPS II
- **[[Galsulfase]]** (Naglazyme) — for MPS VI
- **[[Elosulfase alfa]]** (Vimizim) — for MPS IVA
- **[[Vestronidase alfa]]** (Mepsevii) — for MPS VII

**Ocular impact of ERT:** While ERT significantly improves systemic manifestations, its effect on corneal clouding is limited because the cornea is avascular and the large enzyme molecules have poor penetration into the corneal stroma. Corneal clouding typically persists or progresses despite systemic ERT.

### Hematopoietic Stem Cell Transplantation (HSCT)

**[[Hematopoietic stem cell transplantation]]** (bone marrow transplant) for MPS I (Hurler syndrome) has been shown to stabilize or partially improve corneal clouding, likely through donor-derived monocyte/macrophage engraftment in the cornea that provides local enzyme production. HSCT is most effective when performed early in life (before age 2).

### Surgical Management

#### Corneal Transplantation

**[[Penetrating keratoplasty]]** (PK) or **[[deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]]** (DALK) may be required for visually significant corneal opacification in MCD and MPS:

- **MCD:** PK is the definitive treatment for advanced disease. Recurrence in the graft is possible but typically takes many years (10–20+ years), as host keratocytes repopulate the donor stroma
- **MPS:** PK can improve vision, but recurrence of clouding in the graft is common due to ongoing systemic GAG accumulation; outcomes are better in attenuated MPS phenotypes and in patients receiving concurrent ERT or post-HSCT

#### Glaucoma Surgery

In GAG-related [[glaucoma]] (MPS, pseudoexfoliation), surgical options include:

- **[[Trabeculectomy]]**
- **[[Glaucoma drainage devices]]** (e.g., [[Ahmed valve]], [[Baerveldt implant]])
- **[[Minimally invasive glaucoma surgery]]** (MIGS) — though efficacy may be limited if the TM is heavily infiltrated with GAG deposits

### Emerging Therapies

#### Gene Therapy

**[[Gene therapy]]** approaches for MPS are under active investigation:

- Intravitreal or subretinal delivery of viral vectors encoding the deficient enzyme
- Corneal gene therapy using [[adeno-associated virus]] (AAV) vectors to transduce keratocytes with the [[CHST6]] gene for MCD
- Systemic gene therapy with liver-directed AAV vectors to achieve sustained enzyme production

#### Substrate Reduction Therapy

**[[Substrate reduction therapy]]** (SRT) aims to reduce GAG synthesis to match the residual degradative capacity. **[[Genistein]]** (a soy isoflavone) has shown modest effects in reducing GAG synthesis in preclinical MPS models and small clinical trials.

#### Intracorneal Enzyme Delivery

Experimental approaches to deliver recombinant enzymes directly into the corneal stroma via **[[iontophoresis]]**, **nanoparticle carriers**, or **intrastromal injection** are being explored to address the limitation of poor corneal penetration of systemically administered ERT.

#### Cross-Linked Hyaluronic Acid

Newer formulations of **cross-linked hyaluronic acid** for dry eye provide extended ocular surface residence time and enhanced mucoadhesion compared to standard linear HA preparations.

## Prognosis and Long-Term Considerations

- **MCD:** Visual prognosis is good following successful [[keratoplasty]], though long-term graft survival requires monitoring for recurrence
- **MPS:** Ocular prognosis depends on the specific subtype, severity, and availability of systemic therapy (ERT/HSCT); corneal clouding, glaucoma, and retinal degeneration may all contribute to progressive visual loss
- **Dry eye disease:** GAG-based artificial tears (sodium hyaluronate) provide symptomatic relief and promote ocular surface health but require ongoing use
- **Glaucoma:** GAG-related outflow obstruction may be progressive and require escalating medical and surgical therapy
- **Myopia:** Understanding of scleral GAG remodeling may lead to future therapeutic interventions to slow axial elongation

---

## Summary

Glycosaminoglycans are fundamental structural and regulatory macromolecules in ocular biology. Their roles in maintaining [[corneal transparency]], [[vitreous]] gel structure, [[aqueous humor]] dynamics, [[tear film]] stability, and [[retinal vascular]] integrity make them central to both normal vision and the pathogenesis of numerous eye diseases. For the eye care professional, knowledge of GAG biochemistry and pathophysiology informs the diagnosis and management of conditions ranging from [[corneal dystrophies]] and [[mucopolysaccharidoses]] to [[glaucoma]], [[dry eye disease]], and [[diabetic retinopathy]]. GAG-based therapeutics—particularly [[sodium hyaluronate]] and [[chondroitin sulfate]]—remain indispensable tools in clinical practice and ophthalmic surgery, while emerging gene therapy and targeted enzyme delivery approaches hold promise for addressing currently intractable GAG-related ocular diseases.
