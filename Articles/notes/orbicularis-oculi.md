

# Orbicularis Oculi

The **orbicularis oculi** is a broad, flat, elliptical [[skeletal muscle]] that encircles the [[orbit]] and extends into the [[eyelid]] and surrounding facial regions. It is the principal [[protractor]] of the eyelids and serves as the primary muscle responsible for eyelid closure, blinking, and the lacrimal pump mechanism essential for [[tear drainage]]. As one of the most clinically significant muscles in periocular anatomy, the orbicularis oculi is central to ocular surface protection, [[tear film]] dynamics, facial expression, and cosmetic appearance. Dysfunction of this muscle—whether from neurological, myogenic, traumatic, or age-related causes—can produce a wide spectrum of ophthalmic pathology ranging from [[exposure keratopathy]] to debilitating [[blepharospasm]].

---

## Anatomy

### Gross Anatomy and Subdivisions

The orbicularis oculi is a superficial muscle of [[facial expression]] that lies immediately beneath the skin of the eyelids and the periorbital region. It is broadly divided into three anatomically and functionally distinct parts:

| **Division** | **Location** | **Primary Function** |
|---|---|---|
| **Orbital portion** | Surrounds the orbital rim in a complete ellipse; overlies the [[orbital septum]], [[frontal bone]], [[maxilla]], and [[medial palpebral ligament]] | Forceful, voluntary eyelid closure (e.g., squeezing eyes shut tightly) |
| **Palpebral portion** | Contained within the upper and lower [[eyelid]]s; subdivided into preseptal and pretarsal components | Gentle, involuntary eyelid closure (spontaneous and reflex blinking) |
| **Lacrimal portion (Horner's muscle)** | Deep fibers posterior to the [[lacrimal sac]], arising from the posterior [[lacrimal crest]] and [[lacrimal fascia]] | Lacrimal pump mechanism; facilitates [[tear drainage]] into the [[nasolacrimal duct]] |

#### Orbital Portion

The orbital portion is the thickest and most peripheral component. Its fibers originate from the [[medial palpebral ligament]], the [[nasal process of the frontal bone]], and the [[frontal process of the maxilla]]. They sweep laterally in broad concentric loops around the orbital margin without interruption, forming a complete sphincter. This portion is responsible for forceful, voluntary lid closure and is recruited during protective reflexes such as the [[blink reflex]] in response to bright light or foreign body threat.

#### Palpebral Portion

The palpebral portion is thinner and paler than the orbital portion and is confined to the eyelids proper. It is further subdivided:

- **Preseptal orbicularis**: Lies anterior to the [[orbital septum]]. It contributes to spontaneous blinking and assists in the lacrimal pump mechanism by compressing the [[lacrimal ampullae]].
- **Pretarsal orbicularis**: Lies anterior to the [[tarsal plate]]. Its deep heads (the medial and lateral canthal tendons' attachments) form the [[muscle of Riolan]], a thin strip of muscle fibers immediately adjacent to the lid margin that contributes to meibomian gland expression and maintains eyelid apposition to the globe.

#### Lacrimal Portion (Horner's Muscle)

The lacrimal portion, also known as **Horner's muscle** or the **pars lacrimalis**, consists of thin, deep fibers that arise from the posterior lacrimal crest and the [[lacrimal fascia]] overlying the [[lacrimal sac]]. These fibers pass laterally behind the lacrimal sac and insert into the medial ends of the upper and lower tarsal plates. During blinking, contraction of Horner's muscle dilates the lacrimal sac (creating negative pressure) and shortens the [[canaliculi]], thereby actively pumping tears from the [[conjunctival sac]] into the [[lacrimal drainage system]]. This mechanism is clinically referred to as the **[[lacrimal pump mechanism]]**.

### Innervation

The orbicularis oculi receives its motor innervation exclusively from the **temporal** and **zygomatic branches** of the [[facial nerve]] (cranial nerve VII). The temporal branch innervates the upper orbital and palpebral portions, while the zygomatic branch supplies the lower orbital and palpebral portions. The nerve fibers approach the muscle from its deep (posterior) surface.

Sensory innervation of the overlying skin is provided by branches of the [[trigeminal nerve]] (cranial nerve V):

- **[[Supraorbital nerve]]** and **[[supratrochlear nerve]]** (from [[ophthalmic division]], V1) for the upper eyelid and brow region.
- **[[Infraorbital nerve]]** (from [[maxillary division]], V2) for the lower eyelid and cheek.

### Blood Supply

The arterial supply to the orbicularis oculi is derived from branches of both the [[internal carotid artery]] and [[external carotid artery]] systems:

- **[[Ophthalmic artery]]** → [[supraorbital artery]], [[supratrochlear artery]], medial palpebral arteries
- **[[Facial artery]]** → [[angular artery]]
- **[[Superficial temporal artery]]** → [[transverse facial artery]], [[zygomatico-orbital artery]]
- **[[Infraorbital artery]]** (from [[maxillary artery]])

Venous drainage follows corresponding veins into the [[anterior facial vein]] and [[ophthalmic veins]], with important anastomoses to the [[cavernous sinus]] via the [[superior ophthalmic vein]]—a clinically relevant pathway for the spread of periorbital infection.

### Relationships to Adjacent Structures

The orbicularis oculi has critical anatomical relationships:

- **Superficial**: Lies directly beneath the skin; no deep fascia separates it from the subcutaneous tissue.
- **Deep**: Overlies the [[orbital septum]], [[tarsal plates]], [[levator palpebrae superioris]] aponeurosis (upper lid), [[capsulopalpebral fascia]] (lower lid), and the [[lacrimal sac]].
- **Superior**: Interdigitates with the [[frontalis muscle]], [[corrugator supercilii]], and [[procerus muscle]].
- **Lateral**: Overlaps the [[temporalis fascia]] and the origin of the [[zygomaticus major]].
- **Medial**: Attaches to the [[medial palpebral ligament]] (medial canthal tendon), which anchors it to the [[anterior lacrimal crest]] and [[posterior lacrimal crest]].

## Physiology and Function

### Eyelid Closure

The orbicularis oculi is the sole muscle responsible for eyelid closure. It functions as an antagonist to the [[levator palpebrae superioris]] (which opens the upper eyelid) and [[Müller's muscle]] (which provides sympathetic tone to the upper lid). The balance between the orbicularis oculi and the levator complex determines the [[palpebral fissure]] width and eyelid position.

- **Spontaneous blinking**: Occurs approximately 15–20 times per minute and is mediated primarily by the palpebral (pretarsal and preseptal) portions. Spontaneous blinks are semi-involuntary and are modulated by the [[basal ganglia]] and [[brainstem]] blink centers.
- **Reflex blinking**: Triggered by sensory stimuli (corneal touch, bright light, loud sounds) via the [[trigeminal-facial reflex arc]]. The afferent limb is carried by the [[trigeminal nerve]] (V1), and the efferent limb by the [[facial nerve]] (VII).
- **Voluntary (forced) closure**: Recruits the orbital portion for maximal lid closure, as in squinting or protecting the eye from a perceived threat.

### Lacrimal Pump Mechanism

The lacrimal pump is one of the most clinically important functions of the orbicularis oculi. During each blink:

1. **Closure phase**: The pretarsal orbicularis contracts, compressing the [[canaliculi]] and forcing tears medially toward the lacrimal sac. Simultaneously, Horner's muscle pulls the lateral wall of the lacrimal sac laterally, expanding the sac and creating negative intraluminal pressure.
2. **Opening phase**: As the orbicularis relaxes, the lacrimal sac collapses due to its inherent elasticity, propelling tears inferiorly through the [[nasolacrimal duct]] into the [[inferior meatus]] of the nose. The canaliculi re-expand, drawing tears from the [[lacrimal lake]] via capillary action.

Disruption of this pump—from orbicularis weakness ([[facial nerve palsy]]), eyelid laxity ([[involutional ectropion]]), or surgical damage—results in [[epiphora]] (tearing) even in the absence of lacrimal outflow obstruction.

### Meibomian Gland Expression

The pretarsal orbicularis, particularly the [[muscle of Riolan]], exerts compressive force on the [[meibomian glands]] embedded within the tarsal plates during each blink. This action expresses [[meibum]] (lipid secretion) onto the lid margin, contributing to the lipid layer of the [[tear film]]. Incomplete blinking or orbicularis weakness can lead to [[meibomian gland dysfunction]] (MGD) and [[evaporative dry eye disease]].

### Facial Expression

The orbicularis oculi is a key muscle of facial expression. Contraction of the orbital portion produces "crow's feet" wrinkles at the lateral canthus and is associated with genuine smiling (the **[[Duchenne smile]]**), distinguishing it from a forced or social smile that involves only the [[zygomaticus major]].

## Pathophysiology

Pathology of the orbicularis oculi can be broadly categorized by etiology:

### 1. Neurogenic Dysfunction

#### Facial Nerve Palsy (Orbicularis Weakness/Paralysis)

The most common cause of orbicularis oculi dysfunction is [[facial nerve palsy]] (CN VII palsy), which may be:

- **Idiopathic ([[Bell's palsy]])**: The most frequent cause; presumed viral (often [[herpes simplex virus]]) inflammatory demyelination of the facial nerve within the [[temporal bone]].
- **Traumatic**: Temporal bone fractures, surgical injury (e.g., during [[parotidectomy]], [[acoustic neuroma]] resection, or [[mastoidectomy]]).
- **Infectious**: [[Herpes zoster oticus]] ([[Ramsay Hunt syndrome]]), [[Lyme disease]], [[otitis media]].
- **Neoplastic**: [[Parotid gland tumors]], [[cerebellopontine angle tumors]], [[facial nerve schwannoma]].
- **Central**: [[Stroke]], [[multiple sclerosis]], [[Möbius syndrome]].

In facial nerve palsy, the orbicularis oculi loses tone and contractile ability, resulting in:

- **[[Lagophthalmos]]**: Inability to fully close the eyelids, leading to corneal exposure.
- **[[Ectropion]]**: Lower lid falls away from the globe due to loss of orbicularis tone.
- **[[Exposure keratopathy]]**: Corneal desiccation, [[punctate epithelial erosions]], [[corneal ulceration]], and potentially [[corneal perforation]].
- **[[Epiphora]]**: Paradoxical tearing due to lacrimal pump failure and reflex hypersecretion from corneal exposure.
- **Loss of [[Bell's phenomenon]]**: In some patients, the normal protective upward rotation of the globe during attempted lid closure is absent, compounding exposure risk.

#### Aberrant Regeneration

Following facial nerve injury, misdirected regeneration of nerve fibers can produce [[synkinesis]]—involuntary orbicularis contraction during voluntary movements of other facial muscles (e.g., orbicularis contraction during chewing, known as the **[[Marcus Gunn jaw-winking phenomenon]]** analog for the facial nerve).

### 2. Hyperkinetic Disorders

#### Benign Essential Blepharospasm (BEB)

[[Benign essential blepharospasm]] is a focal [[dystonia]] characterized by involuntary, bilateral, forceful, sustained contractions of the orbicularis oculi. It is a disorder of the [[basal ganglia]] and brainstem motor circuits. Key features include:

- Bilateral, synchronous spasms of the orbicularis oculi
- Progressive worsening over time
- Potential for functional blindness due to inability to open the eyes
- Exacerbated by bright light, stress, fatigue, and wind
- Often associated with [[Meige syndrome]] (blepharospasm plus lower facial/oromandibular dystonia)

#### Hemifacial Spasm

[[Hemifacial spasm]] involves involuntary, unilateral, intermittent contractions of muscles innervated by the facial nerve, including the orbicularis oculi. It is most commonly caused by vascular compression of the facial nerve root exit zone at the [[brainstem]] by an aberrant [[anterior inferior cerebellar artery]] or [[posterior inferior cerebellar artery]] loop.

#### Myokymia

[[Eyelid myokymia]] is a benign, self-limited, fine fascicular twitching of the orbicularis oculi (usually the lower lid pretarsal portion). It is extremely common and is typically associated with:

- Fatigue and sleep deprivation
- Excessive caffeine intake
- Stress
- Eye strain

Myokymia is distinguished from blepharospasm by its unilateral, fine, undulating character and its self-resolving nature.

### 3. Involutional (Age-Related) Changes

With aging, the orbicularis oculi undergoes:

- **Atrophy and loss of tone**: Contributing to [[involutional entropion]] (inward turning of the lid) or [[involutional ectropion]] (outward turning of the lid).
- **Dehiscence of the pretarsal orbicularis from the tarsal plate**: A key factor in involutional entropion, where the preseptal orbicularis overrides the pretarsal orbicularis.
- **Laxity of the [[lateral canthal tendon]]** and **[[medial canthal tendon]]**: Compounding lid malposition.
- **Herniation of [[orbital fat]]**: The weakened orbicularis and septum allow prolapse of orbital fat pads, producing the clinical appearance of "[[dermatochalasis]]" and "bags under the eyes."

### 4. Myogenic Disorders

- **[[Myasthenia gravis]]**: An [[autoimmune]] disorder targeting [[acetylcholine receptors]] at the [[neuromuscular junction]]. Ocular myasthenia frequently presents with orbicularis weakness, [[ptosis]], and [[diplopia]]. The **[[peek sign]]**—inability to maintain eyelid closure, with the lids gradually separating to reveal the sclera—is a hallmark of orbicularis weakness in myasthenia gravis.
- **[[Chronic progressive external ophthalmoplegia]] (CPEO)**: [[Mitochondrial myopathy]] that can affect the orbicularis oculi along with the [[extraocular muscles]].
- **[[Myotonic dystrophy]]**: Characterized by delayed relaxation of the orbicularis after forceful closure (myotonia).

### 5. Traumatic Injury

Lacerations, burns, or surgical trauma to the eyelid can directly damage the orbicularis oculi or its nerve supply, resulting in localized weakness, scarring, and lid malposition ([[cicatricial ectropion]] or [[cicatricial entropion]]).

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Symptoms related to orbicularis oculi dysfunction vary depending on whether the muscle is underactive (paretic/paralytic) or overactive (spastic/dystonic):

| **Condition** | **Key Symptoms** | **Key Signs** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Facial nerve palsy]] | Inability to close eye, tearing, foreign body sensation, blurred vision, photophobia | [[Lagophthalmos]], [[ectropion]], [[Bell's phenomenon]], [[exposure keratopathy]], reduced blink rate |
| [[Benign essential blepharospasm]] | Involuntary eye closure, functional visual impairment, photophobia, difficulty reading/driving | Bilateral forceful orbicularis spasms, increased blink rate, [[Charcot sign]] (eyebrow elevation to compensate), [[apraxia of eyelid opening]] |
| [[Hemifacial spasm]] | Unilateral involuntary eye twitching, facial pulling | Unilateral orbicularis spasm, progressive involvement of other ipsilateral facial muscles |
| [[Eyelid myokymia]] | Fine twitching of eyelid (usually lower), intermittent | Fine fascicular contractions visible on close inspection |
| [[Myasthenia gravis]] | Drooping eyelid, difficulty keeping eyes open, double vision | [[Ptosis]], [[peek sign]], [[Cogan's lid twitch sign]], fatigable orbicularis weakness |
| [[Involutional entropion]] | Foreign body sensation, tearing, redness, irritation | Inward turning of lower lid margin, [[trichiasis]], orbicularis override |
| [[Involutional ectropion]] | Tearing, exposure, redness | Outward turning of lower lid margin, punctal eversion, conjunctival keratinization |

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

A systematic clinical evaluation of orbicularis oculi function includes:

1. **Observation at rest**: Assess for asymmetry of the [[palpebral fissure]], lid position, and spontaneous blink rate.
2. **Gentle eyelid closure**: Ask the patient to close the eyes gently. Observe for completeness of closure and symmetry. Incomplete closure indicates orbicularis weakness.
3. **Forced eyelid closure**: Ask the patient to squeeze the eyes shut tightly. The examiner attempts to pry the lids open. Reduced resistance indicates orbicularis weakness. This is graded on the **[[House-Brackmann scale]]** for facial nerve function.
4. **Snap-back test**: The lower lid is pulled away from the globe and released. Prompt return to apposition indicates good orbicularis tone; delayed return suggests laxity or weakness.
5. **Distraction test**: The lower lid is pulled laterally. Greater than 6 mm of displacement suggests significant [[horizontal lid laxity]].
6. **Peek sign**: The patient is asked to gently close the eyes and maintain closure. Gradual separation of the lids (peeking of the sclera) suggests orbicularis fatigability, as seen in [[myasthenia gravis]].
7. **Assessment of Bell's phenomenon**: During attempted forced closure, observe for upward rotation of the globe. Absence of Bell's phenomenon increases the risk of [[exposure keratopathy]] in the setting of lagophthalmos.
8. **Blink rate and pattern**: Reduced blink rate may indicate [[Parkinson's disease]] or orbicularis weakness. Increased blink rate or forced spasms suggest blepharospasm.
9. **Lacrimal pump assessment**: Evaluate for epiphora. Perform the [[fluorescein dye disappearance test]] and [[Jones test]] to differentiate lacrimal pump failure from nasolacrimal duct obstruction.

### Diagnostic Modalities

| **Test** | **Purpose** | **Relevance to Orbicularis Oculi** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Electromyography]] (EMG)** | Measures electrical activity of the muscle | Differentiates neurogenic from myogenic weakness; assesses denervation and reinnervation |
| **[[Nerve conduction studies]]** | Evaluates facial nerve conduction velocity and latency | Localizes site of facial nerve lesion; prognosticates recovery in Bell's palsy |
| **[[Blink reflex testing]]** (electrical) | Stimulates supraorbital nerve, records orbicularis EMG response | Evaluates integrity of the trigeminal-facial reflex arc; detects subclinical facial nerve or trigeminal nerve lesions |
| **[[MRI]] of brain and temporal bone** | Neuroimaging | Identifies structural causes of facial nerve palsy (tumors, demyelination, vascular compression in hemifacial spasm) |
| **[[CT scan]] of orbits** | Bony and soft tissue imaging | Evaluates traumatic fractures, orbital masses affecting the muscle |
| **[[Anti-acetylcholine receptor antibodies]]** | Serological testing | Diagnosis of [[myasthenia gravis]] |
| **[[Ice pack test]]** | Cooling the eyelid for 2 minutes | Improvement of ptosis/orbicularis weakness suggests myasthenia gravis (cold inhibits acetylcholinesterase) |
| **[[Tensilon (edrophonium) test]]** | Intravenous anticholinesterase challenge | Transient improvement confirms myasthenia gravis |
| **[[High-resolution OCT]] of the eyelid** | Imaging of lid structures | Emerging modality for assessing orbicularis thickness and structural integrity |
| **[[Schirmer's test]] / [[Tear break-up time]]** | Tear film assessment | Evaluates secondary dry eye from incomplete blinking or lagophthalmos |

### Grading of Facial Nerve Function

The **[[House-Brackmann Facial Nerve Grading System]]** is the most widely used scale:

| **Grade** | **Description** | **Orbicularis Function** |
|---|---|---|
| I | Normal | Complete closure with effort |
| II | Mild dysfunction | Complete closure with minimal effort |
| III | Moderate dysfunction | Complete closure with maximal effort |
| IV | Moderately severe | Incomplete closure |
| V | Severe dysfunction | Barely perceptible motion |
| VI | Total paralysis | No movement |

## Treatment and Management

Management of orbicularis oculi pathology is directed at the underlying etiology and the specific functional deficit.

### A. Management of Orbicularis Weakness (Facial Nerve Palsy / Lagophthalmos)

#### Conservative / Medical Management

- **Ocular surface protection**:
  - Frequent [[artificial tears]] (preservative-free formulations preferred)
  - [[Lubricating ointment]] at bedtime (e.g., [[lacrilube]], [[erythromycin ointment]])
  - [[Moisture chamber glasses]] or [[swim goggles]] to reduce evaporation
  - Taping the eyelid closed at night
- **[[Punctal plugs]]**: To increase tear retention in cases of secondary [[dry eye]]
- **Systemic treatment of underlying cause**:
  - [[Corticosteroids]] (e.g., [[prednisone]]) ± [[antiviral agents]] (e.g., [[valacyclovir]]) for [[Bell's palsy]]
  - Antibiotics for [[Lyme disease]]
  - Immunotherapy for [[myasthenia gravis]] ([[pyridostigmine]], [[corticosteroids]], [[azathioprine]], [[mycophenolate mofetil]])

#### Surgical Management

| **Procedure** | **Indication** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Gold weight implant]]** (upper lid loading) | Paralytic lagophthalmos | Gravity-assisted upper lid closure; implanted in the pretarsal space |
| **[[Platinum chain implant]]** | Alternative to gold weight | Thinner, more flexible, lower profile; reduced risk of extrusion |
| **[[Lateral tarsorrhaphy]]** | Severe lagophthalmos, corneal exposure | Partial fusion of lateral lid margins to reduce palpebral fissure width |
| **[[Medial canthoplasty]]** | Lower lid ectropion with medial canthal tendon laxity | Tightens medial canthal tendon to restore lid apposition |
| **[[Lateral tarsal strip procedure]]** | Involutional ectropion/entropion with horizontal lid laxity | Tightens the lower lid by shortening and reattaching the lateral tarsal strip to the [[lateral orbital tubercle]] |
| **[[Fascia lata sling]]** | Chronic, non-recovering facial paralysis | Static or dynamic facial reanimation using autologous fascia |
| **[[Temporalis muscle transfer]]** | Chronic facial paralysis | Dynamic reanimation; temporalis muscle transposed to provide voluntary eyelid closure |
| **[[Cross-face nerve graft]]** | Chronic facial paralysis | Nerve graft from contralateral facial nerve to reinnervate the orbicularis |
| **[[Free gracilis muscle transfer]]** | Complete, irreversible facial paralysis | Microvascular free flap with nerve coaptation for dynamic facial reanimation |

### B. Management of Orbicularis Hyperactivity

#### Botulinum Toxin Injection

**[[Botulinum toxin type A]]** (e.g., [[onabotulinumtoxinA]] [Botox], [[abobotulinumtoxinA]] [Dysport], [[incobotulinumtoxinA]] [Xeomin]) is the **first-line treatment** for:

- [[Benign essential blepharospasm]]
- [[Hemifacial spasm]]
- Refractory [[eyelid myokymia]]

**Mechanism**: Botulinum toxin cleaves [[SNARE proteins]] (specifically [[SNAP-25]]) at the presynaptic terminal of the [[neuromuscular junction]], preventing [[acetylcholine]] release and producing temporary chemodenervation of the orbicularis oculi.

**Injection technique**:
- Multiple subcutaneous injections are placed into the pretarsal and preseptal orbicularis of the upper and lower lids, as well as the lateral orbital portion (crow's feet area).
- Typical doses: 2.5–5.0 units of onabotulinumtoxinA per injection site; total dose 20–40 units per side.
- Onset: 2–5 days; peak effect: 1–2 weeks; duration: 3–4 months.
- Repeat injections are required indefinitely.

**Complications**:
- [[Ptosis]] (from diffusion to the [[levator palpebrae superioris]])
- [[Lagophthalmos]] and [[exposure keratopathy]] (from excessive orbicularis weakening)
- [[Diplopia]] (from diffusion to [[extraocular muscles]])
- Ecchymosis, local pain, dry eye
- Rarely: [[antibody formation]] leading to treatment resistance

#### Surgical Myectomy

**[[Orbicularis myectomy]]** is reserved for patients with severe blepharospasm refractory to botulinum toxin. The procedure involves surgical excision of portions of the orbicularis oculi (typically the preseptal and orbital portions of the upper lid) to reduce the bulk of spasming muscle. It may be combined with [[blepharoplasty]] and [[brow lift]].

#### Microvascular Decompression

For [[hemifacial spasm]] caused by vascular compression of the facial nerve, **[[microvascular decompression]]** (MVD) of the facial nerve root exit zone is a definitive neurosurgical treatment with cure rates exceeding 85%.

### C. Management of Involutional Lid Malposition

#### Involutional Entropion

- **Temporary**: [[Quickert sutures]] (everting sutures through the lower lid) to evert the lid margin.
- **Definitive**: Combination of [[lateral tarsal strip procedure]] (to address horizontal laxity) + [[retractor reinsertion]] (to reattach the [[lower lid retractors]] to the inferior tarsal border) + **orbicularis muscle repositioning** (to prevent preseptal orbicularis override of the pretarsal orbicularis).

#### Involutional Ectropion

- **[[Lateral tarsal strip procedure]]**: Addresses horizontal lid laxity.
- **[[Medial spindle procedure]]**: For medial ectropion with punctal eversion.
- **Orbicularis tightening**: May be incorporated into lower lid [[blepharoplasty]] procedures.

### D. Cosmetic Considerations

The orbicularis oculi is a primary target in aesthetic periocular procedures:

- **[[Blepharoplasty]]**: Excess skin, orbicularis muscle, and herniated orbital fat are excised or repositioned to rejuvenate the periocular appearance.
- **[[Botulinum toxin]] for crow's feet**: Injection into the lateral orbital orbicularis reduces dynamic rhytides (wrinkles) associated with smiling and squinting.
- **[[Brow lift]]**: Addresses brow ptosis caused by gravitational descent and orbicularis-frontalis imbalance.

## Prognosis

The prognosis for orbicularis oculi dysfunction depends on the underlying etiology:

- **[[Bell's palsy]]**: Approximately 70–85% of patients recover completely within 3–6 months. Incomplete recovery may result in residual orbicularis weakness, synkinesis, or contracture.
- **[[Benign essential blepharospasm]]**: Chronic and progressive; no cure exists, but botulinum toxin injections provide effective symptomatic control in >90% of patients.
- **[[Hemifacial spasm]]**: Botulinum toxin provides symptomatic relief; microvascular decompression offers potential cure.
- **[[Myasthenia gravis]]**: Orbicularis weakness is often the earliest manifestation of ocular myasthenia; approximately 50% of patients with ocular myasthenia progress to generalized myasthenia within 2 years.
- **Involutional changes**: Surgical correction of entropion and ectropion has high success rates (>90%) when appropriate procedures are selected.

---

## Summary

The orbicularis oculi is an anatomically complex and functionally indispensable periocular muscle. Its roles in eyelid closure, blinking, tear drainage, meibomian gland expression, and facial expression make it central to ocular surface health and visual function. Clinicians in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] must possess a thorough understanding of its anatomy, physiology, and pathology to accurately diagnose and manage the wide spectrum of conditions—from [[facial nerve palsy]] and [[blepharospasm]] to [[involutional lid malposition]] and [[exposure keratopathy]]—that arise from its dysfunction. A multidisciplinary approach involving optometrists, ophthalmologists, neurologists, and oculoplastic surgeons is often required for optimal patient outcomes.
