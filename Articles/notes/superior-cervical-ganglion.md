

# Superior Cervical Ganglion

---

The **Superior Cervical Ganglion** (SCG) is the largest and most rostral of the three cervical [[sympathetic ganglia]] within the [[sympathetic trunk]]. It is a critical autonomic relay station that provides the principal postganglionic [[sympathetic innervation]] to the structures of the head, neck, and—of paramount importance to the eye care professional—the [[eye]] and its adnexal structures. The SCG plays an indispensable role in the regulation of [[pupil]] diameter, [[eyelid]] position, [[orbital]] vascular tone, [[aqueous humor]] dynamics, and [[choroidal]] blood flow. Disruption of the SCG or its efferent pathways is the defining lesion in the clinical syndrome known as [[Horner syndrome]] (oculosympathetic paresis), one of the most clinically significant neuro-ophthalmic diagnoses encountered in optometric and ophthalmologic practice.

A thorough understanding of the anatomy, physiology, and clinical pathology of the superior cervical ganglion is essential for every optometrist, as it underpins the diagnosis and localization of sympathetic pathway lesions, guides pharmacological testing, and informs the urgency of neuroimaging and referral.

---

## Gross Anatomy and Topographic Relations

The superior cervical ganglion is a fusiform or spindle-shaped structure measuring approximately **2.5 to 3.5 cm in length** and roughly **0.5 to 1.0 cm in width**, making it the largest paravertebral ganglion in the human body. It is situated in the posterior [[carotid sheath]], lying posterior to the [[internal carotid artery]] (ICA) and anterior to the [[longus capitis muscle]], at the level of the **second (C2) and third (C3) cervical vertebrae**. Superiorly, it extends to the base of the [[skull]] near the [[jugular foramen]], and inferiorly it is connected to the [[middle cervical ganglion]] via the sympathetic trunk.

### Key Anatomic Relationships

| Structure | Relationship to SCG |
|---|---|
| [[Internal carotid artery]] | Anterior/anteromedial |
| [[Internal jugular vein]] | Anterolateral |
| [[Vagus nerve]] (CN X) | Lateral, within the carotid sheath |
| [[Glossopharyngeal nerve]] (CN IX) | Crosses superficially |
| [[Hypoglossal nerve]] (CN XII) | Crosses superficially |
| [[Longus capitis muscle]] | Posterior |
| [[Prevertebral fascia]] | Posterior |
| [[Pharyngeal wall]] | Medial |
| [[Superior laryngeal nerve]] | Medial |

The ganglion receives **preganglionic sympathetic fibers** that originate in the [[ciliospinal center of Budge]] (intermediolateral cell column of the spinal cord at levels **C8–T2**, sometimes referred to as T1–T2). These preganglionic neurons (second-order neurons) ascend within the sympathetic trunk without synapsing through the [[stellate ganglion]] (inferior cervical/first thoracic ganglion) and the [[middle cervical ganglion]] before terminating in the SCG, where they synapse with **postganglionic neurons** (third-order neurons).

## Efferent Pathways and Ocular Innervation

The postganglionic sympathetic fibers arising from the SCG are of critical importance to the eye care professional. These unmyelinated, long postganglionic fibers travel via several distinct routes to reach their target organs in the orbit and periorbital region.

### Major Efferent Branches Relevant to the Eye

1. **Internal Carotid Nerve (and Internal Carotid Plexus):** Postganglionic fibers ascend along the [[internal carotid artery]], forming the **internal carotid plexus** (also called the carotid sympathetic plexus). This plexus travels through the [[carotid canal]] in the [[petrous temporal bone]] and enters the [[middle cranial fossa]]. Within the [[cavernous sinus]], fibers join the [[ophthalmic division]] (V1) of the [[trigeminal nerve]], specifically traveling with the [[nasociliary nerve]] and then the [[long ciliary nerves]] to reach the eye. Some fibers also briefly join the [[oculomotor nerve]] (CN III) or pass through the [[ciliary ganglion]] (without synapsing) to reach the globe via the [[short ciliary nerves]].

2. **External Carotid Nerve (and External Carotid Plexus):** Fibers descend along the [[external carotid artery]] to supply vasomotor innervation to the face, [[lacrimal gland]] (partial innervation), and other extracranial structures.

3. **Jugular Nerve:** Ascends to the [[jugular foramen]], communicating with CN IX, CN X, and CN XII.

4. **Gray Rami Communicantes:** To the upper cervical spinal nerves (C1–C4), providing sympathetic fibers to the skin and vasculature of the upper neck and occiput.

5. **Pharyngeal Branches, Laryngopharyngeal Branches, and Cardiac Branch (Superior Cervical Cardiac Nerve):** These supply the pharynx, larynx, and heart, respectively, and are less directly relevant to eye care but are important in understanding the systemic role of the SCG.

### Ocular and Adnexal Targets of SCG Postganglionic Fibers

| Target Structure | Function of Sympathetic Innervation |
|---|---|
| [[Müller's muscle]] (superior tarsal muscle) | Maintains upper [[eyelid]] elevation (~2 mm of lid aperture) |
| [[Inferior tarsal muscle]] | Maintains lower eyelid position (slight retraction) |
| [[Dilator pupillae muscle]] (iris dilator) | [[Mydriasis]] (pupil dilation) |
| [[Orbital smooth muscle of Müller]] (orbitalis muscle) | Maintains forward position of the globe within the orbit |
| [[Conjunctival]] and [[episcleral]] blood vessels | [[Vasoconstriction]] |
| [[Choroidal]] vasculature | Modulation of choroidal blood flow |
| [[Ciliary body]] vasculature and epithelium | Regulation of [[aqueous humor]] production (stimulates secretion via β2-adrenergic receptors) |
| [[Lacrimal gland]] (partial) | Modulation of tear secretion (minor role) |
| Sweat glands of the ipsilateral forehead and face | Sudomotor function (facial sweating) |
| Blood vessels of the ipsilateral face | Vasoconstriction |

## Neurochemistry and Receptor Pharmacology

The preganglionic neurons synapsing in the SCG are **cholinergic**, releasing [[acetylcholine]] (ACh) that acts on **nicotinic receptors** (specifically the ganglionic N2 subtype) on the postganglionic cell bodies. The postganglionic neurons are predominantly **adrenergic** (noradrenergic), releasing [[norepinephrine]] (NE) at their target organs.

At the level of the eye:

- The [[dilator pupillae muscle]] expresses **α1-adrenergic receptors**, which mediate contraction (mydriasis) in response to norepinephrine.
- [[Müller's muscle]] also expresses **α1-adrenergic receptors**, mediating contraction and eyelid elevation.
- The [[ciliary body]] epithelium expresses **β2-adrenergic receptors**, which stimulate aqueous humor production. This is the pharmacological basis for the use of [[beta-blockers]] (e.g., [[timolol]]) in the treatment of [[glaucoma]].
- Vascular smooth muscle in the [[conjunctiva]], [[episclera]], and [[choroid]] expresses both **α1-** and **α2-adrenergic receptors**, mediating vasoconstriction.

The pharmacological sensitivity of denervated structures is the basis for the diagnostic pharmacological testing used in [[Horner syndrome]] (see Diagnosis section below).

## Pathophysiology

### The Three-Neuron Sympathetic Pathway and Lesion Localization

The oculosympathetic pathway is classically described as a **three-neuron arc**:

| Neuron Order | Cell Body Location | Course | Synapse/Termination |
|---|---|---|---|
| **First-order (central)** | [[Hypothalamus]] (posterolateral) | Descends through [[brainstem]] (uncrossed) → lateral [[medulla]] → [[spinal cord]] (intermediolateral column, C8–T2) | Ciliospinal center of Budge |
| **Second-order (preganglionic)** | Ciliospinal center (C8–T2) | Exits spinal cord → over [[lung apex]] → ascends in sympathetic trunk | **Superior cervical ganglion** |
| **Third-order (postganglionic)** | **Superior cervical ganglion** | Ascends along [[internal carotid artery]] → cavernous sinus → orbit via long ciliary nerves and V1 | Dilator pupillae, Müller's muscle, orbital smooth muscle, facial vasculature/sweat glands |

**Lesions at the level of the SCG itself** (second-order neuron synapse or third-order neuron origin) produce a **postganglionic Horner syndrome**. However, the clinical presentation of Horner syndrome is similar regardless of the level of the lesion, and pharmacological testing is required to differentiate preganglionic from postganglionic lesions.

### Etiologies of SCG Disruption

Pathology affecting the SCG or its immediate postganglionic fibers includes:

- **Trauma:** Penetrating or blunt neck injuries, surgical trauma (e.g., [[carotid endarterectomy]], [[thyroidectomy]], [[anterior cervical discectomy]], [[radical neck dissection]], [[tonsillectomy]])
- **Neoplasia:** [[Neuroblastoma]], [[schwannoma]], [[paraganglioma]] (glomus jugulare/carotid body tumors), [[lymphoma]], metastatic disease (especially [[lung carcinoma]], [[breast carcinoma]]), direct invasion by head and neck [[squamous cell carcinoma]]
- **Vascular:** [[Internal carotid artery dissection]] (a critical and potentially life-threatening cause of postganglionic Horner syndrome), [[carotid artery aneurysm]], [[cavernous sinus thrombosis]]
- **Infection/Inflammation:** [[Otitis media]], [[mastoiditis]], [[herpes zoster]], [[sarcoidosis]], [[Lyme disease]]
- **Iatrogenic:** Central venous catheterization, chest tube placement, epidural anesthesia, nerve blocks
- **Congenital:** Birth trauma (e.g., [[Klumpke paralysis]]), congenital [[neuroblastoma]], congenital vascular anomalies
- **Idiopathic:** In a significant proportion of cases, no identifiable cause is found

### Denervation Supersensitivity

A key pathophysiological concept relevant to the SCG is **postganglionic denervation supersensitivity** (Cannon's law of denervation). When the postganglionic neuron is damaged (third-order neuron lesion), the target end-organs (e.g., the [[dilator pupillae]]) lose their tonic norepinephrine supply. Over time (typically 24–48 hours to weeks), the postsynaptic receptors upregulate, becoming hypersensitive to circulating catecholamines and to direct-acting sympathomimetic agents. This phenomenon is exploited diagnostically using dilute [[phenylephrine]] or [[hydroxyamphetamine]] testing (see Diagnosis).

## Clinical Presentation (Symptoms and Signs)

Disruption of the SCG or its postganglionic fibers produces the classic triad of **[[Horner syndrome]]** (also known as oculosympathetic paresis or Bernard-Horner syndrome):

### Classic Triad

1. **[[Miosis]]** (pupillary constriction): Due to unopposed action of the [[sphincter pupillae muscle]] (parasympathetically innervated via [[CN III]] and the [[ciliary ganglion]]). The affected pupil is smaller, and the **anisocoria is greater in dim illumination** (dilation lag). The miotic pupil reacts normally to light and near stimuli but dilates poorly and slowly in the dark.

2. **[[Ptosis]]** (upper eyelid drooping): Typically mild (1–2 mm), due to loss of sympathetic tone to [[Müller's muscle]]. This is in contrast to the more severe ptosis (often >2 mm) seen with [[oculomotor nerve palsy]].

3. **Apparent [[Enophthalmos]]**: Due to loss of tone in the [[orbitalis muscle of Müller]] (smooth muscle spanning the [[inferior orbital fissure]]), resulting in a narrowed [[palpebral fissure]] and the appearance of a sunken eye. True enophthalmos is minimal or absent; the appearance is largely due to the combination of upper lid ptosis and lower lid "reverse ptosis" (slight elevation of the lower lid due to loss of inferior tarsal muscle tone).

### Additional Signs

| Sign | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Dilation lag** | The affected pupil dilates more slowly than the normal pupil when lights are dimmed; anisocoria is maximal at 4–5 seconds in darkness and diminishes over 10–15 seconds |
| **Ipsilateral facial anhidrosis** | Loss of sweating on the affected side of the face (more prominent with preganglionic lesions; postganglionic SCG lesions may spare most facial sweating because sudomotor fibers to the face travel with the external carotid artery) |
| **Conjunctival/facial hyperemia** (acute phase) | Loss of sympathetic vasoconstriction leads to transient vasodilation |
| **Iris heterochromia** (congenital Horner) | The affected iris is lighter (hypopigmented) due to failure of sympathetic-dependent melanocyte stimulation during development |
| **Lower intraocular pressure** (mild) | Reduced aqueous humor production due to loss of β2-adrenergic stimulation of the [[ciliary body]] |
| **Accommodation changes** (variable) | Some reports of increased accommodative amplitude on the affected side |

### Symptoms Reported by Patients

- Noticeable difference in pupil size (often noted by family members or in photographs)
- Mild drooping of the upper eyelid
- Blurred vision (rare, usually not significant)
- Facial flushing or warmth on the affected side (acute phase)
- Decreased sweating on the affected side of the face
- In cases of [[internal carotid artery dissection]]: ipsilateral neck pain, headache, or facial pain (often preceding the Horner syndrome)

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

The diagnosis of a sympathetic pathway lesion involving the SCG begins with a thorough clinical examination:

1. **Pupil assessment:** Measure pupil size in both bright and dim illumination. Anisocoria that increases in dim light suggests a sympathetic deficit (the smaller pupil is the abnormal one). Assess the **dilation lag** by observing pupil responses after rapidly dimming room lights (best observed with infrared videography or careful slit-lamp observation).

2. **Eyelid evaluation:** Measure the [[marginal reflex distance]] (MRD1 and MRD2) and [[palpebral fissure]] height. Mild ptosis (1–2 mm) of the upper lid and slight elevation of the lower lid are characteristic.

3. **Slit-lamp examination:** Evaluate for [[iris heterochromia]], [[conjunctival]] injection, and anterior segment abnormalities.

4. **Facial sweating assessment:** Although difficult to assess clinically, asymmetric facial sweating may be noted.

### Pharmacological Testing

Pharmacological testing is the cornerstone of confirming and localizing the lesion within the three-neuron sympathetic arc.

#### Step 1: Confirmation of Horner Syndrome

| Agent | Mechanism | Expected Result in Horner Syndrome |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Cocaine]] 4–10% topical** | Blocks reuptake of norepinephrine at the synaptic cleft; in a normal eye, NE accumulates and dilates the pupil | The affected pupil **fails to dilate** (or dilates poorly) because there is insufficient NE release at the nerve terminal. Anisocoria increases. A post-cocaine anisocoria of ≥0.8–1.0 mm is considered positive. |
| **[[Apraclonidine]] 0.5–1% topical** (Iopidine) | Weak α1-agonist; in a denervated eye with upregulated α1-receptors, it causes mydriasis ("reversal of anisocoria") | The Horner pupil **dilates** (due to denervation supersensitivity), and the normal pupil may slightly constrict (α2-mediated inhibition of NE release). The anisocoria **reverses**. This is now the most commonly used confirmatory test due to the limited availability of cocaine. |

#### Step 2: Localization of the Lesion (Preganglionic vs. Postganglionic)

| Agent | Mechanism | Result: Preganglionic (2nd-order) Lesion | Result: Postganglionic (3rd-order/SCG) Lesion |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Hydroxyamphetamine]] 1% topical** (Paredrine) | Causes release of stored NE from intact postganglionic nerve terminals | The affected pupil **dilates** (postganglionic neuron is intact, NE stores are present) | The affected pupil **fails to dilate** (postganglionic neuron is damaged, NE stores are depleted) |
| **Dilute [[phenylephrine]] 1% topical** | Direct-acting α1-agonist; denervated receptors are supersensitive | Minimal or no dilation (receptors are not supersensitive because the postganglionic neuron is intact) | The affected pupil **dilates briskly** (denervation supersensitivity of α1-receptors on the dilator pupillae) |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Hydroxyamphetamine is often unavailable commercially. Dilute phenylephrine (1%) is an alternative, though its sensitivity and specificity are somewhat lower. The apraclonidine reversal test has become the most practical first-line test in many clinical settings.

### Neuroimaging

Once Horner syndrome is confirmed and localized, **neuroimaging** is essential to identify the underlying etiology:

- **Postganglionic (third-order) lesion (SCG or distal):** **MRI/MRA of the head and neck** with attention to the [[internal carotid artery]] (to rule out [[carotid dissection]]), [[cavernous sinus]], and skull base. CT angiography (CTA) may also be used.
- **Preganglionic (second-order) lesion:** **MRI of the chest, neck, and spine** (to evaluate the lung apex for [[Pancoast tumor]], mediastinum, and cervical spine).
- **First-order (central) lesion:** **MRI of the brain and brainstem** (to evaluate for [[stroke]], [[demyelination]], or mass lesion).

**Urgent imaging is mandatory** in any patient with acute-onset Horner syndrome, especially with ipsilateral head, neck, or face pain, as [[internal carotid artery dissection]] is a potentially life-threatening cause that requires immediate intervention.

### Additional Diagnostic Studies

- **Complete blood count, ESR, CRP:** If infection or inflammation is suspected
- **Chest X-ray:** To evaluate for apical lung mass (Pancoast tumor)
- **Carotid Doppler ultrasonography:** As an adjunct in suspected carotid dissection
- **Urine catecholamines (VMA, HVA):** In pediatric patients to rule out [[neuroblastoma]]

## Treatment and Management

### General Principles

The treatment of SCG-related pathology is directed at the **underlying etiology** rather than the sympathetic deficit itself. The signs of Horner syndrome (miosis, ptosis, anhidrosis) are generally mild and rarely require direct treatment. However, identification and management of the causative condition is critical, as some etiologies are life-threatening.

### Etiology-Specific Management

| Etiology | Management |
|---|---|
| **[[Internal carotid artery dissection]]** | Anticoagulation (heparin followed by warfarin) or antiplatelet therapy; endovascular intervention in select cases; urgent neurology/neurosurgery referral |
| **[[Pancoast tumor]] / lung apex mass** | Oncologic referral; chemotherapy, radiation, and/or surgical resection |
| **[[Neuroblastoma]] (pediatric)** | Pediatric oncology referral; chemotherapy, surgery, and/or radiation |
| **Traumatic SCG injury** | Observation; surgical exploration if indicated; sympathetic function may or may not recover |
| **Iatrogenic (post-surgical)** | Observation; most cases resolve spontaneously over weeks to months |
| **Infection/Inflammation** | Appropriate antimicrobial or anti-inflammatory therapy |
| **Idiopathic** | Observation with periodic reassessment; neuroimaging to exclude occult pathology |

### Symptomatic Management

- **Ptosis:** If cosmetically or functionally significant, [[phenylephrine]] 2.5% ophthalmic drops can be used as a temporary measure to stimulate Müller's muscle (though efficacy is variable and short-lived). Surgical options include [[Müller's muscle-conjunctival resection]] (MMCR) or [[levator advancement]] in refractory cases.
- **Miosis:** Rarely requires treatment. If pharmacologic dilation is needed (e.g., for fundus examination), standard [[mydriatic]] agents ([[tropicamide]], [[phenylephrine]] 2.5%) are effective.
- **Anhidrosis:** No specific treatment is typically required.

### Optometric Role and Follow-Up

The optometrist plays a pivotal role in:

1. **Detection:** Recognizing the subtle signs of Horner syndrome during routine examination (especially mild ptosis and anisocoria).
2. **Pharmacological confirmation and localization:** Performing apraclonidine, cocaine, hydroxyamphetamine, or dilute phenylephrine testing.
3. **Urgent referral:** Ensuring timely neuroimaging and specialist referral, particularly in acute-onset cases with pain (suspicion for carotid dissection) or in pediatric patients (suspicion for neuroblastoma).
4. **Monitoring:** Long-term follow-up of patients with known Horner syndrome, including monitoring for changes in pupil function, eyelid position, and [[intraocular pressure]].
5. **Patient education:** Counseling patients about the nature of their condition, the importance of follow-up, and warning signs that should prompt immediate medical attention.

## Embryology and Development

The SCG develops from [[neural crest cells]] that migrate from the dorsal [[neural tube]] during the fourth to sixth weeks of embryonic development. These cells aggregate along the developing [[sympathetic trunk]] and differentiate into postganglionic sympathetic neurons under the influence of signaling molecules including [[bone morphogenetic proteins]] (BMPs), [[nerve growth factor]] (NGF), and [[brain-derived neurotrophic factor]] (BDNF). The SCG is one of the earliest autonomic ganglia to form and has been extensively used as a model system in developmental neurobiology.

Disruption of SCG development (e.g., due to birth trauma, congenital tumors, or vascular anomalies) can result in **congenital Horner syndrome**, which is characterized by [[iris heterochromia]] (the affected iris is lighter due to failure of sympathetic-dependent melanocyte maturation) in addition to the classic triad.

## Comparative and Experimental Significance

The SCG has been one of the most extensively studied autonomic ganglia in neuroscience research. It has served as a model for:

- **Synaptic transmission:** Studies of cholinergic (nicotinic) ganglionic transmission
- **Neurotrophic factor biology:** The role of [[nerve growth factor]] (NGF) in neuronal survival and differentiation
- **Denervation supersensitivity:** Cannon's law and receptor upregulation
- **Autonomic pharmacology:** Testing of ganglionic blocking agents, sympathomimetics, and sympatholytics

These experimental insights have directly informed clinical pharmacology relevant to eye care, including the development of [[alpha-adrenergic agonists]] (e.g., [[brimonidine]], [[apraclonidine]]) and [[beta-adrenergic antagonists]] (e.g., [[timolol]], [[betaxolol]]) used in the management of [[glaucoma]] and [[ocular hypertension]].

| **Common causes** | Stroke, MS, tumor | Pancoast tumor, trauma, surgery | Carotid dissection, cavernous sinus lesion, cluster headache |

| **Dilute phenylephrine (1%)** | No/minimal dilation | No/minimal dilation | Brisk dilation (supersensitivity) |

## Prognosis

The prognosis of SCG-related Horner syndrome depends entirely on the underlying etiology:

- **Idiopathic and iatrogenic cases:** Often resolve spontaneously or remain stable with minimal functional impact.
- **Carotid dissection:** With appropriate anticoagulation/antiplatelet therapy, many patients recover sympathetic function over weeks to months, though some have permanent deficits. The risk of [[ischemic stroke]] is the primary concern.
- **Neoplastic causes:** Prognosis depends on the tumor type, stage, and response to treatment.
- **Congenital Horner syndrome:** Typically stable and non-progressive; iris heterochromia is permanent.
