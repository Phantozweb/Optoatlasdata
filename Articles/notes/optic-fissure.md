

# Optic Fissure

---

The **optic fissure** (also termed the **choroidal fissure**, **embryonic fissure**, or **fetal fissure**) is a critical transient groove that forms along the ventral (inferior) aspect of the developing [[optic cup]] and [[optic stalk]] during [[embryogenesis]]. This invagination serves as the conduit through which the [[hyaloid artery]] and its accompanying [[mesenchyme]] enter the developing [[eye]], and its timely closure is essential for the formation of a structurally intact globe. Failure of the optic fissure to close completely results in a spectrum of congenital ocular malformations collectively known as [[coloboma]], which can affect the [[iris]], [[ciliary body]], [[choroid]], [[retina]], and [[optic nerve]]. As such, the optic fissure occupies a central position in [[ocular embryology]], [[developmental biology]], and clinical [[ophthalmology]] and [[optometry]].

---

## Embryological Overview

### Formation of the Optic Vesicle and Optic Cup

During the fourth week of human [[gestation]] (approximately Carnegie stage 10–13), the [[neural plate]] folds to form the [[neural tube]]. Bilateral outpouchings of the [[prosencephalon]] (forebrain) give rise to the **[[optic vesicles]]**, which extend laterally toward the overlying surface [[ectoderm]]. Contact between the optic vesicle and the surface ectoderm induces the formation of the [[lens placode]], which subsequently invaginates to form the [[lens vesicle]].

Simultaneously, the optic vesicle itself undergoes a complex invagination process. The distal portion of the optic vesicle folds inward upon itself, transforming the single-layered vesicle into the double-layered **[[optic cup]]**. The inner layer of the optic cup differentiates into the [[neural retina]], while the outer layer becomes the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE).

### Genesis of the Optic Fissure

The invagination that creates the optic cup is not symmetric. It occurs predominantly along the **ventral (inferior)** surface, creating a linear groove that extends from the rim of the optic cup posteriorly along the ventral aspect of the [[optic stalk]]. This groove is the **optic fissure**. The fissure is lined by [[neuroepithelium]] on its inner margins and is flanked by the two edges (or lips) of the invaginating optic cup.

The optic fissure provides a vital passageway for:

- The **[[hyaloid artery]]** (a branch of the [[ophthalmic artery]]), which enters the optic cup to supply the developing [[lens]] and [[vitreous humor]]
- **[[Mesenchymal cells]]**, which migrate into the interior of the optic cup and contribute to the formation of the [[vitreous body]] and intraocular vasculature
- **[[Axons]]** of [[retinal ganglion cells]], which exit the eye through the region of the optic fissure to form the [[optic nerve]]

### Closure of the Optic Fissure

Closure of the optic fissure is a precisely orchestrated developmental event that begins during the **fifth week of gestation** (approximately Carnegie stage 14–15) and is typically complete by the **seventh week** (Carnegie stage 17–18). Closure initiates near the **equatorial region** of the optic cup (midpoint of the fissure) and proceeds bidirectionally—both anteriorly toward the rim of the cup (future [[pupillary margin]]) and posteriorly along the optic stalk toward the developing [[brain]].

The process of closure involves:

1. **Apposition** of the two lips (edges) of the fissure
2. **Dissolution of the [[basement membrane]]** at the fissure margins
3. **Cellular interdigitation and fusion** of the [[neuroepithelial]] cells from opposing edges
4. **Remodeling** of the fused tissue to establish continuity of the neural retina and RPE layers

Once closure is complete, the hyaloid artery becomes enclosed within the optic stalk (future optic nerve), and the ventral aspect of the eye achieves structural continuity. The portion of the hyaloid artery within the optic nerve persists as the **[[central retinal artery]]**, while the intraocular segments of the hyaloid vasculature normally regress before birth.

## Molecular and Genetic Regulation of Optic Fissure Closure

The closure of the optic fissure is governed by an intricate network of [[transcription factors]], [[signaling pathways]], and [[morphogens]]. Disruption of any of these molecular regulators can lead to incomplete closure and resultant [[coloboma]].

### Key Transcription Factors

| Transcription Factor | Role in Optic Fissure Closure | Associated Human Condition |
|---|---|---|
| **[[PAX2]]** | Expressed at the fissure margins; essential for fusion of the fissure lips | [[Renal-coloboma syndrome]] (Papillorenal syndrome) |
| **[[PAX6]]** | Master regulator of eye development; defines dorsal-ventral patterning | [[Aniridia]], [[Peters anomaly]] |
| **[[VAX1]] / [[VAX2]]** | Ventral optic cup specification; promotes fissure closure | Coloboma in animal models |
| **[[SHH]] (Sonic Hedgehog)** | Ventral midline signaling; influences [[PAX2]] expression | [[Holoprosencephaly]], [[cyclopia]] (excess), coloboma (deficiency) |
| **[[BMP7]]** | Bone morphogenetic protein signaling at fissure margins | Coloboma in knockout models |
| **[[TFAP2A]]** | Regulates [[neural crest cell]] migration and periocular mesenchyme | [[Branchio-oculo-facial syndrome]] |
| **[[CHD7]]** | Chromatin remodeling factor | [[CHARGE syndrome]] |
| **[[MAF]]** | Lens and anterior segment development | Coloboma with [[cataract]] |

### Signaling Pathways

- **[[Sonic Hedgehog (SHH) pathway]]**: SHH secreted from the ventral midline of the [[diencephalon]] and [[prechordal plate]] establishes ventral identity in the optic vesicle. It upregulates [[PAX2]] and [[VAX]] genes in the ventral optic cup and optic stalk, which are critical for fissure formation and subsequent closure.
- **[[BMP signaling]]**: [[Bone morphogenetic proteins]] (particularly [[BMP4]] and [[BMP7]]) are expressed in the dorsal optic cup and at the fissure margins. They interact with SHH signaling to establish the dorsal-ventral axis and regulate apoptosis and cell proliferation at the fissure edges.
- **[[Retinoic acid (RA) signaling]]**: [[Retinoic acid]], synthesized by [[RALDH]] enzymes in the periocular mesenchyme, plays a crucial role in ventral optic cup development and fissure closure. Deficiency of retinoic acid in animal models leads to persistent open fissure (coloboma).
- **[[Wnt signaling]]**: The [[Wnt/β-catenin pathway]] contributes to dorsal optic cup identity, and its dysregulation can alter the balance of dorsal-ventral patterning, indirectly affecting fissure closure.
- **[[FGF signaling]]**: [[Fibroblast growth factors]] from the surface ectoderm and lens influence neural retina differentiation and interact with other pathways to modulate fissure dynamics.

### Role of Periocular Mesenchyme and Neural Crest Cells

The **[[periocular mesenchyme]]** (POM), largely derived from [[neural crest cells]], surrounds the optic cup and plays a non-cell-autonomous role in fissure closure. These cells:

- Secrete [[extracellular matrix]] (ECM) components that facilitate basement membrane remodeling at the fissure margins
- Produce signaling molecules (including [[retinoic acid]]) that regulate neuroepithelial behavior
- Must be cleared from the fissure lumen to allow the opposing lips to appose and fuse

Abnormal persistence of mesenchymal tissue within the fissure, or defective neural crest cell migration, can physically obstruct closure and result in coloboma.

### Apoptosis and Basement Membrane Remodeling

Programmed cell death (**[[apoptosis]]**) at the fissure margins is essential for removing the [[basement membrane]] (composed of [[laminin]], [[collagen IV]], and other ECM molecules) that separates the two lips of the fissure. [[Matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs) and other proteolytic enzymes degrade this basement membrane, permitting direct cell-cell contact and fusion. Disruption of apoptotic signaling or MMP activity has been shown in animal models to prevent fissure closure.

## Pathophysiology: Failure of Optic Fissure Closure (Coloboma)

### Definition and Classification

When the optic fissure fails to close—either partially or completely—the resulting defect is termed a **[[coloboma]]** (from the Greek *koloboma*, meaning "curtailed" or "mutilated"). Colobomas are classified based on their anatomical location, which corresponds to the segment of the fissure that failed to close:

| Type of Coloboma | Anatomical Location | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Iris coloboma]]** | Anterior-most portion of the fissure (pupillary margin) | Keyhole-shaped pupil; cosmetic concern; photophobia |
| **[[Ciliary body coloboma]]** | Region of the ciliary body | May affect [[accommodation]]; often detected on [[gonioscopy]] or [[ultrasound biomicroscopy]] |
| **[[Chorioretinal coloboma]]** (Retinochoroidal) | Posterior segment involving [[choroid]] and [[retina]] | Visual field defects; risk of [[retinal detachment]]; may affect [[macula]] |
| **[[Optic disc coloboma]]** | Posterior-most portion of the fissure (optic nerve head) | Enlarged, excavated disc; reduced [[visual acuity]]; risk of [[serous macular detachment]] |
| **[[Complete coloboma]]** | Entire length of the fissure | Involves iris through optic nerve; severe visual impairment |

All typical colobomas are located in the **inferonasal** quadrant of the eye, corresponding to the anatomical position of the optic fissure. This inferonasal location is a key diagnostic feature that distinguishes true (typical) colobomas from atypical defects caused by other mechanisms.

### Isolated vs. Syndromic Coloboma

Colobomas may occur as:

- **Isolated (non-syndromic)**: Affecting only the eye, with no other systemic abnormalities. May be unilateral or bilateral.
- **Syndromic**: Associated with systemic malformations as part of a recognized genetic syndrome.

| Syndrome | Gene(s) Involved | Ocular Features | Systemic Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[CHARGE syndrome]]** | [[CHD7]] | Coloboma (iris, chorioretinal, optic disc) | Choanal atresia, heart defects, ear anomalies, growth retardation |
| **[[Renal-coloboma syndrome]]** (Papillorenal syndrome) | [[PAX2]] | Optic disc coloboma/dysplasia | Renal hypoplasia, vesicoureteral reflux |
| **[[Cat eye syndrome]]** | Trisomy/tetrasomy 22q11 | Iris coloboma | Anal atresia, preauricular tags, cardiac defects |
| **[[Treacher Collins syndrome]]** | [[TCOF1]], [[POLR1C]], [[POLR1D]] | Coloboma of lower eyelid | Mandibulofacial dysostosis |
| **[[Lenz microphthalmia syndrome]]** | [[BCOR]], [[NAA10]] | Coloboma, [[microphthalmia]] | Skeletal, cardiac, urogenital anomalies |
| **[[Kabuki syndrome]]** | [[KMT2D]], [[KDM6A]] | Coloboma | Intellectual disability, characteristic facies |

### Epidemiology

The prevalence of ocular coloboma (resulting from optic fissure closure failure) is estimated at approximately **0.5 to 2.6 per 10,000 live births**, though this varies by population and ascertainment method. Coloboma accounts for approximately **3.2–11.2%** of childhood blindness worldwide. There is no consistent sex predilection, and bilateral involvement occurs in approximately **33–60%** of cases.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Symptoms Related to Optic Fissure Closure Defects

The clinical manifestations of optic fissure closure failure depend on the extent and location of the colobomatous defect:

#### Visual Symptoms

- **Reduced [[visual acuity]]**: Particularly when the coloboma involves the [[macula]], [[optic disc]], or both. Visual acuity can range from near-normal to severe impairment ([[counting fingers]] or worse).
- **[[Visual field defects]]**: Chorioretinal colobomas produce [[scotomas]] corresponding to the area of absent retina and choroid. Superior visual field defects are characteristic due to the inferonasal location of the lesion.
- **[[Photophobia]]**: Iris colobomas allow excess light to enter the eye through the defect in the iris diaphragm.
- **[[Glare]]**: Related to the irregular pupil and impaired light regulation.
- **[[Nyctalopia]] (night blindness)**: May occur if significant retinal tissue is absent.

#### Cosmetic Concerns

- **Keyhole pupil**: The classic appearance of an iris coloboma, with the pupil extending inferonasally toward the [[limbus]].
- **[[Leukocoria]]**: A white pupillary reflex may be observed if a large chorioretinal coloboma is present, necessitating differentiation from [[retinoblastoma]] and other causes.
- **[[Microphthalmos]]**: The affected eye may be smaller than normal, as optic fissure closure defects are frequently associated with reduced overall globe size ([[microphthalmia]]).

#### Complications

- **[[Retinal detachment]]**: A significant risk in eyes with chorioretinal coloboma. The retina at the margins of the coloboma is thin and poorly supported, predisposing to [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]] or exudative detachment. Reported incidence ranges from **8–40%** in colobomatous eyes.
- **[[Serous macular detachment]]**: Particularly associated with optic disc colobomas, where fluid from the [[subarachnoid space]] or vitreous cavity may track beneath the [[neurosensory retina]].
- **[[Cataract]]**: May coexist due to shared developmental pathways or as part of a syndrome.
- **[[Amblyopia]]**: Especially in unilateral cases with significant visual impairment during the critical period of visual development.
- **[[Nystagmus]]**: May develop in bilateral cases with severe visual impairment.
- **[[Strabismus]]**: Secondary to reduced vision or anomalous binocular development.

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical Examination

| Examination Technique | Findings in Optic Fissure Defects |
|---|---|
| **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]** | Iris coloboma (inferonasal keyhole pupil); lens coloboma (notching of lens equator); anterior segment anomalies |
| **[[Direct ophthalmoscopy]]** | White, well-demarcated area of absent choroid and retina in the inferonasal fundus; enlarged/excavated optic disc |
| **[[Indirect ophthalmoscopy]]** | Panoramic view of chorioretinal coloboma extent; assessment of retinal detachment |
| **[[Gonioscopy]]** | Ciliary body coloboma; angle anomalies |
| **[[Pupillary examination]]** | Irregular pupil; possible [[relative afferent pupillary defect]] (RAPD) if optic nerve involved |
| **[[Refraction]]** | High [[refractive error]] (often [[myopia]] or [[astigmatism]]) common in colobomatous eyes |

### Imaging Studies

- **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)**: High-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the retina and optic nerve head. OCT can delineate the margins of chorioretinal colobomas, identify subclinical [[subretinal fluid]], and assess macular architecture. **Enhanced depth imaging OCT (EDI-OCT)** can evaluate choroidal thickness at coloboma margins.

- **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]**: Essential for evaluating the posterior segment when media opacities (e.g., cataract, vitreous hemorrhage) preclude direct visualization. B-scan reveals a characteristic **posterior excavation** or **cyst-like outpouching** of the globe wall at the site of the coloboma. Also critical for detecting associated [[retinal detachment]] and measuring [[axial length]].

- **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)**: High-frequency ultrasound imaging of the anterior segment. Useful for detecting ciliary body colobomas and assessing the [[zonular apparatus]] of the [[lens]].

- **[[Fundus photography]] and [[fundus autofluorescence]] (FAF)**: Documents the extent and characteristics of chorioretinal colobomas. FAF reveals **hypoautofluorescence** within the coloboma (absent RPE) and may show **hyperautofluorescent margins** indicating RPE stress or [[lipofuscin]] accumulation at the transition zone.

- **[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA)**: Demonstrates absence of [[choriocapillaris]] within the coloboma (hypofluorescence) and may reveal abnormal vasculature at the margins. Useful for detecting [[choroidal neovascularization]] (CNV), a rare but sight-threatening complication.

- **[[Visual field testing]] ([[Perimetry]])**: Automated or manual perimetry documents scotomas corresponding to the colobomatous defect. Typically reveals **superior visual field loss** due to the inferonasal retinal location.

- **[[Electroretinography]] (ERG)**: Full-field [[ERG]] may show reduced amplitudes proportional to the extent of retinal tissue loss. [[Multifocal ERG]] can map regional retinal function.

- **[[Magnetic resonance imaging]] (MRI)**: Indicated when optic nerve coloboma is suspected to evaluate for associated intracranial anomalies (e.g., [[basal encephalocele]], [[corpus callosum]] agenesis). Also useful in syndromic cases to assess brain and renal structures.

- **[[Genetic testing]]**: Chromosomal microarray, targeted gene panels (e.g., [[PAX2]], [[CHD7]], [[SHH]], [[PAX6]]), or whole-exome sequencing may be indicated, particularly in bilateral cases, syndromic presentations, or positive family history.

## Differential Diagnosis

The inferonasal location of typical coloboma is pathognomonic for optic fissure closure failure. However, several conditions must be differentiated:

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| **Atypical coloboma** | Located outside the inferonasal quadrant; not related to optic fissure |
| **[[Morning glory disc anomaly]]** | Funnel-shaped excavation of the optic disc with central glial tuft; distinct from optic disc coloboma |
| **[[Optic disc pit]]** | Small, oval depression in the optic disc, usually temporal; may cause serous macular detachment |
| **[[Retinoblastoma]]** | Leukocoria in children; solid intraocular mass on imaging |
| **[[Toxoplasma]] chorioretinal scar** | Acquired; pigmented borders; positive serology |
| **[[Optic nerve hypoplasia]]** | Small optic disc with [[double ring sign]]; no excavation |
| **[[Staphyloma]]** | Acquired ectasia of sclera and uvea; associated with high [[myopia]] or inflammation |

## Management and Treatment

### General Principles

There is currently **no treatment to reverse or repair** the structural defect caused by incomplete optic fissure closure. Management is directed toward:

1. **Optimizing visual function**
2. **Preventing and treating complications**
3. **Addressing cosmetic concerns**
4. **Genetic counseling and systemic evaluation**

### Optical and Visual Rehabilitation

- **[[Spectacle correction]]**: Accurate refraction and correction of significant refractive errors is paramount, particularly in children, to minimize [[amblyopia]] risk.
- **[[Contact lenses]]**: Cosmetic tinted or prosthetic contact lenses can mask iris colobomas and reduce photophobia and glare. Rigid gas-permeable or soft lenses may also correct irregular astigmatism.
- **[[Low vision aids]]**: Magnifiers, telescopic devices, and electronic assistive technology for patients with significant visual impairment.
- **[[Amblyopia therapy]]**: Patching or [[atropine penalization]] of the better-seeing eye in unilateral cases during the critical period of visual development (typically before age 7–8 years).

### Surgical Interventions

| Surgical Procedure | Indication |
|---|---|
| **[[Vitreoretinal surgery]]** (pars plana [[vitrectomy]], [[scleral buckle]], [[endolaser]], [[silicone oil]] or [[gas tamponade]]) | Retinal detachment associated with chorioretinal coloboma |
| **[[Iris repair]] / [[pupilloplasty]]** | Cosmetic reconstruction of iris coloboma; reduction of photophobia |
| **[[Intraocular lens]] (IOL) implantation** | Cataract extraction in colobomatous eyes (requires careful planning due to zonular and capsular anomalies) |
| **[[Anti-VEGF]] intravitreal injection** | Choroidal neovascularization at coloboma margins |
| **[[Laser photocoagulation]]** | Prophylactic treatment of retinal breaks at coloboma margins; treatment of CNV |
| **[[Prosthetic eye]] fitting** | Severe microphthalmos with non-functional eye |

#### Special Considerations in Retinal Detachment Surgery

Retinal detachment in colobomatous eyes is notoriously challenging to repair due to:

- Absence of RPE and choroid within the coloboma (no substrate for [[chorioretinal adhesion]])
- Thin, atrophic retina at the coloboma margins
- Frequent presence of retinal breaks within or at the edge of the coloboma
- Higher rates of [[proliferative vitreoretinopathy]] (PVR)

Surgical approaches often combine **pars plana vitrectomy** with **endolaser photocoagulation** around the coloboma margins, **internal tamponade** (gas or silicone oil), and sometimes **scleral buckling**. Anatomical success rates range from **50–80%** depending on the complexity, though functional visual outcomes may remain limited.

### Pharmacological Interventions

- **[[Atropine]] 0.01% or 1%**: For amblyopia penalization therapy in the sound eye.
- **[[Anti-VEGF agents]]** (e.g., [[bevacizumab]], [[ranibizumab]], [[aflibercept]]): For choroidal neovascularization complicating chorioretinal coloboma.
- **[[Topical antiglaucoma medications]]**: If secondary [[glaucoma]] develops (rare but reported in colobomatous eyes with angle anomalies).
- **[[Photochromic or tinted lenses]]**: To manage photophobia in iris coloboma.

### Genetic Counseling and Systemic Evaluation

Given the association of optic fissure closure defects with systemic syndromes, a comprehensive evaluation is recommended:

- **[[Genetic counseling]]**: Discussion of inheritance patterns (autosomal dominant, autosomal recessive, X-linked, or sporadic), recurrence risk, and available genetic testing.
- **[[Renal ultrasound]]**: To screen for renal anomalies (especially in suspected [[renal-coloboma syndrome]]).
- **[[Echocardiography]]**: To evaluate for cardiac defects (especially in [[CHARGE syndrome]]).
- **[[Audiological assessment]]**: Hearing evaluation in syndromic cases.
- **[[Neuroimaging]]**: MRI of the brain to assess for midline defects, [[corpus callosum]] anomalies, or [[basal encephalocele]].
- **[[Developmental assessment]]**: In children with bilateral coloboma and visual impairment, early intervention services and developmental monitoring are essential.

## Prognosis

The visual prognosis in optic fissure closure defects is highly variable and depends on:

- **Macular involvement**: Colobomas that spare the macula generally have better visual outcomes.
- **Optic nerve involvement**: Optic disc colobomas may significantly reduce visual acuity.
- **Extent of the defect**: Larger colobomas involving more retinal tissue carry worse prognosis.
- **Complications**: Retinal detachment, if not successfully repaired, leads to profound vision loss.
- **Bilaterality**: Bilateral involvement increases the risk of significant visual disability.
- **Amblyopia management**: Early and aggressive amblyopia treatment in unilateral cases can improve outcomes.

Studies suggest that approximately **50–60%** of eyes with chorioretinal coloboma maintain visual acuity of **20/200 or better**, while eyes with macular or optic disc involvement may have acuity of **20/400 or worse**.

## Research Directions and Future Perspectives

Active areas of research related to the optic fissure include:

- **[[Stem cell therapy]]**: Investigating the potential for retinal progenitor cell or RPE cell transplantation to restore tissue within colobomatous defects.
- **[[Gene therapy]]**: Targeted correction of specific genetic mutations (e.g., [[PAX2]], [[CHD7]]) in animal models, with potential future translation to human therapy.
- **[[CRISPR/Cas9]] genome editing**: Precise correction of coloboma-associated mutations in preclinical models.
- **[[Organoid models]]**: Human [[retinal organoids]] derived from [[induced pluripotent stem cells]] (iPSCs) are being used to model optic fissure closure in vitro and study the molecular mechanisms of closure failure.
- **[[Zebrafish]] and [[mouse]] models**: These animal models continue to provide invaluable insights into the genetic and molecular regulation of optic fissure closure, enabling identification of novel candidate genes and therapeutic targets.
- **[[Neuroprotective strategies]]**: Investigating pharmacological agents that may preserve retinal ganglion cell function in eyes with optic nerve coloboma.

---

## Summary

The optic fissure is a transient but indispensable structure in ocular embryogenesis, serving as the gateway for vascular and mesenchymal ingress into the developing eye. Its closure is a tightly regulated process involving coordinated signaling from multiple molecular pathways, transcription factors, and cellular mechanisms. Failure of optic fissure closure results in coloboma—a congenital defect with significant visual and systemic implications. Optometrists and ophthalmologists play a critical role in the early detection, comprehensive evaluation, visual rehabilitation, and long-term monitoring of patients with optic fissure closure defects. Advances in genetics, imaging, and regenerative medicine hold promise for improved understanding and future therapeutic interventions.
