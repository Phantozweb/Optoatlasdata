

# Relative Accommodation

**Relative Accommodation** is a fundamental clinical measurement in [[optometry]] and [[physiological optics]] that quantifies the range over which the [[accommodative system]] can increase or decrease its dioptric power while maintaining clear, single [[binocular vision]] at a fixed working distance. It is subdivided into two complementary components: **Positive Relative Accommodation (PRA)** and **Negative Relative Accommodation (NRA)**. Together, these values define the flexibility and reserve capacity of the [[crystalline lens]] and [[ciliary muscle]] complex to adjust focus without altering [[vergence]] demand, thereby serving as a critical indicator of visual comfort, binocular stability, and the adequacy of a patient's refractive correction—particularly for sustained near-point tasks such as reading, computer use, and academic work.

Unlike absolute [[accommodation]], which measures the total dioptric range from the [[far point]] to the [[near point]] of the eye, relative accommodation isolates the accommodative component that can be varied independently of the [[convergence]] system at a single, fixed target distance. This distinction is clinically indispensable because it directly reflects the interplay between the [[accommodation-convergence relationship]] ([[AC/A ratio]]) and the patient's ability to sustain comfortable near vision. Abnormalities in relative accommodation are among the earliest and most sensitive indicators of [[accommodative dysfunction]], [[binocular vision disorders]], and inappropriate refractive prescriptions.

---

## Historical Background

The concept of relative accommodation was first formally described by **Franciscus Cornelis Donders** in his landmark 1864 treatise *On the Anomalies of Accommodation and Refraction of the Eye*. Donders recognized that accommodation and convergence, while neurologically linked through the [[near triad]] (accommodation, convergence, and [[miosis]]), are not rigidly coupled—there exists a zone of flexibility within which one can be varied while the other is held relatively constant. This observation laid the groundwork for the clinical measurement of PRA and NRA and profoundly influenced the development of modern [[binocular vision]] assessment.

Subsequent contributions by **Ernst Maddox**, **Alexander Duane**, and later **Morgan** (who established normative data in the 1940s) refined the clinical methodology and established the expected values that remain in widespread use today. The concept was further integrated into the **Sheard** and **Percival** criteria for prescribing comfortable refractive corrections, and it remains a cornerstone of the **OEP (Optometric Extension Program)** analytical system and the **Morgan norms** used in contemporary clinical practice.

## Definitions and Terminology

### Positive Relative Accommodation (PRA)

**Positive Relative Accommodation (PRA)** represents the maximum amount by which the patient can *increase* (stimulate) [[accommodation]] beyond the baseline demand at a fixed near-point distance while maintaining clear, single binocular vision. It is measured by introducing progressively stronger [[minus lenses]] (concave lenses) over the patient's habitual near correction at a standard working distance (typically 40 cm). The minus lenses create an artificial demand for additional accommodative effort. The endpoint is reached when the patient reports the first sustained [[blur]] of the near target.

PRA reflects the **reserve accommodative capacity** available to the patient and is primarily limited by the maximum contractile ability of the [[ciliary muscle]] and the elasticity of the [[crystalline lens]]. A reduced PRA may indicate [[accommodative insufficiency]], [[accommodative fatigue]], or an over-plussed refractive correction.

**Expected Normal Value:** −2.37 D ± 1.00 D (Morgan norms); commonly cited as approximately **−2.50 D** in clinical practice.

### Negative Relative Accommodation (NRA)

**Negative Relative Accommodation (NRA)** represents the maximum amount by which the patient can *decrease* (relax) accommodation below the baseline demand at the same fixed near-point distance while maintaining clear, single binocular vision. It is measured by introducing progressively stronger [[plus lenses]] (convex lenses) over the patient's habitual near correction. The plus lenses reduce the accommodative demand, effectively relaxing the ciliary muscle. The endpoint is again the first sustained blur.

NRA reflects the ability to **relax accommodation** and is primarily limited by the patient's [[depth of focus]], [[pupil size]], and the degree of [[accommodative spasm]] or [[latent hyperopia]] present. A reduced NRA may indicate [[accommodative excess]], [[accommodative spasm]], or an under-plussed refractive correction.

**Expected Normal Value:** +2.00 D ± 0.50 D (Morgan norms); commonly cited as approximately **+2.00 D to +2.50 D**.

### Total Relative Accommodation

The **total relative accommodation** is the arithmetic sum of the absolute values of PRA and NRA, representing the complete range of accommodative flexibility available at the fixed working distance. For example, if PRA = −2.50 D and NRA = +2.00 D, the total relative accommodation is 4.50 D. This total range provides a comprehensive picture of accommodative reserve and flexibility.

## Pathophysiology

### Neuroanatomical Basis

Relative accommodation is governed by the complex neurophysiological interplay between the [[parasympathetic nervous system]] and the [[sympathetic nervous system]] as they innervate the structures of the [[accommodative apparatus]].

The primary effector of accommodation is the [[ciliary muscle]], a ring of smooth muscle located within the [[ciliary body]]. When the ciliary muscle contracts (primarily via [[parasympathetic]] innervation from the [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]] traveling along the [[oculomotor nerve]] (CN III) and synapsing in the [[ciliary ganglion]]), the [[zonular fibers]] (zonules of Zinn) relax, allowing the elastic [[crystalline lens]] to assume a more convex shape, thereby increasing its refractive power. This process constitutes **positive accommodation** (stimulation). Conversely, when the ciliary muscle relaxes, the zonular fibers become taut and flatten the lens, reducing its refractive power—this is **negative accommodation** (relaxation), which is facilitated in part by [[sympathetic innervation]] from the superior cervical ganglion.

### The Accommodation-Convergence Linkage

Accommodation and [[convergence]] are neurologically linked through the [[near triad]]. For every unit of accommodation exerted, a predictable amount of convergence is generated (the [[accommodative convergence]], measured as the [[AC/A ratio]]). Similarly, convergence can drive accommodation through the [[CA/C ratio]] (convergence-accommodation). Relative accommodation specifically measures the range over which accommodation can be varied *without* changing the convergence demand—that is, the patient's eyes remain converged on the fixed near target while only the accommodative state changes.

This is possible because the linkage between accommodation and convergence is not rigid. There exists a zone of **relative flexibility** (sometimes called the **zone of clear single binocular vision**, or ZCSBV) within which the two systems can be partially dissociated. The width of this zone is determined by:

1. **[[Fusional vergence]] reserves** — The ability of the vergence system to compensate for changes in accommodative vergence. When minus lenses are added (PRA testing), accommodative convergence increases, and the patient must exert [[negative fusional vergence]] (divergence) to maintain single vision. When plus lenses are added (NRA testing), accommodative convergence decreases, and the patient must exert [[positive fusional vergence]] (convergence) to maintain single vision.

2. **Accommodative amplitude and flexibility** — The total range and speed of the accommodative response.

3. **Depth of focus** — The dioptric range over which the retinal image remains acceptably clear, influenced by [[pupil diameter]], [[optical aberrations]], and neural processing.

### Pathophysiological Mechanisms of Abnormal Relative Accommodation

| Condition | PRA | NRA | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Accommodative Insufficiency]] | Reduced | Normal or slightly elevated | Inability of the [[ciliary muscle]] to generate sufficient contractile force; reduced [[amplitude of accommodation]] |
| [[Accommodative Excess]] / [[Accommodative Spasm]] | Normal or elevated | Reduced | Inability to relax the ciliary muscle; tonic over-accommodation; parasympathetic hypertonicity |
| [[Accommodative Infacility]] | May be reduced | May be reduced | Sluggish or delayed accommodative response in both directions; reduced speed of ciliary muscle contraction and relaxation |
| Over-plussed near correction | Reduced | Elevated | Excessive plus power reduces accommodative demand, leaving excess relaxation capacity but depleting stimulation reserve |
| Under-plussed near correction / Uncorrected [[hyperopia]] | Elevated | Reduced | Insufficient plus correction forces chronic accommodative effort, depleting relaxation reserve |
| [[Convergence Insufficiency]] | Variable | Variable | Altered vergence-accommodation interaction; may show reduced PRA due to limited negative fusional vergence |
| [[Convergence Excess]] | Variable | Variable | Excessive convergence response may limit NRA through vergence-accommodation coupling |
| [[Presbyopia]] | Progressively reduced | May be relatively preserved initially | Age-related loss of [[crystalline lens]] elasticity and ciliary muscle efficiency reduces total accommodative range |

The balance between PRA and NRA is a sensitive barometer of the accommodative-vergence equilibrium. When this balance is disrupted—whether by refractive error, binocular dysfunction, or age-related changes—patients typically present with characteristic symptom profiles.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Patients with abnormal relative accommodation values may present with a wide spectrum of symptoms, most of which are exacerbated by prolonged near-point tasks. The symptom profile depends on whether PRA, NRA, or both are affected.

### Symptoms Associated with Reduced PRA

- **Blurred vision at near** — Difficulty maintaining clear focus during reading or close work
- **Asthenopia** ([[eye strain]]) — Aching, pulling, or tired sensation in or around the eyes
- **Headaches** — Typically frontal or temporal, worsening with sustained near work
- **Difficulty sustaining near focus** — Print may initially be clear but becomes blurred after minutes of reading
- **Avoidance of near tasks** — Particularly in children, who may resist reading or homework
- **Reduced reading comprehension and speed** — Secondary to intermittent blur

### Symptoms Associated with Reduced NRA

- **Blurred distance vision after near work** — Difficulty clearing distance targets after prolonged reading (pseudomyopia)
- **Intermittent [[diplopia]]** — Particularly at distance following near tasks
- **Variable or fluctuating vision** — Refractive findings may vary between examinations
- **Night myopia symptoms** — Exacerbated in low-light conditions where parasympathetic tone increases
- **Headaches and asthenopia** — Similar to reduced PRA but often more associated with distance blur

### Symptoms Associated with Reduced PRA and NRA (Accommodative Infacility)

- **Difficulty shifting focus** between near and distance (e.g., copying from a whiteboard to a notebook)
- **Slow reading speed** with frequent loss of place
- **General visual fatigue** with any sustained visual task
- **Transient blur** when changing fixation distances

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Measurement Technique

Relative accommodation is measured as part of a comprehensive [[binocular vision assessment]], typically performed during the [[refraction]] or near-point evaluation portion of the eye examination. The test is conducted binocularly with the patient's best-corrected distance prescription in place (in a [[phoropter]] or [[trial frame]]), with a near-point card positioned at the standard working distance of **40 cm** (16 inches).

#### Step-by-Step Procedure for NRA Measurement

1. Position a high-contrast near acuity target (typically a line of [[Snellen]] or [[logMAR]] letters one to two lines above the patient's best near acuity) at 40 cm.
2. Ensure the patient's habitual or best-corrected distance prescription is in place binocularly.
3. Instruct the patient to report when the letters first become blurred and remain blurred (sustained blur, not transient).
4. Add **plus lenses** in **+0.25 D steps** binocularly and simultaneously.
5. Record the total amount of plus power added at the point of first sustained blur.
6. The NRA value is recorded as a **positive number** (e.g., +2.00 D).

#### Step-by-Step Procedure for PRA Measurement

1. Remove any additional lenses added during NRA testing, returning to the baseline correction.
2. Using the same target at 40 cm, add **minus lenses** in **−0.25 D steps** binocularly and simultaneously.
3. Instruct the patient to report first sustained blur.
4. Record the total amount of minus power added at the point of first sustained blur.
5. The PRA value is recorded as a **negative number** (e.g., −2.50 D).

**Clinical Note:** NRA is conventionally measured before PRA to avoid fatiguing the accommodative system, which could artificially reduce the PRA finding.

### Normative Values (Morgan Norms)

| Parameter | Expected Value | Standard Deviation |
|---|---|---|
| NRA | +2.00 D | ± 0.50 D |
| PRA | −2.37 D | ± 1.00 D |

### Interpretation of Results

| Finding | PRA | NRA | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Normal | −2.00 to −3.00 D | +1.50 to +2.50 D | Adequate accommodative reserve and flexibility |
| Low PRA, Normal NRA | < −1.50 D | Normal | Suggests [[accommodative insufficiency]] or over-plussed Rx |
| Normal PRA, Low NRA | Normal | < +1.50 D | Suggests [[accommodative excess]]/spasm or under-plussed Rx |
| Low PRA, Low NRA | < −1.50 D | < +1.50 D | Suggests [[accommodative infacility]] or general accommodative dysfunction |
| High PRA, Low NRA | > −3.50 D | < +1.50 D | Suggests latent hyperopia or under-correction |
| Low PRA, High NRA | < −1.50 D | > +2.50 D | Suggests over-correction or early [[presbyopia]] |
| Equal PRA and NRA | Balanced | Balanced | Suggests well-centered accommodative posture relative to the Rx |

### Relationship to Other Clinical Findings

Relative accommodation values should always be interpreted in the context of the complete binocular vision workup, including:

- **[[Amplitude of Accommodation]]** — Measured monocularly (push-up or minus lens method); reduced amplitude limits PRA.
- **[[Accommodative Facility]]** — Measured with ± 2.00 D flipper lenses; reduced facility correlates with reduced PRA and/or NRA.
- **[[Accommodative Response]] / [[Accommodative Lag]]** — Measured with [[dynamic retinoscopy]] (e.g., [[Monocular Estimation Method (MEM)]] or [[Nott retinoscopy]]); excessive lag suggests accommodative insufficiency; lead suggests accommodative excess.
- **[[Fusional Vergence Ranges]]** — [[Base-in]] and [[base-out]] vergence ranges at near; limited fusional reserves may cause the PRA or NRA endpoint to be reached due to vergence breakdown (diplopia) rather than blur.
- **[[AC/A Ratio]]** — A high AC/A ratio means that small changes in accommodation produce large changes in convergence, potentially limiting relative accommodation range.
- **[[Phoria]] at near** — [[Esophoria]] may limit PRA (as additional accommodation drives further convergence); [[exophoria]] may limit NRA.
- **[[Binocular Cross-Cylinder (BCC) Test]]** — Also known as the fused cross-cylinder test; determines the accommodative posture at near and helps identify the optimal near add. The BCC finding should be consistent with the balance between PRA and NRA.

### Diagnostic Significance in Prescribing

One of the most important clinical applications of relative accommodation is in **verifying and optimizing the near-point prescription**. The principle is as follows:

- An **ideal near prescription** should produce approximately **equal PRA and NRA values**, indicating that the accommodative system is centered within its range of flexibility.
- If NRA is significantly greater than PRA, the prescription may contain **too much plus power** (over-plussed), shifting the accommodative posture toward the relaxation end of the range.
- If PRA is significantly greater than NRA, the prescription may contain **too little plus power** (under-plussed), shifting the accommodative posture toward the stimulation end.

This principle is particularly important when prescribing [[progressive addition lenses]] (PALs), [[bifocals]], or near-point adds for [[presbyopic]] patients, as well as when determining the optimal correction for pre-presbyopic patients with accommodative or binocular vision disorders.

## Differential Diagnosis

When abnormal relative accommodation values are encountered, the clinician must differentiate among several potential etiologies:

| Condition | Key Differentiating Features |
|---|---|
| [[Accommodative Insufficiency]] | Reduced PRA, reduced amplitude of accommodation, high accommodative lag on MEM, receded near point |
| [[Accommodative Excess]] | Reduced NRA, low or lead on MEM, variable refraction, pseudomyopia, difficulty relaxing accommodation |
| [[Accommodative Infacility]] | Reduced PRA and NRA, reduced accommodative facility (< 8 cpm binocularly with ±2.00 D flippers) |
| [[Latent Hyperopia]] | Reduced NRA, significant difference between manifest and cycloplegic refraction |
| Inappropriate Refractive Correction | Asymmetric PRA/NRA; corrected by adjusting the prescription |
| [[Convergence Insufficiency]] | May show reduced PRA; receded [[near point of convergence]], reduced base-out vergence at near, exophoria at near |
| [[Convergence Excess]] | May show reduced NRA; high esophoria at near, high AC/A ratio |
| Early [[Presbyopia]] | Progressively reduced PRA with age; reduced amplitude of accommodation |

## Management and Treatment

### Optical Correction (Lens-Based Management)

The first-line management of abnormal relative accommodation is **optimization of the refractive correction**:

1. **Adjusting the spherical component** — If PRA and NRA are significantly unequal, the near prescription should be modified to balance them. Adding plus power increases NRA and decreases PRA; reducing plus power (or adding minus) increases PRA and decreases NRA.

2. **Prescribing a near add** — For patients with reduced PRA (especially those with [[accommodative insufficiency]] or early [[presbyopia]]), a plus add for near work reduces the accommodative demand and shifts the working point to a more comfortable position within the relative accommodation range.

3. **Reducing an excessive near add** — For patients with reduced NRA who are wearing an overly strong near add, reducing the add power restores accommodative flexibility.

4. **[[Anti-fatigue lenses]]** — Low-powered progressive or occupational lenses (e.g., +0.50 to +0.75 D near support) may benefit pre-presbyopic patients with mildly reduced PRA.

### Vision Therapy (Accommodative Training)

For patients with accommodative dysfunction underlying their abnormal relative accommodation findings, **[[vision therapy]]** (orthoptic exercises / accommodative training) is the gold-standard treatment:

#### Accommodative Insufficiency (Reduced PRA)

- **Minus lens stimulation** — Progressive minus lens loading at near to build accommodative stamina
- **Push-up accommodative training** — Bringing a near target progressively closer to stimulate maximum accommodation
- **[[Hart chart]] exercises** — Alternating fixation between near and distance letter charts to improve accommodative amplitude and facility
- **Accommodative facility training with minus flippers** — Using −2.00 D or progressively stronger minus lenses to train accommodative stimulation

#### Accommodative Excess (Reduced NRA)

- **Plus lens relaxation** — Progressive plus lens loading at near to train accommodative relaxation
- **Distance rock exercises** — Emphasizing the relaxation phase of accommodative facility
- **[[Brock string]] with accommodative component** — Combining vergence and accommodative relaxation training
- **Cognitive relaxation techniques** — Teaching the patient to consciously relax the accommodative system

#### Accommodative Infacility (Reduced PRA and NRA)

- **Binocular accommodative facility training** — Using ±2.00 D flipper lenses with progressive difficulty
- **Computer-based accommodative training programs** — Software that systematically varies accommodative demand
- **Combined lens and prism facility exercises** — Integrating accommodative and vergence flexibility training

### Typical Vision Therapy Protocol

| Phase | Duration | Activities | Goal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 (Monocular) | 2–4 weeks | Monocular accommodative facility, lens sorting, Hart chart | Establish monocular accommodative control |
| Phase 2 (Binocular) | 4–6 weeks | Binocular facility flippers, Brock string, near-far rock | Integrate binocular accommodative-vergence flexibility |
| Phase 3 (Integration) | 2–4 weeks | Sustained reading with periodic facility checks, real-world tasks | Automatize skills for daily visual demands |
| Maintenance | Ongoing | Home-based exercises 3–5 minutes daily | Prevent regression |

### Pharmacological Interventions

Pharmacological management is rarely the primary treatment for relative accommodation abnormalities but may be employed in specific circumstances:

- **[[Cycloplegic]] agents** (e.g., [[atropine]], [[cyclopentolate]]) — Used diagnostically to unmask [[latent hyperopia]] or therapeutically in short courses to break [[accommodative spasm]]. Low-dose atropine (0.01%–0.05%) has been used in cases of refractory accommodative excess.
- **[[Pilocarpine]]** — A [[parasympathomimetic]] agent that stimulates the ciliary muscle; rarely used therapeutically for accommodative insufficiency due to side effects but may have limited application in specific cases.
- **Topical [[NSAIDs]]** — May reduce ciliary muscle inflammation or discomfort in cases of ciliary spasm secondary to inflammatory conditions.

### Ergonomic and Environmental Modifications

- **Proper working distance** — Maintaining a 40 cm (Harmon distance) working distance reduces accommodative demand
- **Adequate lighting** — Proper illumination increases [[depth of focus]] through pupillary constriction, reducing accommodative stress
- **The 20-20-20 rule** — Every 20 minutes, look at a target 20 feet away for 20 seconds to relax accommodation
- **Screen positioning** — Computer monitors should be positioned at or slightly below eye level, at arm's length distance
- **Regular breaks** — Periodic interruption of sustained near work to prevent accommodative fatigue

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Patients

Relative accommodation testing is particularly valuable in the pediatric population, where accommodative and binocular vision disorders are common causes of reading difficulty, academic underperformance, and attention problems that may be misattributed to learning disabilities or [[attention deficit disorder]]. Children typically have large amplitudes of accommodation and should demonstrate robust PRA values. A reduced PRA in a child is a significant finding that warrants thorough investigation.

### Presbyopic Patients

In [[presbyopia]], the progressive loss of [[crystalline lens]] elasticity and ciliary muscle efficiency results in a systematic reduction in total relative accommodation. PRA is typically affected earlier and more severely than NRA. The measurement of relative accommodation in presbyopic patients is essential for:

- Determining the appropriate **near addition** power
- Balancing the add to maximize the range of clear near vision
- Monitoring progression of presbyopia over time

### Post-Refractive Surgery Patients

Patients who have undergone [[LASIK]], [[PRK]], or other [[refractive surgery]] procedures may demonstrate altered relative accommodation values due to changes in corneal optics, higher-order aberrations, or altered depth of focus. Monovision corrections (one eye corrected for distance, one for near) fundamentally alter the binocular accommodative-vergence relationship and require careful assessment of relative accommodation.

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis for patients with abnormal relative accommodation is generally excellent when the underlying cause is identified and appropriately managed:

- **Refractive causes** — Immediate improvement with prescription optimization
- **Accommodative insufficiency** — Vision therapy success rates of 85–95% reported in the literature (CITT studies)
- **Accommodative excess** — Good response to relaxation therapy and appropriate lens management; accommodative spasm may require longer treatment courses
- **Accommodative infacility** — Responds well to systematic facility training; most patients achieve normal values within 8–12 weeks of therapy
- **Presbyopia** — Progressive condition requiring periodic add adjustments; relative accommodation testing guides optimal add selection

---

## Summary

Relative accommodation—comprising PRA and NRA—is an indispensable clinical measurement that provides unique insight into the accommodative reserve, flexibility, and balance of the visual system at a fixed working distance. Its measurement is technically straightforward, requiring only a [[phoropter]] or [[trial frame]], a near-point card, and a systematic lens addition protocol. The clinical value of relative accommodation extends far beyond a simple numerical finding: it serves as a diagnostic indicator of accommodative and binocular dysfunction, a guide for refractive prescription optimization, a baseline for vision therapy planning, and a monitor of treatment progress. Every comprehensive optometric examination should include assessment of relative accommodation, particularly in patients presenting with near-point symptoms, binocular vision complaints, or suspected accommodative disorders.
