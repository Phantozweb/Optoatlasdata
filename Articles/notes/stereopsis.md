

# Stereopsis

**Stereopsis** (from Greek *stereos* meaning "solid" and *opsis* meaning "vision") is the perception of depth and three-dimensional structure obtained on the basis of visual information deriving from two eyes by individuals with normally developed [[binocular vision]]. It is the highest grade of binocular function in the hierarchy described by Worth, following simultaneous perception and [[fusional vergence]]. Stereopsis arises from the horizontal disparity between the slightly different retinal images projected onto each eye due to the lateral separation of the eyes, known as the [[interpupillary distance]] (IPD). The neural processing of these binocular disparities in the [[visual cortex]] yields a vivid, quantifiable sense of relative depth that is unavailable through monocular cues alone.

Clinically, stereopsis serves as a critical biomarker for the integrity of the entire binocular visual system. Its measurement is a cornerstone of optometric and ophthalmologic examination, particularly in the assessment of [[strabismus]], [[amblyopia]], and [[anisometropia]]. Deficient or absent stereopsis may indicate disruption at any level of the binocular pathway—from optical input to cortical processing—and carries significant functional implications for tasks requiring fine depth judgment.

## Neuroanatomy and Pathophysiology

### Optical Basis of Binocular Disparity

Stereopsis is fundamentally rooted in the geometric consequence of having two horizontally separated eyes. The average adult [[interpupillary distance]] is approximately 60–65 mm, meaning that each [[retina]] receives a slightly different projection of the visual scene. Points in space that do not fall on the [[horopter]]—the theoretical curved surface of points in space that project to corresponding retinal points in each eye—produce **binocular retinal disparity**. Objects closer than the horopter produce **crossed disparity** (temporal retinal displacement), while objects farther than the horopter produce **uncrossed disparity** (nasal retinal displacement).

The region surrounding the horopter within which single binocular vision is maintained despite small disparities is known as **[[Panum's fusional area]]**. Disparities falling within Panum's area are fused into a single percept with an associated depth signal; disparities exceeding this area result in [[diplopia]] or suppression.

### Neural Processing Pathway

The neural substrate for stereopsis involves a hierarchical processing chain:

1. **Retinal Input**: Photoreceptors in each [[retina]] transduce the optical image. The quality of the retinal image (affected by [[refractive error]], media opacities, or retinal pathology) directly influences the quality of stereoscopic input.

2. **Lateral Geniculate Nucleus (LGN)**: Signals from each eye are relayed via the [[optic nerve]], partially decussate at the [[optic chiasm]], and project to separate laminae of the [[lateral geniculate nucleus]] (LGN) in the [[thalamus]]. The LGN maintains ocular segregation—monocular signals from the ipsilateral and contralateral eyes are processed in distinct layers (layers 2, 3, and 5 for ipsilateral; layers 1, 4, and 6 for contralateral).

3. **Primary Visual Cortex (V1)**: Binocular convergence first occurs in layer IV of the [[primary visual cortex]] (area V1, or [[Brodmann area 17]]). Here, **binocular disparity-selective neurons** (also called disparity-tuned neurons) respond preferentially to specific magnitudes and directions of retinal disparity. These neurons are classified as:
   - **Tuned excitatory (T0)** cells: maximal response at zero disparity
   - **Tuned near** cells: maximal response to crossed disparities
   - **Tuned far** cells: maximal response to uncrossed disparities
   - **Near (NE)** cells: broad excitation for crossed disparities
   - **Far (FA)** cells: broad excitation for uncrossed disparities

4. **Extrastriate Cortex (V2, V3, V3A, MT/V5)**: Higher-order processing of disparity signals occurs in extrastriate areas. Area [[V2]] processes relative disparity (the difference in disparity between two objects), which is more ecologically relevant than absolute disparity. Area **V3A** and the **[[middle temporal area]] (MT/V5)** integrate disparity with motion signals, contributing to stereomotion perception and the perception of three-dimensional structure from motion.

5. **Ventral and Dorsal Stream Integration**: The [[ventral stream]] ("what" pathway) utilizes disparity information for fine stereoacuity and object recognition, while the [[dorsal stream]] ("where/how" pathway) uses coarser disparity signals for spatial navigation, reaching, and grasping.

### Critical Period and Development

Stereopsis is not present at birth. Its development is contingent upon the maturation of binocular cortical neurons during a **[[critical period]]** of visual development. Key developmental milestones include:

| Age | Developmental Milestone |
|---|---|
| Birth–2 months | No measurable stereopsis; monocular fixation predominates |
| 3–5 months | Onset of coarse stereopsis (crossed disparities detected first) |
| 6–12 months | Rapid improvement; stereoacuity approaches 1 arc minute in some infants |
| 1–3 years | Continued refinement of fine stereopsis |
| 3–7 years | Stereoacuity reaches adult levels (~20–40 arc seconds); critical period remains open |
| 7–9 years | Critical period for binocular development begins to close |

Disruption of binocular input during the critical period—due to [[infantile esotropia]], [[congenital cataract]], [[anisometropia]], or prolonged monocular occlusion—can permanently impair or abolish stereopsis by preventing the normal development of binocular disparity-tuned neurons. This is the pathophysiological basis of **stereoscopic deficiency in [[amblyopia]]**.

### Classification of Stereopsis

Stereopsis can be classified by the type of visual information used:

- **Local (fine) stereopsis**: Based on point-by-point matching of features between the two retinal images. Requires high spatial frequency information and is mediated primarily by the [[parvocellular pathway]]. Measured by contour-based stereotests (e.g., [[Titmus test]], [[Wirt circles]]).

- **Global stereopsis**: Based on the extraction of disparity from random-dot patterns where no monocular form cues exist. Requires cortical integration of large populations of disparity-tuned neurons. Measured by random-dot stereotests (e.g., [[TNO stereotest]], [[Randot stereotest]], [[Lang stereotest]]). Global stereopsis is considered a more rigorous measure of true binocular depth perception because it eliminates monocular cues.

- **Coarse stereopsis**: Detection of large disparities (>200 arc seconds), often preserved in patients with mild binocular anomalies or partially treated strabismus.

- **Fine stereopsis**: Detection of small disparities (<60 arc seconds), indicative of high-grade binocular function and precise cortical disparity processing.

## Clinical Significance and Functional Implications

Stereopsis is essential for numerous daily activities and occupational tasks requiring precise depth judgment. Its clinical significance extends across multiple domains:

### Activities Dependent on Stereopsis

| Activity | Stereoacuity Requirement |
|---|---|
| Threading a needle | ≤40 arc seconds |
| Pouring liquid into a container | ≤60 arc seconds |
| Driving (lane judgment, parking) | ≤80 arc seconds |
| Ball sports (catching, batting) | ≤60 arc seconds |
| Microsurgery | ≤40 arc seconds |
| Piloting aircraft | ≤25–40 arc seconds |
| General navigation | ≤200 arc seconds (coarse) |

Individuals with absent or significantly reduced stereopsis may compensate using **monocular depth cues** including [[motion parallax]], relative size, [[aerial perspective]], texture gradients, occlusion, and accommodation. However, these cues are inferior to binocular stereopsis for tasks requiring fine depth discrimination at near and intermediate distances (within approximately 6 meters, beyond which binocular disparity becomes negligibly small).

### Occupational Standards

Several professions mandate minimum stereoacuity thresholds:

- **Commercial aviation pilots**: Typically require demonstrable stereopsis (often ≤40 arc seconds)
- **Military service**: Variable standards; many branches require ≤60 arc seconds
- **Law enforcement**: Often require functional stereopsis
- **Microsurgeons**: Fine stereopsis is considered essential for operative precision

## Etiology of Stereopsis Deficiency

Impaired or absent stereopsis (sometimes termed **stereodeficiency** or **stereoblindness**) can result from disruption at any point in the binocular visual pathway. The major etiological categories include:

### 1. Strabismus

[[Strabismus]] (ocular misalignment) is the most common cause of absent stereopsis. Constant [[esotropia]] or [[exotropia]] prevents bifoveal fixation, eliminating the prerequisite for fine stereopsis. Key considerations:

- **Constant strabismus**: Typically abolishes fine stereopsis; coarse stereopsis may be retained if the angle is small and peripheral fusion exists (as in [[monofixation syndrome]])
- **Intermittent strabismus**: Stereopsis may be normal during periods of alignment and degraded or absent during manifest deviation
- **[[Monofixation syndrome]]** (Parks): A specific binocular adaptation featuring a small central [[scotoma]] of suppression in one eye with peripheral fusion; stereoacuity is typically reduced to 60–3000 arc seconds

### 2. Amblyopia

[[Amblyopia]] reduces the quality of the neural signal from the affected eye, degrading binocular disparity processing. The degree of stereopsis loss correlates with the severity of the amblyopic deficit:

| Amblyopia Severity (Visual Acuity) | Expected Stereoacuity |
|---|---|
| 20/20–20/25 (mild) | 40–60 arc seconds |
| 20/30–20/50 (moderate) | 60–200 arc seconds |
| 20/60–20/100 (severe) | 200–800 arc seconds or absent |
| Worse than 20/100 (profound) | Typically absent |

### 3. Anisometropia

[[Anisometropia]] (unequal refractive error between the two eyes) degrades stereopsis through two mechanisms:
- **Aniseikonia**: Unequal retinal image sizes (particularly with spectacle correction) disrupt binocular correspondence. A difference of >5% in retinal image size significantly impairs stereopsis.
- **Amblyopia**: Uncorrected anisometropia during the critical period leads to [[anisometropic amblyopia]], compounding the stereoscopic deficit.

### 4. Optical and Media Factors

- **Uncorrected [[refractive error]]**: Bilateral blur (especially [[astigmatism]]) reduces retinal image quality and degrades stereoacuity
- **[[Cataract]]**: Unilateral or asymmetric cataract reduces contrast and spatial frequency content of the retinal image
- **[[Corneal]] irregularity**: [[Keratoconus]], corneal scarring, or post-surgical irregularity can degrade stereopsis
- **[[Monovision]]** correction: Intentional anisometropia for [[presbyopia]] management (one eye corrected for distance, one for near) significantly reduces or eliminates stereopsis at the non-corrected distance

### 5. Neurological and Cortical Causes

- **Cortical lesions**: Damage to V1, V2, or extrastriate areas can selectively impair stereopsis while preserving other visual functions
- **[[Multiple sclerosis]]**: Demyelination of the visual pathways can degrade binocular processing
- **Traumatic brain injury**: May disrupt cortical disparity processing or cause acquired strabismus
- **Aging**: Stereoacuity gradually declines with age, particularly after age 60, due to optical, neural, and oculomotor factors

## Symptoms and Clinical Presentation

Patients with stereopsis deficiency may present with a range of symptoms, though many individuals—particularly those with congenital or longstanding deficits—may be entirely asymptomatic due to effective monocular compensation.

### Common Presenting Complaints

- **Difficulty with depth-dependent tasks**: Trouble parking, pouring liquids, navigating stairs, catching balls, or threading needles
- **Clumsiness or spatial misjudgment**: Bumping into objects, misjudging distances when reaching
- **Difficulty with 3D media**: Inability to perceive depth in 3D movies, virtual reality, or stereoscopic displays
- **Occupational limitations**: Failure to meet stereoacuity requirements for specific professions
- **Asthenopia**: Eye strain, headache, or fatigue during prolonged near work, particularly if stereopsis loss is associated with an underlying binocular vision disorder such as [[convergence insufficiency]] or [[decompensating phoria]]
- **Diplopia**: If the underlying cause is an acquired strabismus or decompensated heterophoria

### Asymptomatic Presentation

Many patients with congenital stereoblindness are unaware of their deficit. They have never experienced stereoscopic depth and have developed robust reliance on monocular cues. These patients are often identified incidentally during routine eye examinations or occupational screening.

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical Stereoacuity Testing

Stereoacuity is quantified in **arc seconds** (1 arc second = 1/3600 of a degree). Normal adult stereoacuity ranges from **15 to 40 arc seconds**. Clinical stereotests are broadly categorized as contour-based or random-dot-based.

#### Contour-Based Stereotests

These tests use laterally displaced contour elements visible to each eye through polarized or red-green (anaglyph) filters. They contain monocular cues that may allow patients with poor stereopsis to "guess" correctly.

| Test | Type | Range (arc seconds) | Viewing Distance | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Titmus stereotest]]** (Wirt fly/circles/animals) | Polarized vectograph | 3000 (fly) to 20 (circles) | 40 cm | Most widely used; fly portion tests gross stereopsis; circles test fine stereopsis; contains monocular cues |
| **[[Frisby stereotest]]** | Real depth (no glasses) | 600 to 15 | 30–80 cm | Uses transparent plates of varying thickness; no dissociating glasses needed; tests real-depth stereopsis |
| **[[Howard-Dolman test]]** | Real depth apparatus | Variable | 6 m (distance) | Two vertical rods; patient judges relative depth; gold standard for distance stereopsis |

#### Random-Dot Stereotests

These tests embed stereoscopic targets within random-dot patterns, eliminating monocular form cues. They are considered more valid measures of true binocular stereopsis.

| Test | Type | Range (arc seconds) | Viewing Distance | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **[[TNO stereotest]]** | Red-green anaglyph | 480 to 15 | 40 cm | Random-dot; requires red-green glasses; no monocular cues; tests global stereopsis |
| **[[Randot stereotest]]** | Polarized random-dot | 500 to 20 | 40 cm | Combines random-dot and contour elements; widely used in clinical practice |
| **[[Lang stereotest]] I & II** | Cylindrical lens array (no glasses) | 1200 to 200 (Lang I); 600 to 200 (Lang II) | 40 cm | No dissociating glasses required; useful for pediatric screening; limited range |
| **[[Randot Preschool Stereoacuity Test]]** | Polarized random-dot | 800 to 40 | 40 cm | Designed for young children; uses recognizable shapes |

#### Digital and Advanced Stereotests

Modern digital platforms offer precise control over stimulus parameters:

- **[[ASTEROID]]** (Accurate STEReoacuity from Optical Illusions on a Display): Tablet-based autostereoscopic test requiring no glasses
- **[[PASS (Pediatric Acuity and Stereopsis Screener)]]**: Automated screening device
- **Computer-based psychophysical methods**: Staircase procedures using random-dot stereograms on 3D-capable displays; used primarily in research settings

### Qualitative Assessment of Binocularity

In addition to quantitative stereoacuity measurement, several clinical tests assess the presence and quality of binocular function related to stereopsis:

- **[[Worth 4-dot test]]**: Assesses fusion and suppression at distance and near; does not directly measure stereopsis but evaluates the prerequisite of binocular single vision
- **[[Bagolini striated lenses]]**: Assesses binocular status under natural viewing conditions; detects suppression and anomalous correspondence
- **[[4-prism diopter base-out test]]** (Irvine test): Detects small central suppression scotomas characteristic of [[monofixation syndrome]]
- **[[Synoptophore]] (major amblyoscope)**: Allows independent assessment of simultaneous perception, fusion, and stereopsis at controlled vergence angles

### Electrophysiological and Imaging Methods

- **[[Visual evoked potentials]] (VEP)**: Dynamic random-dot correlograms can elicit cortical responses specific to binocular disparity processing; useful in preverbal children and non-communicative patients
- **Functional MRI (fMRI)**: Research tool demonstrating activation of disparity-selective neurons in V1, V2, V3A, and MT in response to stereoscopic stimuli
- **Optical coherence tomography ([[OCT]])**: While not directly measuring stereopsis, OCT can identify structural retinal or optic nerve pathology contributing to stereopsis loss

## Management and Treatment

The management of stereopsis deficiency is directed at the underlying etiology. Because stereopsis represents the apex of binocular function, its restoration requires addressing all prerequisite levels: clear retinal imagery, accurate ocular alignment, and cortical binocular integration.

### Optical Correction

**Accurate [[refraction]] and optimal optical correction** is the foundational step in managing any stereopsis deficit:

- **Correction of [[refractive error]]**: Full spectacle or [[contact lens]] correction, particularly in cases of [[anisometropia]] or significant bilateral ametropia, can immediately improve stereoacuity
- **Management of [[aniseikonia]]**: When spectacle-induced aniseikonia impairs stereopsis (particularly in anisometropia >2.00 D), options include:
  - [[Contact lens]] correction (minimizes magnification differences compared to spectacles)
  - **Iseikonic lenses** (size lenses): Custom-designed spectacle lenses that equalize retinal image sizes
  - [[Refractive surgery]]: [[LASIK]], [[PRK]], or [[phakic IOL]] implantation to reduce anisometropia
- **Elimination of [[monovision]]**: Patients with monovision contact lens corrections who require stereopsis should be converted to balanced binocular correction or [[multifocal contact lenses]]

### Treatment of Amblyopia

Restoring visual acuity in the amblyopic eye is necessary but not always sufficient for stereopsis recovery. Treatment modalities include:

- **[[Occlusion therapy]] (patching)**: Gold standard for amblyopia treatment; improves monocular acuity but does not directly train binocular function. Stereopsis improvement following patching is variable and often incomplete.
- **[[Atropine penalization]]**: Pharmacological alternative to patching; 1% [[atropine]] instilled in the dominant eye blurs near vision, encouraging use of the amblyopic eye
- **Binocular treatment approaches**: Emerging evidence supports treatments that specifically target binocular function:
  - **Dichoptic therapy**: Presents different visual stimuli to each eye simultaneously (e.g., dichoptic video games), with reduced contrast to the dominant eye to overcome suppression and promote binocular integration
  - **[[Perceptual learning]]**: Repeated practice on stereoscopic tasks with adaptive difficulty levels
  - **Virtual reality-based binocular therapy**: Immersive environments that manipulate interocular contrast and disparity to promote binocular cooperation

### Treatment of Strabismus

Restoration of ocular alignment is a prerequisite for stereopsis recovery in strabismic patients:

- **[[Strabismus surgery]]**: Recession, resection, or transposition of [[extraocular muscles]] to restore alignment. Stereopsis outcomes depend on:
  - Age at onset of strabismus
  - Duration of misalignment
  - Age at surgical correction
  - Presence of amblyopia
  - Preoperative binocular potential (assessed by prism adaptation or synoptophore)

  Patients with [[infantile esotropia]] corrected surgically rarely achieve fine stereopsis (>60 arc seconds) but may develop coarse stereopsis or [[monofixation syndrome]]. Patients with acquired or intermittent strabismus have better prognoses for stereopsis recovery.

- **[[Prism therapy]]**: Incorporating [[Fresnel prisms]] or ground-in prisms into spectacles can compensate for small residual deviations, facilitating binocular fusion and potentially improving stereopsis
- **[[Botulinum toxin]] injection**: Chemodenervation of extraocular muscles as an alternative or adjunct to surgery for specific strabismus types

### Orthoptic and Vision Therapy

**[[Orthoptics]]** and **[[vision therapy]]** encompass a range of exercises and procedures designed to improve binocular function, including stereopsis:

- **Anti-suppression therapy**: Techniques to eliminate cortical suppression of the non-dominant eye, a prerequisite for binocular disparity processing. Methods include:
  - Red-green bar reading
  - Dichoptic stimulation
  - [[Synoptophore]] exercises
- **Fusional vergence training**: Strengthening [[fusional vergence]] amplitudes to maintain binocular alignment across a range of viewing distances. Techniques include:
  - [[Brock string]] exercises
  - [[Aperture rule]] training
  - [[Vectogram]] and [[tranaglyphic]] exercises
  - Computer-based vergence training programs
- **Stereopsis-specific training**: Direct practice with stereoscopic stimuli of progressively finer disparity:
  - Random-dot stereogram training
  - [[Quoits vectogram]] exercises
  - Computer-based stereopsis training programs (e.g., Diplopia Game, Vivid Vision)

### Pharmacological Interventions

While no pharmacological agent directly enhances stereopsis, several medications play roles in the management of conditions affecting stereopsis:

| Agent | Mechanism | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Atropine]] 1%** | Cycloplegic; penalizes accommodation in dominant eye | Amblyopia treatment (penalization) |
| **[[Levodopa]]/carbidopa** | Dopaminergic enhancement of visual cortex function | Investigational adjunct to amblyopia treatment; may transiently improve contrast sensitivity and stereopsis |
| **[[Citicoline]]** | Neuroprotective; enhances cholinergic and dopaminergic neurotransmission | Investigational; some evidence for improved visual acuity and stereopsis in amblyopia |
| **[[Botulinum toxin A]]** | Neuromuscular blockade of extraocular muscles | Treatment of strabismus to restore alignment |

### Surgical Approaches

Surgical intervention for stereopsis deficiency is directed at the underlying structural or alignment pathology:

- **[[Strabismus surgery]]**: As detailed above; the primary surgical approach for stereopsis restoration in strabismic patients
- **[[Cataract surgery]]**: Removal of visually significant cataract (particularly unilateral or asymmetric) with [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) implantation can restore binocular input and improve stereopsis. IOL power calculation should aim to minimize postoperative anisometropia.
- **[[Refractive surgery]]**: [[LASIK]], [[PRK]], [[SMILE]], or [[phakic IOL]] implantation to correct anisometropia and reduce aniseikonia
- **[[Corneal transplantation]]**: [[Penetrating keratoplasty]] or [[lamellar keratoplasty]] for corneal opacities or irregularities impairing retinal image quality in one eye
- **[[Ptosis]] repair**: Correction of significant unilateral [[ptosis]] that occludes the visual axis and prevents binocular input

### Prognosis for Stereopsis Recovery

The prognosis for stereopsis recovery depends critically on several factors:

| Factor | Favorable Prognosis | Unfavorable Prognosis |
|---|---|---|
| **Age at onset** | Acquired after critical period | Congenital or early infantile onset |
| **Duration of deficit** | Short duration | Longstanding (years) |
| **Age at treatment** | Young (within critical period) | Adult (beyond critical period) |
| **Type of strabismus** | Intermittent; small angle | Constant; large angle |
| **Amblyopia severity** | Mild (20/30 or better) | Severe (20/100 or worse) |
| **Preoperative binocular potential** | Demonstrable peripheral fusion | No fusion; deep suppression |
| **Compliance with therapy** | Good compliance | Poor compliance |

Recent research has challenged the traditional view that the critical period for stereopsis recovery is absolute. Studies on adults with amblyopia and strabismus have demonstrated measurable improvements in stereopsis following intensive binocular therapy, perceptual learning, and dichoptic training, suggesting residual neural plasticity in the adult visual cortex. However, the magnitude of improvement in adults is generally smaller than in children, and fine stereopsis is rarely achieved in patients with longstanding congenital deficits.

## Special Considerations

### Stereopsis and Aging

Stereoacuity declines with age due to multiple factors:
- Reduced retinal illumination (senile [[miosis]], lens yellowing)
- Increased optical aberrations
- Neural processing slowing
- Increased prevalence of [[age-related macular degeneration]], [[cataract]], and [[glaucoma]]

Studies report that mean stereoacuity declines from approximately 20–30 arc seconds in young adults to 60–85 arc seconds in individuals over age 70.

### Stereopsis Testing in Pediatric Populations

Stereopsis testing in children requires age-appropriate methods:
- **Infants (6–12 months)**: Preferential looking techniques using random-dot stereograms; VEP-based methods
- **Toddlers (1–3 years)**: [[Lang stereotest]] (no glasses required); reaching/grasping responses to stereoscopic targets
- **Preschool (3–5 years)**: [[Randot Preschool Stereoacuity Test]]; [[Frisby stereotest]]; Titmus animals
- **School-age (>5 years)**: Full range of clinical stereotests including Wirt circles, TNO, Randot

### Stereopsis and Driving

The role of stereopsis in driving safety remains debated. While stereopsis contributes to depth judgment at near and intermediate distances, most driving-relevant depth cues at highway speeds are monocular (motion parallax, relative size, optic flow). Many jurisdictions do not require stereopsis for standard driver licensing, though commercial vehicle and pilot licensing often include stereoacuity requirements.

### Stereopsis in the Digital Age

The proliferation of 3D displays, virtual reality (VR), and augmented reality (AR) technologies has renewed clinical interest in stereopsis:
- Approximately **5–10% of the population** has insufficient stereopsis to perceive 3D content on stereoscopic displays
- VR/AR platforms are being investigated as therapeutic tools for amblyopia and stereopsis deficiency
- Prolonged use of stereoscopic displays may cause **visual discomfort** (vergence-accommodation conflict) in individuals with marginal binocular function

---

## Summary

Stereopsis represents the pinnacle of binocular visual function, arising from the cortical processing of binocular retinal disparity. Its assessment is a fundamental component of the comprehensive optometric and ophthalmologic examination, providing critical information about the integrity of the binocular visual system. Deficiency in stereopsis may result from strabismus, amblyopia, anisometropia, optical degradation, or neurological insult. Management is directed at the underlying cause and may include optical correction, amblyopia treatment, strabismus surgery, orthoptic therapy, and emerging binocular treatment modalities. While the critical period imposes constraints on stereopsis recovery, accumulating evidence supports the potential for meaningful improvement even in adult patients through targeted binocular interventions.
