# Astigmatism

**Astigmatism** is a common [[refractive error]] in which the [[optical system]] of the eye fails to focus a point object into a sharp, focused image on the [[retina]], due to an asymmetric curvature of the refracting surfaces—most commonly the [[cornea]], and to a lesser extent the [[crystalline lens]]. Rather than producing a single focal point, the astigmatic eye produces two focal lines oriented at right angles to each other, a phenomenon described by the **Interval of Sturm**. Astigmatism affects an estimated 30–60% of the adult population worldwide and is one of the leading causes of correctable visual impairment globally. It may occur in isolation or in combination with [[myopia]] or [[hyperopia]], and its accurate diagnosis and management are fundamental competencies in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]].

---

## Etymology and Historical Background

The term "astigmatism" derives from the Greek prefix *a-* (without) and *stigma* (point), literally meaning "without a point." The concept was first described scientifically by **Thomas Young** in 1801, who recognized the condition in his own eye. The first cylindrical lens correction for astigmatism was prescribed by **Sir George Biddell Airy** in 1825, who ground a lens to correct his own astigmatic error. The development of the [[keratometer]] by **Hermann von Helmholtz** in 1851 and later the [[ophthalmometer]] allowed clinicians to objectively measure corneal curvature and quantify astigmatism, laying the groundwork for modern diagnostic and corrective approaches.

---

## Epidemiology

Astigmatism is among the most prevalent [[refractive errors]] worldwide. Key epidemiological findings include:

| **Population** | **Prevalence (≥0.50 D cylinder)** | **Source/Study** |
|---|---|---|
| Global adults | ~40% | Various meta-analyses |
| US adults (NHANES) | ~36% | Vitale et al., 2008 |
| East Asian children | ~33–50% | He et al., 2004; Fan et al., 2004 |
| Australian adults (Blue Mountains) | ~37% | Attebo et al., 1999 |
| European adults | ~30–35% | Williams et al., 2015 |
| Indigenous Australian children | ~50% | Junghans & Crewther, 2005 |

Astigmatism prevalence varies significantly by age, ethnicity, and geographic region. It is notably higher in certain populations, including individuals of East Asian and Native American descent. The condition may be present at birth ([[congenital astigmatism]]) and often changes in magnitude and axis orientation throughout life. In neonates, high degrees of [[with-the-rule astigmatism]] are common but tend to decrease during the first years of life through a process known as **emmetropization**. In older adults, a shift from [[with-the-rule astigmatism]] to [[against-the-rule astigmatism]] is well documented.

## Classification

Astigmatism is classified according to several systems based on anatomical origin, regularity, axis orientation, and relationship to the retinal focal points.

### By Anatomical Origin

| **Type** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **[[Corneal astigmatism]]** | Caused by asymmetric curvature of the [[anterior corneal surface]] (most common) or [[posterior corneal surface]]. The anterior cornea contributes approximately 90% of total corneal astigmatism. |
| **[[Lenticular astigmatism]]** | Caused by asymmetric curvature, tilt, or decentration of the [[crystalline lens]], or by variations in the [[refractive index]] within the lens. |
| **[[Retinal astigmatism]]** | Extremely rare; caused by an asymmetric shape of the [[retina]] (e.g., tilted [[optic disc]]). |
| **Total (refractive) astigmatism** | The net astigmatism of the entire optical system, measured by [[refraction]]. It is the sum of corneal and internal (primarily lenticular) astigmatism. |

Clinically, the relationship between corneal and total astigmatism is important. The [[crystalline lens]] typically contributes approximately 0.50 D of [[against-the-rule astigmatism]], which partially compensates for corneal [[with-the-rule astigmatism]]—a concept known as the **residual or internal astigmatism**.

### By Regularity

| **Type** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **[[Regular astigmatism]]** | The two principal meridians are perpendicular (90° apart). The refracting surfaces can be described as toric (like the surface of an American football). Correctable with [[spectacle lenses]], [[soft toric contact lenses]], or standard [[cylindrical lenses]]. |
| **[[Irregular astigmatism]]** | The principal meridians are not perpendicular, or the curvature varies across the [[pupil]] in a non-uniform manner. Causes include [[keratoconus]], [[corneal scarring]], [[pellucid marginal degeneration]], [[pterygium]], and post-surgical irregularity. Often requires [[rigid gas permeable contact lenses]] (RGP), [[scleral lenses]], or advanced surgical correction. |

### By Axis Orientation (Regular Astigmatism)

| **Type** | **Steepest Meridian** | **Axis of Minus Cylinder** | **Clinical Notes** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[With-the-rule astigmatism]] (WTR)** | Vertical (~90°) | Near 180° (±15°) | Most common in younger individuals. The vertical meridian has the greatest refractive power. |
| **[[Against-the-rule astigmatism]] (ATR)** | Horizontal (~180°) | Near 90° (±15°) | More common in older adults. The horizontal meridian has the greatest refractive power. |
| **[[Oblique astigmatism]]** | Between 30°–60° or 120°–150° | Between 30°–60° or 120°–150° | Less common; may cause more visual disturbance per diopter than WTR or ATR. |

### By Relationship to the Retina (Focal Line Position)

| **Type** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Simple myopic astigmatism** | One focal line falls on the retina; the other falls anterior to it. Rx: plano/−cylinder |
| **Simple hyperopic astigmatism** | One focal line falls on the retina; the other falls posterior to it. Rx: plano/+cylinder |
| **Compound myopic astigmatism** | Both focal lines fall anterior to the retina. Rx: −sphere/−cylinder |
| **Compound hyperopic astigmatism** | Both focal lines fall posterior to the retina. Rx: +sphere/+cylinder |
| **Mixed astigmatism** | One focal line falls anterior and the other posterior to the retina. Rx: ±sphere/−cylinder (the sphere and cylinder have opposite signs in minus cylinder form) |

## Pathophysiology

### Optical Principles

In an idealized, non-astigmatic ([[stigmatic]]) eye, the [[cornea]] and [[crystalline lens]] refract incoming light to a single focal point on the [[retina]]. In astigmatism, the refracting surfaces possess different curvatures along different meridians, resulting in two principal meridians of maximum and minimum refractive power. Light passing through these meridians is focused at two separate focal lines rather than a single focal point.

The region between these two focal lines is termed the **[[Interval of Sturm]]** (or **Sturm's interval**). At the midpoint of this interval lies the **[[circle of least confusion]]**, which represents the cross-section of the refracted light bundle where the image blur is minimized (though not eliminated). The [[spherical equivalent]] of an astigmatic prescription places the circle of least confusion on the retina, but does not fully correct the astigmatism.

Mathematically, the dioptric interval of Sturm equals the magnitude of the cylindrical component of the refractive error. For example, in a patient with a refraction of −2.00/−1.50 × 180, the two focal lines are separated by 1.50 D.

### Corneal Contributions

The [[anterior corneal surface]] is the most powerful refracting surface of the eye (approximately +48 D) and is the primary contributor to astigmatism. In a normal eye, the cornea is not perfectly spherical; it is a **prolate aspheric** surface that flattens toward the periphery. In astigmatism, the cornea assumes a **toric** shape, with measurably different radii of curvature along the two principal meridians.

The [[posterior corneal surface]] also contributes to total corneal astigmatism. Modern [[Scheimpflug imaging]] devices (e.g., [[Pentacam]], [[Galilei]]) and [[optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)-based anterior segment analyzers can measure posterior corneal astigmatism, which typically contributes 0.30–0.50 D of against-the-rule astigmatism. This posterior corneal contribution is clinically significant in planning [[toric intraocular lens]] (IOL) implantation and [[refractive surgery]].

### Lenticular Contributions

The [[crystalline lens]] contributes to total astigmatism through:

- **Surface curvature asymmetry** of the anterior and posterior lens surfaces.
- **Tilt or decentration** of the lens relative to the [[visual axis]].
- **Gradient refractive index** variations within the lens substance.

In youth, lenticular astigmatism typically partially compensates for corneal astigmatism. With aging and the development of [[nuclear sclerosis]] or [[cortical cataract]], lenticular astigmatism may change, altering the total refractive astigmatism.

### Etiological Factors

| **Factor** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|
| **Genetics** | Strong hereditary component; multiple loci identified (e.g., 2q37, 4q12). Twin studies show high concordance. |
| **Eyelid pressure** | Chronic mechanical force from the [[eyelids]] (e.g., [[chalazion]], [[ptosis]], habitual eye rubbing) can mold corneal curvature. |
| **Corneal ectasia** | [[Keratoconus]], [[pellucid marginal degeneration]], and post-[[LASIK]] ectasia cause progressive irregular astigmatism. |
| **Corneal pathology** | [[Corneal scarring]], [[pterygium]], [[Salzmann nodular degeneration]], and [[corneal dystrophies]] can induce irregular astigmatism. |
| **Surgical factors** | [[Cataract surgery]] incisions, [[corneal transplantation]] ([[penetrating keratoplasty]]), and [[scleral buckling]] can induce or alter astigmatism. |
| **Developmental** | Premature birth, low birth weight, and certain craniofacial syndromes are associated with higher astigmatism. |

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

The symptoms of astigmatism depend on the type, magnitude, axis, and the patient's accommodative capacity and visual demands.

### Common Symptoms

- **Blurred vision** at all distances (unlike pure [[myopia]] or [[hyperopia]], which preferentially affect distance or near vision, respectively). Low amounts of astigmatism may cause only mild blur.
- **[[Asthenopia]]** (eye strain), particularly with prolonged near work, reading, or screen use.
- **Headaches**, often frontal or temporal, exacerbated by visual tasks.
- **Squinting or tilting the head** to achieve a clearer image through a narrower [[pupil]] or to align the visual axis with a less astigmatic meridian.
- **Difficulty with night driving** due to glare, halos, and streaking of lights (especially with higher magnitudes or irregular astigmatism).
- **Monocular diplopia or ghosting** of images, particularly noticeable with high-contrast targets (e.g., letters on a white background).
- **Difficulty distinguishing similar-looking characters** (e.g., confusing "H" with "N," "8" with "0") due to directional blur.

### Symptoms by Type

| **Type** | **Characteristic Symptoms** |
|---|---|
| **Low regular astigmatism (<1.00 D)** | May be asymptomatic or cause mild asthenopia. |
| **Moderate regular astigmatism (1.00–3.00 D)** | Noticeable blur at distance and near; significant asthenopia. |
| **High regular astigmatism (>3.00 D)** | Marked blur; may cause [[amblyopia]] if uncorrected in childhood ([[meridional amblyopia]]). |
| **Irregular astigmatism** | Distorted, "ghosted" images; poor best-corrected [[visual acuity]] with spectacles; often requires specialty contact lenses. |
| **Oblique astigmatism** | May cause more subjective visual disturbance per diopter than WTR or ATR due to the orientation of blur relative to common visual tasks (reading horizontal text). |

### Astigmatism and Amblyopia

Uncorrected significant astigmatism during the critical period of visual development (birth to approximately 7–8 years) can lead to **[[meridional amblyopia]]**, in which the visual cortex fails to develop normal acuity along the more blurred meridian. This is particularly associated with high astigmatism (≥1.50–2.00 D in young children) and oblique axis orientations. Early detection through pediatric [[vision screening]] and timely optical correction are essential to prevent permanent visual impairment.

## Diagnosis

### Subjective Assessment

#### History

A thorough case history should elicit:
- Quality and pattern of visual blur (distance, near, or both)
- Presence of asthenopia, headaches, or squinting
- Occupational and recreational visual demands
- Previous spectacle or contact lens prescriptions
- Family history of astigmatism or [[keratoconus]]
- History of ocular surgery, trauma, or corneal disease

#### Subjective Refraction

[[Subjective refraction]] remains the gold standard for determining the final refractive correction. Key techniques include:

- **[[Jackson cross-cylinder]] (JCC) technique**: Used to refine both the axis and power of the cylindrical correction. The JCC is a lens with equal and opposite cylindrical powers (e.g., ±0.25 D or ±0.50 D) that is flipped before the eye while the patient compares clarity. This technique isolates the astigmatic component with high precision.
- **[[Astigmatic dial (clock dial/sunburst chart)]]**: The patient identifies the darkest or sharpest line on a radial pattern, which corresponds to the axis of the correcting minus cylinder (using the "rule of 30": multiply the clock hour of the darkest line by 30 to approximate the axis).
- **[[Stenopaic slit]]**: A narrow slit aperture is rotated before the eye to isolate individual meridians, allowing separate spherical refraction of each principal meridian. Useful in high or irregular astigmatism.
- **Fogging technique**: Plus sphere is added to fog the eye into myopia, ensuring the circle of least confusion is anterior to the retina, before refining the cylinder.

### Objective Assessment

#### Retinoscopy

[[Retinoscopy]] is a fundamental objective technique for measuring refractive error, including astigmatism. In astigmatism, the retinoscopic reflex exhibits characteristic features:

- **Band-shaped (streak) reflex** rather than a round reflex.
- **Scissors motion** may be observed in irregular astigmatism or [[keratoconus]].
- **Break phenomenon**: The reflex neutralizes at different lens powers in the two principal meridians, and the difference between these powers equals the cylindrical correction.
- The axis of the streak reflex aligns with the principal meridians when the retinoscope streak is rotated.

#### Autorefraction

Modern [[autorefractors]] and [[aberrometers]] provide rapid, objective measurement of sphere, cylinder, and axis. While highly useful as a starting point, autorefraction may overestimate cylindrical power in some patients and should always be refined with subjective refraction.

#### Keratometry

The [[keratometer]] (ophthalmometer) measures the curvature of the central [[anterior corneal surface]] (approximately the central 3–4 mm zone) in the two principal meridians. It provides:

- **Simulated keratometry (SimK) values**: Steep and flat meridian powers (in diopters) and their axes.
- **Corneal astigmatism magnitude**: The difference between the steep and flat K readings.

Limitations include measurement of only the central cornea and inability to detect irregular astigmatism or posterior corneal astigmatism.

#### Corneal Topography

[[Corneal topography]] maps the curvature of the entire corneal surface, providing far more information than keratometry alone. Technologies include:

| **Technology** | **Examples** | **Capabilities** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Placido disc topography]]** | Medmont E300, Topcon KR-1W | Maps anterior corneal curvature; detects irregular astigmatism, [[keratoconus]], and post-surgical changes. |
| **[[Scheimpflug imaging]]** | [[Pentacam]], Galilei G4 | Maps anterior and posterior corneal surfaces, corneal thickness ([[pachymetry]]), and provides total corneal power. |
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (anterior segment)** | Zeiss CIRRUS, Tomey CASIA2 | High-resolution cross-sectional imaging of cornea; measures anterior and posterior curvature. |
| **[[Scanning slit topography]]** | Orbscan II | Combines slit-scanning and Placido disc for anterior/posterior corneal mapping. |

Topographic maps are displayed as **axial (sagittal)**, **tangential (instantaneous)**, **elevation**, and **refractive** maps. Key patterns include:

- **Symmetric bow-tie**: Regular astigmatism.
- **Asymmetric bow-tie**: Suspect for early [[keratoconus]] or [[pellucid marginal degeneration]].
- **Inferior steepening**: Classic [[keratoconus]] pattern.
- **Irregular patterns**: Post-surgical, scarring, or ectatic disease.

#### Wavefront Aberrometry

[[Wavefront aberrometry]] measures the total optical aberrations of the eye, including both lower-order aberrations (sphere, cylinder) and [[higher-order aberrations]] (e.g., [[coma]], [[trefoil]], [[spherical aberration]]). Irregular astigmatism is associated with elevated higher-order aberrations. Wavefront data can guide customized [[refractive surgery]] ([[wavefront-guided LASIK]]) and specialty contact lens fitting.

## Management and Treatment

The management of astigmatism depends on its magnitude, regularity, patient symptoms, visual demands, age, and ocular health. Options range from optical correction to surgical intervention.

### Spectacle Correction

[[Spectacle lenses]] with a cylindrical (toric) component are the most common and accessible method of correcting regular astigmatism.

- **Minus cylinder form**: Convention in optometric practice (e.g., −1.00/−1.50 × 180).
- **Plus cylinder form**: Convention in some ophthalmologic practices (e.g., −2.50/+1.50 × 090).
- **Transposition** between forms is performed using standard optical rules.

#### Prescribing Considerations

| **Consideration** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| **Full correction vs. partial correction** | Full cylinder correction provides optimal acuity but may cause spatial distortion, especially in high or oblique astigmatism or first-time wearers. Gradual adaptation or partial correction may be necessary. |
| **Meridional magnification** | Cylindrical lenses produce differential magnification along the two principal meridians, causing perceived distortion of shapes (circles appear oval). This effect increases with cylinder power and is more noticeable with oblique axes. |
| **Vertex distance** | The effective power of a cylindrical lens changes with vertex distance. This is clinically significant for cylinders >1.50 D and must be accounted for when converting between spectacle and contact lens prescriptions. |
| **Spectacle adaptation** | Patients new to cylindrical correction, or those with significant axis or power changes, may experience transient spatial distortion, tilting of floors/walls, or mild nausea. Adaptation typically occurs within 1–2 weeks. |
| **Children** | Full astigmatic correction is generally recommended in children to prevent or treat [[meridional amblyopia]], even if the child has not previously worn glasses. |

### Contact Lens Correction

Contact lenses offer several advantages over spectacles for astigmatism correction, including elimination of meridional magnification, wider field of corrected vision, and cosmetic preference.

#### Soft Toric Contact Lenses

[[Soft toric contact lenses]] are the most commonly prescribed contact lens modality for regular astigmatism. They incorporate a cylindrical correction and use stabilization mechanisms to maintain proper axis alignment on the eye:

| **Stabilization Method** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Prism ballast** | Thicker inferiorly; gravity and lid interaction maintain orientation. |
| **Peri-ballast (dual thin zones)** | Thin zones superiorly and inferiorly; lid pressure maintains orientation. |
| **Accelerated stabilization design (ASD)** | Four thin zones at cardinal positions; rapid stabilization with blinking. |
| **Truncation** | Flat edge at the bottom of the lens rests on the lower lid margin. Less common in modern designs. |

**Lens rotation assessment**: The clinician must assess rotational stability using the [[slit lamp biomicroscope]]. If the lens rotates from the intended axis, the **LARS rule** (Left Add, Right Subtract) is applied to compensate: if the lens rotates to the patient's left (clockwise from the clinician's perspective), the prescribed axis is adjusted by adding the degrees of rotation; if to the right, by subtracting.

Soft toric lenses are available in daily disposable, bi-weekly, and monthly replacement modalities, and in a wide range of sphere, cylinder, and axis combinations.

#### Rigid Gas Permeable (RGP) Contact Lenses

[[Rigid gas permeable contact lenses]] correct astigmatism through the **tear lens** mechanism: the rigid lens creates a smooth anterior refracting surface, and the tear film between the lens and the cornea fills in the corneal irregularity, effectively neutralizing corneal astigmatism. RGP lenses are particularly effective for:

- Moderate to high regular corneal astigmatism
- [[Irregular astigmatism]] (e.g., [[keratoconus]], post-[[penetrating keratoplasty]], corneal scarring)
- Patients intolerant of spectacle distortion

**Back-surface toric** and **bitoric** RGP designs are used when the corneal toricity is too high for a spherical base curve to achieve adequate fit and centration.

#### Scleral and Hybrid Lenses

- **[[Scleral contact lenses]]**: Large-diameter RGP lenses that vault entirely over the [[cornea]] and rest on the [[sclera]]. They are filled with preservative-free saline, creating a fluid reservoir that masks corneal irregularity. Scleral lenses are the gold standard for managing visually significant [[irregular astigmatism]] from [[keratoconus]], [[corneal ectasia]], severe [[dry eye]], and post-surgical corneal irregularity.
- **[[Hybrid contact lenses]]** (e.g., SynergEyes): Feature a rigid center for optical correction surrounded by a soft skirt for comfort. Useful for patients who desire the optics of an RGP lens with the comfort of a soft lens.

#### Orthokeratology

[[Orthokeratology]] (ortho-K) uses specially designed reverse-geometry RGP lenses worn overnight to temporarily reshape the cornea and reduce refractive error, including mild to moderate astigmatism (typically up to 1.50–1.75 D of corneal astigmatism with toric ortho-K designs). The effect is reversible upon discontinuation.

### Surgical Correction

Surgical options for astigmatism correction are broadly divided into corneal-based procedures and lens-based procedures.

#### Corneal Refractive Surgery

| **Procedure** | **Description** | **Astigmatism Range** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[LASIK]] (Laser-Assisted In Situ Keratomileusis)** | A corneal flap is created (microkeratome or femtosecond laser), and [[excimer laser]] ablation reshapes the underlying [[stroma]] to correct the refractive error. | Up to ~5.00–6.00 D cylinder |
| **[[PRK]] (Photorefractive Keratectomy)** | Surface ablation without flap creation; the [[epithelium]] is removed and excimer laser is applied directly to [[Bowman's layer]]/anterior stroma. | Up to ~4.00–6.00 D cylinder |
| **[[SMILE]] (Small Incision Lenticule Extraction)** | A femtosecond laser creates a lenticule within the stroma, which is extracted through a small incision. Newer platforms support astigmatic correction. | Up to ~5.00 D cylinder (platform-dependent) |
| **[[Wavefront-guided]] / [[Wavefront-optimized]] ablation** | Customized ablation profiles based on wavefront aberrometry data, potentially improving outcomes in eyes with higher-order aberrations contributing to irregular astigmatism. | Variable |
| **[[Topography-guided ablation]]** (e.g., Contoura Vision, T-CAT) | Ablation profile derived from corneal topography data; particularly useful for irregular astigmatism and post-surgical correction. | Variable |

#### Astigmatic Keratotomy (AK) and Limbal Relaxing Incisions (LRI)

**[[Astigmatic keratotomy]]** involves making arcuate incisions in the cornea along the steep meridian to flatten it and reduce astigmatism. **[[Limbal relaxing incisions]]** (LRIs) are placed at the limbus and are a gentler variant with less risk of overcorrection. These techniques are commonly used:

- At the time of [[cataract surgery]] to reduce pre-existing astigmatism.
- After [[corneal transplantation]] to manage graft-related astigmatism.
- **[[Femtosecond laser-assisted AK]]** (e.g., during [[femtosecond laser-assisted cataract surgery]]) offers greater precision in incision depth, length, and placement.

#### Toric Intraocular Lenses (IOLs)

**[[Toric intraocular lenses]]** are implanted during [[cataract surgery]] or [[refractive lens exchange]] to simultaneously correct aphakia/pseudophakia and pre-existing corneal astigmatism. Key considerations include:

- **Preoperative planning**: Accurate measurement of corneal astigmatism (including posterior corneal contribution), axial length ([[biometry]]), and IOL power calculation using modern formulas (e.g., [[Barrett Toric Calculator]], [[Abulafia-Koch regression]]).
- **Intraoperative alignment**: The toric IOL must be precisely aligned to the intended axis. Misalignment of just 1° reduces the astigmatic correction by approximately 3.3%. Digital marking systems, intraoperative [[aberrometry]] (e.g., ORA System), and image-guided systems (e.g., Alcon VERION, Zeiss CALLISTO) improve rotational accuracy.
- **Postoperative rotation**: If the IOL rotates from its intended axis, residual astigmatism results. Significant rotation (>10°) may require surgical repositioning.

Available toric IOL platforms include monofocal toric, [[extended depth of focus]] (EDOF) toric, and [[multifocal toric]] designs.

#### Intracorneal Ring Segments (ICRS)

**[[Intracorneal ring segments]]** (e.g., Intacs, Ferrara rings, Keraring) are PMMA arc segments implanted into the corneal stroma to flatten the central cornea and reduce irregular astigmatism, primarily in [[keratoconus]] and post-LASIK ectasia. They may be combined with [[corneal cross-linking]] (CXL) for stabilization.

#### Corneal Cross-Linking (CXL)

**[[Corneal cross-linking]]** uses [[riboflavin]] (vitamin B2) and [[ultraviolet-A]] (UVA) light to strengthen corneal collagen cross-links, halting the progression of [[keratoconus]] and other ectatic disorders. While CXL primarily stabilizes the cornea rather than directly correcting astigmatism, it may produce modest flattening and reduction of irregular astigmatism in some patients.

## Special Considerations

### Astigmatism in Pediatric Populations

- High astigmatism (≥1.50 D) in early childhood is a significant risk factor for [[meridional amblyopia]].
- The [[American Academy of Ophthalmology]] (AAO) and [[American Association for Pediatric Ophthalmology and Strabismus]] (AAPOS) recommend referral thresholds for astigmatism in vision screening programs (e.g., ≥2.00 D at age 1–2 years, ≥1.50 D at age 3–4 years).
- Full optical correction with spectacles is the first-line treatment; [[occlusion therapy]] or [[atropine penalization]] may be added if amblyopia is present.

### Astigmatism After Cataract Surgery

Surgically induced astigmatism (SIA) is an expected consequence of [[cataract surgery]] incisions. Modern small-incision phacoemulsification (2.2–2.8 mm incisions) typically induces ≤0.50 D of SIA. Surgeons may plan incision placement on the steep corneal meridian (**on-axis incision**) to reduce pre-existing astigmatism, or use toric IOLs and LRIs for larger amounts.

### Astigmatism and Keratoconus

[[Keratoconus]] is the most common cause of progressive irregular astigmatism. Early detection using [[corneal topography]] and [[tomography]] is critical. Management progresses from spectacles (early stages) to RGP or scleral contact lenses, ICRS, CXL, and ultimately [[corneal transplantation]] ([[deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]] or [[penetrating keratoplasty]]) in advanced cases.

### Residual Astigmatism After Refractive Surgery

Residual astigmatism following [[LASIK]], [[PRK]], or toric IOL implantation may result from:
- Inaccurate preoperative measurements
- Surgically induced astigmatism
- Cyclotorsion (rotational eye movement when transitioning from upright to supine position)
- Posterior corneal astigmatism not accounted for in planning
- IOL rotation

Enhancement procedures, lens repositioning, or supplementary IOLs (e.g., [[Sulcoflex toric]]) may be required.

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis for astigmatism is generally excellent with appropriate optical or surgical correction. Regular astigmatism is readily correctable to normal or near-normal [[visual acuity]] in the vast majority of patients. Irregular astigmatism, particularly from [[keratoconus]] or corneal scarring, may limit best-corrected acuity even with specialty contact lenses, though modern scleral lens technology has significantly improved visual outcomes in these populations. Early detection and correction in children are essential to prevent [[amblyopia]], which may result in permanent visual impairment if not treated during the critical period of visual development.
