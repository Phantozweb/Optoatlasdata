

# Non-Pigmented Ciliary Epithelium

---

## Overview

The **non-pigmented ciliary epithelium (NPCE)** is a specialized, single-layered [[neuroectodermal]] tissue that constitutes the innermost epithelial layer of the [[ciliary body]]. Together with the underlying [[pigmented ciliary epithelium]] (PCE), it forms a bilayered epithelial structure unique in the human body in that the two cell layers are arranged apex-to-apex, a configuration that arises from the invagination of the [[optic cup]] during [[embryogenesis]]. The NPCE is the principal tissue responsible for the active secretion of [[aqueous humor]] into the [[posterior chamber]] of the eye, and it plays a critical role in maintaining [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP), providing nutrients to the avascular [[lens]] and [[cornea]], and contributing to the [[blood-aqueous barrier]]. Dysfunction or neoplastic transformation of the NPCE is implicated in a range of ocular pathologies, including [[glaucoma]], [[uveitis]], and rare intraocular tumors such as [[medulloepithelioma]] and [[adenoma of the non-pigmented ciliary epithelium]].

A thorough understanding of the NPCE is essential for optometrists, ophthalmologists, and vision scientists, as its physiological and pathological behavior underpins many of the most common and sight-threatening conditions encountered in clinical practice.

---

## Embryology and Developmental Origin

The NPCE shares a common developmental origin with the [[retina]]. During the fourth to fifth week of [[embryonic development]], the [[optic vesicle]] invaginates to form the double-walled [[optic cup]]. The inner layer of the optic cup differentiates into the [[neural retina]] posteriorly and the NPCE anteriorly, while the outer layer gives rise to the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE) posteriorly and the [[pigmented ciliary epithelium]] (PCE) anteriorly. This invagination results in the characteristic apex-to-apex orientation of the NPCE and PCE, with their basal surfaces facing in opposite directions — the basal surface of the NPCE faces the [[posterior chamber]], and the basal surface of the PCE faces the [[ciliary stroma]].

Because of this shared neuroectodermal lineage, the NPCE retains certain molecular and functional characteristics of neural tissue, including the expression of [[neurofilament proteins]], [[carbonic anhydrase]] isoenzymes, and various [[ion channels]] and [[transporters]] critical for aqueous humor production. This developmental relationship also explains why tumors arising from the NPCE can exhibit features reminiscent of primitive [[medullary epithelium]], as seen in [[medulloepithelioma]].

## Anatomy and Histology

### Gross Anatomy

The [[ciliary body]] is a ring-shaped structure located between the [[iris]] anteriorly and the [[choroid]] posteriorly. It is divided into two regions:

| Region | Description |
|---|---|
| **[[Pars plicata]]** | The anterior, ridged portion containing approximately 70–80 [[ciliary processes]]; the primary site of aqueous humor secretion |
| **[[Pars plana]]** | The posterior, flat portion extending to the [[ora serrata]]; relatively quiescent in terms of secretion |

The NPCE lines the inner surface of both the pars plicata and pars plana, but its secretory function is most active over the ciliary processes of the pars plicata, where the surface area is maximized by extensive infoldings and the underlying stroma is richly vascularized by branches of the [[long posterior ciliary arteries]] and the [[anterior ciliary arteries]].

### Histological Features

Under [[light microscopy]], the NPCE appears as a single layer of cuboidal to columnar, non-pigmented cells situated internal to the PCE. Key histological features include:

- **Apex-to-apex orientation**: The apical surfaces of the NPCE and PCE face each other, connected by [[gap junctions]] and [[desmosomes]]. A potential space exists between the two layers, continuous with the sub-retinal space.
- **Basal lamina**: The basal surface of the NPCE is covered by the [[internal limiting membrane]] (ILM) of the ciliary body, which is continuous with the [[lens zonules]] ([[zonules of Zinn]]) and the [[vitreous base]].
- **Cytoplasmic characteristics**: NPCE cells are rich in [[mitochondria]], [[rough endoplasmic reticulum]], and [[Golgi apparatus]], reflecting their high metabolic and secretory activity.
- **Lack of melanin**: Unlike the adjacent PCE, NPCE cells do not contain [[melanosomes]], hence the designation "non-pigmented."

### Ultrastructural Features

Under [[electron microscopy]], the NPCE demonstrates:

- Extensive **basolateral membrane infoldings** on the surface facing the posterior chamber, dramatically increasing the surface area available for ion and fluid transport.
- Abundant **[[tight junctions]] (zonulae occludentes)** between adjacent NPCE cells at their lateral surfaces near the basal (posterior chamber–facing) aspect. These tight junctions are the anatomical basis of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]] at the level of the ciliary epithelium.
- Numerous **[[gap junctions]]** at the apical interface between NPCE and PCE cells, allowing intercellular communication and the transfer of ions, metabolites, and second messengers between the two epithelial layers. This functional coupling is essential for coordinated aqueous humor secretion.
- High density of **[[Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase]]** pumps localized predominantly on the basolateral membrane of the NPCE, which is the primary driving force for aqueous humor formation.

## Physiology and Function

### Aqueous Humor Production

The NPCE is the **primary effector cell** in the production of [[aqueous humor]], a clear, protein-poor fluid that fills the [[anterior chamber]] and [[posterior chamber]] of the eye. Aqueous humor production occurs at a rate of approximately **2.0–2.5 µL/min** in the healthy adult human eye and involves three mechanisms:

| Mechanism | Contribution | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Active secretion** | ~80–90% | Energy-dependent transport of ions (primarily Na⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻) across the NPCE into the posterior chamber, with water following osmotically |
| **Ultrafiltration** | ~10–15% | Pressure-dependent movement of plasma filtrate from the ciliary stroma across the epithelium |
| **Diffusion** | Minor | Passive movement of lipid-soluble substances along concentration gradients |

#### Active Secretion: Molecular Mechanisms

The active secretion of aqueous humor by the NPCE is a multi-step process involving coordinated ion transport across the bilayered ciliary epithelium:

1. **Uptake from the stroma (PCE side)**: Plasma ultrafiltrate from the fenestrated capillaries of the ciliary stroma enters the PCE. Ions such as Na⁺, Cl⁻, and HCO₃⁻ are taken up by the PCE via various transporters, including the [[Na⁺/K⁺/2Cl⁻ cotransporter (NKCC1)]], [[Na⁺/H⁺ exchanger]], and [[Cl⁻/HCO₃⁻ exchanger]].

2. **Transfer through gap junctions**: Ions and small molecules pass from the PCE to the NPCE through the extensive network of [[gap junctions]] (primarily composed of [[connexin 43]]) at the apical interface.

3. **Secretion into the posterior chamber (NPCE side)**: The NPCE actively transports Na⁺ into the posterior chamber via the [[Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase]] pump located on its basolateral membrane. Cl⁻ exits through [[chloride channels]] (including [[ClC-type channels]] and [[CFTR]]). HCO₃⁻ is generated intracellularly by [[carbonic anhydrase]] isoenzymes (particularly [[carbonic anhydrase II]] and [[carbonic anhydrase IV]]) and is secreted into the posterior chamber. The net movement of solute creates an osmotic gradient that drives water across the epithelium via [[aquaporin-1]] (AQP1) and [[aquaporin-4]] (AQP4) water channels expressed on the NPCE.

4. **Water transport**: [[Aquaporin-1]], highly expressed on the basolateral membrane of the NPCE, is the principal water channel facilitating transcellular water movement into the posterior chamber.

#### Pharmacological Relevance

The molecular machinery of the NPCE is the direct target of several major classes of [[anti-glaucoma medications]]:

| Drug Class | Target on NPCE | Mechanism of IOP Reduction |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]]** (e.g., [[dorzolamide]], [[brinzolamide]], [[acetazolamide]]) | [[Carbonic anhydrase II]] and [[Carbonic anhydrase IV\|IV]] in NPCE | Inhibit HCO₃⁻ formation, reducing Na⁺ and fluid secretion |
| **[[Beta-adrenergic antagonists]]** (e.g., [[timolol]], [[betaxolol]]) | [[β₂-adrenergic receptors]] on NPCE | Reduce cAMP-mediated Cl⁻ secretion and Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase activity |
| **[[Alpha-2 adrenergic agonists]]** (e.g., [[brimonidine]], [[apraclonidine]]) | [[α₂-adrenergic receptors]] on NPCE | Reduce cAMP, decreasing active ion transport and aqueous production |

### Blood-Aqueous Barrier Function

The [[tight junctions]] between adjacent NPCE cells constitute a critical component of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]] (BAB). This barrier restricts the paracellular passage of plasma proteins, immune cells, and large molecules from the ciliary stroma into the aqueous humor, thereby maintaining the optical clarity and immune privilege of the anterior segment. Disruption of the BAB — as occurs in [[anterior uveitis]], surgical trauma, or [[prostaglandin]]-mediated inflammation — leads to the clinical finding of [[flare]] (protein) and [[cells]] in the anterior chamber on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]].

### Secretion of Other Constituents

Beyond water and electrolytes, the NPCE secretes several biologically important molecules into the aqueous humor:

- **[[Ascorbic acid]]** (vitamin C): Present in aqueous humor at concentrations 15–20 times higher than in plasma; functions as an antioxidant protecting the lens and corneal endothelium from [[ultraviolet radiation]]-induced oxidative damage.
- **[[Hyaluronic acid]]**: Contributes to the viscoelastic properties of the vitreous and aqueous.
- **[[Transferrin]]** and other proteins: Involved in iron homeostasis and antimicrobial defense.
- **[[Growth factors]]**: Including [[transforming growth factor-beta]] (TGF-β), which contributes to the immune-privileged status of the anterior chamber (a phenomenon known as [[anterior chamber-associated immune deviation]] or ACAID).

## Pathophysiology

### Role in Glaucoma

The NPCE is central to the pathophysiology of [[glaucoma]], the leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide. While the most common forms of glaucoma (e.g., [[primary open-angle glaucoma]]) are primarily disorders of aqueous outflow rather than overproduction, the NPCE remains the therapeutic target for reducing aqueous inflow. In rare cases, hypersecretory glaucoma — characterized by elevated IOP due to excessive aqueous production by the NPCE — has been described, though this remains a diagnosis of exclusion.

Pharmacological suppression of NPCE secretory function (via [[beta-blockers]], [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]], and [[alpha-2 agonists]]) is a cornerstone of medical glaucoma management. Surgical destruction of the NPCE via [[cyclodestructive procedures]] (e.g., [[transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]], [[endoscopic cyclophotocoagulation]]) is reserved for refractory glaucoma cases.

### Blood-Aqueous Barrier Breakdown

Inflammation, trauma, surgery, or pharmacological agents (e.g., [[prostaglandin analogs]]) can disrupt the tight junctions of the NPCE, leading to BAB breakdown. This results in:

- Increased protein concentration in the aqueous humor ([[plasmoid aqueous]])
- Influx of inflammatory cells
- Clinical signs of [[anterior uveitis]] (flare, cells, [[keratic precipitates]])
- Secondary complications including [[posterior synechiae]], [[cataract]], and [[secondary glaucoma]]

### Neoplasms of the Non-Pigmented Ciliary Epithelium

Tumors arising from the NPCE are rare but clinically significant. They include:

| Tumor Type | Characteristics | Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Medulloepithelioma]]** (diktyoma) | Embryonal tumor of childhood; arises from primitive medullary epithelium of the NPCE; may contain cartilage, brain tissue (teratoid variant) | Can be benign or malignant; may cause [[secondary glaucoma]], [[lens subluxation]], [[leukocoria]] |
| **[[Adenoma of the NPCE]]** | Benign, well-differentiated tumor; typically presents as a pigmented or non-pigmented mass in the ciliary body region | Benign; may be confused with [[ciliary body melanoma]] |
| **[[Adenocarcinoma of the NPCE]]** | Malignant counterpart of adenoma; rare; locally invasive | Malignant; may require [[enucleation]] |
| **[[Glioneuroma]]** | Extremely rare; reflects neural differentiation potential of NPCE | Benign |
| **Acquired epithelial cyst (NPCE cyst)** | Non-neoplastic; fluid-filled cyst arising from the NPCE; may be primary (idiopathic) or secondary (post-traumatic, post-inflammatory) | Benign; may cause angle narrowing and [[secondary angle-closure glaucoma]] |

### NPCE Cysts

[[Ciliary body cysts]] arising from the NPCE are relatively common and are often discovered incidentally on [[ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM) or [[anterior segment optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT). They are typically thin-walled, translucent, and filled with clear fluid. While most are asymptomatic, large or multiple cysts can:

- Displace the [[iris]] anteriorly, narrowing the [[anterior chamber angle]]
- Mimic or cause [[plateau iris configuration]]
- Rarely, cause [[secondary angle-closure glaucoma]]
- Be confused with solid tumors such as [[ciliary body melanoma]] or [[iris melanoma]]

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Disorders of the NPCE rarely produce symptoms in isolation. Clinical manifestations depend on the specific pathology:

### Aqueous Humor Dysfunction
- **Elevated [[intraocular pressure]]**: Headache, eye pain, halos around lights (in acute settings), progressive visual field loss (in chronic glaucoma)
- **Hypotony** (reduced aqueous production): Blurred vision, [[choroidal folds]], [[maculopathy]], [[phthisis bulbi]] in severe cases

### Blood-Aqueous Barrier Disruption
- Photophobia, pain, redness, blurred vision
- [[Anterior chamber flare]] and cells on slit-lamp examination

### NPCE Neoplasms and Cysts
- Often asymptomatic in early stages
- Progressive visual disturbance if the tumor or cyst impinges on the [[lens]], [[iris]], or [[drainage angle]]
- [[Leukocoria]] in children (medulloepithelioma)
- [[Lens subluxation]] or [[cataract]]
- Unilateral [[secondary glaucoma]]
- Visible mass behind the iris or in the ciliary body region

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical Examination

- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: Assessment of anterior chamber depth, flare, cells, iris configuration, and any visible mass behind the iris. [[Gonioscopy]] is essential to evaluate the [[anterior chamber angle]] for narrowing, cysts, or tumor extension.
- **[[Dilated fundus examination]]**: To rule out posterior extension of ciliary body lesions and to assess the [[ora serrata]] region.
- **[[Intraocular pressure]] measurement**: [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]] or other validated methods to assess IOP, which reflects the balance between NPCE secretory function and aqueous outflow.

### Imaging

| Modality | Application in NPCE Assessment |
|---|---|
| **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | Gold standard for imaging the ciliary body; can differentiate solid tumors from cysts; assesses internal reflectivity, dimensions, and relationship to surrounding structures |
| **[[Anterior segment OCT]] (AS-OCT)** | High-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the anterior segment; useful for detecting NPCE cysts and assessing angle configuration; limited penetration through the iris and sclera |
| **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** | Useful for larger ciliary body masses; assesses internal echogenicity, dimensions, and extraocular extension |
| **[[Fluorescein angiography]]** | Can demonstrate hyperfluorescence of ciliary body tumors; useful in assessing BAB integrity |
| **[[Magnetic resonance imaging]] (MRI)** | Differentiates melanotic from amelanotic lesions; assesses extraocular extension of malignant tumors |
| **[[Optical coherence tomography angiography]] (OCTA)** | Emerging role in assessing vascularity of ciliary body lesions |

### Laboratory and Ancillary Testing

- **[[Aqueous humor analysis]]**: Paracentesis with cytological and biochemical analysis may be performed in cases of suspected neoplasm or atypical uveitis. Elevated protein, abnormal cells, or tumor markers may be identified.
- **[[Fluorophotometry]]**: A non-invasive technique that quantifies the rate of aqueous humor production and BAB permeability by measuring the clearance of intravenously administered [[fluorescein]] from the anterior chamber. This is primarily a research tool but has clinical applications in assessing NPCE function.
- **[[Histopathology]] and [[immunohistochemistry]]**: Definitive diagnosis of NPCE tumors requires tissue examination. NPCE-derived tumors typically stain positive for [[cytokeratin]], [[vimentin]], [[S-100 protein]], and [[neuron-specific enolase]] (NSE), reflecting their neuroectodermal origin. They are typically negative for [[HMB-45]] and [[Melan-A]], which helps distinguish them from [[uveal melanoma]].

## Management and Treatment

### Pharmacological Interventions Targeting the NPCE

The primary clinical relevance of the NPCE in everyday optometric and ophthalmic practice is as the target for IOP-lowering medications:

#### Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors (CAIs)

- **Topical**: [[Dorzolamide]] 2%, [[brinzolamide]] 1%
- **Systemic**: [[Acetazolamide]] (oral/IV), [[methazolamide]]
- **Mechanism**: Inhibit [[carbonic anhydrase II]] (cytoplasmic) and [[carbonic anhydrase IV]] (membrane-bound) in the NPCE, reducing HCO₃⁻ and Na⁺ secretion, thereby decreasing aqueous humor production by approximately 15–20%.
- **Side effects**: Topical — stinging, bitter taste, allergic conjunctivitis; Systemic — paresthesias, metabolic acidosis, hypokalemia, renal calculi, aplastic anemia (rare)

#### Beta-Adrenergic Antagonists

- **Non-selective**: [[Timolol]], [[levobunolol]], [[carteolol]]
- **Selective (β₁)**: [[Betaxolol]]
- **Mechanism**: Block [[β₂-adrenergic receptors]] on the NPCE, reducing intracellular [[cyclic AMP]] (cAMP) and thereby decreasing active ion transport and aqueous humor production by approximately 20–25%.
- **Side effects**: Bradycardia, bronchospasm (especially non-selective agents), hypotension, depression, fatigue

#### Alpha-2 Adrenergic Agonists

- **[[Brimonidine]]** 0.1–0.2%, **[[apraclonidine]]** 0.5–1%
- **Mechanism**: Stimulate [[α₂-adrenergic receptors]] on the NPCE, reducing cAMP and suppressing aqueous humor production. Brimonidine also enhances [[uveoscleral outflow]].
- **Side effects**: Allergic conjunctivitis (especially with chronic use), dry mouth, fatigue, CNS depression in infants (brimonidine is contraindicated in children under 2 years)

#### Combination Agents

Fixed-combination drops targeting the NPCE include:
- [[Dorzolamide]]/[[timolol]] (Cosopt®)
- [[Brimonidine]]/[[timolol]] (Combigan®)
- [[Brinzolamide]]/[[brimonidine]] (Simbrinza®)

These combinations provide additive IOP reduction by simultaneously targeting multiple secretory pathways in the NPCE.

### Surgical and Procedural Interventions

#### Cyclodestructive Procedures

When medical therapy fails to adequately control IOP, surgical destruction of the NPCE (and adjacent PCE) can be performed to reduce aqueous humor production:

| Procedure | Technique | Indications |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]] (TSCPC)** | [[Diode laser]] (810 nm) applied through the sclera to destroy the ciliary epithelium | Refractory glaucoma, especially in eyes with poor visual potential |
| **[[Endoscopic cyclophotocoagulation]] (ECP)** | Diode laser delivered under direct endoscopic visualization of the ciliary processes | Refractory glaucoma; can be combined with [[cataract surgery]]; more precise targeting of NPCE |
| **[[Micropulse transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]] (MP-TSCPC)** | Sub-threshold, pulsed diode laser energy; less destructive than continuous-wave TSCPC | Moderate-to-advanced glaucoma; lower risk of hypotony and phthisis compared to traditional TSCPC |
| **[[Cyclocryotherapy]]** | Cryoprobe applied to the sclera overlying the ciliary body | Largely replaced by laser-based methods; higher complication rate |

**Complications of cyclodestructive procedures** include [[hypotony]], [[phthisis bulbi]], chronic inflammation, [[sympathetic ophthalmia]] (rare), and loss of visual acuity.

#### Management of NPCE Cysts

- **Observation**: Most NPCE cysts are benign and asymptomatic; serial monitoring with UBM or AS-OCT is appropriate.
- **[[Laser cystotomy]]**: [[Nd:YAG laser]] or [[argon laser]] can be used to rupture cysts causing angle narrowing or secondary glaucoma.
- **Surgical excision**: Rarely required; indicated for large, symptomatic cysts or when malignancy cannot be excluded.

#### Management of NPCE Neoplasms

- **Observation**: Small, stable, presumed benign lesions may be monitored with serial imaging.
- **[[Local resection]] ([[iridocyclectomy]])**: Partial excision of the ciliary body tumor with preservation of the globe; suitable for small-to-medium, well-circumscribed tumors.
- **[[Plaque brachytherapy]]**: [[Iodine-125]] or [[Ruthenium-106]] radioactive plaque applied to the sclera overlying the tumor; used for medium-sized tumors.
- **[[Enucleation]]**: Removal of the entire globe; indicated for large, malignant, or recurrent tumors, or when the eye has no useful vision.
- **Systemic evaluation**: Malignant NPCE tumors (adenocarcinoma, malignant medulloepithelioma) require systemic staging to rule out metastatic disease, although distant metastasis is rare.

## Differential Diagnosis of NPCE Lesions

When a mass is identified in the ciliary body region, the differential diagnosis includes:

| Lesion | Key Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| **[[Uveal melanoma]] (ciliary body melanoma)** | Pigmented (usually), solid, high internal reflectivity on UBM, HMB-45/Melan-A positive |
| **NPCE adenoma** | Non-pigmented or variably pigmented, well-circumscribed, cytokeratin positive, HMB-45 negative |
| **NPCE cyst** | Thin-walled, anechoic on UBM, transilluminates |
| **[[Metastatic tumor]]** | History of systemic malignancy, often bilateral, irregular margins |
| **[[Leiomyoma]]** | Rare, arises from ciliary muscle smooth muscle, well-circumscribed |
| **[[Medulloepithelioma]]** | Childhood, may contain cystic spaces, cartilage, or heteroplastic elements |

## Research and Emerging Concepts

### NPCE as a Source of Stem/Progenitor Cells

Recent research has identified the NPCE as a potential reservoir of [[retinal progenitor cells]]. Given its developmental origin from the same neuroectodermal tissue as the neural retina, the NPCE retains the capacity to express retinal progenitor markers such as [[Nestin]], [[Pax6]], and [[CHX10]]. In vitro studies have demonstrated that NPCE cells can be induced to differentiate into [[photoreceptor]]-like cells and [[retinal ganglion cell]]-like cells under appropriate culture conditions. This has generated significant interest in the NPCE as a potential autologous cell source for [[retinal regeneration]] therapies in diseases such as [[age-related macular degeneration]] and [[retinitis pigmentosa]], though clinical translation remains in early stages.

### Gene Therapy and the NPCE

The NPCE has been explored as a target for [[gene therapy]] approaches to glaucoma. Viral vectors (e.g., [[adeno-associated virus]], AAV) can be used to transduce NPCE cells to express therapeutic genes that modulate aqueous humor production or enhance outflow facility. For example, overexpression of [[aquaporin-1]] antisense RNA in the NPCE has been shown in animal models to reduce aqueous humor production and lower IOP.

### NPCE and Circadian Regulation of IOP

Aqueous humor production by the NPCE follows a [[circadian rhythm]], with production rates approximately 2.5 µL/min during waking hours and decreasing to approximately 1.0–1.5 µL/min during sleep. This diurnal variation is mediated in part by [[sympathetic nervous system]] tone acting on [[β₂-adrenergic receptors]] on the NPCE. Understanding this circadian regulation has clinical implications: [[beta-blockers]] are less effective at lowering IOP during sleep (when sympathetic tone is already low), while [[prostaglandin analogs]] (which act on outflow rather than production) maintain efficacy throughout the 24-hour cycle.

### Wnt Signaling and NPCE

The [[Wnt signaling pathway]] has been identified as a key regulator of NPCE function. The NPCE is a major source of [[Wnt]] ligands (particularly [[Wnt2b]] and [[Wnt3a]]) and [[Wnt inhibitors]] (such as [[secreted frizzled-related proteins]], sFRPs, and [[Dickkopf-1]], DKK-1) in the anterior segment. Dysregulation of Wnt signaling in the NPCE has been implicated in the pathogenesis of [[exfoliation syndrome]] and may contribute to trabecular meshwork dysfunction in [[primary open-angle glaucoma]].
