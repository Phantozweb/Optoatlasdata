

# Ophthalmic Corticosteroids

---

**Ophthalmic corticosteroids** are a class of [[steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs]] formulated for ocular use that represent one of the most powerful and widely prescribed pharmacological tools in the management of [[ocular inflammation]], [[allergic eye disease]], and postoperative care in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]]. These agents exert their therapeutic effects by modulating the [[inflammatory cascade]] at multiple levels, suppressing both the early and late phases of inflammation. While indispensable in clinical practice, ophthalmic corticosteroids carry a well-characterized risk profile—including [[steroid-induced glaucoma]], [[posterior subcapsular cataract]], and increased susceptibility to [[ocular infection]]—that demands careful patient selection, vigilant monitoring, and a thorough understanding of their [[pharmacology]], [[pharmacokinetics]], and [[pharmacodynamics]].

This article provides a comprehensive clinical reference on ophthalmic corticosteroids for the eye care professional, encompassing their mechanism of action, classification, clinical indications, adverse effects, monitoring protocols, and evidence-based prescribing strategies.

---

## Historical Background

The therapeutic use of corticosteroids in ophthalmology dates to the early 1950s, shortly after [[Philip Hench]] and colleagues demonstrated the dramatic anti-inflammatory effects of [[cortisone]] in [[rheumatoid arthritis]], earning the 1950 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine. [[Cortisone acetate]] ophthalmic drops were among the first topical steroid preparations applied to the eye, and their remarkable efficacy in controlling [[anterior uveitis]], [[scleritis]], and postoperative inflammation rapidly established corticosteroids as a cornerstone of ocular therapeutics. Over the subsequent decades, pharmaceutical development yielded agents with improved [[ocular bioavailability]], enhanced anti-inflammatory potency, and—more recently—engineered metabolic profiles designed to reduce systemic and local side effects (so-called "soft steroids" or "retrometabolically designed" corticosteroids such as [[loteprednol etabonate]]).

## Pharmacology and Mechanism of Action

### Molecular Mechanism

Ophthalmic corticosteroids are synthetic analogs of endogenous [[glucocorticoids]] produced by the [[adrenal cortex]]. Their mechanism of action is multifaceted and operates at both genomic and non-genomic levels:

#### Genomic Pathway

1. **Receptor Binding:** The corticosteroid molecule, being lipophilic, diffuses across the [[cell membrane]] and binds to the intracellular [[glucocorticoid receptor]] (GR), a member of the [[nuclear receptor superfamily]].
2. **Receptor Activation and Nuclear Translocation:** Binding induces a conformational change in the GR, causing dissociation from [[heat shock proteins]] (HSP90, HSP70) and [[immunophilins]]. The activated GR-steroid complex then translocates to the [[cell nucleus]].
3. **Transactivation:** The GR complex binds to [[glucocorticoid response elements]] (GREs) in the promoter regions of target genes, upregulating the transcription of anti-inflammatory proteins, most notably:
   - **[[Lipocortin-1]] (Annexin A1):** Inhibits [[phospholipase A2]] (PLA2), thereby blocking the release of [[arachidonic acid]] from membrane [[phospholipids]]. This is the critical upstream step that prevents the synthesis of both [[prostaglandins]] (via the [[cyclooxygenase]] pathway) and [[leukotrienes]] (via the [[lipoxygenase]] pathway).
   - **[[IκBα]]:** Sequesters [[NF-κB]] in the cytoplasm, preventing its nuclear translocation and the transcription of pro-inflammatory [[cytokines]].
   - **[[MAPK phosphatase 1]] (MKP-1):** Deactivates [[mitogen-activated protein kinases]], reducing inflammatory signaling.
4. **Transrepression:** The activated GR complex also directly interacts with and inhibits pro-inflammatory [[transcription factors]] such as [[NF-κB]] and [[AP-1]] (activator protein-1), suppressing the expression of:
   - Pro-inflammatory [[cytokines]]: [[IL-1]], [[IL-6]], [[TNF-α]], [[IL-8]]
   - [[Chemokines]] and [[adhesion molecules]]: [[ICAM-1]], [[VCAM-1]], [[E-selectin]]
   - Inflammatory [[enzymes]]: [[COX-2]], [[iNOS]], [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs)

#### Non-Genomic Pathway

Rapid effects (within seconds to minutes) include:
- Direct stabilization of [[cell membranes]] and [[lysosomal membranes]], reducing the release of proteolytic enzymes.
- Modulation of [[ion channel]] activity and intracellular [[calcium]] signaling.
- Interaction with membrane-associated GRs affecting [[endothelial cell]] permeability.

### Net Anti-Inflammatory Effects on Ocular Tissue

The combined genomic and non-genomic actions produce the following clinically relevant effects in the eye:

| **Effect** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|
| Reduction of [[vasodilation]] | Decreased prostaglandin and nitric oxide synthesis |
| Decreased vascular permeability | Stabilization of endothelial tight junctions; reduced VEGF expression |
| Inhibition of [[leukocyte]] migration | Downregulation of adhesion molecules and chemokines |
| Suppression of [[fibrin]] deposition | Reduced exudation of plasma proteins |
| Inhibition of [[fibroblast]] proliferation | Decreased collagen synthesis and scar formation |
| Reduction of [[neovascularization]] | Suppression of [[VEGF]] and [[bFGF]] |
| Stabilization of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]] and [[blood-retinal barrier]] | Multiple mechanisms above acting in concert |

This broad-spectrum suppression distinguishes corticosteroids from [[nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs]] (NSAIDs), which inhibit only the cyclooxygenase pathway and do not affect leukotriene synthesis or leukocyte trafficking to the same degree.

## Classification and Formulations

Ophthalmic corticosteroids are classified by their **anti-inflammatory potency**, **formulation type**, and **route of administration**.

### Classification by Potency

| **Potency Category** | **Agent** | **Relative Anti-Inflammatory Potency** | **Common Concentration** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **High Potency** | [[Prednisolone acetate]] | High | 1.0% |
| | [[Dexamethasone]] | Very High | 0.1% |
| | [[Difluprednate]] | Very High | 0.05% |
| **Moderate Potency** | [[Loteprednol etabonate]] | Moderate | 0.5%, 0.25% |
| | [[Prednisolone sodium phosphate]] | Moderate-High | 1.0% |
| | [[Rimexolone]] | Moderate | 1.0% |
| **Low Potency** | [[Fluorometholone]] | Low-Moderate | 0.1%, 0.25% |
| | [[Fluorometholone acetate]] | Moderate | 0.1% |
| | [[Medrysone]] | Low | 1.0% |
| | [[Loteprednol etabonate]] (low-dose) | Low | 0.2% |

### Formulation Types

- **Suspensions** (e.g., prednisolone acetate 1%): Require vigorous shaking before instillation to ensure uniform drug distribution. The acetate ester form provides superior [[corneal]] penetration due to its lipophilicity.
- **Solutions** (e.g., prednisolone sodium phosphate 1%, dexamethasone sodium phosphate 0.1%): Do not require shaking; however, the phosphate salt form has lower corneal penetration compared to acetate suspensions.
- **Emulsions** (e.g., difluprednate 0.05%): Provide uniform drug distribution without shaking and enhanced [[bioavailability]].
- **Ointments** (e.g., dexamethasone 0.05%): Provide prolonged contact time; useful for nocturnal dosing or in pediatric patients.
- **Gels** (e.g., loteprednol etabonate 0.5% gel): Improved residence time on the [[ocular surface]].
- **Intravitreal implants** (e.g., [[dexamethasone intravitreal implant]] [Ozurdex®], [[fluocinolone acetonide]] implant [Retisert®, Iluvien®]): Sustained-release devices for posterior segment disease.
- **Periocular injections** (sub-Tenon's, subconjunctival, orbital floor): [[Triamcinolone acetonide]], [[methylprednisolone acetate]].
- **Intravitreal injections**: [[Triamcinolone acetonide]] (preservative-free formulations preferred).

### Routes of Administration

| **Route** | **Primary Target Tissue** | **Common Agents** |
|---|---|---|
| Topical (drops, ointment, gel) | [[Conjunctiva]], [[Cornea]], [[Anterior chamber]] | Prednisolone acetate, dexamethasone, loteprednol, difluprednate, fluorometholone |
| Periocular (sub-Tenon's, subconjunctival) | [[Anterior segment]], [[Posterior segment]] | Triamcinolone acetonide, methylprednisolone, dexamethasone |
| Intravitreal (injection or implant) | [[Retina]], [[Vitreous]], [[Macula]] | Triamcinolone acetonide, dexamethasone implant, fluocinolone acetonide implant |
| Systemic (oral, intravenous) | Entire eye and orbit | [[Prednisone]], [[methylprednisolone]], [[dexamethasone]] |

## Pharmacokinetics

### Topical Ocular Pharmacokinetics

The pharmacokinetic behavior of topical ophthalmic corticosteroids is governed by several factors:

1. **Corneal Penetration:** The [[cornea]] presents a trilaminar barrier—the lipophilic [[corneal epithelium]], the hydrophilic [[corneal stroma]], and the lipophilic [[corneal endothelium]]. Optimal penetration requires a drug with balanced lipophilicity and hydrophilicity. Acetate and alcohol ester forms (e.g., prednisolone acetate) penetrate the intact corneal epithelium more effectively than phosphate salts.
2. **Precorneal Residence Time:** Factors reducing residence time include [[tear turnover]] (approximately 16% per minute), [[blinking]] (every 5–7 seconds), and [[nasolacrimal drainage]]. Viscosity-enhancing agents, gels, and ointments improve residence time.
3. **Aqueous Humor Concentration:** After topical instillation of prednisolone acetate 1%, peak [[aqueous humor]] concentrations are achieved within 30–120 minutes. The concentration achieved is sufficient to suppress anterior segment inflammation.
4. **Posterior Segment Penetration:** Topical corticosteroids achieve limited concentrations in the [[vitreous humor]] and [[retina]]. For posterior segment disease, periocular or intravitreal routes are preferred.

### Soft Steroids: Retrometabolic Design

[[Loteprednol etabonate]] exemplifies the "soft steroid" concept. It is designed with a C-20 ester group (chloromethyl ester at the C-20 position) that is rapidly hydrolyzed by ubiquitous [[esterases]] to inactive metabolites (Δ1-cortienic acid etabonate and Δ1-cortienic acid) after exerting its local anti-inflammatory effect. This retrometabolic design results in:
- Reduced risk of [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) elevation
- Lower incidence of [[cataract]] formation
- Minimal systemic absorption and side effects

[[Difluprednate]] is a difluorinated prednisolone derivative with very high receptor binding affinity. Despite its potency, it is also rapidly deacetylated in the eye to its active metabolite (DFB) and subsequently inactivated, contributing to a favorable safety profile relative to its potency.

## Clinical Indications

Ophthalmic corticosteroids are indicated in a wide range of inflammatory, allergic, and immune-mediated ocular conditions:

### Anterior Segment Indications

- **[[Anterior uveitis]] (iritis, iridocyclitis):** First-line therapy. Prednisolone acetate 1% or difluprednate 0.05% are standard agents for acute episodes.
- **Postoperative inflammation:** Following [[cataract surgery]], [[LASIK]], [[PRK]], [[corneal transplantation]] ([[penetrating keratoplasty]], [[DSAEK]], [[DMEK]]), [[glaucoma surgery]], and [[vitreoretinal surgery]].
- **[[Allergic conjunctivitis]]:** Severe [[vernal keratoconjunctivitis]] (VKC), [[atopic keratoconjunctivitis]] (AKC), and [[giant papillary conjunctivitis]] (GPC) unresponsive to [[mast cell stabilizers]] and [[antihistamines]].
- **[[Corneal graft rejection]]:** Aggressive topical corticosteroid therapy (e.g., prednisolone acetate 1% hourly) is critical in managing [[endothelial rejection]] episodes.
- **[[Episcleritis]] and [[scleritis]]:** Mild episcleritis may respond to topical steroids; scleritis typically requires systemic therapy.
- **[[Interstitial keratitis]]:** Associated with [[herpes simplex virus]] (HSV), [[herpes zoster ophthalmicus]] (HZO), or [[syphilis]].
- **[[Corneal immune infiltrates]] and [[marginal keratitis]]**
- **[[Dry eye disease]]:** Short-term pulse therapy to break the inflammatory cycle (e.g., loteprednol etabonate 0.5% or fluorometholone 0.1%).
- **[[Chemical burns]]:** Early use to control inflammation; must be balanced against risk of [[corneal melting]] with prolonged use.
- **[[Stevens-Johnson syndrome]] / [[toxic epidermal necrolysis]]:** Acute ocular management.

### Posterior Segment Indications

- **[[Posterior uveitis]], [[intermediate uveitis]], and [[panuveitis]]:** Periocular or intravitreal corticosteroids, often in conjunction with systemic therapy.
- **[[Diabetic macular edema]] (DME):** Intravitreal triamcinolone acetonide or dexamethasone implant (Ozurdex®), particularly in pseudophakic eyes or those refractory to [[anti-VEGF]] therapy.
- **[[Macular edema]] secondary to [[retinal vein occlusion]] (RVO):** Dexamethasone intravitreal implant is FDA-approved for this indication.
- **[[Cystoid macular edema]] (CME, Irvine-Gass syndrome):** Post-surgical CME responsive to topical, periocular, or intravitreal corticosteroids.
- **[[Non-infectious posterior uveitis]]:** Fluocinolone acetonide intravitreal implant (Retisert®, Iluvien®) for chronic, recurrent disease.
- **[[Sympathetic ophthalmia]]**
- **[[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]]**

### Periocular and Orbital Indications

- **[[Thyroid eye disease]] (Graves' orbitopathy):** Systemic or periocular corticosteroids for acute inflammatory phase.
- **[[Orbital inflammatory pseudotumor]]**
- **[[Optic neuritis]]:** Intravenous methylprednisolone per the [[Optic Neuritis Treatment Trial]] (ONTT) protocol.

## Adverse Effects and Complications

The adverse effect profile of ophthalmic corticosteroids is well-characterized and represents the primary limitation to their use. Clinicians must weigh the therapeutic benefit against these risks in every prescribing decision.

### Steroid-Induced Ocular Hypertension and Glaucoma

**[[Steroid-induced glaucoma]]** is the most clinically significant adverse effect of ophthalmic corticosteroid use.

#### Pathophysiology of IOP Elevation

Corticosteroids increase [[intraocular pressure]] by reducing [[aqueous humor]] outflow through the [[trabecular meshwork]] (TM). The proposed mechanisms include:

1. **Increased deposition of [[extracellular matrix]] (ECM) material** in the TM, including [[glycosaminoglycans]] (GAGs), [[fibronectin]], [[elastin]], and [[type IV collagen]].
2. **Inhibition of [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs)** and upregulation of [[tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases]] (TIMPs), reducing ECM turnover and increasing outflow resistance.
3. **Alterations in TM cell [[cytoskeleton]]:** Increased formation of [[cross-linked actin networks]] (CLANs) in TM cells, altering cell shape and reducing phagocytic activity.
4. **Inhibition of TM cell [[phagocytosis]]:** Reduced clearance of debris from the outflow pathway.
5. **Changes in TM cell [[tight junctions]]** and increased TM cell stiffness.
6. **Upregulation of [[myocilin]] (MYOC/TIGR)** gene expression in TM cells—the same gene implicated in [[primary open-angle glaucoma]] (POAG).

#### Steroid Responder Classification

The population can be stratified by IOP response to corticosteroids (based on the classic studies by [[Armaly]] and [[Becker]]):

| **Responder Category** | **Prevalence (General Population)** | **IOP Rise with Topical Dexamethasone 0.1% QID × 4–6 weeks** |
|---|---|---|
| High responders | ~5% | >15 mmHg rise (IOP >31 mmHg) |
| Moderate responders | ~33% | 6–15 mmHg rise (IOP 20–31 mmHg) |
| Non-responders | ~62% | <6 mmHg rise (IOP <20 mmHg) |

**Risk factors for steroid response:**
- Pre-existing [[POAG]] or [[ocular hypertension]] (OHT): ~90% are steroid responders
- Family history of glaucoma
- [[High myopia]]
- [[Diabetes mellitus]]
- [[Connective tissue disease]] (especially [[rheumatoid arthritis]])
- Age extremes (children and elderly)
- Prior steroid response

#### Clinical Features

- Typically develops after 2–6 weeks of topical corticosteroid use, but can occur within days (especially with potent agents) or after months/years of use.
- Usually reversible upon discontinuation of the steroid, but prolonged, unrecognized elevation can cause irreversible [[optic nerve]] damage indistinguishable from POAG.
- Intravitreal and periocular depot corticosteroids can cause sustained IOP elevation lasting weeks to months, sometimes requiring [[glaucoma surgery]].

### Posterior Subcapsular Cataract

**[[Posterior subcapsular cataract]] (PSC)** is a well-established complication of chronic corticosteroid use.

- **Mechanism:** Corticosteroids are thought to alter [[lens epithelial cell]] migration and differentiation, leading to aberrant posterior migration and formation of swollen, granular "bladder cells" (Wedl cells) at the posterior pole of the [[crystalline lens]].
- **Risk factors:** Duration and dose of corticosteroid exposure; systemic steroids carry higher risk than topical; individual susceptibility varies.
- **Clinical presentation:** Patients report [[glare]], reduced [[contrast sensitivity]], and decreased [[visual acuity]], particularly in bright light conditions.
- **Reversibility:** PSC formation is generally considered irreversible; definitive treatment is [[cataract extraction]] with [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) implantation.

### Increased Susceptibility to Infection

Corticosteroids suppress the local immune response, increasing the risk of:

- **[[Herpes simplex keratitis]]:** Corticosteroids are contraindicated in active [[epithelial keratitis]] (dendritic/geographic ulcer) as they promote viral replication and can lead to [[corneal perforation]]. However, they are used judiciously (with concurrent [[antiviral]] therapy) in [[stromal keratitis]] and [[endotheliitis]].
- **[[Fungal keratitis]]:** Corticosteroid use is a major risk factor. Fungal infection should be suspected in any worsening [[corneal ulcer]] on steroid therapy.
- **[[Bacterial keratitis]]:** Steroids may mask signs of infection and delay diagnosis.
- **[[Acanthamoeba keratitis]]:** Steroid use may exacerbate infection.

### Delayed Wound Healing

Corticosteroids inhibit [[fibroblast]] proliferation, [[collagen]] synthesis, and [[angiogenesis]], which can:
- Delay [[corneal epithelial]] wound healing
- Increase risk of [[corneal thinning]] and [[corneal perforation]] (especially in [[corneal ulcers]], [[chemical burns]], and post-surgical corneas)
- Impair [[scleral]] wound healing after glaucoma surgery

### Other Adverse Effects

- **[[Mydriasis]]** and [[ptosis]] (rare, with chronic use)
- **[[Periocular skin]] changes:** Thinning, telangiectasia (with chronic periocular application)
- **Systemic absorption:** Particularly in children and with frequent dosing; can cause [[adrenal suppression]], [[Cushing syndrome]] (rare with topical use alone)
- **[[Central serous chorioretinopathy]] (CSCR):** Associated with both systemic and topical corticosteroid use
- **Reactivation of [[herpes zoster ophthalmicus]]**

## Prescribing Principles and Monitoring

### General Prescribing Guidelines

1. **Use the lowest effective potency and frequency** for the shortest duration necessary.
2. **Taper gradually** rather than abruptly discontinuing, to prevent rebound inflammation (especially in [[uveitis]]).
3. **Choose the appropriate agent** based on the clinical scenario:
   - **Severe anterior uveitis or graft rejection:** Prednisolone acetate 1% or difluprednate 0.05%
   - **Mild-to-moderate allergic eye disease or dry eye:** Loteprednol etabonate 0.5% or fluorometholone 0.1%
   - **Post-LASIK/PRK:** Fluorometholone or loteprednol (lower IOP risk)
   - **Posterior segment disease:** Intravitreal dexamethasone implant or triamcinolone acetonide
4. **Educate patients** on proper instillation technique, the importance of shaking suspensions, and adherence to the prescribed taper schedule.
5. **Document baseline IOP** before initiating therapy.

### Monitoring Protocol

| **Parameter** | **Frequency** | **Rationale** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Intraocular pressure]] (IOP) | Baseline, 2 weeks, 4–6 weeks, then every 1–3 months during ongoing therapy | Detect steroid-induced IOP elevation |
| [[Slit-lamp examination]] | Each visit | Assess therapeutic response, detect cataract formation, corneal changes, infection |
| [[Dilated fundus examination]] | Baseline and periodically | Evaluate [[optic nerve head]] for glaucomatous damage; assess posterior segment |
| [[Visual acuity]] | Each visit | Monitor for cataract-related decline or other complications |
| [[Visual field testing]] | If IOP elevation detected or prolonged therapy | Detect [[glaucomatous visual field loss]] |
| [[OCT]] of [[RNFL]]/[[optic nerve]] | If IOP elevation or prolonged therapy | Structural assessment for glaucomatous damage |
| [[Pachymetry]] | Baseline (if not previously obtained) | [[Central corneal thickness]] affects IOP measurement interpretation |

### Management of Steroid-Induced IOP Elevation

1. **Discontinue or reduce** the corticosteroid if clinically feasible.
2. **Switch to a lower-potency agent** (e.g., from prednisolone acetate to loteprednol etabonate or fluorometholone).
3. **Initiate [[IOP-lowering therapy]]:** [[Topical beta-blockers]], [[prostaglandin analogs]], [[alpha-agonists]], [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]], or [[Rho kinase inhibitors]] as appropriate.
4. **For depot steroid-induced IOP elevation** (intravitreal or periocular): If medical therapy is insufficient, surgical intervention may be required, including [[trabeculectomy]], [[glaucoma drainage device]] implantation, or [[vitrectomy]] to remove intravitreal triamcinolone.
5. **Refer to [[glaucoma specialist]]** for refractory cases.

## Specific Agents: Detailed Profiles

### Prednisolone Acetate 1% (Pred Forte®)

- **Classification:** High-potency topical corticosteroid
- **Formulation:** Ophthalmic suspension (requires shaking)
- **Indications:** [[Anterior uveitis]], postoperative inflammation, [[corneal graft rejection]], severe [[keratitis]]
- **Dosing:** Acute uveitis: 1 drop every 1–2 hours initially, tapered over weeks to months
- **Key considerations:** Gold standard for anterior segment inflammation; significant IOP elevation risk with prolonged use

### Difluprednate 0.05% (Durezol®)

- **Classification:** Very high-potency topical corticosteroid
- **Formulation:** Ophthalmic emulsion (no shaking required)
- **Indications:** Postoperative inflammation and pain, anterior uveitis
- **Dosing:** Typically QID; may be used BID in some postoperative protocols
- **Key considerations:** Difluorinated structure provides enhanced tissue penetration and potency; emulsion formulation improves bioavailability; higher risk of IOP elevation than loteprednol; demonstrated posterior segment penetration with topical use

### Loteprednol Etabonate 0.5% (Lotemax®)

- **Classification:** Moderate-potency "soft steroid"
- **Formulation:** Suspension, gel, ointment
- **Indications:** Allergic conjunctivitis, postoperative inflammation, [[dry eye disease]] (short-term pulse therapy), post-refractive surgery
- **Dosing:** QID, tapered as clinically indicated
- **Key considerations:** Retrometabolic design minimizes IOP elevation and cataract risk; preferred for steroid-responsive conditions where prolonged therapy may be needed

### Loteprednol Etabonate 0.25% (Eysuvis®)

- **Classification:** Low-potency soft steroid
- **Formulation:** Ophthalmic suspension (preservative-free, submicron particle)
- **Indications:** Short-term (up to 2 weeks) treatment of signs and symptoms of [[dry eye disease]]
- **Dosing:** QID for up to 2 weeks
- **Key considerations:** First FDA-approved corticosteroid specifically for short-term DED management; submicron nanoparticle technology enhances bioavailability

### Fluorometholone 0.1% (FML®)

- **Classification:** Low-to-moderate potency
- **Formulation:** Ophthalmic suspension
- **Indications:** Mild-to-moderate allergic conjunctivitis, mild anterior segment inflammation, post-refractive surgery
- **Dosing:** BID to QID
- **Key considerations:** Lower corneal penetration limits efficacy in deep anterior segment inflammation; lower IOP elevation risk than prednisolone acetate; suitable for surface inflammation

### Dexamethasone 0.1% (Maxidex®)

- **Classification:** Very high potency
- **Formulation:** Ophthalmic suspension or solution
- **Indications:** Severe anterior segment inflammation, postoperative inflammation
- **Key considerations:** Highest anti-inflammatory potency among commonly used topical agents; correspondingly higher risk of IOP elevation and cataract

### Dexamethasone Intravitreal Implant 0.7 mg (Ozurdex®)

- **Classification:** Sustained-release intravitreal corticosteroid
- **Formulation:** Biodegradable poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) implant
- **Indications:** [[Macular edema]] following [[branch retinal vein occlusion]] (BRVO) or [[central retinal vein occlusion]] (CRVO); [[diabetic macular edema]]; [[non-infectious posterior uveitis]]
- **Duration of action:** Approximately 3–6 months
- **Key considerations:** Requires in-office intravitreal injection; IOP elevation occurs in ~30% of patients; cataract progression in phakic eyes

### Fluocinolone Acetonide Intravitreal Implant (Retisert® / Iluvien®)

- **Retisert® (0.59 mg):** Surgically implanted, non-biodegradable; releases fluocinolone acetonide for approximately 30 months; indicated for chronic [[non-infectious posterior uveitis]]; very high rates of IOP elevation (~60%) and cataract (~100% in phakic eyes)
- **Iluvien® (0.19 mg):** Injectable, non-biodegradable micro-insert; releases fluocinolone acetonide for up to 36 months; indicated for [[diabetic macular edema]] in patients previously treated with corticosteroids without clinically significant IOP rise

### Triamcinolone Acetonide (Kenalog®, Triesence®)

- **Routes:** Intravitreal injection (typically 1–4 mg), sub-Tenon's injection (20–40 mg)
- **Indications:** DME, CME, uveitis, visualization during [[vitrectomy]] (Triesence®)
- **Key considerations:** Preservative-free formulation (Triesence®) preferred for intravitreal use; risk of [[sterile endophthalmitis]] (especially with preserved formulations), IOP elevation, cataract

## Combination Products

Several ophthalmic corticosteroids are available in fixed-dose combinations with [[antibiotics]] for postoperative prophylaxis and treatment of steroid-responsive conditions with concurrent bacterial infection risk:

| **Combination** | **Brand Example** | **Typical Use** |
|---|---|---|
| Dexamethasone + [[Tobramycin]] | TobraDex®, TobraDex ST® | Post-surgical inflammation with infection prophylaxis |
| Dexamethasone + [[Neomycin]] + [[Polymyxin B]] | Maxitrol® | Post-surgical, external ocular inflammation |
| Prednisolone acetate + [[Moxifloxacin]] | — | Post-cataract surgery |
| Loteprednol + Tobramycin | Zylet® | Steroid-responsive conditions with infection risk |
| Dexamethasone + [[Netilmicin]] | — (available in some markets) | Post-surgical inflammation |

**Note:** Combination products should not be used as a substitute for targeted therapy. The antibiotic component should be appropriate for the suspected or confirmed pathogen, and prolonged use of combination products increases the risk of [[antibiotic resistance]] and corticosteroid side effects.

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Patients

- Children are at **higher risk** for steroid-induced IOP elevation and systemic absorption (due to lower body mass and higher surface-area-to-volume ratio).
- [[Amblyopia]] may develop if steroid-induced cataract or uncontrolled IOP causes visual deprivation during the critical period of [[visual development]].
- Close monitoring with frequent IOP checks (under sedation or [[examination under anesthesia]] if necessary) is essential.
- Prefer lower-potency agents (loteprednol, fluorometholone) when clinically appropriate.

### Pregnant and Lactating Patients

- Topical ophthalmic corticosteroids are generally considered **low risk** due to minimal systemic absorption, but should be used only when the benefit justifies the potential risk.
- Systemic corticosteroids in pregnancy are associated with a small increased risk of [[oral cleft]] in the first trimester.
- [[Punctal occlusion]] and eyelid closure after instillation can further minimize systemic absorption.

### Contact Lens Wearers

- Patients should generally **remove [[contact lenses]]** before instilling corticosteroid drops and wait at least 10–15 minutes before reinsertion.
- Preservatives (e.g., [[benzalkonium chloride]]) in some formulations may be absorbed by soft contact lenses.

## Emerging Therapies and Future Directions

- **Novel drug delivery systems:** [[Nanoparticle]]-based formulations, [[liposomal]] carriers, [[hydrogel]] inserts, and [[punctal plug]] drug delivery systems aim to improve bioavailability, reduce dosing frequency, and minimize side effects.
- **Sustained-release topical formulations:** Extended-release technologies (e.g., KPI-121 submicron loteprednol) are being developed for chronic conditions like dry eye disease.
- **Selective glucocorticoid receptor agonists (SEGRAs):** Compounds designed to preferentially activate transrepression (anti-inflammatory) over transactivation (side effect-producing) pathways, potentially offering anti-inflammatory efficacy with reduced IOP elevation and cataract risk.
- **Gene therapy approaches:** Targeting the [[myocilin]] gene or TM cell biology to prevent steroid-induced IOP elevation.
- **Suprachoroidal drug delivery:** Injection of triamcinolone acetonide into the [[suprachoroidal space]] (e.g., Xipere® [triamcinolone acetonide injectable suspension for suprachoroidal use]) for targeted posterior segment delivery with potentially reduced anterior segment side effects. Xipere® is FDA-approved for [[macular edema]] associated with [[uveitis]].

## Contraindications

Ophthalmic corticosteroids are **contraindicated** or should be used with extreme caution in the following situations:

- Active [[herpes simplex]] epithelial keratitis (dendritic or geographic ulcer) without concurrent antiviral therapy
- Active [[fungal keratitis]] or suspected fungal infection
- Active [[bacterial keratitis]] without appropriate antibiotic coverage
- [[Mycobacterial]] ocular infection
- [[Acanthamoeba keratitis]]
- Known hypersensitivity to the specific corticosteroid or any formulation component
- [[Vaccinia]], [[varicella]], and other viral diseases of the cornea and conjunctiva (relative contraindication)

---

## Summary

Ophthalmic corticosteroids remain indispensable in the management of ocular inflammation across a vast spectrum of anterior and posterior segment diseases. Their unparalleled anti-inflammatory potency—achieved through suppression of the [[arachidonic acid]] cascade, inhibition of pro-inflammatory transcription factors, and stabilization of vascular and cellular barriers—makes them the treatment of choice for conditions ranging from acute [[anterior uveitis]] to postoperative inflammation and [[macular edema]]. However, their well-documented adverse effects—particularly [[steroid-induced glaucoma]], [[posterior subcapsular cataract]], and increased infection susceptibility—mandate a disciplined approach to prescribing: selecting the appropriate agent and potency, employing the shortest effective duration, implementing rigorous IOP and lens monitoring, and educating patients about the importance of adherence to tapering schedules and follow-up appointments. The development of soft steroids, sustained-release implants, novel delivery systems, and selective glucocorticoid receptor agonists continues to expand the therapeutic armamentarium while striving to improve the safety profile of this essential drug class.
