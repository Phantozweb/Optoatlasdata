# Anisocoria

**Anisocoria** is a clinical condition defined by an inequality in the diameter of the two [[pupils]]. Derived from the Greek words *anisos* (unequal) and *kore* (pupil), anisocoria is one of the most commonly encountered neuro-ophthalmic signs in clinical optometric and ophthalmologic practice. While a small degree of pupillary asymmetry (up to 0.4 mm) may be observed in a significant proportion of the normal population—termed **simple** or **physiologic anisocoria**—clinically significant anisocoria demands a systematic and thorough evaluation to differentiate benign etiologies from potentially life-threatening neurological, pharmacological, or structural causes. The condition may be transient or persistent, unilateral or alternating, and may involve either the larger or the smaller pupil as the abnormal one. Accurate diagnosis hinges on a detailed understanding of the [[autonomic nervous system]] pathways governing pupillary function, meticulous clinical examination, and judicious use of pharmacological testing.

---

## Epidemiology

Anisocoria is exceedingly common in the general population. Studies have demonstrated that approximately **15–30%** of healthy individuals exhibit a measurable difference in pupil size at any given time, though this difference is typically ≤1 mm and is termed **[[physiologic anisocoria]]** (also known as **simple anisocoria** or **essential anisocoria**). Physiologic anisocoria is characterized by the following features:

- The degree of asymmetry remains relatively constant in both dim and bright illumination.
- Both pupils react normally to light and near stimuli.
- The asymmetry may vary from day to day and may even alternate sides.
- It is more commonly detected in lighter-colored [[irides]].

Pathologic anisocoria, by contrast, is far less common and may present at any age. Its prevalence is directly tied to the underlying etiology, ranging from benign conditions such as [[Adie tonic pupil]] (more common in young women) to emergent conditions such as [[third cranial nerve palsy]] secondary to posterior communicating artery [[aneurysm]].

## Anatomy and Physiology of the Pupillary Pathways

A thorough understanding of anisocoria requires mastery of the dual autonomic innervation of the [[iris]] musculature.

### Parasympathetic (Pupilloconstrictor) Pathway

The [[parasympathetic nervous system]] mediates pupillary constriction ([[miosis]]) via the following pathway:

1. **Pretectal nucleus** in the [[midbrain]] receives afferent input from the [[retina]] via the [[optic nerve]], [[optic chiasm]], and [[optic tract]].
2. Signals project bilaterally to the **[[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]]** (the parasympathetic subnucleus of the [[oculomotor nerve]] complex).
3. Preganglionic parasympathetic fibers travel within the [[oculomotor nerve]] (cranial nerve III), running superficially along the nerve—making them vulnerable to compression by aneurysms or mass lesions.
4. These fibers synapse in the **[[ciliary ganglion]]**, located in the posterior [[orbit]].
5. Postganglionic fibers travel via the **[[short ciliary nerves]]** to innervate the **[[sphincter pupillae]]** muscle of the iris.

### Sympathetic (Pupillodilator) Pathway

The [[sympathetic nervous system]] mediates pupillary dilation ([[mydriasis]]) via a three-neuron arc:

1. **First-order (central) neuron**: Originates in the posterolateral [[hypothalamus]], descends through the [[brainstem]] (uncrossed), and synapses in the **ciliospinal center of Budge** at the C8–T2 level of the [[spinal cord]].
2. **Second-order (preganglionic) neuron**: Exits the spinal cord, traverses the pulmonary apex (near the [[lung]] apex and [[subclavian artery]]), ascends along the [[carotid artery]], and synapses in the **[[superior cervical ganglion]]**.
3. **Third-order (postganglionic) neuron**: Ascends along the [[internal carotid artery]], enters the [[cavernous sinus]], joins the [[ophthalmic division of the trigeminal nerve]] (V1), and reaches the orbit via the [[nasociliary nerve]] and **[[long ciliary nerves]]** to innervate the **[[dilator pupillae]]** muscle, as well as **[[Müller's muscle]]** of the upper eyelid and smooth muscle of the lower eyelid.

Disruption at any point along either pathway produces anisocoria—parasympathetic disruption results in a larger pupil on the affected side, while sympathetic disruption results in a smaller pupil on the affected side.

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of anisocoria is fundamentally determined by which limb of the autonomic pupillary pathway is affected and at what anatomical level the lesion occurs.

### Anisocoria with a Pathologically Large Pupil (Parasympathetic Defect)

When the parasympathetic pathway is disrupted, the [[sphincter pupillae]] muscle on the affected side fails to constrict adequately, resulting in a **mydriatic** (dilated) pupil. The anisocoria is **greater in bright light** because the normal pupil constricts while the affected pupil cannot. Key etiologies include:

| Condition | Mechanism | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Third cranial nerve palsy]]** | Compression or ischemia of CN III disrupts parasympathetic fibers | Ptosis, ophthalmoplegia, "down and out" eye; compressive lesions (e.g., [[posterior communicating artery aneurysm]]) more likely to involve pupil than ischemic (e.g., diabetic) |
| **[[Adie tonic pupil]]** | Damage to the [[ciliary ganglion]] or [[short ciliary nerves]], often post-viral | Dilated pupil with light-near dissociation, segmental iris sphincter palsy, tonic near response, denervation supersensitivity to dilute pilocarpine (0.1%) |
| **Pharmacologic mydriasis** | Topical exposure to [[anticholinergic]] or [[sympathomimetic]] agents (e.g., [[atropine]], [[tropicamide]], [[cyclopentolate]], [[scopolamine]]) | Fixed, dilated pupil; does not constrict to pilocarpine 1%; history of exposure (occupational, accidental, or intentional) |
| **Traumatic mydriasis** | Direct blunt trauma to the [[globe]] causing sphincter tears or [[iridodialysis]] | Irregular pupil, history of trauma, may see sphincter tears on [[slit lamp]] examination |
| **Iris damage** | Surgical trauma (e.g., post-[[cataract surgery]]), [[anterior uveitis]], [[angle-closure glaucoma]] | Irregular pupil, posterior [[synechiae]], sector atrophy |

### Anisocoria with a Pathologically Small Pupil (Sympathetic Defect)

When the sympathetic pathway is disrupted, the [[dilator pupillae]] muscle on the affected side fails to dilate adequately, resulting in a **miotic** (constricted) pupil. The anisocoria is **greater in dim illumination** (or darkness) because the normal pupil dilates while the affected pupil cannot. The hallmark condition is:

| Condition | Mechanism | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Horner syndrome]]** | Disruption of the three-neuron sympathetic pathway at any level (central, preganglionic, or postganglionic) | Classic triad: ipsilateral [[miosis]], [[ptosis]] (1–2 mm due to Müller's muscle paresis), and facial [[anhidrosis]] (variable depending on lesion level); dilation lag of the affected pupil in darkness |
| **Pharmacologic miosis** | Topical exposure to [[cholinergic]] agents (e.g., [[pilocarpine]]) or [[opioids]] | Small pupil that may not dilate to topical sympathomimetics; history of exposure |
| **Mechanical miosis** | [[Posterior synechiae]], chronic [[anterior uveitis]], iris sphincter fibrosis | Irregular pupil, visible adhesions on slit lamp |

### Physiologic Anisocoria

In **[[physiologic anisocoria]]**, there is no underlying autonomic defect. The asymmetry is thought to arise from a mild imbalance in supranuclear inhibitory input to the [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]]. The degree of anisocoria remains essentially constant across all lighting conditions, and both pupils react briskly and symmetrically to light and near stimuli.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Anisocoria itself is a **sign** rather than a symptom. Many patients are entirely asymptomatic and the pupillary asymmetry is discovered incidentally during routine eye examination or noted by a family member or friend. However, depending on the underlying etiology, patients may present with a variety of associated symptoms:

### Symptoms Directly Related to Pupillary Asymmetry
- **Cosmetic concern**: Patients or their contacts may notice the unequal pupil sizes.
- **Photophobia**: A pathologically dilated pupil allows excessive light entry, causing discomfort in bright environments.
- **Blurred near vision**: Parasympathetic dysfunction (e.g., [[Adie tonic pupil]]) may impair [[accommodation]], leading to difficulty with near tasks, particularly in younger patients.
- **Dim vision in low light**: A pathologically miotic pupil (e.g., [[Horner syndrome]]) may reduce retinal illumination in scotopic conditions, though this is rarely a significant complaint.

### Symptoms Related to the Underlying Etiology
- **Headache, diplopia, ptosis**: Suggestive of [[third cranial nerve palsy]], potentially from an [[intracranial aneurysm]], [[cavernous sinus]] lesion, or [[uncal herniation]].
- **Ipsilateral ptosis and anhidrosis**: Classic for [[Horner syndrome]]; associated symptoms depend on the level of the lesion (e.g., neck pain or arm pain with [[Pancoast tumor]], lateral medullary symptoms with [[Wallenberg syndrome]]).
- **Pain**: Ocular or periorbital pain may accompany [[acute angle-closure glaucoma]], [[anterior uveitis]], [[optic neuritis]], or compressive CN III palsy.
- **Recent trauma or surgery**: Relevant history for traumatic or iatrogenic causes.

## Diagnosis

The diagnostic evaluation of anisocoria follows a systematic, stepwise approach designed to localize the abnormal pupil and identify the underlying etiology.

### Step 1: Determine Which Pupil Is Abnormal

This is the single most critical step. The clinician must observe the pupils under **both bright and dim illumination**:

| Observation | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Anisocoria **greater in bright light** | The **larger** pupil is abnormal (parasympathetic defect—failure to constrict) |
| Anisocoria **greater in dim light (darkness)** | The **smaller** pupil is abnormal (sympathetic defect—failure to dilate) |
| Anisocoria **equal in bright and dim light** | Likely **[[physiologic anisocoria]]** |

**Dilation lag** is a particularly useful sign: in dim illumination, the affected pupil in [[Horner syndrome]] dilates more slowly than the normal pupil. This can be observed at 5 seconds versus 15 seconds after lights are dimmed—the anisocoria is greater at 5 seconds and diminishes by 15 seconds as the Horner pupil slowly catches up.

### Step 2: Assess Pupillary Light Reflexes

Both the **direct** and **consensual** [[pupillary light reflex]] should be tested. A [[relative afferent pupillary defect]] (RAPD or [[Marcus Gunn pupil]]) should be assessed with the **[[swinging flashlight test]]**. It is critical to note that an RAPD indicates an **afferent** (optic nerve or retinal) defect and does **not** cause anisocoria in ambient lighting—it is a separate finding that may coexist with anisocoria.

### Step 3: Examine the Iris and Anterior Segment

[[Slit lamp biomicroscopy]] should be performed to evaluate for:
- Sphincter tears or [[iridodialysis]] (traumatic mydriasis)
- [[Posterior synechiae]] (uveitis)
- Iris atrophy or transillumination defects (Adie pupil, [[herpes zoster ophthalmicus]])
- Iris neovascularization ([[neovascular glaucoma]])
- Segmental vermiform movements of the iris sphincter (characteristic of [[Adie tonic pupil]])
- Evidence of prior surgery (e.g., [[peripheral iridotomy]], [[cataract surgery]])

### Step 4: Pharmacological Testing

Pharmacological testing is the cornerstone of the diagnostic workup for anisocoria and allows precise localization of the lesion.

#### Testing for Parasympathetic Defect (Large Pupil)

| Agent | Concentration | Expected Result | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Pilocarpine]]** (dilute) | **0.1%** | Constriction of the affected (large) pupil | Positive for **[[Adie tonic pupil]]** (denervation supersensitivity of the sphincter) |
| **[[Pilocarpine]]** (standard) | **1%** | Constriction of both pupils | Rules out pharmacologic blockade; if the large pupil does NOT constrict to 1% pilocarpine, suspect **pharmacologic mydriasis** (e.g., atropine exposure) |

#### Testing for Sympathetic Defect (Small Pupil) — Confirming Horner Syndrome

| Agent | Concentration | Expected Result | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Cocaine]]** (topical) | **4–10%** | Normal pupil dilates; Horner pupil fails to dilate (or dilates poorly) | Confirms **[[Horner syndrome]]** (cocaine blocks norepinephrine reuptake; if sympathetic pathway is disrupted, there is insufficient norepinephrine at the synapse) |
| **[[Apraclonidine]]** (topical) | **0.5–1%** | Reversal of anisocoria—Horner pupil dilates (due to denervation supersensitivity to alpha-1 agonism), normal pupil constricts slightly (alpha-2 effect) | Confirms **[[Horner syndrome]]**; increasingly used as an alternative to cocaine due to availability |

#### Localizing the Level of Horner Syndrome

| Agent | Concentration | Expected Result | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Hydroxyamphetamine]]** (Paredrine) | **1%** | Causes release of norepinephrine from intact postganglionic nerve terminals | **Dilation** of Horner pupil → lesion is **preganglionic** (1st or 2nd order); **No dilation** → lesion is **postganglionic** (3rd order, nerve terminal is damaged and cannot release norepinephrine) |

> **Clinical Pearl**: Hydroxyamphetamine testing must be performed on a separate day from cocaine or apraclonidine testing to avoid confounding results.

### Step 5: Neuroimaging and Further Workup

The need for neuroimaging depends on the clinical context:

- **Acute third cranial nerve palsy with pupil involvement**: This is a **neurological emergency** until proven otherwise. Urgent **[[CT angiography]]** (CTA) or **[[MR angiography]]** (MRA) of the head is required to rule out a [[posterior communicating artery aneurysm]]. If non-invasive imaging is negative but clinical suspicion remains high, **[[catheter angiography]]** (digital subtraction angiography, DSA) may be necessary.
- **Horner syndrome**: Imaging is guided by the localization:
  - **Central (1st order)**: **[[MRI]]** of the brain and [[brainstem]] (to evaluate for stroke, demyelination, tumor).
  - **Preganglionic (2nd order)**: **[[CT]]** or **[[MRI]]** of the chest, neck, and upper thoracic spine (to evaluate for [[Pancoast tumor]], [[lymphadenopathy]], [[carotid dissection]], thyroid pathology).
  - **Postganglionic (3rd order)**: **[[MRI]]/[[MRA]]** of the head and neck with attention to the [[cavernous sinus]] and [[internal carotid artery]] (to evaluate for [[carotid dissection]], cavernous sinus lesion, or cluster headache).
- **Adie tonic pupil**: Typically benign; neuroimaging is generally not required unless atypical features are present.
- **Physiologic anisocoria**: No further workup is needed once confirmed.

### Examination of Old Photographs

Reviewing old photographs (e.g., driver's license, social media) is an invaluable and often underutilized diagnostic tool. If anisocoria is present in old photographs, it suggests a longstanding or congenital etiology and may reduce the urgency of the workup.

## Differential Diagnosis

The differential diagnosis of anisocoria can be organized by the size of the abnormal pupil:

### Large (Mydriatic) Abnormal Pupil

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| [[Third cranial nerve palsy]] | Ptosis, ophthalmoplegia, "down and out" eye; pupil involvement suggests compressive etiology |
| [[Adie tonic pupil]] | Light-near dissociation, segmental palsy, tonic accommodation, supersensitivity to dilute pilocarpine |
| Pharmacologic mydriasis | Fixed, dilated pupil; no response to 1% pilocarpine; history of agent exposure |
| Traumatic mydriasis / [[iridodialysis]] | History of trauma, sphincter tears visible on slit lamp |
| [[Acute angle-closure glaucoma]] | Mid-dilated, fixed pupil; severe pain, nausea, corneal edema, elevated [[intraocular pressure]] |
| Iris sphincter damage (surgical/inflammatory) | History of surgery or [[uveitis]], posterior synechiae, irregular pupil |
| [[Tadpole pupil]] | Transient, segmental dilation with a "tadpole" shape; benign, episodic |

### Small (Miotic) Abnormal Pupil

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| [[Horner syndrome]] | Miosis, ptosis (1–2 mm), anhidrosis (variable), dilation lag |
| Pharmacologic miosis | History of pilocarpine, opioid, or organophosphate exposure |
| [[Anterior uveitis]] with posterior synechiae | Pain, photophobia, cells and flare in [[anterior chamber]], irregular pupil |
| Chronic miotic use | History of topical miotic therapy (e.g., for [[glaucoma]]) |
| [[Argyll Robertson pupil]] | Bilateral small, irregular pupils; light-near dissociation; associated with neurosyphilis ([[Treponema pallidum]]) |

## Management and Treatment

The management of anisocoria is directed entirely at the **underlying etiology**, as anisocoria itself is a sign rather than a disease. There is no treatment for the pupillary asymmetry per se in most cases, though symptomatic relief may be provided.

### Physiologic Anisocoria
- **No treatment required.** Reassurance and patient education are sufficient.

### Adie Tonic Pupil
- Generally benign and self-limited.
- **Dilute [[pilocarpine]]** (0.1–0.125%) may be prescribed for cosmetic improvement or to alleviate photophobia and accommodative symptoms, though its use is often impractical due to frequent dosing requirements.
- **Reading glasses** or **[[progressive addition lenses]]** may be prescribed for accommodative insufficiency.
- Long-term follow-up is recommended, as the pupil may become progressively miotic over years due to aberrant reinnervation, and [[deep tendon reflexes]] may diminish (Holmes-Adie syndrome).

### Third Cranial Nerve Palsy
- **Compressive etiology** (e.g., [[posterior communicating artery aneurysm]]): **Neurosurgical emergency**. Immediate referral for neurosurgical or neurointerventional management (clipping or coiling of the aneurysm).
- **Ischemic etiology** (e.g., diabetic or hypertensive microvasculopathy): Typically spares the pupil; management involves control of vascular risk factors ([[diabetes mellitus]], [[hypertension]], [[hyperlipidemia]]). Spontaneous recovery usually occurs within 3 months. If the pupil is involved in a presumed ischemic palsy, imaging should still be performed.
- **Other compressive lesions** (e.g., tumor, [[uncal herniation]]): Directed neurosurgical management.
- **Symptomatic management**: [[Occlusion therapy]] or [[prism]] for diplopia; [[ptosis crutch]] or surgical correction for ptosis after stable alignment is achieved.

### Horner Syndrome
- Treatment is directed at the underlying cause:
  - **[[Carotid dissection]]**: Anticoagulation or antiplatelet therapy as directed by neurology/vascular surgery.
  - **[[Pancoast tumor]]**: Oncologic management (surgery, radiation, chemotherapy).
  - **[[Brainstem stroke]]**: Acute stroke management and secondary prevention.
  - **Idiopathic**: Observation and follow-up.
- The miosis and ptosis of Horner syndrome are generally mild and rarely require specific treatment. In rare cases where cosmesis is a concern, **[[phenylephrine]]** 2.5% drops may temporarily improve the ptosis and anisocoria, though this is not a practical long-term solution.

### Pharmacologic Anisocoria
- **Identification and removal of the offending agent** is the primary intervention.
- Patient education regarding avoidance of inadvertent exposure (e.g., [[scopolamine]] patches, gardening with [[Datura]] or [[Brugmansia]] plants, occupational exposure to aerosolized medications).
- The pupil will return to normal once the pharmacologic effect wears off (hours to days depending on the agent).

### Traumatic Mydriasis
- Observation in most cases; the pupil may recover partially or fully over weeks to months.
- If symptomatic photophobia is significant, **tinted lenses** or **photochromic lenses** may be prescribed.
- Surgical repair of iris sphincter tears is rarely performed but may be considered in severe cases with debilitating symptoms.

### Anterior Uveitis with Synechiae
- Treatment of the underlying [[uveitis]] with topical [[corticosteroids]] and [[cycloplegic agents]] (e.g., [[cyclopentolate]], [[atropine]]) to reduce inflammation and prevent further synechiae formation.
- Lysis of synechiae may be attempted with intensive mydriatic/cycloplegic therapy.

## Special Considerations

### Anisocoria in the Pediatric Population

Anisocoria in infants and children warrants careful evaluation. **Congenital Horner syndrome** may present with [[iris heterochromia]] (the affected iris is lighter due to impaired melanocyte stimulation by the sympathetic nervous system), miosis, and ptosis. This may be associated with [[neuroblastoma]], and urine catecholamine levels (vanillylmandelic acid [VMA] and homovanillic acid [HVA]) and chest/abdominal imaging should be obtained. Physiologic anisocoria is also common in children and should be differentiated from pathologic causes.

### Anisocoria in the Emergency Department

In the acute care setting, anisocoria—particularly a **new, unilateral fixed dilated pupil**—is a critical finding that may indicate:
- **[[Uncal herniation]]** (transtentorial herniation compressing CN III): A neurosurgical emergency.
- **Ruptured [[intracranial aneurysm]]**: Requires emergent vascular imaging.
- **Traumatic CN III injury**: Associated with head trauma.

Rapid assessment and neuroimaging are paramount in these scenarios.

### Anisocoria and Contact Lens Wear

Rarely, [[contact lens]] wear—particularly rigid gas permeable ([[RGP]]) lenses—may cause transient mechanical irritation of the iris or corneal nerves, leading to mild anisocoria. This is typically benign and resolves with lens removal.

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis of anisocoria depends entirely on the underlying etiology:

- **Physiologic anisocoria**: Excellent; no clinical significance.
- **Adie tonic pupil**: Generally excellent; the condition is benign, though the pupil may become progressively miotic and accommodation may remain impaired.
- **Horner syndrome**: Prognosis depends on the cause; idiopathic cases have an excellent prognosis, while those secondary to malignancy or carotid dissection carry the prognosis of the underlying disease.
- **Third cranial nerve palsy**: Ischemic palsies typically resolve within 3 months; compressive palsies carry a guarded prognosis depending on the nature and treatability of the compressive lesion.
- **Pharmacologic anisocoria**: Excellent; resolves completely once the agent is eliminated.
- **Traumatic mydriasis**: Variable; some patients recover full pupillary function, while others have permanent mydriasis.

## Summary Algorithm for Clinical Evaluation

```
Anisocoria Detected
        │
        ▼
Measure in BRIGHT and DIM light
        │
        ├── Greater in BRIGHT light → Larger pupil is abnormal
        │       │
        │       ├── Check for CN III signs (ptosis, motility) → Yes → URGENT imaging (CTA/MRA)
        │       │
        │       ├── Slit lamp: sphincter tears? → Traumatic mydriasis
        │       │
        │       ├── Pilocarpine 1%: No constriction → Pharmacologic mydriasis
        │       │
        │       └── Pilocarpine 0.1%: Constriction → Adie tonic pupil
        │
        ├── Greater in DIM light → Smaller pupil is abnormal
        │       │
        │       ├── Cocaine 4-10% or Apraclonidine 0.5-1% → Confirms Horner syndrome
        │       │
        │       └── Hydroxyamphetamine 1% → Localizes lesion (pre- vs. postganglionic)
        │
        └── EQUAL in both conditions → Physiologic anisocoria (reassure)
```
