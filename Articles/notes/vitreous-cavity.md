

# Vitreous Cavity

---

The **vitreous cavity** (also referred to as the **vitreous chamber**, **posterior cavity**, or **camera vitrea bulbi**) is the largest anatomical compartment of the [[human eye]], occupying approximately two-thirds of the total ocular volume. It is bounded anteriorly by the [[crystalline lens]], [[zonular fibers]], and the [[ciliary body]], and posteriorly by the [[retina]]. The vitreous cavity contains the [[vitreous humor]] (also called the vitreous body or *corpus vitreum*), a transparent, avascular, gel-like substance that plays critical roles in maintaining ocular structure, optical clarity, and metabolic homeostasis. Understanding the anatomy, physiology, pathophysiology, and clinical significance of the vitreous cavity is essential for optometrists, ophthalmologists, and all eye care professionals, as numerous sight-threatening conditions originate within or involve this space.

---

## Gross and Microanatomy

### Dimensions and Volume

The vitreous cavity in the adult human eye has an approximate volume of **4.0–5.0 mL**, accounting for roughly 80% of the globe's total volume. Its axial length from the posterior surface of the [[crystalline lens]] to the [[retina]] is approximately **16.5 mm** in an emmetropic eye. The cavity is roughly spherical, with a slight anterior concavity (the **patellar fossa** or **hyaloid fossa**) that conforms to the posterior curvature of the lens.

### Boundaries

| Boundary | Structure |
|---|---|
| **Anterior** | Posterior surface of the [[crystalline lens]], [[zonular fibers]] (of [[Zinn]]), [[ciliary body]] |
| **Lateral** | [[Pars plana]] and [[pars plicata]] of the ciliary body |
| **Posterior** | Internal limiting membrane ([[ILM]]) of the [[retina]] |
| **Inferior** | Retinal surface overlying the [[inferior retina]] |

### Vitreous Body Composition

The vitreous humor that fills the vitreous cavity is composed of approximately **98–99% water**, with the remaining 1–2% consisting of:

- **[[Collagen]] fibrils** — predominantly [[type II collagen]], with smaller amounts of types V/XI, VI, and IX. These fibrils form a delicate meshwork that provides structural integrity.
- **[[Hyaluronic acid]]** (hyaluronan) — a large glycosaminoglycan (GAG) that fills the spaces between collagen fibrils, contributing to the gel-like consistency and viscoelastic properties.
- **Electrolytes and low-molecular-weight solutes** — including sodium, potassium, chloride, glucose, lactate, and ascorbic acid (present in notably high concentrations, serving as an antioxidant).
- **Proteins** — present in very low concentrations, including albumin, transferrin, and various growth factors.

### Structural Regions

The vitreous body is not homogeneous. Several distinct structural regions are recognized:

- **Vitreous cortex** — the outermost layer, approximately 100–300 μm thick, which is denser and contains a higher concentration of collagen fibrils. The **posterior vitreous cortex** is adherent to the [[internal limiting membrane]] (ILM) of the retina, while the **anterior vitreous cortex** (anterior hyaloid face) lies behind the lens.
- **Central vitreous (core vitreous)** — the bulk of the vitreous body, with a looser collagen network.
- **[[Cloquet's canal]]** (hyaloid canal) — a narrow, tubular remnant of the embryonic [[hyaloid artery]] that runs from the [[optic disc]] to the posterior surface of the lens. In the adult, it is typically optically empty but may be visible on [[optical coherence tomography]] (OCT) or [[B-scan ultrasonography]].
- **[[Berger's space]]** — a potential space between the anterior vitreous face and the posterior lens capsule.
- **[[Erggelet's space]]** (or the space of Erggelet) — a potential space within the vitreous near the patellar fossa.
- **Vitreous base** — a band approximately 3–6 mm wide that straddles the [[ora serrata]], extending 1.5–2 mm anterior to and 1–3 mm posterior to it. This is the site of the **strongest vitreoretinal adhesion** and is clinically significant because it is virtually impossible to separate the vitreous from the retina at this location.

### Key Vitreoretinal Adhesion Sites

The vitreous cortex is attached to the retina at several clinically important locations:

| Adhesion Site | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|
| **[[Vitreous base]]** | Strongest adhesion; virtually inseparable; site of [[retinal tears]] during [[posterior vitreous detachment]] (PVD) |
| **[[Optic disc]] margins** | Peripapillary adhesion; [[Weiss ring]] forms upon separation |
| **[[Fovea]] / [[Macula]]** | Vitreomacular adhesion; pathological traction leads to [[vitreomacular traction syndrome]] (VMT) and [[macular hole]] |
| **Retinal blood vessels** | Adhesion to major [[retinal vessels]]; traction may cause [[vitreous hemorrhage]] |
| **Sites of [[lattice degeneration]]** | Abnormally strong adhesion at areas of retinal thinning; predisposes to [[retinal tears]] and [[retinal detachment]] |

## Embryology and Development

The vitreous cavity and its contents develop through three sequential stages during [[embryogenesis]]:

### Primary Vitreous

During the first month of gestation (approximately weeks 3–6), the **primary vitreous** forms between the developing [[lens vesicle]] and the [[optic cup]]. It is a vascularized, cellular structure containing mesenchymal cells, [[hyaloid vasculature]], and fibrils. The [[hyaloid artery]], a branch of the [[ophthalmic artery]], traverses the primary vitreous from the [[optic disc]] to the posterior lens surface, providing nutrition to the developing lens.

### Secondary Vitreous

From approximately weeks 6–13, the **secondary vitreous** begins to form around the primary vitreous. It is avascular, acellular, and transparent — closely resembling the adult vitreous. The secondary vitreous gradually compresses the primary vitreous centrally, forming what will become [[Cloquet's canal]].

### Tertiary Vitreous

The **tertiary vitreous** develops during the third to fourth month and gives rise to the [[zonular fibers]] (zonules of Zinn) that suspend the crystalline lens from the [[ciliary body]].

### Regression of the Hyaloid System

The [[hyaloid artery]] and its associated vasculature (the **vasa hyaloidea propria** and the **tunica vasculosa lentis**) normally undergo complete regression by the end of the seventh to eighth month of gestation. Failure of complete regression results in clinical entities such as:

- **[[Persistent fetal vasculature]]** (PFV), formerly known as persistent hyperplastic primary vitreous (PHPV)
- **[[Mittendorf's dot]]** — a small opacity on the posterior lens capsule representing the remnant of the anterior attachment of the hyaloid artery
- **[[Bergmeister's papilla]]** — a small glial tuft on the optic disc representing the remnant of the posterior attachment of the hyaloid artery

## Physiology and Biochemical Functions

The vitreous cavity and its contents serve multiple critical physiological functions:

### Structural Support

The vitreous body acts as a **space-filling, shock-absorbing medium** that maintains the shape of the globe and keeps the [[retina]] apposed to the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE). Its viscoelastic properties dampen mechanical forces transmitted to the eye during saccadic eye movements and external trauma.

### Optical Transparency

The vitreous humor must remain optically clear to allow unimpeded transmission of light from the [[crystalline lens]] to the [[retina]]. This transparency is maintained by:

- The extremely low concentration of cells and macromolecules
- The regular spacing of collagen fibrils (maintained by hyaluronic acid)
- The absence of blood vessels
- High concentrations of [[ascorbic acid]], which acts as an antioxidant and UV filter

### Metabolic Functions

The vitreous cavity serves as a **metabolic reservoir and transport medium**:

- It facilitates the diffusion of nutrients (glucose, amino acids) and oxygen to the avascular [[crystalline lens]] and inner retina.
- It removes metabolic waste products (lactate, CO₂) from the lens and retina.
- The high concentration of **ascorbic acid** (approximately 2 mM, roughly 20 times the plasma concentration) protects the lens and retina from oxidative damage, particularly from UV radiation and reactive oxygen species (ROS).

### Pharmacokinetic Reservoir

The vitreous cavity is increasingly recognized as a critical pharmacokinetic compartment for **intravitreal drug delivery**. The gel structure of the vitreous influences the distribution, diffusion, and clearance of intravitreally injected medications, including [[anti-VEGF]] agents (e.g., [[ranibizumab]], [[aflibercept]], [[bevacizumab]]), [[corticosteroids]] (e.g., [[triamcinolone acetonide]], [[dexamethasone]] implant), and [[antibiotics]].

## Pathophysiology

### Age-Related Vitreous Degeneration (Vitreous Syneresis and Synchysis)

The most common pathological process affecting the vitreous cavity is **age-related vitreous degeneration**, which involves two concurrent processes:

1. **[[Vitreous syneresis]]** — liquefaction of the vitreous gel. With aging, the collagen-hyaluronic acid structural relationship breaks down. Hyaluronic acid dissociates from collagen fibrils, leading to the formation of liquid-filled lacunae (**synchysis**) within the vitreous body. By age 80, more than 50% of the vitreous may be liquefied.

2. **Collagen fibril aggregation** — as hyaluronic acid separates from collagen, the fibrils aggregate into visible bundles, clinically perceived as **[[floaters]]** (muscae volitantes).

These degenerative changes are accelerated by:

- [[Myopia]] (particularly high myopia > -6.00 D)
- Prior [[cataract surgery]] or other intraocular surgery
- [[Ocular trauma]]
- [[Ocular inflammation]] ([[uveitis]])
- [[Diabetes mellitus]]

### Posterior Vitreous Detachment (PVD)

**[[Posterior vitreous detachment]]** (PVD) is the separation of the posterior vitreous cortex from the [[internal limiting membrane]] (ILM) of the retina. It is the most clinically significant consequence of vitreous degeneration and represents a watershed event in vitreous cavity pathology.

**Mechanism:** As vitreous liquefaction progresses, liquid vitreous dissects through a weakened posterior vitreous cortex, entering the potential space between the cortex and the ILM (the **subhyaloid space** or **retrohyaloid space**). This leads to progressive separation of the vitreous from the retina, typically beginning in the perifoveal region and extending peripherally.

**Epidemiology:** PVD occurs in approximately 24–27% of individuals aged 60–69 and in over 60% of those aged 70 and older. It is more common and occurs earlier in myopic eyes, post-surgical eyes, and eyes with a history of trauma or inflammation.

**Clinical Significance:** While PVD is usually benign, it can cause:

| Complication | Mechanism | Incidence |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Retinal tear]]** | Vitreous traction at sites of firm vitreoretinal adhesion (vitreous base, lattice degeneration) | 8–15% of acute symptomatic PVDs |
| **[[Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]]** | Liquid vitreous passes through a retinal tear, separating the neurosensory retina from the RPE | ~33–50% of untreated retinal tears |
| **[[Vitreous hemorrhage]]** | Traction on retinal blood vessels during PVD | 3–7% of acute PVDs |
| **[[Vitreomacular traction]] (VMT)** | Incomplete PVD with persistent foveal adhesion | Variable |
| **[[Macular hole]]** | Tangential or anteroposterior traction on the fovea | ~0.1–0.3% of PVDs |

The **[[Weiss ring]]** — a circular or oval opacity visible on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] — represents the detached peripapillary vitreous cortex and is pathognomonic of PVD.

### Anomalous Posterior Vitreous Detachment

When PVD is **incomplete** or **anomalous**, persistent vitreoretinal adhesion at specific sites can lead to chronic traction and a spectrum of pathologies collectively termed **vitreoretinal interface disorders**:

- **[[Vitreomacular traction syndrome]]** (VMT)
- **[[Epiretinal membrane]]** (ERM) / [[macular pucker]]
- **[[Full-thickness macular hole]]** (FTMH)
- **[[Lamellar macular hole]]**
- **[[Vitreomacular schisis]]**

### Vitreous Hemorrhage

**[[Vitreous hemorrhage]]** refers to the presence of blood within the vitreous cavity. It is one of the most common causes of acute, painless vision loss. Etiologies include:

- **[[Proliferative diabetic retinopathy]]** (PDR) — the most common cause in adults
- **PVD with retinal tear** — the second most common cause
- **[[Retinal vein occlusion]]** (RVO) with neovascularization
- **[[Ocular trauma]]** — blunt or penetrating
- **[[Subarachnoid hemorrhage]]** ([[Terson syndrome]])
- **[[Sickle cell retinopathy]]**
- **[[Age-related macular degeneration]]** (wet/neovascular AMD) — breakthrough hemorrhage
- **[[Retinal macroaneurysm]]**
- Anticoagulant therapy

### Vitreous Inflammation (Vitritis)

**[[Vitritis]]** refers to inflammatory cells within the vitreous cavity and is a hallmark of **[[intermediate uveitis]]** and **[[posterior uveitis]]**. Causes include:

- **Infectious:** [[Toxoplasmosis]], [[cytomegalovirus]] (CMV) retinitis, [[endophthalmitis]] (bacterial, fungal), [[toxocariasis]], [[syphilis]], [[tuberculosis]]
- **Non-infectious/autoimmune:** [[Sarcoidosis]], [[multiple sclerosis]]-associated intermediate uveitis, [[Behçet disease]], [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada]] (VKH) disease
- **Masquerade syndromes:** [[Primary vitreoretinal lymphoma]] (PVRL), [[retinoblastoma]] (in children)

Vitritis is graded clinically using the **National Eye Institute (NEI) / Standardization of Uveitis Nomenclature (SUN) grading scale** based on the clarity of the fundus view through the vitreous haze.

### Asteroid Hyalosis

**[[Asteroid hyalosis]]** is a common, usually unilateral, degenerative condition in which calcium-lipid complexes (calcium soaps, primarily calcium stearate and calcium palmitate) are suspended within the vitreous gel. It is found in approximately 1–2% of the general population and is more common in older individuals and those with [[diabetes mellitus]] and [[hypercholesterolemia]]. Despite the dramatic clinical appearance of numerous white, refractile bodies suspended in the vitreous, asteroid hyalosis is typically **asymptomatic** and rarely requires treatment.

### Synchysis Scintillans

**[[Synchysis scintillans]]** is a rare condition characterized by freely mobile, golden-brown **cholesterol crystals** within a liquefied vitreous. Unlike asteroid hyalosis, synchysis scintillans is associated with prior ocular disease (chronic inflammation, vitreous hemorrhage, or trauma) and the crystals settle inferiorly with gravity when the eye is stationary.

### Persistent Fetal Vasculature (PFV)

**[[Persistent fetal vasculature]]** (formerly persistent hyperplastic primary vitreous, PHPV) is a congenital condition resulting from failure of the embryonic [[hyaloid vasculature]] to regress. It may present as an anterior form (retrolental fibrovascular membrane, microphthalmos, shallow anterior chamber), a posterior form (stalk from disc to lens, retinal folds), or a combined form. PFV is typically unilateral and must be differentiated from [[retinoblastoma]].

### Vitreous Involvement in Systemic Disease

The vitreous cavity may be affected in several systemic conditions:

- **[[Amyloidosis]]** — vitreous opacities composed of amyloid deposits, particularly in familial amyloid polyneuropathy (FAP) associated with transthyretin (TTR) mutations
- **[[Whipple disease]]** — vitritis with *Tropheryma whipplei* organisms
- **[[Lymphoma]]** — primary vitreoretinal lymphoma (PVRL) presenting as vitritis refractory to corticosteroids

## Symptoms Associated with Vitreous Cavity Pathology

Patients with vitreous cavity disorders may present with a range of visual symptoms:

| Symptom | Possible Etiology |
|---|---|
| **[[Floaters]]** (muscae volitantes) | Vitreous syneresis, PVD, vitritis, vitreous hemorrhage, asteroid hyalosis |
| **[[Photopsia]]** (flashes of light) | Vitreoretinal traction (PVD, VMT), retinal tear |
| **Sudden increase in floaters** | Acute PVD, vitreous hemorrhage, retinal tear |
| **"Curtain" or "veil" over vision** | [[Retinal detachment]], dense vitreous hemorrhage |
| **Blurred or decreased vision** | Vitreous hemorrhage, vitritis, VMT, macular hole, vitreous opacities |
| **Metamorphopsia** | VMT, epiretinal membrane, macular hole |
| **Painless vision loss** | Vitreous hemorrhage, retinal detachment |

**Clinical Pearl:** The acute onset of floaters and/or photopsia warrants urgent dilated fundus examination to rule out retinal tears and retinal detachment, as approximately 8–15% of patients with acute symptomatic PVD will have a retinal tear.

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical Examination

- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]** with a **[[+90D lens]]**, **[[+78D lens]]**, or **[[Goldmann three-mirror contact lens]]** — allows detailed examination of the vitreous cavity, vitreoretinal interface, and peripheral retina. The presence of a [[Weiss ring]], [[Shafer's sign]] (tobacco dust / pigmented cells in the anterior vitreous indicating a retinal tear), vitreous cells, or vitreous hemorrhage can be assessed.
- **[[Binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy]]** (BIO) with **[[scleral indentation]]** — essential for examining the peripheral retina and vitreous base, particularly when retinal tears or detachment are suspected.
- **[[Direct ophthalmoscopy]]** — limited utility for vitreous assessment but may reveal gross vitreous opacities or hemorrhage.

### Imaging

| Modality | Application in Vitreous Cavity Assessment |
|---|---|
| **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** | Gold standard when media opacity (e.g., dense vitreous hemorrhage, cataract) precludes fundus visualization. Detects PVD, vitreous hemorrhage, retinal detachment, intraocular tumors, foreign bodies. |
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)** | High-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the vitreoretinal interface. Essential for diagnosing VMT, epiretinal membrane, macular hole, and vitreoschisis. Swept-source OCT and enhanced depth imaging (EDI-OCT) provide improved vitreous visualization. |
| **[[OCT angiography]] (OCTA)** | Evaluates retinal and choroidal vasculature; useful in assessing neovascularization associated with vitreous hemorrhage. |
| **[[Fundus photography]]** | Documents vitreous opacities, hemorrhage, and retinal pathology. |
| **[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA)** | Identifies sources of vitreous hemorrhage (neovascularization, macroaneurysm), evaluates retinal vascular disease. |
| **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | High-frequency ultrasound for anterior vitreous, ciliary body, and zonular assessment. |
| **[[Dynamic light scattering]] (DLS)** | Experimental technique for quantifying vitreous liquefaction and molecular changes. |

### Laboratory and Ancillary Testing

- **[[Vitreous biopsy]]** — indicated when intraocular lymphoma, infectious endophthalmitis, or atypical vitritis is suspected. Specimens are sent for cytology, flow cytometry, microbiology (culture, PCR for infectious agents), and interleukin analysis (IL-10:IL-6 ratio elevated in PVRL).
- **[[Cytokine analysis]]** — elevated IL-10 relative to IL-6 in vitreous aspirate is suggestive of [[primary vitreoretinal lymphoma]].
- **[[Polymerase chain reaction]] (PCR)** — for detection of viral DNA (e.g., CMV, HSV, VZV) or bacterial/fungal DNA in cases of infectious vitritis or endophthalmitis.

## Management and Treatment

### Observation

Many vitreous cavity conditions are benign and require only observation and patient education:

- **Uncomplicated PVD** without retinal tears — reassurance, education about warning symptoms (increase in floaters, flashes, visual field loss), and follow-up examination in 4–6 weeks.
- **Asteroid hyalosis** — typically no treatment required unless it significantly impairs vision or interferes with diagnostic imaging (e.g., fundus photography, OCT).
- **Mild vitreous floaters** — most patients adapt over time as floaters settle or become less noticeable.

### Pharmacological Interventions

#### Intravitreal Injections

The vitreous cavity is the target compartment for a rapidly expanding array of intravitreal pharmacotherapies:

| Drug Class | Examples | Indications |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Anti-VEGF]] agents** | [[Ranibizumab]] (Lucentis), [[Aflibercept]] (Eylea), [[Bevacizumab]] (Avastin), [[Brolucizumab]] (Beovu), [[Faricimab]] (Vabysmo) | [[Neovascular AMD]], [[diabetic macular edema]] (DME), [[retinal vein occlusion]] (RVO), [[proliferative diabetic retinopathy]] |
| **[[Corticosteroids]]** | [[Triamcinolone acetonide]], [[Dexamethasone]] intravitreal implant (Ozurdex), [[Fluocinolone acetonide]] implant (Iluvien, Retisert) | DME, uveitic macular edema, RVO |
| **[[Antibiotics]]** | [[Vancomycin]], [[ceftazidime]], [[amikacin]], intravitreal [[voriconazole]] | [[Endophthalmitis]] (bacterial, fungal) |
| **[[Antivirals]]** | Intravitreal [[ganciclovir]], [[foscarnet]] | [[CMV retinitis]] |
| **Enzymatic vitreolysis** | [[Ocriplasmin]] (Jetrea) | Symptomatic VMT, small full-thickness macular holes (≤400 μm) |

#### Ocriplasmin (Enzymatic Vitreolysis)

**[[Ocriplasmin]]** (Jetrea®) is a recombinant truncated form of human [[plasmin]] that enzymatically cleaves [[fibronectin]] and [[laminin]] at the vitreoretinal interface, inducing pharmacological vitreolysis. It is FDA-approved for the treatment of symptomatic [[vitreomacular traction]] (VMT) with or without a small (≤400 μm) full-thickness [[macular hole]]. The MIVI-TRUST clinical trials demonstrated VMT resolution in approximately 26.5% of ocriplasmin-treated eyes versus 10.1% of placebo-treated eyes.

### Surgical Approaches

#### Pars Plana Vitrectomy (PPV)

**[[Pars plana vitrectomy]]** (PPV) is the primary surgical intervention for vitreous cavity pathology. It involves the removal of vitreous humor through small-gauge instruments inserted through the [[pars plana]] (typically 3.5–4.0 mm posterior to the [[limbus]]).

**Gauge Systems:**

| Gauge | Instrument Diameter | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **20-gauge** | 0.89 mm | Traditional; requires conjunctival peritomy and sutured sclerotomies |
| **23-gauge** | 0.64 mm | Sutureless, transconjunctival; widely used |
| **25-gauge** | 0.51 mm | Sutureless, transconjunctival; most commonly used currently |
| **27-gauge** | 0.40 mm | Ultra-small gauge; reduced surgical trauma, faster recovery |

**Indications for PPV involving the vitreous cavity:**

- Non-clearing [[vitreous hemorrhage]] (typically observed for 1–3 months unless retinal detachment is suspected)
- [[Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]]
- [[Tractional retinal detachment]] (e.g., in PDR)
- [[Vitreomacular traction syndrome]] refractory to observation or ocriplasmin
- [[Full-thickness macular hole]]
- [[Epiretinal membrane]] removal
- [[Endophthalmitis]] (vitreous tap/biopsy and intravitreal antibiotic injection, with or without vitrectomy)
- Retained [[lens fragments]] after complicated [[cataract surgery]]
- [[Intraocular foreign body]] removal
- Diagnostic vitreous biopsy (suspected lymphoma, atypical uveitis)
- [[Vitreous opacities]] causing significant visual impairment (e.g., amyloidosis, severe asteroid hyalosis)
- Dislocated [[intraocular lens]] (IOL)

**Tamponade Agents:** Following vitrectomy, the vitreous cavity may be filled with tamponade agents to support the retina:

| Agent | Properties | Duration | Indications |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Balanced salt solution (BSS)** | Isotonic; no tamponade effect | Immediate absorption | Simple vitrectomy without retinal breaks |
| **Air** | Short-acting gas | 1–2 days | Minor retinal breaks |
| **[[SF₆]]** (sulfur hexafluoride) | Expansile gas; expands 2× | 10–14 days | Retinal detachment, macular hole |
| **[[C₃F₈]]** (perfluoropropane) | Expansile gas; expands 4× | 55–65 days | Complex retinal detachment, macular hole |
| **[[Silicone oil]]** | Long-acting liquid tamponade | Indefinite (requires removal) | Complex/recurrent retinal detachment, proliferative vitreoretinopathy (PVR) |

**Complications of PPV:**

- [[Iatrogenic retinal tears]] (1–5%)
- [[Cataract]] progression (nuclear sclerotic cataract develops in >80% of phakic eyes within 2 years)
- [[Endophthalmitis]] (0.03–0.05%)
- [[Elevated intraocular pressure]]
- [[Vitreous hemorrhage]] (recurrent)
- [[Retinal detachment]]
- [[Hypotony]]
- [[Sympathetic ophthalmia]] (extremely rare)

#### YAG Vitreolysis (Laser Floater Treatment)

**[[Nd:YAG laser vitreolysis]]** (laser floater treatment, LFT) is an emerging, minimally invasive approach for the treatment of symptomatic vitreous floaters. Using a Q-switched [[Nd:YAG laser]], the clinician delivers focused laser pulses to vaporize or fragment vitreous opacities (collagen aggregates) within the vitreous cavity. The laser converts the collagen into a gas bubble that is subsequently absorbed.

**Patient selection criteria:**

- Symptomatic floaters significantly affecting quality of life
- Floaters located at least 2 mm from the retina and 2 mm from the posterior lens capsule
- Well-defined, discrete floaters (Weiss ring-type floaters respond best)

**Efficacy:** Studies report subjective improvement in 53–93% of patients, though evidence remains mixed and long-term safety data are still accumulating.

## Vitreous Substitutes and Future Directions

Following vitrectomy, the vitreous cavity is filled with aqueous humor produced by the [[ciliary body]], which lacks the viscoelastic and structural properties of native vitreous. Research into **vitreous substitutes** is an active area of investigation:

- **Hydrogels** — synthetic polymers (e.g., polyethylene glycol-based, polyvinyl alcohol-based) designed to mimic the viscoelastic properties of native vitreous
- **Smart hydrogels** — temperature-responsive or photo-crosslinkable gels that can be injected as liquids and gel in situ
- **Hyaluronic acid-based substitutes** — biocompatible gels that more closely replicate native vitreous composition
- **Foldable capsular vitreous body (FCVB)** — a silicone capsule that can be filled with balanced salt solution or silicone oil, mimicking the vitreous body's shape and function

Additionally, advances in **sustained-release intravitreal drug delivery systems** (e.g., the [[port delivery system]] for ranibizumab, biodegradable implants) are transforming the pharmacological management of diseases affecting the vitreous cavity and posterior segment.

## Clinical Pearls for the Optometrist

1. **Acute floaters and flashes require urgent evaluation.** Approximately 8–15% of patients presenting with acute symptomatic PVD will have a retinal tear. A dilated fundus examination with scleral indentation is mandatory.

2. **[[Shafer's sign]]** (pigmented cells or "tobacco dust" in the anterior vitreous) is highly suggestive of a retinal tear or detachment and warrants immediate referral.

3. **Vitreous hemorrhage obscuring the fundus view** requires [[B-scan ultrasonography]] to rule out retinal detachment.

4. **Vitritis refractory to corticosteroid therapy** in an older adult should raise suspicion for **[[primary vitreoretinal lymphoma]]** (PVRL), a masquerade syndrome.

5. **Post-vitrectomy patients** with gas tamponade must avoid air travel and nitrous oxide anesthesia due to the risk of gas expansion and dangerously elevated [[intraocular pressure]].

6. **Myopic patients** are at higher risk for earlier and more symptomatic vitreous degeneration, PVD, and associated complications.

7. **Following intravitreal injections**, patients should be educated about symptoms of [[endophthalmitis]] (pain, redness, decreased vision) and instructed to seek immediate care if these occur.

---

## Summary

The vitreous cavity is the largest and most voluminous compartment of the eye, and its contents — the vitreous humor — play indispensable roles in maintaining ocular structure, optical clarity, and metabolic homeostasis. Age-related vitreous degeneration, posterior vitreous detachment, vitreous hemorrhage, vitritis, and vitreoretinal interface disorders represent the major pathological processes affecting this space. A thorough understanding of vitreous cavity anatomy, physiology, and pathophysiology is essential for all eye care professionals, as timely recognition and appropriate management of vitreous cavity disorders can prevent irreversible vision loss. Advances in imaging (OCT, ultrasonography), pharmacotherapy (intravitreal injections, enzymatic vitreolysis), and surgical techniques (small-gauge vitrectomy, YAG vitreolysis) continue to expand the diagnostic and therapeutic armamentarium available for managing diseases of the vitreous cavity.
