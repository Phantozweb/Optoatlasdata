

# Gonioscopy

**Gonioscopy** is a fundamental clinical examination technique in [[Optometry]] and [[Ophthalmology]] used to visualize and evaluate the [[Anterior Chamber Angle]] (also known as the iridocorneal angle or drainage angle) of the eye. This diagnostic procedure is indispensable in the assessment, classification, and management of [[Glaucoma]] and numerous other ocular conditions that affect the aqueous humor outflow pathways. The term derives from the Greek words *gonia* (angle) and *skopein* (to observe). Since the anterior chamber angle cannot be directly visualized due to [[Total Internal Reflection]] at the [[Cornea]]–air interface, specialized optical devices known as [[Goniolens|Goniolenses]] (gonioscopy lenses) are required to overcome this optical barrier and permit clinical inspection of the angle structures.

Gonioscopy remains the **gold standard** for anterior chamber angle assessment and is considered an essential component of a comprehensive glaucoma evaluation. Despite advances in imaging technologies such as [[Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography]] (AS-OCT) and [[Ultrasound Biomicroscopy]] (UBM), gonioscopy provides unparalleled qualitative information about angle anatomy, pigmentation, neovascularization, synechiae, and other pathological findings that imaging alone cannot fully characterize.

---

## Historical Background

The concept of gonioscopy was first introduced by **Alexios Trantas** in 1898, who used direct ophthalmoscopy combined with indentation of the [[Limbus]] to observe the angle. However, the modern era of gonioscopy began with **Maximilian Salzmann**, who in 1914 described the use of a contact lens to eliminate total internal reflection and allow visualization of the angle. **Otto Barkan** further advanced the technique in the 1930s and 1940s, popularizing direct gonioscopy and establishing its clinical importance in differentiating [[Open-Angle Glaucoma]] from [[Angle-Closure Glaucoma]]. **Hans Goldmann** subsequently developed the indirect gonioscopy lens bearing his name in the 1930s, which remains one of the most widely used gonioscopy lenses in clinical practice today. **Sussman**, **Zeiss**, and **Posner** later contributed portable four-mirror indirect lenses that enabled dynamic (indentation) gonioscopy.

## Optical Principles and Pathophysiology of Angle Visualization

### The Problem of Total Internal Reflection

Understanding why gonioscopy requires specialized instrumentation necessitates a review of the optical physics governing light behavior at the corneal surface. Light rays originating from the [[Anterior Chamber Angle]] strike the posterior corneal surface at an angle that exceeds the **critical angle** for the cornea–air interface. The [[Refractive Index]] of the cornea is approximately 1.376, and the critical angle for the cornea–air interface is approximately 46°. Because light from the angle structures strikes the anterior corneal surface at angles greater than this critical angle, **total internal reflection** occurs, preventing the light from exiting the eye and reaching the observer.

### How Gonioscopy Overcomes Total Internal Reflection

A goniolens placed on the corneal surface, coupled with a viscous medium (such as [[Methylcellulose]] or [[Goniosol]]) or by direct corneal contact (in the case of non-flanged lenses), replaces the cornea–air interface with a cornea–lens interface. Because the goniolens material (typically glass or polymethylmethacrylate, [[PMMA]]) has a refractive index similar to that of the cornea, total internal reflection is eliminated, and light from the angle structures can now exit the eye and be observed by the clinician.

### Relevance to Aqueous Humor Dynamics

The anterior chamber angle is the primary site of [[Aqueous Humor]] outflow. Aqueous humor, produced by the [[Ciliary Body]], flows from the [[Posterior Chamber]] through the [[Pupil]] into the [[Anterior Chamber]], and exits predominantly through the **conventional (trabecular) outflow pathway** via the [[Trabecular Meshwork]], [[Schlemm's Canal]], collector channels, and [[Episcleral Veins]]. A secondary **unconventional (uveoscleral) outflow pathway** also exists. Any pathological process that obstructs, narrows, or alters the anterior chamber angle can impede aqueous outflow, elevate [[Intraocular Pressure]] (IOP), and potentially lead to [[Glaucomatous Optic Neuropathy]]. Gonioscopy is the definitive method for directly evaluating the structural integrity and patency of these outflow pathways.

## Indications for Gonioscopy

Gonioscopy should be performed in a wide range of clinical scenarios, including but not limited to:

| **Indication** | **Clinical Rationale** |
|---|---|
| Suspected or confirmed [[Glaucoma]] | Classification of glaucoma mechanism (open-angle vs. angle-closure); assessment of angle structures |
| Elevated [[Intraocular Pressure]] (IOP) | Determine if angle pathology contributes to IOP elevation |
| Narrow anterior chamber on [[Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy]] (e.g., shallow [[Van Herick]] grade) | Assess risk of [[Angle Closure]] and need for [[Laser Peripheral Iridotomy]] (LPI) |
| [[Ocular Trauma]] | Evaluate for [[Angle Recession]], [[Cyclodialysis Cleft]], or [[Hyphema]] |
| [[Uveitis]] | Detect [[Peripheral Anterior Synechiae]] (PAS), angle inflammation, or secondary angle closure |
| [[Neovascular Glaucoma]] (NVG) | Identify [[Neovascularization of the Angle]] (NVA) |
| [[Pigment Dispersion Syndrome]] / [[Pigmentary Glaucoma]] | Assess degree of [[Trabecular Meshwork]] pigmentation |
| [[Pseudoexfoliation Syndrome]] / [[Pseudoexfoliative Glaucoma]] | Identify exfoliative material and pigment in the angle |
| Congenital/developmental glaucoma ([[Primary Congenital Glaucoma]]) | Evaluate for [[Barkan Membrane]] or angle dysgenesis |
| Pre-operative assessment for [[Minimally Invasive Glaucoma Surgery]] (MIGS) | Identify angle landmarks for surgical planning |
| Intraoperative guidance during angle-based surgery | Direct visualization during [[Goniotomy]], [[Trabeculotomy]], or MIGS procedures |
| [[Iris]] or [[Ciliary Body]] tumors | Evaluate extent of angle involvement |
| Unexplained asymmetric IOP or [[Cup-to-Disc Ratio]] asymmetry | Rule out unilateral angle pathology |

## Types of Gonioscopy

Gonioscopy techniques are broadly classified into **direct** and **indirect** methods, each with distinct optical principles, advantages, and limitations.

### Direct Gonioscopy

In direct gonioscopy, the clinician views the angle structures directly through a goniolens that provides an upright, unreversed image of the angle. The lens refracts light from the angle so that it exits the lens perpendicular to its anterior surface, allowing direct observation.

**Common Direct Goniolenses:**

| **Lens** | **Features** |
|---|---|
| [[Koeppe Lens]] | Dome-shaped, requires patient in supine position, used with a portable slit lamp or operating microscope; excellent for comparison of both eyes simultaneously; commonly used in pediatric gonioscopy under anesthesia |
| [[Barkan Lens]] | Similar to Koeppe; used primarily for surgical goniotomy |
| [[Swan-Jacob Lens]] | Used intraoperatively for direct visualization during angle surgery |
| [[Richardson Lens]] | Modified direct goniolens for surgical use |

**Advantages of Direct Gonioscopy:**
- Provides a panoramic, direct view of the angle
- Excellent for intraoperative use
- Allows simultaneous comparison of corresponding angle sectors in both eyes
- Particularly useful in pediatric examinations under anesthesia

**Disadvantages of Direct Gonioscopy:**
- Requires the patient to be supine
- Requires a separate light source and magnification system
- Less practical for routine office-based examination

### Indirect Gonioscopy

In indirect gonioscopy, the angle is viewed via a mirror or prism within the goniolens that reflects the image of the opposite angle to the observer. The image is reversed: the mirror shows the angle 180° opposite to the mirror's position. Indirect gonioscopy is performed at the [[Slit-Lamp Biomicroscope]] with the patient seated upright, making it the preferred method for routine clinical examination.

**Common Indirect Goniolenses:**

| **Lens** | **Mirrors** | **Coupling Agent** | **Indentation Capability** | **Key Features** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [[Goldmann Single-Mirror Lens]] | 1 | Required (viscous coupling agent, e.g., [[Methylcellulose]]) | No (flanged lens) | Provides excellent view; must rotate lens to view all quadrants |
| [[Goldmann Two-Mirror Lens]] | 2 | Required | No | Two opposing mirrors; requires less rotation |
| [[Goldmann Three-Mirror Lens]] | 3 (+ central lens for fundus) | Required | No | Most versatile; mirrors at 59°, 66°, and 73° for angle, peripheral retina, and equatorial retina respectively; the 59° mirror is used for gonioscopy |
| [[Zeiss Four-Mirror Lens]] | 4 | Not required (uses tear film) | Yes | Small footprint; allows dynamic/indentation gonioscopy; must be handheld |
| [[Posner Four-Mirror Lens]] | 4 | Not required | Yes | Similar to Zeiss; slightly different handle design |
| [[Sussman Four-Mirror Lens]] | 4 | Not required | Yes | Handheld without handle; compact design |
| [[Magna View Lens]] | 1 | Required | No | Provides magnified view of the angle |
| [[Volk G-Series Lenses]] | Various | Varies | Varies | Modern lens designs with enhanced optics |

**Advantages of Indirect Gonioscopy:**
- Performed at the slit lamp with the patient seated
- Practical for routine clinical use
- Four-mirror lenses allow dynamic (indentation/compression) gonioscopy
- Excellent magnification and illumination via the slit-lamp system

**Disadvantages of Indirect Gonioscopy:**
- Image is reversed (mirror shows opposite angle)
- Flanged lenses (e.g., Goldmann) cannot perform indentation gonioscopy
- Learning curve for interpretation

## Dynamic (Indentation/Compression) Gonioscopy

**Dynamic gonioscopy** (also called indentation or compression gonioscopy) is a critically important technique performed with small-diameter, non-flanged four-mirror lenses (e.g., [[Zeiss Four-Mirror Lens|Zeiss]], [[Posner Four-Mirror Lens|Posner]], or [[Sussman Four-Mirror Lens|Sussman]] lenses). By applying gentle posterior pressure on the cornea, the clinician mechanically displaces [[Aqueous Humor]] posteriorly, which deepens the central anterior chamber and forces the peripheral [[Iris]] posteriorly, artificially opening the angle.

### Clinical Significance of Dynamic Gonioscopy

Dynamic gonioscopy is essential for distinguishing between:

- **Appositional angle closure**: The iris is in contact with the [[Trabecular Meshwork]] but is not adherent. Indentation will open the angle, revealing the underlying angle structures. This is a reversible process.
- **Synechial angle closure ([[Peripheral Anterior Synechiae]], PAS)**: The iris is permanently adhered to the trabecular meshwork or other angle structures. Indentation will NOT open the angle in areas of PAS. This represents irreversible structural damage.

This distinction has profound implications for management: appositional closure may respond to [[Laser Peripheral Iridotomy]] (LPI) or pharmacological pupil constriction, whereas synechial closure may require surgical intervention such as [[Goniosynechialysis]].

### Technique for Dynamic Gonioscopy

1. Instill topical [[Anesthetic]] (e.g., [[Proparacaine]] 0.5%)
2. Place the four-mirror lens on the cornea using only the tear film as a coupling agent (no viscous coupling)
3. Observe the angle in the standard fashion
4. Apply gentle, deliberate posterior pressure on the cornea through the lens
5. Observe whether previously closed angle sectors open (appositional) or remain closed (synechial)
6. Document findings in all four quadrants

## Gonioscopic Anatomy of the Anterior Chamber Angle

Systematic identification of angle structures is essential for accurate gonioscopic assessment. The structures are viewed from anterior (corneal side) to posterior (iris side) and include:

### Angle Structures (Anterior to Posterior)

| **Structure** | **Appearance** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Schwalbe's Line]]** | A fine, often glistening white or translucent ridge at the termination of [[Descemet's Membrane]]; marks the anterior border of the trabecular meshwork | Landmark for the anterior limit of the angle; a prominent, anteriorly displaced Schwalbe's line is called [[Posterior Embryotoxon]] (seen in [[Axenfeld-Rieger Syndrome]]) |
| **Non-pigmented (anterior) [[Trabecular Meshwork]]** | Pale, translucent band immediately posterior to Schwalbe's line | Does not filter aqueous humor; less clinically significant for outflow |
| **Pigmented (posterior) [[Trabecular Meshwork]]** | Darker, more pigmented band posterior to the non-pigmented TM; overlies [[Schlemm's Canal]] | The functional filtration zone; the primary site of aqueous outflow resistance; increased pigmentation seen in [[Pigment Dispersion Syndrome]], [[Pseudoexfoliation Syndrome]], and after [[Uveitis]] |
| **[[Schlemm's Canal]]** | Not directly visible unless filled with blood (appears as a reddish band deep to the pigmented TM) | Blood reflux into Schlemm's canal can occur with low IOP, elevated [[Episcleral Venous Pressure]], or during gonioscopy with excessive pressure; seen in conditions such as [[Sturge-Weber Syndrome]] and [[Carotid-Cavernous Fistula]] |
| **[[Scleral Spur]]** | A prominent white band posterior to the trabecular meshwork; represents the posterior lip of the scleral sulcus | Important landmark; site of attachment of the [[Longitudinal Muscle of the Ciliary Body]]; often the most easily identifiable angle structure |
| **[[Ciliary Body Band]]** | A gray, brown, or slate-colored band posterior to the scleral spur | Represents the anterior face of the [[Ciliary Body]]; width varies with angle configuration; wider in myopic eyes, narrower or absent in hyperopic or angle-closure eyes |
| **[[Iris Root]] (Iris Insertion)** | The most posterior structure; the peripheral iris as it inserts into the ciliary body | The configuration and level of iris insertion are critical in angle-closure assessment |

### Mnemonic for Angle Structures

A commonly used mnemonic for remembering the angle structures from anterior to posterior is:

> **"S-T-S-C-I"** — **S**chwalbe's line, **T**rabecular meshwork, **S**cleral spur, **C**iliary body band, **I**ris root

Some clinicians use: **"I Can See The Surgeon"** (Iris, Ciliary body, Scleral spur, Trabecular meshwork, Schwalbe's line) — from posterior to anterior.

## Grading Systems for Gonioscopy

Several standardized grading systems exist for documenting gonioscopic findings. Consistent use of a grading system is essential for clinical communication, longitudinal monitoring, and research.

### Shaffer Grading System

The **[[Shaffer Classification]]** grades the angular width of the anterior chamber angle recess in degrees and assigns a numerical grade:

| **Shaffer Grade** | **Angle Width (degrees)** | **Structures Visible** | **Configuration** | **Risk of Closure** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Grade 4 | 35–45° | All structures to ciliary body band | Wide open | Impossible |
| Grade 3 | 25–35° | Scleral spur visible | Open | Impossible |
| Grade 2 | 20° | Trabecular meshwork visible | Moderately narrow | Possible |
| Grade 1 | 10° | Only Schwalbe's line visible | Very narrow | Probable |
| Grade 0 (Slit) | 0° | No structures visible | Closed/slit | Angle closure present |

### Scheie Classification

The **[[Scheie Classification]]** grades the angle based on the most posterior structure visible:

| **Scheie Grade** | **Structures Visible** | **Interpretation** |
|---|---|---|
| Wide open | All structures including ciliary body band | Normal |
| Grade I | Ciliary body band obscured | Slight narrowing |
| Grade II | Scleral spur obscured | Moderate narrowing |
| Grade III | Posterior trabecular meshwork obscured | Marked narrowing |
| Grade IV | No angle structures visible | Closed |

*Note: The Scheie system is counterintuitive because higher grades indicate narrower angles, opposite to the Shaffer system.*

### Spaeth Gonioscopy Grading System

The **[[Spaeth Classification]]** is the most comprehensive and descriptive system, incorporating three parameters:

1. **Iris insertion level** (labeled A through E):
   - **A**: Anterior to Schwalbe's line
   - **B**: Behind (at) Schwalbe's line
   - **C**: At the scleral spur (most common)
   - **D**: Deep — into the ciliary body band
   - **E**: Extremely deep — wide ciliary body band visible

2. **Angular width of the angle recess** (in degrees, 0–40°)

3. **Iris configuration/contour**:
   - **s** (steep): Convex iris — bowing anteriorly (seen in [[Pupillary Block]])
   - **r** (regular): Flat/planar iris
   - **q** (queer): Concave iris — bowing posteriorly (seen in [[Pigment Dispersion Syndrome]] and [[Plateau Iris]])

**Example Spaeth notation**: **C 30 r** = Iris inserting at the scleral spur, 30° angle width, regular (flat) iris configuration.

The Spaeth system also incorporates documentation of **peripheral anterior synechiae (PAS)** and **pigmentation grade** (0 to 4+).

### Trabecular Meshwork Pigmentation Grading

Pigmentation of the [[Trabecular Meshwork]] is graded on a scale of 0 to 4+:

| **Grade** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| 0 | No pigmentation |
| 1+ | Faint, minimal pigmentation |
| 2+ | Moderate pigmentation; defined pigment band |
| 3+ | Dense pigmentation |
| 4+ | Intense, heavy pigmentation obscuring TM detail |

Heavy pigmentation (3–4+) is characteristic of [[Pigment Dispersion Syndrome]], [[Pseudoexfoliation Syndrome]], [[Uveitic Glaucoma]], and post-[[Trauma]] angle changes. A dense, homogeneous pigment band concentrated at the posterior (inferior) trabecular meshwork is the hallmark of pigment dispersion, while patchy or irregular pigmentation is more typical of pseudoexfoliation or uveitis.

## Gonioscopic Findings in Specific Clinical Conditions

### Open-Angle Glaucoma

In [[Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma]] (POAG), gonioscopy reveals an open angle (typically Shaffer grade 3–4) with no mechanical obstruction. The trabecular meshwork may show variable pigmentation. The angle appears structurally normal, and the pathology lies at the microscopic level within the trabecular meshwork and [[Schlemm's Canal]].

### Angle-Closure Glaucoma

In [[Primary Angle-Closure Glaucoma]] (PACG), gonioscopy demonstrates a narrow or closed angle with iridotrabecular contact. Dynamic gonioscopy is essential to differentiate appositional from synechial closure. The iris configuration is often convex (steep), suggesting [[Pupillary Block]] as the underlying mechanism. In [[Plateau Iris Configuration]], the iris root is anteriorly positioned with a flat or slightly convex iris plane, and the angle closes despite a patent [[Laser Peripheral Iridotomy|iridotomy]].

### Angle Recession

Following blunt [[Ocular Trauma]], gonioscopy may reveal [[Angle Recession]] — an irregular widening of the [[Ciliary Body Band]] due to a tear between the longitudinal and circular fibers of the [[Ciliary Muscle]]. The affected area appears as an abnormally wide, deepened angle with a torn iris root and disrupted angle architecture. Comparison with the fellow eye is essential. Angle recession of >180° is associated with a significant risk of developing secondary [[Glaucoma]], sometimes years to decades after the initial injury.

### Neovascularization of the Angle

In [[Neovascular Glaucoma]] (NVG), gonioscopy reveals fine, arborizing new blood vessels ([[Neovascularization of the Angle]], NVA) crossing the [[Ciliary Body Band]] and [[Scleral Spur]] onto the [[Trabecular Meshwork]]. These vessels may be accompanied by a fibrovascular membrane that can contract and cause synechial angle closure. NVA is most commonly associated with [[Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy]], [[Central Retinal Vein Occlusion]], and [[Ocular Ischemic Syndrome]]. Early detection of NVA by gonioscopy is critical, as it may precede [[Neovascularization of the Iris]] (NVI, rubeosis iridis) and allows for timely intervention with [[Anti-VEGF]] therapy and [[Panretinal Photocoagulation]].

### Pigment Dispersion Syndrome

Gonioscopy in [[Pigment Dispersion Syndrome]] (PDS) classically reveals dense, homogeneous pigmentation of the trabecular meshwork (often 3–4+), most prominent in the inferior angle. A **[[Sampaolesi Line]]** — a wavy line of pigment anterior to [[Schwalbe's Line]] on the corneal endothelium — may be present. The iris configuration is typically concave (posterior bowing), which can be documented using the Spaeth system as a "q" configuration.

### Pseudoexfoliation Syndrome

In [[Pseudoexfoliation Syndrome]] (PXF), gonioscopy may reveal patchy, irregular pigmentation of the trabecular meshwork (often more prominent inferiorly), a Sampaolesi line, and white, flaky pseudoexfoliative material deposited on angle structures. Pigment distribution tends to be more irregular and patchy compared to the homogeneous pattern of pigment dispersion syndrome.

### Iridocorneal Endothelial (ICE) Syndrome

In [[Iridocorneal Endothelial Syndrome]] (ICE syndrome), gonioscopy reveals broad-based [[Peripheral Anterior Synechiae]] (PAS) that extend anterior to [[Schwalbe's Line]], often with a characteristic high, irregular PAS pattern. The abnormal corneal endothelium may be visible extending across the angle.

### Anterior Chamber Tumors

Gonioscopy is valuable in evaluating [[Iris Melanoma]], [[Iris Cyst]], [[Ciliary Body Melanoma]], and other anterior segment tumors for angle involvement, extent of tumor spread, and secondary angle closure.

## Technique and Clinical Procedure

### Patient Preparation

1. Explain the procedure to the patient and obtain informed consent
2. Instill topical [[Anesthetic]] (e.g., [[Proparacaine]] 0.5% or [[Tetracaine]] 0.5%)
3. For flanged lenses (e.g., Goldmann), apply a viscous coupling agent (e.g., [[Methylcellulose]] 1% or [[Goniosol]]) to the concave surface of the lens
4. For non-flanged lenses (e.g., Zeiss, Posner, Sussman), no coupling agent is needed — the tear film provides adequate coupling

### Lens Placement

1. Ask the patient to look up
2. Retract the lower lid and place the inferior edge of the lens on the inferior [[Sclera]]/[[Conjunctiva]]
3. Tilt the lens onto the cornea while the patient looks straight ahead
4. Ensure the lens is centered and there are no air bubbles between the lens and cornea (air bubbles degrade the view and must be eliminated)

### Systematic Examination

- Examine all **360 degrees** of the angle systematically (superior, inferior, nasal, temporal)
- The **inferior angle** is typically the widest and most pigmented; the **superior angle** is typically the narrowest and least pigmented
- For indirect gonioscopy, remember that the mirror shows the **opposite** angle (e.g., the inferior mirror shows the superior angle)
- Use a **narrow slit beam** offset slightly to avoid light entering the pupil (which causes pupillary constriction and artificially opens the angle)
- Avoid inadvertent pressure on the globe with flanged lenses, as this can artificially open the angle and give a falsely reassuring appearance

### Documentation

Document findings for each quadrant, including:
- Angle width (Shaffer, Scheie, or Spaeth grade)
- Most posterior structure visible
- Iris configuration (flat, convex, concave)
- Iris insertion level
- Trabecular meshwork pigmentation grade
- Presence of [[Peripheral Anterior Synechiae]] (PAS) — document extent in clock hours or degrees
- Presence of neovascularization, exfoliative material, angle recession, or other abnormalities
- Results of dynamic (indentation) gonioscopy if performed

## Role of Gonioscopy in Treatment Planning

Gonioscopy findings directly influence therapeutic decision-making across the spectrum of glaucoma management:

### Pharmacological Implications

- **Open angles** with elevated IOP: [[Prostaglandin Analogs]] (e.g., [[Latanoprost]], [[Bimatoprost]]), [[Beta-Blockers]] (e.g., [[Timolol]]), [[Alpha-Agonists]] (e.g., [[Brimonidine]]), [[Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors]] (e.g., [[Dorzolamide]]), and [[Rho Kinase Inhibitors]] (e.g., [[Netarsudil]]) may be appropriate
- **Narrow angles** with pupillary block: [[Pilocarpine]] (a [[Miotic]]) may be used acutely to break pupillary block, but definitive treatment is [[Laser Peripheral Iridotomy]]
- **Neovascularization of the angle**: Intravitreal [[Anti-VEGF]] agents (e.g., [[Bevacizumab]], [[Ranibizumab]], [[Aflibercept]]) combined with treatment of the underlying ischemic condition

### Laser Interventions Guided by Gonioscopy

| **Laser Procedure** | **Gonioscopic Indication** |
|---|---|
| [[Laser Peripheral Iridotomy]] (LPI) | Narrow/occludable angles, pupillary block mechanism, acute angle closure |
| [[Argon Laser Trabeculoplasty]] (ALT) | Open-angle glaucoma with visible, pigmented trabecular meshwork |
| [[Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty]] (SLT) | Open-angle glaucoma; targets pigmented TM cells; can be performed on lightly pigmented meshwork |
| [[Laser Peripheral Iridoplasty]] (LPIP/Gonioplasty) | Plateau iris syndrome; persistent appositional closure after LPI |
| [[Endocyclophotocoagulation]] (ECP) | Refractory glaucoma; performed under gonioscopic guidance intraoperatively |

### Surgical Interventions Guided by Gonioscopy

Gonioscopy is essential for preoperative planning and intraoperative guidance in numerous surgical procedures:

| **Surgical Procedure** | **Role of Gonioscopy** |
|---|---|
| [[Goniotomy]] | Direct visualization of angle for incision of trabecular meshwork (used in congenital glaucoma and increasingly in adult MIGS) |
| [[Trabeculotomy]] | Assessment of angle anatomy preoperatively |
| [[Goniosynechialysis]] | Identification and lysis of PAS under gonioscopic visualization |
| [[iStent]] / [[iStent Inject]] | Intraoperative gonioscopic guidance for trabecular micro-bypass stent placement into [[Schlemm's Canal]] |
| [[Hydrus Microstent]] | Gonioscopic placement into Schlemm's canal |
| [[Kahook Dual Blade]] (KDB) Goniotomy | Excisional goniotomy performed under intraoperative gonioscopy |
| [[OMNI Surgical System]] | Gonioscopy-assisted transluminal trabeculotomy (GATT) and viscodilation of Schlemm's canal |
| [[Trabectome]] | Electrosurgical ablation of TM under gonioscopic guidance |
| [[Ab Interno Canaloplasty]] (ABiC) | Gonioscopic catheterization and viscodilation of Schlemm's canal |
| [[Xen Gel Stent]] | Gonioscopic confirmation of stent placement (ab interno approach) |

The emergence of [[Minimally Invasive Glaucoma Surgery]] (MIGS) has dramatically increased the importance of gonioscopy in modern glaucoma practice, as virtually all trabecular and canal-based MIGS procedures require intraoperative gonioscopic visualization using surgical goniolenses such as the [[Swan-Jacob Lens]] or specialized direct surgical gonioprisms.

## Gonioscopy vs. Advanced Imaging Technologies

While gonioscopy remains the clinical gold standard, several imaging technologies provide complementary information:

| **Modality** | **Advantages** | **Limitations** | **Comparison to Gonioscopy** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Anterior Segment OCT]] (AS-OCT) | Non-contact, objective, quantitative measurements of angle parameters (e.g., [[Angle Opening Distance]], [[Trabecular-Iris Space Area]]) | Cannot visualize angle structures in detail; cannot detect PAS, NVA, or pigmentation; limited by iris pigment epithelium shadowing | Complementary; does not replace gonioscopy |
| [[Ultrasound Biomicroscopy]] (UBM) | Visualizes structures posterior to the iris (ciliary body, zonules); useful for plateau iris, ciliary body tumors, cyclodialysis clefts | Contact technique; requires immersion; operator-dependent; lower resolution than OCT | Complementary; provides information gonioscopy cannot (posterior structures) |
| [[Scheimpflug Imaging]] (e.g., [[Pentacam]]) | Quantitative anterior chamber depth and volume measurements | Cannot directly image angle structures | Screening tool; does not replace gonioscopy |
| [[Gonioscopy-Assisted Transluminal Trabeculotomy]] (GATT) imaging | Intraoperative visualization | Procedure-specific | Gonioscopy is integral to the procedure |
| [[EyeCam]] / [[Automated Gonioscopy]] | Photographic documentation of angle; potential for telemedicine | Limited availability; cannot perform dynamic gonioscopy | Emerging technology; may supplement but not replace clinical gonioscopy |

## Potential Complications and Limitations

Gonioscopy is a safe procedure with minimal risks, but clinicians should be aware of the following:

- **Corneal epithelial abrasion**: Rare; minimized by proper technique and adequate lubrication
- **Transient corneal edema**: May occur with prolonged lens contact
- **Allergic reaction to coupling agent or anesthetic**: Uncommon
- **Inadvertent globe pressure**: Can artificially open the angle (with flanged lenses) or artificially close the angle (with excessive pressure on non-flanged lenses), leading to inaccurate assessment
- **Patient discomfort or anxiety**: Minimized with adequate topical anesthesia and patient education
- **Transmission of infection**: Proper lens disinfection between patients is mandatory to prevent transmission of [[Adenoviral Keratoconjunctivitis]] and other pathogens
- **Subjectivity**: Gonioscopy is inherently subjective and operator-dependent; inter-observer variability exists, underscoring the importance of standardized grading systems and training

## Pearls and Pitfalls in Gonioscopy

### Clinical Pearls

1. **Always examine the superior angle**: The superior angle is the narrowest and most likely to close first; it is the most critical quadrant in angle-closure assessment
2. **Use a narrow beam offset from the pupil**: Prevents light-induced miosis, which artificially opens the angle
3. **Compare both eyes**: Asymmetry in angle findings may indicate unilateral pathology (e.g., angle recession, unilateral PAS, tumor)
4. **Perform dynamic gonioscopy when the angle appears narrow or closed**: Essential for differentiating appositional from synechial closure
5. **Examine the angle before dilation**: Pharmacological [[Mydriasis]] alters angle configuration and may precipitate angle closure in susceptible eyes
6. **Look for the corneal wedge**: A useful technique to identify [[Schwalbe's Line]] — when the slit beam is narrowed, two bright lines (representing the anterior and posterior corneal surfaces) converge at Schwalbe's line, forming a "corneal wedge"
7. **Blood in Schlemm's canal**: If observed, consider elevated episcleral venous pressure ([[Sturge-Weber Syndrome]], [[Carotid-Cavernous Fistula]], [[Thyroid Eye Disease]]) or hypotony

### Common Pitfalls

1. **Failing to perform gonioscopy**: Gonioscopy is underutilized in clinical practice; it should be performed on every glaucoma suspect and glaucoma patient
2. **Inadvertent indentation with flanged lenses**: Pressing on the globe with a Goldmann lens artificially opens the angle
3. **Excessive room illumination**: Bright ambient light causes miosis and artificially opens the angle; perform gonioscopy in a dimly lit room
4. **Misidentifying structures**: Confusing Schwalbe's line with the scleral spur, or misidentifying iris processes as PAS
5. **Incomplete examination**: Failing to examine all 360° of the angle

## Iris Processes vs. Peripheral Anterior Synechiae

A common diagnostic challenge in gonioscopy is distinguishing normal **[[Iris Processes]]** from pathological **[[Peripheral Anterior Synechiae]]** (PAS):

| **Feature** | **Iris Processes** | **Peripheral Anterior Synechiae (PAS)** |
|---|---|---|
| **Nature** | Normal anatomical variant | Pathological adhesion |
| **Appearance** | Fine, lacy, thread-like strands | Broad, tent-shaped, or irregular adhesions |
| **Extent** | Typically do not extend beyond scleral spur | May extend to trabecular meshwork, Schwalbe's line, or beyond |
| **Distribution** | Usually bilateral and symmetric | Often asymmetric; may be focal or diffuse |
| **Response to indentation** | Move with iris; angle opens behind them | Do not release with indentation; angle remains closed |
| **Pigmentation** | Same color as iris | May be associated with pigment disruption |
| **Clinical significance** | None (normal variant) | Indicates prior or ongoing angle pathology (inflammation, angle closure, NVG) |

---

## Gonioscopy in Pediatric Patients

Gonioscopy in pediatric patients, particularly infants with suspected [[Primary Congenital Glaucoma]] (PCG) or developmental glaucoma, presents unique challenges. Examination is typically performed under general anesthesia (examination under anesthesia, EUA) using a direct goniolens (e.g., [[Koeppe Lens]]) with a portable slit lamp or operating microscope.

Key gonioscopic findings in congenital/developmental glaucoma include:
- **[[Barkan Membrane]]**: A translucent membrane covering the trabecular meshwork (historically described, though its existence as a distinct membrane is debated)
- **Anterior iris insertion**: Iris inserting directly into the trabecular meshwork
- **Absence of normal angle recess**: Flat iris insertion without visible ciliary body band
- **Angle dysgenesis**: Abnormal development of angle structures seen in conditions such as [[Axenfeld-Rieger Syndrome]], [[Peters Anomaly]], and [[Aniridia]]

---

## Summary

Gonioscopy is an indispensable diagnostic technique in the evaluation and management of [[Glaucoma]] and numerous other anterior segment conditions. It provides direct, qualitative assessment of the [[Anterior Chamber Angle]] that cannot be fully replicated by any current imaging technology. Mastery of gonioscopy — including proper lens selection, technique, systematic examination, accurate grading, and interpretation of findings — is a core competency for all optometrists and ophthalmologists involved in glaucoma care. The increasing importance of angle-based [[Minimally Invasive Glaucoma Surgery]] (MIGS) procedures has further elevated the clinical relevance of gonioscopic skills in contemporary practice.
