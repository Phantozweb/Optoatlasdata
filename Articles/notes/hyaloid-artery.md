

# Hyaloid Artery

---

The **hyaloid artery** (Latin: *arteria hyaloidea*) is a transient embryonic blood vessel that traverses the [[vitreous humor]] from the [[optic disc]] to the posterior surface of the [[crystalline lens]] during fetal development. It serves as the primary vascular supply to the developing [[lens]] and anterior segment of the eye during [[embryogenesis]]. Under normal physiological conditions, the hyaloid artery undergoes complete regression by the end of the third trimester or shortly after birth. Failure of this regression results in a spectrum of clinical entities collectively referred to as **persistent hyaloid artery** (PHA) or, when associated with broader developmental anomalies, **[[persistent fetal vasculature]]** (PFV), formerly known as **[[persistent hyperplastic primary vitreous]]** (PHPV). Understanding the hyaloid artery's embryology, normal involution, and pathological persistence is essential for optometrists and ophthalmologists who encounter remnants of this vessel during routine and specialized ocular examinations.

---

## Embryology and Normal Development

### Formation of the Hyaloid Vascular System

The hyaloid artery originates as a branch of the [[ophthalmic artery]], itself a branch of the [[internal carotid artery]]. During the fourth week of [[gestation]] (approximately 4–5 mm embryonic stage), the hyaloid artery enters the developing [[optic cup]] through the [[embryonic fissure]] (also called the [[choroidal fissure]] or [[optic fissure]]). It courses through the primordial [[vitreous body]]—the **[[primary vitreous]]**—and reaches the posterior pole of the developing lens vesicle.

Upon reaching the lens, the hyaloid artery arborizes into a dense capillary network known as the **[[tunica vasculosa lentis]]** (TVL), which envelops the entire posterior and lateral surfaces of the lens. The anterior portion of this vascular network is termed the **[[anterior tunica vasculosa lentis]]** or **[[pupillary membrane]]**, which covers the anterior lens surface and is supplied by anastomoses with the **[[long posterior ciliary arteries]]**. Together, these structures form the **hyaloid vascular system**, which is the sole nutritive supply to the avascular lens and the developing anterior segment during early embryogenesis.

Additionally, the hyaloid artery gives rise to the **[[vasa hyaloidea propria]]**, a network of fine vessels that permeate the primary vitreous and provide metabolic support to the surrounding developing ocular tissues.

### Timeline of Development

| Gestational Period | Developmental Event |
|---|---|
| Week 4 (4–5 mm) | Hyaloid artery enters the optic cup through the embryonic fissure |
| Weeks 5–6 | Hyaloid artery reaches the posterior lens; tunica vasculosa lentis begins to form |
| Weeks 7–9 | Maximal development of the hyaloid vascular system; pupillary membrane fully formed |
| Week 9 (approximately 40 mm) | Secondary vitreous begins to form; onset of hyaloid regression |
| Weeks 12–13 | Vasa hyaloidea propria begin to atrophy |
| Weeks 28–36 | Pupillary membrane and tunica vasculosa lentis regress; hyaloid artery undergoes apoptosis |
| Birth to 4 weeks postnatal | Complete regression in most individuals; Cloquet's canal remains as a vestigial structure |

### Mechanism of Regression

The regression of the hyaloid artery is a highly orchestrated process involving **[[apoptosis]]** (programmed cell death) of the vascular [[endothelial cells]], mediated by several molecular and cellular mechanisms:

1. **Macrophage-mediated apoptosis**: [[Hyalocytes]] and [[macrophages]] within the vitreous secrete factors such as **[[angiopoietin-2]]** (Ang-2) and **[[Wnt7b]]** that promote endothelial cell death. Studies in murine models have demonstrated that macrophages are essential for hyaloid regression, and their absence leads to persistent fetal vasculature.

2. **Withdrawal of pro-angiogenic signals**: As the lens matures and becomes avascular, the production of **[[vascular endothelial growth factor]]** ([[VEGF]]) by lens epithelial cells decreases, removing the survival signal for hyaloid endothelial cells.

3. **Lens-derived anti-angiogenic factors**: The maturing lens produces anti-angiogenic molecules, including **[[pigment epithelium-derived factor]]** ([[PEDF]]) and **[[thrombospondin-1]]**, which actively promote vascular regression.

4. **Oxygen tension changes**: Increasing oxygen tension in the vitreous cavity as the [[retinal vasculature]] matures contributes to the downregulation of VEGF and promotes hyaloid involution.

5. **Autophagy and endothelial cell remodeling**: Recent research has implicated **[[autophagy]]** pathways in the orderly dismantling of hyaloid vessel endothelial cells.

Following complete regression, the only remnant of the hyaloid artery is **[[Cloquet's canal]]** (also known as the **[[hyaloid canal]]** or **[[canal of Stilling]]**), a transparent, optically empty tubular channel that extends from the optic disc to the posterior lens surface (the area known as **[[Mittendorf's dot]]** on the posterior lens capsule, and **[[Bergmeister's papilla]]** at the optic disc). These remnants are considered normal anatomical variants and are clinically benign.

## Anatomy

### Gross Anatomy

The hyaloid artery is a single, unbranched vessel (prior to its terminal arborization at the lens) that measures approximately 15–18 mm in length in the fully developed fetal eye. It courses through the central axis of the vitreous body within the **[[primary vitreous]]** (Cloquet's canal region). Its origin is at the [[optic nerve head]], where it emerges from the center of the disc as a continuation of the central retinal artery or directly from the ophthalmic artery. Its termination is at the posterior pole of the lens, where it fans out into the tunica vasculosa lentis.

### Histology

Histologically, the hyaloid artery is a small muscular artery with a typical vascular wall structure:

- **[[Tunica intima]]**: A single layer of endothelial cells resting on a thin basement membrane.
- **[[Tunica media]]**: A thin layer of smooth muscle cells, fewer in number compared to systemic arteries of similar caliber.
- **[[Tunica adventitia]]**: A loose connective tissue layer that blends with the surrounding primary vitreous.

The vessel is surrounded by a sheath of [[glial cells]] (astrocytes) near the optic disc, which contributes to the formation of Bergmeister's papilla when regression is incomplete.

### Vestigial Remnants

| Remnant | Location | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Cloquet's canal]]** | Central vitreous, from optic disc to posterior lens | Optically empty tube; visible on [[OCT]] and [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]; benign |
| **[[Bergmeister's papilla]]** | Optic disc surface | Small glial tuft on the disc; may be mistaken for disc pathology; benign |
| **[[Mittendorf's dot]]** | Posterior lens capsule (slightly nasal and inferior to posterior pole) | Small white opacity; may be mistaken for posterior subcapsular cataract; benign |

## Pathophysiology of Persistent Hyaloid Artery

### Definition and Classification

**Persistent hyaloid artery** refers to the failure of the hyaloid artery to undergo complete regression. This condition exists on a clinical spectrum:

1. **Isolated persistent hyaloid artery**: A remnant vessel, often non-perfused (atrophic cord or strand), extending partially or completely from the optic disc toward the lens. This is the mildest and most common form.

2. **Patent (perfused) persistent hyaloid artery**: A rare finding in which the vessel retains blood flow, sometimes visible as a pulsating or blood-filled strand in the vitreous. This may be observed in premature infants.

3. **[[Persistent fetal vasculature]] (PFV)**: A more severe and comprehensive failure of regression involving not only the hyaloid artery but also the tunica vasculosa lentis, pupillary membrane, and/or vasa hyaloidea propria. PFV is classified as:
   - **Anterior PFV**: Predominantly involves the anterior structures (retrolental fibrovascular membrane, persistent pupillary membrane, shallow [[anterior chamber]], [[cataract]])
   - **Posterior PFV**: Predominantly involves the posterior structures (persistent hyaloid stalk, retinal folds, [[retinal detachment]], optic disc abnormalities)
   - **Combined (anterior and posterior) PFV**: Features of both

### Molecular Pathogenesis

The failure of hyaloid regression is linked to disruptions in the molecular pathways governing vascular apoptosis:

- **VEGF overexpression or failure of VEGF downregulation**: Sustained VEGF signaling from the lens or surrounding tissues prevents endothelial apoptosis.
- **Macrophage dysfunction**: Deficiency or dysfunction of vitreous macrophages (as demonstrated in *Wnt7b* knockout and *Ang-2* knockout mouse models) leads to persistent hyaloid vasculature.
- **Mutations in [[ATOH7]] (Math5)**: This transcription factor, critical for retinal ganglion cell development, has been implicated in PFV when mutated.
- **[[NORRIN]]/[[FZD4]]/[[LRP5]] pathway abnormalities**: Mutations in genes encoding components of the Norrin-Frizzled-4 signaling pathway, which are also implicated in [[familial exudative vitreoretinopathy]] (FEVR) and [[Norrie disease]], can result in persistent fetal vasculature.
- **[[PAX6]] and other developmental gene mutations**: Rarely, mutations in master regulatory genes of ocular development may be associated with PFV.

### Associated Conditions

Persistent hyaloid artery and PFV may occur in isolation (sporadic) or in association with systemic and ocular conditions:

| Associated Condition | Relationship |
|---|---|
| [[Prematurity]] | Incomplete regression due to early birth; often resolves spontaneously |
| [[Trisomy 13]] (Patau syndrome) | PFV is a recognized ocular feature |
| [[Norrie disease]] | X-linked; mutations in *NDP* gene; bilateral PFV with retinal dysplasia |
| [[Walker-Warburg syndrome]] | Congenital muscular dystrophy with ocular anomalies including PFV |
| [[Osteoporosis-pseudoglioma syndrome]] | *LRP5* mutations; may present with PFV-like findings |
| [[Familial exudative vitreoretinopathy]] (FEVR) | Shared genetic pathways (Norrin/Wnt signaling) |
| [[Microphthalmia]] | PFV frequently associated with a smaller globe |
| [[Congenital cataract]] | Anterior PFV commonly causes lens opacification |

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Asymptomatic Remnants

The vast majority of hyaloid artery remnants are **asymptomatic** and discovered incidentally during routine [[dilated fundus examination]], [[optical coherence tomography]] (OCT), or [[B-scan ultrasonography]]. These include:

- **Cloquet's canal**: Seen as a faint, optically empty tube in the central vitreous on OCT or as subtle vitreous strands on slit-lamp biomicroscopy.
- **Bergmeister's papilla**: A small, white, glial tuft on the optic disc surface, visible on [[fundoscopy]].
- **Mittendorf's dot**: A tiny, white, round opacity on the posterior lens capsule, visible on [[slit-lamp examination]].

These findings are present in a significant proportion of the population. Studies have reported Mittendorf's dot in approximately **3%** of eyes and Bergmeister's papilla in up to **17–25%** of autopsy eyes.

### Symptomatic Persistent Hyaloid Artery

When the hyaloid artery persists as a more substantial structure, or when it is part of PFV, the following symptoms and signs may be present:

#### In Neonates and Infants
- **[[Leukocoria]]** (white pupillary reflex): The most common presenting sign, often prompting urgent evaluation to rule out [[retinoblastoma]].
- **[[Strabismus]]**: Secondary to poor visual development in the affected eye.
- **[[Microphthalmos]]**: The affected eye may be smaller than normal.
- **[[Nystagmus]]**: If bilateral or if significant visual deprivation occurs early.
- **Absent or abnormal [[red reflex]]**: Detected on newborn screening examination.

#### In Older Children and Adults
- **Reduced [[visual acuity]]**: Ranging from mild to severe, depending on the extent of the remnant and associated complications.
- **[[Floaters]]**: A persistent strand or cord in the vitreous may cause symptomatic floaters.
- **[[Amblyopia]]**: Deprivation amblyopia if the remnant causes significant visual axis obstruction during the critical period of visual development.
- **Monocular visual disturbance**: Rarely, a patent hyaloid artery may cause intermittent visual obscuration.

### Complications of Persistent Hyaloid Artery / PFV

| Complication | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **[[Cataract]]** (posterior subcapsular or total) | Traction on the posterior lens capsule; disruption of lens metabolism by persistent vasculature |
| **[[Glaucoma]]** (angle-closure or neovascular) | Anterior PFV causing shallow anterior chamber; neovascularization of the angle |
| **[[Retinal detachment]]** (tractional) | Posterior PFV with fibrovascular traction on the retina |
| **[[Vitreous hemorrhage]]** | Rupture of a patent persistent hyaloid vessel |
| **[[Phthisis bulbi]]** | End-stage complication of severe, untreated PFV |
| **[[Amblyopia]]** | Visual deprivation during critical developmental period |
| **Retrolental fibrovascular membrane** | Anterior PFV; may cause progressive lens opacification and angle closure |

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

A thorough clinical examination is the cornerstone of diagnosing hyaloid artery remnants and PFV:

1. **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: Allows direct visualization of Mittendorf's dot on the posterior lens capsule, persistent pupillary membrane remnants, and anterior vitreous strands. A persistent hyaloid artery may appear as a fine, thread-like strand extending from the posterior lens surface into the vitreous.

2. **[[Dilated fundus examination]]** (with [[binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy]] and/or [[90D]]/[[78D]] lens): Bergmeister's papilla is visible as a small, white, elevated tuft of tissue on the optic disc. A persistent hyaloid stalk may be seen as a cord extending anteriorly from the disc into the vitreous cavity.

3. **[[Direct ophthalmoscopy]]**: May reveal a linear opacity in the vitreous or a white reflex in severe cases.

4. **[[Red reflex testing]]**: Essential in neonatal screening. An abnormal or absent red reflex may indicate PFV and warrants urgent referral.

5. **[[Gonioscopy]]**: In cases of anterior PFV, gonioscopy may reveal a shallow anterior chamber, peripheral anterior synechiae, or elongated ciliary processes drawn centrally by the retrolental membrane.

### Imaging Modalities

| Modality | Findings in Persistent Hyaloid Artery / PFV |
|---|---|
| **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** | Hyperechoic linear strand extending from the optic disc to the posterior lens; retrolental membrane; retinal detachment in severe PFV; useful when media opacity prevents fundoscopy |
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)** | Visualization of Cloquet's canal as a hypo-reflective tubular structure in the central vitreous; Bergmeister's papilla as a hyperreflective elevation on the disc; enhanced depth imaging (EDI-OCT) may delineate posterior remnants |
| **[[Swept-source OCT]]** | Superior vitreous imaging; can delineate the full extent of Cloquet's canal and any persistent hyaloid remnant |
| **[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA)** | A patent hyaloid artery will fill with fluorescein, confirming blood flow; useful to differentiate perfused from non-perfused remnants; may show leakage from the vessel |
| **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | High-resolution imaging of the anterior segment; demonstrates retrolental membrane, elongated ciliary processes, and shallow anterior chamber in anterior PFV |
| **[[MRI]] of the orbit** | Useful in differentiating PFV from retinoblastoma (PFV typically shows a smaller globe, enhancing retrolental tissue, and absence of calcification; retinoblastoma shows calcification and a normal or enlarged globe) |
| **[[CT scan]] of the orbit** | Demonstrates microphthalmos and absence of calcification (distinguishing PFV from retinoblastoma, which typically calcifies); however, MRI is preferred in pediatric patients to avoid radiation |
| **[[Color Doppler imaging]]** | Can detect blood flow within a patent persistent hyaloid artery |

### Differential Diagnosis

The differential diagnosis of persistent hyaloid artery and PFV is critically important, particularly in the pediatric population where leukocoria is the presenting sign:

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| **[[Retinoblastoma]]** | Calcification on CT/ultrasound; normal or enlarged globe; no microphthalmos; may be bilateral; urgent oncologic evaluation required |
| **[[Retinopathy of prematurity]] (ROP)** | History of prematurity and oxygen therapy; bilateral; characteristic retinal findings (ridge, neovascularization) |
| **[[Coats disease]]** | Unilateral; male predominance; telangiectatic retinal vessels; subretinal exudation; no microphthalmos |
| **[[Toxocara canis]] (ocular toxocariasis)** | Granuloma; vitreous inflammation; positive serology; older child |
| **[[Congenital cataract]]** (isolated) | Lens opacity without retrolental membrane or vitreous stalk; normal globe size |
| **[[Familial exudative vitreoretinopathy]] (FEVR)** | Bilateral; peripheral avascular retina; family history; shared genetic pathways |
| **[[Norrie disease]]** | Bilateral; X-linked; associated deafness and intellectual disability |
| **[[Retinal detachment]] (rhegmatogenous)** | Typically older patients; retinal break identified; no hyaloid stalk |

## Management and Treatment

### Observation

- **Asymptomatic remnants** (Mittendorf's dot, Bergmeister's papilla, Cloquet's canal, small non-perfused hyaloid strand): **No treatment is required.** These are benign findings. Documentation and patient education are appropriate. Periodic monitoring may be considered, particularly in pediatric patients, to ensure no progression or associated complications.

- **Premature infants with visible hyaloid artery**: In many premature neonates, the hyaloid artery is still visible and may even be patent. In the majority of cases, spontaneous regression occurs within weeks to months after birth. Serial examinations are recommended.

### Medical (Non-Surgical) Management

- **[[Amblyopia]] therapy**: In cases where a persistent hyaloid remnant or associated cataract causes visual axis obstruction in a child, aggressive amblyopia management (patching, atropine penalization of the fellow eye) is essential following any surgical intervention to maximize visual potential.

- **[[Intraocular pressure]] (IOP) management**: If secondary [[glaucoma]] develops (angle-closure or neovascular), topical and/or systemic IOP-lowering agents are indicated. Agents may include:
  - **[[Timolol]]** (beta-blocker)
  - **[[Dorzolamide]]** or **[[Brinzolamide]]** (carbonic anhydrase inhibitors)
  - **[[Brimonidine]]** (alpha-2 agonist; caution in infants due to CNS depression risk)
  - **[[Latanoprost]]** or other [[prostaglandin analogs]]
  - **Oral [[acetazolamide]]** in refractory cases

- **[[Anti-VEGF]] therapy**: In rare cases of a patent, leaking persistent hyaloid artery or associated neovascularization, intravitreal injection of anti-VEGF agents (e.g., **[[bevacizumab]]**, **[[ranibizumab]]**, **[[aflibercept]]**) has been reported as an adjunctive treatment, though evidence is limited to case reports and small series.

### Surgical Management

Surgical intervention is indicated when persistent hyaloid artery or PFV causes or threatens significant visual morbidity. The goals of surgery are to:

1. Clear the visual axis
2. Prevent or treat complications (glaucoma, retinal detachment)
3. Optimize conditions for visual rehabilitation

#### Surgical Approaches

| Procedure | Indication | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Lensectomy]]** | Cataract secondary to anterior PFV | Removal of the opacified lens, often combined with excision of the retrolental membrane; may be performed via limbal or pars plicata approach in infants |
| **[[Membranectomy]]** | Retrolental fibrovascular membrane causing visual axis obstruction | Careful excision of the membrane; may be combined with lensectomy |
| **[[Pars plana vitrectomy]] (PPV)** | Posterior PFV with vitreous traction, tractional retinal detachment, or vitreous hemorrhage | Standard 23-, 25-, or 27-gauge vitrectomy; careful dissection of the hyaloid stalk and associated fibrovascular tissue; avoidance of traction on the retina |
| **[[Pars plicata vitrectomy/lensectomy]]** | Combined anterior and posterior PFV in infants | Approach through the pars plicata (since the pars plana is not fully developed in infants); combined lens and membrane removal with anterior vitrectomy |
| **[[Glaucoma surgery]]** | Secondary glaucoma refractory to medical therapy | Options include [[goniotomy]], [[trabeculotomy]], [[trabeculectomy]], [[glaucoma drainage device]] implantation (e.g., [[Ahmed valve]], [[Baerveldt implant]]), or [[cyclodestructive procedures]] (e.g., [[transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]]) |
| **[[Nd:YAG laser vitreolysis]]** | Symptomatic floaters from a non-perfused hyaloid remnant in adults | Laser disruption of the strand; performed with caution to avoid retinal damage; limited evidence |
| **[[Nd:YAG laser posterior capsulotomy/membranectomy]]** | Thin, non-vascularized membrane or Mittendorf's dot causing visual axis obstruction | Laser disruption of the membrane; only appropriate for thin, avascular membranes |

#### Surgical Considerations and Outcomes

- **Timing**: Early surgical intervention (within the first few weeks to months of life) is recommended in cases of significant visual axis obstruction to minimize deprivation amblyopia.
- **Intraocular lens (IOL) implantation**: In infants undergoing lensectomy, primary IOL implantation is controversial. Many surgeons prefer aphakic correction with [[contact lenses]] or [[spectacles]] in very young children, with secondary IOL implantation at a later age.
- **Visual prognosis**: The visual outcome depends heavily on the severity of the PFV, the timing of intervention, and the success of amblyopia therapy. Isolated, mild persistent hyaloid artery remnants carry an excellent prognosis. Severe PFV, particularly with retinal involvement, carries a guarded visual prognosis despite surgical intervention.
- **Complications of surgery**: Include [[retinal detachment]], [[vitreous hemorrhage]], [[endophthalmitis]], [[glaucoma]], [[phthisis bulbi]], and recurrence of fibrovascular proliferation.

### Rehabilitation

- **Optical correction**: Aphakic spectacles or contact lenses following lensectomy; refractive correction of any residual error.
- **Amblyopia therapy**: Aggressive and prolonged patching or penalization therapy is critical in unilateral cases.
- **Low vision services**: In cases with poor visual outcome, referral to [[low vision]] specialists and early intervention programs is appropriate.
- **Prosthetic shell**: In cases of phthisis bulbi or enucleation (rare, end-stage), a prosthetic shell may be fitted for cosmesis.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of hyaloid artery remnants varies dramatically based on the clinical spectrum:

| Clinical Entity | Visual Prognosis |
|---|---|
| Mittendorf's dot (isolated) | Excellent; no visual impact |
| Bergmeister's papilla (isolated) | Excellent; no visual impact |
| Cloquet's canal (visible) | Excellent; no visual impact |
| Isolated non-perfused hyaloid strand | Excellent to good; rarely causes symptoms |
| Patent persistent hyaloid artery (premature infant) | Good; usually regresses spontaneously |
| Mild anterior PFV with cataract | Fair to good with early surgery and amblyopia therapy |
| Severe anterior PFV with glaucoma | Guarded; depends on IOP control and amblyopia management |
| Posterior PFV with retinal detachment | Poor; significant risk of permanent visual loss |
| Combined severe PFV (bilateral) | Poor; often associated with systemic syndromes |

## Epidemiology

- **Hyaloid artery remnants** (Mittendorf's dot, Bergmeister's papilla) are common, found in up to **3%** and **17–25%** of the general population, respectively.
- **Clinically significant persistent hyaloid artery / PFV** is rare, with an estimated incidence of approximately **3 per 100,000 live births**.
- PFV is **unilateral** in approximately **90%** of cases.
- There is **no sex predilection** for isolated PFV, though some syndromic forms (e.g., Norrie disease) are X-linked.
- PFV accounts for approximately **5%** of childhood blindness in developed countries.

## Historical Perspective

The hyaloid artery was first described in detail by early anatomists studying fetal ocular development. The term **"persistent hyperplastic primary vitreous" (PHPV)** was coined by **Dr. Algernon Reese** in **1955** to describe the clinical syndrome of failed hyaloid regression with associated fibrovascular proliferation. In **1997**, Goldberg proposed the term **"persistent fetal vasculature" (PFV)** to more accurately encompass the full spectrum of developmental vascular anomalies, recognizing that the condition involves not only the primary vitreous but also the tunica vasculosa lentis and hyaloid artery system. The term PFV has since gained widespread acceptance in the ophthalmic literature.

## Key Clinical Pearls for Optometrists

1. **Mittendorf's dot and Bergmeister's papilla are benign**: Recognize these common remnants and reassure patients. No treatment or referral is needed for isolated findings.

2. **Leukocoria in an infant demands urgent evaluation**: While PFV is a cause of leukocoria, [[retinoblastoma]] must be excluded emergently. Immediate referral to a pediatric ophthalmologist or ocular oncologist is mandatory.

3. **Unilateral microphthalmos with leukocoria**: This combination is highly suggestive of PFV rather than retinoblastoma (which typically presents with a normal or enlarged globe).

4. **Absence of calcification on imaging**: PFV does not calcify, unlike retinoblastoma. This is a critical differentiating feature on CT or ultrasound.

5. **Premature infants**: A visible hyaloid artery in a premature infant is a normal finding that typically regresses. Document and monitor.

6. **Cloquet's canal on OCT**: With modern swept-source and spectral-domain OCT, Cloquet's canal is increasingly visualized. It should not be confused with pathology such as a [[posterior vitreous detachment]] or [[epiretinal membrane]].

7. **Genetic counseling**: In cases of bilateral PFV or PFV associated with systemic findings, genetic evaluation and counseling should be recommended, as several hereditary conditions (Norrie disease, FEVR, Walker-Warburg syndrome) may be responsible.

---

## Summary

The hyaloid artery is a critical but transient vascular structure in embryonic ocular development. Its orderly regression, mediated by macrophage-driven apoptosis, VEGF downregulation, and anti-angiogenic factors from the maturing lens, is essential for normal visual development. Benign remnants such as Cloquet's canal, Bergmeister's papilla, and Mittendorf's dot are common and clinically insignificant. However, failure of regression—ranging from an isolated persistent hyaloid strand to severe persistent fetal vasculature—can result in significant ocular morbidity, including cataract, glaucoma, retinal detachment, and amblyopia. Early diagnosis through comprehensive clinical examination and advanced imaging, timely surgical intervention when indicated, and aggressive amblyopia management are the cornerstones of optimizing visual outcomes in affected patients.
