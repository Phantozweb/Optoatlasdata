

# Diabetic Retinopathy

**Diabetic Retinopathy** (DR) is a chronic, progressive, sight-threatening [[microvascular]] complication of [[diabetes mellitus]] that affects the [[retina]]. It is the leading cause of new-onset blindness among working-age adults (20–74 years) in industrialized nations and represents a major global public health burden. The disease arises from sustained [[hyperglycemia]]-induced damage to the retinal microvasculature, culminating in a spectrum of clinical findings that range from mild, asymptomatic non-proliferative changes to severe, vision-threatening proliferative disease and [[diabetic macular edema]] (DME). Early detection through systematic screening, timely intervention, and rigorous systemic metabolic control remain the cornerstones of preventing irreversible visual loss.

---

## Epidemiology

Diabetic retinopathy affects approximately one-third of all individuals living with diabetes worldwide. According to meta-analytic data, the global prevalence of any DR among diabetic patients is approximately 34.6%, with 6.96% having proliferative diabetic retinopathy (PDR) and 6.81% having clinically significant [[diabetic macular edema]] (CSME).

| **Epidemiological Parameter** | **Approximate Value** |
|---|---|
| Global diabetes prevalence (2023) | ~537 million adults |
| Prevalence of any DR among diabetics | ~34.6% |
| Prevalence of vision-threatening DR | ~10.2% |
| Prevalence of PDR | ~6.96% |
| Prevalence of DME | ~6.81% |
| Leading cause of blindness in age group | 20–74 years |

Key risk factors for the development and progression of DR include:

- **Duration of diabetes**: The single strongest predictor. After 20 years of disease, nearly all patients with [[Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus]] and over 60% of patients with [[Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus]] will have some degree of retinopathy.
- **Glycemic control**: Elevated [[glycated hemoglobin]] (HbA1c) is strongly correlated with DR incidence and progression. The Diabetes Control and Complications Trial (DCCT) and the United Kingdom Prospective Diabetes Study (UKPDS) demonstrated that intensive glycemic control reduces the risk of DR development by 76% and progression by 54%.
- **Hypertension**: Elevated [[blood pressure]] accelerates retinal microvascular damage and is an independent risk factor for DR progression.
- **Dyslipidemia**: Elevated serum [[lipids]], particularly [[low-density lipoprotein]] (LDL) cholesterol and [[triglycerides]], are associated with the development of [[hard exudates]] and DME.
- **Pregnancy**: Pregnancy can accelerate DR progression, particularly in patients with pre-existing retinopathy.
- **Nephropathy**: The presence of [[diabetic nephropathy]] and [[proteinuria]] is strongly associated with more severe DR.
- **Ethnicity**: Hispanic, African American, and South Asian populations demonstrate higher prevalence rates.
- **Smoking**: While the relationship is complex, smoking contributes to endothelial dysfunction and oxidative stress.

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of diabetic retinopathy is multifactorial and involves a complex interplay of metabolic, hemodynamic, inflammatory, and neurodegenerative mechanisms triggered by chronic [[hyperglycemia]]. Understanding these pathways is essential for the clinician to appreciate the rationale behind current and emerging therapeutic strategies.

### Biochemical Pathways

Chronic hyperglycemia activates several interconnected biochemical cascades that converge on retinal microvascular damage:

1. **[[Polyol Pathway]]**: Excess intracellular glucose is converted to [[sorbitol]] by the enzyme [[aldose reductase]], consuming [[NADPH]] in the process. Sorbitol accumulation increases intracellular osmotic stress, while NADPH depletion impairs the regeneration of [[glutathione]], a critical antioxidant. This leads to oxidative damage to [[pericytes]] and [[endothelial cells]].

2. **[[Advanced Glycation End-Products]] (AGEs) Pathway**: Non-enzymatic glycation of proteins and lipids produces AGEs, which bind to their receptor ([[RAGE]]) on endothelial cells, pericytes, and [[Müller cells]]. AGE-RAGE interaction activates [[nuclear factor kappa-B]] (NF-κB), promoting the transcription of pro-inflammatory cytokines, [[vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF), and adhesion molecules. AGEs also cause irreversible cross-linking of basement membrane collagen, leading to [[basement membrane thickening]].

3. **[[Protein Kinase C]] (PKC) Pathway**: Hyperglycemia increases the synthesis of [[diacylglycerol]] (DAG), which activates various PKC isoforms (particularly PKC-β). PKC activation leads to increased [[VEGF]] expression, enhanced vascular permeability, altered retinal blood flow, stimulation of [[extracellular matrix]] production, and activation of [[NADPH oxidase]] with consequent generation of [[reactive oxygen species]] (ROS).

4. **[[Hexosamine Pathway]]**: Diversion of excess fructose-6-phosphate into the hexosamine biosynthetic pathway results in increased production of [[uridine diphosphate N-acetylglucosamine]] (UDP-GlcNAc), which modifies transcription factors (e.g., [[Sp1]]) and increases expression of [[transforming growth factor-beta]] (TGF-β) and [[plasminogen activator inhibitor-1]] (PAI-1), promoting vascular occlusion and fibrosis.

5. **Oxidative Stress**: All of the above pathways converge on the overproduction of [[superoxide]] by the mitochondrial [[electron transport chain]]. Superoxide activates [[poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase]] (PARP), which in turn inhibits [[glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase]] (GAPDH), shunting glycolytic intermediates into the damaging pathways described above. This creates a self-amplifying cycle of metabolic injury.

### Cellular and Structural Changes

The retinal microvasculature is composed of [[endothelial cells]], [[pericytes]], and a shared [[basement membrane]]. The earliest histopathological hallmark of DR is **selective pericyte loss**. Pericytes provide structural support, regulate capillary tone, and inhibit endothelial cell proliferation. Their loss leads to:

- **Microaneurysm formation**: Focal outpouchings of the capillary wall at sites of pericyte loss, representing the earliest clinically detectable sign of DR.
- **Basement membrane thickening**: Accumulation of glycated collagen IV and other extracellular matrix components.
- **Increased vascular permeability**: Disruption of [[tight junctions]] (particularly [[occludin]], [[claudin-5]], and [[ZO-1]]) between endothelial cells compromises the inner [[blood-retinal barrier]] (BRB), leading to leakage of plasma constituents into the retinal parenchyma, manifesting as [[retinal edema]] and [[hard exudates]].
- **Capillary non-perfusion**: Progressive endothelial cell loss and thrombus formation lead to capillary closure and retinal [[ischemia]].

### Neurodegeneration

Emerging evidence recognizes DR as a **neurovascular disease**. Retinal neurodegeneration—including apoptosis of [[retinal ganglion cells]], thinning of the [[nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL), and [[Müller cell]] gliosis—may precede clinically detectable vascular changes. Neurodegeneration contributes to functional deficits such as reduced [[contrast sensitivity]], impaired [[color vision]], and abnormal [[electroretinography]] (ERG) responses even before visible retinopathy is apparent.

### Inflammation

Chronic low-grade inflammation plays a central role in DR pathogenesis. Key inflammatory mediators include:

- **[[VEGF]]**: The master regulator of angiogenesis and vascular permeability in DR.
- **[[Interleukin-1β]] (IL-1β)**, **[[Interleukin-6]] (IL-6)**, **[[Tumor Necrosis Factor-alpha]] (TNF-α)**: Pro-inflammatory cytokines that promote leukocyte adhesion and BRB breakdown.
- **[[Intercellular Adhesion Molecule-1]] (ICAM-1)**: Facilitates [[leukostasis]]—the adhesion of leukocytes to the retinal vascular endothelium—which causes endothelial cell damage and capillary occlusion.
- **[[Monocyte Chemoattractant Protein-1]] (MCP-1)**: Recruits macrophages to sites of retinal injury.

### Angiogenesis in Proliferative Disease

When retinal ischemia becomes sufficiently widespread, hypoxic retinal cells upregulate [[hypoxia-inducible factor-1α]] (HIF-1α), which drives massive overexpression of [[VEGF]], [[placental growth factor]] (PlGF), [[erythropoietin]], and other angiogenic factors. These stimulate the growth of fragile, disorganized [[neovascularization]] from the retinal surface into the [[vitreous cavity]]. These new vessels lack normal pericyte coverage and tight junctions, making them prone to hemorrhage and fibrotic contraction, which can lead to [[vitreous hemorrhage]] and [[tractional retinal detachment]].

## Classification

The internationally accepted classification system for diabetic retinopathy is based on the **International Clinical Diabetic Retinopathy Disease Severity Scale** (ICDR), which was developed from the Early Treatment Diabetic Retinopathy Study (ETDRS) classification.

### Non-Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (NPDR)

| **Severity Level** | **Clinical Findings** |
|---|---|
| **Mild NPDR** | [[Microaneurysms]] only |
| **Moderate NPDR** | Microaneurysms plus [[dot-and-blot hemorrhages]], [[hard exudates]], [[cotton-wool spots]], and/or [[venous beading]] in fewer than 2 quadrants |
| **Severe NPDR** | Any one of the following ("4-2-1 rule"): **≥20 intraretinal hemorrhages** in each of 4 quadrants, **definite venous beading** in ≥2 quadrants, or **prominent [[intraretinal microvascular abnormalities]] (IRMA)** in ≥1 quadrant; no signs of [[neovascularization]] |
| **Very Severe NPDR** | Two or more of the criteria for severe NPDR |

The **"4-2-1 rule"** is a critical clinical mnemonic for identifying severe NPDR, as approximately 50% of eyes with severe NPDR will progress to PDR within one year.

### Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (PDR)

PDR is characterized by the presence of [[neovascularization]] arising from the [[optic disc]] (**NVD** — neovascularization of the disc) or elsewhere on the retinal surface (**NVE** — neovascularization elsewhere). PDR is further classified as:

| **PDR Category** | **Features** |
|---|---|
| **Early PDR** | NVE or NVD not meeting high-risk criteria |
| **High-Risk PDR (HR-PDR)** | Any 3 of 4 Diabetic Retinopathy Study (DRS) risk factors: (1) presence of neovascularization, (2) NVD location, (3) severity of NV (NVD ≥ ¼–⅓ disc area or NVE ≥ ½ disc area), (4) presence of [[vitreous hemorrhage]] or [[preretinal hemorrhage]] |
| **Advanced PDR** | [[Tractional retinal detachment]], extensive [[fibrovascular proliferation]], dense non-clearing vitreous hemorrhage |

### Diabetic Macular Edema (DME)

[[Diabetic macular edema]] can occur at any stage of DR and is the most common cause of visual impairment in diabetic patients. It is classified separately:

| **DME Classification** | **Definition** |
|---|---|
| **DME absent** | No apparent retinal thickening or hard exudates in the [[macula]] |
| **DME present** | Retinal thickening or hard exudates in the posterior pole |
| **Center-involving DME (CI-DME)** | Retinal thickening involving the central subfield (central 1 mm zone on [[optical coherence tomography]]) |
| **Non-center-involving DME** | Retinal thickening in the macula not involving the central subfield |
| **Clinically Significant Macular Edema (CSME)** (ETDRS definition) | (1) Thickening of the retina at or within 500 μm of the center of the macula, (2) hard exudates at or within 500 μm of the center with adjacent retinal thickening, or (3) a zone of retinal thickening ≥1 disc area, any part of which is within 1 disc diameter of the center |

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Early Disease

In its early stages, diabetic retinopathy is characteristically **asymptomatic**. This is the fundamental reason why systematic screening programs are essential. Patients with mild to moderate NPDR typically have normal [[visual acuity]] and no subjective visual complaints unless DME is present.

### Progressive Disease

As DR advances, patients may report:

- **Blurred vision**: Most commonly due to [[diabetic macular edema]], which causes distortion and central visual loss.
- **Floaters**: Dark spots or cobweb-like opacities in the visual field, often caused by small [[vitreous hemorrhages]] from neovascular tissue.
- **Fluctuating vision**: Visual acuity may vary with blood glucose levels due to osmotic changes in the [[crystalline lens]].
- **Impaired color vision**: Particularly blue-yellow ([[tritan]]) axis defects, reflecting early retinal neurodegeneration.
- **Reduced contrast sensitivity**: May precede detectable vascular changes.
- **Scotomata**: Corresponding to areas of retinal ischemia or macular pathology.
- **Sudden, painless vision loss**: A hallmark of dense [[vitreous hemorrhage]] or [[tractional retinal detachment]] involving the macula in advanced PDR.
- **Nyctalopia**: Night vision difficulties due to rod photoreceptor dysfunction in ischemic retina.

### Ocular Examination Findings

| **Finding** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|
| [[Microaneurysms]] | Earliest clinical sign; focal outpouchings of capillary walls |
| [[Dot-and-blot hemorrhages]] | Intraretinal hemorrhages in the inner nuclear and outer plexiform layers |
| [[Hard exudates]] | Yellow, waxy deposits of lipid and proteinaceous material leaked from damaged capillaries; often arranged in a circinate pattern around leaking microaneurysms |
| [[Cotton-wool spots]] | White, fluffy lesions representing focal areas of [[nerve fiber layer]] infarction due to arteriolar occlusion |
| [[Venous beading]] | Focal dilation and constriction of retinal veins; a strong predictor of progression to PDR |
| [[Intraretinal microvascular abnormalities]] (IRMA) | Dilated, tortuous intraretinal capillaries representing either remodeled capillary beds or early intraretinal neovascularization; do not cross major retinal vessels |
| [[Neovascularization]] (NVD/NVE) | Fine, irregular new vessels growing from the retinal surface; pathognomonic of PDR |
| [[Preretinal hemorrhage]] | Boat-shaped or dome-shaped hemorrhage between the retina and posterior [[vitreous]] face |
| [[Vitreous hemorrhage]] | Blood in the vitreous cavity; may obscure fundus view |
| [[Fibrovascular proliferation]] | Fibrous tissue accompanying neovascularization; can contract and cause tractional retinal detachment |
| [[Tractional retinal detachment]] | Separation of the [[neurosensory retina]] from the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] due to contraction of fibrovascular membranes |
| [[Neovascular glaucoma]] (NVG) | Neovascularization of the [[iris]] ([[rubeosis iridis]]) and [[anterior chamber angle]], leading to secondary angle-closure [[glaucoma]]; a devastating complication of severe retinal ischemia |

## Diagnosis and Diagnostic Modalities

### Comprehensive Dilated Fundus Examination

The cornerstone of DR diagnosis is a thorough **dilated [[fundoscopy]]** using [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] with a high-powered condensing lens (e.g., [[78D lens]], [[90D lens]], or [[Volk Super Field lens]]) and/or [[binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy]] (BIO). This allows stereoscopic evaluation of the [[optic disc]], [[macula]], retinal vasculature, and peripheral retina.

### Fundus Photography

Standardized **[[fundus photography]]** provides objective documentation of retinal findings and enables comparison over time. The ETDRS 7-standard field protocol remains the gold standard for clinical trials, though wide-field and ultra-wide-field imaging systems (e.g., [[Optos]]) have expanded the ability to capture peripheral retinal pathology in a single image.

### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

**[[Optical coherence tomography]]** (OCT) is indispensable in the evaluation and management of diabetic retinopathy, particularly DME. Spectral-domain OCT (SD-OCT) and swept-source OCT (SS-OCT) provide high-resolution, cross-sectional images of the retina, enabling:

- Quantitative measurement of **central subfield thickness** (CST) to diagnose and monitor center-involving DME.
- Identification of morphological features: [[intraretinal cysts]], [[subretinal fluid]], [[disorganization of retinal inner layers]] (DRIL), [[hyperreflective foci]] (thought to represent activated microglia or lipoprotein extravasation), and [[epiretinal membrane]] formation.
- Assessment of **[[ellipsoid zone]]** (photoreceptor inner segment/outer segment junction) integrity as a biomarker for visual prognosis.
- Measurement of [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL) and [[ganglion cell-inner plexiform layer]] (GC-IPL) thickness to detect early neurodegeneration.
- Monitoring treatment response to anti-VEGF therapy and other interventions.

### OCT Angiography (OCTA)

**[[OCT angiography]]** (OCTA) is a non-invasive imaging modality that generates depth-resolved maps of retinal and choroidal vasculature without the need for intravenous dye injection. In DR, OCTA is valuable for:

- Delineating the **[[foveal avascular zone]]** (FAZ) and detecting FAZ enlargement or irregularity, which correlates with macular ischemia.
- Identifying areas of **capillary non-perfusion** in the superficial and deep capillary plexuses.
- Detecting subclinical **microaneurysms** and **neovascularization**.
- Quantifying **vascular density** as a biomarker for disease severity.

Limitations include motion artifacts, projection artifacts, a limited field of view (though widefield OCTA is improving), and inability to assess vascular leakage.

### Fluorescein Angiography (FA)

**[[Fluorescein angiography]]** remains a critical diagnostic tool, particularly for:

- Identifying areas of **capillary non-perfusion** and retinal ischemia.
- Confirming the presence and extent of **neovascularization** (which demonstrates profuse leakage of fluorescein).
- Detecting the source of **macular edema** (focal vs. diffuse leakage patterns).
- Guiding **focal/grid laser photocoagulation** treatment.
- Differentiating IRMA (which does not leak) from true neovascularization (which leaks profusely).

The procedure involves intravenous injection of [[sodium fluorescein]] dye and sequential photography. Risks include nausea (most common), allergic reactions, and rarely, [[anaphylaxis]].

### B-Scan Ultrasonography

**[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** is essential when the fundus view is obscured by dense [[vitreous hemorrhage]] or [[cataract]]. It allows assessment of:

- Presence and density of vitreous hemorrhage.
- Status of the retina (attached vs. detached).
- Presence of [[posterior vitreous detachment]] (PVD).
- Fibrovascular proliferation and tractional membranes.

### Artificial Intelligence (AI) Screening

**[[Artificial intelligence]]**-based DR screening systems (e.g., IDx-DR/LumineticsCore, EyeArt) have received regulatory approval (FDA) for autonomous detection of more-than-mild DR and DME from fundus photographs. These systems demonstrate high sensitivity (>87%) and specificity (>90%) and are increasingly deployed in primary care settings to expand screening access.

### Visual Function Testing

- **[[Visual acuity]]** (best-corrected): Standard metric for monitoring visual function.
- **[[Amsler grid]]**: Useful for patient self-monitoring of metamorphopsia from DME.
- **[[Contrast sensitivity]]**: May be reduced before visual acuity is affected.
- **[[Electroretinography]]** (ERG): Multifocal ERG can detect localized retinal dysfunction; full-field ERG may show reduced oscillatory potentials, reflecting inner retinal ischemia.
- **[[Visual field testing]]**: May reveal scotomata corresponding to ischemic areas or laser scars.

## Screening Guidelines

Given the asymptomatic nature of early DR, systematic screening is paramount:

| **Patient Population** | **Recommended Initial Screening** | **Follow-Up Interval** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus]] | Within 5 years of diagnosis (after puberty) | Annually |
| [[Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus]] | At the time of diagnosis | Annually (or more frequently based on severity) |
| Pregnancy with pre-existing diabetes | Before conception or in the first trimester | Each trimester and 1 year postpartum |
| No DR or mild NPDR | — | Every 1–2 years |
| Moderate NPDR | — | Every 6–12 months |
| Severe NPDR | — | Every 2–4 months |
| PDR or DME | — | Every 1–3 months (or as dictated by treatment) |

## Management and Treatment

The management of diabetic retinopathy is multifaceted, encompassing systemic risk factor optimization, pharmacological therapy, laser photocoagulation, and vitreoretinal surgery.

### Systemic Risk Factor Control

Optimal systemic management is the foundation of DR prevention and treatment:

- **Glycemic control**: Target HbA1c ≤ 7.0% (per ADA guidelines). The DCCT/EDIC and UKPDS trials demonstrated that intensive glycemic control significantly reduces the incidence and progression of DR. However, rapid normalization of blood glucose in patients with longstanding poor control can paradoxically cause transient **early worsening** of retinopathy.
- **Blood pressure control**: Target < 130/80 mmHg. The UKPDS showed that tight blood pressure control reduced DR progression by 34% and the risk of visual loss by 47%. [[Angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors]] (ACE inhibitors) and [[angiotensin II receptor blockers]] (ARBs) may have retinoprotective effects beyond blood pressure lowering (DIRECT and RASS studies).
- **Lipid management**: [[Fenofibrate]] has demonstrated a specific benefit in reducing DR progression independent of its lipid-lowering effects (FIELD and ACCORD Eye studies), possibly through anti-inflammatory and anti-apoptotic mechanisms. [[Statins]] are recommended for cardiovascular risk reduction.
- **Smoking cessation**: Counseling and support for tobacco cessation.
- **Renal function monitoring**: Nephropathy and retinopathy often progress in parallel.

### Intravitreal Anti-VEGF Therapy

**[[Anti-VEGF]]** agents have revolutionized the treatment of both DME and PDR and are now considered first-line therapy for center-involving DME and are increasingly used for PDR.

| **Agent** | **Mechanism** | **Dosing** | **Key Trials** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Ranibizumab]]** (Lucentis®) | Monoclonal antibody fragment binding VEGF-A | 0.3 mg or 0.5 mg intravitreal injection | RISE/RIDE, RESTORE, DRCR.net Protocol I, Protocol S |
| **[[Aflibercept]]** (Eylea®) | Recombinant fusion protein binding VEGF-A, VEGF-B, and PlGF | 2 mg intravitreal injection | VISTA/VIVID, DRCR.net Protocol T |
| **[[Bevacizumab]]** (Avastin®) | Full-length monoclonal antibody binding VEGF-A (off-label) | 1.25 mg intravitreal injection | BOLT, DRCR.net Protocol T |
| **[[Brolucizumab]]** (Beovu®) | Single-chain antibody fragment binding VEGF-A | 6 mg intravitreal injection | KESTREL/KITE (for DME) |
| **[[Faricimab]]** (Vabysmo®) | Bispecific antibody binding VEGF-A and [[Angiopoietin-2]] (Ang-2) | 6 mg intravitreal injection | YOSEMITE/RHINE |

The **DRCR.net Protocol T** trial compared ranibizumab, aflibercept, and bevacizumab for CI-DME and found that all three agents were effective. In eyes with worse baseline visual acuity (20/50 or worse), aflibercept demonstrated superior visual acuity gains at one year, though differences diminished by two years.

The **DRCR.net Protocol S** trial demonstrated that intravitreal ranibizumab was non-inferior to [[panretinal photocoagulation]] (PRP) for PDR at two years, with the added benefit of less visual field loss and lower rates of DME development.

**Treatment regimens** include:

- **Monthly loading doses** (typically 4–6 injections) followed by a treat-and-extend or pro re nata (PRN) approach.
- **Treat-and-extend**: Injection intervals are gradually lengthened if the macula is stable, reducing treatment burden.
- **PRN (as needed)**: Injections are given only when disease activity recurs, guided by OCT and visual acuity.

**Faricimab**, with its dual mechanism targeting both VEGF-A and Ang-2, has demonstrated the potential for extended dosing intervals (up to 16 weeks) in the YOSEMITE and RHINE trials, addressing the significant treatment burden of frequent injections.

### Intravitreal Corticosteroids

Corticosteroids address the inflammatory component of DME and are typically reserved for eyes that are **refractory to anti-VEGF therapy**, [[pseudophakic]] eyes, or patients who cannot tolerate frequent anti-VEGF injections.

| **Agent** | **Formulation** | **Duration of Action** | **Key Considerations** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Dexamethasone intravitreal implant]]** (Ozurdex®) | Biodegradable sustained-release implant | ~4–6 months | MEAD trial; risk of [[cataract]] progression and [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) elevation |
| **[[Fluocinolone acetonide intravitreal implant]]** (Iluvien®) | Non-biodegradable sustained-release implant | Up to 36 months | FAME trial; significant risk of IOP elevation (requiring filtering surgery in ~5%) and cataract |
| **[[Triamcinolone acetonide]]** (intravitreal) | Suspension injection | ~3 months | DRCR.net Protocol B/I; off-label; significant cataract and IOP risks |

### Laser Photocoagulation

Although anti-VEGF therapy has become the primary treatment for DME and is increasingly used for PDR, **laser photocoagulation** remains an important therapeutic modality.

#### Panretinal Photocoagulation (PRP)

**[[Panretinal photocoagulation]]** (PRP) involves the application of 1,200–2,000+ laser burns (typically 200–500 μm spot size) to the peripheral retina, sparing the [[macula]] and [[optic disc]]. The mechanism is thought to involve destruction of ischemic retinal tissue, thereby reducing the overall VEGF drive and promoting regression of neovascularization.

- **Indications**: High-risk PDR (standard of care), severe NPDR or early PDR in patients at high risk of loss to follow-up, and as adjunctive therapy to anti-VEGF in PDR.
- **Complications**: Peripheral [[visual field]] constriction, reduced [[scotopic vision]] (night vision), exacerbation of DME, mild reduction in visual acuity, pain during treatment, and rarely, choroidal effusion or exudative retinal detachment.
- **Technique**: Can be performed with [[argon laser]], [[pattern scanning laser]] (PASCAL), or [[navigated laser]] systems. Subthreshold or micropulse laser techniques are being investigated to reduce collateral damage.

#### Focal/Grid Macular Laser

**[[Focal laser photocoagulation]]** targets specific leaking microaneurysms identified on fluorescein angiography, while **[[grid laser]]** is applied in a grid pattern to areas of diffuse macular thickening and capillary non-perfusion. Per the ETDRS, focal/grid laser reduces the risk of moderate visual loss from CSME by approximately 50%. While anti-VEGF therapy has largely supplanted macular laser as first-line treatment for CI-DME, focal/grid laser remains useful for:

- Non-center-involving DME.
- Persistent DME despite anti-VEGF therapy (adjunctive role).
- Settings where frequent anti-VEGF injections are not feasible.

### Vitreoretinal Surgery

**[[Pars plana vitrectomy]]** (PPV) is indicated for advanced complications of PDR:

| **Indication** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| Non-clearing [[vitreous hemorrhage]] | Typically observed for 1–3 months before surgical intervention (shorter in Type 1 DM or monocular patients) |
| [[Tractional retinal detachment]] involving or threatening the macula | Urgent surgical intervention required |
| Combined tractional-[[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]] | Surgical emergency |
| Dense [[premacular hemorrhage]] | Vitrectomy with or without tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) |
| Severe [[fibrovascular proliferation]] | Membrane peeling and endolaser |
| Persistent DME with vitreomacular traction | Vitrectomy may relieve traction and improve edema |

Modern vitrectomy techniques employ small-gauge (23-, 25-, or 27-gauge) instrumentation, wide-angle viewing systems, and intraoperative [[endolaser]] photocoagulation. Adjunctive intravitreal anti-VEGF injection (typically 1–7 days preoperatively) can reduce intraoperative bleeding from neovascular tissue.

## Emerging Therapies and Future Directions

The therapeutic landscape for DR is rapidly evolving:

- **[[Port Delivery System]]** (PDS) with ranibizumab (Susvimo®): A surgically implanted, refillable intraocular reservoir that continuously releases ranibizumab, potentially reducing treatment burden to refills every 6 months (Archway trial). (Note: temporarily withdrawn from market for device-related issues but represents the concept of sustained delivery.)
- **[[Biosimilars]]**: Biosimilar anti-VEGF agents (e.g., ranibizumab biosimilars) are entering the market, potentially improving access and reducing costs.
- **Gene therapy**: Intravitreal or subretinal delivery of genes encoding anti-VEGF proteins (e.g., [[RGX-314]], [[ADVM-022]]) aims to provide long-lasting VEGF suppression from a single treatment.
- **Tie-2 pathway activators**: Agents targeting the [[Angiopoietin]]-[[Tie-2]] pathway (beyond faricimab) to restore vascular stability.
- **Neuroprotective agents**: Targeting retinal neurodegeneration with agents such as [[somatostatin analogs]], [[brimonidine]], and [[citicoline]].
- **Anti-inflammatory agents**: Novel intravitreal anti-inflammatory biologics targeting specific cytokines (e.g., IL-6 inhibitors).
- **Sustained-release drug delivery**: Biodegradable and non-biodegradable implants, hydrogel depots, and nanoparticle formulations to extend dosing intervals.
- **Artificial intelligence**: Expanding role in screening, risk stratification, and treatment response prediction.

## Prognosis

The prognosis for diabetic retinopathy has improved dramatically with modern therapies. With timely detection and appropriate treatment:

- Over 90% of severe visual loss from PDR can be prevented with PRP.
- Anti-VEGF therapy for CI-DME results in a mean improvement of 10–13 ETDRS letters at one year, with approximately 30–40% of patients gaining ≥15 letters (3 lines).
- However, despite optimal treatment, some patients experience progressive visual loss due to chronic macular ischemia, persistent edema, photoreceptor damage, or optic neuropathy.
- Long-term visual outcomes are strongly correlated with baseline visual acuity, duration of DME, integrity of the [[ellipsoid zone]], degree of [[disorganization of retinal inner layers]] (DRIL), and systemic metabolic control.

## Role of the Optometrist

[[Optometrists]] play a critical role in the management of diabetic retinopathy across the continuum of care:

1. **Screening and early detection**: Performing comprehensive dilated fundus examinations, fundus photography, and OCT in diabetic patients.
2. **Risk assessment and patient education**: Counseling patients on the importance of glycemic control, blood pressure management, and adherence to screening schedules.
3. **Monitoring**: Serial assessment of DR severity, DME status, and visual function.
4. **Co-management**: Timely referral to [[retinal specialists]] for vision-threatening DR (severe NPDR, PDR, CI-DME) and collaborative post-treatment monitoring.
5. **Telehealth and AI-assisted screening**: Utilizing remote imaging and AI-based screening platforms to expand access in underserved populations.
6. **Low vision rehabilitation**: Providing [[low vision]] services for patients with irreversible visual impairment from advanced DR.

---

## Summary

Diabetic retinopathy remains a leading cause of preventable blindness worldwide. Its pathogenesis involves a complex interplay of metabolic, inflammatory, and neurodegenerative mechanisms driven by chronic hyperglycemia. The disease progresses through well-defined stages from mild NPDR to sight-threatening PDR and DME. Modern diagnostic tools—including OCT, OCTA, fluorescein angiography, and AI-based screening—enable early detection and precise monitoring. Treatment has been transformed by intravitreal anti-VEGF therapy, which is now first-line for CI-DME and increasingly for PDR, complemented by laser photocoagulation and vitreoretinal surgery for advanced disease. Systemic risk factor control remains paramount. Optometrists serve as essential frontline providers in screening, monitoring, co-management, and patient education, ensuring that patients with diabetes receive timely, evidence-based eye care to preserve vision.
