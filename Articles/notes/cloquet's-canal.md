

# Cloquet's Canal

---

**Cloquet's Canal** (also known as the **hyaloid canal**, **canalis hyaloideus**, or **Stilling's canal**) is a transparent, narrow anatomical channel that traverses the [[vitreous body]] of the [[eye]] in an anteroposterior direction, extending from the [[optic disc]] posteriorly to the retrolental space (the area immediately behind the [[crystalline lens]]) anteriorly. Named after the French anatomist **Jules Germain Cloquet** (1790–1883), who first described it in 1821, this vestigial structure represents the remnant of the embryonic [[hyaloid artery]] and its surrounding tissue, which served as the primary vascular supply to the developing [[lens]] and primary [[vitreous humor]] during fetal ocular development. In the mature, healthy eye, Cloquet's canal is an optically empty, fluid-filled tube that is generally of no clinical consequence. However, its incomplete regression, pathological persistence, or involvement in vitreoretinal disease processes makes it a structure of significant clinical relevance to practitioners of [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]].

---

## Historical Background and Nomenclature

Jules Germain Cloquet, a professor of anatomy in Paris, provided the first detailed anatomical description of this canal in his work on the vitreous body. The structure has since been referred to by several synonymous terms in the ophthalmic literature:

| **Term** | **Origin / Context** |
|---|---|
| Cloquet's Canal | Named after Jules Germain Cloquet (1821) |
| Hyaloid Canal | Refers to its origin from the [[hyaloid artery]] system |
| Canalis Hyaloideus | Latin anatomical nomenclature (Terminologia Anatomica) |
| Stilling's Canal | Named after Jakob Stilling, who independently described the structure |
| Central Canal of the Vitreous | Descriptive anatomical term |

The term "Cloquet's canal" remains the most widely used eponym in clinical optometric and ophthalmologic practice, while "hyaloid canal" is preferred in formal anatomical nomenclature. The structure is listed in the **Terminologia Anatomica** as *canalis hyaloideus* (TA: A15.2.06.009).

## Embryology and Development

A thorough understanding of Cloquet's canal requires detailed knowledge of the embryological development of the [[vitreous humor]] and the fetal vasculature of the eye. The canal's existence is inextricably linked to the lifecycle of the [[hyaloid artery]] system.

### The Hyaloid Vascular System

During early embryonic development, the [[optic cup]] and developing [[lens vesicle]] require a dedicated vascular supply. By approximately the **fourth week of gestation** (around 6 mm embryonic stage), the [[hyaloid artery]]—a branch of the primitive [[ophthalmic artery]] (itself a branch of the [[internal carotid artery]])—enters the developing eye through the [[optic fissure]] (embryonic fissure). This artery courses through the primary vitreous, traversing the entire length of the vitreous cavity from the [[optic nerve head]] to the posterior surface of the developing [[crystalline lens]].

Upon reaching the lens, the hyaloid artery arborizes into a dense capillary network known as the **[[tunica vasculosa lentis]]** (TVL), which envelops the posterior and lateral surfaces of the lens. The anterior portion of this vascular network is termed the **[[anterior pupillary membrane]]**. Together, these structures form the **[[vasa hyaloidea propria]]**, a complex vascular network embedded within the primary vitreous that provides essential nutrition to the avascular lens and surrounding structures during the critical period of ocular organogenesis.

### Formation of the Canal

The hyaloid artery does not simply course freely through the vitreous. Rather, it is surrounded by a condensation of primary vitreous and a sheath of [[glial cells]] and [[basement membrane]] material. As the secondary vitreous (the definitive, mature vitreous body composed primarily of [[collagen]] type II fibrils and [[hyaluronic acid]]) begins to form around the **ninth week of gestation**, it progressively fills the vitreous cavity from the periphery inward. The secondary vitreous condenses around the hyaloid artery and its surrounding sheath, effectively creating a tubular channel—the nascent Cloquet's canal.

### Regression of the Hyaloid System

The regression of the hyaloid vascular system is a precisely orchestrated process of programmed cell death ([[apoptosis]]) and vascular remodeling that begins during the **third month of gestation** (approximately the 9th week) and is normally complete by the **eighth to ninth month of gestation** (approximately 35–36 weeks). The process proceeds in a characteristic anterior-to-posterior sequence:

1. **Anterior pupillary membrane** regresses first (months 3–6)
2. **Tunica vasculosa lentis** regresses next (months 4–7)
3. **Hyaloid artery trunk** regresses last (months 7–9)

The molecular mechanisms governing hyaloid regression involve several key pathways:

- **[[Vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF) withdrawal**: As the lens matures and becomes avascular, local VEGF levels decrease, removing the survival signal for hyaloid endothelial cells.
- **[[Macrophage]]-mediated apoptosis**: [[Hyalocytes]] and macrophages in the vitreous actively induce endothelial cell apoptosis through [[Wnt signaling pathway]] (specifically Wnt7b) and [[Angiopoietin-2]] signaling.
- **Lens crystallin expression**: Maturation of the lens with increasing [[crystallin]] protein expression correlates with reduced paracrine support for the hyaloid vasculature.
- **[[Autophagy]]**: Programmed autophagic processes contribute to the orderly dismantling of vascular endothelial cells.

Following complete regression of the hyaloid artery, the tubular space it once occupied persists as Cloquet's canal—a transparent, optically clear channel within the vitreous body.

## Anatomy

### Gross Anatomy

Cloquet's canal is a narrow, tubular channel approximately **1–2 mm in diameter** that extends through the central vitreous body along a slightly S-shaped (sigmoid) or gently curving course. Its total length corresponds roughly to the axial length of the vitreous cavity, approximately **16–18 mm** in the adult emmetropic eye.

| **Anatomical Feature** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Anterior Terminus** | Retrolental space (Berger's space / [[erger's space]]), posterior to the [[crystalline lens]] |
| **Posterior Terminus** | [[Optic disc]] (area of Martegiani) |
| **Diameter** | Approximately 1–2 mm (variable) |
| **Length** | Approximately 16–18 mm |
| **Course** | Slightly S-shaped, traversing the central vitreous |
| **Contents** | Optically clear fluid (aqueous-like); no cellular elements in normal state |
| **Wall Composition** | Condensed vitreous cortex (secondary vitreous) |

### Anterior Attachment: Berger's Space

The anterior end of Cloquet's canal opens into a small potential space between the posterior surface of the [[crystalline lens]] and the anterior [[hyaloid face]] (anterior vitreous cortex), known as **[[Berger's space]]** (retrolental space of Berger, or *spatium retrolentale*). This space, described by Emil Berger in 1882, is bounded:

- **Anteriorly**: By the posterior capsule of the [[crystalline lens]]
- **Posteriorly**: By the anterior hyaloid membrane (anterior vitreous cortex)
- **Laterally**: By the [[hyaloideocapsular ligament]] (ligament of Wieger), a circular attachment of the anterior vitreous face to the posterior lens capsule, approximately 8–9 mm in diameter

The ligament of Wieger ([[Wieger's ligament]]) forms a ring-shaped adhesion that effectively seals Berger's space from the surrounding retrolental vitreous. This anatomical relationship is clinically significant during [[cataract surgery]], particularly in the context of [[posterior capsule]] integrity and vitreous prolapse.

### Posterior Attachment: Area of Martegiani

The posterior terminus of Cloquet's canal opens into a funnel-shaped expansion overlying the [[optic disc]], known as the **[[area of Martegiani]]** (or Martegiani's funnel). This region represents the posterior anchorage point of the canal and corresponds to the site where the embryonic hyaloid artery originally emerged from the optic nerve head. The area of Martegiani is bordered by the peripapillary vitreous cortex and is in close anatomical proximity to the [[central retinal artery]] and [[central retinal vein]] as they emerge from the optic disc.

### Relationship to Vitreous Structure

The vitreous body is not a homogeneous gel but possesses a complex internal architecture of [[collagen]] fibrils and [[hyaluronic acid]] (hyaluronan) organized into lamellae and tracts. Cloquet's canal is situated within the central vitreous, surrounded by the denser cortical vitreous. The walls of the canal are formed by condensed vitreous lamellae that represent the boundary between the primary vitreous remnant (within the canal) and the secondary vitreous (surrounding the canal).

Key anatomical relationships include:

- **[[Vitreous base]]**: The annular zone of firm vitreoretinal adhesion straddling the [[ora serrata]], located peripherally and distinct from Cloquet's canal
- **[[Anterior hyaloid face]]**: The anterior boundary of the vitreous, to which the canal's anterior end is related via Berger's space
- **[[Posterior hyaloid face]]**: The posterior vitreous cortex overlying the [[retina]], through which the canal's posterior end communicates with the area of Martegiani
- **[[Cloquet's canal]] vs. [[Petit's canal]]**: Petit's canal (canalis Petiti) is a distinct circumferential space within the [[zonular fibers]] of the lens and should not be confused with Cloquet's canal

## Pathophysiology

### Normal Physiological State

In the fully developed, healthy adult eye, Cloquet's canal is a clinically insignificant vestigial structure. It contains optically clear fluid that is slightly less viscous than the surrounding vitreous gel. The canal does not normally impede light transmission, does not affect [[visual acuity]], and is not visible on routine [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] without specialized techniques. It may become more apparent with age-related [[vitreous liquefaction]] ([[synchysis]]) and [[vitreous syneresis]], as the surrounding vitreous gel structure degrades.

### Persistent Hyaloid Artery (Incomplete Regression)

The most clinically significant pathological condition directly related to Cloquet's canal is the **[[persistent hyaloid artery]]** (PHA), also termed **persistent hyaloid vasculature** when referring to the broader vascular remnant. This condition results from failure of the normal apoptotic regression of the hyaloid artery during fetal development.

The spectrum of persistent hyaloid remnants includes:

| **Condition** | **Description** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Mittendorf's dot]]** | Small, white opacity on the posterior lens capsule at the anterior attachment of the hyaloid artery | Usually benign; common incidental finding |
| **[[Bergmeister's papilla]]** | Glial remnant on the optic disc at the posterior attachment of the hyaloid artery | Usually benign; may be associated with [[epipapillary membrane]] |
| **Persistent hyaloid artery (partial)** | Incomplete regression leaving a fibrous cord or patent vessel within Cloquet's canal | Variable; may cause [[vitreous hemorrhage]] if patent |
| **Persistent hyaloid artery (complete)** | Fully patent vessel extending from optic disc to lens | Rare; may cause significant visual impairment |
| **[[Persistent fetal vasculature]] (PFV)** | Comprehensive term for persistent hyaloid system with associated developmental anomalies | Potentially severe; may cause [[microphthalmos]], [[cataract]], [[retinal detachment]] |

#### Mittendorf's Dot

**[[Mittendorf's dot]]** is the most common remnant of the hyaloid system, found in approximately **3% of the general population** (some studies suggest prevalence as high as 20–30% when examined with high-resolution imaging). It appears as a small (0.5–1.0 mm), round, white or grayish opacity on the posterior capsule of the lens, typically located slightly nasal and inferior to the posterior pole of the lens. It represents the anterior attachment point of the regressed hyaloid artery. Mittendorf's dot is almost always an incidental finding of no visual consequence, though rarely it may be associated with a localized [[posterior subcapsular cataract]].

#### Bergmeister's Papilla

**[[Bergmeister's papilla]]** is a small tuft of glial tissue overlying the [[optic disc]], representing the posterior remnant of the hyaloid artery's attachment. It is typically a small, white, elevated tissue mass on the disc surface. It is generally asymptomatic and discovered incidentally during [[fundoscopy]]. In rare cases, it may be associated with [[prepapillary vascular loops]] or [[epipapillary membranes]].

#### Persistent Fetal Vasculature (PFV)

**[[Persistent fetal vasculature]]** (PFV), formerly known as **persistent hyperplastic primary vitreous** (PHPV), represents the most severe end of the spectrum of hyaloid regression failure. PFV involves persistence of the entire hyaloid vascular system along with the primary vitreous, and is classified as:

- **Anterior PFV**: Predominantly involves the retrolental space, with a fibrovascular membrane behind the lens, often causing [[leukocoria]], [[cataract]], and [[shallow anterior chamber]]
- **Posterior PFV**: Involves the posterior vitreous and retina, with retinal folds, [[retinal detachment]], and [[optic nerve hypoplasia]]
- **Combined PFV**: Features of both anterior and posterior involvement

PFV is typically **unilateral** (90% of cases), which helps distinguish it from [[retinoblastoma]] and other causes of [[leukocoria]] in the differential diagnosis. It is associated with [[microphthalmos]] in the affected eye.

### Role in Vitreoretinal Pathology

Cloquet's canal may play a secondary role in several vitreoretinal conditions:

- **[[Posterior vitreous detachment]] (PVD)**: During PVD, the posterior hyaloid face separates from the retina. The area of Martegiani and the posterior opening of Cloquet's canal may be involved in the mechanics of this separation. The [[Weiss ring]]—a circular glial remnant seen floating in the vitreous after PVD—originates from the peripapillary tissue at the posterior terminus of Cloquet's canal.
- **[[Asteroid hyalosis]]**: Calcium-lipid complexes (calcium soaps) suspended in the vitreous may occasionally be observed within or adjacent to Cloquet's canal.
- **[[Vitreous hemorrhage]]**: In rare cases of patent persistent hyaloid artery, spontaneous hemorrhage within Cloquet's canal may occur.
- **Pathways for disease spread**: Cloquet's canal has been theorized to serve as a potential conduit for the anteroposterior spread of inflammatory mediators or infectious agents within the vitreous cavity, though this remains largely theoretical.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Asymptomatic Presentation (Most Common)

In the vast majority of individuals, Cloquet's canal is entirely **asymptomatic** and is never detected during routine clinical examination. The canal is a normal anatomical variant of no clinical consequence in its typical regressed state.

### Symptomatic Presentations

When symptoms do occur, they are invariably related to **pathological persistence** of the hyaloid system or to **secondary involvement** of the canal in other vitreoretinal disease processes:

| **Presentation** | **Associated Condition** | **Symptoms** |
|---|---|---|
| Asymptomatic white dot on lens | [[Mittendorf's dot]] | None (incidental finding) |
| Asymptomatic disc finding | [[Bergmeister's papilla]] | None (incidental finding) |
| [[Floaters]] (muscae volitantes) | Visible remnant cord within canal; [[PVD]] with Weiss ring | Perception of floating spots or threads |
| [[Leukocoria]] (white pupil) | [[Persistent fetal vasculature]] (anterior) | White pupillary reflex; noted by parents in infants |
| Decreased [[visual acuity]] | PFV with [[cataract]], [[amblyopia]] | Reduced vision, often unilateral |
| [[Strabismus]] | Secondary to unilateral visual deprivation from PFV | Ocular misalignment |
| [[Vitreous hemorrhage]] | Patent persistent hyaloid artery | Sudden painless vision loss, floaters, red haze |
| [[Nystagmus]] | Severe bilateral PFV (rare) | Involuntary rhythmic eye movements |

### Pediatric Considerations

In neonates and infants, the hyaloid system may still be in the process of regression. Remnants of the hyaloid artery within Cloquet's canal are commonly observed in **premature infants** and may be visible as fine, thread-like structures extending from the optic disc toward the lens. These remnants typically regress spontaneously within the first few weeks to months of postnatal life. The presence of a prominent hyaloid remnant in a premature infant should be documented but does not necessarily indicate pathology unless it persists beyond the expected period of regression or is associated with other ocular abnormalities.

## Diagnosis and Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical Examination

#### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

**[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]** with a narrow, focused beam can occasionally reveal Cloquet's canal as a faint, optically empty tube within the vitreous, particularly in eyes with early vitreous liquefaction. The use of a **[[Goldmann three-mirror lens]]** or a **[[90-diopter lens]]** / **[[78-diopter lens]]** for posterior segment examination may enhance visualization. Mittendorf's dot on the posterior lens capsule is best visualized with the slit lamp using retroillumination technique.

#### Direct and Indirect Ophthalmoscopy

**[[Direct ophthalmoscopy]]** and **[[binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy]]** (BIO) are essential for evaluating the optic disc for Bergmeister's papilla and for detecting any persistent hyaloid remnants extending from the disc into the vitreous cavity. BIO with [[scleral indentation]] may be useful for evaluating the peripheral vitreous and vitreous base in cases of suspected PFV.

### Advanced Imaging

#### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

**[[Optical coherence tomography]]** (OCT), particularly **[[spectral-domain OCT]]** (SD-OCT) and **[[swept-source OCT]]** (SS-OCT), has revolutionized the visualization of Cloquet's canal. Enhanced depth imaging (EDI) and vitreous imaging protocols can demonstrate the canal as a hyporeflective (dark) tubular structure within the vitreous body. Key OCT findings include:

- **Hyporeflective tubular channel** traversing the central vitreous
- **Posterior funnel-shaped opening** at the optic disc (area of Martegiani)
- **Anterior attachment** to the retrolental space
- **Hyperreflective walls** representing condensed vitreous cortex bordering the canal
- **Remnant tissue** within the canal in cases of persistent hyaloid artery

**Intraoperative OCT** has also been used to visualize Cloquet's canal during [[vitrectomy]] procedures, providing real-time anatomical guidance.

#### Ultrasonography (B-Scan)

**[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** (10 MHz) can demonstrate Cloquet's canal as a hypoechoic linear structure within the vitreous, particularly when the canal contains remnant tissue or blood. In cases of PFV, B-scan ultrasonography is invaluable for demonstrating:

- A hyperechoic band extending from the optic disc to the posterior lens surface
- Retrolental membrane or mass
- Associated [[retinal detachment]] or [[retinal fold]]
- [[Microphthalmos]] (reduced axial length)

**[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]]** (UBM, 35–50 MHz) provides higher resolution imaging of the anterior segment and can visualize the anterior attachment of persistent hyaloid remnants to the posterior lens capsule and the relationship to [[Berger's space]].

#### Fluorescein Angiography

**[[Fluorescein angiography]]** (FA) is not routinely used to evaluate Cloquet's canal itself but may be employed when a patent persistent hyaloid artery is suspected. A patent vessel will demonstrate fluorescein filling, confirming active blood flow. FA is also useful for evaluating associated retinal vascular abnormalities in PFV.

#### Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

**[[Magnetic resonance imaging]]** (MRI) of the orbits may be utilized in complex cases of PFV, particularly to differentiate PFV from [[retinoblastoma]] or other intraocular tumors causing [[leukocoria]]. On MRI, PFV typically demonstrates:

- Enhancement of the persistent fibrovascular stalk within Cloquet's canal on post-gadolinium T1-weighted images
- Absence of calcification (unlike retinoblastoma)
- Microphthalmos of the affected eye

#### Computed Tomography (CT)

**[[Computed tomography]]** (CT) may be used in the differential diagnosis of leukocoria. PFV typically shows no intraocular calcification on CT, in contrast to [[retinoblastoma]], which characteristically demonstrates calcification in over 90% of cases.

### Diagnostic Summary Table

| **Modality** | **Findings in Normal Cloquet's Canal** | **Findings in Pathological States** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] | Faint optically empty channel; Mittendorf's dot | Visible cord/vessel; retrolental opacity |
| [[Ophthalmoscopy]] | Bergmeister's papilla (incidental) | Persistent vessel from disc; retinal folds |
| [[OCT]] (SD/SS) | Hyporeflective tubular channel | Hyperreflective remnant tissue within canal |
| [[B-scan ultrasonography]] | Usually not visualized | Hyperechoic band disc-to-lens; retrolental mass |
| [[Fluorescein angiography]] | Not applicable | Fluorescein filling of patent vessel |
| [[MRI]] | Not routinely performed | Enhancing stalk; microphthalmos; no calcification |
| [[CT]] | Not routinely performed | No calcification (vs. retinoblastoma) |

## Differential Diagnosis

When pathological remnants within Cloquet's canal are identified, particularly in the pediatric population presenting with [[leukocoria]], the following differential diagnoses must be considered:

| **Condition** | **Key Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| **[[Retinoblastoma]]** | Calcification on CT; bilateral in 30–40%; no microphthalmos |
| **[[Persistent fetal vasculature]]** | Unilateral; microphthalmos; no calcification; fibrovascular stalk |
| **[[Retinopathy of prematurity]]** (ROP) | Bilateral; history of prematurity and oxygen therapy |
| **[[Coats disease]]** | Unilateral; male predominance; telangiectatic vessels; exudative retinal detachment |
| **[[Toxocariasis]]** | Unilateral; granuloma; positive serology |
| **[[Familial exudative vitreoretinopathy]]** (FEVR) | Bilateral; peripheral avascular retina; family history |
| **[[Norrie disease]]** | Bilateral; X-linked; associated deafness and intellectual disability |

## Management and Treatment

### Observation (No Treatment Required)

The vast majority of Cloquet's canal findings require **no treatment**. Specifically:

- **Normal Cloquet's canal**: No intervention needed. It is a normal anatomical structure.
- **[[Mittendorf's dot]]**: Benign incidental finding. Document and reassure the patient. No treatment required unless associated with visually significant posterior subcapsular opacity (exceedingly rare).
- **[[Bergmeister's papilla]]**: Benign incidental finding. Document and monitor. No treatment required.
- **Hyaloid remnants in premature infants**: Typically regress spontaneously. Serial observation is recommended with documentation of regression.

### Medical Management

There are no pharmacological treatments directed specifically at Cloquet's canal itself. However, medical management may be relevant in associated conditions:

- **[[Amblyopia]] therapy**: In cases where persistent hyaloid remnants or PFV cause unilateral visual deprivation, aggressive amblyopia management with [[occlusion therapy]] (patching) and/or [[atropine penalization]] of the fellow eye is critical, particularly in the first years of life during the critical period of visual development.
- **[[Intravitreal anti-VEGF]] therapy**: In rare cases of neovascularization associated with persistent hyaloid vasculature or PFV, [[anti-VEGF agents]] such as [[bevacizumab]] or [[ranibizumab]] have been used off-label, though evidence is limited to case reports and small series.
- **[[Topical corticosteroids]]**: May be used postoperatively following surgical intervention for PFV to manage inflammation.

### Surgical Management

Surgical intervention is reserved for clinically significant pathology related to persistent hyaloid vasculature or PFV:

#### Indications for Surgery

- Visually significant [[cataract]] associated with PFV
- Progressive [[retinal detachment]] associated with posterior PFV
- [[Vitreous hemorrhage]] from a patent persistent hyaloid artery
- Retrolental fibrovascular membrane causing pupillary block or [[angle-closure glaucoma]]
- Cosmetic improvement (leukocoria)

#### Surgical Techniques

| **Procedure** | **Indication** | **Description** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Lensectomy]]** | Cataract with PFV | Removal of the opacified lens, often combined with membranectomy |
| **[[Pars plana vitrectomy]]** (PPV) | Persistent hyaloid stalk; vitreous hemorrhage; retinal detachment | Core and peripheral vitrectomy with removal of hyaloid remnant; may include [[endolaser]] and tamponade |
| **[[Membranectomy]]** | Retrolental fibrovascular membrane | Excision of the persistent retrolental membrane |
| **[[Anterior vitrectomy]]** | Anterior PFV with lens involvement | Vitreous cutter used to remove anterior vitreous and hyaloid remnant |
| **Combined lensectomy/vitrectomy** | Combined anterior and posterior PFV | Comprehensive approach for severe cases |
| **[[Nd:YAG laser]] vitreolysis** | Isolated persistent hyaloid cord causing symptomatic floaters | Laser disruption of the remnant cord (limited evidence; used selectively) |

#### Surgical Considerations

- **Timing**: Early surgical intervention (within the first few months of life) is recommended for visually significant PFV to minimize [[amblyopia]] and optimize visual development.
- **Vascular risk**: A patent persistent hyaloid artery carries the risk of intraoperative hemorrhage. Preoperative [[fluorescein angiography]] or [[OCT angiography]] (OCTA) may help assess vessel patency.
- **Aphakic correction**: Following lensectomy in infants, optical rehabilitation with [[aphakic contact lenses]] or [[aphakic spectacles]] is necessary. Secondary [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) implantation may be considered at a later age.
- **Prognosis**: Visual outcomes in PFV are highly variable and depend on the severity of the condition, the presence of associated anomalies (retinal detachment, microphthalmos), and the timing of intervention. Anterior PFV generally carries a better visual prognosis than posterior or combined PFV.

## Clinical Pearls for the Optometrist

1. **Cloquet's canal is a normal anatomical structure** present in all eyes. Its identification on OCT or during clinical examination should not alarm the clinician or the patient.

2. **Mittendorf's dot** is the most common hyaloid remnant and is almost always an incidental, benign finding. It should be documented in the patient record and differentiated from a true [[posterior subcapsular cataract]].

3. **Bergmeister's papilla** should be differentiated from [[optic disc drusen]], [[papilledema]], and [[optic disc neovascularization]].

4. **In premature infants**, hyaloid remnants are common and usually regress. However, persistent or prominent remnants warrant close follow-up and possible referral to a pediatric ophthalmologist.

5. **Leukocoria in an infant** is an ophthalmic emergency until [[retinoblastoma]] is excluded. While PFV is a common cause of leukocoria, urgent referral for comprehensive evaluation including imaging is mandatory.

6. **Enhanced vitreous imaging** with modern SD-OCT and SS-OCT platforms has made Cloquet's canal more readily visible, and clinicians should be familiar with its normal OCT appearance to avoid misinterpretation.

7. **During [[posterior vitreous detachment]]**, the [[Weiss ring]] originates from the peripapillary glial tissue at the posterior opening of Cloquet's canal (area of Martegiani). This is a common cause of acute floaters in middle-aged and elderly patients.

8. **Cloquet's canal may serve as a conduit** for the migration of silicone oil droplets or other tamponade agents toward the retrolental space following [[vitrectomy]] with tamponade, which is a consideration in postoperative management.

---

## Summary

Cloquet's canal is a vestigial anatomical channel within the [[vitreous body]] representing the remnant of the embryonic [[hyaloid artery]]. While it is clinically insignificant in its normal state, its pathological persistence—ranging from benign remnants such as [[Mittendorf's dot]] and [[Bergmeister's papilla]] to the severe developmental anomaly of [[persistent fetal vasculature]]—makes it a structure of considerable importance in clinical optometry and ophthalmology. Modern imaging modalities, particularly [[optical coherence tomography]] and [[ultrasonography]], have greatly enhanced our ability to visualize and evaluate this structure. Management ranges from simple observation for benign remnants to complex vitreoretinal surgery for visually threatening conditions. A thorough understanding of the embryology, anatomy, and clinical significance of Cloquet's canal is essential for all eye care practitioners.
