

# Corneal Stroma

The **corneal stroma** (also referred to as the **substantia propria**) is the thickest layer of the [[cornea]], constituting approximately 90% of total corneal thickness (roughly 450–500 µm of the average 540 µm central corneal thickness). It is a highly organized, avascular, transparent connective tissue situated between the anterior [[Bowman's layer]] and the posterior [[Descemet's membrane]]. The unique ultrastructural arrangement of [[collagen]] fibrils, [[proteoglycans]], and [[keratocytes]] within the stroma is the principal determinant of corneal transparency and refractive power. Disruption of stromal architecture—whether by genetic, inflammatory, degenerative, infectious, traumatic, or iatrogenic mechanisms—underlies a vast spectrum of clinically significant corneal pathology encountered in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] practice.

---

## Anatomy and Ultrastructure

### Gross Organization

The corneal stroma is bounded anteriorly by [[Bowman's layer]] (a 8–12 µm acellular condensation of collagen) and posteriorly by the pre-Descemet's layer ([[Dua's layer]]) and [[Descemet's membrane]]. The stroma is composed of approximately 200–300 stacked **lamellae**, each 1.5–2.5 µm thick, running parallel to the corneal surface but at varying angles relative to adjacent lamellae. This orthogonal arrangement is critical for transparency.

### Collagen Fibrils

The predominant structural protein is **[[type I collagen]]**, which accounts for approximately 70–80% of stromal dry weight. Minor collagens include [[type V collagen]] (which regulates fibril diameter), [[type III collagen]] (found in wound healing and scar tissue), and [[type VI collagen]] (which forms microfibrillar networks connecting fibrils to the extracellular matrix). Stromal collagen fibrils have a remarkably uniform diameter of approximately **25–35 nm** with an equally uniform interfibrillar spacing of approximately **41.4 ± 0.5 nm**. This regularity is essential for destructive interference of scattered light, as described by the **Maurice lattice theory** of corneal transparency.

### Proteoglycans and Glycosaminoglycans

The interfibrillar matrix is rich in [[proteoglycans]], which regulate collagen fibril spacing and stromal hydration:

| Proteoglycan | Core Protein | GAG Chain | Primary Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Decorin]]** | ~40 kDa | [[Dermatan sulfate]] | Regulates fibril diameter and spacing |
| **[[Lumican]]** | ~38 kDa | [[Keratan sulfate]] | Maintains fibril uniformity and transparency |
| **[[Keratocan]]** | ~38 kDa | [[Keratan sulfate]] | Cornea-specific; maintains transparency |
| **[[Mimecan]]** (Osteoglycin) | ~25 kDa | [[Keratan sulfate]] | Fibril organization |

[[Keratan sulfate]] proteoglycans (KSPGs) are the dominant glycosaminoglycans in the mature corneal stroma and are critical for maintaining the precise hydration state (78% water content) necessary for transparency. Alterations in KSPG expression or sulfation are implicated in several [[corneal dystrophies]].

### Keratocytes

**[[Keratocytes]]** (corneal fibroblasts) are the resident cells of the stroma, comprising approximately 3–5% of stromal volume. They are quiescent, flattened, dendritic cells that form an interconnected syncytium via [[gap junctions]]. Keratocytes are responsible for:

- Synthesis and maintenance of collagen and proteoglycans
- Production of **[[corneal crystallins]]** (aldehyde dehydrogenase class 3 [ALDH3A1] and transketolase), which contribute to cellular transparency by minimizing light scattering
- Wound healing responses, during which they transform into activated **fibroblasts** and subsequently **[[myofibroblasts]]**

The anterior stroma has a higher keratocyte density (~20,000–25,000 cells/mm³) compared to the posterior stroma (~15,000 cells/mm³). Keratocyte density declines with age at a rate of approximately 0.45–0.7% per year.

### Nerve Supply

The corneal stroma receives sensory innervation from the **[[ophthalmic division]]** (V₁) of the [[trigeminal nerve]] via the [[long ciliary nerves]]. Stromal nerve bundles enter the cornea radially at the mid-stromal level in the peripheral cornea and course anteriorly, eventually penetrating [[Bowman's layer]] to form the **sub-basal nerve plexus**. Stromal nerves are predominantly unmyelinated within the cornea (they lose their [[myelin sheath]] approximately 1 mm after entering the corneal periphery). These nerves play a critical role in maintaining corneal epithelial health, tear film homeostasis, and the blink reflex.

### Anterior vs. Posterior Stroma

The stroma is not homogeneous. Important regional differences exist:

| Feature | Anterior Stroma | Posterior Stroma |
|---|---|---|
| **Lamellar arrangement** | Interwoven, oblique, branching | Parallel, orthogonal |
| **Lamellar width** | Narrower (0.5–30 µm) | Wider (100–200 µm) |
| **Keratocyte density** | Higher | Lower |
| **Swelling behavior** | Resists swelling | Swells preferentially |
| **Mechanical strength** | Greater cohesive tensile strength | Lower cohesive tensile strength |
| **Refractive surgery relevance** | Maintains corneal shape post-LASIK | More susceptible to ectasia if excessively ablated |

This anterior-posterior gradient has profound implications for [[refractive surgery]] planning, [[corneal cross-linking]], and understanding ectatic disease.

## Physiology

### Corneal Transparency

Stromal transparency depends on several interdependent factors:

1. **Regular collagen fibril spacing**: The uniform arrangement produces destructive interference of scattered light (Maurice's lattice theory, 1957; refined by Goldman and Benedek's short-range order theory, 1967).
2. **Stromal hydration**: Normal hydration is approximately 78% (3.5 mg H₂O/mg dry weight). The stroma has an inherent tendency to imbibe water (swelling pressure ~55 mmHg) due to the negatively charged [[glycosaminoglycans]]. This is counterbalanced by the **endothelial pump-leak mechanism** and the relative barrier function of the [[corneal epithelium]].
3. **Keratocyte crystallins**: ALDH3A1 and transketolase minimize intracellular light scattering.
4. **Avascularity**: The absence of blood vessels eliminates a major source of light scatter. Corneal [[angiogenic privilege]] is maintained by the balance of anti-angiogenic factors (e.g., [[soluble VEGF receptor-1]], [[thrombospondin-1]], [[angiostatin]]) over pro-angiogenic factors.

### Stromal Hydration Control

The **[[corneal endothelium]]** is the primary regulator of stromal hydration through active transport of ions (primarily via Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase and bicarbonate-dependent mechanisms), creating an osmotic gradient that draws water from the stroma into the [[aqueous humor]]. The [[corneal epithelium]] provides a secondary barrier. Loss of endothelial cell density below a critical threshold (~400–700 cells/mm²) results in stromal edema and loss of transparency.

### Biomechanical Properties

The stroma provides the cornea's structural integrity and biomechanical strength. Key parameters include:

- **Young's modulus**: Approximately 0.2–0.9 MPa (varies with depth, hydration, and age)
- **Corneal hysteresis (CH)**: Measured by the [[Ocular Response Analyzer]] (ORA), reflects viscoelastic damping
- **Corneal resistance factor (CRF)**: Reflects overall corneal rigidity

The anterior one-third of the stroma contributes disproportionately to corneal rigidity due to its interwoven lamellar architecture. This has critical implications for residual stromal bed calculations in [[LASIK]] and other keratorefractive procedures.

## Pathophysiology

Stromal pathology can be broadly categorized into the following mechanisms:

### 1. Corneal Dystrophies Affecting the Stroma

[[Corneal dystrophies]] are inherited, bilateral, typically symmetric, progressive conditions without systemic association (by the original definition, though some exceptions exist). The **IC3D classification** categorizes stromal dystrophies as follows:

| Dystrophy | Gene/Protein | Inheritance | Key Stromal Finding |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Macular corneal dystrophy]]** (MCD) | *CHST6* / Carbohydrate sulfotransferase 6 | [[Autosomal recessive]] | Abnormal [[keratan sulfate]] → diffuse stromal haze with focal opacities |
| **[[Granular corneal dystrophy]] type 1** (GCD1) | *TGFBI* / [[Transformelin]] | [[Autosomal dominant]] | Hyaline (eosinophilic) deposits; "breadcrumb" or "snowflake" opacities |
| **[[Granular corneal dystrophy]] type 2** (GCD2, Avellino) | *TGFBI* | Autosomal dominant | Combined hyaline and amyloid deposits |
| **[[Lattice corneal dystrophy]] type 1** (LCD1) | *TGFBI* | Autosomal dominant | [[Amyloid]] deposits; refractile branching lines |
| **[[Schnyder corneal dystrophy]]** (SCD) | *UBIAD1* | Autosomal dominant | Cholesterol and phospholipid crystalline deposits |
| **[[Congenital stromal corneal dystrophy]]** (CSCD) | *DCN* (Decorin) | Autosomal dominant | Diffuse bilateral corneal clouding from birth; flaky lamellar disruption |
| **[[Fleck corneal dystrophy]]** | *PIKFYVE* | Autosomal dominant | Small white fleck-like opacities throughout stroma; usually asymptomatic |
| **[[Pre-Descemet corneal dystrophy]]** | Unknown | Variable | Fine opacities in deep posterior stroma |

#### Pathophysiology of TGFBI-Related Dystrophies

The *TGFBI* gene (chromosome 5q31) encodes **transformelin** (also called keratoepithelin or βig-h3), an extracellular matrix protein. Specific point mutations produce distinct phenotypes:

- **R124C** → Lattice corneal dystrophy type 1 (amyloid)
- **R555W** → Granular corneal dystrophy type 1 (hyaline)
- **R124H** → Granular corneal dystrophy type 2/Avellino (mixed)
- **R124L** → Lattice corneal dystrophy type IIIA

These mutations cause misfolding and abnormal aggregation of the protein within the stroma, leading to progressive opacification.

### 2. Corneal Ectasia

**[[Keratoconus]]** is the most common corneal ectasia, characterized by progressive stromal thinning, weakening, and conical protrusion. Pathophysiological mechanisms include:

- Decreased total collagen content and altered collagen cross-linking
- Increased levels of [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMP-2, MMP-9) and decreased tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases ([[TIMPs]])
- Keratocyte [[apoptosis]] (particularly in the anterior stroma)
- Disruption and loss of lamellar interweaving
- Oxidative stress and reduced antioxidant defenses (decreased ALDH3A1, [[superoxide dismutase]])
- Possible role of inflammatory mediators (IL-6, TNF-α) — challenging the traditional view that keratoconus is purely non-inflammatory

Other ectatic conditions involving the stroma include **[[pellucid marginal degeneration]]**, **[[keratoglobus]]**, and **post-refractive surgery ectasia** (iatrogenic).

### 3. Infectious Keratitis

Microbial invasion of the stroma constitutes a sight-threatening emergency. Organisms and their stromal effects include:

| Organism | Stromal Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **[[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]]** | Rapid stromal lysis ("melting"); ring infiltrate; mucopurulent discharge |
| **[[Staphylococcus aureus]]** | Well-defined stromal abscess; may produce immune ring |
| **[[Acanthamoeba]]** | Perineural infiltrates (radial keratoneuritis); ring infiltrate; severe pain |
| **[[Fusarium]]** / [[Aspergillus]] | Feathery margins; satellite lesions; endothelial plaque; slow progression |
| **[[Herpes simplex virus]]** (stromal keratitis) | Immune-mediated stromal inflammation; neovascularization; lipid keratopathy |
| **[[Mycobacteria]]** | Indolent, non-healing infiltrate; "cracked windshield" appearance |

**[[Herpes simplex stromal keratitis]]** (HSK) deserves special emphasis. It can manifest as:
- **Immune stromal keratitis (ISK)**: Disc-shaped or diffuse stromal edema/haze without ulceration; mediated by T-cell response to viral antigens
- **Necrotizing stromal keratitis**: Dense infiltration with ulceration and possible perforation; involves active viral replication and immune destruction

### 4. Stromal Wound Healing

Following injury (trauma, surgery, infection), the stromal wound healing cascade involves:

1. **Keratocyte apoptosis** in the region adjacent to the wound (mediated by IL-1, Fas-FasL, TNF-α released from damaged epithelium)
2. **Keratocyte activation** → transformation to **fibroblasts** (stimulated by [[TGF-β]], [[PDGF]], [[FGF]])
3. **Myofibroblast transformation** (driven primarily by [[TGF-β1]] and TGF-β2) → expression of [[α-smooth muscle actin]] (α-SMA), loss of corneal crystallins, production of disorganized [[type III collagen]] and abnormal extracellular matrix
4. **Scar formation**: Irregular collagen fibril diameter and spacing → loss of transparency → clinically visible **corneal haze** or **scar**

This cascade is directly relevant to post-[[PRK]] haze, post-infectious scarring, and traumatic corneal opacification. Mitomycin C (MMC) applied intraoperatively during PRK inhibits myofibroblast formation and reduces haze.

### 5. Stromal Edema

Stromal edema results from disruption of the hydration control mechanisms:

- **Endothelial dysfunction**: [[Fuchs' endothelial corneal dystrophy]], post-surgical endothelial cell loss ([[pseudophakic bullous keratopathy]]), [[iridocorneal endothelial syndrome]]
- **Epithelial disruption**: Allows aqueous tear fluid to enter the stroma
- **Elevated [[intraocular pressure]]**: Overwhelms endothelial pump capacity
- **Inflammation**: Breakdown of endothelial barrier function

Edematous stroma loses transparency because the increased water content disrupts the regular collagen fibril spacing, creating "lakes" of fluid that scatter light.

### 6. Corneal Neovascularization

The normally avascular stroma can develop **[[corneal neovascularization]]** when the balance between pro-angiogenic and anti-angiogenic factors is disrupted. Causes include:

- Chronic [[contact lens]] wear (especially extended-wear soft lenses) → hypoxia
- [[Herpes simplex keratitis]]
- Chemical/thermal burns
- Chronic inflammation (e.g., [[atopic keratoconjunctivitis]], [[Stevens-Johnson syndrome]])
- Limbal stem cell deficiency

Neovascularization involves ingrowth of vessels from the [[limbal]] vascular arcades into the stroma, accompanied by lipid exudation, inflammatory cell infiltration, and scarring.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Stromal pathology presents with a range of symptoms depending on the underlying etiology, location, and severity:

| Symptom | Mechanism | Common Associations |
|---|---|---|
| **Decreased visual acuity** | Stromal opacification, edema, irregular astigmatism | Dystrophies, scars, keratoconus, edema |
| **Glare and halos** | Light scatter from stromal irregularity | Edema, scars, dystrophies |
| **Pain and photophobia** | Stromal inflammation, nerve involvement | Infectious keratitis, HSV stromal keratitis |
| **Tearing (epiphora)** | Reflex tearing from corneal irritation | Infectious/inflammatory stromal disease |
| **Foreign body sensation** | Epithelial irregularity overlying stromal pathology | Recurrent erosions over dystrophic deposits |
| **Monocular diplopia/polyopia** | Irregular stromal surface | Keratoconus, advanced dystrophies |
| **Progressive myopia and astigmatism** | Stromal thinning and ectasia | Keratoconus, pellucid marginal degeneration |
| **Asymptomatic** | Central clarity preserved; deposits peripheral | Fleck dystrophy, early Schnyder dystrophy |

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

The **[[slit-lamp]]** remains the cornerstone of stromal assessment. Key techniques include:

- **Direct illumination** (diffuse, focal, slit beam): Identifies location, depth, and character of stromal opacities, infiltrates, edema, and neovascularization
- **Retroillumination**: Highlights subtle stromal opacities, edema, and endothelial abnormalities against the red reflex
- **Sclerotic scatter**: Detects faint stromal haze and edema
- **Specular reflection**: Assesses endothelial mosaic (indirectly relevant to stromal hydration)

### Corneal Topography and Tomography

| Modality | Technology | Stromal Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Placido disc topography]]** | Reflected ring analysis | Detects surface irregularity from stromal ectasia |
| **[[Scheimpflug imaging]]** ([[Pentacam]], [[Galilei]]) | Rotating camera; cross-sectional imaging | Pachymetry maps, anterior/posterior elevation, densitometry |
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]]** (AS-OCT) | Interferometry | High-resolution cross-sectional stromal imaging; epithelial thickness mapping |

**Corneal densitometry** (available on Pentacam) quantifies stromal light backscatter and is useful for monitoring haze, dystrophic deposits, and treatment response.

### Corneal Pachymetry

Measurement of corneal (and thus predominantly stromal) thickness is essential for:

- Detecting stromal thinning (keratoconus, pellucid marginal degeneration)
- Monitoring stromal edema
- Pre-operative refractive surgery screening (minimum residual stromal bed of 250–300 µm recommended post-LASIK)
- Adjusting [[intraocular pressure]] measurements (thinner corneas may underestimate true IOP)

Methods include [[ultrasound pachymetry]], Scheimpflug-based pachymetry, and AS-OCT.

### In Vivo Confocal Microscopy (IVCM)

**[[In vivo confocal microscopy]]** (e.g., Heidelberg Retina Tomograph with Rostock Cornea Module) provides cellular-level resolution of the stroma:

- Keratocyte density and morphology
- Identification of dystrophic deposits (e.g., hyaline, amyloid, lipid)
- Detection of infectious organisms (Acanthamoeba cysts, fungal hyphae)
- Assessment of nerve morphology (sub-basal nerve plexus density, tortuosity)
- Monitoring post-surgical healing and haze

### Corneal Biomechanical Assessment

- **[[Ocular Response Analyzer]]** (ORA): Measures corneal hysteresis (CH) and corneal resistance factor (CRF)
- **[[Corvis ST]]** (Scheimpflug-based dynamic tonometry): Provides deformation amplitude, stiffness parameters (SP-A1), and the Tomographic Biomechanical Index (TBI) when combined with Pentacam data

These are particularly valuable in detecting early keratoconus and assessing ectasia risk before refractive surgery.

### Histopathology and Special Stains

When tissue is available (e.g., from [[penetrating keratoplasty]] or [[deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]]):

| Stain | Target |
|---|---|
| **Masson trichrome** | Collagen (blue/green) |
| **Congo red** (with polarized light) | Amyloid (apple-green birefringence) — lattice dystrophy |
| **Alcian blue** | Glycosaminoglycans — macular dystrophy |
| **Oil red O** (frozen sections) | Lipid — Schnyder dystrophy |
| **Periodic acid-Schiff (PAS)** | Basement membranes, fungi |
| **Gram stain / Giemsa** | Bacteria, Acanthamoeba |

### Genetic Testing

For suspected corneal dystrophies, **genetic testing** (e.g., *TGFBI* mutation analysis, *CHST6* sequencing) provides definitive diagnosis and is increasingly important for:

- Pre-operative screening before refractive surgery (TGFBI dystrophies can be exacerbated by LASIK)
- Genetic counseling
- Distinguishing phenotypically similar dystrophies

## Management and Treatment

### Pharmacological Interventions

#### Infectious Stromal Keratitis

| Pathogen | First-Line Treatment | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Bacterial** (empiric) | Fortified [[tobramycin]] (14 mg/mL) + fortified [[cefazolin]] (50 mg/mL) or fluoroquinolone monotherapy ([[moxifloxacin]] 0.5%) | Culture-guided adjustment; hourly initially |
| **Fungal (filamentary)** | [[Natamycin]] 5% suspension | Voriconazole 1% topical as adjunct or alternative |
| **Fungal (yeast)** | [[Amphotericin B]] 0.15–0.25% | Fluconazole topical/systemic |
| **Acanthamoeba** | [[Polyhexamethylene biguanide]] (PHMB) 0.02% + [[propamidine]] 0.1% (Brolene) | Prolonged treatment (months); chlorhexidine 0.02% as alternative to PHMB |
| **HSV stromal keratitis** | Oral [[acyclovir]] 400 mg 5×/day or [[valacyclovir]] 500 mg TID + topical [[prednisolone]] 1% (tapered slowly) | HEDS trial protocol; antiviral must accompany steroid |

#### Anti-Inflammatory and Immunomodulatory Therapy

- **Topical [[corticosteroids]]**: Prednisolone acetate 1%, dexamethasone 0.1% — used for immune stromal keratitis, graft rejection, and post-operative inflammation. Must be used cautiously due to risks of IOP elevation, cataract, and potentiation of infection.
- **Topical [[cyclosporine A]]** (0.05–2%): Steroid-sparing agent for chronic stromal inflammation and graft rejection prophylaxis.
- **Topical [[tacrolimus]]** (0.03–0.1%): Alternative immunomodulator.
- **[[Mitomycin C]]** (MMC, 0.02%): Applied intraoperatively during PRK to prevent stromal haze by inhibiting myofibroblast transformation.

#### Anti-Angiogenic Therapy

- **Topical/subconjunctival [[bevacizumab]]** (anti-VEGF): Reduces corneal neovascularization
- **Topical [[ranibizumab]]**
- These agents are often used as adjuncts before or after corneal transplantation in vascularized corneas

#### Collagenase Inhibitors

In cases of progressive stromal melting (e.g., sterile ulceration, alkali burns):

- **Topical [[medroxyprogesterone]]** 1%
- **Oral [[doxycycline]]** 100 mg BID (MMP inhibitor)
- **Topical [[N-acetylcysteine]]** 10–20%
- **Tissue adhesives** ([[cyanoacrylate]]) for impending or micro-perforations

### Corneal Cross-Linking (CXL)

**[[Corneal cross-linking]]** (CXL) is the standard treatment for progressive [[keratoconus]] and post-refractive surgery ectasia. The procedure strengthens the stroma by creating covalent bonds between collagen fibrils:

- **Standard (Dresden) protocol**: Epithelium removal → [[riboflavin]] 0.1% (with dextran) application for 30 minutes → [[UVA]] irradiation (370 nm, 3 mW/cm²) for 30 minutes (total energy 5.4 J/cm²)
- **Accelerated CXL**: Higher irradiance (9–30 mW/cm²) for shorter duration (maintaining equivalent total energy)
- **Epi-on (transepithelial) CXL**: Epithelium intact; enhanced riboflavin penetration strategies (e.g., iontophoresis, chemical enhancers)

CXL increases stromal stiffness by approximately 300% in the anterior 200–300 µm. Minimum corneal thickness of 400 µm (after epithelial removal) is required to protect the [[corneal endothelium]] from UVA damage.

### Surgical Approaches

#### Phototherapeutic Keratectomy (PTK)

**[[Phototherapeutic keratectomy]]** uses the [[excimer laser]] (193 nm) to ablate superficial stromal opacities and dystrophic deposits. Indications include:

- Recurrent TGFBI-related dystrophies (granular, lattice)
- Anterior stromal scars
- Band keratopathy (with EDTA chelation)
- Salzmann nodular degeneration

Typical ablation depth: 50–100 µm. Masking agents (e.g., methylcellulose) can be used to smooth irregular surfaces.

#### Keratoplasty

| Procedure | Description | Stromal Indications |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Penetrating keratoplasty]]** (PK) | Full-thickness corneal replacement | Advanced stromal dystrophies, deep scars, perforations |
| **[[Deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]]** (DALK) | Selective replacement of stroma while preserving host [[Descemet's membrane]] and [[endothelium]] | Keratoconus, stromal dystrophies, anterior/mid-stromal scars |
| **[[Anterior lamellar keratoplasty]]** (ALK) | Partial-thickness stromal replacement | Superficial stromal opacities |

**DALK** (particularly the **big-bubble technique** described by Anwar and Teichmann) has become the preferred procedure for stromal pathology with healthy endothelium, as it eliminates endothelial rejection and preserves long-term endothelial cell density.

#### Intrastromal Corneal Ring Segments (ICRS)

**[[Intrastromal corneal ring segments]]** (e.g., [[Intacs]], [[Ferrara rings]], [[Kerarings]]) are PMMA arc segments implanted into the mid-peripheral stroma (typically at 70–80% depth) to flatten the central cornea in keratoconus. They work by an **arc-shortening effect** that redistributes stromal tissue. They can be combined with CXL ("CXL-plus" protocols).

#### Femtosecond Laser-Assisted Procedures

The **[[femtosecond laser]]** enables precise intrastromal dissection and is used for:

- LASIK flap creation (stromal interface)
- ICRS channel creation
- DALK lamellar dissection
- [[Small incision lenticule extraction]] (SMILE/SMILE Pro) — removal of an intrastromal lenticule for myopic correction
- Corneal pocket creation for drug delivery or intrastromal implants

### Emerging and Investigational Therapies

#### Stromal Cell Therapy

Injection of cultured human corneal stromal stem cells (CSSCs) or mesenchymal stem cells into the stroma is under investigation for:

- Reducing corneal scarring
- Restoring transparency
- Treating stromal dystrophies

Early clinical trials have shown promising results in reducing corneal haze and improving transparency.

#### Bioengineered Stromal Substitutes

- **Recombinant human collagen-based implants** (e.g., biosynthetic corneas)
- **Decellularized stromal lenticules** (from SMILE procedures) repurposed as lamellar grafts
- **3D bioprinted stromal constructs** using collagen bioinks seeded with keratocytes

#### Gene Therapy

For monogenic stromal dystrophies (e.g., *TGFBI* mutations), **CRISPR-Cas9**-based gene editing and antisense oligonucleotide approaches are in preclinical development to correct or silence pathogenic mutations.

#### Intrastromal Drug Delivery

Femtosecond laser-created stromal pockets can serve as reservoirs for sustained drug delivery (e.g., antifungal agents, anti-inflammatory drugs, riboflavin for CXL).

## Prognosis and Clinical Considerations

The prognosis for stromal pathology varies widely:

- **Corneal dystrophies**: Slowly progressive; may require repeated interventions (PTK, keratoplasty); recurrence in grafts is common for TGFBI dystrophies
- **Keratoconus**: CXL has transformed prognosis; most patients stabilize with timely treatment; DALK outcomes are excellent
- **Infectious keratitis**: Prognosis depends on organism, time to treatment, and location; central stromal scars may require keratoplasty
- **Post-surgical haze**: Usually self-limited; MMC prophylaxis effective in PRK

**Key clinical pearls for the optometrist:**

1. Always assess stromal depth of pathology — this determines surgical approach (PTK vs. ALK vs. DALK vs. PK)
2. Screen for *TGFBI* mutations before refractive surgery in patients with any suspicious corneal deposits
3. Monitor keratoconus patients with serial topography/tomography; refer for CXL at first sign of progression
4. In HSV stromal keratitis, never use topical steroids without antiviral cover
5. Corneal thickness measurements must account for stromal edema when interpreting [[IOP]] readings

---

## Summary

The corneal stroma is a remarkably specialized tissue whose transparency, biomechanical integrity, and avascularity are essential for vision. Its unique ultrastructure—uniform collagen fibrils, precisely regulated hydration, quiescent keratocytes, and organized lamellar architecture—makes it both optically elegant and clinically vulnerable. A thorough understanding of stromal anatomy, physiology, and pathophysiology is fundamental to the diagnosis and management of a wide range of corneal conditions encountered in clinical optometry and ophthalmology. Advances in cross-linking, lamellar surgery, confocal imaging, biomechanical assessment, and regenerative medicine continue to expand the therapeutic armamentarium for stromal disease.
