

# Corneal Stroma

The **corneal stroma** (also referred to as the **substantia propria**) is the thickest layer of the [[cornea]], constituting approximately 90% of total corneal thickness (roughly 450–500 µm of the average 540 µm central corneal thickness). It is a highly organized, avascular connective tissue situated between the anterior [[Bowman's layer]] and the posterior [[Descemet's membrane]]. The unique ultrastructural arrangement of [[collagen]] fibrils, [[proteoglycans]], and [[keratocytes]] within the stroma is the principal determinant of corneal transparency and refractive power. Disruption of stromal architecture—whether through genetic, inflammatory, degenerative, infectious, traumatic, or iatrogenic mechanisms—underlies a vast spectrum of clinically significant corneal pathology encountered in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] practice.

---

## Anatomy and Ultrastructure

### Gross Organization

The corneal stroma is bounded anteriorly by [[Bowman's layer]] (a 8–12 µm acellular condensation of collagen) and posteriorly by the pre-Descemet's layer ([[Dua's layer]]) and [[Descemet's membrane]]. It is composed of approximately 200–300 stacked **lamellae**, each 1.5–2.5 µm thick, that run parallel to the corneal surface but at varying angles relative to one another. This orthogonal arrangement of successive lamellae is critical for the destructive interference of scattered light, which is the physical basis of [[corneal transparency]].

### Collagen Fibrils

The predominant structural protein is **[[type I collagen]]**, which accounts for approximately 70–80% of stromal dry weight. Smaller quantities of **[[type V collagen]]** are co-distributed with type I and play a regulatory role in fibril diameter. Each collagen fibril has a remarkably uniform diameter of approximately **25–35 nm** with an equally uniform interfibrillar spacing of approximately **41.4 nm (±0.5 nm)**. This regularity is essential: Maurice's lattice theory (1957) demonstrated that when fibril diameter and spacing are much smaller than the wavelength of visible light, destructive interference of scattered light occurs in all directions except the forward direction, thereby maintaining transparency.

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Stromal thickness (central) | ~450–500 µm |
| Percentage of total corneal thickness | ~90% |
| Number of lamellae | 200–300 |
| Predominant collagen type | [[Type I collagen]] |
| Collagen fibril diameter | 25–35 nm |
| Interfibrillar spacing | ~41.4 nm |
| Water content | ~78% |
| Refractive index | ~1.376 |

### Extracellular Matrix and Proteoglycans

The interfibrillar matrix is rich in **[[glycosaminoglycans]]** (GAGs) covalently linked to core proteins to form **[[proteoglycans]]**. The two principal stromal proteoglycans are:

- **[[Keratan sulfate]] proteoglycans (KSPGs):** Including **[[lumican]]**, **[[keratocan]]**, and **[[mimecan]]** (osteoglycin). These are the most abundant proteoglycans in the stroma and are critical regulators of collagen fibril spacing and hydration.
- **[[Chondroitin sulfate]]/[[dermatan sulfate]] proteoglycans (CS/DSPGs):** Including **[[decorin]]** and **[[biglycan]]**. These contribute to lamellar adhesion and biomechanical integrity.

Proteoglycans maintain the precise interfibrillar spacing by their hydrophilic GAG side chains, which attract water and generate a swelling pressure counterbalanced by the [[corneal endothelium]]'s active pump mechanism. This delicate balance maintains stromal hydration at approximately **78% water content**—the level required for transparency.

### Keratocytes

**[[Keratocytes]]** (corneal fibroblasts) are the resident cells of the stroma, comprising only 2–3% of stromal volume but forming an interconnected syncytium via **[[gap junctions]]** (primarily [[connexin 43]]). They are quiescent, flattened, dendritic cells that reside between lamellae and are responsible for:

- Synthesis and maintenance of collagen and proteoglycans
- Production of **[[corneal crystallins]]** (primarily [[aldehyde dehydrogenase 3A1]] [ALDH3A1] and [[transketolase]]), which contribute to cellular transparency by matching the refractive index of the cytoplasm to the surrounding matrix
- Wound healing responses when activated to **[[fibroblasts]]** or **[[myofibroblasts]]**

### Anterior vs. Posterior Stroma

The stroma is not homogeneous. Important regional differences exist:

| Feature | Anterior Stroma | Posterior Stroma |
|---|---|---|
| Lamellar arrangement | Interwoven, oblique, branching | Parallel, orthogonal |
| Lamellar width | Narrower (0.5–30 µm) | Wider (100–200 µm) |
| Keratocyte density | Higher | Lower |
| Swelling tendency | Less (more compact) | Greater |
| Biomechanical strength | Greater (cohesive strength) | Lesser |
| Refractive index gradient | Slightly higher | Slightly lower |

This anterior-posterior gradient has significant implications for [[refractive surgery]] (e.g., [[LASIK]], [[PRK]], [[SMILE]]), corneal ectasia risk, and wound healing.

### Corneal Nerves in the Stroma

Branches of the **[[long ciliary nerves]]** (derived from the [[ophthalmic division]] of the [[trigeminal nerve]], CN V₁) enter the cornea radially at the mid-stromal level. These myelinated nerve bundles lose their [[myelin sheath]] within 1–2 mm of the [[limbus]] and traverse the stroma before penetrating [[Bowman's layer]] to form the **[[sub-basal nerve plexus]]**. Stromal nerve density is clinically relevant in conditions such as [[neurotrophic keratitis]] and following refractive surgery.

## Physiology

### Transparency

Corneal stromal transparency depends on several interrelated factors:

1. **Regular collagen fibril arrangement** — Uniform diameter and spacing produce destructive interference of scattered light (Maurice's lattice theory).
2. **Controlled hydration** — Maintained at ~78% by the active [[Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase]] pump of the [[corneal endothelium]] and the barrier function of both the [[corneal epithelium]] and endothelium.
3. **Avascularity** — The stroma is devoid of blood vessels under normal conditions. The [[corneal limbus]] provides nutritional support, and the stroma receives nutrients via diffusion from the [[aqueous humor]], [[tear film]], and limbal vasculature.
4. **Keratocyte crystallins** — Intracellular crystallin proteins minimize light scattering from the cellular component.
5. **Absence of pigment and cellular infiltrates** — Inflammatory cells, pigment deposition, or neovascularization disrupt transparency.

### Biomechanics

The stroma provides the cornea's structural integrity and biomechanical properties. **[[Corneal hysteresis]]** and **[[corneal resistance factor]]**, measured by the [[Ocular Response Analyzer]] (ORA) or [[Corvis ST]], are largely determined by stromal collagen cross-linking, lamellar interweaving, and hydration. The anterior one-third of the stroma contributes disproportionately to corneal rigidity due to its more interwoven lamellar architecture.

### Metabolism

The stroma is relatively metabolically quiescent. Keratocytes have low metabolic demands and derive energy primarily through **[[glycolysis]]** and the **[[hexose monophosphate shunt]]**. Glucose diffuses from the aqueous humor and limbal vessels. Oxygen reaches the stroma primarily from the [[tear film]] (open-eye) and the [[palpebral conjunctiva]] vasculature (closed-eye), with a smaller contribution from the limbal and aqueous routes.

## Pathophysiology

Pathological processes affecting the corneal stroma can be broadly categorized as follows:

### 1. Corneal Stromal Dystrophies

[[Corneal dystrophies]] affecting the stroma are bilateral, inherited, typically progressive conditions characterized by abnormal deposition of material within the stromal extracellular matrix. The **IC3D (International Committee for Classification of Corneal Dystrophies)** classification system categorizes them as follows:

| Dystrophy | Gene/Protein | Deposit | Inheritance | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Macular corneal dystrophy]]** (MCD) | *[[CHST6]]* / Carbohydrate sulfotransferase 6 | Abnormal [[keratan sulfate]] / GAGs | [[Autosomal recessive]] | Diffuse stromal haze, extends limbus-to-limbus; most severe stromal dystrophy |
| **[[Granular corneal dystrophy]] type 1** (GCD1) | *[[TGFBI]]* (R555W) / [[Keratoepithelin]] | Hyaline (eosinophilic) | [[Autosomal dominant]] | Discrete, breadcrumb-like central opacities; Masson trichrome–positive |
| **[[Granular corneal dystrophy]] type 2** (GCD2 / Avellino) | *[[TGFBI]]* (R124H) | Hyaline + [[amyloid]] | [[Autosomal dominant]] | Combined granular and lattice features |
| **[[Lattice corneal dystrophy]] type 1** (LCD1) | *[[TGFBI]]* (R124C) | [[Amyloid]] | [[Autosomal dominant]] | Refractile branching lines; Congo red–positive with apple-green birefringence |
| **[[Schnyder corneal dystrophy]]** (SCD) | *[[UBIAD1]]* | [[Cholesterol]] and phospholipids | [[Autosomal dominant]] | Central crystalline deposits (in ~50%), arcus lipoides, stromal haze |
| **[[Fleck corneal dystrophy]]** | *[[PIKFYVE]]* | GAGs and lipids in keratocytes | [[Autosomal dominant]] | Small, flat, white fleck-like opacities; usually non-progressive and asymptomatic |
| **[[Congenital stromal corneal dystrophy]]** (CSCD) | *[[DCN]]* / [[Decorin]] | Abnormal lamellar structure | [[Autosomal dominant]] | Diffuse, bilateral corneal clouding from birth |

### 2. Corneal Ectasias

**[[Keratoconus]]** is the most common corneal ectasia and is fundamentally a disorder of the corneal stroma. Pathophysiological features include:

- Progressive thinning and protrusion of the stroma, typically inferotemporal
- Loss of collagen lamellae and reduction in lamellar interweaving
- Decreased keratocyte density with increased [[apoptosis]]
- Degradation of proteoglycans (reduced keratan sulfate)
- Upregulation of [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs), particularly [[MMP-2]] and [[MMP-9]]
- Increased oxidative stress with decreased [[superoxide dismutase]] and [[aldehyde dehydrogenase]]
- Breaks in [[Bowman's layer]] with stromal scarring

Other ectatic conditions involving the stroma include **[[pellucid marginal degeneration]]**, **[[keratoglobus]]**, and **[[post-LASIK ectasia]]**.

### 3. Infectious Keratitis Involving the Stroma

Microbial invasion of the stroma constitutes a sight-threatening emergency:

- **[[Bacterial keratitis]]:** Organisms such as *[[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]]*, *[[Staphylococcus aureus]]*, and *[[Streptococcus pneumoniae]]* cause stromal infiltration, necrosis, and ulceration. Pseudomonas produces [[elastase]] and [[protease]] enzymes that rapidly liquefy stromal collagen ("corneal melting").
- **[[Fungal keratitis]]:** *[[Fusarium]]*, *[[Aspergillus]]*, and *[[Candida]]* species cause deep stromal infiltrates with feathery margins, satellite lesions, and endothelial plaques.
- **[[Acanthamoeba keratitis]]:** Causes a characteristic **ring-shaped stromal infiltrate** and severe [[radial keratoneuritis]].
- **[[Herpes simplex stromal keratitis]]:** An immune-mediated response to [[herpes simplex virus]] (HSV) antigens in the stroma, causing stromal necrosis or non-necrotizing immune stromal keratitis (ISK) with intact epithelium, stromal edema, and [[Descemet's folds]].

### 4. Immune-Mediated Stromal Disease

- **[[Interstitial keratitis]]** (IK): A non-ulcerative stromal inflammation classically associated with congenital [[syphilis]] (Hutchinson's triad) but also seen with [[tuberculosis]], [[Lyme disease]], [[Epstein-Barr virus]], and [[Cogan syndrome]]. Features include deep stromal vascularization ("ghost vessels"), stromal haze, and lipid keratopathy.
- **[[Corneal graft rejection]]:** Stromal rejection manifests as a focal or diffuse stromal infiltrate/haze in a [[corneal transplant]] recipient.
- **[[Mooren's ulcer]]** and **[[peripheral ulcerative keratitis]]** (PUK): Autoimmune-mediated stromal lysis at the corneal periphery, often associated with [[rheumatoid arthritis]], [[granulomatosis with polyangiitis]], or other systemic vasculitides.

### 5. Degenerative Conditions

- **[[Corneal arcus]]** (arcus senilis): Lipid deposition in the peripheral stroma.
- **[[Band keratopathy]]:** Calcium deposition in the superficial stroma and [[Bowman's layer]].
- **[[Salzmann nodular degeneration]]:** Subepithelial/anterior stromal hyaline deposits.
- **[[Spheroidal degeneration]]** (climatic droplet keratopathy): Proteinaceous deposits in the anterior stroma.

### 6. Traumatic and Iatrogenic Stromal Pathology

- **Corneal scarring:** Following trauma, infection, or surgery, activated keratocytes transform into [[myofibroblasts]] that produce disorganized collagen (types I, III, and V) and excessive extracellular matrix, resulting in opaque scar tissue.
- **Post-refractive surgery haze:** Particularly after [[PRK]], subepithelial fibrosis can occur due to myofibroblast activation.
- **[[Corneal ectasia]] post-LASIK:** Excessive stromal tissue removal or creation of a flap in a predisposed cornea leads to progressive ectasia.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Symptoms of stromal pathology vary depending on the underlying etiology but commonly include:

| Symptom | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Decreased visual acuity** | Stromal opacification, edema, irregular astigmatism, or scarring disrupting transparency |
| **Glare and halos** | Light scattering from stromal haze, deposits, or edema |
| **Photophobia** | Stromal inflammation stimulating corneal nerves |
| **Pain and foreign body sensation** | Epithelial breakdown secondary to stromal disease; nerve involvement |
| **Tearing (epiphora)** | Reflex tearing from corneal irritation |
| **Redness** | Limbal/conjunctival injection from associated inflammation or [[neovascularization]] |
| **Monocular diplopia or ghost images** | Irregular astigmatism from stromal thinning or scarring |

In many stromal dystrophies, patients may be **asymptomatic** in early stages, with opacities detected incidentally on [[slit-lamp examination]].

## Diagnostic Modalities

### 1. Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

The cornerstone of stromal assessment. Techniques include:

- **Direct illumination (broad beam and optical section):** Evaluates depth, density, and extent of stromal opacities, infiltrates, edema, and thinning.
- **Retroillumination:** Highlights subtle stromal opacities, deposits, and ghost vessels against the red reflex.
- **Sclerotic scatter:** Detects diffuse stromal haze.
- **Specular reflection:** Assesses endothelial integrity (indirectly relevant to stromal hydration).

### 2. Corneal Topography and Tomography

- **[[Placido disc topography]]** (e.g., [[Medmont]], [[Atlas]]): Maps anterior corneal curvature; detects irregular astigmatism from stromal disease.
- **[[Scheimpflug imaging]]** (e.g., [[Pentacam]], [[Galilei]]): Provides full pachymetry maps, anterior and posterior elevation maps, and densitometry. **Corneal densitometry** quantitatively measures stromal light backscatter (haze) and is invaluable for monitoring dystrophies, ectasia, and post-surgical changes.
- **[[Anterior segment OCT]]** (AS-OCT) (e.g., [[Optovue]], [[Zeiss Cirrus]], [[Heidelberg Anterion]]): High-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the stroma, enabling precise measurement of stromal thickness, depth of opacities, and assessment of lamellar interfaces post-surgery.

### 3. Corneal Pachymetry

- **[[Ultrasound pachymetry]]:** Gold standard for central corneal thickness measurement.
- **Optical pachymetry** (via Scheimpflug or OCT): Provides full-thickness mapping, essential for detecting focal thinning in ectasia.

### 4. In Vivo Confocal Microscopy (IVCM)

**[[Confocal microscopy]]** (e.g., [[Heidelberg Retina Tomograph]] with Rostock Cornea Module) provides cellular-level resolution of the stroma, enabling:

- Keratocyte density quantification
- Identification of abnormal deposits (e.g., amyloid, hyaline, lipid)
- Detection of fungal hyphae, *Acanthamoeba* cysts, and inflammatory cells
- Assessment of corneal nerve morphology (sub-basal nerve plexus density, tortuosity)

### 5. Corneal Biomechanical Assessment

- **[[Ocular Response Analyzer]]** (ORA): Measures [[corneal hysteresis]] (CH) and [[corneal resistance factor]] (CRF).
- **[[Corvis ST]]**: Dynamic Scheimpflug-based tonometry providing deformation amplitude, stiffness parameters, and the **Tomographic Biomechanical Index (TBI)** when combined with Pentacam data—highly sensitive for detecting subclinical ectasia.

### 6. Histopathology and Special Stains

When tissue is available (e.g., post-[[keratoplasty]]):

| Stain | Target |
|---|---|
| **Hematoxylin & Eosin (H&E)** | General architecture |
| **Masson trichrome** | Hyaline deposits (red in GCD) |
| **Congo red** | Amyloid (apple-green birefringence under polarized light in LCD) |
| **Alcian blue / Colloidal iron** | GAGs (positive in MCD) |
| **Oil Red O** | Lipid deposits (Schnyder dystrophy) |
| **Periodic acid-Schiff (PAS)** | Fungal elements, basement membranes |

### 7. Genetic Testing

Molecular genetic testing for *[[TGFBI]]*, *[[CHST6]]*, *[[UBIAD1]]*, and other genes is increasingly available and is particularly valuable for:

- Confirming dystrophy diagnosis
- Pre-operative screening (e.g., before [[refractive surgery]] to exclude GCD2/Avellino dystrophy, which is exacerbated by LASIK)
- Genetic counseling

## Management and Treatment

### Conservative / Medical Management

**1. Observation and Monitoring**

Many stromal dystrophies (e.g., [[fleck corneal dystrophy]]) and mild degenerations require only periodic monitoring with [[slit-lamp examination]], [[corneal topography]], and [[visual acuity]] assessment.

**2. Refractive Correction**

- **[[Spectacles]]:** For regular astigmatism and mild visual impairment.
- **[[Rigid gas-permeable (RGP) contact lenses]]:** Mainstay for visual rehabilitation in [[keratoconus]] and other ectasias by masking irregular astigmatism with a smooth refracting surface.
- **[[Scleral contact lenses]]:** Increasingly used for advanced ectasia, post-graft irregularity, and severe surface disease. They vault the cornea entirely, providing optical correction and a fluid reservoir.
- **[[Hybrid contact lenses]]** (e.g., [[SynergEyes]]): Combine RGP center with soft skirt for comfort and centration.
- **[[Piggyback lens systems]]:** RGP lens over a soft lens for comfort in intolerant patients.

**3. Pharmacological Interventions**

| Condition | Pharmacological Agent | Mechanism/Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **HSV stromal keratitis** | Topical [[prednisolone acetate 1%]] + oral [[acyclovir]] or [[valacyclovir]] | Corticosteroid suppresses immune-mediated stromal inflammation; antiviral prevents viral replication |
| **Bacterial keratitis** | Fortified [[tobramycin]] + fortified [[cefazolin]] (or fluoroquinolone monotherapy, e.g., [[moxifloxacin]]) | Broad-spectrum empiric coverage; adjust per culture/sensitivity |
| **Fungal keratitis** | Topical [[natamycin 5%]] (filamentous) or [[amphotericin B]] (yeast); oral/topical [[voriconazole]] | Natamycin is first-line for filamentous fungi per MUTT trial |
| **Acanthamoeba keratitis** | [[Polyhexamethylene biguanide]] (PHMB) 0.02% + [[propamidine isethionate]] 0.1% (Brolene) | Dual cysticidal therapy; prolonged treatment (months) |
| **Interstitial keratitis** | Topical [[corticosteroids]] + treatment of underlying systemic cause | Treat syphilis with [[penicillin G]]; TB with anti-tubercular therapy |
| **Post-PRK haze prophylaxis** | Topical [[mitomycin C]] 0.02% (intraoperative, 30–120 seconds) | Inhibits myofibroblast transformation; reduces subepithelial fibrosis |
| **Corneal melting** | Topical [[medroxyprogesterone]] 1%, oral [[doxycycline]] 100 mg BID, [[N-acetylcysteine]] 10–20% | Anti-collagenase properties; MMP inhibition |

**4. Hyperosmotic Agents**

Topical **[[sodium chloride 5%]]** (drops or ointment) can temporarily reduce stromal edema in conditions where endothelial dysfunction contributes to stromal swelling (e.g., early [[Fuchs endothelial dystrophy]] with stromal edema).

### Surgical Management

**1. [[Corneal Cross-Linking]] (CXL)**

The **Dresden protocol** (epithelium-off CXL) uses topical **[[riboflavin]]** (vitamin B2) and **[[ultraviolet-A (UVA)]]** light (370 nm, 3 mW/cm², 30 minutes) to induce covalent cross-links between collagen fibrils in the anterior stroma. This increases stromal biomechanical rigidity by 300–400% and halts progression of [[keratoconus]] and post-LASIK ectasia. Accelerated protocols (higher irradiance, shorter time) and transepithelial (epi-on) approaches are also in clinical use.

**Indications:**
- Progressive [[keratoconus]]
- Progressive [[pellucid marginal degeneration]]
- Post-[[LASIK]] ectasia
- Infectious keratitis (adjunctive "PACK-CXL")

**2. [[Phototherapeutic Keratectomy]] (PTK)**

Excimer laser ablation of the anterior stroma can remove superficial stromal opacities (e.g., granular dystrophy deposits, recurrent erosion-related scarring, Salzmann nodules). Typically limited to the anterior 100–150 µm. A masking agent (e.g., methylcellulose) may be used to smooth irregular surfaces.

**3. Keratoplasty (Corneal Transplantation)**

| Procedure | Description | Indications |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Penetrating keratoplasty]]** (PK) | Full-thickness corneal replacement | Advanced stromal dystrophies, deep scarring, keratoconus with hydrops scarring |
| **[[Deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]]** (DALK) | Selective replacement of stroma while preserving host [[Descemet's membrane]] and [[endothelium]] | Stromal dystrophies, keratoconus, anterior/mid-stromal scars. Eliminates endothelial rejection risk |
| **[[Anterior lamellar keratoplasty]]** (ALK) | Partial-thickness stromal replacement | Superficial stromal opacities |

**DALK** has become the preferred technique for isolated stromal disease because it:
- Eliminates the risk of endothelial graft rejection
- Preserves host endothelial cell count
- Has superior long-term graft survival compared to PK for stromal indications
- The **big-bubble technique** (Anwar) achieves a smooth interface at Descemet's membrane, optimizing visual outcomes

**4. Intrastromal Corneal Ring Segments (ICRS)**

Devices such as **[[Intacs]]** and **[[Ferrara rings]]** are implanted into the mid-peripheral stroma (typically at 70–80% depth) to flatten the central cornea and reduce irregular astigmatism in [[keratoconus]]. Femtosecond laser-assisted channel creation has improved precision and safety.

**5. Femtosecond Laser-Assisted Procedures**

- **[[SMILE]]** (Small Incision Lenticule Extraction): A refractive procedure that creates and extracts a stromal lenticule through a small incision, preserving the anterior stroma and Bowman's layer more than LASIK.
- **[[LASIK]]**: Creates a stromal flap (typically 90–120 µm) and ablates the exposed stromal bed. Residual stromal bed thickness of ≥250–300 µm is critical to minimize ectasia risk.

**6. Stromal Stem Cell and Tissue Engineering Approaches (Emerging)**

- **Corneal stromal stem cells** (CSSCs) derived from the [[limbal stroma]] can differentiate into keratocytes and produce organized collagen. Early clinical trials show promise for treating stromal scars.
- **Bioengineered stromal substitutes** using recombinant human collagen or decellularized porcine corneal stroma are under investigation.
- **[[Corneal stromal lenticule implantation]]:** Refractive lenticules extracted during SMILE procedures can be cryopreserved and implanted into recipient corneas for stromal augmentation in ectasia or hyperopia.

## Wound Healing in the Corneal Stroma

Understanding stromal wound healing is essential for managing post-surgical and post-traumatic outcomes:

1. **Keratocyte apoptosis:** Within hours of injury, keratocytes adjacent to the wound undergo [[apoptosis]], creating an acellular zone.
2. **Keratocyte activation and proliferation:** Surviving keratocytes transform into **repair fibroblasts** under the influence of [[TGF-β]], [[PDGF]], and [[IL-1]].
3. **Myofibroblast transformation:** Under sustained TGF-β stimulation (particularly [[TGF-β2]]), fibroblasts differentiate into **[[myofibroblasts]]** expressing **[[α-smooth muscle actin]]** (α-SMA). Myofibroblasts produce disorganized collagen and lose crystallin expression, causing **corneal haze and scarring**.
4. **Remodeling:** Over months to years, partial remodeling may occur, but complete restoration of normal lamellar architecture is rarely achieved.

**Clinical relevance:** Mitomycin C applied intraoperatively during PRK or PTK inhibits myofibroblast formation and reduces haze. Corticosteroids modulate the inflammatory component of wound healing.

## Corneal Stromal Neovascularization

Under normal conditions, the stroma maintains its avascularity through a balance of **anti-angiogenic factors** (e.g., **[[soluble VEGF receptor-1]]** [sFlt-1], **[[angiostatin]]**, **[[endostatin]]**, **[[thrombospondin-1]]**) and pro-angiogenic stimuli. Disruption of this balance—by chronic inflammation, hypoxia (e.g., [[contact lens]] overwear), infection, or chemical injury—leads to **[[corneal neovascularization]]**, with blood vessels invading the stroma from the limbal arcade.

**Treatment options for stromal neovascularization include:**
- Topical [[corticosteroids]]
- Subconjunctival/intrastromal **[[anti-VEGF]]** agents ([[bevacizumab]], [[ranibizumab]])
- **[[Fine-needle diathermy]]** or **[[argon laser photocoagulation]]** of feeder vessels
- Treatment of the underlying cause (e.g., refitting contact lenses, treating infection)

## Prognosis

The prognosis for stromal conditions varies widely:

- **Stromal dystrophies:** Slowly progressive; many patients maintain functional vision for decades with conservative management. Recurrence in grafts is possible (especially GCD and LCD in PK; less common in DALK).
- **Keratoconus:** CXL has transformed the prognosis by halting progression. Most patients achieve functional vision with contact lenses; ~10–20% ultimately require keratoplasty.
- **Infectious keratitis:** Prognosis depends on organism virulence, time to treatment, and depth of involvement. Deep stromal infections carry a higher risk of scarring and perforation.
- **Post-surgical outcomes:** DALK for stromal disease has excellent long-term graft survival (>90% at 5 years) with low rejection rates.

---

## Summary

The corneal stroma is a uniquely organized tissue whose transparency, biomechanical strength, and refractive properties are essential for vision. Its pathology encompasses a broad spectrum of dystrophic, ectatic, infectious, inflammatory, degenerative, and traumatic conditions. Advances in diagnostic imaging (AS-OCT, Scheimpflug tomography, confocal microscopy), corneal cross-linking, selective lamellar keratoplasty (DALK), and emerging regenerative therapies have significantly improved the clinical management and visual outcomes for patients with stromal disease. A thorough understanding of stromal anatomy, physiology, and pathophysiology is fundamental to evidence-based optometric and ophthalmic practice.
