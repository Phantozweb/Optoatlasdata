

# Anterior Ciliary Arteries

---

The **anterior ciliary arteries** (ACAs) are a critical group of blood vessels that constitute a major component of the [[anterior segment]] vascular supply of the [[eye]]. Arising from the [[muscular branches]] of the [[ophthalmic artery]], these arteries travel along the surface of the [[extraocular muscles]] to reach the [[globe]], where they penetrate the [[sclera]] near the [[limbus]] and contribute to the formation of the [[major arterial circle of the iris]] (MAC). The anterior ciliary arteries are of paramount clinical significance in [[ophthalmology]] and [[optometry]] because of their indispensable role in nourishing the [[anterior segment]], including the [[iris]], [[ciliary body]], [[conjunctiva]], [[episclera]], and [[corneal limbus]]. Disruption of these vessels—whether through surgical intervention, disease, or trauma—can lead to devastating consequences, most notably [[anterior segment ischemia]] (ASI). A thorough understanding of the anatomy, physiology, pathophysiology, and clinical relevance of the anterior ciliary arteries is essential for every eye care professional.

---

## Anatomy

### Origin and Course

The anterior ciliary arteries originate as branches of the [[muscular arteries]] (also called [[muscular branches of the ophthalmic artery]]), which themselves are derived from the [[ophthalmic artery]]—a branch of the [[internal carotid artery]]. Each [[rectus muscle]] of the [[extraocular muscles]] is supplied by its own muscular branch, and from these muscular branches, the anterior ciliary arteries emerge and travel anteriorly along the surface of the rectus muscles toward the [[globe]].

The classical anatomical description is as follows:

| Rectus Muscle | Number of Anterior Ciliary Arteries |
|---|---|
| [[Medial rectus muscle]] | 2 |
| [[Superior rectus muscle]] | 2 |
| [[Inferior rectus muscle]] | 2 |
| [[Lateral rectus muscle]] | 1 |

Thus, there are typically **seven** anterior ciliary arteries supplying each eye. The [[lateral rectus muscle]] is notable for carrying only **one** anterior ciliary artery, a fact of critical surgical importance. Anatomical variations do exist, and some individuals may have accessory or absent vessels, which can influence the risk profile during [[strabismus surgery]] or other procedures involving the rectus muscles.

### Penetration and Intrascleral Course

As the anterior ciliary arteries approach the [[limbus]], they course through the [[episcleral tissue]] and then penetrate the [[sclera]] approximately 3–5 mm posterior to the surgical limbus. Upon entering the sclera, they traverse the [[scleral stroma]] in an oblique fashion and enter the [[ciliary body]]. Within the ciliary body, the anterior ciliary arteries anastomose with the [[long posterior ciliary arteries]] (LPCAs)—two vessels (one nasal and one temporal) that also arise from the ophthalmic artery—to form the [[major arterial circle of the iris]] (also known as the circulus arteriosus iridis major).

### Major Arterial Circle of the Iris

The [[major arterial circle of the iris]] is an incomplete or complete arterial ring located in the [[ciliary body]] near the root of the [[iris]]. It is formed by the anastomosis of:

- The **anterior ciliary arteries** (7 vessels)
- The **[[long posterior ciliary arteries]]** (2 vessels: nasal and temporal)

From the major arterial circle, radial branches extend into the [[iris stroma]] to supply the iris tissue, and recurrent branches supply the [[ciliary body]] and the [[ciliary processes]], which are responsible for [[aqueous humor]] production. A secondary, more distal vascular ring—the [[minor arterial circle of the iris]]—is formed near the [[pupillary margin]] by anastomoses of these radial iris vessels.

### Superficial Branches

Before penetrating the sclera, the anterior ciliary arteries give off important superficial branches:

- **[[Anterior conjunctival arteries]]**: Supply the [[bulbar conjunctiva]] near the limbus.
- **[[Episcleral arteries]]**: Supply the [[episclera]] and contribute to the [[limbal vascular arcades]].
- **[[Limbal arcades]]**: These perilimbal vascular networks are critical for the nutrition of the peripheral [[cornea]], which is avascular in its central portion but receives nutrients from the limbal vasculature at its periphery.

These superficial branches are clinically visible on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] and are important landmarks during clinical examination and surgical procedures.

## Physiology

### Anterior Segment Blood Supply

The anterior ciliary arteries are the **dominant** arterial supply to the [[anterior segment]] of the eye. Together with the [[long posterior ciliary arteries]], they maintain perfusion of the following structures:

| Structure | Primary Vascular Supply |
|---|---|
| [[Iris]] | Major arterial circle (ACAs + LPCAs) |
| [[Ciliary body]] | Major arterial circle (ACAs + LPCAs) |
| [[Ciliary processes]] | Major arterial circle → recurrent ciliary branches |
| [[Conjunctiva]] (perilimbal) | Anterior conjunctival arteries (from ACAs) |
| [[Episclera]] | Episcleral arteries (from ACAs) |
| [[Peripheral cornea]] / [[Limbus]] | Limbal arcades (from ACAs) |
| [[Anterior sclera]] | Perforating branches of ACAs |

The anterior ciliary arteries contribute approximately **70%** of the blood supply to the anterior segment, while the long posterior ciliary arteries contribute the remaining **30%**. This ratio underscores the vulnerability of the anterior segment to ischemia when multiple anterior ciliary arteries are disrupted.

### Aqueous Humor Production

The [[ciliary processes]], which are responsible for the active secretion and ultrafiltration of [[aqueous humor]], are critically dependent on adequate blood flow from the major arterial circle. Reduction in anterior ciliary artery perfusion can impair aqueous humor production, leading to [[hypotony]] and its associated complications, including [[choroidal effusion]], [[maculopathy]], and [[phthisis bulbi]] in severe cases.

### Thermoregulation and Metabolic Support

The anterior ciliary arteries also play a role in thermoregulation of the anterior segment and in delivering oxygen and metabolic substrates to the metabolically active tissues of the [[ciliary body]] and [[iris]]. The [[iris sphincter muscle]] and [[iris dilator muscle]], both smooth muscles requiring continuous metabolic support, are dependent on this vascular supply for normal [[pupillary function]].

## Pathophysiology

### Anterior Segment Ischemia (ASI)

[[Anterior segment ischemia]] is the most clinically significant pathological consequence of anterior ciliary artery disruption. ASI occurs when the blood supply to the anterior segment is critically reduced, leading to ischemic damage to the [[iris]], [[ciliary body]], [[cornea]], and related structures.

#### Etiology of ASI Related to ACA Disruption

The most common causes of anterior ciliary artery compromise include:

1. **[[Strabismus surgery]]**: Disinsertion or transposition of rectus muscles during strabismus surgery necessarily disrupts the anterior ciliary arteries traveling along those muscles. Surgery on two or more rectus muscles in the same eye, particularly when the [[lateral rectus muscle]] (with its single ACA) is involved, significantly increases the risk of ASI.

2. **[[Scleral buckling surgery]]**: Encircling bands or segmental buckles used in [[retinal detachment]] repair can compress or occlude the anterior ciliary arteries as they penetrate the sclera.

3. **[[Carotid artery disease]]**: Severe [[carotid artery stenosis]] or [[carotid artery occlusion]] can reduce perfusion pressure in the [[ophthalmic artery]] and its branches, including the anterior ciliary arteries, leading to chronic or acute anterior segment ischemia (also termed [[ocular ischemic syndrome]]).

4. **[[Sickle cell disease]]**: [[Sickling]] of erythrocytes within the small-caliber anterior ciliary arteries and their branches can cause vaso-occlusion and anterior segment ischemia.

5. **[[Giant cell arteritis]]** and other forms of [[vasculitis]]: Inflammatory vasculopathies can affect the anterior ciliary arteries, leading to ischemic damage.

6. **[[Radiation therapy]]**: Orbital or periorbital radiation can cause [[radiation vasculopathy]] affecting the anterior ciliary arteries.

7. **Trauma**: Blunt or penetrating [[ocular trauma]] can directly damage the anterior ciliary arteries.

#### Pathological Cascade in ASI

When anterior ciliary artery flow is critically reduced, the following pathological cascade ensues:

1. **Ischemia of the iris and ciliary body** → cellular hypoxia → release of [[inflammatory mediators]] and [[vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF)
2. **Breakdown of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]]** → [[anterior chamber]] flare and cells (sterile [[anterior uveitis]])
3. **Iris stromal edema and necrosis** → [[iris atrophy]], [[corectopia]], and [[pupillary abnormalities]]
4. **Ciliary body dysfunction** → decreased [[aqueous humor]] production → [[hypotony]]
5. **[[Neovascularization]] of the iris** ([[rubeosis iridis]]) → [[neovascular glaucoma]] in severe or chronic cases
6. **Corneal edema** → [[corneal decompensation]] due to limbal ischemia and [[endothelial cell]] dysfunction
7. **[[Cataract]] formation** → ischemic damage to the [[lens epithelium]]
8. **In severe cases**: [[Phthisis bulbi]] (end-stage shrunken, non-functional eye)

#### Risk Factors for ASI

| Risk Factor | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Surgery on ≥2 rectus muscles simultaneously | Disruption of multiple ACAs |
| Pre-existing [[carotid artery disease]] | Reduced baseline perfusion |
| [[Diabetes mellitus]] | [[Microvascular disease]] affecting small vessels |
| [[Sickle cell disease]] / [[sickle cell trait]] | Vaso-occlusion in small arteries |
| Advanced age | Atherosclerotic changes in feeder vessels |
| Previous strabismus surgery on the same eye | Cumulative ACA loss |
| [[Thyroid eye disease]] | Fibrotic/inflammatory changes in rectus muscles |
| [[Systemic vasculitis]] | Inflammatory vessel compromise |

## Clinical Presentation (Symptoms and Signs)

### Symptoms

Patients with anterior ciliary artery compromise—most commonly presenting as [[anterior segment ischemia]]—may report:

- **Pain**: Dull, aching periorbital or ocular pain due to ischemic inflammation
- **Blurred vision**: From corneal edema, anterior chamber inflammation, or cataract formation
- **Photophobia**: Due to [[anterior uveitis]] and iris inflammation
- **Redness**: From conjunctival and episcleral vascular congestion or injection
- **Decreased vision**: Progressive, particularly if [[neovascular glaucoma]] or corneal decompensation develops

In mild or subclinical cases, patients may be asymptomatic, with findings detected only on [[slit-lamp examination]].

### Clinical Signs

The clinical signs of anterior ciliary artery disruption and resultant anterior segment ischemia include:

| Sign | Description |
|---|---|
| [[Corneal edema]] | Stromal and epithelial edema, especially in the sector corresponding to the disrupted ACAs; [[Descemet folds]] may be present |
| [[Anterior chamber]] flare and cells | Breakdown of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]]; sterile anterior uveitis |
| [[Iris atrophy]] | Sectoral or diffuse iris stromal thinning and transillumination defects |
| [[Corectopia]] | Displacement of the [[pupil]] toward the ischemic sector |
| [[Pupillary abnormalities]] | Poorly reactive or fixed pupil; irregular pupil shape |
| [[Hypotony]] | Low [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) due to decreased aqueous production |
| [[Rubeosis iridis]] | [[Neovascularization]] of the iris; a sign of chronic ischemia |
| [[Neovascular glaucoma]] | Elevated IOP due to neovascular membrane obstructing the [[trabecular meshwork]] |
| [[Cataract]] | Ischemic lens changes; may develop rapidly |
| [[Conjunctival injection]] | Sectoral or diffuse redness |
| [[Episcleral vascular changes]] | Dilation or absence of episcleral vessels in the affected sector |
| [[Anterior chamber fibrin]] | Fibrinous exudate in severe cases |
| [[Posterior synechiae]] | Adhesions between the iris and [[lens capsule]] due to inflammation |
| [[Phthisis bulbi]] | End-stage globe shrinkage in severe, untreated cases |

### Grading of Anterior Segment Ischemia

ASI can be graded by severity:

| Grade | Features |
|---|---|
| **Mild (Subclinical)** | Mild flare, subtle corneal edema, minimal iris changes |
| **Moderate** | Significant flare/cells, sectoral iris atrophy, mild hypotony, corneal edema |
| **Severe** | Dense flare/fibrin, diffuse iris atrophy, marked hypotony, rubeosis iridis, corneal decompensation |
| **End-stage** | Phthisis bulbi, neovascular glaucoma, total iris atrophy |

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: The cornerstone of diagnosis. Allows direct visualization of corneal edema, anterior chamber reaction (flare and cells), iris atrophy, pupillary changes, rubeosis iridis, and posterior synechiae. Sectoral patterns of ischemia corresponding to the territory of disrupted ACAs are characteristic.
- **[[Intraocular pressure]] measurement ([[tonometry]])**: Hypotony (IOP < 6 mmHg) suggests ciliary body shutdown; elevated IOP may indicate neovascular glaucoma.
- **[[Gonioscopy]]**: Examination of the [[anterior chamber angle]] to detect neovascularization of the angle (NVA), [[peripheral anterior synechiae]] (PAS), or angle closure.
- **[[Pupillary examination]]**: Assessment of pupillary shape, reactivity, and position. Corectopia and poor reactivity suggest iris sphincter ischemia.
- **[[Transillumination]]**: Retroillumination of the iris to detect atrophic areas (transillumination defects).

### Imaging Modalities

- **[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA) of the anterior segment (Anterior Segment Fluorescein Angiography, ASFA)**: This is the **gold standard** for evaluating anterior ciliary artery perfusion. Intravenous [[fluorescein]] is injected, and sequential photographs of the anterior segment are taken using a slit-lamp camera or specialized anterior segment angiography system. Key findings include:
  - Delayed or absent filling of anterior ciliary arteries in the affected sector
  - Leakage from iris vessels (indicating blood-aqueous barrier breakdown)
  - Neovascularization of the iris or angle
  - Ischemic zones of the iris stroma appearing as non-perfused dark areas

- **[[Indocyanine green angiography]] (ICGA) of the anterior segment**: ICG dye can be used as an alternative or adjunct to fluorescein for anterior segment angiography, particularly useful for visualizing deeper vascular structures due to its near-infrared fluorescence properties and higher protein binding.

- **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)**: [[Anterior segment OCT]] (AS-OCT) can demonstrate corneal edema, anterior chamber inflammation, iris structural changes, and ciliary body morphology. While not directly visualizing blood flow, it provides high-resolution structural information.

- **[[Optical coherence tomography angiography]] (OCTA)**: An emerging, non-invasive modality that can visualize the microvasculature of the anterior segment, including the [[iris vasculature]], [[limbal vasculature]], and [[conjunctival vasculature]], without the need for dye injection. OCTA can detect areas of capillary dropout, reduced vascular density, and neovascularization. Its role in assessing anterior ciliary artery perfusion is an active area of research.

- **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)**: High-frequency [[ultrasound]] (typically 35–50 MHz) provides cross-sectional imaging of the anterior segment, including the [[ciliary body]], [[iris root]], [[anterior chamber angle]], and [[sclera]]. UBM can detect ciliary body edema, effusion, detachment, or atrophy—all potential consequences of ACA disruption. It is particularly useful for visualizing structures behind the iris that are not accessible to optical imaging.

- **[[Laser Doppler flowmetry]]**: A research tool that can quantify blood flow in the iris and ciliary body. Reduced flow velocities in the territory of disrupted ACAs can be measured, though this modality is not widely available in clinical practice.

### Systemic Workup

When anterior segment ischemia is suspected in the absence of a clear surgical or traumatic etiology, a systemic workup may be warranted:

- **[[Carotid Doppler ultrasound]]**: To evaluate for [[carotid artery stenosis]] or occlusion
- **[[Hemoglobin electrophoresis]]**: To screen for [[sickle cell disease]] or [[sickle cell trait]]
- **[[Erythrocyte sedimentation rate]] (ESR) and [[C-reactive protein]] (CRP)**: To evaluate for [[giant cell arteritis]] or other systemic vasculitides
- **[[HbA1c]] and fasting glucose**: To assess for [[diabetes mellitus]]
- **[[Echocardiography]]**: If embolic disease is suspected
- **[[Magnetic resonance angiography]] (MRA) or [[computed tomography angiography]] (CTA)**: For detailed evaluation of the carotid and ophthalmic vasculature

## Treatment and Management

### Prevention

Prevention of anterior ciliary artery disruption is the most effective management strategy, particularly in the surgical setting.

#### Surgical Considerations in Strabismus Surgery

- **Limit the number of rectus muscles operated on simultaneously**: The traditional guideline is to avoid operating on more than two rectus muscles in a single session in the same eye, particularly in high-risk patients. If surgery on three or more muscles is required, staged procedures should be considered.
- **Vessel-sparing techniques**: During [[strabismus surgery]], techniques have been developed to preserve the anterior ciliary arteries while still achieving the desired muscle repositioning. These include:
  - **Vessel-sparing recession/resection**: Careful dissection to identify and preserve the ACAs during muscle disinsertion
  - **Partial tendon surgery (e.g., [[marginal myotomy]])**: Weakening procedures that do not require complete muscle disinsertion
  - **[[Botulinum toxin]] injection**: Chemodenervation of extraocular muscles as an alternative to surgical disinsertion, thereby preserving the ACAs entirely
- **Preoperative assessment of vascular risk**: Identify patients with [[diabetes]], [[sickle cell disease]], previous strabismus surgery, [[carotid artery disease]], or other risk factors before planning surgery.
- **Intraoperative vessel identification**: Use of the [[operating microscope]] to identify and preserve visible anterior ciliary arteries during surgery. Transillumination of the muscle tendon can help identify the vessels.

#### Scleral Buckling Surgery

- Use of segmental rather than encircling buckles when possible to minimize ACA compression
- Awareness of ACA location when placing sutures and buckle elements

### Medical (Pharmacological) Management

When anterior segment ischemia occurs, medical management is directed at controlling inflammation, preventing complications, and supporting tissue recovery.

| Medication Class | Examples | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| [[Topical corticosteroids]] | [[Prednisolone acetate]] 1%, [[Dexamethasone]] 0.1%, [[Difluprednate]] 0.05% | Suppress anterior segment inflammation; reduce blood-aqueous barrier breakdown |
| [[Cycloplegic agents]] | [[Atropine]] 1%, [[Cyclopentolate]] 1%, [[Homatropine]] 2% | Relieve ciliary spasm and pain; prevent posterior synechiae formation; stabilize the blood-aqueous barrier |
| [[Topical NSAIDs]] | [[Ketorolac]] 0.5%, [[Nepafenac]] 0.1%, [[Bromfenac]] 0.07% | Adjunctive anti-inflammatory therapy; reduce prostaglandin-mediated inflammation |
| [[Anti-VEGF agents]] | [[Bevacizumab]] (intravitreal or intracameral), [[Ranibizumab]] | Treat or prevent [[rubeosis iridis]] and [[neovascular glaucoma]] |
| [[Topical IOP-lowering agents]] | [[Timolol]], [[Brimonidine]], [[Dorzolamide]] | Manage elevated IOP if neovascular glaucoma develops |
| [[Systemic corticosteroids]] | [[Prednisone]] | For severe or bilateral ASI, or when systemic vasculitis is the underlying cause |
| [[Oral analgesics]] | [[Acetaminophen]], [[NSAIDs]] | Pain management |

#### Treatment Protocol for Acute ASI

1. **Intensive topical corticosteroids**: Prednisolone acetate 1% every 1–2 hours initially, with gradual taper based on clinical response
2. **Cycloplegia**: Atropine 1% two to three times daily to prevent synechiae and reduce pain
3. **Close IOP monitoring**: Treat elevated IOP aggressively; avoid [[miotics]] (e.g., [[pilocarpine]]) as they may worsen ischemia
4. **Anti-VEGF therapy**: If rubeosis iridis or neovascular glaucoma develops, intravitreal injection of [[bevacizumab]] or [[ranibizumab]] should be considered promptly
5. **Frequent follow-up**: Daily or every-other-day examinations in the acute phase to monitor for progression

### Surgical Management of Complications

- **[[Panretinal photocoagulation]] (PRP)** or **[[anterior segment photocoagulation]]**: For persistent rubeosis iridis or neovascular glaucoma, laser photocoagulation may be required to reduce the ischemic drive for neovascularization.
- **[[Glaucoma drainage device]]** implantation (e.g., [[Ahmed valve]], [[Baerveldt implant]]): For refractory neovascular glaucoma unresponsive to medical therapy and anti-VEGF treatment.
- **[[Trabeculectomy]]** with [[mitomycin C]]: May be considered for IOP control, though outcomes in neovascular glaucoma are generally less favorable.
- **[[Cyclophotocoagulation]]** (e.g., [[transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]], [[endocyclophotocoagulation]]): For refractory glaucoma or painful blind eyes.
- **[[Penetrating keratoplasty]]** or **[[endothelial keratoplasty]]** (e.g., [[DSAEK]], [[DMEK]]): For corneal decompensation secondary to limbal and endothelial ischemia, though prognosis may be guarded due to ongoing ischemia.
- **[[Cataract surgery]]**: For visually significant ischemic cataract, though surgical planning must account for the compromised anterior segment vasculature.

### Prognosis

The prognosis of anterior segment ischemia depends on the severity and duration of ischemia, the number of anterior ciliary arteries disrupted, the presence of collateral circulation, and the patient's underlying systemic vascular status.

| Severity | Prognosis |
|---|---|
| Mild/Subclinical | Generally excellent; full recovery with appropriate anti-inflammatory treatment |
| Moderate | Good with prompt treatment; some residual iris atrophy or pupillary irregularity may persist |
| Severe | Guarded; risk of permanent iris atrophy, chronic hypotony, neovascular glaucoma, corneal decompensation |
| End-stage (Phthisis) | Poor; irreversible globe damage |

Collateral circulation from the [[long posterior ciliary arteries]] and remaining intact anterior ciliary arteries can partially compensate for lost vessels over time, and revascularization of transposed or reattached muscles has been documented angiographically within weeks to months following surgery.

## Special Considerations

### Anterior Ciliary Arteries in Strabismus Surgery

Strabismus surgery remains the most common clinical context in which anterior ciliary artery integrity is a concern. Key principles include:

- The **[[lateral rectus muscle]]** carries only **one** ACA, making it the most vulnerable muscle in terms of proportional vascular loss when operated upon.
- **Vertical rectus muscles** ([[superior rectus muscle|superior rectus]] and [[inferior rectus muscle|inferior rectus]]) each carry **two** ACAs and are also supplied by branches that contribute to the [[vertical rectus muscle]] blood supply.
- **Reoperation** on previously operated muscles carries increased risk because the ACAs may not have fully revascularized.
- In patients with **[[thyroid eye disease]]** (Graves ophthalmopathy), the rectus muscles are often fibrotic and inflamed, potentially compromising ACA flow even before surgery.

### Anterior Ciliary Arteries and Contact Lens Wear

Chronic [[contact lens]] wear, particularly with poorly fitting or low-oxygen-permeability lenses, can cause [[limbal vascular changes]], including compression or distortion of the limbal arcades derived from the anterior ciliary arteries. This can contribute to [[corneal neovascularization]], [[limbal stem cell deficiency]], and [[contact lens-related red eye]] (CLARE). Proper contact lens fitting and the use of high-[[Dk/t]] materials are essential to minimize vascular compromise.

### Anterior Ciliary Arteries in Ocular Ischemic Syndrome

[[Ocular ischemic syndrome]] (OIS), caused by severe [[carotid artery stenosis]] (typically >90%), results in global hypoperfusion of the eye, including reduced flow through the anterior ciliary arteries. Clinical features overlap with ASI but also include [[retinal]] findings such as [[dot-and-blot hemorrhages]], [[dilated retinal veins]], and [[neovascularization of the disc]] (NVD) or [[neovascularization elsewhere]] (NVE). The anterior segment findings in OIS—including flare, rubeosis iridis, and hypotony—are directly attributable to anterior ciliary artery hypoperfusion.

### Embryology

The anterior ciliary arteries develop from the [[hyaloid vascular system]] and [[periocular mesenchyme]] during [[embryonic development]]. The definitive anterior ciliary arteries are established by the end of the first trimester, paralleling the development of the [[extraocular muscles]] and [[anterior segment]] structures. Congenital anomalies in ACA number or distribution are rare but may predispose certain individuals to anterior segment ischemia with less provocation.

---

## Summary

The anterior ciliary arteries are indispensable vessels that provide the majority of the blood supply to the [[anterior segment]] of the eye. Their intimate anatomical relationship with the [[extraocular muscles]] makes them vulnerable to disruption during [[strabismus surgery]] and other orbital/ocular procedures. [[Anterior segment ischemia]], the principal pathological consequence of ACA compromise, can range from mild, self-limited inflammation to devastating, vision-threatening complications including [[neovascular glaucoma]], [[corneal decompensation]], and [[phthisis bulbi]]. Clinicians must be vigilant in identifying risk factors, employing vessel-sparing surgical techniques, and initiating prompt anti-inflammatory and supportive therapy when ischemia occurs. Advances in imaging, particularly [[anterior segment fluorescein angiography]] and [[OCTA]], continue to enhance our ability to assess and monitor anterior ciliary artery perfusion in both clinical and research settings.
