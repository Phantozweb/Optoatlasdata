

# Soft Contact Lenses

**Soft Contact Lenses** (SCLs) are medical devices composed of flexible [[hydrogel]] or [[silicone hydrogel]] polymers designed to conform to the anterior surface of the [[cornea]] for the correction of [[refractive error]], therapeutic management of [[ocular surface disease]], and cosmetic modification of iris appearance. Since their introduction in the early 1970s following the pioneering work of Otto Wichterle, soft contact lenses have become the most widely prescribed form of contact lens worldwide, accounting for approximately 85–90% of all contact lens fits globally. Their clinical success is attributable to immediate comfort, ease of adaptation, and versatility in correcting [[myopia]], [[hyperopia]], [[astigmatism]], and [[presbyopia]]. However, their use is not without risk; soft contact lenses are associated with a spectrum of complications ranging from mild [[contact lens-induced papillary conjunctivitis]] (CLPC) to sight-threatening [[microbial keratitis]]. This article provides a comprehensive clinical reference for eye care professionals regarding the materials science, fitting philosophy, pathophysiology of complications, diagnostic evaluation, and management strategies associated with soft contact lens wear.

---

## History and Evolution

The concept of contact lenses dates to Leonardo da Vinci's 1508 sketches, but practical soft contact lenses emerged only after Wichterle and Lím synthesized [[poly(2-hydroxyethyl methacrylate)]] (pHEMA) in 1961. The United States [[Food and Drug Administration]] (FDA) approved the first commercial soft lens (Soflens, Bausch & Lomb) in 1971. Early hydrogel lenses were limited by low [[oxygen permeability]] (Dk), which restricted extended wear potential and contributed to [[corneal hypoxia]]. The introduction of [[silicone hydrogel]] materials in 1999 (Lotrafilcon A, marketed as Focus Night & Day by CIBA Vision) represented a paradigm shift, offering Dk values five to six times greater than conventional hydrogels. Subsequent generations of silicone hydrogels have progressively improved surface wettability, modulus optimization, and comfort profiles. Today, the market includes daily disposable, bi-weekly, monthly, and custom soft lenses in both hydrogel and silicone hydrogel formulations.

## Materials Science and Classification

### Polymer Chemistry

Soft contact lenses are broadly classified into two material families:

1. **Conventional Hydrogels** – Polymers based on [[pHEMA]] or its copolymers (e.g., with [[methacrylic acid]], [[N-vinyl pyrrolidone]], or [[glyceryl methacrylate]]). Water content ranges from approximately 38% to 79%. Oxygen transmission is primarily water-dependent; higher water content yields higher Dk but may compromise mechanical strength and increase [[dehydration]]-related discomfort.

2. **Silicone Hydrogels** – Incorporate [[siloxane]] macromer chains into the hydrogel matrix, creating a dual-phase system where oxygen transport occurs predominantly through the silicone phase rather than the aqueous phase. This decouples oxygen permeability from water content, enabling high Dk/t values (typically 80–175 Dk units) even at moderate water contents (33–48%).

### FDA Classification of Soft Contact Lens Materials

The FDA classifies soft lens materials into four groups based on water content and ionicity, with a fifth group added for silicone hydrogels:

| **FDA Group** | **Water Content** | **Ionicity** | **Examples** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Group I | Low (< 50%) | Non-ionic | Polymacon (38%), Tefilcon |
| Group II | High (≥ 50%) | Non-ionic | Omafilcon A (62%), Nelfilcon A (69%) |
| Group III | Low (< 50%) | Ionic | Bufilcon A (45%), Deltafilcon A |
| Group IV | High (≥ 50%) | Ionic | Etafilcon A (58%), Ocufilcon D (55%) |
| Group V | Any | Silicone hydrogel | Senofilcon A (38%), Lotrafilcon B (33%), Delefilcon A (33%/> 80% surface), Somofilcon A (56%) |

Ionic materials (Groups III and IV) are more susceptible to [[protein]] deposition (particularly [[lysozyme]]) due to electrostatic attraction, which has implications for deposit-related complications such as [[contact lens-induced papillary conjunctivitis]].

### Key Material Properties

| **Property** | **Clinical Relevance** |
|---|---|
| **Oxygen Permeability (Dk)** | Determines oxygen flux to the [[corneal epithelium]]; insufficient Dk leads to [[corneal hypoxia]], [[corneal neovascularization]], and [[epithelial microcysts]] |
| **Dk/t (Transmissibility)** | Accounts for lens thickness; Holden-Mertz criterion requires Dk/t ≥ 24 for daily wear and ≥ 87 for overnight wear to avoid clinically significant [[corneal edema]] |
| **Water Content (%)** | Affects comfort, dehydration behavior, and (in hydrogels) oxygen transport |
| **Modulus of Elasticity (MPa)** | Higher modulus improves handling but may increase mechanical complications such as [[superior epithelial arcuate lesion]] (SEAL) and [[mucin ball]] formation |
| **Wettability (Contact Angle)** | Determines tear film stability over the lens surface; poor wettability leads to [[lipid deposition]], visual fluctuation, and discomfort |
| **Coefficient of Friction** | Influences lid-lens interaction and end-of-day comfort; lower friction correlates with improved subjective comfort |
| **UV Blocking** | Class 1 and Class 2 UV-blocking lenses absorb significant [[ultraviolet]] radiation, providing supplementary (not primary) [[UV protection]] |

## Indications

Soft contact lenses are indicated for a wide range of clinical scenarios:

- **Refractive Correction**: [[Myopia]], [[hyperopia]], [[regular astigmatism]] (toric designs), and [[presbyopia]] (multifocal/bifocal designs)
- **Therapeutic/Bandage Use**: Management of [[recurrent corneal erosion]], [[persistent epithelial defect]], [[bullous keratopathy]], post-[[photorefractive keratectomy]] (PRK) pain control, and [[corneal perforation]] (temporary tamponade)
- **Cosmetic**: Prosthetic tinted lenses for [[iris]] defects ([[aniridia]], [[coloboma]], [[heterochromia]]), enhancement tints, and opaque cosmetic lenses
- **Drug Delivery**: Emerging applications as vehicles for sustained [[ophthalmic drug delivery]] of agents such as [[timolol]], [[ketotifen]], or [[antibiotics]]
- **Orthokeratology Adjunct**: While [[orthokeratology]] primarily uses [[rigid gas-permeable lenses]], certain soft lens designs (e.g., extended depth-of-focus) are used in [[myopia control]] strategies
- **Myopia Management**: Specifically designed soft multifocal or dual-focus lenses (e.g., MiSight 1 day by CooperVision) are FDA-approved for slowing [[myopia progression]] in children

## Lens Design Parameters

### Spherical Lenses

Standard spherical SCLs correct [[myopia]] or [[hyperopia]]. Key parameters include:

- **Base Curve (BC)**: Typically 8.3–9.0 mm; determines sagittal depth and fit relationship with the [[cornea]]
- **Diameter (TD)**: Usually 13.8–14.5 mm; must extend 1.0–1.5 mm beyond the [[limbus]] for adequate centration and coverage
- **Power (D)**: Available in standard ranges (e.g., −0.25 to −12.00 D, +0.25 to +8.00 D) with custom options beyond
- **Center Thickness (CT)**: Varies with power; minus lenses are thinner centrally, plus lenses thicker centrally

### Toric Lenses

[[Toric contact lenses]] incorporate cylindrical correction and require rotational stabilization mechanisms:

- **Prism Ballast**: Inferior thickening using base-down [[prism]] to gravitationally orient the lens
- **Peri-Ballast / Dual Thin Zones**: Thin zones at 3 and 9 o'clock positions allow lid interaction to stabilize rotation
- **Truncation**: Flat inferior edge (rarely used in modern soft torics)
- **Accelerated Stabilization Design (ASD)**: Proprietary designs (e.g., Johnson & Johnson's ASD) using four stability zones

Axis misalignment of as little as 5° can degrade visual acuity; the **LARS** (Left Add, Right Subtract) rule is used to compensate for lens rotation during over-refraction.

### Multifocal Lenses

[[Multifocal contact lenses]] for [[presbyopia]] correction employ:

- **Concentric Ring Designs**: Alternating distance and near zones (e.g., Proclear Multifocal)
- **Aspheric Designs**: Gradual power change from center to periphery; may be center-distance or center-near
- **Extended Depth of Focus (EDOF)**: Non-diffractive designs that elongate the focal range
- **Simultaneous Vision Principle**: All multifocal SCLs rely on [[simultaneous vision]], where the brain selects the appropriate image from multiple foci presented simultaneously on the [[retina]]

## Fitting Philosophy and Procedure

### Pre-Fitting Evaluation

A thorough pre-fitting examination includes:

1. **Comprehensive [[ocular history]]**: Previous contact lens experience, [[dry eye]] symptoms ([[OSDI]] or [[SPEED]] questionnaire), systemic medications (e.g., [[isotretinoin]], [[antihistamines]]), allergies, and occupational/environmental demands
2. **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: Evaluation of [[eyelid]] anatomy (lid tension, [[meibomian gland]] function, presence of [[blepharitis]]), [[conjunctiva]] (papillae, follicles, injection), [[cornea]] (scarring, [[neovascularization]], [[dystrophy]]), and [[tear film]] quality ([[tear break-up time]] [TBUT], [[tear meniscus height]])
3. **[[Keratometry]] / [[Corneal topography]]**: Measurement of corneal curvature to guide base curve selection; detection of [[irregular astigmatism]] or [[keratoconus]] that may preclude soft lens fitting
4. **[[Refraction]]**: Accurate [[manifest refraction]] and [[vertex distance]] correction for powers exceeding ±4.00 D
5. **[[Pupillometry]]**: Particularly important for multifocal fitting; large [[scotopic pupil]] size may affect optical zone performance
6. **[[Tear film assessment]]**: [[Schirmer test]], [[phenol red thread test]], [[tear osmolarity]], and [[meibography]] as indicated

### Lens Selection and Trial Fitting

Base curve selection is guided by keratometry readings and manufacturer fitting guides. Most modern SCLs are available in limited base curves (one or two options), relying on the lens's flexibility to accommodate a range of corneal curvatures.

**Assessment of Fit (after 10–20 minutes of settling):**

| **Parameter** | **Ideal Finding** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|---|
| **Centration** | Well-centered, covering [[limbus]] by ~1 mm 360° | Decentration causes visual disturbance, edge awareness, and [[corneal desiccation]] |
| **Movement on Blink** | 0.25–0.50 mm vertical movement | Excessive movement → discomfort, visual instability; insufficient movement → [[lens binding]], [[corneal hypoxia]], debris trapping |
| **Push-Up Test** | Moderate resistance with easy return to center | Tight lens: minimal movement, difficult to displace; loose lens: excessive lag, edge standoff |
| **Coverage** | Full corneal coverage at all gaze positions | Incomplete coverage exposes [[corneal epithelium]] to desiccation |
| **Comfort** | Minimal lens awareness after adaptation | Persistent discomfort suggests poor fit, surface defect, or material incompatibility |

### Over-Refraction

A [[spherical over-refraction]] (and spherocylindrical for torics) is performed with the trial lens in situ to determine the final prescription. For toric lenses, rotational position is assessed using the scribe marks and the LARS rule is applied.

## Pathophysiology of Soft Contact Lens Complications

Soft contact lens wear disrupts the normal [[ocular surface]] homeostasis through several interrelated mechanisms:

### Hypoxia

The soft contact lens acts as a barrier to atmospheric oxygen reaching the [[corneal epithelium]]. Despite the high Dk/t of modern silicone hydrogels, any lens reduces oxygen availability compared to the open-eye state. Chronic hypoxia triggers:

- **[[Corneal edema]]**: Stromal swelling due to impaired endothelial pump function; clinically manifests as [[striae]] (2–6% edema), [[folds]] (6–10%), and haze (>10%)
- **[[Corneal neovascularization]]**: Hypoxia-driven upregulation of [[vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF) stimulates limbal vessel ingrowth into the normally avascular [[corneal stroma]]; classified as superficial or deep, and graded by extent of penetration
- **[[Epithelial microcysts]]**: Small, refractile, reversed-illumination-positive inclusions in the [[corneal epithelium]] representing cellular debris from accelerated epithelial turnover; typically appear after weeks of extended wear and may paradoxically increase initially upon refitting with higher-Dk lenses (as metabolic recovery accelerates cell turnover)
- **[[Limbal stem cell]] stress**: Chronic hypoxia at the [[limbus]] may compromise the [[limbal stem cell]] niche, though clinically significant [[limbal stem cell deficiency]] from contact lens wear alone is rare

### Mechanical Trauma

The physical interaction between the lens, [[eyelid]], and [[ocular surface]] produces:

- **[[Superior epithelial arcuate lesion]] (SEAL)**: Arc-shaped epithelial disruption near the superior [[limbus]], associated with high-modulus silicone hydrogel lenses and tight-fitting designs; caused by mechanical compression between the lens edge and the superior lid margin
- **[[Mucin ball]] formation**: Small, translucent spheres trapped between the posterior lens surface and the [[corneal epithelium]] during extended wear; composed of [[mucin]], [[lipid]], and cellular debris; generally benign but may leave transient epithelial impressions
- **[[Contact lens-induced papillary conjunctivitis]] (CLPC)**: Formerly termed [[giant papillary conjunctivitis]] (GPC); characterized by papillae ≥ 0.3 mm on the upper [[tarsal conjunctiva]]; pathogenesis involves combined mechanical irritation and [[type I hypersensitivity]] / [[type IV hypersensitivity]] immune responses to deposited [[protein]] antigens (particularly denatured [[lysozyme]] and [[lactoferrin]])
- **[[Corneal abrasion]]**: Epithelial defects from lens insertion/removal trauma, foreign body trapping beneath the lens, or damaged lens edges

### Inflammatory and Immune Responses

- **[[Contact lens-induced acute red eye]] (CLARE)**: Acute unilateral inflammatory reaction occurring during overnight wear; mediated by [[endotoxin]] release from gram-negative bacteria (especially [[Haemophilus influenzae]], [[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]]) colonizing the lens surface; presents with pain, [[conjunctival hyperemia]], and [[corneal infiltrates]] (typically peripheral, non-ulcerative)
- **[[Contact lens peripheral ulcer]] (CLPU)**: Small (< 1.5 mm), round, peripheral [[corneal infiltrate]] with overlying epithelial excavation; represents a localized [[immune response]] to bacterial antigens rather than active infection; usually culture-negative
- **[[Infiltrative keratitis]] (IK)**: Focal or multifocal [[corneal infiltrates]] without significant epithelial defect; sterile inflammatory response; must be differentiated from [[microbial keratitis]]
- **[[Solution-induced corneal staining]] (SICS)**: Diffuse punctate [[corneal staining]] occurring 2–4 hours after lens application, associated with specific lens-solution combinations (particularly [[polyhexamethylene biguanide]] [PHMB]-preserved solutions with certain Group IV hydrogels); represents a transient toxic/hypersensitivity reaction

### Infectious Complications

- **[[Microbial keratitis]] (MK)**: The most serious complication of soft contact lens wear; incidence approximately 1.2–4.1 per 10,000 daily-wear users per year and 13.3–20.0 per 10,000 extended-wear users per year. Predominant organisms include [[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]] (most common gram-negative), [[Staphylococcus aureus]] (most common gram-positive), [[Serratia marcescens]], and [[Acanthamoeba]] species. Risk factors include overnight wear, poor hygiene, water exposure (swimming, showering), lens case contamination, and smoking. [[Acanthamoeba keratitis]] is particularly associated with water exposure and inadequate disinfection; it presents with severe pain disproportionate to clinical signs, [[perineural infiltrates]] (pathognomonic [[radial keratoneuritis]]), and a ring-shaped [[stromal infiltrate]] in advanced cases
- **[[Fungal keratitis]]**: Less common but may occur with organic matter contamination; organisms include [[Fusarium]] and [[Aspergillus]] species; the 2005–2006 Fusarium keratitis outbreak was linked to ReNu with MoistureLoc multipurpose solution

### Tear Film Disruption and Dry Eye

Soft contact lens wear divides the [[tear film]] into pre-lens and post-lens components, reducing overall tear film stability. Lens dehydration draws water from the post-lens tear film and underlying [[corneal epithelium]], contributing to:

- **[[Contact lens-related dry eye]] (CLDE)**: Reported by 30–50% of contact lens wearers; symptoms include end-of-day dryness, grittiness, and reduced comfortable wearing time; multifactorial etiology involving tear film thinning, increased [[tear evaporation]], reduced [[lipid layer]] thickness, and [[meibomian gland dysfunction]]
- **[[Lid wiper epitheliopathy]] (LWE)**: Staining of the marginal [[conjunctival epithelium]] of the upper lid (the "lid wiper" zone) caused by increased friction between the lid and the lens surface; correlates strongly with contact lens discomfort

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Symptoms Associated with Soft Contact Lens Wear

Patients may present with a spectrum of symptoms:

| **Symptom** | **Possible Etiology** |
|---|---|
| End-of-day dryness and discomfort | [[Contact lens-related dry eye]], lens dehydration, [[meibomian gland dysfunction]], poor lens wettability |
| Redness | [[CLARE]], [[CLPU]], [[microbial keratitis]], [[CLPC]], solution toxicity, [[hypoxia]] |
| Pain (mild to severe) | [[Corneal abrasion]], [[CLPU]], [[microbial keratitis]], [[Acanthamoeba keratitis]], tight lens syndrome |
| Blurred vision | Deposit accumulation, lens dehydration, [[corneal edema]], incorrect power, lens rotation (torics), [[irregular astigmatism]] |
| Photophobia | [[Corneal infiltrates]], [[anterior uveitis]] (secondary to keratitis), [[corneal abrasion]] |
| Mucous discharge | [[CLPC]], [[allergic conjunctivitis]], secondary [[bacterial conjunctivitis]] |
| Foreign body sensation | Damaged lens edge, trapped debris, [[SEAL]], [[corneal erosion]] |
| Itching | [[CLPC]], [[seasonal allergic conjunctivitis]], solution hypersensitivity |
| Halos/glare | [[Corneal edema]], multifocal optics, large [[pupil]] relative to optical zone |

## Diagnostic Evaluation

### Slit-Lamp Examination Protocol for Contact Lens Wearers

A systematic [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] examination should include:

1. **Lens-on assessment**: Evaluate lens centration, movement, surface wettability (pre-lens tear film break-up), and deposits (using direct and retroillumination)
2. **Lens removal**: Examine the lens for defects, deposits, and parameter verification
3. **[[Sodium fluorescein]] staining**: Assess [[corneal epithelium]] integrity; staining patterns include:
   - **Diffuse punctate staining**: SICS, dry eye, toxicity
   - **Central corneal staining**: Lens binding, flat fit, dehydration
   - **Peripheral arcuate staining**: SEAL, tight lens
   - **Focal staining with infiltrate**: CLPU, microbial keratitis
   - **3 and 9 o'clock staining**: Lens edge desiccation (more common with RGP but can occur with SCL)
4. **[[Lissamine green]] staining**: Evaluates [[conjunctival]] epithelial damage and [[lid wiper epitheliopathy]]
5. **Upper lid eversion**: Mandatory to assess for [[CLPC]] (papillae grading: Grade 0 = none; Grade 1 = mild, < 0.3 mm; Grade 2 = moderate, 0.3–1.0 mm; Grade 3 = severe, > 1.0 mm with apical staining)
6. **[[Anterior chamber]] assessment**: Rule out [[anterior uveitis]] (cells and flare) in cases of keratitis

### Ancillary Diagnostic Testing

| **Test** | **Indication** |
|---|---|
| **[[Corneal topography]]** | Suspected [[corneal warpage]], [[keratoconus]] screening, irregular astigmatism evaluation |
| **[[Anterior segment OCT]]** | Measurement of [[corneal thickness]] (edema monitoring), tear film analysis, lens-cornea relationship |
| **[[Corneal culture]] and sensitivity** | Suspected [[microbial keratitis]] (corneal scraping for [[Gram stain]], culture on [[blood agar]], [[chocolate agar]], [[Sabouraud agar]], non-nutrient agar with *E. coli* overlay for [[Acanthamoeba]]) |
| **[[Confocal microscopy]]** | In vivo identification of [[Acanthamoeba]] cysts, [[fungal]] elements, assessment of [[corneal nerve]] morphology and [[endothelial cell]] density |
| **[[Meibography]]** | Assessment of [[meibomian gland]] dropout in patients with contact lens-related dry eye |
| **[[Tear osmolarity]]** | Objective measurement of [[dry eye]] severity (> 308 mOsm/L or > 8 mOsm/L interocular difference is diagnostic) |
| **[[MMP-9]] testing (InflammaDry)** | Detection of [[ocular surface inflammation]] in symptomatic contact lens wearers |

## Management and Treatment

### Management of Uncomplicated Contact Lens Wear

**Lens Care Systems:**

| **System Type** | **Mechanism** | **Considerations** |
|---|---|---|
| **Multipurpose Solution (MPS)** | Single solution for cleaning, rinsing, disinfection, and storage; active agents include [[PHMB]] (e.g., Biotrue, ReNu), [[polyquaternium-1]] (POLYQUAD, e.g., OPTI-FREE) | Most convenient; compliance-dependent; certain lens-solution combinations may cause SICS |
| **Hydrogen Peroxide (H₂O₂)** | 3% [[hydrogen peroxide]] provides broad-spectrum disinfection; requires neutralization (platinum disc catalyst or catalase tablet) before lens insertion | Superior disinfection efficacy; preservative-free after neutralization; recommended for patients with solution sensitivity; effective against [[Acanthamoeba]] trophozoites (but variable against cysts) |
| **Daily Disposable** | No care system required; fresh lens each day | Eliminates solution-related complications and deposit accumulation; lowest risk profile for [[microbial keratitis]]; highest compliance; recommended as first-line modality when feasible |

**Patient Education and Compliance:**

Non-compliance with care regimens is the single greatest modifiable risk factor for contact lens complications. Key education points include:

- **Hand hygiene**: Thorough handwashing with soap and water before lens handling
- **"Rub and rinse"**: Even with "no-rub" solutions, mechanical rubbing for 5–10 seconds per side significantly reduces microbial bioburden
- **Case hygiene**: Empty, rinse with fresh solution (never water), and air-dry the case inverted after each use; replace the case at least every 1–3 months
- **Water avoidance**: No swimming, showering, or hot tub use with lenses; water exposure dramatically increases [[Acanthamoeba]] risk
- **Replacement schedule adherence**: Do not overwear lenses beyond the prescribed replacement interval
- **No overnight wear** (unless specifically prescribed and monitored): Extended/continuous wear increases microbial keratitis risk 5–15-fold compared to daily wear

### Management of Specific Complications

#### Contact Lens-Related Dry Eye (CLDE)

- Refit to daily disposable lenses (eliminates deposit-related discomfort)
- Switch to lower-dehydration materials (lower water content silicone hydrogels, e.g., Delefilcon A with water-gradient technology)
- Preservative-free [[artificial tears]] (compatible with contact lens wear; e.g., [[sodium hyaluronate]], [[carboxymethylcellulose]])
- Treat underlying [[meibomian gland dysfunction]] with warm compresses, [[lid hygiene]], and [[omega-3 fatty acid]] supplementation
- Consider [[punctal plugs]] for moderate-to-severe aqueous deficiency
- Reduce wearing time; implement "lens holidays"
- Environmental modifications (humidifiers, avoidance of forced-air heating/cooling)

#### Contact Lens-Induced Papillary Conjunctivitis (CLPC)

- Discontinue lens wear until papillae resolve (typically 2–4 weeks)
- Switch to daily disposable lenses upon resumption
- Topical [[mast cell stabilizer]] / [[antihistamine]] (e.g., [[olopatadine]] 0.1% BID, [[ketotifen]] 0.025% BID)
- Short course of topical [[corticosteroid]] (e.g., [[loteprednol etabonate]] 0.5% QID for 2 weeks) for severe cases, with [[intraocular pressure]] monitoring
- Switch from ionic (Group IV) to non-ionic materials to reduce protein deposition
- Hydrogen peroxide disinfection system if reusable lenses are continued

#### Contact Lens-Induced Acute Red Eye (CLARE)

- Discontinue lens wear immediately
- Topical [[fluoroquinolone]] antibiotic (e.g., [[moxifloxacin]] 0.5%) QID as prophylaxis, though the condition is typically sterile
- Topical [[NSAID]] (e.g., [[ketorolac]] 0.5% QID) or short-course [[corticosteroid]] for comfort
- Refit to daily disposable lenses; discontinue overnight wear permanently

#### Contact Lens Peripheral Ulcer (CLPU)

- Discontinue lens wear
- Topical broad-spectrum [[antibiotic]] (e.g., [[moxifloxacin]] 0.5% or [[ofloxacin]] 0.3%) QID for 5–7 days
- Monitor for progression; if infiltrate is > 1.5 mm, central, or associated with significant anterior chamber reaction, treat as presumed [[microbial keratitis]]
- Resume lens wear (daily disposable) only after complete resolution

#### Microbial Keratitis

This is an **ophthalmic emergency** requiring aggressive management:

1. **Corneal scraping**: Obtain samples for [[Gram stain]], [[culture]], and [[sensitivity testing]] before initiating therapy (for infiltrates > 1 mm, central, or with significant anterior chamber reaction)
2. **Empiric fortified antibiotic therapy**:
   - **[[Fortified cefazolin]]** (50 mg/mL) or **[[fortified vancomycin]]** (25–50 mg/mL) alternating with **[[fortified tobramycin]]** (14 mg/mL) or **[[fortified gentamicin]]** (14 mg/mL) — every 30–60 minutes around the clock initially
   - Alternatively, **fluoroquinolone monotherapy** (e.g., [[moxifloxacin]] 0.5% every 1 hour) may be used for small-to-moderate, non-sight-threatening ulcers
3. **Cycloplegia**: [[Cyclopentolate]] 1% or [[atropine]] 1% for pain relief and prevention of [[posterior synechiae]]
4. **No patching or contact lens bandage** in the acute phase
5. **Daily follow-up** until clinical improvement is documented
6. **Modify therapy** based on culture results and clinical response
7. **Corticosteroids**: Controversial; may be considered 48–72 hours after initiation of appropriate antibiotic therapy if culture-confirmed bacterial etiology and clinical improvement; the Steroids for Corneal Ulcers Trial (SCUT) showed modest benefit for central and deep ulcers but no overall difference in best-corrected visual acuity at 12 months
8. **Surgical intervention**: [[Therapeutic penetrating keratoplasty]] for perforated or non-responsive ulcers; [[amniotic membrane transplantation]] for persistent epithelial defects

For suspected **[[Acanthamoeba keratitis]]**:
- **Dual anti-amoebic therapy**: [[Polyhexamethylene biguanide]] (PHMB) 0.02% or [[chlorhexidine]] 0.02% combined with [[propamidine isethionate]] (Brolene) 0.1% — hourly initially, then tapered over months
- Treatment duration: Typically 3–6 months minimum
- [[Corneal cross-linking]] has been explored as adjunctive therapy

#### Superior Epithelial Arcuate Lesion (SEAL)

- Refit to a lower-modulus lens material
- Flatten the base curve or increase diameter to reduce edge-cornea interaction
- Switch to daily disposable lenses
- Topical [[lubricant]] and prophylactic [[antibiotic]] until epithelium heals

## Wearing Schedules and Replacement Modalities

| **Modality** | **Wearing Schedule** | **Replacement** | **Risk Profile** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Daily Disposable (DD)** | Daily wear only | New lens each day | Lowest risk; gold standard for safety |
| **Bi-weekly (2-week)** | Daily wear (remove nightly) | Every 14 days | Moderate risk; requires care system |
| **Monthly** | Daily wear (remove nightly) | Every 30 days | Moderate risk; requires care system |
| **Extended Wear (EW)** | Up to 6 consecutive nights | Per manufacturer schedule | Elevated risk of MK (5–15× vs. DD) |
| **Continuous Wear (CW)** | Up to 30 consecutive nights | Monthly | Highest risk; reserved for specific indications with informed consent |

## Special Populations and Considerations

### Pediatric Contact Lens Fitting

Children as young as 8 years can be successfully fitted with soft contact lenses. The ACHIEVE and CLIP studies demonstrated that children adapt well to daily disposable lenses. [[Myopia control]] lenses (e.g., MiSight 1 day, dual-focus design) are specifically indicated for children aged 8–12 years at initiation, with studies showing approximately 59% reduction in [[axial length]] progression over 3 years.

### Pregnancy

Hormonal changes during pregnancy may alter [[corneal curvature]], [[tear film]] composition, and [[refractive error]], leading to contact lens intolerance. Refitting or temporary discontinuation may be necessary; definitive refractive changes should not be assessed until several months postpartum.

### Systemic Disease Considerations

- **[[Diabetes mellitus]]**: Reduced [[corneal sensitivity]], impaired [[epithelial healing]], and increased infection risk; close monitoring required
- **[[Sjögren syndrome]]** and other [[autoimmune]] conditions: Severe [[aqueous-deficient dry eye]] may preclude comfortable lens wear; [[scleral lenses]] may be a better option
- **[[Immunosuppression]]**: Increased risk of opportunistic [[corneal infection]]; daily disposable lenses preferred if wear is necessary
- **Medications**: [[Isotretinoin]] (severe dry eye), [[antihistamines]] (reduced tear production), [[oral contraceptives]] (tear film changes)

## Emerging Technologies

- **Smart Contact Lenses**: Integration of biosensors for continuous [[intraocular pressure]] monitoring (e.g., Sensimed Triggerfish), [[glucose]] monitoring for [[diabetes]], and augmented reality displays
- **Drug-Eluting Lenses**: Sustained release of [[anti-glaucoma]] medications, [[antibiotics]], [[antihistamines]], and [[anti-inflammatory]] agents directly to the [[ocular surface]]
- **3D-Printed Custom Lenses**: Additive manufacturing enabling truly customized lens geometries for irregular corneas
- **Water-Gradient and Biomimetic Materials**: Lenses mimicking the [[corneal epithelium]]'s natural water content gradient (e.g., DAILIES TOTAL1 with > 80% water at the surface and 33% at the core)
- **Light-Adaptive Lenses**: Photochromic contact lenses (e.g., Acuvue Oasys with Transitions) that darken in response to UV and visible light exposure

---

## Prognosis

The vast majority of soft contact lens wearers experience safe, comfortable, and effective vision correction when proper fitting, material selection, and compliance are maintained. Complications, when identified early, are generally reversible with appropriate management. However, severe [[microbial keratitis]] can result in permanent [[corneal scarring]], [[irregular astigmatism]], and vision loss requiring [[corneal transplantation]]. The annual incidence of permanent vision loss from contact lens-related microbial keratitis is estimated at approximately 1 in 50,000 daily-wear users. Patient education, regular follow-up (at minimum annually, with more frequent visits for extended-wear patients), and adherence to evidence-based care guidelines remain the cornerstones of safe soft contact lens practice.
