

# Anomalous Retinal Correspondence

---

**Anomalous Retinal Correspondence (ARC)** is a sensory adaptation of the [[binocular visual system]] in which the [[fovea]] of one eye becomes neurologically paired with a non-foveal (extrafoveal) retinal point in the fellow eye for the purpose of achieving a rudimentary form of [[binocular single vision]]. This cortical remapping occurs almost exclusively in the context of [[strabismus]], where the normal geometric relationship between corresponding retinal points is disrupted by the ocular misalignment. ARC represents one of the most clinically significant and complex [[sensory adaptations]] encountered in [[binocular vision]] disorders, and its presence profoundly influences the prognosis and management strategy for patients with [[strabismus]], particularly in the context of [[orthoptic]] therapy and surgical intervention.

Unlike [[normal retinal correspondence]] (NRC), in which the two foveae serve as the principal corresponding points and all other retinal loci maintain a fixed angular relationship dictated by [[Vieth-Müller circle]] geometry, ARC involves a shift in the subjective directional values assigned to retinal elements. This shift allows the patient to experience some degree of simultaneous perception and even rudimentary [[stereopsis]] despite the presence of a manifest deviation. ARC is overwhelmingly associated with early-onset, constant [[strabismus]] and is far less common in acquired or intermittent deviations.

---

## Historical Background

The concept of retinal correspondence dates to the foundational work of [[Johannes Kepler]] and later [[Christiaan Huygens]], who described the geometric principles underlying binocular projection. The phenomenon of anomalous correspondence was first systematically described in the 19th century by investigators studying patients with [[concomitant strabismus]] who did not experience [[diplopia]] despite measurable ocular misalignment. Early pioneers including [[Albrecht von Graefe]], [[Ewald Hering]], and [[Hermann von Helmholtz]] debated whether the adaptation was retinal, cortical, or a combination of both.

In the 20th century, the work of [[Cüppers]], [[Bagolini]], and [[Flom]] significantly advanced the clinical understanding and diagnostic methodology for ARC. Bagolini's development of the [[Bagolini striated lenses]] test provided a particularly naturalistic method for evaluating retinal correspondence under habitual viewing conditions, and this test remains a cornerstone of clinical assessment today.

## Definitions and Classification

### Normal Retinal Correspondence (NRC)

In [[normal retinal correspondence]], the [[fovea]] of the right eye and the [[fovea]] of the left eye share a common subjective visual direction (the principal visual direction). All other pairs of retinal points maintain a fixed angular relationship such that objects stimulating geometrically corresponding points are perceived as being in the same spatial location. The angular separation between corresponding points is zero at the foveae and increases systematically toward the [[peripheral retina]], following the geometry of the [[horopter]].

### Anomalous Retinal Correspondence (ARC)

In ARC, the fovea of the fixating eye becomes paired not with the fovea of the deviating eye, but with an extrafoveal point in the deviating eye. This extrafoveal point—termed the **anomalous corresponding point** or **pseudo-fovea**—assumes the same subjective visual direction as the fixating fovea. The angular distance between the true fovea of the deviating eye and the anomalous corresponding point is termed the **angle of anomaly (A)**.

### Key Angular Relationships

| Term | Symbol | Definition |
|------|--------|------------|
| **Objective angle of deviation** | **H** | The measurable angle of [[strabismus]] (e.g., by [[prism cover test]]) |
| **Subjective angle of deviation** | **S** | The angle measured by subjective tests (e.g., [[after-image test]], [[Hering-Bielschowsky after-image test]]) |
| **Angle of anomaly** | **A** | The difference between the objective and subjective angles: **A = H − S** |

### Types of ARC

ARC is classified based on the relationship between the angle of anomaly and the objective angle of deviation:

| Type | Relationship | Clinical Significance |
|------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Harmonious ARC (HARC)** | A = H (i.e., S = 0) | The angle of anomaly exactly equals the objective deviation. The patient perceives no subjective deviation. This is the most common form and represents a complete sensory adaptation to the strabismus. |
| **Unharmonious ARC (UHARC)** | A < H (i.e., S ≠ 0, but S ≠ H) | The angle of anomaly is less than the objective deviation. The sensory adaptation is incomplete, and a residual subjective angle persists. |
| **Paradoxical ARC** | A > H | Extremely rare. The angle of anomaly exceeds the objective deviation, resulting in a subjective deviation in the opposite direction to the objective deviation. |

## Pathophysiology

### Neurophysiological Basis

ARC is fundamentally a **cortical sensory adaptation** rather than a structural retinal change. The retinal photoreceptors and their anatomical connections remain unchanged; rather, the alteration occurs at the level of cortical processing within the [[primary visual cortex]] (area [[V1]]), the [[extrastriate cortex]], and higher-order binocular integration areas.

Under normal binocular conditions, neurons in [[V1]] and [[V2]] are organized into [[ocular dominance columns]] that receive input from corresponding retinal loci in each eye. These neurons are tuned to respond to stimuli falling on geometrically corresponding points, and their coordinated activity underlies [[fusion]] and [[stereopsis]]. In the presence of early-onset strabismus, the images falling on the two foveae are chronically disparate (non-fusible), creating a conflict that the developing visual cortex must resolve.

The immature visual cortex resolves this conflict through several potential mechanisms:

1. **[[Suppression]]**: Active cortical inhibition of the image from the deviating eye, particularly in the foveal region, to eliminate [[diplopia]] and [[visual confusion]].
2. **Anomalous Retinal Correspondence**: Reassignment of subjective visual direction values such that the fovea of the fixating eye and an extrafoveal point in the deviating eye acquire a common visual direction.
3. **[[Amblyopia]]**: Chronic reduction in best-corrected [[visual acuity]] in the deviating eye due to abnormal visual experience during the [[critical period]] of visual development.

These three adaptations frequently coexist. ARC and [[suppression]] are not mutually exclusive; indeed, most patients with ARC also demonstrate suppression of the foveal region of the deviating eye (the so-called **suppression scotoma**), while the anomalous correspondence operates in the peripheral retinal areas surrounding the suppression zone.

### Critical Period and Plasticity

ARC develops almost exclusively during the [[critical period]] of visual development, generally considered to extend from birth to approximately 6–8 years of age, with the greatest susceptibility in the first 2–3 years. The developing [[visual cortex]] possesses sufficient [[neuroplasticity]] to remap binocular connections in response to abnormal visual input. After the critical period closes, the capacity for this remapping diminishes dramatically, which is why ARC is rarely observed in adult-onset strabismus.

The depth and tenacity of ARC are generally proportional to:
- **Age of onset of strabismus**: Earlier onset produces more deeply ingrained ARC.
- **Duration of strabismus**: Longer duration reinforces the anomalous adaptation.
- **Constancy of the deviation**: Constant deviations are more likely to produce ARC than [[intermittent strabismus]].
- **Magnitude of the deviation**: Small to moderate angles of [[esotropia]] are most commonly associated with harmonious ARC.

### Relationship to Specific Types of Strabismus

ARC is most commonly associated with:

- **[[Infantile esotropia]]** (also known as [[congenital esotropia]]): The prototypical condition associated with deeply ingrained ARC. The early onset and constant nature of the deviation provide the ideal substrate for cortical remapping.
- **[[Acquired non-accommodative esotropia]]** of early childhood.
- **Small-angle [[esotropia]]** ([[microtropia]] / [[monofixation syndrome]]): In [[microtropia]], harmonious ARC is a defining feature. The small angle of deviation (typically ≤ 10 [[prism diopters]]) allows for peripheral fusion with a central suppression scotoma and anomalous correspondence.
- **Constant [[exotropia]]**: ARC can occur but is less common than in esotropia, partly because many exotropias are intermittent.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Absence of Diplopia

The hallmark clinical feature of ARC is the **absence of [[diplopia]]** despite the presence of a manifest [[strabismus]]. In a patient with NRC and a manifest deviation, one would expect either diplopia or suppression. In ARC, the cortical remapping provides an alternative mechanism for eliminating diplopia: the anomalous pairing of retinal points creates a unified (albeit spatially distorted) perceptual experience.

### Anomalous Binocular Single Vision

Patients with ARC may demonstrate a form of binocular single vision that, while not normal, provides functional advantages over complete suppression. This **anomalous binocular single vision (ABSV)** may include:

- **Simultaneous perception**: Both eyes contribute to the visual percept simultaneously.
- **Rudimentary fusion**: Some capacity to merge the images from the two eyes, though the quality of fusion is inferior to that achieved with NRC.
- **Coarse [[stereopsis]]**: Some patients with ARC, particularly those with [[microtropia]], may demonstrate measurable (though reduced) stereoacuity, typically in the range of 60–3000 [[seconds of arc]], compared to the 20–40 seconds of arc achievable with NRC.

### Associated Findings

| Finding | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **[[Amblyopia]]** | Frequently coexists with ARC, particularly in unilateral strabismus. The deviating eye often has reduced acuity. |
| **[[Eccentric fixation]]** | The deviating eye may adopt a non-foveal fixation pattern. Eccentric fixation and ARC are related but distinct phenomena; eccentric fixation is a monocular adaptation, while ARC is binocular. |
| **Central [[suppression scotoma]]** | A region of suppression surrounding the fovea of the deviating eye is commonly present, even in the context of ARC. |
| **Reduced [[stereopsis]]** | Stereoacuity is invariably reduced compared to individuals with NRC and normal binocular vision. |
| **[[Horror fusionis]]** | In some patients with deeply ingrained ARC, attempts to establish NRC through prism correction or surgery may result in intractable diplopia, as the visual cortex resists the imposition of normal correspondence. |

## Diagnostic Modalities

The diagnosis of ARC requires careful comparison of the **objective angle of deviation** (measured by methods that do not require subjective binocular responses) with the **subjective angle of deviation** (measured by tests that rely on the patient's binocular perceptual experience). A discrepancy between these two measurements is the hallmark of ARC.

### Objective Measurement of the Angle of Deviation

| Test | Method |
|------|--------|
| **[[Prism cover test]]** | The gold standard for measuring the objective angle. Prisms are placed before one eye while the examiner observes for neutralization of the cover test movement. |
| **[[Hirschberg test]]** | Estimation of the deviation based on the position of the [[corneal light reflex]]. Less precise but useful in uncooperative patients. |
| **[[Krimsky test]]** | Prisms are placed before one eye to center the corneal light reflex, providing an estimate of the objective angle. |
| **[[Synoptophore]] (objective angle)** | The arms of the [[synoptophore]] are set to the angle at which the corneal reflexes are centered (objective angle measurement). |

### Subjective Tests for Retinal Correspondence

#### 1. Bagolini Striated Lenses Test

The [[Bagolini striated lenses]] test is widely regarded as the most clinically useful test for retinal correspondence because it evaluates binocular status under the most **naturalistic viewing conditions**. The lenses are plano lenses with fine striations oriented at 45° and 135° before the right and left eyes, respectively. A point light source produces a streak of light perpendicular to the striations in each eye.

**Interpretation:**

| Response | Interpretation |
|----------|---------------|
| Two streaks forming a symmetric **X** centered on the light | **NRC** with bifoveal fixation (or HARC if a manifest deviation is present on cover test) |
| Two streaks forming an **X** with the intersection displaced from the light | **Unharmonious ARC** |
| One streak only | **Suppression** of one eye |
| Two streaks but one has a gap centrally | **Suppression scotoma** with peripheral ARC or NRC |

A critical point: if the Bagolini test shows an apparently normal response (symmetric X) but the cover test reveals a manifest deviation, the diagnosis is **harmonious ARC**, not NRC.

#### 2. Worth Four-Dot Test

The [[Worth four-dot test]] uses red-green dissociation to evaluate binocular status. While primarily a test for fusion and suppression, the responses can provide indirect evidence of ARC. If a patient with a manifest strabismus reports fusion (four dots) at distance and/or near, this suggests ARC (or, less commonly, a very large fusional amplitude).

#### 3. After-Image Test (Hering-Bielschowsky After-Image Test)

This test provides the most **dissociated** assessment of retinal correspondence and is considered the gold standard for determining the "true" state of correspondence.

**Procedure:**
- A vertical after-image is created on the fovea of one eye using a bright linear flash.
- A horizontal after-image is created on the fovea of the fellow eye.
- The patient is asked to describe the relative position of the two after-images.

**Interpretation:**

| Response | Interpretation |
|----------|---------------|
| Cross centered (after-images intersect at their midpoints) | **NRC** |
| Cross displaced (after-images do not intersect at midpoints) | **ARC** (the degree of displacement indicates the angle of anomaly) |
| One after-image not perceived | **Suppression** |

Because after-images are "burned" onto the fovea and cannot be moved by eye movements or cortical remapping, this test reveals the underlying correspondence state independent of the current motor alignment. A patient with HARC will typically show NRC on the after-image test (because the test maximally dissociates the eyes and disrupts the anomalous adaptation) but ARC on less dissociated tests like Bagolini lenses.

#### 4. Synoptophore (Major Amblyoscope)

The [[synoptophore]] allows independent measurement of the objective and subjective angles of deviation.

- **Objective angle**: Determined by centering the corneal reflexes.
- **Subjective angle**: Determined by asking the patient to superimpose or fuse the targets.
- **Angle of anomaly**: Objective angle minus subjective angle.

The synoptophore provides a controlled environment for quantifying the angle of anomaly and assessing the grade of binocular vision (simultaneous perception, fusion, stereopsis) at both the objective and subjective angles.

#### 5. Prism Adaptation Test

When prisms equal to the full objective deviation are placed before the eyes of a patient with ARC, the anomalous correspondence may "follow" the prism, maintaining the anomalous pairing despite the optical correction of the deviation. This phenomenon is observed clinically when the subjective angle shifts to maintain the ARC relationship even after prismatic correction—a finding that has significant implications for treatment planning.

### Diagnostic Algorithm

A systematic approach to diagnosing ARC involves:

1. **Measure the objective angle** (prism cover test).
2. **Perform the Bagolini striated lenses test** (least dissociating, most naturalistic).
3. **Perform the Worth four-dot test** (moderate dissociation).
4. **Perform the after-image test** (maximum dissociation).
5. **Compare results across tests**: ARC is most evident on less dissociated tests and may revert to NRC on highly dissociated tests.

This hierarchy reflects the concept of the **"dissociation spectrum"**: the more a test disrupts normal viewing conditions, the more likely it is to break down the anomalous adaptation and reveal the underlying NRC.

## Differential Considerations

It is essential to distinguish ARC from other sensory adaptations to strabismus:

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Feature |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| **[[Suppression]]** | Complete inhibition of the image from one eye; no binocular interaction. ARC involves active binocular pairing, albeit anomalous. |
| **[[Normal Retinal Correspondence]] with large fusional amplitudes** | Rare; the subjective and objective angles are equal, and the patient maintains true bifoveal fusion through motor vergence. |
| **[[Eccentric fixation]]** | A monocular phenomenon in which the deviating eye uses a non-foveal point for fixation under monocular conditions. ARC is a binocular phenomenon. However, the two frequently coexist. |
| **[[Monofixation syndrome]]** ([[microtropia]])** | A specific clinical entity characterized by a small-angle strabismus (≤ 10 PD), central suppression scotoma, peripheral fusion, and harmonious ARC. It represents a stable endpoint of binocular adaptation. |

## Management and Treatment

The management of ARC is one of the most challenging aspects of clinical [[binocular vision]] care. The approach depends on the patient's age, the type and depth of ARC, the magnitude and type of strabismus, the presence of [[amblyopia]], and the treatment goals.

### Treatment Philosophy: Competing Perspectives

There are two broad philosophical approaches to ARC in clinical practice:

1. **Attempt to eliminate ARC and restore NRC**: This approach aims to break down the anomalous adaptation and establish normal bifoveal correspondence, with the ultimate goal of achieving high-grade stereopsis. This is most feasible in younger patients with recent-onset or shallow ARC.

2. **Accept ARC as a functional adaptation**: This approach recognizes that deeply ingrained ARC (particularly HARC in long-standing infantile esotropia) may be essentially irreversible and that attempts to disrupt it may result in intractable diplopia without achieving NRC. In such cases, the goal may be to optimize the existing anomalous binocular vision or to achieve an acceptable cosmetic result.

### Optical Correction

- **Full [[cycloplegic refraction]]** correction is the first step in any strabismus management. In patients with [[accommodative esotropia]], correction of [[hyperopia]] may reduce or eliminate the deviation, potentially allowing NRC to be re-established if ARC has not become deeply ingrained.
- **[[Prism]] correction**: Prisms equal to the objective angle of deviation can be prescribed to optically realign the visual axes. However, in patients with ARC, the anomalous correspondence may "eat up" the prism (the subjective angle shifts to maintain the anomalous pairing), rendering the prism ineffective for restoring NRC. This phenomenon is known as **prism adaptation** in the context of ARC.

### Orthoptic / Vision Therapy

[[Orthoptic]] exercises and [[vision therapy]] represent the primary non-surgical approach to treating ARC, particularly in children within the critical period.

#### Goals of Therapy
1. **Eliminate suppression** in the deviating eye.
2. **Disrupt the anomalous correspondence**.
3. **Establish normal retinal correspondence**.
4. **Develop normal fusional vergence amplitudes**.
5. **Achieve bifoveal fusion and stereopsis**.

#### Therapeutic Techniques

| Technique | Description | Mechanism |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **[[Occlusion therapy]]** | Patching of the fixating eye to treat amblyopia and disrupt the binocular conditions that maintain ARC. | Eliminates the binocular input that sustains the anomalous pairing; improves acuity in the amblyopic eye. |
| **Direct occlusion (inverse occlusion / sector occlusion)** | Strategic occlusion designed to disrupt ARC while promoting use of the fovea of the deviating eye. | Targets the specific retinal area involved in the anomalous pairing. |
| **[[Synoptophore]] exercises** | Training at the objective angle to stimulate the true corresponding points (both foveae) simultaneously. | Provides controlled binocular stimulation at the correct angular relationship to reinforce NRC. |
| **Bi-nasal occlusion** | Opaque tape or occluders placed on the nasal portion of each spectacle lens. | Disrupts the conditions that maintain ARC in esotropia by eliminating the temporal retinal input from the deviating eye that participates in the anomalous pairing. |
| **Red filter / [[anaglyph]] therapy** | A dense red filter is placed before the deviating eye to create a controlled dissociation while maintaining some binocular interaction. | Disrupts ARC by altering the visual input to one eye, making the anomalous pairing less stable. |
| **[[Diplopia awareness training]]** | Encouraging the patient to become aware of physiological diplopia and the diplopic image from the deviating eye. | Breaking suppression is a prerequisite for disrupting ARC; awareness of diplopia indicates that suppression is being overcome. |
| **Anti-suppression exercises** | Various techniques (e.g., [[bar reading]], [[aperture rule]], [[cheiroscope]]) designed to force simultaneous use of both eyes. | Addresses the suppression component that frequently accompanies ARC. |

#### Prognosis of Vision Therapy for ARC

The prognosis for eliminating ARC through vision therapy depends heavily on:

- **Age**: Younger patients (within the critical period) have a significantly better prognosis.
- **Type of ARC**: Unharmonious ARC is generally more amenable to treatment than harmonious ARC, as the incomplete adaptation suggests residual NRC potential.
- **Depth of ARC**: Shallow ARC (small angle of anomaly) is easier to disrupt than deep ARC.
- **Duration**: Recent-onset ARC is more treatable than long-standing ARC.
- **Constancy**: ARC associated with intermittent strabismus may be less deeply ingrained.
- **Motor alignment**: Successful treatment of ARC requires that the motor deviation be corrected (optically, surgically, or through vergence therapy) to provide the geometric conditions necessary for NRC.

### Surgical Management

[[Strabismus surgery]] is frequently indicated in patients with ARC, particularly when the deviation is too large for optical or orthoptic correction alone.

#### Surgical Goals in the Context of ARC

1. **Cosmetic alignment**: Achieving an acceptable cosmetic appearance is a valid and important goal, even if NRC cannot be restored.
2. **Facilitating NRC**: By reducing or eliminating the objective angle of deviation, surgery creates the geometric conditions necessary for NRC. However, surgery alone does not guarantee that NRC will replace ARC.
3. **Optimizing anomalous binocular vision**: In patients with deeply ingrained HARC, surgery that achieves a small residual deviation may allow the patient to maintain their anomalous binocular vision with peripheral fusion (essentially creating a [[monofixation syndrome]]).

#### Post-Surgical Considerations

- **Post-operative diplopia**: A significant concern in patients with long-standing ARC. If surgery achieves perfect alignment but the cortex retains the anomalous mapping, the patient may experience intractable diplopia because the images now fall on points that the cortex does not recognize as corresponding. This is particularly problematic in older patients.
- **[[Horror fusionis]]**: Some patients with deeply ingrained ARC experience extreme discomfort and an inability to fuse when the eyes are surgically aligned, as the cortex actively resists the imposition of NRC.
- **Prism adaptation testing pre-operatively**: Some surgeons use pre-operative prism adaptation (placing prisms equal to the full deviation for a period before surgery) to assess whether the patient can tolerate alignment and to determine the "true" surgical target.
- **Consecutive deviation**: Over-correction or under-correction may result in a new deviation that disrupts the existing ARC without establishing NRC.

### Pharmacological Interventions

While there are no pharmacological agents that directly treat ARC, certain medications play a role in the broader management:

| Agent | Role |
|-------|------|
| **[[Atropine]] penalization** | Used as an alternative to occlusion therapy for amblyopia treatment. By blurring the fixating eye pharmacologically, atropine penalization can also disrupt the binocular conditions maintaining ARC. |
| **[[Botulinum toxin]] (Botox) injection** | Injection into an [[extraocular muscle]] can temporarily alter the angle of deviation, potentially disrupting ARC and allowing assessment of the patient's binocular potential. Some studies have explored its use as an alternative or adjunct to surgery. |
| **[[Cycloplegic]] agents** | Used diagnostically to determine the full hyperopic refractive error, which is essential for managing accommodative components of esotropia associated with ARC. |

## Special Considerations

### ARC and Microtropia (Monofixation Syndrome)

[[Microtropia]], as described by [[Lang]] and further characterized by [[Parks]] as the [[monofixation syndrome]], represents a stable binocular adaptation in which a small-angle strabismus (typically ≤ 8–10 [[prism diopters]]) coexists with:

- **Harmonious ARC**
- **A central [[suppression scotoma]]** in the deviating eye (typically 2–5° in diameter)
- **Peripheral [[fusional vergence]]**
- **Reduced but measurable [[stereopsis]]** (typically 60–3000 seconds of arc)

Microtropia with HARC is considered by many authorities to be a **stable endpoint** of binocular adaptation that is resistant to further treatment. Many clinicians consider it an acceptable outcome following strabismus surgery, as it provides functional binocular vision with peripheral fusion and some stereopsis.

### ARC in Intermittent Strabismus

In [[intermittent exotropia]], ARC may be present during the tropic phase but NRC is maintained during the phoric phase. This alternation between ARC and NRC suggests that the cortical remapping in intermittent deviations is less deeply ingrained and potentially more amenable to treatment.

### ARC and Amblyopia Treatment

The treatment of [[amblyopia]] in a patient with ARC requires careful sequencing:

1. **Treat amblyopia first** (through occlusion or penalization) to equalize visual acuity.
2. **Address the motor deviation** (through optical correction, surgery, or both).
3. **Attempt to disrupt ARC and establish NRC** (through orthoptic exercises).

Treating amblyopia without addressing the motor deviation and ARC may improve monocular acuity but will not restore normal binocular function.

### Measurement Challenges and Clinical Pitfalls

- **Test-dependent results**: ARC may be demonstrated on one test but not another, depending on the degree of dissociation. Clinicians must use a battery of tests and interpret results in the context of the dissociation spectrum.
- **Variability**: The state of correspondence may fluctuate, particularly in patients with intermittent deviations or those undergoing treatment.
- **False NRC on dissociated tests**: The after-image test may show NRC in a patient who functionally operates with ARC under habitual conditions, because the extreme dissociation breaks down the anomalous adaptation.
- **Confusion with NRC in microtropia**: A patient with microtropia and HARC may appear to have NRC on Bagolini lenses (symmetric X response) because the subjective angle is zero. The cover test is essential to reveal the underlying manifest deviation.

## Prognosis

The long-term prognosis for patients with ARC depends on multiple factors:

| Factor | Favorable Prognosis | Unfavorable Prognosis |
|--------|---------------------|-----------------------|
| **Age at intervention** | < 6 years | > 8 years |
| **Type of ARC** | Unharmonious | Harmonious |
| **Duration of strabismus** | Short | Long |
| **Constancy of deviation** | Intermittent | Constant |
| **Amblyopia** | Mild or absent | Deep |
| **Motor alignment achievable** | Orthotropia or microtropia | Large residual deviation |
| **Patient compliance** | Good | Poor |

In many patients with long-standing, deeply ingrained HARC (particularly those with infantile esotropia), the realistic goal of treatment is not restoration of NRC with high-grade stereopsis, but rather achievement of a stable [[monofixation syndrome]] with acceptable cosmesis, peripheral fusion, and functional (though reduced) stereopsis.

---

## Summary

Anomalous Retinal Correspondence is a complex cortical sensory adaptation to strabismus in which the normal geometric relationship between corresponding retinal points is remapped to accommodate the ocular misalignment. It develops during the critical period of visual development and is most commonly associated with early-onset, constant esotropia. Diagnosis requires a systematic comparison of objective and subjective angles of deviation using a battery of tests spanning the dissociation spectrum. Management is challenging and must be individualized, taking into account the patient's age, the type and depth of ARC, the nature of the strabismus, and realistic treatment goals. While restoration of NRC and high-grade stereopsis is achievable in some patients (particularly young children with recent-onset, unharmonious ARC), many patients with deeply ingrained HARC are best managed with the goal of achieving a stable monofixation syndrome with functional binocular vision.
