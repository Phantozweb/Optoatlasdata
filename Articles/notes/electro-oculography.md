

# Electro-oculography (EOG)

---

## Overview

**Electro-oculography (EOG)** is a non-invasive electrophysiological diagnostic technique used to measure the **standing electrical potential** (corneo-retinal potential) that exists between the [[cornea]] (electrically positive) and the posterior pole of the [[retina]] (electrically negative). This resting potential, generated primarily by the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE), is recorded by placing surface electrodes on the skin adjacent to the eye—typically at the medial and lateral [[canthi]]. The patient performs standardized horizontal eye movements, and the resulting voltage changes are captured over time under both dark-adapted and light-adapted conditions. The ratio of the maximum light-adapted response to the minimum dark-adapted response yields the **Arden ratio** (also known as the **light peak to dark trough ratio**), which serves as the principal clinical metric derived from the EOG.

EOG is an indispensable tool in the clinical assessment of diseases affecting the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] and the interface between the RPE and the [[photoreceptor]] outer segments. It is recognized as a standard electrophysiological test by the **International Society for Clinical Electrophysiology of Vision (ISCEV)**, which publishes guidelines for its standardized performance and interpretation. While the [[electroretinogram]] (ERG) evaluates the function of the neural retina (photoreceptors, bipolar cells, Müller cells), the EOG uniquely and specifically interrogates the functional integrity of the RPE and its metabolic interaction with the photoreceptor layer. This distinction makes EOG the test of choice for certain inherited and acquired RPE disorders, most notably [[Best vitelliform macular dystrophy]] (Best disease).

---

## Historical Background

The existence of a standing electrical potential across the eye was first described by **Emil du Bois-Reymond** in 1849. However, the clinical application of this phenomenon was not realized until the mid-20th century. In 1956, **Geoffrey Arden** and colleagues developed the standardized clinical EOG protocol, introducing the concept of measuring the light rise of the standing potential as a diagnostic parameter. Arden demonstrated that the ratio of the light peak amplitude to the dark trough amplitude—subsequently named the **Arden ratio** in his honor—provided a reliable, quantifiable index of RPE function. This work established EOG as a cornerstone of clinical [[electrophysiology]] in ophthalmology and optometry.

The ISCEV first published standardized guidelines for the clinical EOG in 1993, with subsequent revisions (most recently updated in 2017) to ensure uniformity in recording techniques, stimulus parameters, and interpretation criteria across clinical laboratories worldwide.

## Anatomical and Physiological Basis

### The Corneo-Retinal Potential

The eye functions as an electrical dipole. The [[cornea]] carries a relative positive charge, while the fundus (posterior pole near the [[retinal pigment epithelium]]) carries a relative negative charge. This **corneo-retinal potential** (also called the **standing potential** or **resting potential**) ranges from approximately **0.4 to 1.0 millivolts (mV)** in the normal human eye. The potential is not static; it fluctuates in response to changes in retinal illumination.

### Role of the Retinal Pigment Epithelium

The [[retinal pigment epithelium]] is a monolayer of pigmented cells situated between the [[photoreceptor]] outer segments and [[Bruch's membrane]]/[[choroid]]. The RPE performs numerous critical functions including:

- Phagocytosis of shed photoreceptor outer segment discs
- Transport of nutrients and metabolites between the [[choroidal vasculature]] and the photoreceptors
- Absorption of stray light
- Maintenance of the [[blood-retinal barrier]] (outer blood-retinal barrier)
- Isomerization of all-trans-retinal to 11-cis-retinal in the [[visual cycle]]
- Ion and fluid transport

The standing potential is generated primarily by the **differential distribution of ion channels and transporters** on the apical (photoreceptor-facing) and basal (Bruch's membrane-facing) membranes of the RPE cells. Specifically:

| Membrane | Key Channels/Transporters | Function |
|----------|--------------------------|----------|
| **Apical membrane** | Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, K⁺ channels, Cl⁻ channels, bestrophin-1 (BEST1) | Maintains apical membrane potential; mediates light-evoked Cl⁻ conductance changes |
| **Basal membrane** | Cl⁻ channels (including [[bestrophin-1]]), K⁺ channels, inwardly rectifying K⁺ channels | Generates basolateral membrane depolarization during light adaptation |

### The Light Rise Mechanism

When the retina is exposed to light after a period of dark adaptation, [[photoreceptors]] undergo hyperpolarization and reduce their release of [[glutamate]]. This triggers a cascade of biochemical events at the RPE-photoreceptor interface:

1. **Dark adaptation phase**: In darkness, the subretinal space composition reaches a steady state. The standing potential gradually decreases to a minimum value—the **dark trough**—typically reached after approximately **8–12 minutes** of dark adaptation.

2. **Light adaptation phase**: Upon exposure to a diffuse, uniform light stimulus, photoreceptor activity generates chemical signals (likely involving a decrease in subretinal K⁺ concentration and changes in other metabolites) that are transduced by the RPE.

3. **RPE depolarization**: The RPE basolateral membrane depolarizes in response to these signals. This depolarization is mediated in large part by **chloride channels**, particularly the **[[bestrophin-1]] (BEST1) protein**, encoded by the *BEST1* gene (formerly *VMD2*). The depolarization of the basolateral membrane relative to the apical membrane increases the transepithelial potential.

4. **Light peak**: The standing potential rises to a maximum value—the **light peak**—typically reached approximately **7–12 minutes** after the onset of light adaptation.

The **Arden ratio** is calculated as:

> **Arden Ratio = Light Peak Amplitude / Dark Trough Amplitude**

A normal Arden ratio is generally considered to be **≥ 1.80** (or ≥ 1.85 in some laboratories), though reference ranges may vary slightly between clinical facilities.

| Arden Ratio | Interpretation |
|-------------|---------------|
| ≥ 1.80 – 1.85 | **Normal** |
| 1.60 – 1.80 | **Subnormal / Borderline** |
| 1.50 – 1.60 | **Abnormal** |
| < 1.50 | **Markedly abnormal** |
| ~1.0 (absent light rise) | **Abolished EOG** – severe RPE dysfunction |

## Pathophysiology

The EOG light rise is fundamentally dependent on:

1. **Intact photoreceptor function** – Photoreceptors must be capable of responding to light to generate the chemical signals that modulate RPE membrane potentials.
2. **Intact RPE function** – The RPE must possess functional ion channels (especially bestrophin-1 Cl⁻ channels) and transporters to generate the transepithelial potential change.
3. **Normal RPE-photoreceptor interface** – The subretinal space must allow normal metabolic communication between photoreceptors and RPE.

### Conditions Causing Abnormal EOG

#### Primary RPE Dysfunction (Abnormal EOG with Normal or Near-Normal ERG)

The hallmark clinical scenario in which the EOG is diagnostically indispensable is when the RPE is selectively or disproportionately affected while photoreceptor function (as measured by [[electroretinogram|ERG]]) remains relatively preserved.

- **[[Best vitelliform macular dystrophy]] (Best disease)**: This is the prototypical condition diagnosed by EOG. Caused by mutations in the *[[BEST1]]* gene (chromosome 11q12-q13), Best disease is an [[autosomal dominant]] macular dystrophy characterized by bilateral, symmetric, egg-yolk-like (vitelliform) lesions in the [[macula]]. The EOG is **markedly abnormal** (Arden ratio typically < 1.50, often approaching 1.0) even in early stages when visual acuity may be normal and the ERG is entirely normal. Importantly, **carriers of the BEST1 mutation who have no visible fundus lesions will still demonstrate an abnormal EOG**, making it an essential tool for genetic screening and family studies.

- **[[Adult-onset vitelliform macular dystrophy]]**: Also known as adult-onset foveomacular vitelliform dystrophy or pattern dystrophy with vitelliform lesions. The EOG may be normal or mildly subnormal, helping to distinguish this entity from classic Best disease.

- **[[Bestrophinopathies]]**: A spectrum of diseases caused by *BEST1* mutations, including Best disease, [[autosomal recessive bestrophinopathy]] (ARB), [[autosomal dominant vitreoretinochoroidopathy]] (ADVIRC), and [[retinitis pigmentosa]] associated with *BEST1* mutations. All may demonstrate abnormal EOG findings.

#### Secondary RPE Dysfunction (Abnormal EOG with Abnormal ERG)

In conditions where both the photoreceptors and the RPE are affected, both the ERG and EOG will be abnormal. In these cases, the EOG abnormality may be partly secondary to photoreceptor loss (since the light rise requires photoreceptor input) and partly due to direct RPE damage.

- **[[Retinitis pigmentosa]]**: Generalized photoreceptor degeneration leads to reduced ERG responses and a reduced or abolished EOG light rise.
- **[[Choroideremia]]**: Progressive degeneration of the [[choroid]], RPE, and photoreceptors results in abnormal EOG.
- **[[Toxic maculopathies]]**: Certain medications, notably [[chloroquine]] and [[hydroxychloroquine]], can damage the RPE and photoreceptors, leading to abnormal EOG findings. EOG may serve as an adjunctive test in monitoring for [[hydroxychloroquine retinopathy]], though [[optical coherence tomography]] (OCT) and [[multifocal electroretinogram]] (mfERG) are more commonly used in current screening protocols.
- **[[Siderosis bulbi]]**: Intraocular iron foreign bodies can cause progressive RPE and retinal toxicity with abnormal EOG.
- **Extensive [[retinal detachment]]**: Separation of the neurosensory retina from the RPE disrupts the RPE-photoreceptor interface, reducing the EOG light rise.

#### Conditions with Supranormal EOG (Elevated Light Rise)

Rarely, the EOG light rise may be supranormal:

- **[[Albinism]]**: Some patients with [[oculocutaneous albinism]] or [[ocular albinism]] demonstrate an enhanced light rise, possibly due to increased light penetration through the hypopigmented RPE.

## Clinical Indications

The primary clinical indications for performing an EOG include:

1. **Diagnosis of [[Best vitelliform macular dystrophy]]** – The single most important indication. An abnormal EOG in the setting of a normal full-field ERG is virtually pathognomonic.
2. **Screening of family members** in known Best disease pedigrees – Asymptomatic carriers with normal fundi will have abnormal EOGs.
3. **Differentiation of Best disease from other vitelliform or pattern dystrophies** – Adult-onset vitelliform dystrophy typically has a normal or only mildly reduced EOG.
4. **Assessment of generalized RPE function** in suspected RPE dystrophies or toxic retinopathies.
5. **Evaluation of unexplained visual loss** when RPE disease is suspected.
6. **Monitoring RPE function** in patients on potentially retinotoxic medications (adjunctive role).
7. **Pre-operative assessment** in certain retinal conditions.

## Diagnostic Technique and Protocol

### Patient Preparation

- The patient should be informed about the procedure and provide consent.
- [[Pupil]] dilation is **not required** for standard EOG, though some protocols may use dilated pupils for consistency.
- The patient should be comfortably seated in a room that can be completely darkened and uniformly illuminated.
- Skin at the electrode sites (medial and lateral canthi of each eye) should be cleaned and gently abraded to reduce impedance.

### Electrode Placement

| Electrode | Position | Purpose |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Active (recording) electrodes** | Skin overlying the medial and lateral canthi of each eye (one pair per eye) | Detect voltage changes as the eye rotates |
| **Ground (reference) electrode** | Forehead or earlobe | Provides electrical reference |

Standard **Ag/AgCl (silver/silver chloride) skin electrodes** are used. Electrode impedance should be maintained below **5 kΩ** (ideally < 3 kΩ).

### Recording Protocol (ISCEV Standard)

The ISCEV-standardized EOG protocol consists of the following phases:

1. **Pre-adaptation period**: The patient is adapted to a moderate room light level for at least 15 minutes prior to testing to establish a stable baseline.

2. **Dark phase (12 minutes)**: The room lights are extinguished. The patient performs standardized horizontal saccadic eye movements (typically alternating fixation between two red fixation LEDs placed at a known angular separation, usually **±15° from primary gaze**, i.e., 30° total excursion). Eye movements are performed for approximately **10 seconds every minute** (or every 2 minutes, per laboratory protocol). The amplitude of the resulting EOG signal is recorded at each time point. The **dark trough** is identified as the minimum amplitude during this phase.

3. **Light phase (12 minutes)**: A uniform, diffuse background light (Ganzfeld stimulus) is turned on. The standard luminance is **100 cd/m²** (candelas per square meter). The patient continues the same saccadic eye movements at the same intervals. The amplitude is recorded at each time point. The **light peak** is identified as the maximum amplitude during this phase.

4. **Calculation**: The Arden ratio is computed as the ratio of the light peak amplitude to the dark trough amplitude.

### Signal Characteristics

- The EOG signal amplitude is typically in the range of **50–600 microvolts (µV)** for the saccadic excursions.
- The signal is a **slow, DC-like potential** that changes over minutes, not milliseconds (unlike the ERG).
- Amplifiers with appropriate low-frequency bandwidth (DC to ~30 Hz) and high input impedance are required.

### Sources of Artifact and Error

| Source | Effect | Mitigation |
|--------|--------|------------|
| Poor electrode contact / high impedance | Noisy, unreliable recordings | Proper skin preparation; impedance < 5 kΩ |
| Inconsistent eye movements | Variable amplitudes | Clear fixation targets; patient coaching |
| Incomplete dark adaptation | Artificially elevated dark trough | Ensure full 12-minute dark phase |
| Pupil size variation | Altered retinal illuminance during light phase | Consider mydriasis for standardization |
| Eyelid closure / blinking | Signal artifacts | Instruct patient to minimize blinking during recording epochs |
| Medications (e.g., [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]], [[acetazolamide]]) | May alter RPE ion transport and EOG | Document all medications |
| Age | Arden ratio may decline slightly with age | Use age-matched normative data |

## Interpretation of Results

### Normal EOG

A normal EOG demonstrates:
- A clear decline in standing potential during the dark phase, reaching a dark trough at approximately 8–12 minutes.
- A robust rise in standing potential during the light phase, reaching a light peak at approximately 7–12 minutes.
- An **Arden ratio ≥ 1.80** (laboratory-specific norms apply).

### Abnormal EOG Patterns

| Pattern | Arden Ratio | ERG Status | Likely Diagnosis |
|---------|-------------|------------|------------------|
| Markedly reduced/absent light rise | < 1.50 (often ~1.0) | **Normal** | [[Best vitelliform macular dystrophy]], [[bestrophinopathy]] |
| Reduced light rise | 1.50 – 1.80 | **Normal** | Carrier state of Best disease; early/mild RPE dysfunction; adult-onset vitelliform dystrophy |
| Reduced light rise | < 1.50 | **Abnormal** | [[Retinitis pigmentosa]], [[choroideremia]], advanced toxic retinopathy, widespread RPE atrophy |
| Supranormal light rise | > 2.50 – 3.00 | Variable | [[Albinism]], some normal variants |
| Normal | ≥ 1.80 | Normal | Normal RPE and photoreceptor function |

### The Fast Oscillation (FO) and Other Components

In addition to the standard light peak (slow oscillation), the EOG also contains a **fast oscillation (FO)** component, which occurs within the first 60–75 seconds of light onset or offset. The fast oscillation is generated by different mechanisms than the slow light rise and is thought to reflect apical membrane conductance changes in the RPE. While the FO is not routinely measured in standard clinical EOG, it may provide additional diagnostic information in certain research settings. Notably, the FO may be **normal in Best disease** even when the slow light rise is abolished, suggesting that the BEST1/bestrophin-1 channel is specifically involved in the slow oscillation pathway.

## Relationship to Other Electrophysiological Tests

| Test | Primary Structure Assessed | Relationship to EOG |
|------|---------------------------|---------------------|
| **[[Electroretinogram]] (full-field ERG)** | Photoreceptors (rods, cones), bipolar cells, Müller cells | ERG assesses neural retina; EOG assesses RPE. A normal ERG with abnormal EOG = selective RPE disease (e.g., Best disease). |
| **[[Multifocal electroretinogram]] (mfERG)** | Localized cone-driven retinal function (primarily macular) | mfERG may show localized macular dysfunction in Best disease; EOG provides generalized RPE assessment. |
| **[[Visual evoked potential]] (VEP)** | Visual pathway from retina to [[visual cortex]] | VEP assesses post-retinal pathway; not directly related to RPE function. |
| **[[Electroretinogram#Pattern ERG|Pattern ERG]] (PERG)** | [[Retinal ganglion cells]] and macular function | PERG may be abnormal in macular disease associated with RPE dysfunction. |

## Clinical Significance in Specific Conditions

### Best Vitelliform Macular Dystrophy (BVMD)

[[Best vitelliform macular dystrophy]] remains the condition for which EOG is most clinically critical. Key points:

- **Inheritance**: [[Autosomal dominant]] with variable expressivity and incomplete penetrance.
- **Gene**: *[[BEST1]]* (previously *VMD2*), encoding the bestrophin-1 chloride channel protein located on the basolateral membrane of the RPE.
- **EOG finding**: Markedly abnormal Arden ratio (typically 1.0–1.30), often **abolished light rise**.
- **ERG finding**: **Normal** full-field ERG in early and moderate stages.
- **Clinical staging**:

| Stage | Fundus Appearance | Visual Acuity | EOG |
|-------|-------------------|---------------|-----|
| **Previtelliform (Stage 0)** | Normal fundus | Normal | **Abnormal** |
| **Vitelliform (Stage II)** | Classic "egg-yolk" lesion | Normal to mildly reduced | **Abnormal** |
| **Pseudohypopyon (Stage III)** | Layering of material within lesion | Variable | **Abnormal** |
| **Vitelliruptive (Stage IV)** | "Scrambled egg" appearance | Reduced | **Abnormal** |
| **Atrophic (Stage V)** | RPE atrophy, possible [[choroidal neovascularization]] | Significantly reduced | **Abnormal** |

The EOG is abnormal at **all stages**, including the previtelliform stage when the fundus appears entirely normal. This makes EOG invaluable for:
- Confirming the diagnosis in patients with vitelliform lesions
- Identifying asymptomatic gene carriers in affected families
- Distinguishing Best disease from phenocopies (e.g., adult-onset vitelliform dystrophy, which typically has a normal or only mildly reduced EOG)

### Genetic Confirmation

While EOG remains the functional gold standard for diagnosing Best disease, **molecular genetic testing** for *BEST1* mutations is now widely available and provides definitive confirmation. However, EOG retains its clinical role because:
- Genetic testing may identify variants of uncertain significance (VUS)
- EOG provides functional confirmation of pathogenicity
- EOG can rapidly screen multiple family members
- Some bestrophinopathies may involve novel or uncharacterized mutations

## Limitations of Electro-oculography

Despite its clinical utility, EOG has several recognized limitations:

1. **Low spatial resolution**: EOG measures the global, summed RPE response across the entire retina. It cannot localize focal RPE lesions. A small macular lesion may not significantly alter the Arden ratio.
2. **Dependence on photoreceptor function**: Because the light rise requires intact photoreceptor signaling, any condition that severely reduces photoreceptor function (e.g., advanced [[retinitis pigmentosa]]) will secondarily reduce the EOG, making it impossible to determine whether the RPE is independently affected.
3. **Patient cooperation**: The test requires the patient to perform consistent, accurate saccadic eye movements for approximately 24–30 minutes. This can be challenging for young children, elderly patients, or those with cognitive impairment or [[nystagmus]].
4. **Variability**: Inter-session and inter-individual variability can be significant. Careful standardization and use of laboratory-specific normative data are essential.
5. **Limited disease specificity**: An abnormal EOG indicates RPE dysfunction but does not specify the etiology. Clinical correlation with fundus examination, [[optical coherence tomography]] (OCT), [[fundus autofluorescence]] (FAF), and genetic testing is necessary.

## Advances and Modern Context

### EOG in the Era of Multimodal Imaging

Modern retinal imaging technologies—including [[spectral-domain OCT]], [[enhanced depth imaging OCT]], [[fundus autofluorescence]], and [[OCT angiography]]—have dramatically improved the ability to visualize RPE structure and detect subtle abnormalities. However, these imaging modalities assess **structure**, not **function**. EOG remains the only clinical test that directly and specifically measures **RPE functional integrity** in vivo. Therefore, EOG and modern imaging are complementary:

- **FAF** can reveal lipofuscin accumulation patterns in Best disease and other RPE disorders.
- **OCT** can demonstrate subretinal fluid, vitelliform material, and RPE detachments.
- **EOG** confirms that the RPE is functionally abnormal, even when structural changes are subtle or absent.

### Emerging Research Applications

- **Gene therapy trials**: As gene therapy for *BEST1*-related diseases advances, EOG may serve as a functional biomarker to assess treatment efficacy.
- **Pharmacological modulation**: Research into drugs that modulate RPE ion channel function may use EOG as an outcome measure.
- **Stem cell-derived RPE transplantation**: EOG could theoretically be used to assess the functional integration of transplanted RPE cells, though spatial resolution limitations apply.

---

## EOG in Non-Ophthalmic Applications

While this article focuses on the ophthalmic application of EOG, it is worth noting that the same principle of measuring the corneo-retinal potential is used in other fields:

- **[[Polysomnography]]**: EOG is a standard component of sleep studies, where it is used to detect [[rapid eye movement]] (REM) sleep by recording eye movements.
- **Human-computer interaction**: EOG-based eye tracking systems have been developed for assistive technology in patients with severe motor disabilities.
- **Vestibular testing**: Eye movement recordings using EOG principles are used in [[electronystagmography]] (ENG) for evaluating [[vestibular]] disorders.

In these non-ophthalmic contexts, the EOG is used purely as an **eye movement detector** and does not involve the dark/light adaptation protocol used in clinical ophthalmology.

---

## Summary

Electro-oculography is a well-established, ISCEV-standardized electrophysiological test that uniquely evaluates the functional integrity of the [[retinal pigment epithelium]]. Its principal clinical application is in the diagnosis and family screening of [[Best vitelliform macular dystrophy]] and other [[bestrophinopathies]], where it demonstrates a characteristically abnormal Arden ratio in the presence of a normal [[electroretinogram]]. While modern imaging modalities have expanded the diagnostic toolkit for RPE diseases, EOG remains irreplaceable as a direct measure of RPE function. Proper technique, adherence to ISCEV standards, and integration with clinical findings, imaging, and genetic testing are essential for optimal diagnostic utility.
