

# Iris Bombé

**Iris bombé** is a serious ophthalmic condition characterized by the anterior bowing or convex displacement of the [[iris]] toward the [[cornea]], resulting from the accumulation of [[aqueous humor]] in the [[posterior chamber]] of the eye. This pathological configuration arises when the normal flow of aqueous humor from the posterior chamber through the [[pupil]] into the [[anterior chamber]] is obstructed, most commonly due to the formation of [[posterior synechiae]] — adhesions between the posterior surface of the iris and the [[anterior lens capsule]]. The resultant pressure differential causes the peripheral iris to balloon forward, potentially occluding the [[trabecular meshwork]] and [[iridocorneal angle]], leading to acute or chronic [[secondary angle-closure glaucoma]]. Iris bombé constitutes an ophthalmic emergency when associated with acute elevations in [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) and demands prompt recognition and intervention to prevent irreversible [[optic nerve]] damage and permanent vision loss.

---

## Etymology and Terminology

The term *bombé* derives from the French word meaning "convex" or "bulging," aptly describing the dome-shaped anterior displacement of the iris. In clinical literature, iris bombé is also referred to as **iris bombe syndrome**, **pupillary block with iris bombé**, or simply as a manifestation of **pupillary block glaucoma**. It is classified under the broader category of [[secondary angle-closure glaucoma]] mechanisms and is distinguished from [[primary angle-closure glaucoma]] by its identifiable underlying etiology — most frequently inflammatory in nature.

---

## Epidemiology

Iris bombé is not a primary disease entity but rather a complication of other ocular pathologies. Its incidence is therefore closely tied to the prevalence and management of its underlying causes. Key epidemiological considerations include:

- **Association with anterior uveitis**: Iris bombé most commonly develops as a complication of chronic or recurrent [[anterior uveitis]] (iridocyclitis). Populations with higher prevalence of uveitis — including patients with [[HLA-B27]]-associated spondyloarthropathies, [[sarcoidosis]], [[Behçet disease]], [[juvenile idiopathic arthritis]] (JIA), and [[herpetic uveitis]] — are at increased risk.
- **Postoperative occurrence**: Patients who have undergone [[cataract surgery]], [[penetrating keratoplasty]], or other intraocular procedures may develop iris bombé secondary to postoperative inflammation and synechiae formation.
- **Pediatric considerations**: Children with JIA-associated uveitis are particularly vulnerable due to the often insidious, chronic nature of their inflammation and the challenges of regular monitoring.
- **Geographic and demographic variation**: The condition is observed worldwide, with no definitive sex predilection, though the underlying etiologies may show demographic patterns (e.g., HLA-B27-associated uveitis is more common in young males; JIA-associated uveitis is more common in young females).

| Risk Factor | Associated Condition | Relative Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Chronic anterior uveitis | [[Sarcoidosis]], [[Behçet disease]], [[HLA-B27]] spondyloarthropathy | High |
| Juvenile idiopathic arthritis | Chronic iridocyclitis in children | High |
| Herpetic eye disease | [[Herpes simplex virus]] (HSV), [[Varicella-zoster virus]] (VZV) keratouveitis | Moderate–High |
| Prior intraocular surgery | [[Cataract extraction]], [[penetrating keratoplasty]], [[vitrectomy]] | Moderate |
| Inadequate cycloplegia during uveitis treatment | Failure to prevent posterior synechiae | Moderate |
| Trauma | Blunt or penetrating [[ocular trauma]] with secondary inflammation | Moderate |
| Intraocular tumors | [[Iris melanoma]], [[ciliary body melanoma]], [[retinoblastoma]] | Low–Moderate |

## Anatomy and Aqueous Humor Dynamics

A thorough understanding of iris bombé requires familiarity with the normal anatomy and physiology of [[aqueous humor]] production and drainage.

### Normal Aqueous Humor Pathway

1. **Production**: Aqueous humor is actively secreted by the non-pigmented epithelium of the [[ciliary body]] (specifically the [[ciliary processes]]) into the [[posterior chamber]].
2. **Flow through the pupil**: Aqueous humor flows from the posterior chamber, between the posterior iris surface and the anterior lens capsule, through the [[pupil]] into the [[anterior chamber]].
3. **Drainage via the conventional pathway**: Approximately 80–90% of aqueous humor exits through the [[trabecular meshwork]], into [[Schlemm's canal]], and subsequently into the [[episcleral venous system]].
4. **Drainage via the uveoscleral pathway**: The remaining 10–20% drains through the [[ciliary body]] interstitial spaces into the [[suprachoroidal space]] (the [[uveoscleral outflow]] pathway).

### Disruption in Iris Bombé

In iris bombé, the critical disruption occurs at **step 2** — the passage of aqueous humor through the pupil. When 360 degrees of [[posterior synechiae]] (termed **seclusio pupillae**) form, the pupillary aperture is completely sealed. Aqueous humor continues to be produced by the ciliary body but cannot pass into the anterior chamber. The resulting pressure buildup in the posterior chamber pushes the iris anteriorly, creating the characteristic convex, dome-shaped configuration. This anterior displacement of the peripheral iris appositionally closes the [[iridocorneal angle]], obstructing aqueous outflow through the trabecular meshwork and precipitating a dramatic rise in [[intraocular pressure]].

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of iris bombé is a sequential cascade of events that can be delineated as follows:

### Step 1: Initiating Inflammatory or Structural Insult

The process typically begins with an inflammatory insult to the anterior segment. [[Anterior uveitis]] causes the release of [[inflammatory mediators]], [[fibrin]], and [[proteins]] into the aqueous humor. These inflammatory products promote adhesion between the posterior iris pigment epithelium and the anterior lens capsule. Other initiating events include:

- **Post-surgical inflammation**: Following cataract surgery or keratoplasty, fibrinous exudates may form adhesions.
- **Trauma**: Blunt or penetrating ocular trauma can cause inflammation and hemorrhage, promoting synechiae.
- **Neovascularization**: In conditions such as [[proliferative diabetic retinopathy]] or [[central retinal vein occlusion]], [[neovascularization of the iris]] ([[rubeosis iridis]]) can lead to the formation of synechiae and angle closure through a related but distinct mechanism ([[neovascular glaucoma]]).
- **Intraocular tumors**: Mass lesions can physically obstruct aqueous flow or incite inflammation.

### Step 2: Formation of Posterior Synechiae

[[Posterior synechiae]] are fibrous adhesions between the iris and the lens. They may be:

- **Focal (segmental)**: Involving only a portion of the pupillary margin. Focal synechiae alone do not typically cause iris bombé, as aqueous can still flow through the unaffected portions of the pupil.
- **Annular (360-degree, seclusio pupillae)**: Complete circumferential adhesion of the pupillary margin to the lens capsule. This is the critical prerequisite for iris bombé, as it completely blocks the pupillary passage of aqueous humor.

In some cases, an **occlusio pupillae** — a fibrous or inflammatory membrane occluding the entire pupillary aperture — may coexist with or substitute for seclusio pupillae, further impeding aqueous flow.

### Step 3: Posterior Chamber Pressure Buildup

With the pupil sealed, aqueous humor produced by the ciliary body accumulates in the posterior chamber. The pressure in the posterior chamber progressively exceeds that in the anterior chamber, creating a **trans-iris pressure gradient**.

### Step 4: Anterior Bowing of the Iris

The iris, being a thin and relatively compliant diaphragm, responds to the pressure differential by bowing anteriorly. The degree of bowing depends on:

- The magnitude of the pressure differential
- The rigidity and thickness of the iris stroma
- The duration of the obstruction
- The rate of aqueous humor production

### Step 5: Iridocorneal Angle Closure

The anteriorly displaced peripheral iris comes into apposition with the [[corneal endothelium]] and the trabecular meshwork, mechanically obstructing aqueous outflow. This results in:

- **Acute angle closure**: Sudden, dramatic IOP elevation (often >40–60 mmHg)
- **Chronic angle closure**: Gradual, progressive angle narrowing with formation of [[peripheral anterior synechiae]] (PAS), leading to sustained IOP elevation

### Step 6: Secondary Glaucomatous Damage

Elevated IOP causes damage to the [[retinal ganglion cells]] and [[optic nerve head]], resulting in [[glaucomatous optic neuropathy]]. If untreated, this leads to progressive, irreversible [[visual field]] loss and potentially complete blindness.

| Pathophysiological Stage | Mechanism | Clinical Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Inflammatory insult | Release of fibrin, proteins, inflammatory cells | Aqueous flare, cells, keratic precipitates |
| Posterior synechiae formation | Adhesion of iris to lens capsule | Irregular pupil, impaired dilation |
| Seclusio pupillae (360°) | Complete pupillary block | Aqueous trapped in posterior chamber |
| Posterior chamber pressure rise | Aqueous accumulation behind iris | Trans-iris pressure gradient |
| Iris bombé | Anterior bowing of iris | Shallow anterior chamber, iris-cornea touch |
| Angle closure | Trabecular meshwork obstruction | Elevated IOP |
| Optic neuropathy | Retinal ganglion cell death | Visual field loss, optic disc cupping |

## Etiology

The etiologies of iris bombé can be broadly categorized as follows:

### Inflammatory Causes (Most Common)

- **[[Anterior uveitis]]** (iridocyclitis) of any etiology:
  - [[HLA-B27]]-associated acute anterior uveitis
  - [[Sarcoidosis]]-associated granulomatous uveitis
  - [[Behçet disease]]
  - [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]]
  - [[Fuchs heterochromic iridocyclitis]] (less commonly, as this condition typically does not form synechiae)
  - [[Juvenile idiopathic arthritis]]-associated chronic anterior uveitis
  - [[Herpetic anterior uveitis]] (HSV, VZV)
  - [[Tuberculosis]]-associated uveitis
  - [[Syphilitic uveitis]]
  - [[Lens-induced uveitis]] ([[phacoantigenic uveitis]])

### Post-Surgical Causes

- Post-[[cataract surgery]] inflammation (especially in eyes with pre-existing uveitis)
- Post-[[penetrating keratoplasty]]
- Post-[[glaucoma surgery]] (e.g., after [[trabeculectomy]] with excessive inflammation)
- Post-[[vitrectomy]]
- Post-[[intraocular lens]] (IOL) implantation with chronic inflammation

### Traumatic Causes

- Blunt [[ocular trauma]] with secondary iritis
- Penetrating ocular injury
- Chemical burns with secondary anterior segment inflammation

### Neoplastic Causes

- [[Iris melanoma]]
- [[Ciliary body melanoma]]
- [[Retinoblastoma]] (in pediatric patients)
- [[Medulloepithelioma]]
- Metastatic tumors to the iris or ciliary body

### Other Causes

- [[Nanophthalmos]] (predisposition due to small eye anatomy)
- [[Intumescent cataract]] (swollen lens pushing iris forward — a relative pupillary block mechanism)
- [[Silicone oil]] migration into the anterior chamber after vitreoretinal surgery
- [[Aqueous misdirection syndrome]] ([[malignant glaucoma]]) — a related but distinct entity involving posterior aqueous misdirection

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

Patients with iris bombé may present with a spectrum of symptoms depending on the acuity and severity of the condition:

| Symptom | Description | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| **Ocular pain** | Moderate to severe, deep aching pain; may be periorbital | Very common in acute presentations |
| **Blurred vision** | Decreased visual acuity due to corneal edema, elevated IOP, or underlying uveitis | Very common |
| **Photophobia** | Light sensitivity, often associated with underlying uveitis | Common |
| **Halos around lights** | Due to [[corneal edema]] from elevated IOP | Common in acute angle closure |
| **Redness** | [[Conjunctival injection]], [[ciliary flush]] | Common |
| **Tearing** | Reflex [[lacrimation]] secondary to pain and irritation | Common |
| **Headache** | Ipsilateral frontal or temporal headache | Moderate frequency |
| **Nausea and vomiting** | Vagal response to acute IOP elevation | Occasional (in acute presentations) |

In chronic or subacute cases, patients may be relatively asymptomatic until significant visual field loss has occurred, particularly in the pediatric population where JIA-associated uveitis may be clinically silent.

### Signs

#### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy Findings

- **Anterior bowing of the iris**: The hallmark sign. The iris assumes a convex, dome-shaped configuration, with the peripheral iris displaced anteriorly toward the cornea. The central iris may appear relatively flat or even posteriorly displaced if the pupillary margin is firmly adherent to the lens.
- **Shallow anterior chamber**: The peripheral anterior chamber depth is markedly reduced. In severe cases, the peripheral iris may be in direct contact with the corneal endothelium ([[iridocorneal touch]]).
- **[[Posterior synechiae]]**: Visible adhesions between the pupillary margin and the anterior lens capsule. The pupil may appear irregular, festooned, or fixed in a mid-dilated position. In seclusio pupillae, the entire pupillary margin is bound to the lens.
- **[[Occlusio pupillae]]**: A fibrous or inflammatory membrane may be visible across the pupillary aperture.
- **[[Aqueous flare]] and cells**: Indicative of active or prior anterior uveitis. Graded on the [[Standardization of Uveitis Nomenclature]] (SUN) scale.
- **[[Keratic precipitates]] (KPs)**: Inflammatory deposits on the corneal endothelium. May be fine (non-granulomatous) or mutton-fat (granulomatous) depending on the underlying etiology.
- **[[Corneal edema]]**: Microcystic or stromal edema secondary to elevated IOP or endothelial compromise from iridocorneal touch.
- **[[Iris atrophy]]**: May be present in chronic cases or in herpetic uveitis.
- **[[Fibrin]] in the anterior chamber**: Particularly in acute or severe inflammatory episodes.
- **[[Rubeosis iridis]]**: Neovascularization of the iris, if the underlying cause involves ischemic retinal disease.

#### Gonioscopy Findings

- **Appositional angle closure**: The anteriorly displaced iris is in contact with the trabecular meshwork, occluding the [[iridocorneal angle]]. This may be 360 degrees or segmental.
- **[[Peripheral anterior synechiae]] (PAS)**: In chronic cases, the prolonged iridotrabecular contact leads to the formation of permanent adhesions between the peripheral iris and the angle structures. PAS indicate irreversible angle damage.
- **Narrow or closed angle**: [[Shaffer grading]] or [[Spaeth grading]] systems may be used to document the degree of angle narrowing.

#### Intraocular Pressure

- IOP is typically **elevated**, often markedly so (>30 mmHg, and frequently >40–60 mmHg in acute presentations).
- In some cases, IOP may be paradoxically normal or low if the ciliary body is severely inflamed and aqueous production is suppressed ([[ciliary body shutdown]]).

#### Fundoscopy

- **[[Optic disc]] assessment**: May reveal [[optic disc cupping]], [[disc hemorrhage]], or [[pallor]] in cases with established glaucomatous damage.
- **[[Vitreous]] haze**: May be present if there is associated intermediate or posterior uveitis.
- Fundoscopic examination may be limited by corneal edema, shallow anterior chamber, or posterior synechiae.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis

The diagnosis of iris bombé is primarily **clinical**, based on the characteristic slit-lamp biomicroscopic appearance of anterior iris bowing in the setting of posterior synechiae and elevated IOP. A systematic diagnostic approach includes:

1. **Comprehensive history**: Duration and nature of symptoms, history of uveitis, prior ocular surgery or trauma, systemic inflammatory conditions, and medication history (particularly [[corticosteroid]] use).
2. **Visual acuity assessment**: [[Best-corrected visual acuity]] (BCVA) using a [[Snellen chart]] or [[LogMAR chart]].
3. **Slit-lamp biomicroscopy**: Detailed anterior segment examination to identify iris bombé, posterior synechiae, anterior chamber depth, inflammatory signs, and corneal status.
4. **[[Intraocular pressure]] measurement**: Using [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]] (gold standard), [[Tono-Pen]], or [[iCare]] rebound tonometry.
5. **[[Gonioscopy]]**: Essential to assess the iridocorneal angle. [[Indentation gonioscopy]] (using a [[Posner lens]], [[Sussman lens]], or [[Zeiss lens]]) can help differentiate appositional closure (reversible) from synechial closure (irreversible with PAS).
6. **Dilated fundus examination**: When possible, to assess the optic nerve head and retina. May require deferral if the anterior chamber is extremely shallow.

### Imaging Modalities

| Imaging Modality | Role in Iris Bombé | Key Findings |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Anterior segment optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT)** | Non-contact, high-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the anterior segment | Anterior iris bowing, shallow anterior chamber, iridocorneal contact, quantification of angle closure |
| **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | High-frequency (35–50 MHz) ultrasound imaging of the anterior segment, including structures behind the iris | Posterior synechiae, iris-lens contact, ciliary body pathology, posterior chamber fluid accumulation, angle closure. Superior to AS-OCT for imaging structures posterior to the iris pigment epithelium |
| **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** | Assessment of the posterior segment when direct visualization is limited | Vitreous opacities, retinal detachment, intraocular tumors, choroidal effusion |
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT) of the optic nerve** | Assessment of glaucomatous damage | [[Retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL) thinning, [[ganglion cell complex]] (GCC) loss, optic disc cupping |
| **[[Visual field testing]] ([[Perimetry]])** | Assessment of functional glaucomatous damage | Scotomas, arcuate defects, nasal step, generalized depression |
| **[[Anterior segment photography]]** | Documentation of clinical findings | Photographic record of iris bombé, synechiae, and anterior chamber configuration |

### Differential Diagnosis

It is critical to distinguish iris bombé from other causes of shallow anterior chamber and angle closure:

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| **[[Primary angle-closure glaucoma]]** | No posterior synechiae; relative pupillary block due to anatomical predisposition (short axial length, thick lens, anterior lens position); no inflammatory signs |
| **[[Aqueous misdirection]] ([[malignant glaucoma]])** | Uniformly shallow anterior chamber (central and peripheral); aqueous misdirected posteriorly; no posterior synechiae; typically post-surgical |
| **[[Choroidal effusion]] / [[suprachoroidal hemorrhage]]** | Anterior rotation of the ciliary body pushing the lens-iris diaphragm forward; may be associated with hypotony or post-surgical; B-scan shows choroidal detachment |
| **[[Plateau iris syndrome]]** | Anteriorly positioned ciliary processes push the peripheral iris forward; central anterior chamber depth is normal; gonioscopy shows "double hump" sign; UBM confirms ciliary body configuration |
| **[[Phacomorphic glaucoma]]** | Intumescent (swollen) lens pushes iris forward; asymmetric anterior chamber shallowing; lens opacity visible |
| **[[Neovascular glaucoma]]** | Rubeosis iridis with fibrovascular membrane causing synechial angle closure; associated with ischemic retinal disease; may coexist with iris bombé |

## Management

The management of iris bombé is multifaceted and involves addressing the acute IOP elevation, breaking or bypassing the pupillary block, controlling the underlying inflammation, and managing any chronic sequelae such as glaucomatous optic neuropathy.

### Emergency Management of Acute IOP Elevation

When a patient presents with iris bombé and acutely elevated IOP, immediate medical therapy is initiated to lower IOP and reduce the risk of optic nerve damage:

#### Pharmacological IOP Reduction

| Drug Class | Agent(s) | Mechanism | Route | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Topical beta-blockers]]** | [[Timolol]] 0.5% | Reduces aqueous humor production by blocking β2-adrenergic receptors on ciliary epithelium | Topical | Contraindicated in asthma, COPD, bradycardia |
| **[[Topical alpha-2 agonists]]** | [[Brimonidine]] 0.2% | Reduces aqueous production and increases uveoscleral outflow | Topical | Avoid in children <2 years (CNS depression risk) |
| **[[Topical carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]]** | [[Dorzolamide]] 2%, [[Brinzolamide]] 1% | Reduces aqueous production by inhibiting carbonic anhydrase in ciliary epithelium | Topical | Sulfonamide allergy caution |
| **[[Systemic carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]]** | [[Acetazolamide]] 250–500 mg | Reduces aqueous production systemically | Oral or IV | Monitor electrolytes; contraindicated in sulfonamide allergy, sickle cell disease |
| **[[Hyperosmotic agents]]** | [[Mannitol]] 20% (1–2 g/kg IV), [[Glycerol]] oral | Osmotic dehydration of the vitreous, reducing vitreous volume and IOP | IV or Oral | Reserved for severe acute cases; monitor renal function |
| **[[Topical corticosteroids]]** | [[Prednisolone acetate]] 1%, [[Difluprednate]] 0.05% | Reduces intraocular inflammation; prevents further synechiae formation | Topical | Essential in inflammatory etiology; frequency depends on severity (may be q1h initially) |
| **[[Cycloplegic/mydriatic agents]]** | [[Atropine]] 1%, [[Cyclopentolate]] 1% | Relaxes ciliary muscle, stabilizes blood-aqueous barrier, attempts to break posterior synechiae | Topical | Critical to attempt synechiae lysis; may paradoxically worsen angle closure if synechiae are not broken — use with caution and close monitoring |

> **Important Clinical Note**: [[Topical prostaglandin analogs]] (e.g., [[latanoprost]], [[travoprost]]) are generally **avoided** in the acute setting of uveitis-related iris bombé, as they may exacerbate intraocular inflammation. [[Pilocarpine]] (a [[miotic]]) is also typically **contraindicated** in iris bombé secondary to uveitis, as it can worsen posterior synechiae formation, increase inflammation, and paradoxically worsen the pupillary block.

### Definitive Treatment: Breaking the Pupillary Block

The cornerstone of definitive management is to **re-establish communication between the posterior and anterior chambers**, thereby equalizing the pressure differential and relieving the iris bombé.

#### Laser Peripheral Iridotomy (LPI)

**[[Laser peripheral iridotomy]]** (LPI) is the **first-line definitive treatment** for iris bombé. It creates an alternative pathway for aqueous humor to flow from the posterior chamber to the anterior chamber, bypassing the obstructed pupil.

- **Technique**: A full-thickness hole is created in the peripheral iris using a [[Nd:YAG laser]] (photodisruptive mechanism) or an [[argon laser]] (photocoagulative mechanism), or a combination of both (sequential technique). The iridotomy is typically placed in the superior peripheral iris (10–2 o'clock position), ideally under an area covered by the upper eyelid to minimize visual symptoms such as glare or diplopia.
- **Pre-treatment**: Topical [[pilocarpine]] may be used to constrict the pupil and thin the peripheral iris in primary angle closure, but in iris bombé secondary to uveitis, pilocarpine is generally avoided. Instead, the iris may be pre-treated with argon laser spots to thin the stroma before Nd:YAG application. Topical [[apraclonidine]] 1% or [[brimonidine]] 0.2% is applied before and after the procedure to prevent post-laser IOP spikes.
- **Challenges in iris bombé**: LPI may be technically difficult due to corneal edema (limiting laser delivery), a very shallow anterior chamber (risk of corneal endothelial damage), thick or inflamed iris tissue, and the presence of inflammatory membranes. Multiple treatment sessions may be required.
- **Success criteria**: A patent, full-thickness iridotomy is confirmed by visualization of the anterior lens capsule or a gush of aqueous and pigment through the opening. Deepening of the anterior chamber and flattening of the iris bombé should be observed.
- **Complications**: Post-laser IOP spike, hyphema, iritis, corneal endothelial damage, posterior synechiae formation at the iridotomy site, closure of the iridotomy (especially in inflamed eyes), and rarely, focal lens opacity.

#### Surgical Peripheral Iridectomy

If LPI is not feasible or has failed (e.g., due to corneal opacity, extremely shallow anterior chamber, thick inflammatory membranes, or repeated closure of laser iridotomies), a **[[surgical iridectomy]]** (also called a **peripheral iridectomy** or **sector iridectomy**) is performed.

- **Technique**: A small incision is made at the limbus, and a portion of the peripheral iris is excised, creating a permanent full-thickness opening. This is typically performed under local or general anesthesia in an operating room setting.
- **Indications**: Failed LPI, corneal opacity precluding laser delivery, very thick iris, presence of occlusio pupillae requiring membranectomy, or when combined with other surgical procedures.

#### Synechialysis

In cases where posterior synechiae are not yet 360 degrees, or in conjunction with iridotomy/iridectomy, **[[synechialysis]]** — the surgical or pharmacological breaking of synechiae — may be attempted.

- **Pharmacological synechialysis**: Intensive topical [[cycloplegic agents]] (e.g., [[atropine]] 1%) and [[corticosteroids]] may break fresh, non-fibrotic posterior synechiae.
- **Surgical synechialysis**: Using a spatula, viscoelastic, or other instruments introduced through a limbal incision, the surgeon mechanically separates the iris from the lens capsule. This is often combined with injection of [[viscoelastic]] material (e.g., [[sodium hyaluronate]]) into the anterior and/or posterior chamber to deepen the chamber and maintain separation.

#### Goniosynechialysis

If [[peripheral anterior synechiae]] (PAS) have formed due to prolonged iridotrabecular contact, **[[goniosynechialysis]]** may be performed to strip the peripheral iris from the angle structures, potentially restoring trabecular meshwork function. This is most effective when PAS are of recent onset (generally <6 months) and the trabecular meshwork has not undergone irreversible damage.

### Management of Underlying Inflammation

Controlling the underlying inflammatory process is **paramount** to prevent recurrence of posterior synechiae and iris bombé:

- **Topical corticosteroids**: [[Prednisolone acetate]] 1% or [[Difluprednate]] 0.05% are the mainstays of topical anti-inflammatory therapy. Frequency is titrated based on the severity of inflammation (e.g., every 1–2 hours in acute flares, with gradual taper).
- **Cycloplegic agents**: [[Atropine]] 1%, [[homatropine]] 5%, or [[cyclopentolate]] 1% are used to prevent posterior synechiae formation by maintaining pupillary dilation and reducing ciliary spasm. Alternating mydriatics (e.g., [[phenylephrine]] 2.5% with atropine) can help keep the pupil mobile.
- **Periocular or intravitreal corticosteroids**: [[Sub-Tenon's triamcinolone acetonide]] injection or [[intravitreal dexamethasone implant]] ([[Ozurdex]]) may be used for severe or refractory uveitis.
- **Systemic immunosuppressive therapy**: For patients with recurrent or chronic uveitis requiring long-term control, systemic agents may be necessary, including:
  - [[Methotrexate]]
  - [[Mycophenolate mofetil]]
  - [[Azathioprine]]
  - [[Cyclosporine]]
  - [[Biologic agents]] (e.g., [[adalimumab]], [[infliximab]]) — particularly in JIA-associated uveitis
- **Identification and treatment of the underlying systemic disease**: Collaboration with [[rheumatology]], [[infectious disease]], or [[internal medicine]] specialists is essential.

### Long-Term Glaucoma Management

If the IOP remains elevated despite resolution of the pupillary block, or if chronic angle damage (PAS) has occurred, long-term [[glaucoma]] management may be required:

- **Topical IOP-lowering agents**: [[Beta-blockers]], [[alpha-2 agonists]], [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]], and [[Rho kinase inhibitors]] ([[netarsudil]]) may be used. [[Prostaglandin analogs]] should be used with caution in uveitic eyes due to potential pro-inflammatory effects, though they may be considered in quiescent disease.
- **Glaucoma surgery**: If medical therapy is insufficient, surgical options include:
  - **[[Trabeculectomy]]** with [[mitomycin C]] (MMC): Filtering surgery to create an alternative aqueous drainage pathway. Higher failure rates in uveitic eyes due to inflammation-driven fibrosis.
  - **[[Glaucoma drainage devices]]** (e.g., [[Ahmed valve]], [[Baerveldt implant]]): Often preferred over trabeculectomy in uveitic glaucoma due to more predictable long-term IOP control.
  - **[[Minimally invasive glaucoma surgery]] (MIGS)**: Options such as [[iStent]], [[Kahook Dual Blade]] goniotomy, or [[ab interno canaloplasty]] may be considered in select cases, though evidence in uveitic glaucoma is still evolving.
  - **[[Cyclodestructive procedures]]**: [[Transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]] (TSCPC) or [[endoscopic cyclophotocoagulation]] (ECP) may be used as a last resort to reduce aqueous production in refractory cases.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of iris bombé depends on several factors:

| Prognostic Factor | Favorable | Unfavorable |
|---|---|---|
| **Timeliness of intervention** | Early recognition and treatment | Delayed presentation or diagnosis |
| **Duration of angle closure** | Brief appositional closure | Prolonged closure with extensive PAS |
| **Extent of PAS** | Minimal or no PAS | >180° of synechial closure |
| **Underlying etiology** | Single episode of anterior uveitis | Chronic, recurrent, or refractory uveitis |
| **Pre-existing optic nerve damage** | No prior glaucomatous damage | Advanced cupping or visual field loss |
| **Patient age** | Adults with good compliance | Children (especially JIA) with silent disease |
| **Response to LPI** | Patent iridotomy with chamber deepening | Iridotomy closure, need for repeat procedures |

With prompt diagnosis and appropriate management — including successful laser or surgical iridotomy, control of inflammation, and management of IOP — many patients can achieve good visual outcomes. However, patients with chronic uveitis, extensive PAS, or delayed treatment may develop irreversible glaucomatous optic neuropathy and permanent visual field loss.

---

## Prevention

Prevention of iris bombé centers on the **aggressive and timely management of anterior uveitis** and the **prevention of posterior synechiae formation**:

1. **Prompt initiation of topical corticosteroids** in acute anterior uveitis to suppress inflammation.
2. **Routine use of cycloplegic agents** (e.g., [[atropine]], [[cyclopentolate]]) during active uveitis to keep the pupil dilated and mobile, preventing iris-lens adhesion.
3. **Regular ophthalmic screening** in high-risk populations (e.g., children with JIA, patients with known HLA-B27 positivity).
4. **Close postoperative monitoring** after intraocular surgery, with aggressive anti-inflammatory prophylaxis.
5. **Patient education** regarding the importance of medication compliance and prompt reporting of symptoms such as pain, redness, or vision changes.

## Special Considerations

### Iris Bombé in Aphakic and Pseudophakic Eyes

In [[aphakic]] eyes (after lens removal without IOL implantation) or [[pseudophakic]] eyes (with an IOL), iris bombé can still occur. In aphakic eyes, the iris may adhere to the anterior [[vitreous face]], creating a pupillary block. In pseudophakic eyes, synechiae may form between the iris and the IOL optic or haptics. The principles of management remain the same — LPI or surgical iridectomy to relieve the block.

### Iris Bombé in Pediatric Patients

Children with JIA-associated uveitis represent a particularly challenging population. The uveitis is often chronic, bilateral, and asymptomatic, leading to delayed diagnosis. Posterior synechiae and iris bombé may be the presenting finding. Management requires:

- Close collaboration with pediatric rheumatology
- Frequent ophthalmic examinations (per [[American Academy of Ophthalmology]] screening guidelines)
- Aggressive anti-inflammatory therapy, often including systemic immunosuppression
- Low threshold for surgical intervention if LPI fails
- Long-term monitoring for glaucoma and [[amblyopia]]

### Iris Bombé Following Penetrating Keratoplasty

After [[penetrating keratoplasty]], iris bombé may develop due to postoperative inflammation, synechiae formation, or anterior chamber shallowing. Management may be complicated by the need to protect the corneal graft. LPI should be performed carefully to avoid damage to the graft endothelium, and surgical iridectomy may be preferred in some cases.

---

## Summary

Iris bombé is a vision-threatening ophthalmic condition resulting from complete pupillary block due to 360-degree posterior synechiae (seclusio pupillae), most commonly as a complication of anterior uveitis. The resultant anterior bowing of the iris leads to secondary angle-closure glaucoma through mechanical obstruction of the trabecular meshwork. Diagnosis is clinical, supported by gonioscopy, AS-OCT, and UBM. Management requires a multi-pronged approach: acute IOP reduction, definitive relief of the pupillary block (primarily via laser peripheral iridotomy), control of underlying inflammation, and long-term glaucoma management. Prevention through aggressive uveitis treatment and cycloplegia remains the most effective strategy. Early recognition and intervention are critical to preserving vision.
