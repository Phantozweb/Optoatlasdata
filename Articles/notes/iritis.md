

# Iritis

**Iritis**, also referred to as **anterior [[uveitis]]** (specifically involving the [[iris]]), is the most common form of [[uveitis]] and represents an inflammatory condition localized primarily to the [[anterior chamber]] of the eye. It is classified under the Standardization of Uveitis Nomenclature (SUN) Working Group as inflammation of the [[iris]] tissue, with the predominant site of inflammation being the anterior chamber. Iritis accounts for approximately 50–70% of all uveitis cases seen in clinical practice and represents a significant cause of ocular morbidity, particularly in the working-age population. Prompt recognition, accurate diagnosis, and appropriate management by [[optometrists]] and [[ophthalmologists]] are essential to prevent sight-threatening complications such as [[posterior synechiae]], [[secondary glaucoma]], [[cystoid macular edema]], and [[cataract]] formation.

Iritis may present as an isolated, acute, self-limiting episode or may follow a chronic, recurrent course. It can be idiopathic in nature or associated with a wide spectrum of systemic autoimmune, infectious, and inflammatory conditions. Understanding the nuanced clinical features, pathophysiological mechanisms, differential diagnoses, and evidence-based treatment strategies is paramount for all eye care professionals.

---

## Epidemiology

Iritis is the most prevalent form of intraocular inflammation encountered in both optometric and ophthalmologic practice. The annual incidence of anterior uveitis, of which iritis constitutes the majority, is estimated at approximately **17–52 per 100,000 persons per year** in Western populations, with a prevalence of roughly **58–115 per 100,000 persons**. It affects males and females with roughly equal frequency, although certain associated systemic conditions (e.g., [[ankylosing spondylitis]]) demonstrate a male predominance.

The peak age of onset is typically between **20 and 50 years of age**, though iritis can occur at any age, including in pediatric populations (notably in association with [[juvenile idiopathic arthritis]]). The condition is more common in individuals who are [[HLA-B27]] positive, with studies demonstrating that up to **50% of acute anterior uveitis cases** are HLA-B27-associated.

| **Epidemiological Parameter** | **Value** |
|---|---|
| Annual incidence (anterior uveitis) | 17–52 per 100,000 |
| Prevalence | 58–115 per 100,000 |
| Proportion of all uveitis cases | 50–70% |
| Peak age of onset | 20–50 years |
| HLA-B27 positivity in acute cases | ~50% |
| Gender predilection | Roughly equal (male predominance in HLA-B27-associated) |
| Bilateral involvement | ~20–30% of cases (more common in chronic/granulomatous forms) |

## Classification

Iritis is classified according to several clinical parameters, each of which carries diagnostic and prognostic significance:

### By Onset and Duration

- **Acute iritis**: Sudden onset with symptomatic presentation; typically lasts less than 3 months with complete resolution between episodes.
- **Chronic iritis**: Persistent inflammation lasting longer than 3 months, often with insidious onset and minimal symptoms; relapse occurs within 3 months of discontinuing therapy.
- **Recurrent iritis**: Repeated episodes separated by periods of inactivity (without treatment) lasting at least 3 months.

### By Laterality

- **Unilateral**: More common in acute, HLA-B27-associated iritis; may alternate between eyes in recurrent episodes.
- **Bilateral**: More frequently seen in chronic and granulomatous forms (e.g., [[sarcoidosis]], [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]]).

### By Pathological Type

- **Non-granulomatous iritis**: Characterized by fine [[keratic precipitates]] (KPs), diffuse anterior chamber cells and flare, and is the most common type. Typically associated with HLA-B27-related conditions.
- **Granulomatous iritis**: Characterized by large, greasy "mutton-fat" keratic precipitates, [[Busacca nodules]] (on the iris stroma), and [[Koeppe nodules]] (at the pupillary margin). Associated with [[sarcoidosis]], [[tuberculosis]], [[syphilis]], and [[herpes simplex virus]] (HSV)/[[herpes zoster virus]] (HZV) infections.

## Anatomy and Pathophysiology

### Relevant Anatomy

The [[iris]] is a thin, circular, pigmented diaphragm situated between the [[cornea]] and the [[crystalline lens]], dividing the anterior segment into the [[anterior chamber]] and the [[posterior chamber]]. It is composed of:

- **Anterior border layer**: A discontinuous layer of fibroblasts and melanocytes.
- **Stroma**: Loose connective tissue containing blood vessels, nerves, melanocytes, clump cells, and the [[sphincter pupillae]] muscle.
- **Posterior pigmented epithelium**: A bilayered epithelium continuous with the non-pigmented and pigmented epithelium of the [[ciliary body]].
- **Dilator pupillae muscle**: Located within the posterior epithelial layers.

The iris receives its blood supply from the [[major arterial circle of the iris]], formed by anastomoses of the [[long posterior ciliary arteries]] and the [[anterior ciliary arteries]]. This rich vascular network is central to the pathophysiology of iritis, as disruption of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]] (BAB) is the hallmark of the inflammatory process.

### Pathophysiological Mechanisms

The pathophysiology of iritis involves a complex interplay of immune-mediated mechanisms that result in breakdown of the blood-aqueous barrier and subsequent intraocular inflammation:

1. **Blood-Aqueous Barrier Disruption**: Under normal physiological conditions, the BAB—maintained by tight junctions of the [[iris]] vascular endothelium and the non-pigmented [[ciliary epithelium]]—prevents the passage of plasma proteins and inflammatory cells into the [[aqueous humor]]. In iritis, immune-mediated or infectious insults cause disruption of these tight junctions, leading to increased vascular permeability.

2. **Immune Cell Infiltration**: Breakdown of the BAB permits the influx of [[leukocytes]] (predominantly [[T-lymphocytes]], [[macrophages]], and [[neutrophils]]) into the anterior chamber. In non-granulomatous iritis, the infiltrate is predominantly lymphocytic and neutrophilic. In granulomatous iritis, [[epithelioid cells]] and [[multinucleated giant cells]] aggregate to form granulomas.

3. **Cytokine and Chemokine Cascade**: Activated immune cells release a cascade of pro-inflammatory mediators, including [[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] (TNF-α), [[interleukin-1]] (IL-1), [[interleukin-6]] (IL-6), [[interleukin-17]] (IL-17), [[interferon-gamma]] (IFN-γ), and [[prostaglandins]]. These mediators amplify the inflammatory response, recruit additional immune cells, and contribute to tissue damage.

4. **Molecular Mimicry and Autoimmunity**: In HLA-B27-associated iritis, the prevailing theory involves [[molecular mimicry]], wherein microbial antigens share structural homology with self-antigens presented by the HLA-B27 molecule. This triggers an autoimmune response directed against iris and ciliary body tissues. The "arthritogenic peptide hypothesis" and the "unfolded protein response" (UPR) theory of HLA-B27 misfolding are additional proposed mechanisms.

5. **Prostaglandin-Mediated Effects**: [[Prostaglandins]], particularly [[PGE2]] and [[PGF2α]], are released during the inflammatory cascade and contribute to vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, [[miosis]], and pain. They also play a role in the reduction of [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) observed in some cases of acute iritis due to decreased aqueous production by the inflamed [[ciliary body]].

6. **Fibrin Exudation and Synechiae Formation**: Severe BAB disruption leads to fibrin exudation into the anterior chamber. Fibrin deposition between the [[iris]] and the [[anterior lens capsule]] promotes the formation of [[posterior synechiae]], which, if circumferential (360°), can result in [[iris bombé]] and [[secondary angle-closure glaucoma]].

## Etiology and Associations

Iritis may be idiopathic (approximately 30–50% of cases) or associated with a wide range of systemic and ocular conditions. A thorough systemic review is essential in all patients presenting with iritis, particularly those with recurrent, bilateral, or granulomatous disease.

### Systemic Associations

| **Category** | **Associated Conditions** |
|---|---|
| **HLA-B27-associated spondyloarthropathies** | [[Ankylosing spondylitis]], [[reactive arthritis]] (formerly Reiter syndrome), [[psoriatic arthritis]], [[inflammatory bowel disease]]-associated arthritis |
| **Autoimmune/Inflammatory** | [[Sarcoidosis]], [[Behçet disease]], [[systemic lupus erythematosus]], [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]], [[multiple sclerosis]], [[tubulointerstitial nephritis and uveitis]] (TINU syndrome) |
| **Infectious** | [[Herpes simplex virus]], [[herpes zoster virus]] (varicella-zoster), [[syphilis]], [[tuberculosis]], [[Lyme disease]], [[toxoplasmosis]] (when anterior chamber reaction accompanies posterior segment disease), [[cytomegalovirus]] |
| **Juvenile/Pediatric** | [[Juvenile idiopathic arthritis]] (particularly oligoarticular, ANA-positive subtype) |
| **Drug-induced** | [[Rifabutin]], [[bisphosphonates]], [[sulfonamides]], [[moxifloxacin]], [[cidofovir]], [[checkpoint inhibitor]] immunotherapy agents, [[prostaglandin analogue]] eye drops (rare) |
| **Traumatic** | Blunt or penetrating [[ocular trauma]], post-surgical inflammation |
| **Masquerade syndromes** | [[Intraocular lymphoma]], [[retinoblastoma]], [[leukemia]] (must be considered in atypical presentations) |

### HLA-B27 and Iritis

The association between [[HLA-B27]] and acute anterior uveitis is one of the strongest known HLA-disease associations in medicine. Approximately **50–60% of patients with acute, non-granulomatous, unilateral, recurrent iritis** are HLA-B27 positive, compared to a background prevalence of approximately 6–8% in Caucasian populations. HLA-B27-positive iritis tends to present with:

- Acute, unilateral onset (may alternate eyes)
- Severe anterior chamber reaction with fibrin
- [[Hypopyon]] in severe cases
- Male predominance
- Association with low back pain or sacroiliitis
- Good response to topical [[corticosteroids]]

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

The symptom profile of iritis varies depending on whether the presentation is acute or chronic:

| **Symptom** | **Acute Iritis** | **Chronic Iritis** |
|---|---|---|
| **Pain** | Moderate to severe, deep, aching periorbital pain | Minimal or absent |
| **Photophobia** | Marked; often the presenting complaint | Mild or absent |
| **Lacrimation** | Present | Variable |
| **Blurred vision** | Mild to moderate | May be significant if complications present |
| **Redness** | [[Ciliary flush]] (circumcorneal injection) | Mild or absent |
| **Consensual photophobia** | Present (pathognomonic for iritis) | Variable |
| **Onset** | Sudden | Insidious |

**Consensual photophobia**—pain in the affected eye when light is shone into the contralateral eye—is a highly characteristic symptom of iritis and results from consensual pupillary constriction causing movement of the inflamed iris.

### Signs

#### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy Findings

[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] is the cornerstone of iritis diagnosis. Key findings include:

1. **Anterior Chamber Cells**: The hallmark of iritis. Inflammatory cells (leukocytes) are visible in the anterior chamber as bright particles moving in the aqueous humor, best observed using a narrow, high-intensity slit beam against a dark background. Graded according to the SUN Working Group criteria:

| **Grade** | **Cells per Field (1 mm × 1 mm slit beam)** |
|---|---|
| 0 | < 1 |
| 0.5+ | 1–5 |
| 1+ | 6–15 |
| 2+ | 16–25 |
| 3+ | 26–50 |
| 4+ | > 50 |

2. **Anterior Chamber Flare**: Represents protein exudation into the aqueous humor due to BAB breakdown. Visible as a "Tyndall effect" (light scattering) in the slit beam. Graded as:

| **Grade** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| 0 | None |
| 1+ | Faint (barely detectable) |
| 2+ | Moderate (iris and lens details clear) |
| 3+ | Marked (iris and lens details hazy) |
| 4+ | Intense (fibrin or plastic aqueous) |

3. **Keratic Precipitates (KPs)**: Inflammatory cell deposits on the [[corneal endothelium]], typically distributed in a triangular pattern (Arlt's triangle) in the inferior cornea due to aqueous convection currents.
   - **Fine, stellate KPs**: Non-granulomatous iritis
   - **Mutton-fat KPs**: Large, greasy, yellowish-white deposits; granulomatous iritis (e.g., sarcoidosis, tuberculosis, syphilis, herpetic disease)
   - **Pigmented KPs**: Old or resolving inflammation

4. **Posterior Synechiae**: Adhesions between the posterior iris surface and the [[anterior lens capsule]]. May be focal or extensive. Circumferential synechiae (seclusio pupillae) can lead to [[iris bombé]] and [[secondary angle-closure glaucoma]].

5. **Iris Nodules**:
   - **[[Koeppe nodules]]**: Small, white nodules at the [[pupillary margin]]; seen in both granulomatous and non-granulomatous iritis.
   - **[[Busacca nodules]]**: Larger nodules on the iris stroma surface; pathognomonic for granulomatous iritis.
   - **Berlin nodules**: Nodules at the angle of the anterior chamber.

6. **Fibrin**: Visible as strands or a membrane in the anterior chamber in severe cases; indicates significant BAB breakdown.

7. **Hypopyon**: A layered collection of white blood cells (predominantly [[neutrophils]]) in the inferior anterior chamber. Seen in severe HLA-B27-associated iritis, [[Behçet disease]], and infectious etiologies. Must be differentiated from [[pseudohypopyon]] seen in masquerade syndromes.

8. **Miosis**: The inflamed iris tends toward constriction due to sphincter pupillae spasm mediated by prostaglandins and irritation of the [[parasympathetic]] nerve fibers.

9. **Ciliary Flush**: A ring of deep, violaceous injection surrounding the [[limbus]], reflecting inflammation of the [[ciliary body]] vasculature. Distinct from the bright red, superficial injection of [[conjunctivitis]].

10. **Intraocular Pressure Changes**:
    - **Decreased IOP**: Common in acute iritis due to reduced aqueous production by the inflamed ciliary body and increased uveoscleral outflow mediated by prostaglandins.
    - **Increased IOP**: May occur due to [[trabeculitis]], trabecular meshwork obstruction by inflammatory cells and debris, peripheral anterior synechiae (PAS), or pupillary block from posterior synechiae. This is termed **[[inflammatory glaucoma]]** or **[[uveitic glaucoma]]**.

11. **Lens Changes**: Chronic or recurrent iritis may lead to [[posterior subcapsular cataract]] formation due to chronic inflammation and/or prolonged corticosteroid use.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis

The diagnosis of iritis is primarily clinical, based on the characteristic history and slit-lamp biomicroscopy findings described above. The presence of anterior chamber cells and flare in the context of appropriate symptoms (pain, photophobia, redness, blurred vision) establishes the diagnosis.

### Differential Diagnosis

It is critical to differentiate iritis from other causes of a red, painful eye:

| **Condition** | **Key Differentiating Features** |
|---|---|
| [[Acute angle-closure glaucoma]] | Markedly elevated IOP, mid-dilated fixed pupil, corneal edema, shallow anterior chamber |
| [[Conjunctivitis]] | Discharge (purulent, mucoid, or watery), diffuse conjunctival injection, no cells/flare, no photophobia |
| [[Episcleritis]]/[[Scleritis]] | Sectoral or diffuse redness, tenderness, no anterior chamber reaction (unless scleritis with secondary uveitis) |
| [[Keratitis]] | Corneal infiltrate or ulcer, epithelial defect, positive fluorescein staining; may have secondary anterior chamber reaction |
| [[Endophthalmitis]] | Severe pain, marked vision loss, hypopyon, vitritis; often post-surgical or post-traumatic |
| [[Traumatic iritis]] | History of blunt or penetrating trauma; may have [[hyphema]], [[angle recession]], [[lens subluxation]] |

### Laboratory Investigation

A first episode of mild, unilateral, non-granulomatous acute iritis in an otherwise healthy young adult often does not require laboratory investigation, as it is frequently idiopathic and self-limiting. However, laboratory workup is indicated in the following scenarios:

- **Recurrent iritis** (≥2 episodes)
- **Bilateral iritis**
- **Granulomatous iritis**
- **Chronic iritis**
- **Pediatric iritis**
- **Iritis unresponsive to standard therapy**
- **Iritis with associated systemic symptoms**
- **Severe iritis with hypopyon**

#### Recommended Laboratory Investigations

| **Test** | **Condition Screened** |
|---|---|
| [[HLA-B27]] | Ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, HLA-B27-associated uveitis |
| [[Erythrocyte sedimentation rate]] (ESR) / [[C-reactive protein]] (CRP) | General inflammatory markers |
| [[Complete blood count]] (CBC) | Infection, leukemia, sarcoidosis |
| [[Angiotensin-converting enzyme]] (ACE) / [[Lysozyme]] | [[Sarcoidosis]] |
| Chest X-ray or CT chest | Sarcoidosis, tuberculosis |
| [[Tuberculin skin test]] (PPD) / [[Interferon-gamma release assay]] (QuantiFERON-TB Gold, T-SPOT) | [[Tuberculosis]] |
| [[RPR]]/[[VDRL]] and [[FTA-ABS]] | [[Syphilis]] |
| [[Lyme serology]] (ELISA, Western blot) | [[Lyme disease]] (in endemic areas) |
| [[Antinuclear antibody]] (ANA) | [[Juvenile idiopathic arthritis]] (pediatric patients), [[SLE]] |
| [[Sacroiliac joint X-ray]] or [[MRI]] | Ankylosing spondylitis, sacroiliitis |
| [[Urinalysis]] | [[TINU syndrome]] |
| [[Aqueous humor PCR]] | Herpetic uveitis (HSV, VZV, CMV), toxoplasmosis |

### Imaging

- **[[Anterior segment optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT)**: Can quantify anterior chamber flare and detect subtle structural changes.
- **[[Laser flare photometry]]**: Provides objective, quantitative measurement of anterior chamber flare; useful in research settings and monitoring chronic iritis.
- **[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA)**: Not routinely indicated for isolated iritis but may be useful if [[cystoid macular edema]] is suspected.
- **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT) of the macula**: Essential to evaluate for [[cystoid macular edema]], a common complication of chronic or recurrent iritis.
- **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]**: Indicated if posterior segment visualization is limited due to severe anterior chamber inflammation, to rule out posterior segment involvement.

## Complications

Iritis, particularly when chronic, recurrent, or inadequately treated, can lead to several sight-threatening complications:

| **Complication** | **Mechanism** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Posterior synechiae]]** | Adhesion of iris to anterior lens capsule due to fibrin deposition | Irregular pupil, risk of seclusio pupillae and iris bombé |
| **[[Secondary glaucoma]]** | Trabeculitis, trabecular meshwork obstruction, PAS, pupillary block, steroid-induced | Major cause of vision loss in chronic iritis |
| **[[Cataract]]** (posterior subcapsular) | Chronic inflammation, prolonged corticosteroid use | Progressive vision loss requiring surgical intervention |
| **[[Cystoid macular edema]] (CME)** | Inflammatory mediator-induced breakdown of the [[blood-retinal barrier]] | Leading cause of visual morbidity in chronic uveitis |
| **[[Band keratopathy]]** | Calcium deposition in [[Bowman's layer]] due to chronic inflammation | Corneal opacity, reduced vision; more common in pediatric JIA-associated iritis |
| **[[Iris bombé]]** | 360° posterior synechiae causing aqueous trapping behind iris | Acute secondary angle-closure glaucoma |
| **[[Hypotony]]** | Ciliary body shutdown from chronic inflammation | Risk of [[phthisis bulbi]] in severe cases |
| **[[Phthisis bulbi]]** | End-stage chronic inflammation with globe shrinkage | Irreversible vision loss |

## Management and Treatment

### Principles of Treatment

The goals of iritis management are:

1. **Reduce inflammation** and alleviate symptoms
2. **Prevent and break posterior synechiae**
3. **Prevent complications** (glaucoma, cataract, CME)
4. **Identify and treat underlying systemic disease**
5. **Minimize treatment-related side effects**

### Pharmacological Treatment

#### 1. Topical Corticosteroids

Topical [[corticosteroids]] are the mainstay of iritis treatment. They suppress the inflammatory cascade by inhibiting [[phospholipase A2]], thereby reducing prostaglandin and leukotriene synthesis, and by suppressing leukocyte migration and cytokine production.

| **Agent** | **Potency** | **Typical Dosing (Acute Iritis)** | **Notes** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Prednisolone acetate]] 1% | High | Every 1–2 hours initially, then taper | Gold standard; shake well before use (suspension) |
| [[Difluprednate]] 0.05% (Durezol) | Very high | Every 2–4 hours initially, then taper | Emulsion; no shaking required; potent—monitor IOP closely |
| [[Dexamethasone]] 0.1% | High | Every 2–4 hours | Available as solution; good penetration |
| [[Loteprednol etabonate]] 0.5% | Moderate | Every 4–6 hours | Lower risk of IOP elevation; suitable for mild cases or taper |
| [[Fluorometholone]] 0.1% | Low-moderate | Every 4–6 hours | Less effective for significant iritis; useful for taper |

**Dosing Protocol**: In acute, moderate-to-severe iritis, prednisolone acetate 1% is typically initiated at **one drop every 1–2 hours while awake** for the first 24–72 hours, then gradually tapered over **4–6 weeks** based on clinical response. Rapid tapering or premature discontinuation is a common cause of recurrence. A general taper schedule may follow:

- Every 1–2 hours × 1 week
- Every 2 hours × 1 week
- Every 4 hours (QID) × 1 week
- Every 6 hours (TID) × 1 week
- Every 8 hours (BID) × 1 week
- Once daily × 1 week, then discontinue

**Monitoring**: [[Intraocular pressure]] must be monitored at every follow-up visit, as corticosteroid-induced [[ocular hypertension]] occurs in approximately **30–40% of the general population** (steroid responders), with a higher incidence in patients with [[primary open-angle glaucoma]], children, and those on prolonged therapy.

#### 2. Cycloplegic/Mydriatic Agents

[[Cycloplegic]] agents serve dual purposes in iritis management: **pain relief** (by relaxing the ciliary muscle spasm that causes deep aching pain) and **prevention/breaking of posterior synechiae** (by dilating the pupil and pulling the iris away from the lens).

| **Agent** | **Duration of Action** | **Typical Dosing** | **Notes** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Cyclopentolate]] 1% | 12–24 hours | BID–TID | Most commonly used for moderate iritis |
| [[Homatropine]] 2%–5% | 1–3 days | BID–TID | Good balance of efficacy and duration |
| [[Atropine]] 1% | 7–14 days | Once daily–BID | Reserved for severe iritis with dense synechiae or fibrin; prolonged mydriasis |
| [[Tropicamide]] 1% | 4–6 hours | QID | Short-acting; generally insufficient for iritis management alone |
| [[Phenylephrine]] 2.5%–10% | 3–6 hours | As adjunct | Sympathomimetic mydriatic; used in combination to break synechiae |

For established posterior synechiae, an intensive mydriatic regimen may be employed, sometimes referred to as a **"mydriatic cocktail"** combining cyclopentolate 1%, phenylephrine 10%, and tropicamide 1% administered in rapid succession.

#### 3. Oral and Periocular Corticosteroids

In cases of severe iritis unresponsive to aggressive topical therapy, or when posterior segment involvement is suspected:

- **Oral [[prednisone]]**: 0.5–1 mg/kg/day with gradual taper; requires monitoring for systemic side effects (hyperglycemia, hypertension, osteoporosis, adrenal suppression).
- **[[Periocular corticosteroid injection]]** (sub-Tenon's or orbital floor): [[Triamcinolone acetonide]] 40 mg/mL; provides sustained local anti-inflammatory effect; risk of IOP elevation.

#### 4. Topical and Oral NSAIDs

[[Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs]] (NSAIDs) may be used as adjunctive therapy:

- **Topical NSAIDs** (e.g., [[ketorolac]] 0.5%, [[nepafenac]] 0.1%, [[bromfenac]] 0.07%): Useful as adjunctive agents for pain control and to reduce CME risk; **not sufficient as monotherapy** for active iritis.
- **Oral NSAIDs** (e.g., [[indomethacin]], [[naproxen]]): May be used for mild recurrent iritis or as steroid-sparing agents in HLA-B27-associated disease.

#### 5. IOP-Lowering Agents

When iritis is complicated by elevated IOP ([[uveitic glaucoma]]):

- **[[Aqueous suppressants]]**: [[Timolol]], [[dorzolamide]], [[brinzolamide]], [[brimonidine]] are first-line agents.
- **Avoid [[prostaglandin analogues]]**: These may theoretically exacerbate inflammation (though evidence is debated, caution is advised in active iritis).
- **Avoid [[pilocarpine]]**: Contraindicated as it promotes posterior synechiae formation and increases ciliary spasm/pain.
- **Oral [[acetazolamide]]**: May be used for acute IOP spikes.

#### 6. Immunomodulatory Therapy (IMT)

For chronic, recurrent, or steroid-dependent iritis requiring long-term immunosuppression:

| **Agent** | **Class** | **Indication** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Methotrexate]] | Antimetabolite | First-line steroid-sparing agent for chronic non-infectious iritis |
| [[Mycophenolate mofetil]] | Antimetabolite | Alternative to methotrexate |
| [[Azathioprine]] | Antimetabolite | Used in Behçet disease and other systemic associations |
| [[Cyclosporine A]] | Calcineurin inhibitor | Refractory cases |
| [[Adalimumab]] (Humira) | Anti-TNF-α biologic | FDA-approved for non-infectious intermediate, posterior, and panuveitis; used off-label for refractory anterior uveitis |
| [[Infliximab]] | Anti-TNF-α biologic | Severe refractory uveitis, Behçet disease |
| [[Tocilizumab]] | Anti-IL-6 receptor | Refractory uveitis |

Immunomodulatory therapy is typically co-managed with a [[rheumatologist]] or [[uveitis specialist]] and requires regular monitoring of blood counts, liver function, renal function, and infection risk.

### Surgical Management

Surgical intervention in iritis is reserved for the management of complications:

- **[[Laser peripheral iridotomy]] (LPI)**: Indicated for iris bombé with secondary angle closure to relieve pupillary block.
- **[[Synechiolysis]]**: Surgical or pharmacological lysis of posterior synechiae.
- **[[Cataract surgery]]**: [[Phacoemulsification]] with [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) implantation for visually significant cataract; requires meticulous perioperative inflammation control (typically 1–3 months of quiescence before surgery).
- **[[Glaucoma surgery]]**: [[Trabeculectomy]], [[glaucoma drainage device]] implantation, or [[minimally invasive glaucoma surgery]] (MIGS) for medically uncontrolled uveitic glaucoma.
- **[[Intravitreal implants]]**: [[Dexamethasone intravitreal implant]] (Ozurdex) or [[fluocinolone acetonide implant]] (Retisert, Yutiq) for chronic, refractory inflammation with macular edema.

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Iritis

Iritis in children warrants special attention due to its frequently **asymptomatic, chronic, bilateral** presentation, particularly in association with [[juvenile idiopathic arthritis]] (JIA). Key considerations include:

- **JIA-associated iritis** is the most common cause of chronic anterior uveitis in children.
- Highest risk: oligoarticular JIA, ANA-positive, female, age of arthritis onset < 6 years.
- Often detected only on routine screening slit-lamp examination.
- Complications (band keratopathy, posterior synechiae, cataract, glaucoma, amblyopia) are common at presentation due to the insidious nature.
- **Screening guidelines** (American Academy of Pediatrics/American College of Rheumatology): High-risk children should undergo slit-lamp examination every **3 months**.

### Iritis in Pregnancy

Management of iritis during pregnancy requires careful consideration of medication safety:

- Topical prednisolone acetate is generally considered safe.
- Cyclopentolate and homatropine may be used with caution.
- Systemic corticosteroids (prednisone) can be used when necessary but require obstetric co-management.
- Methotrexate and mycophenolate are **absolutely contraindicated** (teratogenic).

## Prognosis

The prognosis of iritis depends on the underlying etiology, chronicity, and adequacy of treatment:

- **Acute, non-granulomatous, HLA-B27-associated iritis**: Generally carries a good visual prognosis with appropriate treatment. Episodes typically resolve within 4–8 weeks. Recurrence is common (~50% recurrence rate), but individual episodes usually respond well to topical corticosteroids.
- **Chronic iritis** (e.g., sarcoidosis-associated, JIA-associated): Carries a more guarded prognosis due to the cumulative risk of complications from both the disease and its treatment.
- **Vision-threatening complications** (CME, glaucoma, cataract) are the primary determinants of long-term visual outcome.

## Follow-Up and Monitoring

| **Visit** | **Timing** | **Assessment** |
|---|---|---|
| Initial follow-up | 1 week after initiation of treatment | Anterior chamber cell/flare response, IOP, synechiae assessment |
| Subsequent visits | Every 1–2 weeks during active inflammation | Taper adjustment, IOP monitoring, complication screening |
| Post-resolution | 4–6 weeks after discontinuation of therapy | Confirm quiescence, screen for recurrence |
| Long-term monitoring | Every 3–6 months for recurrent/chronic cases | IOP, cataract screening, OCT for CME, visual acuity |

---

## Patient Education

Patients diagnosed with iritis should be counseled regarding:

- The importance of **medication compliance**, particularly with the corticosteroid taper schedule
- Recognition of **recurrence symptoms** (pain, redness, photophobia, blurred vision) and the need for prompt re-evaluation
- The potential for **recurrence** and the importance of long-term follow-up
- **Side effects** of corticosteroid therapy, including IOP elevation and cataract formation
- The possible need for **systemic workup** and referral to rheumatology or internal medicine
- Wearing **sunglasses** for photophobia management during active episodes

---

## Summary

Iritis is the most common form of intraocular inflammation and a condition frequently encountered by eye care professionals. Its clinical spectrum ranges from a single, self-limiting acute episode to a chronic, sight-threatening disease requiring long-term immunosuppression. A systematic approach to diagnosis—including thorough slit-lamp examination, appropriate grading of inflammation, identification of granulomatous versus non-granulomatous features, and targeted laboratory investigation—is essential. Treatment centers on topical corticosteroids and cycloplegic agents, with escalation to systemic immunomodulatory therapy in refractory cases. Vigilant monitoring for complications, particularly [[posterior synechiae]], [[secondary glaucoma]], [[cataract]], and [[cystoid macular edema]], is critical to preserving long-term visual function.
