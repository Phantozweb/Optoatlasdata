

# Optic Vesicle

---

The **optic vesicle** is a paired, laterally projecting evagination of the embryonic [[neuroectoderm]] arising from the [[prosencephalon]] (forebrain) during the early stages of [[embryogenesis]]. It represents one of the most critical morphogenetic structures in vertebrate eye development, serving as the primordial anlage from which the [[retina]], [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE), [[optic nerve]], and portions of the [[optic stalk]] ultimately derive. Understanding the optic vesicle is foundational for optometrists and ophthalmologists, as disruptions in its formation, signaling, or invagination underlie a broad spectrum of congenital ocular malformations including [[anophthalmia]], [[microphthalmia]], [[coloboma]], and [[optic nerve hypoplasia]].

Although the optic vesicle is a transient embryological structure and does not persist as a recognizable entity in postnatal life, its developmental biology is of paramount clinical significance. Aberrant molecular signaling during the optic vesicle stage is now recognized as the etiological basis for numerous hereditary and sporadic ocular anomalies encountered in clinical practice. This article provides a comprehensive, clinically oriented review of the optic vesicle, encompassing its embryology, molecular pathophysiology, associated congenital anomalies, diagnostic modalities, and management strategies relevant to the eye care professional.

## Embryology and Morphogenesis

### Formation of the Optic Vesicle

The development of the optic vesicle begins during the **third week of human gestation** (approximately **embryonic day 22**, Carnegie stage 10–11). The process unfolds in a tightly regulated sequence:

1. **Neural plate stage**: The [[neural plate]], a thickened region of [[ectoderm]] induced by the underlying [[notochord]] and [[prechordal mesoderm]], forms the precursor of the entire [[central nervous system]].
2. **Optic sulci formation**: Bilateral grooves known as the **optic sulci** (or optic pits) appear in the neural folds at the level of the [[diencephalon]], specifically within the region that will become the [[hypothalamus]].
3. **Neural tube closure**: As the neural folds elevate and fuse to form the [[neural tube]], the optic sulci become incorporated into the lateral walls of the [[prosencephalon]].
4. **Evagination**: The optic sulci progressively evaginate laterally, forming hollow, balloon-like protrusions—the **optic vesicles**—that extend toward the overlying [[surface ectoderm]]. Each optic vesicle remains connected to the forebrain via a constricted stalk, the **[[optic stalk]]**.
5. **Contact with surface ectoderm**: By approximately **embryonic day 25–27**, the distal tip of each optic vesicle contacts the surface ectoderm. This physical apposition is a critical inductive event that triggers the formation of the **[[lens placode]]** in the overlying ectoderm.

### Transition to the Optic Cup

The optic vesicle is a transient structure. Between **embryonic days 28 and 33** (Carnegie stages 13–15), the distal wall of the optic vesicle undergoes coordinated **invagination**, transforming the single-layered vesicle into a bilayered structure known as the **[[optic cup]]**. This invagination occurs simultaneously with the invagination of the lens placode to form the **[[lens vesicle]]**.

| **Developmental Stage** | **Approximate Gestational Day** | **Carnegie Stage** | **Key Event** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Optic sulci | Day 22 | 10 | Bilateral grooves appear in neural folds |
| Optic vesicle formation | Day 24–25 | 11–12 | Lateral evagination from diencephalon |
| Contact with surface ectoderm | Day 25–27 | 12–13 | Induction of [[lens placode]] |
| Optic cup formation | Day 28–33 | 13–15 | Invagination of optic vesicle; formation of bilayered cup |
| Optic fissure closure | Day 33–37 | 15–16 | Closure of the ventral (choroidal) fissure |

The **inner layer** of the optic cup differentiates into the **[[neural retina]]** (sensory retina), while the **outer layer** becomes the **[[retinal pigment epithelium]]** (RPE). The cavity between these two layers—the remnant of the optic vesicle lumen—is the **intraretinal space**, which persists as a potential space in the adult eye and is the plane along which [[retinal detachment]] occurs.

### The Optic Stalk

The **[[optic stalk]]** is the narrow connection between the optic vesicle and the diencephalon. As development proceeds, [[retinal ganglion cell]] axons grow along the inner wall of the optic stalk toward the brain, converting it into the **[[optic nerve]]**. The lumen of the optic stalk is gradually obliterated by these axons. The ventral surface of the optic stalk features the **[[optic fissure]]** (choroidal fissure), through which the [[hyaloid artery]] enters the developing eye. Failure of this fissure to close results in **[[coloboma]]**.

## Molecular Pathophysiology

### Signaling Pathways Governing Optic Vesicle Development

The formation, patterning, and morphogenesis of the optic vesicle are orchestrated by an intricate network of transcription factors, morphogens, and intercellular signaling cascades. Disruption of any of these pathways can result in severe congenital ocular malformations.

#### Eye Field Specification

The earliest step in optic vesicle development is the specification of the **eye field**, a region of the anterior neural plate fated to become the eyes. This process is governed by a group of transcription factors collectively termed the **eye field transcription factors (EFTFs)**:

| **Transcription Factor** | **Gene** | **Role in Optic Vesicle Development** | **Associated Human Disorder** |
|---|---|---|---|
| PAX6 | *[[PAX6]]* | Master regulator of eye development; essential for optic vesicle evagination and lens induction | [[Aniridia]], [[microphthalmia]], [[Peters anomaly]] |
| RX/RAX | *[[RAX]]* | Required for optic vesicle evagination from the diencephalon | [[Anophthalmia]], [[microphthalmia]] |
| SIX3 | *[[SIX3]]* | Anterior neural plate patterning; represses [[Wnt]] signaling | [[Holoprosencephaly]] with ocular anomalies |
| SIX6 (OPTX2) | *SIX6* | Proliferation of optic vesicle progenitors | [[Microphthalmia]], [[bilateral anophthalmia]] |
| LHX2 | *LHX2* | Optic vesicle identity and progression to optic cup | Anophthalmia in animal models |
| TBX3 | *TBX3* | Ventral optic vesicle patterning | Ulnar-mammary syndrome (with ocular features) |
| OTX2 | *[[OTX2]]* | Forebrain and RPE specification | [[Anophthalmia]], [[microphthalmia]], [[Leber congenital amaurosis]] |

#### Morphogen Gradients

Several secreted signaling molecules establish concentration gradients that pattern the optic vesicle along its dorsoventral, proximodistal, and nasotemporal axes:

- **[[Sonic Hedgehog]] (SHH)**: Secreted from the ventral midline ([[prechordal plate]] and [[ventral diencephalon]]), SHH is essential for splitting the single eye field into two bilateral optic primordia. Loss of SHH signaling results in **[[cyclopia]]** (a single midline eye), as seen in severe [[holoprosencephaly]]. Conversely, excessive SHH signaling can suppress eye formation entirely.
- **[[Bone Morphogenetic Proteins]] (BMPs)**: BMP4 and BMP7 are expressed in the optic vesicle and overlying ectoderm, playing roles in lens induction and dorsal-ventral patterning. BMP signaling in the distal optic vesicle is critical for the induction of the [[lens placode]].
- **[[Fibroblast Growth Factors]] (FGFs)**: FGF signaling from the surface ectoderm promotes neural retina differentiation in the inner layer of the optic cup, while suppressing RPE fate. FGF1 and FGF2 are particularly important in this context.
- **[[Wnt signaling pathway]]**: Canonical Wnt signaling must be actively repressed in the anterior neural plate for eye field specification to occur. Later, Wnt signaling contributes to the differentiation of the RPE and ciliary margin.
- **[[Retinoic acid]]**: Synthesized by [[retinaldehyde dehydrogenases]] (RALDH enzymes) in the periocular mesenchyme, retinoic acid is essential for ventral optic cup morphogenesis and optic fissure closure. Deficiency of retinoic acid signaling is associated with [[coloboma]].

#### Tissue Interactions

The optic vesicle does not develop in isolation. Reciprocal inductive interactions between the optic vesicle and adjacent tissues are essential:

- **Optic vesicle → Surface ectoderm**: The optic vesicle induces the overlying surface ectoderm to thicken and form the [[lens placode]]. This induction requires direct contact and involves BMP, FGF, and PAX6-dependent signaling.
- **Surface ectoderm → Optic vesicle**: Signals from the lens placode (particularly FGFs) reciprocally instruct the distal optic vesicle to differentiate as neural retina rather than RPE.
- **Periocular mesenchyme → Optic vesicle**: [[Neural crest cell]]-derived mesenchyme surrounding the optic vesicle provides retinoic acid and other signals necessary for proper morphogenesis, including optic fissure closure.

## Congenital Anomalies Arising from Optic Vesicle Defects

Disruptions at the optic vesicle stage of development produce some of the most severe congenital eye malformations. These conditions are of direct clinical relevance to optometrists, who may be the first to evaluate affected infants and children.

### Anophthalmia

**[[Anophthalmia]]** refers to the complete absence of the eye (globe). **True anophthalmia** results from a failure of the optic vesicle to form or from its complete regression. It may be unilateral or bilateral and can occur in isolation or as part of a syndrome (e.g., **SOX2-related anophthalmia**, **OTX2 mutations**). The incidence is approximately **3 per 100,000 live births**.

- **Molecular basis**: Mutations in *[[SOX2]]* (the most common genetic cause, accounting for ~15–20% of bilateral cases), *OTX2*, *RAX*, *PAX6*, and *BMP4* have been implicated.
- **Clinical features**: Absent globe, small or absent [[orbit]], reduced [[eyelid]] fissure, absent [[extraocular muscles]] or their remnants.

### Microphthalmia

**[[Microphthalmia]]** is defined as an eye with a total axial length at least two standard deviations below the mean for age (generally <21 mm in an adult, <16 mm at birth). It results from arrested or incomplete development of the optic vesicle or optic cup.

- **Classification**:
  - **Simple microphthalmia**: Small but anatomically normal eye.
  - **Complex microphthalmia**: Small eye with associated structural anomalies (e.g., [[coloboma]], [[cataract]], [[persistent fetal vasculature]]).
  - **Clinical anophthalmia (extreme microphthalmia)**: Severely reduced globe detectable only on imaging.
- **Molecular basis**: Mutations in *SOX2*, *OTX2*, *PAX6*, *CHX10/VSX2*, *FOXE3*, *GDF6*, *GDF3*, *BMP4*, and *SHH* pathway genes.

### Coloboma

**[[Coloboma]]** results from incomplete closure of the **[[optic fissure]]** (choroidal fissure), which normally closes during the 5th–7th week of gestation. Since the optic fissure is a structural feature of the optic vesicle/optic cup, coloboma is directly linked to optic vesicle morphogenesis.

- **Types**: [[Iris coloboma]], [[chorioretinal coloboma]], [[optic disc coloboma]], [[lens coloboma]].
- **Location**: Characteristically located in the **inferonasal** quadrant, corresponding to the position of the optic fissure.
- **Associated syndromes**: [[CHARGE syndrome]] (*CHD7* mutations), [[Cat eye syndrome]] (chromosome 22 partial tetrasomy), [[Treacher Collins syndrome]], [[Patau syndrome]] (trisomy 13).

### Cyclopia and Synophthalmia

**[[Cyclopia]]** (a single midline eye) and **[[synophthalmia]]** (partial fusion of two eyes) result from failure of the single eye field to split into bilateral optic vesicles, typically due to deficient [[Sonic Hedgehog]] signaling. These are invariably lethal and are most commonly associated with **[[holoprosencephaly]]**.

### Optic Nerve Hypoplasia

**[[Optic nerve hypoplasia]]** (ONH) involves a reduced number of [[retinal ganglion cell]] axons in the [[optic nerve]], resulting in a small optic disc. While ONH can arise from multiple etiologies, some cases are attributable to defective optic vesicle/optic stalk development. ONH is a key feature of **[[septo-optic dysplasia]]** (de Morsier syndrome), associated with mutations in *HESX1*, *SOX2*, and *OTX2*.

### Congenital Cystic Eye

**Congenital cystic eye** is a rare malformation in which the optic vesicle fails to invaginate to form the optic cup, instead persisting as a fluid-filled cyst lined by neuroectodermal tissue. No recognizable ocular structures (lens, retina, or optic nerve) are present. This condition is distinct from [[microphthalmos with cyst]], in which a small globe coexists with a cyst that herniates through an unclosed optic fissure.

## Clinical Presentation (Symptoms and Signs)

Because the optic vesicle is an embryonic structure, "symptoms" of optic vesicle pathology manifest as **congenital ocular anomalies** detected at birth or in early infancy. The clinical presentation depends on the specific anomaly and its severity.

| **Anomaly** | **Clinical Presentation** | **Visual Prognosis** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Anophthalmia]] | Absent globe; sunken orbit; small palpebral fissure | No light perception (NLP) |
| [[Microphthalmia]] (simple) | Small eye; may have reduced vision | Variable; may retain useful vision |
| [[Microphthalmia]] (complex) | Small eye with coloboma, cataract, or other anomalies | Often severely reduced |
| [[Coloboma]] (iris) | Keyhole-shaped pupil (inferonasal); photophobia | Usually good unless macular/optic disc involved |
| [[Coloboma]] (chorioretinal) | Inferonasal fundus defect; may involve macula or disc | Poor if macula/disc involved |
| [[Optic nerve hypoplasia]] | Small optic disc; "double ring" sign; nystagmus; reduced vision | Variable; often significantly reduced |
| Congenital cystic eye | Cystic mass in orbit; absent globe | NLP |
| [[Cyclopia]] | Single midline eye; proboscis; lethal | Incompatible with life |

### Systemic Associations

Optic vesicle-related anomalies frequently occur in the context of systemic syndromes. The eye care professional must be vigilant for associated findings:

- **[[CHARGE syndrome]]**: Coloboma, Heart defects, Atresia choanae, Retardation of growth, Genital anomalies, Ear anomalies.
- **[[Septo-optic dysplasia]]**: Optic nerve hypoplasia, midline brain defects (absent [[septum pellucidum]]), [[pituitary]] dysfunction (growth hormone deficiency, [[hypothyroidism]], [[adrenal insufficiency]]).
- **[[Patau syndrome]] (Trisomy 13)**: Microphthalmia, coloboma, cyclopia, holoprosencephaly, cleft lip/palate, polydactyly.
- **[[Walker-Warburg syndrome]]**: Microphthalmia, retinal dysplasia, congenital muscular dystrophy, brain malformations.
- **[[Lenz microphthalmia syndrome]]**: Microphthalmia/anophthalmia, skeletal anomalies, urogenital anomalies (X-linked).

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Prenatal Diagnosis

Advances in prenatal imaging and genetic testing have enabled the detection of optic vesicle-related anomalies before birth:

- **Prenatal [[ultrasonography]]**: Transvaginal and transabdominal ultrasound can detect anophthalmia and severe microphthalmia as early as the **first trimester** (11–14 weeks). The fetal orbits and lens echoes are routinely assessed during the **second-trimester anomaly scan** (18–22 weeks). Absent or small orbits, absent lens echoes, and orbital cysts are key findings.
- **Fetal [[magnetic resonance imaging]] (MRI)**: Provides superior soft tissue contrast compared to ultrasound and can delineate the globe, optic nerve, and intracranial structures. Particularly useful for confirming anophthalmia versus extreme microphthalmia and for evaluating associated brain anomalies (e.g., holoprosencephaly, absent septum pellucidum).
- **Genetic testing**: Chromosomal microarray, targeted gene panels (e.g., *SOX2*, *OTX2*, *PAX6*, *RAX*, *CHD7*), and whole exome sequencing (WES) can identify causative mutations. Amniocentesis or chorionic villus sampling provides fetal DNA for analysis.

### Postnatal Diagnosis

- **Clinical examination**: External inspection, measurement of corneal diameter (normal newborn: ~9.5–10.5 mm), assessment of palpebral fissure width, and pupillary examination. A keyhole-shaped pupil suggests iris coloboma.
- **[[Ophthalmoscopy]] / [[Fundoscopy]]**: Dilated fundus examination reveals chorioretinal colobomas (white, well-demarcated inferonasal defects), optic disc colobomas, or optic nerve hypoplasia (small disc, "double ring" sign).
- **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]**: Essential for measuring axial length (to confirm microphthalmia) and for evaluating intraocular structures when media opacity prevents direct visualization. Also detects orbital cysts associated with microphthalmos with cyst.
- **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)**: High-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the retina and optic nerve head. Useful for characterizing the retinal architecture in colobomatous eyes and for documenting optic nerve hypoplasia.
- **[[MRI]] of the orbits and brain**: Gold standard for differentiating true anophthalmia from extreme microphthalmia. Evaluates the optic nerves, chiasm, and brain for associated anomalies (e.g., absent septum pellucidum in septo-optic dysplasia, holoprosencephaly).
- **[[Computed tomography]] (CT)**: Useful for evaluating bony orbital anatomy, particularly when planning surgical reconstruction or prosthetic fitting.
- **[[Electroretinography]] (ERG) and [[Visual evoked potentials]] (VEP)**: Functional tests to assess retinal and visual pathway integrity in eyes with structural anomalies.
- **Genetic testing**: Postnatal genetic evaluation is critical for establishing a molecular diagnosis, guiding prognosis, enabling genetic counseling, and identifying systemic associations. Recommended panels include genes such as *SOX2*, *OTX2*, *PAX6*, *RAX*, *VSX2*, *FOXE3*, *GDF6*, *GDF3*, *BMP4*, *SHH*, *CHD7*, and *HESX1*.

### Diagnostic Algorithm

A structured approach to evaluating suspected optic vesicle-related anomalies is recommended:

1. **External examination** → Assess globe presence, size, palpebral fissure, corneal diameter.
2. **Anterior segment examination** → Slit-lamp biomicroscopy (if possible); assess for iris coloboma, cataract, anterior segment dysgenesis.
3. **Dilated fundus examination** → Assess for coloboma, optic nerve hypoplasia, retinal dysplasia.
4. **B-scan ultrasonography** → Measure axial length; evaluate for cyst, intraocular disorganization.
5. **MRI orbits and brain** → Confirm diagnosis; evaluate optic nerves and brain.
6. **Electrophysiology** (ERG/VEP) → Assess functional visual potential.
7. **Genetic testing** → Targeted panel or whole exome sequencing.
8. **Systemic evaluation** → Pediatric, endocrine, cardiac, audiological assessments as indicated.

## Management and Treatment

There is no treatment that can restore or recreate the optic vesicle postnatally. Management of optic vesicle-related anomalies is therefore directed toward **maximizing visual function**, **promoting normal orbital and facial growth**, **addressing systemic comorbidities**, and **providing psychosocial support**.

### Orbital and Prosthetic Management

In anophthalmia and severe microphthalmia, the absence or severe reduction of the globe leads to **orbital hypoplasia** due to the lack of a growth stimulus. Early intervention is critical:

- **Conformers and expanders**: Custom-fitted, progressively larger solid conformers or inflatable hydrogel expanders are placed in the [[conjunctival]] socket beginning in the first weeks of life. These devices stimulate orbital bone growth and expand the soft tissues to accommodate a prosthetic eye.
- **[[Ocular prosthesis]]**: A custom-painted prosthetic eye is fitted once the socket is adequately expanded (typically by age 1–2 years). The prosthesis is periodically replaced as the child grows.
- **Dermis-fat grafts**: In cases of severely contracted sockets, autologous dermis-fat grafts (harvested from the abdomen or buttock) may be used to augment orbital volume.
- **Orbital osteotomy / distraction osteogenesis**: In severe orbital hypoplasia, surgical expansion of the bony orbit may be necessary, typically performed by a craniofacial surgeon.

### Optical and Visual Rehabilitation

- **Refractive correction**: Eyes with microphthalmia or coloboma may have significant [[refractive error]] (often high [[hyperopia]]). Spectacles or [[contact lenses]] should be prescribed as early as possible to optimize visual development and prevent [[amblyopia]].
- **Low vision aids**: Magnifiers, telescopic devices, electronic magnification systems, and other [[low vision]] aids may be beneficial for patients with reduced visual acuity.
- **Amblyopia treatment**: In unilateral cases, [[patching]] or [[atropine penalization]] of the better eye may be indicated to maximize vision in the affected eye, provided there is sufficient visual potential.

### Surgical Interventions

- **Coloboma-related complications**: Chorioretinal colobomas predispose to [[retinal detachment]] (reported in up to 40% of eyes with large colobomas). Retinal detachment in colobomatous eyes is challenging to repair and may require [[pars plana vitrectomy]], [[endolaser photocoagulation]], [[scleral buckle]], or [[silicone oil]] tamponade.
- **Cataract surgery**: Congenital [[cataract]] associated with microphthalmia or coloboma may require [[lensectomy]] with or without [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) implantation. IOL power calculation is complicated by the abnormal axial length.
- **Glaucoma management**: [[Glaucoma]] can occur in colobomatous and microphthalmic eyes. Medical therapy ([[topical antiglaucoma agents]]) is first-line; surgical options include [[goniotomy]], [[trabeculotomy]], [[trabeculectomy]], or [[glaucoma drainage device]] implantation.
- **Strabismus surgery**: [[Strabismus]] is common in eyes with optic vesicle-related anomalies and may require surgical correction of [[extraocular muscles]].

### Endocrine and Systemic Management

Patients with [[septo-optic dysplasia]] require lifelong endocrine monitoring and hormone replacement therapy as needed:

- **Growth hormone deficiency** → Recombinant growth hormone.
- **[[Hypothyroidism]]** → Levothyroxine.
- **[[Adrenal insufficiency]]** → Hydrocortisone (potentially life-threatening if unrecognized).
- **[[Diabetes insipidus]]** → Desmopressin (DDAVP).
- **Delayed puberty** → Sex hormone replacement.

Patients with [[CHARGE syndrome]] require multidisciplinary management including cardiac surgery, hearing aids or cochlear implants, and feeding support.

### Genetic Counseling

Genetic counseling is an essential component of management for all families affected by optic vesicle-related anomalies:

- **Inheritance patterns**: Autosomal dominant (*PAX6*, *OTX2*), autosomal recessive (*RAX*, *VSX2*, *FOXE3*), X-linked (*BCOR* in Lenz microphthalmia), or de novo mutations (*SOX2* in most cases).
- **Recurrence risk**: Varies by gene and inheritance pattern; de novo *SOX2* mutations carry a low recurrence risk (~1%), while autosomal recessive conditions carry a 25% recurrence risk.
- **Prenatal testing**: Available for families with known mutations; preimplantation genetic diagnosis (PGD) may be offered.

### Psychosocial Support

The impact of congenital ocular anomalies on the patient and family is profound. Referral to support organizations, psychological counseling, and early intervention programs for visually impaired children is strongly recommended. Multidisciplinary care involving the optometrist, ophthalmologist, oculoplastic surgeon, ocularist, geneticist, pediatrician, endocrinologist, and psychologist is the standard of care.

## Experimental and Emerging Research

### Optic Vesicle Organoids

One of the most exciting developments in optic vesicle research is the generation of **optic vesicle-like organoids** from human [[induced pluripotent stem cells]] (iPSCs) and [[embryonic stem cells]] (ESCs). Pioneering work by Yoshiki Sasai and colleagues demonstrated that three-dimensional culture of stem cells can spontaneously recapitulate optic vesicle formation and invagination, producing layered retinal tissue containing photoreceptors, bipolar cells, and ganglion cells. These organoids are being used for:

- **Disease modeling**: Patient-derived iPSC organoids can model optic vesicle defects caused by specific mutations (e.g., *VSX2*, *PAX6*).
- **Drug screening**: High-throughput testing of compounds that may rescue developmental defects.
- **Regenerative medicine**: Long-term goal of transplanting organoid-derived retinal tissue to restore vision in degenerative diseases such as [[retinitis pigmentosa]] and [[age-related macular degeneration]].

### Gene Therapy

Advances in [[gene therapy]] offer the potential to correct genetic defects underlying optic vesicle-related anomalies. While current gene therapy for ocular conditions (e.g., [[voretigene neparvovec]] for *RPE65*-associated [[Leber congenital amaurosis]]) targets postnatal retinal disease, future applications may include prenatal or early postnatal gene correction for developmental eye disorders.

### CRISPR-Based Approaches

[[CRISPR-Cas9]] genome editing technology is being explored in animal models to correct mutations in optic vesicle-related genes (*Pax6*, *Sox2*, *Otx2*). While clinical application remains distant, these studies provide proof-of-concept for genetic correction of developmental eye anomalies.

## Summary

The optic vesicle is a transient but critically important embryonic structure whose proper formation and morphogenesis are prerequisites for normal eye development. Arising from the diencephalon during the third week of gestation, the optic vesicle gives rise to the retina, RPE, and optic nerve through a precisely orchestrated sequence of evagination, induction, and invagination events governed by transcription factors (PAX6, SOX2, OTX2, RAX) and signaling pathways (SHH, BMP, FGF, Wnt, retinoic acid). Disruptions in optic vesicle development produce a spectrum of congenital ocular anomalies—anophthalmia, microphthalmia, coloboma, optic nerve hypoplasia, and congenital cystic eye—that are among the most visually devastating conditions encountered in clinical practice. Diagnosis relies on clinical examination, imaging (ultrasound, MRI, OCT), electrophysiology, and genetic testing. Management is multidisciplinary and includes prosthetic rehabilitation, optical correction, surgical intervention for complications, endocrine management, genetic counseling, and psychosocial support. Emerging research in stem cell-derived optic vesicle organoids and gene therapy holds promise for future therapeutic advances.
