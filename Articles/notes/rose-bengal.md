# Rose Bengal

**Rose Bengal** (4,5,6,7-tetrachloro-2',4',5',7'-tetraiodofluorescein) is a vital [[ophthalmic dye]] of the [[fluorescein]] derivative family, historically and clinically indispensable in the diagnosis and evaluation of [[ocular surface disease]]. A water-soluble [[xanthene dye]], Rose Bengal has been employed in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] for over a century, serving as a critical diagnostic staining agent for the assessment of [[corneal]] and [[conjunctival]] epithelial integrity. In recent decades, Rose Bengal has also garnered significant attention for its photodynamic therapeutic properties, expanding its role from a purely diagnostic tool to a potential therapeutic agent in both ophthalmic and oncologic medicine.

Rose Bengal is classified by its **Chemical Abstracts Service (CAS) number 632-69-9** and carries the **Colour Index designation C.I. 45440**. It is available in ophthalmic preparations as a **1% solution** or as **impregnated diagnostic strips**, both designed for topical application to the [[ocular surface]].

---

## History and Background

Rose Bengal was first synthesized in the late 19th century and was introduced into ophthalmic practice by **Sjögren in 1933**, who utilized the dye to identify devitalized [[epithelial cells]] on the [[cornea]] and [[conjunctiva]] in patients with [[keratoconjunctivitis sicca]] (KCS). This landmark application cemented Rose Bengal's role in the diagnosis of [[dry eye disease]] and related [[ocular surface disorders]]. For decades, Rose Bengal was considered the gold standard vital dye for detecting ocular surface damage, particularly in the context of [[Sjögren syndrome]].

The dye's prominence was somewhat diminished with the introduction of [[lissamine green]], which provides similar staining characteristics with significantly less patient discomfort. Nevertheless, Rose Bengal remains a critically important diagnostic agent, and its unique photochemical properties have opened new avenues of clinical and research interest, particularly in **Rose Bengal photodynamic therapy (RB-PDT)** for [[corneal]] infections and neoplastic conditions.

## Chemical Properties and Pharmacology

### Chemical Structure

Rose Bengal is a **polyhalogenated xanthene dye** — specifically, it is the **disodium salt of 4,5,6,7-tetrachloro-2',4',5',7'-tetraiodofluorescein**. Its molecular formula is **C₂₀H₂Cl₄I₄Na₂O₅**, and it has a molecular weight of approximately **1,017.64 g/mol**. The molecule features:

- A central [[xanthene]] ring system responsible for its chromophoric (color-producing) properties
- Four [[chlorine]] atoms and four [[iodine]] atoms, which confer its unique staining and photosensitizing characteristics
- Two [[sodium]] ions, rendering the compound water-soluble for ophthalmic formulation

|---|---|
| **Chemical Name** | 4,5,6,7-Tetrachloro-2',4',5',7'-tetraiodofluorescein disodium salt |
| **Molecular Formula** | C₂₀H₂Cl₄I₄Na₂O₅ |
| **Molecular Weight** | ~1,017.64 g/mol |

| **Colour Index** | C.I. 45440 |

| **Solubility** | Freely soluble in water |

| **Emission Maximum** | ~572 nm |
| **Ophthalmic Concentration** | 1% solution or impregnated strips |

### Mechanism of Staining

The precise mechanism by which Rose Bengal stains the [[ocular surface]] has been the subject of considerable scientific debate and revision over the decades.

#### Classical Theory: Dead and Devitalized Cell Staining

The traditional understanding, dating from Sjögren's original work, held that Rose Bengal selectively stains **dead or degenerated [[epithelial cells]]** and **[[mucus]] strands** on the ocular surface. Under this paradigm, healthy cells with intact [[cell membranes]] were thought to exclude the dye, while damaged or devitalized cells with compromised membranes would take up the stain, appearing as bright **rose-pink to magenta punctate spots** upon [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] examination.

#### Revised Theory: Absence of Mucin Protection

More contemporary research, particularly the work of **Feenstra and Tseng (1992)**, fundamentally revised this understanding. Their studies demonstrated that Rose Bengal does **not** preferentially stain dead cells per se, but rather stains **any epithelial cell that is not adequately protected by the [[precorneal tear film]]**, specifically the [[mucin layer]]. In this model:

- Healthy [[epithelial cells]] covered by a normal [[glycocalyx]] and [[mucin]] layer repel the dye due to the **electrostatic and steric barrier** provided by membrane-associated [[mucins]] (e.g., [[MUC1]], [[MUC4]], [[MUC16]]).
- Cells that have lost their protective mucin coating — whether due to [[dry eye]], [[inflammation]], or other pathology — become accessible to the dye and stain positively.
- This explains why Rose Bengal staining is so prominent in conditions characterized by **mucin deficiency**, such as [[Sjögren syndrome]] and [[mucin-deficient dry eye]].

#### Photosensitizing Properties

Rose Bengal is a potent **[[photosensitizer]]**. Upon absorption of light in the green spectrum (~548 nm), the dye undergoes excitation to a singlet excited state, followed by [[intersystem crossing]] to a long-lived triplet state. This triplet state can then transfer energy to molecular [[oxygen]], generating highly cytotoxic **[[reactive oxygen species]] (ROS)**, including **[[singlet oxygen]] (¹O₂)** and **superoxide radicals**. This photodynamic mechanism underlies both the dye's inherent toxicity to ocular surface cells upon light exposure and its therapeutic application in **[[photodynamic therapy]]**.

## Pathophysiology of Rose Bengal Staining

Understanding the pathophysiology of Rose Bengal staining requires a thorough appreciation of [[ocular surface]] physiology and the [[tear film]] architecture.

### Normal Ocular Surface Defense

The [[ocular surface]] is protected by a multi-layered defense system:

1. **[[Tear film]]**: Composed of an outer [[lipid layer]] (secreted by [[meibomian glands]]), a middle [[aqueous layer]] (produced by the [[lacrimal gland]] and accessory lacrimal glands of [[Krause]] and [[Wolfring]]), and an inner [[mucin layer]] (produced by [[goblet cells]] and surface epithelial cells).
2. **[[Glycocalyx]]**: A dense carbohydrate-rich layer composed of transmembrane mucins ([[MUC1]], [[MUC4]], [[MUC16]]) expressed on the apical surface of [[corneal]] and [[conjunctival]] epithelial cells.
3. **[[Epithelial barrier]]**: Tight junctions ([[zonula occludens]]) between surface epithelial cells prevent paracellular penetration of dyes and pathogens.

### Conditions Leading to Positive Rose Bengal Staining

Rose Bengal staining occurs when the protective mucin barrier is disrupted or absent. The pathophysiological cascade typically involves:

1. **Mucin deficiency or dysfunction**: Loss of [[goblet cells]] (as in [[Sjögren syndrome]], [[Stevens-Johnson syndrome]], [[ocular cicatricial pemphigoid]], [[chemical burns]], or [[vitamin A deficiency]]) leads to inadequate mucin coverage.
2. **Tear film instability**: Reduced [[tear breakup time]] (TBUT) exposes epithelial cells to desiccation and environmental insult.
3. **Epithelial cell damage**: Chronic desiccation, [[inflammation]], or toxic exposure leads to [[apoptosis]], loss of surface [[microvilli]], and disruption of the [[glycocalyx]].
4. **Inflammatory mediators**: Pro-inflammatory [[cytokines]] (e.g., [[IL-1β]], [[IL-6]], [[TNF-α]], [[MMP-9]]) released in [[dry eye disease]] and other inflammatory conditions further degrade the mucin layer and damage epithelial cells.

The result is a population of surface epithelial cells that, while potentially still viable, lack adequate mucin protection and therefore stain positively with Rose Bengal.

### Staining Patterns and Their Clinical Significance

The distribution and pattern of Rose Bengal staining provide critical diagnostic information:

| Staining Pattern | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|
| **Interpalpebral conjunctival staining (nasal and temporal)** | Classic pattern of [[aqueous-deficient dry eye]] / [[keratoconjunctivitis sicca]]; hallmark of [[Sjögren syndrome]] |
| **Inferior corneal and conjunctival staining** | Suggestive of [[exposure keratopathy]], [[lagophthalmos]], or [[inferior tear film insufficiency]] |
| **Superior conjunctival/corneal staining** | Associated with [[superior limbic keratoconjunctivitis]] (SLK of Theodore), [[floppy eyelid syndrome]] |
| **Diffuse corneal staining** | Severe [[dry eye]], [[neurotrophic keratopathy]], [[chemical injury]], or [[toxic keratopathy]] |
| **Lid margin staining (Marx's line)** | Staining of the [[mucocutaneous junction]] of the [[eyelid margin]]; associated with [[meibomian gland dysfunction]] and [[lid wiper epitheliopathy]] |
| **Filamentary staining** | [[Mucus filaments]] and [[epithelial debris]] stain intensely; seen in [[filamentary keratitis]] |
| **Perilimbal staining** | May indicate [[limbal stem cell deficiency]] |

## Clinical Indications and Diagnostic Applications

### Primary Diagnostic Uses

Rose Bengal is indicated for the clinical evaluation of the following conditions:

1. **[[Keratoconjunctivitis sicca]] (KCS) / [[Dry eye disease]]**: Rose Bengal is one of the most sensitive indicators of ocular surface damage in dry eye, particularly in **[[aqueous-deficient dry eye]]** and **[[Sjögren syndrome]]**.
2. **[[Sjögren syndrome]]**: Rose Bengal staining of the [[conjunctiva]] is one of the objective criteria in the **American-European Consensus Group (AECG)** and **ACR/EULAR classification criteria** for Sjögren syndrome.
3. **[[Ocular surface disease]]** secondary to:
   - [[Stevens-Johnson syndrome]] / [[toxic epidermal necrolysis]]
   - [[Ocular cicatricial pemphigoid]]
   - [[Graft-versus-host disease]] (GVHD)
   - [[Chemical burns]]
   - [[Radiation keratopathy]]
   - [[Neurotrophic keratopathy]]
4. **[[Superior limbic keratoconjunctivitis]]** (SLK of Theodore)
5. **[[Exposure keratopathy]]** and [[lagophthalmos]]
6. **[[Lid wiper epitheliopathy]]**
7. **Assessment of [[contact lens]]-related ocular surface changes**
8. **Evaluation of [[limbal stem cell deficiency]]**

### Grading Systems

Several standardized grading systems have been developed to quantify Rose Bengal staining:

#### Van Bijsterveld Scoring System

The most widely used grading system for Rose Bengal staining is the **van Bijsterveld score**, introduced by **van Bijsterveld in 1969**. The ocular surface is divided into **three zones**:

1. **Nasal [[conjunctiva]]**
2. **[[Cornea]]**
3. **Temporal [[conjunctiva]]**

Each zone is graded on a scale of **0 to 3**:

| Grade | Description |
|---|---|
| **0** | No staining |
| **1** | Sparse, scattered punctate staining (few dots) |
| **2** | Moderate punctate staining (easily countable dots) |
| **3** | Confluent staining (too many dots to count; coalescent patches) |

The **maximum total score is 9** (3 per zone × 3 zones). A score of **≥ 4** is generally considered clinically significant and is one of the diagnostic criteria for [[Sjögren syndrome]].

#### SICCA Ocular Staining Score (OSS)

The **Ocular Staining Score (OSS)**, developed for the **Sjögren's International Collaborative Clinical Alliance (SICCA)** registry, is a more refined grading system that can be applied to Rose Bengal, [[fluorescein]], or [[lissamine green]] staining. The OSS divides the ocular surface into the same three zones but allows for a maximum score of **12** (0–6 for the cornea, 0–3 for each conjunctival zone), with additional points awarded for specific corneal findings such as **confluent staining**, **central corneal staining**, or the presence of **[[corneal filaments]]**.

## Technique of Application

### Preparation and Instillation

Rose Bengal is available in two primary formulations for ophthalmic use:

1. **1% Rose Bengal ophthalmic solution**: Instilled as a single drop into the [[inferior conjunctival fornix]].
2. **Rose Bengal-impregnated paper strips**: Wetted with a drop of **preservative-free [[saline]]** or **non-preserved artificial tears** and touched to the [[inferior conjunctival cul-de-sac]].

**Important procedural considerations:**

- **Topical [[anesthetic]]** (e.g., [[proparacaine]] 0.5%) is **not routinely recommended** prior to Rose Bengal instillation for diagnostic purposes, as anesthetics may alter the staining pattern. However, given the significant discomfort associated with Rose Bengal, some clinicians elect to use a topical anesthetic, accepting the potential for minor artifact.
- The patient should be instructed to **blink several times** after instillation to distribute the dye evenly across the [[ocular surface]].
- Examination should be performed **within 1–2 minutes** of instillation using a [[slit-lamp biomicroscope]].
- A **white light** or a **green (red-free) filter** on the slit lamp enhances visualization of Rose Bengal staining. Unlike [[fluorescein]], Rose Bengal does **not** require a [[cobalt blue filter]] or a [[Wratten #12 yellow barrier filter]] for observation — it is visible under standard white illumination as bright **rose-pink to magenta** punctate spots.

### Comparison with Other Vital Dyes

| Feature | [[Rose Bengal]] | [[Fluorescein Sodium]] | [[Lissamine Green]] |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Dye Class** | Xanthene (halogenated fluorescein) | Xanthene | Aminotriarylmethane |
| **Staining Target** | Cells lacking mucin protection; dead/devitalized cells; mucus | Epithelial defects (areas of absent epithelium); tear film assessment | Cells lacking mucin protection; dead/devitalized cells |
| **Observation Method** | White light or red-free filter | Cobalt blue light ± Wratten #12 filter | White light or red-free filter |
| **Color of Staining** | Rose-pink / magenta | Bright green/yellow-green | Green |
| **Patient Comfort** | **Significant stinging/discomfort** | Minimal discomfort | Minimal discomfort |
| **Photosensitizing** | Yes (potent) | No | No |
| **Toxicity to Cells** | Yes (dose- and light-dependent) | Minimal | Minimal |
| **Availability** | Decreasing (limited commercial availability in some markets) | Widely available | Widely available |
| **Primary Clinical Use** | Dry eye, Sjögren syndrome, ocular surface disease | Corneal abrasions, TBUT, contact lens fitting, applanation tonometry | Dry eye, ocular surface disease (preferred alternative to Rose Bengal) |

## Symptoms and Patient Experience

### Discomfort Upon Instillation

One of the most clinically significant limitations of Rose Bengal is the **substantial ocular discomfort** it causes upon instillation. Patients typically report:

- **Intense stinging and burning** immediately upon application
- **Tearing** ([[epiphora]])
- **[[Blepharospasm]]** (reflexive eyelid squeezing)
- **Conjunctival [[hyperemia]]** (redness)
- Discomfort may persist for **several minutes to hours** after instillation

The mechanism of this discomfort is multifactorial:

1. **Direct cytotoxicity**: Rose Bengal is inherently toxic to [[epithelial cells]], particularly in the presence of light, due to its photosensitizing properties and generation of [[reactive oxygen species]].
2. **Osmotic effects**: The dye solution may have osmolarity characteristics that irritate the ocular surface.
3. **Staining of damaged nerve endings**: In patients with pre-existing ocular surface disease, exposed [[corneal nerves]] and damaged epithelium are particularly sensitive.

This discomfort profile is the primary reason that [[lissamine green]] has largely supplanted Rose Bengal in routine clinical practice, as lissamine green provides comparable staining information with markedly less patient discomfort.

### Photosensitivity Considerations

Due to its photosensitizing properties, patients should be advised to **avoid bright light exposure** immediately following Rose Bengal instillation, as light activation of the dye on the ocular surface can exacerbate cellular damage and discomfort. This is particularly relevant in clinical settings with intense slit-lamp illumination.

## Diagnosis: Role of Rose Bengal in Clinical Assessment

### Dry Eye Disease

Rose Bengal staining is a cornerstone of the diagnostic evaluation of [[dry eye disease]], particularly in the identification and grading of **ocular surface damage**. In the context of the **DEWS II (Dry Eye Workshop II)** diagnostic algorithm, vital dye staining (including Rose Bengal, [[fluorescein]], and [[lissamine green]]) is classified as a **marker of ocular surface damage** and is used to confirm the diagnosis and assess severity.

In [[aqueous-deficient dry eye]], the classic Rose Bengal staining pattern consists of:

- **Bilateral interpalpebral conjunctival staining**, most prominent in the **nasal** and **temporal** exposed zones
- **Corneal staining** in more advanced cases, typically beginning in the **inferior** and **interpalpebral** zones
- **Staining of mucus filaments and debris** in the [[tear film]]

### Sjögren Syndrome

Rose Bengal staining holds particular diagnostic importance in [[Sjögren syndrome]]. The **van Bijsterveld score ≥ 4** is included as one of the objective ocular criteria in multiple classification systems for Sjögren syndrome, including:

- **American-European Consensus Group (AECG) criteria (2002)**
- **ACR/EULAR classification criteria (2016)** (which use the OSS with a threshold of ≥ 5 using fluorescein or lissamine green, though Rose Bengal remains acceptable)

### Differential Staining Interpretation

It is essential for the clinician to distinguish Rose Bengal staining from other vital dye staining patterns:

- **Rose Bengal positive / Fluorescein negative**: Indicates cells that are present but lack mucin protection — the epithelium is intact but dysfunctional. This is characteristic of early or moderate [[dry eye disease]].
- **Rose Bengal positive / Fluorescein positive**: Indicates areas of frank epithelial loss or severe epithelial damage. This is seen in advanced [[dry eye]], [[corneal ulceration]], or [[epithelial erosions]].
- **Rose Bengal negative / Fluorescein positive**: Uncommon but may occur in acute epithelial defects where the underlying cells have not yet lost mucin protection.

## Treatment and Therapeutic Applications

### Rose Bengal Photodynamic Therapy (RB-PDT)

In recent years, Rose Bengal has been investigated extensively as a **photosensitizer for [[photodynamic therapy]] (PDT)** in ophthalmic applications. This represents a paradigm shift from its traditional role as a purely diagnostic agent.

#### Mechanism of RB-PDT

Rose Bengal photodynamic therapy exploits the dye's potent photosensitizing properties:

1. **Rose Bengal is applied** to the target tissue (e.g., infected [[cornea]], [[conjunctival]] or [[corneal]] neoplasm).
2. **Green light (wavelength ~525–550 nm)** is applied to the treated area.
3. The dye absorbs the light energy, undergoes excitation, and generates **[[singlet oxygen]]** and other **[[reactive oxygen species]]** via **Type I** (electron transfer) and **Type II** (energy transfer) photochemical reactions.
4. These ROS cause **oxidative damage** to target cells, including microbial organisms, neoplastic cells, and pathological tissue, leading to cell death.

#### Clinical Applications of RB-PDT

##### Infectious Keratitis

RB-PDT has shown promising results in the treatment of **[[infectious keratitis]]**, particularly in cases resistant to conventional antimicrobial therapy:

- **[[Bacterial keratitis]]**: RB-PDT has demonstrated efficacy against a broad spectrum of [[Gram-positive]] and [[Gram-negative]] bacteria, including [[Staphylococcus aureus]], [[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]], and [[methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus]] (MRSA).
- **[[Fungal keratitis]]**: Studies have shown activity against [[Fusarium]], [[Aspergillus]], and [[Candida]] species.
- **[[Acanthamoeba keratitis]]**: RB-PDT has been investigated as an adjunctive therapy for this notoriously difficult-to-treat [[parasitic]] infection.

The **PACK-CXL (Photoactivated Chromophore for Keratitis–Corneal Cross-Linking)** protocol, while primarily utilizing [[riboflavin]] and [[ultraviolet-A]] light, has inspired parallel research into Rose Bengal-based photoactivation protocols. Rose Bengal offers potential advantages including a **broader antimicrobial spectrum** and the ability to use **visible green light** rather than UV-A, potentially reducing collateral damage to deeper [[corneal]] structures.

##### Corneal Cross-Linking

**Rose Bengal/Green Light (RGX) corneal cross-linking** has been investigated as an alternative to traditional [[riboflavin]]/[[UVA]] corneal cross-linking ([[CXL]]) for the treatment of [[keratoconus]] and [[corneal ectasia]]. Potential advantages include:

- Use of **visible green light** instead of [[UVA]], reducing potential toxicity to the [[corneal endothelium]], [[lens]], and [[retina]]
- No requirement for [[epithelial debridement]] in some protocols
- Effective cross-linking at **lower irradiance levels**

Preclinical and early clinical studies have demonstrated that RGX cross-linking can increase [[corneal]] biomechanical stiffness, though large-scale randomized controlled trials are still needed to establish its efficacy relative to standard CXL.

##### Ocular Surface Neoplasia

RB-PDT has been explored as a treatment for **[[ocular surface squamous neoplasia]] (OSSN)** and other [[conjunctival]] and [[corneal]] neoplasms. The photodynamic destruction of neoplastic cells offers a potential alternative or adjunct to surgical excision, [[cryotherapy]], and topical chemotherapy (e.g., [[mitomycin C]], [[5-fluorouracil]], [[interferon alpha-2b]]).

### Rose Bengal as an Antiseptic Agent

Independent of its photodynamic properties, Rose Bengal has demonstrated intrinsic **antimicrobial activity** in vitro against various bacterial and fungal pathogens. This antimicrobial effect is enhanced in the presence of light but is also present, to a lesser degree, in dark conditions, suggesting both photodynamic and non-photodynamic mechanisms of microbial killing.

## Adverse Effects and Contraindications

### Adverse Effects

| Adverse Effect | Mechanism | Severity |
|---|---|---|
| **Ocular stinging/burning** | Direct cytotoxicity; nerve stimulation | Common; moderate to severe |
| **Epiphora** | Reflex tearing from irritation | Common; mild |
| **Blepharospasm** | Pain-mediated reflex | Common; transient |
| **Conjunctival hyperemia** | Inflammatory response | Common; mild to moderate |
| **Epithelial toxicity** | Photodynamic ROS generation; direct membrane disruption | Dose- and light-dependent |
| **Allergic/hypersensitivity reaction** | Rare immunologic response | Rare; potentially severe |
| **Skin staining** | Dye deposition on periocular skin | Common; cosmetically bothersome but temporary |
| **Corneal epithelial damage** | Phototoxicity with prolonged light exposure after instillation | Dose- and light-dependent |

### Contraindications

- **Known hypersensitivity** to Rose Bengal or any component of the formulation
- **Known allergy to [[iodine]]** (Rose Bengal contains four iodine atoms; although cross-reactivity with iodine allergy is debated and likely rare, caution is advised)
- **Relative contraindication**: Patients with severe [[ocular surface disease]] in whom the additional cytotoxic insult of Rose Bengal may exacerbate existing damage (in such cases, [[lissamine green]] is preferred)

## Current Status and Availability

The clinical availability of Rose Bengal ophthalmic preparations has **declined significantly** in many markets over the past two decades. In the United States, the commercially manufactured **1% Rose Bengal ophthalmic solution** (formerly marketed by Akorn, Inc.) has been **discontinued**, and availability is now largely limited to:

- **Compounding pharmacies** that can prepare Rose Bengal solutions on a per-prescription basis
- **Rose Bengal-impregnated diagnostic strips** (available from select ophthalmic supply companies)
- **Research-grade preparations** for investigational use in clinical trials

This declining availability has further accelerated the clinical transition to [[lissamine green]] as the preferred vital dye for ocular surface staining in routine practice. However, Rose Bengal remains available and actively used in research settings, particularly for photodynamic therapy investigations.
