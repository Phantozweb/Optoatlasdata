# Lissamine Green

---

**Lissamine Green** is a synthetic organic dye of the aminotriarylmethane class that is widely employed in clinical [[Optometry]] and [[Ophthalmology]] as a vital stain for the assessment of [[Ocular Surface Disease]]. It selectively stains devitalized, damaged, or desiccated cells of the [[Conjunctiva]] and, to a lesser extent, the [[Cornea]], making it an indispensable diagnostic tool in the evaluation of [[Dry Eye Disease]] (DED), [[Sjögren Syndrome]], [[Superior Limbic Keratoconjunctivitis]], and other conditions affecting the integrity of the [[Ocular Surface]]. Lissamine green has largely supplanted [[Rose Bengal]] in modern clinical practice due to its equivalent diagnostic sensitivity but markedly superior patient comfort profile, as it does not induce the significant stinging and cytotoxicity associated with rose bengal instillation.

---

## History and Development

The use of vital dyes in ophthalmology dates back to the early 20th century. [[Fluorescein Sodium]] was introduced for corneal staining in 1882 by Paul Ehrlich, while [[Rose Bengal]] was adopted for conjunctival and corneal staining in the 1930s by Sjögren himself during his seminal work on keratoconjunctivitis sicca. However, rose bengal's significant drawbacks—namely its intense stinging upon instillation and its inherent phototoxic and cytotoxic properties—limited patient acceptance and complicated clinical interpretation, as the dye itself could cause iatrogenic cellular damage.

Lissamine green B was first proposed as an ophthalmic vital stain by Manning, Wehrly, and Foulks in 1995. Their landmark study demonstrated that lissamine green stained the same ocular surface structures as rose bengal with comparable sensitivity and specificity, but without the associated patient discomfort or cellular toxicity. This pivotal finding led to the rapid adoption of lissamine green in clinical practice and its incorporation into standardized grading systems for ocular surface disease, including the [[Oxford Grading Scale]], the [[van Bijsterveld Scoring System]], and the [[DEWS (Dry Eye Workshop)]] diagnostic criteria. Today, lissamine green is considered a first-line vital stain for conjunctival assessment and is recommended by the [[Tear Film and Ocular Surface Society]] (TFOS) [[DEWS II]] report as an essential component of the dry eye diagnostic workup.

## Chemical Properties and Pharmacology

### Chemical Structure

Lissamine green B (also known as Acid Green 50, or C.I. 44090) is a synthetic acid dye belonging to the aminotriarylmethane family. Its molecular formula is **C₂₇H₂₅N₂NaO₇S₂**, and it has a molecular weight of approximately **576.6 Da**. The dye is characterized by two sulfonate groups that confer its high aqueous solubility and its net negative charge at physiological pH. This anionic character is central to its mechanism of staining, as it preferentially binds to sites where the normal electrostatic repulsion of the negatively charged [[Glycocalyx]] is disrupted.

### Mechanism of Staining

The precise mechanism by which lissamine green stains ocular surface cells has been the subject of considerable investigation. The prevailing theory, supported by the work of Bhatt, Kim, and Bhatt (2016) and others, holds that lissamine green stains cells that have lost the protective integrity of their [[Membrane-Associated Mucins]] (particularly [[MUC1]], [[MUC4]], and [[MUC16]]) and the underlying [[Glycocalyx]]. In healthy tissue, the dense, negatively charged glycocalyx layer on the apical surface of [[Conjunctival Epithelial Cells]] electrostatically repels the anionic lissamine green molecule, preventing dye uptake. When cells are damaged, desiccated, or devitalized—but not necessarily dead—the glycocalyx is disrupted or absent, allowing the dye to penetrate and bind to intracellular proteins and the exposed cell membrane.

This mechanism distinguishes lissamine green from [[Fluorescein Sodium]], which primarily highlights intercellular spaces and areas of frank epithelial loss (i.e., it pools in gaps between cells or in areas of denuded [[Basement Membrane]]). Lissamine green, by contrast, stains the cells themselves, providing complementary information about cellular health rather than merely epithelial continuity.

| Property | Lissamine Green B | [[Rose Bengal]] | [[Fluorescein Sodium]] |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Dye Class** | Aminotriarylmethane | Xanthene (fluorescein derivative) | Xanthene |
| **Charge at pH 7.4** | Anionic (negative) | Anionic (negative) | Anionic (negative) |
| **Primary Staining Target** | Devitalized/damaged cells with disrupted glycocalyx | Devitalized/damaged cells with disrupted glycocalyx | Epithelial defects, intercellular spaces |
| **Tissue Preference** | [[Conjunctiva]] > [[Cornea]] | [[Conjunctiva]] > [[Cornea]] | [[Cornea]] > [[Conjunctiva]] |
| **Observation Method** | White (diffuse) light; enhanced with red-free (green) filter or yellow barrier filter | White (diffuse) light | [[Cobalt Blue Light]] with [[Wratten #12 Yellow Filter]] |
| **Patient Comfort** | Excellent (minimal to no stinging) | Poor (significant stinging) | Excellent |
| **Cytotoxicity** | None at clinical concentrations | Significant (phototoxic, cytotoxic) | None at clinical concentrations |
| **Fluorescence** | Minimal (not clinically useful) | Minimal | Strong (peak emission ~520 nm) |

### Formulation and Preparation

Lissamine green is commercially available as impregnated dry strips (e.g., GreenGlo™ strips by HUB Pharmaceuticals) containing approximately **1.5 mg** of lissamine green B dye. The strip is wetted with a drop of sterile, non-preserved [[Normal Saline]] (0.9% NaCl) or, alternatively, a drop of the patient's own tears, and the resulting solution is instilled into the [[Inferior Conjunctival Fornix]]. Some practitioners use a solution prepared by dissolving lissamine green powder in saline to a concentration of **1.0%**, though pre-manufactured strips are preferred for standardization and convenience.

The dye is non-toxic at clinical concentrations, does not require preservatives, and has no known systemic side effects. It is not absorbed systemically in any clinically meaningful quantity following topical ocular application.

## Pathophysiology of Lissamine Green Staining

Understanding the pathophysiology underlying positive lissamine green staining is essential for accurate clinical interpretation. Staining does not simply indicate "dead cells"; rather, it reflects a spectrum of cellular compromise:

### 1. Disruption of the Glycocalyx and Membrane-Associated Mucins

The [[Glycocalyx]] is a carbohydrate-rich layer on the apical surface of ocular surface epithelial cells, composed largely of the transmembrane mucins [[MUC1]], [[MUC4]], and [[MUC16]]. This layer serves as a hydrophilic barrier that stabilizes the [[Tear Film]], prevents pathogen adhesion, and provides a negatively charged shield that repels anionic molecules. In conditions such as [[Dry Eye Disease]], [[Sjögren Syndrome]], [[Allergic Conjunctivitis]], and [[Chemical Injury]], inflammatory mediators (including [[Matrix Metalloproteinases]] such as [[MMP-7]] and [[MMP-9]]), desiccation stress, and mechanical trauma lead to shedding or degradation of these mucins. The loss of the glycocalyx exposes the underlying cell membrane, allowing lissamine green to bind.

### 2. Cell Membrane Damage and Increased Permeability

In more advanced stages of ocular surface disease, the cell membrane itself becomes compromised. Oxidative stress, [[Hyperosmolarity]] of the tear film, and inflammatory cytokines (e.g., [[Interleukin-1β]], [[Tumor Necrosis Factor-α]], [[Interleukin-6]]) cause lipid peroxidation and disruption of the phospholipid bilayer. This increased membrane permeability allows lissamine green to enter the cell and bind to intracellular proteins, producing more intense staining.

### 3. Apoptosis and Cell Death

Cells undergoing [[Apoptosis]] or [[Necrosis]] lose all membrane integrity and glycocalyx coverage, resulting in maximal dye uptake. These cells stain most intensely and are often seen in severe dry eye, [[Stevens-Johnson Syndrome]], [[Ocular Cicatricial Pemphigoid]], and other cicatrizing conditions.

### 4. Desiccation Without Irreversible Damage

Importantly, lissamine green can also stain cells that are transiently desiccated but not irreversibly damaged. In the [[Lid Wiper Epitheliopathy]] (LWE) pattern, for example, staining of the marginal conjunctiva of the upper [[Eyelid]] reflects frictional desiccation of the lid wiper zone during blinking, a finding strongly associated with symptomatic dry eye even in the absence of significant tear film deficiency.

## Clinical Indications

Lissamine green staining is indicated in the diagnostic evaluation of a wide range of ocular surface conditions:

- **[[Dry Eye Disease]]** (both [[Aqueous Deficient Dry Eye]] and [[Evaporative Dry Eye]])
- **[[Sjögren Syndrome]]** (primary and secondary)
- **[[Superior Limbic Keratoconjunctivitis]]** (SLK of Theodore)
- **[[Lid Wiper Epitheliopathy]]** (LWE)
- **[[Exposure Keratopathy]]**
- **[[Neurotrophic Keratopathy]]**
- **[[Allergic Conjunctivitis]]** and [[Atopic Keratoconjunctivitis]]
- **[[Ocular Cicatricial Pemphigoid]]**
- **[[Stevens-Johnson Syndrome]]** / [[Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis]]
- **[[Graft-versus-Host Disease]]** (ocular manifestations)
- **[[Contact Lens]]-related ocular surface disease**
- **[[Chemical Injury]]** and [[Thermal Burn]] of the ocular surface
- **[[Medication Toxicity]]** (e.g., [[Preservative Toxicity]] from [[Benzalkonium Chloride]])
- **Post-[[Refractive Surgery]] ocular surface assessment**

## Diagnostic Technique and Clinical Procedure

### Step-by-Step Instillation Protocol

1. **Preparation:** Remove a lissamine green–impregnated strip from its sterile packaging. Wet the strip with **one drop** of sterile, non-preserved saline (0.9% NaCl). Avoid over-wetting, as excessive dilution reduces staining intensity.

2. **Instillation:** With the patient seated at the [[Slit-Lamp Biomicroscope]], gently retract the lower eyelid and touch the moistened strip to the [[Inferior Conjunctival Fornix]] or the inferior [[Palpebral Conjunctiva]]. Alternatively, allow the dye to drip from the strip into the fornix without direct contact.

3. **Blinking:** Instruct the patient to blink naturally **three to four times** to distribute the dye across the ocular surface.

4. **Timing:** Optimal observation should occur between **1 and 4 minutes** after instillation. Observation too early (< 1 minute) may show excessive background staining due to pooling, while observation too late (> 5 minutes) may result in fading as the dye is diluted and cleared by the tear film.

5. **Observation:** Examine the ocular surface using the slit-lamp biomicroscope under **diffuse white illumination**. Staining is best visualized using a **red-free (green) filter** or a **yellow barrier filter** (Wratten #12 or #15), which enhances contrast by absorbing the green wavelength and making stained areas appear dark against a lighter background. High magnification (10–16×) is recommended for detailed assessment.

6. **Documentation:** Record the location, pattern, and severity of staining using a standardized grading system.

### Observation Filters and Illumination

| Filter/Illumination | Effect on Lissamine Green Visualization |
|---|---|
| **Diffuse white light** | Adequate; staining appears green against pink/white conjunctiva |
| **Red-free (green) filter** | Enhanced contrast; stained areas appear darker |
| **Yellow barrier filter (Wratten #12)** | Optimal contrast; strongly recommended for subtle staining |
| **Cobalt blue light** | Not useful (lissamine green is not fluorescent) |

## Grading Systems and Interpretation

Several validated grading systems are used to quantify lissamine green staining:

### van Bijsterveld Scoring System

Originally developed for [[Rose Bengal]], this system is directly applicable to lissamine green. The exposed [[Interpalpebral Fissure]] is divided into three zones:

| Zone | Description |
|---|---|
| **Nasal Conjunctiva** | Nasal bulbar conjunctiva within the interpalpebral zone |
| **Cornea** | Central corneal surface |
| **Temporal Conjunctiva** | Temporal bulbar conjunctiva within the interpalpebral zone |

Each zone is graded from **0 to 3**:

| Grade | Description |
|---|---|
| **0** | No staining |
| **1** | Sparse, scattered punctate staining |
| **2** | Moderate, diffuse punctate staining |
| **3** | Confluent staining covering the majority of the zone |

**Maximum total score: 9.** A score of **≥ 3.5** (or ≥ 4 in some protocols) is considered clinically significant and is one of the objective criteria for the diagnosis of [[Dry Eye Disease]] and [[Sjögren Syndrome]] per the [[American-European Consensus Group]] (AECG) classification criteria.

### Oxford Grading Scale

The [[Oxford Grading Scale]] (Bron, Evans, and Smith, 2003) uses a series of reference panels (Panels 0–V) depicting increasing density of punctate staining across the entire exposed ocular surface. While originally designed for fluorescein, it is applicable to lissamine green staining of the conjunctiva.

| Panel | Severity | Approximate Dot Count |
|---|---|---|
| **0** | Absent | 0 |
| **I** | Minimal | 1–5 |
| **II** | Mild | 6–30 |
| **III** | Moderate | >30 (scattered) |
| **IV** | Marked | >30 (confluent areas) |
| **V** | Severe | Extensive confluent staining |

### TFOS DEWS II Grading

The [[TFOS DEWS II]] Diagnostic Methodology Report (2017) recommends the use of lissamine green for conjunctival staining as part of the comprehensive dry eye diagnostic battery. A **nasal or temporal conjunctival staining score ≥ grade 2** (on the Oxford scale) or a **van Bijsterveld score ≥ 4** is considered a positive diagnostic marker for dry eye disease.

### SICCA Ocular Staining Score (OSS)

Developed for the classification of [[Sjögren Syndrome]], the [[Ocular Staining Score]] (OSS) combines fluorescein corneal staining with lissamine green conjunctival staining. The conjunctiva is divided into nasal and temporal zones, each graded 0–3 (as per van Bijsterveld), with an additional point added for each zone if:

- Staining is present in the **peri-limbal** region (within 2 mm of the [[Limbus]])
- **Confluent** staining is present anywhere in the zone

**Maximum conjunctival score: 10** (5 per zone). Combined with the corneal fluorescein score (maximum 6), the total OSS ranges from **0 to 12**. An OSS of **≥ 5** is one of the classification criteria for Sjögren syndrome per the [[American College of Rheumatology]] (ACR)/[[European League Against Rheumatism]] (EULAR) 2016 criteria.

## Staining Patterns and Their Clinical Significance

The distribution and pattern of lissamine green staining provide critical diagnostic information:

| Staining Pattern | Location | Associated Condition(s) |
|---|---|---|
| **Interpalpebral nasal and temporal conjunctival staining** | Exposed bulbar conjunctiva | [[Dry Eye Disease]] (aqueous deficient and evaporative), [[Sjögren Syndrome]] |
| **Inferior conjunctival/corneal staining** | Inferior third of cornea and conjunctiva | [[Lagophthalmos]], [[Exposure Keratopathy]], incomplete blink |
| **Superior bulbar conjunctival staining** | Superior conjunctiva (under upper lid) | [[Superior Limbic Keratoconjunctivitis]] (SLK) |
| **Lid wiper staining** | Marginal conjunctiva of upper lid (Marx's line and lid wiper zone) | [[Lid Wiper Epitheliopathy]] (LWE) |
| **Diffuse conjunctival staining** | Widespread | [[Medication Toxicity]], [[Preservative Toxicity]], [[Ocular Cicatricial Pemphigoid]], [[Stevens-Johnson Syndrome]] |
| **Perilimbal staining** | Conjunctiva adjacent to limbus | [[Limbal Stem Cell Deficiency]], severe dry eye |
| **Linear staining along Marx's line** | Mucocutaneous junction of lid margin | Normal finding (physiologic); excessive staining suggests lid margin disease |
| **Corneal staining (punctate)** | Corneal epithelium | Severe dry eye, [[Neurotrophic Keratopathy]] (note: fluorescein is preferred for corneal assessment) |

### Lid Wiper Epitheliopathy (LWE)

[[Lid Wiper Epitheliopathy]] deserves special mention as a clinical entity uniquely well-demonstrated by lissamine green. The "lid wiper" is the portion of the marginal [[Palpebral Conjunctiva]] of the upper eyelid that contacts the [[Bulbar Conjunctiva]] and [[Cornea]] during each blink. In patients with inadequate tear film lubrication, this zone undergoes frictional damage, which is revealed by lissamine green staining. LWE is graded by the **horizontal length** and **sagittal width** of staining:

| Grade | Horizontal Length | Sagittal Width |
|---|---|---|
| **0** | None | None |
| **1** | < 2 mm and < 25% of lid width | < 2 mm |
| **2** | 2–4 mm or 25–50% of lid width | 2–3 mm |
| **3** | > 5 mm or > 50% of lid width | > 3 mm |

LWE has been shown to correlate strongly with dry eye symptoms (as measured by the [[OSDI]] and [[SPEED]] questionnaires) and may be present even when other signs of dry eye are minimal, making it a valuable early diagnostic marker.

## Advantages Over Rose Bengal

The clinical superiority of lissamine green over [[Rose Bengal]] for routine ocular surface assessment is well-established:

| Parameter | Lissamine Green | Rose Bengal |
|---|---|---|
| **Diagnostic sensitivity** | Equivalent | Equivalent |
| **Patient comfort** | Excellent (no stinging) | Poor (significant stinging) |
| **Cytotoxicity** | None | Significant (dose-dependent) |
| **Phototoxicity** | None | Present (generates reactive oxygen species upon light exposure) |
| **Effect on cell viability** | Does not kill cells | Can kill viable cells, confounding interpretation |
| **Need for topical anesthesia** | No | Often required to reduce discomfort |
| **Availability** | Widely available (strips) | Increasingly difficult to obtain; discontinued in some markets |
| **Regulatory status** | Available as diagnostic strips | Limited availability; compounding may be required |

Because rose bengal can itself cause cell death (particularly in the presence of light), its use can create a "self-fulfilling prophecy" in which the staining agent damages the very tissue it is meant to assess. Lissamine green avoids this confound entirely, providing a more accurate representation of pre-existing ocular surface damage.

## Limitations and Considerations

Despite its many advantages, clinicians should be aware of certain limitations:

1. **Conjunctival preference:** Lissamine green stains conjunctival tissue more readily than corneal tissue. For corneal epithelial assessment, [[Fluorescein Sodium]] remains the preferred dye. A comprehensive ocular surface evaluation should employ **both** fluorescein (for the cornea) and lissamine green (for the conjunctiva).

2. **Timing sensitivity:** As noted, staining must be observed within the optimal 1–4 minute window. Early observation may show false-positive pooling, while delayed observation may miss subtle staining.

3. **Volume dependence:** Excessive wetting of the strip or instillation of too large a volume of dye solution can dilute the stain and reduce sensitivity. Conversely, too little dye may produce false-negative results.

4. **Background staining:** In patients with very thin or atrophic conjunctiva (e.g., elderly patients, those with chronic cicatrizing disease), background uptake may complicate interpretation.

5. **Not fluorescent:** Unlike fluorescein, lissamine green does not fluoresce, so it cannot be used with cobalt blue excitation. It requires white light or filtered white light for observation.

6. **Corneal staining interpretation:** While lissamine green can stain the cornea in severe disease, its corneal staining is less sensitive and less well-characterized than fluorescein staining. Clinicians should not rely on lissamine green alone for corneal assessment.

## Role in Diagnostic Algorithms

Lissamine green staining is integrated into multiple contemporary diagnostic algorithms for ocular surface disease:

### TFOS DEWS II Diagnostic Algorithm (2017)

The TFOS DEWS II report recommends a staged diagnostic approach:

1. **Screening:** Symptom questionnaires ([[OSDI]], [[DEQ-5]])
2. **Triaging:** Risk factor assessment, [[Visual Acuity]], [[Tear Meniscus Height]], [[Non-Invasive Tear Break-Up Time]] (NIBUT)
3. **Classification:** [[Tear Break-Up Time]] (TBUT), [[Tear Osmolarity]], **ocular surface staining** (fluorescein for cornea, **lissamine green for conjunctiva**)
4. **Subtype classification:** [[Meibography]], [[Lipid Layer Thickness]], [[Schirmer Test]]

Lissamine green conjunctival staining is a **homeostasis marker** in this algorithm, and a positive result (≥ grade 2 Oxford or ≥ 4 van Bijsterveld) confirms the diagnosis of dry eye disease in the presence of symptoms.

### Sjögren Syndrome Classification (ACR/EULAR 2016)

The 2016 ACR/EULAR classification criteria for [[Sjögren Syndrome]] assign a weighted score to the [[Ocular Staining Score]] (OSS ≥ 5), which incorporates lissamine green conjunctival staining. This criterion carries a weight of **1 point** in the classification system (total threshold ≥ 4 points for classification).

## Storage, Handling, and Safety

- **Storage:** Lissamine green strips should be stored at **room temperature** (15–30°C / 59–86°F) in their original sealed packaging, protected from light and moisture.
- **Shelf life:** Typically **24–36 months** from the date of manufacture when stored properly.
- **Single use:** Each strip is intended for **single-patient use** and should be discarded after instillation.
- **Allergy:** Allergic reactions to lissamine green are exceedingly rare. No significant adverse events have been reported in the ophthalmic literature at clinical concentrations.
- **Pregnancy/Lactation:** No systemic absorption occurs at clinically relevant levels; the dye is considered safe for use during pregnancy and lactation, though formal FDA pregnancy category classification has not been assigned (as it is a diagnostic agent, not a therapeutic drug).
- **Contact lenses:** Patients should be advised to **remove [[Contact Lenses]]** prior to instillation, as the dye may temporarily discolor soft hydrogel and silicone hydrogel lenses. Lenses may be reinserted after the examination is complete and the dye has been cleared from the tear film (typically 15–30 minutes).

## Emerging Applications and Research

### Quantitative Digital Image Analysis

Advances in digital imaging and artificial intelligence are enabling **automated, objective quantification** of lissamine green staining. High-resolution slit-lamp photography combined with image analysis software can measure staining area, density, and distribution with greater reproducibility than subjective clinical grading. Several research groups are developing machine learning algorithms trained on annotated lissamine green staining images to provide standardized, clinician-independent grading.

### Combination Staining Protocols

Some investigators have explored **simultaneous dual staining** with fluorescein and lissamine green to assess both corneal and conjunctival damage in a single instillation. Because the two dyes have different spectral properties and are visualized under different illumination conditions (cobalt blue with yellow filter for fluorescein; white light with red-free or yellow filter for lissamine green), they can be used sequentially or in combination without significant interference.

### Biomarker Correlation Studies

Research is ongoing to correlate lissamine green staining patterns and severity with molecular biomarkers of ocular surface inflammation, including [[MMP-9]] (as measured by point-of-care testing such as [[InflammaDry]]), [[Tear Osmolarity]] (as measured by [[TearLab]]), and [[Lactoferrin]] levels. These studies aim to establish lissamine green staining as a validated surrogate endpoint for clinical trials of dry eye therapeutics.

### Use in Clinical Trials

Lissamine green conjunctival staining is increasingly used as a **primary or secondary endpoint** in clinical trials evaluating novel dry eye treatments, including [[Cyclosporine A]] ophthalmic emulsion, [[Lifitegrast]], [[Varenicline]] nasal spray, [[Perfluorohexyloctane]] (NOV03/Miebo), and various [[Biological Tear Substitutes]]. The FDA and EMA have accepted lissamine green staining scores as valid efficacy endpoints in pivotal dry eye trials.

---

## Summary

Lissamine green is a safe, well-tolerated, and highly informative vital dye that plays a central role in the modern diagnostic evaluation of [[Ocular Surface Disease]]. Its ability to selectively stain devitalized and desiccated conjunctival (and corneal) epithelial cells—without causing the cytotoxicity or patient discomfort associated with [[Rose Bengal]]—has made it the preferred conjunctival stain in clinical [[Optometry]] and [[Ophthalmology]]. When used in conjunction with [[Fluorescein Sodium]] for corneal assessment, lissamine green provides a comprehensive picture of ocular surface health that is essential for the accurate diagnosis, classification, and monitoring of [[Dry Eye Disease]], [[Sjögren Syndrome]], and a wide spectrum of other ocular surface disorders. Mastery of its instillation technique, optimal observation methods, and standardized grading systems is a core competency for all eye care practitioners.
