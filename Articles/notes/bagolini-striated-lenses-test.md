

# Bagolini Striated Lenses Test

---

## Overview

The **Bagolini Striated Lenses Test** (also referred to as the **Bagolini Striated Glass Test** or simply the **Bagolini Test**) is a subjective clinical assessment of [[binocular vision]] widely employed in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] to evaluate the state of [[sensory fusion]], detect the presence or absence of [[suppression]], and characterize [[diplopia]] and [[anomalous retinal correspondence]] (ARC). Developed by the Italian ophthalmologist **Bruno Bagolini** in 1958, this elegantly simple test has become a cornerstone of the [[orthoptic]] examination because it assesses binocular sensory status under conditions that closely approximate natural, everyday seeing — a critical advantage over many other tests of binocularity.

The test utilizes a pair of plano (non-powered) lenses upon which fine, parallel striations have been ground at specific orientations. When a patient views a small point light source through these lenses, each eye perceives a linear streak of light. The orientation, completeness, and perceived relationship of the two streaks provide the clinician with rich diagnostic information about the patient's [[binocular single vision]], the presence of [[strabismus]], the nature of retinal correspondence, and the existence of any [[scotoma]] or suppression zone.

Because the Bagolini striated lenses introduce minimal dissociation of the visual environment — the patient can still see the room, the examiner, and all surrounding objects — the test is considered a **minimal dissociation test** or a test performed under **free-space conditions**. This stands in contrast to highly dissociative tests such as the [[Worth 4-Dot Test]] or the [[synoptophore]], which impose artificial viewing conditions that may not reflect the patient's habitual binocular status.

---

## Historical Background

Bruno Bagolini introduced the striated lens test in 1958 as a practical, office-based method for evaluating sensory binocular function. Prior to its introduction, clinicians relied heavily on instruments such as the [[major amblyoscope]] (synoptophore), the [[Hess screen]], and various red-green dissociation tests. While these instruments remain valuable, Bagolini recognized that the degree of dissociation they imposed could alter the very binocular status they sought to measure. A patient who demonstrates [[normal retinal correspondence]] (NRC) on a highly dissociative test might actually function with [[anomalous retinal correspondence]] under everyday conditions, and vice versa.

Bagolini's innovation was to create a test that would probe sensory fusion with the least possible disruption to the patient's habitual binocular state. The fine striations on the lenses are so subtle that the patient is often unaware that anything unusual has been placed before the eyes, aside from the appearance of the linear streaks when viewing a point light. This minimal interference allows the clinician to assess the patient's **habitual** binocular sensory status — the way the visual system actually operates in daily life.

## Principles and Optics

### Mechanism of Streak Formation

Each Bagolini striated lens is a flat ([[plano lens]]) glass or plastic lens upon which extremely fine, closely spaced, parallel grooves (striations) have been ground or etched. These striations act as a crude [[diffraction grating]] or cylindrical lens array. When a small, bright [[point light source]] (such as a [[Maddox rod]] spotlight or a penlight) is viewed through the striated lens, the striations scatter light perpendicular to their orientation, producing a **linear streak** of light that passes through the point source and extends in the direction perpendicular to the striations.

- If the striations are oriented at **135°** (from the patient's perspective), the streak appears along the **45° meridian** (i.e., extending from lower-left to upper-right).
- If the striations are oriented at **45°**, the streak appears along the **135° meridian** (i.e., extending from upper-left to lower-right).

By convention, the lens before the **right eye** is oriented with striations at **135°** and the lens before the **left eye** is oriented with striations at **45°** (or vice versa). This arrangement ensures that each eye generates a streak in a different orientation, allowing the clinician to determine which streak belongs to which eye.

### Minimal Dissociation Principle

The key optical property of the Bagolini striated lens is that it does **not** significantly alter the overall visual scene. The patient can still see the room, the examiner's face, and all other objects in the visual field. Only the point light source is transformed into a streak. This means the test does not disrupt [[fusional vergence]] mechanisms or [[peripheral fusion]] cues. The binocular visual system is therefore assessed in a state as close to its natural operating condition as possible.

This is fundamentally different from tests that use red-green filters (which eliminate binocular color matching), polarized filters (which completely separate the two eyes' images), or mechanical separation (as in a synoptophore). The degree of dissociation is a critical variable in binocular vision testing, and the Bagolini test occupies the **least dissociative** end of the spectrum.

## Indications

The Bagolini Striated Lenses Test is indicated in the following clinical scenarios:

| **Indication** | **Clinical Rationale** |
|---|---|
| Evaluation of [[binocular single vision]] (BSV) | To determine whether the patient achieves simultaneous perception and fusion under near-natural conditions |
| Detection of [[suppression]] | To identify monocular or regional suppression in patients with [[strabismus]] or [[amblyopia]] |
| Differentiation of [[normal retinal correspondence]] (NRC) vs. [[anomalous retinal correspondence]] (ARC) | To classify the sensory adaptation in strabismic patients |
| Assessment of [[diplopia]] | To confirm and characterize diplopia in patients with recent-onset strabismus, [[cranial nerve palsy]], or post-surgical misalignment |
| Pre- and post-operative binocular status evaluation | To document changes in sensory fusion before and after [[strabismus surgery]] or other interventions |
| Monitoring of [[orthoptic]] treatment progress | To track improvements in binocular function during vision therapy or [[occlusion therapy]] |
| Evaluation of [[microtropia]] (monofixation syndrome) | To detect small central suppression scotomas characteristic of [[microtropia]] |
| Differentiation of functional vs. organic visual complaints | To help distinguish between genuine binocular dysfunction and non-organic visual symptoms |

## Equipment and Setup

### Required Equipment

1. **A pair of Bagolini striated lenses** — These are typically mounted in a trial frame or clip-on holder. The lenses are plano (no refractive power) and have fine parallel striations. They are available commercially from ophthalmic instrument suppliers.
2. **A point light source** — A small, bright spotlight (such as a [[muscle light]] or penlight) positioned at the desired testing distance. The light should be small enough to produce a well-defined streak but bright enough to be easily visible.
3. **A [[trial frame]]** — To hold the striated lenses at the correct orientation before each eye. Some clinicians use a dedicated Bagolini lens holder.
4. **A dimly lit or normally lit examination room** — The test can be performed in normal room illumination, which further supports the minimal-dissociation philosophy. However, a slightly dimmed room may enhance streak visibility.

### Lens Orientation

The two lenses are placed before the patient's eyes with their striations oriented at complementary oblique angles:

- **Right eye**: Striations at 135° (producing a streak along the 45° axis — lower-left to upper-right)
- **Left eye**: Striations at 45° (producing a streak along the 135° axis — upper-left to lower-right)

This arrangement produces two streaks that, if both are perceived simultaneously, form an **X** pattern (or a cross) centered on the point light source. The specific orientation allows the examiner to identify which streak corresponds to which eye.

### Testing Distance

The test is typically performed at both **distance** (6 meters / 20 feet) and **near** (33–40 cm / 13–16 inches) fixation to assess binocular status at different working distances, as sensory adaptations may differ between distance and near.

---

## Test Procedure

### Step-by-Step Protocol

1. **Correct any refractive error**: The patient should wear their habitual [[spectacle correction]] or [[contact lenses]]. The Bagolini lenses are placed over the correction in a trial frame.

2. **Position the lenses**: Insert the Bagolini striated lenses into the trial frame with the right lens striations at 135° and the left lens striations at 45° (or the reverse, as long as the examiner knows which orientation corresponds to which eye).

3. **Present the point light source**: Position a small spotlight at the desired testing distance (typically 6 meters for distance and 33 cm for near). The room should be at normal or slightly reduced illumination.

4. **Instruct the patient**: Ask the patient to fixate on the point light and describe what they see. Specific questions include:
   - "How many lights do you see?"
   - "Do you see any lines or streaks of light?"
   - "How many streaks do you see?"
   - "Do the streaks cross each other, or are they separated?"
   - "Is any part of a streak missing or broken?"
   - "Does the pattern change or does any part disappear?"

5. **Record the response**: Document the patient's description using standard notation and diagrams.

6. **Repeat at the other testing distance**: If initially tested at distance, repeat at near, and vice versa.

7. **Perform with and without prism (if needed)**: In some protocols, the test is repeated with [[prism]] correction in place to assess whether neutralization of a deviation restores fusion.

## Interpretation of Responses

The patient's response to the Bagolini Striated Lenses Test falls into several well-defined categories. Each response pattern provides specific diagnostic information.

### 1. Normal Binocular Single Vision (BSV) — Bifoveal Fusion

**Patient's report**: Two complete streaks forming a symmetric **X** (cross) pattern, intersecting at the point light source. Only **one** light is seen at the center.

**Interpretation**: Both [[fovea]]e are simultaneously fixating the point light, and the images from both eyes are being fused. This indicates **[[normal retinal correspondence]]** (NRC) with intact [[bifoveal fusion]]. There is no suppression and no manifest deviation.

```
        \  /
         \/
         /\
        /  \
```

### 2. Suppression

**Patient's report**: Only **one** streak is seen. The other streak is completely absent.

**Interpretation**: The eye whose streak is not perceived is being **suppressed**. This is common in longstanding [[strabismus]] or deep [[amblyopia]]. The suppression may be:
- **Total suppression**: The entire streak from one eye is absent.
- **Central suppression**: The streak is present peripherally but has a **gap** or **break** in the center where it should cross the point light. This pattern is characteristic of a **central suppression [[scotoma]]** and is highly suggestive of [[microtropia]] (monofixation syndrome).

| **Suppression Pattern** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|
| Complete absence of one streak | Deep, total suppression of one eye (common in constant [[unilateral strabismus]]) |
| Central gap in one streak | Central suppression scotoma — hallmark of [[microtropia]] / [[monofixation syndrome]] |
| Intermittent disappearance of one streak | Intermittent suppression (may be seen in [[intermittent exotropia]]) |

### 3. Diplopia (Without Anomalous Correspondence)

**Patient's report**: Two complete streaks are seen, but they **do not intersect at the same point**. The patient may report seeing **two lights**, each with its own streak, or two streaks that are displaced relative to each other.

**Interpretation**: The patient has a manifest [[strabismus]] with **[[normal retinal correspondence]]** but **without fusion**. The displacement of the streaks corresponds to the direction and magnitude of the deviation:

- **[[Esotropia]]**: The uncrossed (homonymous) displacement of the streaks — the right eye's streak is displaced to the right, and the left eye's streak is displaced to the left.
- **[[Exotropia]]**: The crossed (heteronymous) displacement — the right eye's streak is displaced to the left, and the left eye's streak is displaced to the right.
- **[[Hypertropia]]/[[Hypotropia]]**: Vertical displacement of the streaks.

This response is typical of **recently acquired strabismus** (e.g., from [[cranial nerve palsy]]) where the sensory system has not yet developed suppression or anomalous correspondence.

### 4. Anomalous Retinal Correspondence (ARC) — Harmonious

**Patient's report**: Two complete streaks forming a symmetric **X** pattern, intersecting at the point light source — **identical to the normal BSV response** — despite the presence of a manifest strabismus (observable on [[cover test]]).

**Interpretation**: The patient has a manifest deviation, but the sensory system has adapted so that the [[fovea]] of the fixating eye and an **extrafoveal point** in the deviating eye have developed a common subjective visual direction. The angle of anomaly equals the angle of deviation, resulting in **harmonious anomalous retinal correspondence**. The patient perceives the streaks as crossing at the light because the anomalous correspondence provides a form of binocular "fusion" (though not true bifoveal fusion).

This is a critical finding because it indicates a deeply ingrained sensory adaptation. The clinical significance is:
- It suggests the strabismus is longstanding.
- It may complicate surgical outcomes, as restoring ocular alignment may paradoxically produce diplopia if the anomalous correspondence persists.
- It must be differentiated from true NRC by performing a [[cover test]] simultaneously to confirm the presence of a manifest deviation.

### 5. Anomalous Retinal Correspondence — Unharmonious

**Patient's report**: Two streaks are seen, and they intersect, but **not at the point light source**. The intersection point is displaced from the light.

**Interpretation**: The angle of anomaly does not fully compensate for the angle of deviation. This is **unharmonious ARC** and is less commonly encountered. It may be seen in patients whose deviation has changed (e.g., after partial surgical correction) and whose anomalous correspondence has not fully readjusted.

### Summary Table of Responses

| **Response** | **Streaks Seen** | **Intersection** | **Cover Test** | **Diagnosis** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Two complete streaks, X at light | 2 complete | At the light | No deviation | **Normal BSV with NRC** |
| One streak only | 1 | N/A | Deviation present or absent | **Suppression** |
| Two streaks, central gap in one | 2 (one broken) | Near the light | Small deviation may be present | **Microtropia with central suppression scotoma** |
| Two streaks, displaced (not crossing at light) | 2 complete | Separated | Deviation present | **Diplopia with NRC (no fusion)** |
| Two complete streaks, X at light | 2 complete | At the light | Deviation present | **Harmonious ARC** |
| Two streaks, crossing away from light | 2 complete | Displaced from light | Deviation present | **Unharmonious ARC** |

## Pathophysiology of Conditions Detected

### Normal Retinal Correspondence (NRC)

In [[normal retinal correspondence]], corresponding retinal points in the two eyes share the same subjective visual direction. The [[fovea]] of the right eye and the [[fovea]] of the left eye are corresponding points, as are symmetrically located peripheral retinal points. When both foveae fixate the same object, the images are fused into a single percept via [[cortical fusion]] mechanisms in the [[visual cortex]] (primarily [[area V1]] and higher binocular processing areas). The Bagolini test confirms NRC when the two streaks intersect at the fixation light in the absence of a manifest deviation.

### Anomalous Retinal Correspondence (ARC)

[[Anomalous retinal correspondence]] is a **sensory adaptation** to chronic [[strabismus]], predominantly developing in childhood during the period of [[visual plasticity]]. In ARC, the fovea of the fixating eye becomes paired with an extrafoveal retinal area in the deviating eye, establishing a new (anomalous) common subjective visual direction. This adaptation serves to reduce or eliminate [[diplopia]] and [[visual confusion]] that would otherwise result from the misalignment.

The neural substrate of ARC involves cortical remapping of binocular connections, likely in [[area V1]] and [[area V2]], though the precise mechanisms remain incompletely understood. The Bagolini test is particularly sensitive to ARC because its minimal dissociation allows the anomalous correspondence to manifest — unlike highly dissociative tests, which may "break" the anomalous adaptation and reveal the underlying NRC.

### Suppression

[[Suppression]] is an active cortical inhibition of the visual input from one eye, serving as another adaptation to prevent diplopia and confusion in strabismus. Suppression can be:

- **Facultative**: Present only under binocular conditions and absent during monocular viewing.
- **Obligatory**: Deeply ingrained, approaching the characteristics of [[amblyopia]].
- **Central**: Involving only the foveal and parafoveal region of the deviating eye (as in [[microtropia]]).
- **Peripheral**: Involving the peripheral retina.
- **Total**: Involving the entire visual field of one eye.

The Bagolini test detects suppression by the absence or interruption of the streak corresponding to the suppressed eye. Central suppression scotomas, characteristic of [[monofixation syndrome]] (described by [[Parks]]), produce the pathognomonic finding of a streak with a central gap.

### Microtropia (Monofixation Syndrome)

[[Microtropia]] is a small-angle strabismus (typically ≤ 8–10 [[prism diopters]]) associated with:
- A small central suppression scotoma in the deviating eye
- Peripheral fusion (which maintains the small, stable angle)
- Reduced [[stereoacuity]] (typically worse than 60 seconds of arc on [[Titmus stereo test]] or [[TNO stereo test]])
- Often associated with mild [[amblyopia]] and [[anisometropia]]

The Bagolini test is one of the most sensitive clinical tests for detecting microtropia. The characteristic finding is two streaks that appear to form an X, but one streak has a **central gap** (break) where it should pass through the point light. This gap corresponds to the central suppression scotoma. The peripheral portions of both streaks are intact, confirming the presence of peripheral fusion.

## Advantages and Limitations

### Advantages

1. **Minimal dissociation**: Assesses binocular status under near-natural viewing conditions, providing the most ecologically valid assessment of habitual binocular function.
2. **Simplicity**: Requires minimal equipment and is quick to perform.
3. **Patient comfort**: Non-invasive and well-tolerated, even by children (though cooperation is required for verbal responses).
4. **Sensitivity to ARC**: Because of minimal dissociation, the test is highly sensitive to anomalous retinal correspondence, which may be masked by more dissociative tests.
5. **Detection of microtropia**: The test is particularly valuable for identifying the central suppression scotoma of monofixation syndrome.
6. **Versatility**: Can be performed at both distance and near, and can be combined with prism to assess fusional reserves or the effect of deviation neutralization.

### Limitations

1. **Subjective nature**: The test relies entirely on the patient's verbal report, making it unsuitable for preverbal children, patients with cognitive impairment, or unreliable historians.
2. **Cannot quantify the deviation**: Unlike the [[prism cover test]] or synoptophore, the Bagolini test does not directly measure the magnitude of a strabismic deviation (though the direction of displacement can be inferred).
3. **Ambiguous responses**: Some patients give inconsistent or difficult-to-interpret responses, particularly those with intermittent deviations or variable suppression.
4. **Does not assess motor fusion**: The test evaluates sensory fusion only; motor fusion (vergence amplitudes) must be assessed separately.
5. **Requires a point light source**: The test is specific to the viewing of a point light and may not fully represent the patient's binocular status when viewing complex scenes (though the minimal dissociation mitigates this concern).
6. **Difficulty distinguishing NRC from harmonious ARC**: Both conditions produce the same response (two complete streaks crossing at the light). A simultaneous [[cover test]] must be performed to differentiate them.

## Clinical Pearls and Special Considerations

### Simultaneous Cover Test

To differentiate **normal BSV** from **harmonious ARC**, the clinician should perform a [[cover-uncover test]] or [[alternate cover test]] while the patient is viewing through the Bagolini lenses. If the patient reports two complete streaks crossing at the light (suggesting fusion) but the cover test reveals a manifest deviation, the diagnosis is **harmonious ARC**, not normal BSV.

### Use in Pre-Surgical Planning

Before [[strabismus surgery]], the Bagolini test provides critical information about the patient's sensory status:
- Patients with **NRC and diplopia** are likely to achieve fusion after successful surgical alignment.
- Patients with **harmonious ARC** may develop intractable diplopia after surgery if the anomalous correspondence does not readapt to the new alignment.
- Patients with **deep suppression** may not achieve binocular fusion even after perfect surgical alignment, though suppression may gradually resolve, especially in younger patients.

### Use in Post-Surgical Assessment

After strabismus surgery, the Bagolini test can be used to:
- Confirm restoration of binocular single vision
- Detect post-operative diplopia
- Monitor the resolution or persistence of suppression
- Assess whether ARC has converted to NRC following alignment

### Pediatric Considerations

While the Bagolini test is most reliable in cooperative, verbal patients (typically age 5 and older), modified approaches can sometimes be used with younger children. The examiner may ask the child to point to or trace the streaks they see, or use simplified language ("Do you see lines? How many?"). However, for preverbal or uncooperative children, objective tests such as the [[4 prism diopter base-out test]], [[Brückner test]], or preferential looking methods may be more appropriate.

### Torsional Assessment

By carefully noting the orientation of the perceived streaks, the Bagolini test can provide qualitative information about [[cyclotorsion]]. If the patient reports that the streaks are not at the expected 45° and 135° orientations but are rotated, this may suggest the presence of [[cyclotropia]] (torsional misalignment), which can occur in [[superior oblique palsy]] or other conditions affecting the [[cyclovertical muscles]].

## Role in the Diagnosis and Management of Specific Conditions

### Strabismus

The Bagolini test is integral to the sensory evaluation of all forms of [[strabismus]], including:
- **[[Esotropia]]** (infantile, accommodative, non-accommodative, sensory)
- **[[Exotropia]]** (intermittent, constant, sensory)
- **[[Hypertropia]]** and **[[hypotropia]]** (from cranial nerve palsies, thyroid eye disease, orbital fractures)
- **[[Microtropia]]** / [[monofixation syndrome]]

### Amblyopia

In [[amblyopia]], the Bagolini test helps determine whether the amblyopic eye is being suppressed under binocular conditions. This information guides treatment decisions, including the use of [[occlusion therapy]], [[penalization]], or [[binocular vision therapy]] approaches.

### Post-Surgical Diplopia

Patients who develop [[diplopia]] after [[cataract surgery]], [[refractive surgery]] ([[LASIK]], [[PRK]]), [[retinal detachment repair]], or other ocular procedures can be evaluated with the Bagolini test to characterize the nature of the diplopia and guide management.

### Convergence and Divergence Disorders

In patients with [[convergence insufficiency]], [[convergence excess]], [[divergence insufficiency]], or [[divergence excess]], the Bagolini test can reveal whether suppression or ARC develops at the distance or near where the deviation is manifest.

## Documentation and Recording

Clinicians should document Bagolini test results using standardized notation. A common approach includes:

1. **Drawing the perceived streak pattern** as reported by the patient.
2. **Noting the testing distance** (distance vs. near).
3. **Recording the interpretation** (NRC with BSV, suppression [which eye, central vs. total], ARC [harmonious vs. unharmonious], diplopia).
4. **Noting the cover test result** performed simultaneously.

Example documentation:

```
Bagolini Striated Lenses Test:
- Distance (6m): Two complete streaks, X at light. Cover test: no deviation. 
  → Normal BSV, NRC.
- Near (33cm): Two streaks, right eye streak has central gap. Cover test: 4Δ L eso.
  → Microtropia OS with central suppression scotoma, peripheral fusion.
```

## Pharmacological and Therapeutic Relevance

While the Bagolini test itself is a diagnostic tool and does not involve pharmacological intervention, its findings directly influence treatment decisions:

| **Bagolini Finding** | **Therapeutic Implication** |
|---|---|
| Normal BSV with NRC | No binocular intervention needed; address any refractive error |
| Suppression (total) | Consider [[occlusion therapy]], [[atropine penalization]], or [[binocular therapy]] to reduce suppression |
| Central suppression scotoma (microtropia) | Monitor; often stable and may not require intervention; optimize refractive correction |
| Diplopia with NRC | Consider [[prism therapy]], [[strabismus surgery]], or [[botulinum toxin injection]] to restore alignment |
| Harmonious ARC | Counsel regarding surgical risks (post-operative diplopia); consider [[vision therapy]] to attempt conversion to NRC pre-operatively |
| Unharmonious ARC | May indicate unstable sensory status; close monitoring and possible intervention |

### Prism Therapy

[[Fresnel prisms]] or ground-in [[prism]] can be prescribed to neutralize a deviation and restore fusion. The Bagolini test can be performed with prisms in place to verify that the prism correction achieves binocular single vision.

### Vision Therapy / Orthoptic Exercises

In patients undergoing [[vision therapy]] for conditions such as [[convergence insufficiency]] or intermittent strabismus, the Bagolini test serves as a useful outcome measure to document improvements in binocular fusion and reduction of suppression over the course of treatment.

### Botulinum Toxin and Surgical Intervention

For patients with acquired strabismus and diplopia, the Bagolini test helps predict the likelihood of achieving post-intervention fusion. Patients who demonstrate NRC with diplopia (rather than suppression or ARC) are generally the best candidates for surgical or [[botulinum toxin]] correction, as their sensory system is primed for fusion once alignment is restored.

---

## Summary

The **Bagolini Striated Lenses Test** remains one of the most valuable and widely used clinical tests in the assessment of [[binocular vision]]. Its unique advantage lies in its minimal dissociation of the visual environment, allowing the clinician to evaluate the patient's **habitual** sensory binocular status. The test provides critical information about [[sensory fusion]], [[suppression]], [[normal retinal correspondence]], [[anomalous retinal correspondence]], and [[diplopia]] — all of which are essential for the diagnosis and management of [[strabismus]], [[amblyopia]], and related binocular vision disorders.

Its simplicity, low cost, and clinical sensitivity make it an indispensable tool in the [[orthoptic]] examination. When combined with the [[cover test]], [[prism cover test]], [[stereoacuity testing]], and other components of the binocular vision assessment, the Bagolini test provides a comprehensive picture of the patient's sensory and motor binocular status, guiding evidence-based clinical decision-making.
