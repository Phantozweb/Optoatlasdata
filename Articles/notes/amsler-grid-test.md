

# Amsler Grid Test

---

The **Amsler Grid Test** is a simple, widely utilized psychophysical screening tool in [[clinical optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] designed to detect and monitor abnormalities of the [[central visual field]], particularly those affecting the central 10 degrees (and up to 20 degrees) of [[visual field]] surrounding [[fixation]]. Originally developed by Swiss ophthalmologist **Marc Amsler** in 1947, the grid remains one of the most clinically significant, cost-effective, and accessible methods for the early detection of [[macular]] pathology, most notably [[age-related macular degeneration]] (AMD), [[macular edema]], [[epiretinal membrane]], [[central serous chorioretinopathy]], and other conditions that produce [[metamorphopsia]], [[scotoma]], or distortion within the [[macula]]. The test is routinely employed both in clinical settings and as a home-monitoring tool for patients at risk of progression from dry to wet (neovascular) AMD.

---

## Historical Background

Marc Amsler, a professor of ophthalmology at the University of Zurich, introduced the Amsler Grid in 1947 as a refinement of earlier [[tangent screen]] testing methods. Amsler recognized that the existing perimetric techniques of the era were insufficient for detecting subtle macular dysfunction, particularly the fine distortions and small scotomata that characterize early macular disease. He designed a series of seven charts—each with a specific diagnostic purpose—to systematically evaluate the integrity of the [[central visual field]]. The original publication, *L'examen qualitatif de la fonction maculaire* (1947), described the charts and their clinical applications in detail.

Over the subsequent decades, the Amsler Grid became a standard component of the ophthalmic examination. Its simplicity, portability, and sensitivity to macular pathology ensured its adoption worldwide. In the modern era, the test has been supplemented—but not replaced—by advanced technologies such as [[optical coherence tomography]] (OCT), [[microperimetry]], and [[preferential hyperacuity perimetry]] (PHP), yet it remains a cornerstone of macular disease screening and patient self-monitoring.

## Principles and Pathophysiology

### Underlying Pathophysiology Detected by the Amsler Grid

The Amsler Grid does not treat or cause pathology; rather, it serves as a diagnostic instrument that reveals functional consequences of structural macular disease. Understanding the pathophysiology of the conditions it detects is essential for proper clinical interpretation.

#### Metamorphopsia

[[Metamorphopsia]]—the perception of visual distortion in which straight lines appear wavy, bent, or irregular—is the hallmark symptom detected by the Amsler Grid. Metamorphopsia arises from displacement or disruption of [[photoreceptor]] alignment within the [[retina]], particularly the [[fovea]] and [[parafoveal]] region. Causes include:

- **Subretinal fluid accumulation**: In [[choroidal neovascularization]] (CNV) associated with wet [[age-related macular degeneration]], abnormal blood vessels grow beneath the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE) or into the subretinal space, causing fluid exudation, hemorrhage, and mechanical displacement of photoreceptors. This displacement alters the spatial relationship between photoreceptors and their corresponding [[receptive fields]], producing perceived distortion of the visual image.
- **Intraretinal edema**: [[Macular edema]] from [[diabetic retinopathy]], [[retinal vein occlusion]], or post-surgical inflammation (e.g., [[Irvine-Gass syndrome]]) causes cystoid spaces within the retinal layers, physically separating and displacing photoreceptors.
- **Epiretinal membrane (ERM)**: [[Epiretinal membrane]] formation on the [[internal limiting membrane]] (ILM) exerts tangential traction on the inner retinal surface, causing wrinkling and distortion of the underlying photoreceptor mosaic.
- **Central serous chorioretinopathy (CSC)**: [[Central serous chorioretinopathy]] involves focal detachment of the neurosensory retina from the RPE due to serous fluid leakage through defects in the RPE, resulting in photoreceptor displacement and metamorphopsia.
- **Macular hole**: Full-thickness or lamellar [[macular hole]] disrupts the foveal architecture, producing central scotoma and surrounding distortion.

#### Scotoma

A [[scotoma]] is an area of diminished or absent vision within the visual field. On the Amsler Grid, scotomata appear as missing, blurred, or darkened areas. Central or paracentral scotomata detected by the grid may result from:

- Photoreceptor loss or dysfunction (e.g., geographic atrophy in dry AMD)
- RPE atrophy
- Subretinal hemorrhage obscuring photoreceptors
- Optic nerve pathology affecting the [[papillomacular bundle]]

#### Micropsia and Macropsia

[[Micropsia]] (objects appearing smaller than actual size) and [[macropsia]] (objects appearing larger) can also be detected with careful Amsler Grid testing. These phenomena result from changes in photoreceptor density or spacing—photoreceptor crowding produces micropsia, while photoreceptor separation produces macropsia.

### Psychophysical Basis

The Amsler Grid exploits the principle of [[hyperacuity]]—the human visual system's ability to detect spatial misalignment with a precision far exceeding the theoretical resolution limit imposed by photoreceptor spacing (approximately 3–6 arc seconds versus the ~60 arc second resolution limit). When macular pathology displaces even a small number of photoreceptors, the resulting spatial distortion is perceptible as waviness or irregularity of the grid lines, often before significant [[visual acuity]] loss occurs. This makes the Amsler Grid particularly valuable for early detection.

## Description of the Amsler Grid Charts

Marc Amsler originally designed **seven distinct charts**, each serving a specific diagnostic function. The most commonly used in clinical practice is **Chart 1 (the standard grid)**.

| Chart Number | Description | Primary Clinical Use |
|:---:|---|---|
| **1** | White grid on black background; 5 mm squares forming a 10 cm × 10 cm grid with a central white fixation dot | Standard screening for metamorphopsia and scotomata |
| **2** | Similar to Chart 1 but with two diagonal lines added to the grid | Assists patients with central scotomata in maintaining fixation |
| **3** | Red grid on black background; identical layout to Chart 1 | Evaluates color scotomata; useful in detecting subtle [[optic nerve]] and macular dysfunction |
| **4** | Random dot pattern (no lines) with a central fixation point | Differentiates scotomata from metamorphopsia; detects scotomata without the "completion" phenomenon |
| **5** | Horizontal parallel lines with central fixation dot | Specifically evaluates metamorphopsia along the horizontal meridian; useful for detecting subtle distortion |
| **6** | Similar to Chart 5 but with closer line spacing at center | Detailed evaluation of paracentral metamorphopsia |
| **7** | Central fine grid (2 mm squares) within the standard 5 mm grid | Provides higher resolution testing of the central 8 degrees of visual field |

### Standard Grid Specifications (Chart 1)

- **Grid dimensions**: 10 cm × 10 cm
- **Individual square size**: 5 mm × 5 mm (each subtending 1 degree of visual angle at 28–30 cm)
- **Total grid coverage**: 20 degrees × 20 degrees of visual field (10 degrees in each direction from fixation)
- **Testing distance**: 28–30 cm (approximately 14 inches), with appropriate [[near correction]] in place
- **Fixation target**: Central white dot
- **Background**: Traditionally black with white lines (high contrast); some modern versions use white background with black lines

## Clinical Indications

The Amsler Grid Test is indicated in the following clinical scenarios:

1. **Screening for [[age-related macular degeneration]]**: Particularly in patients with intermediate AMD (large [[drusen]], pigmentary changes) who are at risk for conversion to neovascular (wet) AMD
2. **Monitoring known macular disease**: Serial testing to detect progression or recurrence of [[choroidal neovascularization]], [[macular edema]], or [[epiretinal membrane]]
3. **Evaluation of metamorphopsia**: Any patient presenting with complaints of visual distortion
4. **Assessment of central/paracentral scotomata**: Patients reporting missing areas in central vision
5. **Pre- and post-operative evaluation**: Before and after [[vitreoretinal surgery]], [[cataract surgery]], or [[intravitreal injection]] therapy
6. **Monitoring patients on [[hydroxychloroquine]]**: As part of screening for [[hydroxychloroquine retinopathy]] (Plaquenil toxicity), though [[OCT]] and [[visual field testing]] with 10-2 protocol are now preferred primary screening methods per the 2016 American Academy of Ophthalmology guidelines
7. **Home self-monitoring**: Patients at high risk for wet AMD conversion are instructed to use the grid daily at home
8. **Evaluation of [[optic neuropathy]]**: Central or cecocentral scotomata from optic nerve disease may be detected

## Clinical Procedure and Administration

### Standard Testing Protocol

The Amsler Grid Test must be administered under standardized conditions to ensure reliability and reproducibility:

1. **Correction**: The patient must wear their appropriate [[near correction]] (reading glasses, bifocal add, or progressive addition lens). Uncorrected [[refractive error]] can produce artifactual blur that mimics scotomata.

2. **Testing distance**: The grid is held at **28–30 cm** (approximately 14 inches) from the patient's eye. At this distance, each 5 mm square subtends exactly **1 degree of visual angle**, and the entire grid covers the central **20 degrees** of visual field.

3. **Monocular testing**: Each eye is tested **separately**. The non-tested eye is occluded with an opaque occluder or the patient's palm. Binocular testing is not performed, as the normal fellow eye may compensate for defects in the affected eye (a phenomenon related to [[binocular rivalry]] and cortical filling-in).

4. **Fixation**: The patient is instructed to fixate steadily on the **central dot** throughout the test. Maintaining fixation is critical; eye movements will cause the grid to shift on the retina and produce false results.

5. **Illumination**: The grid should be evenly illuminated with standard room lighting. Excessive glare or dim lighting may affect results.

6. **Questioning sequence**: The clinician systematically asks the patient:
   - "Can you see the central dot?"
   - "While looking at the central dot, can you see all four corners and all four sides of the grid?"
   - "Are all the lines straight and parallel, or do any appear wavy, bent, or distorted?"
   - "Are all the small squares the same size, or do any appear larger or smaller?"
   - "Are there any areas that appear blurry, missing, dark, or different from the rest of the grid?"

7. **Documentation**: The patient (or clinician) marks any abnormalities directly on a recording sheet—a paper copy of the grid. Areas of distortion, scotomata, or other abnormalities are outlined or shaded. The recording sheet is dated and filed in the patient's medical record for serial comparison.

### Recording Abnormalities

Standardized documentation should include:

| Finding | Description on Grid | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Metamorphopsia** | Wavy, bent, or irregular lines | Photoreceptor displacement; suggests active macular pathology (fluid, traction, edema) |
| **Scotoma (absolute)** | Missing or black area | Complete loss of retinal function in that area; geographic atrophy, dense hemorrhage |
| **Scotoma (relative)** | Blurred, hazy, or faded area | Partial loss of function; early atrophy, mild edema |
| **Micropsia** | Squares appear smaller than surrounding | Photoreceptor crowding (e.g., from subretinal fluid pushing receptors together) |
| **Macropsia** | Squares appear larger than surrounding | Photoreceptor separation (e.g., from edema spreading receptors apart) |
| **Central scotoma** | Missing area at or near fixation point | Foveal pathology; macular hole, advanced AMD, optic neuropathy |
| **Paracentral scotoma** | Missing area adjacent to fixation | Parafoveal pathology; early AMD, branch retinal artery occlusion |

## Interpretation of Results

### Normal Result

A normal Amsler Grid result consists of the patient reporting:
- The central fixation dot is clearly visible
- All four corners and sides of the grid are visible
- All lines appear straight and parallel
- All squares appear equal in size
- No areas of blurring, distortion, or absence

### Abnormal Results and Clinical Correlation

Abnormal findings on the Amsler Grid must always be correlated with:

- **[[Dilated fundus examination]]**: To identify structural macular pathology
- **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)**: To evaluate retinal layer architecture, subretinal/intraretinal fluid, RPE detachment, and membrane formation
- **[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA)**: To identify choroidal neovascularization, leakage, or ischemia
- **[[Indocyanine green angiography]] (ICG)**: For choroidal pathology evaluation
- **[[OCT angiography]] (OCTA)**: Non-invasive vascular imaging of the macula
- **[[Visual field testing]]**: Formal automated perimetry (e.g., [[Humphrey visual field]] 10-2 or 24-2) for quantitative assessment

### Patterns of Abnormality

| Pattern | Likely Etiology |
|---|---|
| Central metamorphopsia with acute onset | [[Choroidal neovascularization]] (wet AMD), [[central serous chorioretinopathy]] |
| Progressive central scotoma | [[Geographic atrophy]] (dry AMD), [[macular hole]] |
| Paracentral distortion | [[Epiretinal membrane]], [[vitreomacular traction]] |
| Diffuse central blur | [[Macular edema]] (diabetic, venous occlusion, post-surgical) |
| Ring scotoma | [[Hydroxychloroquine retinopathy]] (bull's eye maculopathy) |
| Cecocentral scotoma | [[Optic neuritis]], [[toxic optic neuropathy]], [[Leber hereditary optic neuropathy]] |

## Sensitivity, Specificity, and Limitations

### Diagnostic Performance

The Amsler Grid, while clinically valuable, has well-documented limitations in sensitivity and specificity:

- **Sensitivity for detecting CNV in AMD**: Studies report sensitivity ranging from **34% to 69%**, depending on the study population and methodology. The landmark study by **Schuchard (1993)** demonstrated that the Amsler Grid missed approximately 50% of scotomata detected by more sensitive methods such as [[scanning laser ophthalmoscope]] (SLO) microperimetry.
- **Specificity**: Generally high (approximately **85–95%**) when properly administered, meaning false-positive results are relatively uncommon.
- **Sensitivity for metamorphopsia**: Higher than for scotomata, as distortion is more readily perceived by patients.

### Limitations

1. **Cortical completion (filling-in phenomenon)**: The most significant limitation. The [[visual cortex]] has a tendency to "fill in" missing information, particularly in areas of gradual visual field loss. Patients may perceive the grid as complete even when scotomata are present, because the brain interpolates the missing grid lines from surrounding intact areas. This phenomenon significantly reduces sensitivity for scotoma detection.

2. **Fixation instability**: Patients with central scotomata may develop [[eccentric fixation]], making it impossible to maintain steady fixation on the central dot. This produces unreliable results and may cause the patient to scan the grid rather than fixate centrally.

3. **Patient compliance and understanding**: The test requires adequate cognitive function, attention, and comprehension of instructions. Patients with [[cognitive impairment]], language barriers, or poor motivation may produce unreliable results.

4. **Low spatial resolution**: The 5 mm grid spacing (1 degree per square) provides relatively coarse sampling of the central visual field. Small scotomata or subtle distortions may fall between grid lines and go undetected.

5. **Lack of quantification**: The Amsler Grid provides qualitative rather than quantitative data. It cannot measure the depth of a scotoma or the magnitude of distortion in standardized units.

6. **Refractive error effects**: Uncorrected [[presbyopia]], [[astigmatism]], or other refractive errors can produce blur that mimics pathological scotomata.

7. **Media opacities**: [[Cataract]], [[corneal opacity]], or [[vitreous hemorrhage]] may produce generalized blur that confounds interpretation.

## Modifications and Advanced Versions

### Threshold Amsler Grid (Modified Amsler Grid)

The **Threshold Amsler Grid**, described by **Wall and Sadun (1986)**, represents a significant improvement in sensitivity. This modification uses:

- A **white grid on a black background** (standard Chart 1)
- **Cross-polarizing filters**: The patient wears cross-polarized lenses while viewing the grid through a second polarizing filter, reducing the effective contrast of the grid to near-threshold levels
- By reducing contrast, the filling-in phenomenon is minimized, and scotomata become more apparent

Studies have demonstrated that the Threshold Amsler Grid improves sensitivity for scotoma detection to approximately **85%**, compared with 34–50% for the standard grid.

### Preferential Hyperacuity Perimetry (PHP) / ForeseeHome

[[Preferential hyperacuity perimetry]] (PHP), commercialized as the **ForeseeHome** monitoring device (Notal Vision), represents a technologically advanced evolution of the Amsler Grid concept. PHP:

- Presents a series of dotted lines on a screen, with artificial distortions ("signals") embedded among them
- The patient identifies the location of perceived distortion
- Sophisticated algorithms differentiate pathological distortion from the artificial signals
- The **HOME Study (2014)** demonstrated that PHP-based home monitoring detected CNV conversion earlier and with better visual acuity outcomes than standard Amsler Grid home monitoring

### Digital and App-Based Amsler Grids

Numerous smartphone applications and web-based platforms now offer digital Amsler Grid testing. While convenient, these digital versions introduce variables including:

- Screen size and resolution differences
- Variable viewing distances
- Ambient lighting conditions
- Lack of standardized calibration

Clinicians should exercise caution when interpreting results from non-standardized digital platforms, though validated digital tools (e.g., **myVisionTrack**, **Alleye**) have shown promise in clinical trials.

### M-Charts

**M-Charts** (Inami Co., Tokyo, Japan) are a specialized quantitative tool for measuring the degree of metamorphopsia. Unlike the standard Amsler Grid, M-Charts present dotted lines with varying degrees of dot spacing. The minimum dot spacing at which the patient perceives a straight line (rather than a distorted one) provides a quantitative **metamorphopsia score (M-score)**. M-Charts are particularly useful in:

- Pre- and post-operative assessment of [[epiretinal membrane]] surgery
- Monitoring metamorphopsia in [[macular hole]] repair
- Research applications requiring quantitative distortion measurement

## Role in Specific Clinical Conditions

### Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)

The Amsler Grid's most critical clinical application is in [[age-related macular degeneration]]. Patients with intermediate AMD (AREDS category 3) have an approximately **6.3% annual risk** of progressing to neovascular AMD. Early detection of CNV is paramount because:

- [[Anti-VEGF therapy]] (e.g., [[ranibizumab]], [[aflibercept]], [[bevacizumab]], [[faricimab]], [[brolucizumab]]) is most effective when initiated early, before irreversible photoreceptor damage occurs
- Delayed treatment is associated with worse visual acuity outcomes
- The Amsler Grid serves as a first-line home monitoring tool, alerting patients to seek immediate evaluation if new distortion or scotomata develop

**Patient instructions for home monitoring**:
- Test each eye separately, every day, at the same time
- Wear reading glasses
- Hold the grid at 12–14 inches
- Look at the center dot
- Note any new waviness, blurring, dark spots, or missing areas
- If any change is detected, contact the eye care provider **immediately** (same day if possible)

### Diabetic Macular Edema

In [[diabetic retinopathy]], the Amsler Grid can detect [[diabetic macular edema]] (DME)-related metamorphopsia and central scotomata. However, OCT is the gold standard for DME diagnosis and monitoring, and the Amsler Grid serves a supplementary role.

### Hydroxychloroquine Retinopathy Screening

While the Amsler Grid was historically included in [[hydroxychloroquine]] toxicity screening protocols, the **2016 AAO Revised Recommendations** de-emphasized its role due to insufficient sensitivity. Current guidelines recommend [[spectral-domain OCT]] and [[automated visual field testing]] (10-2) as primary screening modalities, with [[multifocal electroretinography]] (mfERG) and [[fundus autofluorescence]] (FAF) as ancillary tests.

## Patient Education and Compliance

Effective use of the Amsler Grid for home monitoring requires thorough patient education. Key elements include:

1. **Demonstration in the office**: The clinician should administer the test in the office first, ensuring the patient understands the procedure and can perform it independently
2. **Written instructions**: Provide clear, written instructions with the grid
3. **Emphasis on monocular testing**: Patients frequently forget to cover one eye; reinforce this critical step
4. **Urgency of reporting changes**: Patients must understand that new distortion or scotomata may indicate sight-threatening disease requiring **urgent** evaluation
5. **Consistency**: Encourage daily testing at the same time, in the same location, with the same lighting
6. **Limitations acknowledgment**: Inform patients that a normal Amsler Grid result does not guarantee the absence of macular disease; regular comprehensive eye examinations remain essential

Studies have shown that patient compliance with home Amsler Grid monitoring is variable, with adherence rates declining significantly over time. The **AREDS2 HOME Study** found that adding PHP-based electronic monitoring improved detection rates compared with Amsler Grid alone.

## Pharmacological and Therapeutic Relevance

The Amsler Grid itself is not a therapeutic intervention; however, its findings directly influence treatment decisions:

1. **New metamorphopsia on Amsler Grid** → Urgent [[dilated fundus examination]] and [[OCT]] → If CNV confirmed → Initiation of [[anti-VEGF]] intravitreal injection therapy
2. **Progressive scotoma on serial testing** → Evaluation for [[geographic atrophy]] progression → Consideration of [[pegcetacoplan]] (Syfovre) or [[avacincaptad pegol]] (Izervay) for complement-mediated GA
3. **Stable Amsler Grid findings** → Continued monitoring; reinforcement of [[AREDS2 supplementation]] in appropriate AMD patients
4. **Post-injection monitoring** → Amsler Grid used between clinic visits to detect recurrence of CNV activity, prompting earlier retreatment

---

## Medicolegal Considerations

The Amsler Grid has medicolegal significance in optometric and ophthalmologic practice:

- **Standard of care**: Documenting Amsler Grid testing (or equivalent macular function assessment) is considered part of the standard of care for patients with known or suspected macular disease
- **Patient education documentation**: Recording that the patient was instructed in home Amsler Grid use, including the urgency of reporting changes, is important for medicolegal protection
- **Failure to detect**: Given the known limitations in sensitivity, clinicians should not rely solely on the Amsler Grid for macular disease screening; supplementary testing with OCT and other modalities is strongly recommended
- **Informed consent**: Patients should be informed that the Amsler Grid is a screening tool with limitations, not a definitive diagnostic test

---

## Summary

The Amsler Grid Test remains an indispensable tool in the optometric and ophthalmologic armamentarium for the detection and monitoring of macular pathology. Its simplicity, low cost, and suitability for home monitoring make it uniquely valuable, particularly for patients at risk of neovascular AMD conversion. However, clinicians must be cognizant of its limitations—particularly the filling-in phenomenon and moderate sensitivity—and should integrate Amsler Grid findings with advanced imaging modalities such as [[OCT]], [[fluorescein angiography]], and [[microperimetry]] for comprehensive macular assessment. Patient education regarding proper technique and the urgency of reporting new symptoms remains a critical component of effective Amsler Grid utilization.
