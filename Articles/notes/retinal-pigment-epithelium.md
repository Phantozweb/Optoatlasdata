

# Retinal Pigment Epithelium

---

The **Retinal Pigment Epithelium (RPE)** is a monolayer of highly specialized, pigmented cells situated between the [[neurosensory retina]] and [[Bruch's membrane]] of the [[choroid]]. It constitutes one of the most metabolically active cell layers in the human body and is indispensable for the maintenance of normal visual function. The RPE performs a constellation of critical physiological roles—including [[phagocytosis]] of shed [[photoreceptor]] outer segments, transport of nutrients and metabolic waste, absorption of stray light, secretion of essential growth factors, maintenance of the [[outer blood-retinal barrier]], and participation in the [[visual cycle]] through retinoid recycling. Dysfunction or degeneration of the RPE is a central pathogenic event in numerous sight-threatening conditions, most notably [[age-related macular degeneration]] (AMD), [[central serous chorioretinopathy]] (CSCR), and various inherited [[retinal dystrophies]]. A thorough understanding of RPE biology is therefore foundational for every eye care professional.

---

## Embryology and Development

The RPE is derived from the **outer layer of the [[optic cup]]**, which itself originates from the [[neuroectoderm]] of the [[prosencephalon]] (forebrain). During embryogenesis, the optic vesicle invaginates to form the bilayered optic cup. The outer layer differentiates into the RPE, while the inner layer gives rise to the [[neurosensory retina]]. Melanogenesis within RPE cells begins as early as the fifth week of gestation in humans, making the RPE one of the first structures in the eye to become pigmented.

Key developmental signaling pathways involved in RPE specification include:

| Signaling Pathway | Role in RPE Development |
|---|---|
| **[[Microphthalmia-associated transcription factor]] (MITF)** | Master regulator of RPE differentiation and melanogenesis |
| **[[Orthodenticle homeobox 2]] (OTX2)** | Essential for RPE fate determination and maintenance |
| **[[Wingless-related integration site]] (WNT) signaling** | Modulates RPE vs. neural retina fate decisions |
| **[[Sonic hedgehog]] (SHH)** | Influences patterning of the optic vesicle |
| **[[Bone morphogenetic protein]] (BMP) signaling** | Promotes RPE differentiation in the dorsal optic cup |
| **[[Fibroblast growth factor]] (FGF) signaling** | Inhibits RPE fate; promotes neural retina differentiation |

Disruption of these pathways during development can result in congenital anomalies such as [[coloboma]], [[microphthalmos]], and [[retinal dysplasia]].

## Anatomy and Histology

### Gross Anatomy

The RPE extends from the margins of the [[optic disc]] anteriorly to the [[ora serrata]], where it transitions into the pigmented epithelium of the [[ciliary body]]. The RPE monolayer comprises approximately **3.5 to 6 million cells** in the human eye. RPE cells exhibit regional variation in morphology:

- **Macular RPE cells**: Taller, more columnar, more densely packed (~5,000 cells/mm²), and contain greater quantities of [[melanin]] granules. These cells are critical for supporting the high metabolic demands of the [[foveal]] [[cone photoreceptors]].
- **Peripheral RPE cells**: Flatter, larger in diameter, less densely packed (~2,000 cells/mm²), and contain relatively fewer melanosomes but accumulate more [[lipofuscin]] with age.

### Cellular Ultrastructure

Each RPE cell is a polarized epithelial cell with distinct apical, lateral, and basal domains:

| Domain | Structural Features | Functional Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Apical surface** | Long microvilli (5–7 µm) that interdigitate with photoreceptor outer segments; melanosomes concentrated apically | Phagocytosis of shed outer segment discs; light absorption; nutrient exchange with photoreceptors |
| **Lateral surface** | [[Tight junctions]] (zonulae occludentes), [[adherens junctions]], and [[gap junctions]] between adjacent RPE cells | Formation of the [[outer blood-retinal barrier]]; intercellular communication |
| **Basal surface** | Extensive basal infoldings; rests on the RPE [[basement membrane]], which forms the innermost layer of [[Bruch's membrane]] | Transport of nutrients from the [[choriocapillaris]]; waste removal; structural attachment |

The RPE cell cytoplasm contains abundant [[mitochondria]] (concentrated basally, reflecting high metabolic activity), [[smooth endoplasmic reticulum]] (involved in retinoid metabolism), [[melanosomes]] (containing melanin pigment), and [[lipofuscin]] granules (which accumulate with age as a byproduct of incomplete photoreceptor outer segment digestion). Compound **melanolipofuscin** granules also form with aging.

### Pigmentation

RPE cells contain two principal pigments:

1. **[[Melanin]]**: Synthesized during fetal development and early postnatal life; concentrated in melanosomes in the apical cytoplasm. Melanin absorbs scattered light to reduce optical noise, scavenges free radicals, and chelates heavy metal ions. RPE melanin content decreases with age.
2. **[[Lipofuscin]]**: A heterogeneous mixture of bisretinoid fluorophores, the most prominent being **[[A2E]]** (N-retinylidene-N-retinylethanolamine). Lipofuscin accumulates progressively with age as a consequence of incomplete lysosomal degradation of phagocytosed photoreceptor outer segment material. Excessive lipofuscin accumulation is cytotoxic and is implicated in the pathogenesis of [[age-related macular degeneration]] and [[Stargardt disease]].

## Physiology and Functions

The RPE performs an extraordinary range of functions essential for retinal homeostasis. These can be categorized as follows:

### 1. Phagocytosis of Photoreceptor Outer Segments

[[Photoreceptor]] outer segments undergo continuous renewal. The distal tips of outer segment discs are shed daily in a circadian-regulated process—[[rod photoreceptor]] outer segments are shed predominantly after light onset, while [[cone photoreceptor]] outer segments are shed after dark onset. Each RPE cell is responsible for phagocytosing the shed outer segment material from approximately **30–50 overlying photoreceptors**. This process involves:

- Recognition and binding via receptors including **[[αvβ5 integrin]]**, **[[MerTK]] (Mer tyrosine kinase)**, and **[[CD36]]**
- Internalization into [[phagosomes]]
- Fusion with [[lysosomes]] for enzymatic degradation
- Recycling of essential components (e.g., retinoids, docosahexaenoic acid)

Mutations in **MerTK** lead to defective phagocytosis and are associated with [[retinitis pigmentosa]] (RP).

### 2. Visual Cycle (Retinoid Recycling)

The RPE is the principal site of the **[[visual cycle]]** (retinoid cycle), the biochemical pathway that regenerates the visual chromophore **[[11-cis-retinal]]** from **[[all-trans-retinal]]** after photoisomerization in photoreceptors.

Key enzymatic steps within the RPE include:

| Enzyme | Function |
|---|---|
| **[[Lecithin retinol acyltransferase]] (LRAT)** | Esterifies all-trans-retinol to all-trans-retinyl esters for storage |
| **[[Retinal pigment epithelium-specific 65 kDa protein]] (RPE65)** | Isomerohydrolase that converts all-trans-retinyl esters to 11-cis-retinol—the rate-limiting step |
| **[[11-cis-retinol dehydrogenase]] (RDH5)** | Oxidizes 11-cis-retinol to 11-cis-retinal |

Mutations in **RPE65** cause [[Leber congenital amaurosis]] type 2 (LCA2) and certain forms of [[retinitis pigmentosa]], and this gene is the target of the FDA-approved gene therapy **[[voretigene neparvovec-rzyl]]** (Luxturna).

### 3. Outer Blood-Retinal Barrier

The [[tight junctions]] between adjacent RPE cells form the **[[outer blood-retinal barrier]]** (oBRB), which strictly regulates the paracellular and transcellular transport of ions, water, nutrients, and metabolic waste between the [[choriocapillaris]] and the subretinal space. This barrier is essential for maintaining the unique ionic and molecular composition of the subretinal microenvironment required for photoreceptor function.

Breakdown of the oBRB is a hallmark of conditions such as [[central serous chorioretinopathy]], [[diabetic macular edema]], and [[uveitis]].

### 4. Transport Functions

The RPE actively transports:

- **Glucose** from the choroidal blood supply to photoreceptors via [[GLUT1]] transporters (located on both apical and basolateral membranes)
- **Retinol** (vitamin A) bound to [[retinol-binding protein]] (RBP) via the receptor **[[STRA6]]**
- **Water and ions** from the subretinal space to the choroid, maintaining retinal adhesion and preventing subretinal fluid accumulation. Key channels include **[[aquaporins]]**, **Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase** (uniquely located on the apical membrane), and **[[CFTR]]** chloride channels
- **Omega-3 fatty acids** (particularly [[docosahexaenoic acid]], DHA) essential for photoreceptor membrane integrity

### 5. Secretory Functions

The RPE secretes numerous growth factors and cytokines in a polarized fashion:

| Factor | Direction of Secretion | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF)** | Predominantly basolateral | Maintenance of choriocapillaris fenestrations and health |
| **[[Pigment epithelium-derived factor]] (PEDF)** | Predominantly apical | Anti-angiogenic; neuroprotective for photoreceptors |
| **[[Fibroblast growth factor]] (FGF)** | Bidirectional | Trophic support |
| **[[Tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinases]] (TIMPs)** | Basolateral | Regulation of extracellular matrix remodeling |
| **[[Platelet-derived growth factor]] (PDGF)** | Basolateral | Pericyte recruitment and vascular stability |

An imbalance between pro-angiogenic (VEGF) and anti-angiogenic (PEDF) factors secreted by the RPE is a critical event in the development of [[choroidal neovascularization]] (CNV) in [[wet age-related macular degeneration]].

### 6. Light Absorption and Photoprotection

Melanin within RPE melanosomes absorbs stray and scattered light that has passed through the neurosensory retina, thereby reducing light scatter and improving image quality. Additionally, melanin acts as a free radical scavenger, protecting against [[photo-oxidative damage]]. With aging, the ratio of melanin to lipofuscin decreases, reducing the RPE's photoprotective capacity.

### 7. Immune Modulation

The RPE contributes to the **[[immune privilege]]** of the subretinal space by:

- Expressing [[Fas ligand]] (FasL/CD95L), which induces apoptosis of infiltrating inflammatory cells
- Secreting immunosuppressive cytokines such as **[[transforming growth factor-beta]]** (TGF-β)
- Expressing **[[complement regulatory proteins]]** (e.g., [[complement factor H]], [[CD46]], [[CD59]]) to modulate [[complement system]] activation
- Participating in antigen presentation via [[MHC class I]] and [[MHC class II]] molecules

Dysregulation of RPE-mediated immune modulation, particularly involving the [[complement cascade]], is strongly implicated in the pathogenesis of [[age-related macular degeneration]].

## Pathophysiology of RPE Dysfunction

RPE dysfunction and degeneration are central to the pathogenesis of numerous retinal diseases. The major pathophysiological mechanisms include:

### Oxidative Stress

The RPE exists in a uniquely pro-oxidative environment due to:
- High oxygen tension from the underlying [[choriocapillaris]]
- Chronic light exposure
- High metabolic rate
- Accumulation of lipofuscin (particularly [[A2E]]), which is a photosensitizer generating [[reactive oxygen species]] (ROS)

Chronic oxidative stress leads to mitochondrial dysfunction, DNA damage, protein misfolding, and lipid peroxidation within RPE cells, contributing to the development of [[age-related macular degeneration]].

### Lipofuscin Accumulation and Bisretinoid Toxicity

Excessive accumulation of lipofuscin bisretinoids, especially **A2E**, within RPE lysosomes:
- Inhibits lysosomal enzyme activity
- Generates ROS upon light exposure (phototoxicity)
- Activates the [[complement system]]
- Induces RPE cell apoptosis
- Is a hallmark of [[Stargardt disease]] (caused by mutations in [[ABCA4]]) and is prominent in [[age-related macular degeneration]]

### Drusen Formation

**[[Drusen]]** are extracellular deposits that accumulate between the RPE basement membrane and the inner collagenous layer of [[Bruch's membrane]]. They contain lipids, proteins (including complement components, [[apolipoprotein E]], [[amyloid beta]]), and cellular debris. Drusen are a clinical hallmark of [[age-related macular degeneration]] and reflect chronic RPE metabolic dysfunction and impaired waste clearance through an aging, thickened Bruch's membrane.

### Bruch's Membrane Changes

With aging, [[Bruch's membrane]] undergoes:
- Thickening (from ~2 µm in youth to ~4.7 µm by age 90)
- Increased lipid deposition (forming a **lipid wall**)
- Cross-linking of collagen and elastin
- Calcification
- Decreased hydraulic conductivity

These changes impair the bidirectional transport of nutrients and waste between the RPE and choriocapillaris, contributing to RPE stress and dysfunction.

### Complement Dysregulation

Genetic variants in complement pathway genes—particularly **[[complement factor H]]** (CFH, Y402H polymorphism), **[[complement factor B]]** (CFB), **[[complement component 2]]** (C2), and **[[complement component 3]]** (C3)—are major genetic risk factors for [[age-related macular degeneration]]. Uncontrolled complement activation at the RPE–Bruch's membrane interface leads to chronic inflammation, RPE damage, and drusen formation.

### Epithelial-Mesenchymal Transition (EMT)

Under pathological conditions (e.g., [[proliferative vitreoretinopathy]], [[retinal detachment]]), RPE cells can undergo **[[epithelial-mesenchymal transition]]**, losing their epithelial characteristics and acquiring a fibroblastic, migratory phenotype. This contributes to epiretinal and subretinal membrane formation and tractional complications.

## Clinical Conditions Associated with RPE Dysfunction

### Primary RPE Diseases and Dystrophies

| Condition | Etiology/Gene | Key RPE Pathology |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Age-related macular degeneration]]** (dry/atrophic) | Multifactorial (CFH, ARMS2, HTRA1, C3, etc.) | Drusen, RPE atrophy ([[geographic atrophy]]), lipofuscin accumulation |
| **[[Age-related macular degeneration]]** (wet/neovascular) | Multifactorial | RPE-derived VEGF overexpression → [[choroidal neovascularization]] |
| **[[Stargardt disease]]** | [[ABCA4]] mutations (autosomal recessive) | Massive lipofuscin/A2E accumulation → RPE cell death |
| **[[Best vitelliform macular dystrophy]]** | [[BESTROPHIN-1]] (BEST1) mutations | Abnormal chloride channel function → subretinal fluid and lipofuscin accumulation |
| **[[Leber congenital amaurosis]] type 2** | [[RPE65]] mutations | Defective visual cycle → severe visual impairment from birth |
| **[[Central serous chorioretinopathy]]** | Idiopathic; associated with corticosteroids, type A personality | Focal RPE barrier breakdown → serous [[retinal detachment]] |
| **[[Retinitis pigmentosa]]** (select forms) | [[MerTK]], [[RPE65]], [[LRAT]], [[RDH12]] mutations | Defective phagocytosis or visual cycle → photoreceptor degeneration |
| **[[Reticular pseudodrusen]]** (subretinal drusenoid deposits) | Associated with AMD | Deposits located above the RPE (subretinal space) |
| **[[Proliferative vitreoretinopathy]]** (PVR) | Complication of [[retinal detachment]] or surgery | RPE EMT → membrane formation and tractional detachment |
| **[[RPE tears/rips]]** | Complication of [[pigment epithelial detachment]] (PED), often in wet AMD | Mechanical rupture of RPE monolayer |

## Symptoms of RPE Dysfunction

Patients with RPE dysfunction may present with a spectrum of visual symptoms depending on the location, extent, and nature of the pathology:

- **[[Metamorphopsia]]**: Distortion of straight lines, often an early symptom when the macular RPE is affected (e.g., in wet AMD or CSCR)
- **[[Central scotoma]]**: A blind or dark spot in the central visual field, indicative of foveal RPE and photoreceptor loss (e.g., geographic atrophy)
- **Decreased [[visual acuity]]**: Gradual or sudden reduction in central vision
- **[[Micropsia]] or [[macropsia]]**: Perceived change in the size of objects, common in [[central serous chorioretinopathy]]
- **[[Dyschromatopsia]]**: Impaired color vision, particularly affecting blue-yellow discrimination in early RPE disease
- **Reduced [[contrast sensitivity]]**: Often an early functional deficit preceding measurable acuity loss
- **[[Nyctalopia]]** (night blindness): Prominent in conditions affecting the visual cycle (e.g., RPE65-related dystrophies, fundus albipunctatus)
- **[[Photopsia]]**: Flashes of light, which may occur with RPE tears or acute serous detachments
- **Delayed [[dark adaptation]]**: A sensitive early marker of RPE dysfunction, particularly in early AMD; measurable with [[dark adaptometry]]

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical Examination

- **[[Dilated fundus examination]]**: Direct visualization of RPE changes including pigmentary mottling, hypo/hyperpigmentation, drusen, atrophy, and detachments
- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]** with fundus lenses (e.g., [[78D lens]], [[90D lens]], [[Goldmann three-mirror lens]]): Detailed stereoscopic examination of the macula and RPE

### Imaging

| Modality | RPE-Specific Findings |
|---|---|
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)** | Visualization of RPE as a hyperreflective band; detection of [[pigment epithelial detachment]] (PED), drusen, subretinal fluid, RPE atrophy, and disruption of the [[ellipsoid zone]] |
| **[[OCT angiography]] (OCTA)** | Detection of [[choroidal neovascularization]] at the RPE level; visualization of choriocapillaris flow voids associated with RPE atrophy |
| **[[Fundus autofluorescence]] (FAF)** | Maps lipofuscin distribution; hyper-autofluorescence indicates lipofuscin accumulation (e.g., Stargardt disease, peri-atrophic zones in AMD); hypo-autofluorescence indicates RPE atrophy or absence |
| **[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA)** | Window defects (transmission hyperfluorescence) from RPE atrophy; blocked fluorescence from RPE hyperpigmentation; leakage patterns in CSCR and CNV; staining of drusen |
| **[[Indocyanine green angiography]] (ICGA)** | Evaluation of choroidal vasculature underlying RPE; detection of polypoidal choroidal vasculopathy (PCV) and choroidal hyperpermeability in CSCR |
| **[[Enhanced depth imaging OCT]] (EDI-OCT)** | Assessment of choroidal thickness and structure beneath the RPE; useful in pachychoroid spectrum diseases |
| **[[Adaptive optics]] imaging** | Cellular-level resolution of individual RPE cells; research tool for monitoring RPE mosaic integrity |
| **[[Electrooculography]] (EOG)** | Measures the standing potential of the eye, which is generated by the RPE; the **Arden ratio** (light peak/dark trough) is specifically an index of RPE function; abnormal in [[Best disease]] (reduced Arden ratio) |
| **[[Multifocal electroretinography]] (mfERG)** | Topographic assessment of retinal function; can reveal localized dysfunction corresponding to RPE pathology |

### Laboratory and Genetic Testing

- **[[Genetic testing]]**: Essential for confirming inherited RPE dystrophies (e.g., ABCA4, BEST1, RPE65, MerTK mutations); increasingly important for determining eligibility for gene therapy
- **[[Dark adaptometry]]**: Quantitative measurement of dark adaptation kinetics; the **AdaptDx** device measures the rod intercept time, which is prolonged in early AMD due to impaired RPE-mediated retinoid recycling

## Management and Treatment

### Monitoring and Preventive Strategies

- **Regular dilated fundus examinations** and **OCT monitoring** for patients with early RPE changes (e.g., drusen, pigmentary changes)
- **[[Amsler grid]]** home monitoring for early detection of metamorphopsia
- **Lifestyle modifications**: Smoking cessation (smoking is the strongest modifiable risk factor for AMD), UV protection with appropriate [[spectacle lenses]], and a diet rich in [[lutein]], [[zeaxanthin]], [[omega-3 fatty acids]], and antioxidants
- **[[AREDS2]] supplementation**: For patients with intermediate AMD—vitamin C (500 mg), vitamin E (400 IU), lutein (10 mg), zeaxanthin (2 mg), zinc (80 mg), and copper (2 mg)—shown to reduce the risk of progression to advanced AMD by approximately 25%

### Pharmacological Interventions

| Agent | Mechanism | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Anti-VEGF]] agents** (e.g., [[ranibizumab]], [[aflibercept]], [[bevacizumab]], [[brolucizumab]], [[faricimab]]) | Inhibit VEGF, reducing CNV growth and vascular permeability | Neovascular (wet) [[age-related macular degeneration]]; secondary CNV |
| **[[Pegcetacoplan]]** (Syfovre) | [[Complement C3]] inhibitor | [[Geographic atrophy]] secondary to AMD (FDA-approved 2023); slows RPE atrophy progression |
| **[[Avacincaptad pegol]]** (Izervay) | [[Complement C5]] inhibitor | [[Geographic atrophy]] secondary to AMD (FDA-approved 2023) |
| **[[Voretigene neparvovec-rzyl]]** (Luxturna) | [[Gene therapy]]: AAV2 vector delivering functional RPE65 gene | [[RPE65]]-associated [[Leber congenital amaurosis]] and [[retinitis pigmentosa]] |
| **[[Verteporfin]] [[photodynamic therapy]]** (PDT) | Selective vascular occlusion of CNV or areas of choroidal hyperpermeability | Chronic [[central serous chorioretinopathy]]; select cases of [[polypoidal choroidal vasculopathy]] |
| **[[Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists]]** (e.g., [[eplerenone]], [[spironolactone]]) | Reduce choroidal vascular hyperpermeability | Chronic [[central serous chorioretinopathy]] (off-label) |
| **[[Visual cycle modulators]]** (e.g., emixustat, ALK-001/gildeuretinol) | Slow the visual cycle to reduce toxic bisretinoid (A2E) accumulation | Under investigation for [[Stargardt disease]] and [[geographic atrophy]] |

### Surgical and Interventional Approaches

- **[[Intravitreal injections]]**: The primary route of administration for anti-VEGF agents and complement inhibitors
- **[[Subretinal injection]]**: Used for gene therapy delivery (e.g., Luxturna) to directly target RPE cells
- **[[Macular translocation surgery]]**: Historically performed to relocate the fovea over healthier RPE in cases of subfoveal CNV; largely supplanted by anti-VEGF therapy
- **[[RPE transplantation]]**: Experimental approaches include:
  - Autologous RPE-choroid patch graft (translocation of a peripheral RPE-Bruch's membrane-choroid patch to the macula)
  - Transplantation of RPE cells derived from [[human embryonic stem cells]] (hESCs) or [[induced pluripotent stem cells]] (iPSCs)—multiple clinical trials are ongoing (e.g., studies by Schwartz et al. using hESC-derived RPE; the CPCB-RPE1 implant; the London Project to Cure Blindness)

### Emerging and Investigational Therapies

| Therapy | Mechanism/Approach | Status |
|---|---|---|
| **Stem cell-derived RPE transplantation** | Replacement of degenerated RPE with hESC- or iPSC-derived RPE cells on bioengineered scaffolds | Phase I/II clinical trials |
| **[[Optogenetics]]** | Introduction of light-sensitive proteins into surviving retinal cells to restore light sensitivity independent of RPE/photoreceptors | Preclinical/early clinical |
| **[[CRISPR/Cas9]] gene editing** | Correction of specific RPE gene mutations in situ | Preclinical |
| **Complement pathway inhibitors** (next-generation) | Targeting various complement components (e.g., factor D, factor B, properdin) | Phase II/III trials |
| **Neuroprotective agents** (e.g., [[ciliary neurotrophic factor]], [[brimonidine]] intravitreal implant) | Slow photoreceptor and RPE degeneration | Phase II/III trials |
| **Artificial/bionic RPE** | Microelectronic devices mimicking RPE transport and support functions | Early research |

## RPE in Aging

Even in the absence of overt disease, the RPE undergoes significant age-related changes that predispose to pathology:

- **Lipofuscin accumulation**: Progressive increase in lipofuscin granules, peaking in the seventh to eighth decades
- **Melanin loss**: Decrease in melanosome number and melanin content, reducing photoprotective capacity
- **Mitochondrial dysfunction**: Decline in mitochondrial DNA integrity and oxidative phosphorylation efficiency
- **Cell loss**: Gradual reduction in RPE cell density (approximately 0.3% per year), with remaining cells spreading to cover the denuded area (cellular pleomorphism and polymegethism)
- **Bruch's membrane thickening**: Impaired transport across an increasingly calcified and lipid-laden Bruch's membrane
- **Reduced phagocytic capacity**: Slower and less efficient processing of photoreceptor outer segments
- **Altered gene expression**: Downregulation of antioxidant enzymes and upregulation of pro-inflammatory mediators

These cumulative changes create a vulnerable RPE that is susceptible to the additional insults (genetic, environmental) that drive the transition from normal aging to disease states such as [[age-related macular degeneration]].

## RPE and the Interphotoreceptor Matrix

The **[[interphotoreceptor matrix]]** (IPM) occupies the subretinal space between the RPE apical surface and the photoreceptor outer segments. The RPE contributes significantly to the composition and maintenance of the IPM, which contains:

- **[[Interphotoreceptor retinoid-binding protein]]** (IRBP): Facilitates retinoid transport between RPE and photoreceptors during the visual cycle
- **[[Glycosaminoglycans]]** (e.g., chondroitin sulfate, hyaluronic acid): Provide structural support and facilitate molecular transport
- **Growth factors and cytokines**: Secreted by the RPE to support photoreceptor survival

Disruption of the IPM composition, as occurs with RPE dysfunction, compromises photoreceptor-RPE adhesion and can predispose to [[retinal detachment]].

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis for patients with RPE-related disease varies widely depending on the specific condition, its stage at diagnosis, and the availability of treatment:

- **Early/intermediate AMD with drusen**: Generally good visual prognosis with appropriate monitoring and AREDS2 supplementation; however, risk of progression to advanced disease persists
- **Geographic atrophy**: Progressive and currently irreversible, though complement inhibitors (pegcetacoplan, avacincaptad pegol) can slow the rate of atrophy expansion by approximately 20–36% over 12–24 months
- **Neovascular AMD**: Prognosis dramatically improved with anti-VEGF therapy; most patients can maintain or improve vision with consistent treatment, though the disease requires long-term management
- **RPE65-related dystrophies**: Gene therapy (Luxturna) can provide meaningful and sustained improvement in functional vision, particularly in navigational ability under low-light conditions
- **Stargardt disease**: Progressive central vision loss; no approved treatment currently, though visual cycle modulators and gene therapy are under investigation
- **Central serous chorioretinopathy**: Acute episodes often self-resolve within 3–6 months; chronic CSCR may lead to permanent RPE atrophy and visual impairment

---

## Summary

The **Retinal Pigment Epithelium** is a multifunctional cell layer whose health is inextricably linked to the integrity of the overlying [[photoreceptors]] and the underlying [[choriocapillaris]]. Its roles in phagocytosis, the visual cycle, barrier function, transport, secretion, light absorption, and immune modulation make it one of the most physiologically demanding cell types in the body. Age-related changes and genetic predispositions can compromise RPE function, leading to a spectrum of diseases that collectively represent the leading causes of irreversible vision loss in the developed world. Advances in imaging (particularly [[OCT]] and [[fundus autofluorescence]]), molecular genetics, pharmacotherapy (anti-VEGF agents, complement inhibitors), gene therapy, and regenerative medicine (stem cell-derived RPE transplantation) are transforming the diagnosis and management of RPE-related diseases and offer hope for conditions that were previously untreatable.
