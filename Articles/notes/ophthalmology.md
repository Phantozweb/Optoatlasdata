

# Ophthalmology

**Ophthalmology** (from Greek: *ὀφθαλμός* (*ophthalmos*), "eye"; and *-λογία* (*-logia*), "study of") is the branch of medicine and surgery that deals with the diagnosis, treatment, and prevention of diseases and disorders of the [[Eye]]. As a medical and surgical specialty, ophthalmology encompasses the comprehensive management of conditions affecting the [[Globe (eye)|globe]], [[Orbit (anatomy)|orbit]], [[Eyelid|eyelids]], [[Lacrimal apparatus]], [[Extraocular muscles]], [[Visual pathway|visual pathways]], and associated [[Adnexa|adnexal structures]]. Ophthalmology is distinct from, yet deeply interrelated with, [[Optometry]], which focuses primarily on refractive correction, primary eye care, and the detection and co-management of ocular disease. The ophthalmologist—a physician who has completed medical school followed by residency training in ophthalmology—is uniquely qualified to provide the full spectrum of eye care, including complex medical therapy, laser procedures, and intraocular surgery.

This article provides a comprehensive clinical overview of the field of ophthalmology, including its scope, the pathophysiology of major ophthalmic diseases, clinical presentation and symptomatology, diagnostic modalities, pharmacological interventions, surgical approaches, and the critical interface between ophthalmology and optometry in collaborative patient care.

## Ocular Anatomy Relevant to Ophthalmology

A thorough understanding of [[Ocular anatomy]] is foundational to all ophthalmic practice. The eye is a complex sensory organ approximately 24 mm in axial length in the emmetropic adult. Key anatomical structures include:

- **[[Cornea]]**: The transparent, avascular anterior structure responsible for approximately two-thirds of the eye's total refractive power (~43 diopters). It is composed of five layers: [[Epithelium (cornea)|epithelium]], [[Bowman's layer]], [[Stroma (cornea)|stroma]], [[Descemet's membrane]], and [[Endothelium (cornea)|endothelium]].
- **[[Anterior chamber]]**: The fluid-filled space between the cornea and the [[Iris (anatomy)|iris]], containing [[Aqueous humor]] produced by the [[Ciliary body]].
- **[[Crystalline lens]]**: A biconvex, transparent structure posterior to the iris, responsible for [[Accommodation]] and approximately one-third of the eye's refractive power.
- **[[Iris (anatomy)|Iris]]**: The pigmented diaphragm controlling [[Pupil]] size and regulating light entry.
- **[[Vitreous humor]]**: The transparent gel filling the posterior segment of the eye, composed primarily of water, [[Collagen]], and [[Hyaluronic acid]].
- **[[Retina]]**: The neurosensory tissue lining the inner posterior wall of the globe, containing [[Photoreceptor cell|photoreceptors]] ([[Rod cell|rods]] and [[Cone cell|cones]]), [[Bipolar cell|bipolar cells]], [[Ganglion cell|retinal ganglion cells]] (RGCs), and supporting cells such as [[Müller cell|Müller glia]] and [[Retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE).
- **[[Optic nerve]]**: Formed by approximately 1.2 million RGC axons, it transmits visual information from the retina to the [[Lateral geniculate nucleus]] and ultimately the [[Visual cortex]].
- **[[Choroid]]**: The highly vascular layer between the retina and sclera, providing oxygen and nutrients to the outer retina.
- **[[Sclera]]**: The dense, white, fibrous outer coat of the eye providing structural integrity.
- **[[Orbit (anatomy)|Orbit]]**: The bony cavity housing the globe, [[Extraocular muscles]], nerves, blood vessels, and fat.

## Pathophysiology of Major Ophthalmic Diseases

### Cataract

A [[Cataract]] is an opacification of the [[Crystalline lens]] that results in degradation of visual quality. The pathophysiology involves progressive denaturation and aggregation of [[Crystallin]] proteins within the lens fibers, leading to light scattering and absorption. Oxidative stress, ultraviolet (UV) radiation exposure, osmotic stress (as in [[Diabetes mellitus]]), and age-related post-translational protein modifications (glycation, carbamylation, deamidation) are central mechanisms. Cataracts are classified morphologically as [[Nuclear sclerotic cataract|nuclear sclerotic]], [[Cortical cataract|cortical]], and [[Posterior subcapsular cataract|posterior subcapsular]] (PSC). Nuclear cataracts involve central lens hardening and yellowing (brunescence), cortical cataracts feature spoke-like opacities in the lens cortex, and PSC cataracts develop on the posterior lens capsule, often associated with [[Corticosteroid]] use or [[Uveitis]].

### Glaucoma

[[Glaucoma]] encompasses a group of progressive [[Optic neuropathy|optic neuropathies]] characterized by the degeneration of [[Ganglion cell|retinal ganglion cells]] and their axons, resulting in characteristic patterns of [[Visual field]] loss. The primary modifiable risk factor is elevated [[Intraocular pressure]] (IOP). In [[Primary open-angle glaucoma]] (POAG), impaired outflow of [[Aqueous humor]] through the [[Trabecular meshwork]] and [[Schlemm's canal]] leads to IOP elevation. In [[Angle-closure glaucoma]], the peripheral [[Iris (anatomy)|iris]] physically obstructs the [[Trabecular meshwork]], impeding aqueous drainage. Pathophysiologically, elevated IOP causes mechanical compression and ischemia at the [[Lamina cribrosa]], disrupting axoplasmic transport in RGC axons. Additional mechanisms include vascular dysregulation, excitotoxicity mediated by [[Glutamate]], mitochondrial dysfunction, neuroinflammation, and oxidative stress. [[Normal-tension glaucoma]] (NTG) involves glaucomatous optic neuropathy at statistically normal IOP levels, implicating IOP-independent mechanisms such as vascular insufficiency and cerebrospinal fluid pressure dynamics.

### Diabetic Retinopathy

[[Diabetic retinopathy]] (DR) is a microvascular complication of [[Diabetes mellitus]] and the leading cause of blindness in working-age adults. Chronic hyperglycemia activates multiple biochemical pathways—the [[Polyol pathway]], [[Advanced glycation end-products]] (AGEs) formation, [[Protein kinase C]] (PKC) activation, and the [[Hexosamine pathway]]—leading to [[Pericyte]] loss, [[Basement membrane]] thickening, endothelial cell dysfunction, and breakdown of the [[Blood-retinal barrier]]. The disease progresses from [[Non-proliferative diabetic retinopathy]] (NPDR), characterized by [[Microaneurysm|microaneurysms]], [[Retinal hemorrhage|hemorrhages]], [[Hard exudate|hard exudates]], and [[Cotton-wool spot|cotton-wool spots]], to [[Proliferative diabetic retinopathy]] (PDR), marked by [[Neovascularization]] driven by [[Vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF) upregulation secondary to retinal ischemia. [[Diabetic macular edema]] (DME) can occur at any stage and involves fluid accumulation in the [[Macula]] due to vascular leakage.

### Age-Related Macular Degeneration

[[Age-related macular degeneration]] (AMD) is the leading cause of irreversible central vision loss in individuals over 50 years of age. The pathophysiology involves progressive dysfunction and loss of the [[Retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE), accumulation of [[Drusen]] (extracellular deposits of lipids, proteins, and complement components between the RPE and [[Bruch's membrane]]), and chronic low-grade inflammation involving the [[Complement system]]. **Dry (atrophic) AMD** is characterized by [[Drusen]], RPE changes, and eventual [[Geographic atrophy]]. **Wet (neovascular or exudative) AMD** involves [[Choroidal neovascularization]] (CNV), where abnormal blood vessels grow from the [[Choroid]] through Bruch's membrane into the subretinal space, causing hemorrhage, exudation, and scarring. VEGF is a critical mediator of CNV.

### Retinal Detachment

[[Retinal detachment]] (RD) involves separation of the neurosensory [[Retina]] from the underlying [[Retinal pigment epithelium]]. The three types are:

- **[[Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]]**: Caused by a full-thickness retinal break (tear or hole) allowing liquefied [[Vitreous humor]] to enter the subretinal space. Often preceded by [[Posterior vitreous detachment]] (PVD).
- **[[Tractional retinal detachment]]**: Caused by fibrovascular proliferative membranes exerting traction on the retina, commonly seen in [[Proliferative diabetic retinopathy]] and [[Retinopathy of prematurity]].
- **[[Exudative retinal detachment]]**: Caused by subretinal fluid accumulation without a retinal break, seen in conditions such as [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]], [[Posterior scleritis]], and choroidal tumors.

### Uveitis

[[Uveitis]] refers to inflammation of the [[Uvea]] (iris, ciliary body, choroid) and is a significant cause of visual morbidity. It is classified anatomically as [[Anterior uveitis]] (iritis/iridocyclitis), [[Intermediate uveitis]] (vitritis/pars planitis), [[Posterior uveitis]] (choroiditis/retinitis/chorioretinitis), and [[Panuveitis]]. Pathophysiology involves immune-mediated mechanisms, including autoimmune (e.g., [[HLA-B27]]-associated), infectious (e.g., [[Toxoplasmosis]], [[Herpes simplex virus]], [[Cytomegalovirus]], [[Tuberculosis]], [[Syphilis]]), and idiopathic etiologies. Inflammatory cascades involve [[T-cell|T-lymphocyte]] activation, [[Cytokine]] release (including [[Tumor necrosis factor-alpha|TNF-α]], [[Interleukin-6|IL-6]], [[Interleukin-17|IL-17]]), and disruption of the [[Blood-ocular barrier]].

### Keratoconus

[[Keratoconus]] is a progressive, non-inflammatory ectatic disorder of the [[Cornea]] characterized by progressive corneal thinning and conical protrusion, leading to irregular [[Astigmatism]] and visual distortion. The pathophysiology involves degradation of corneal [[Collagen]] cross-links, increased activity of [[Matrix metalloproteinase|matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs), oxidative stress, and apoptosis of [[Keratocyte|keratocytes]]. Genetic predisposition and environmental factors (e.g., chronic eye rubbing, [[Atopy]]) contribute to disease onset and progression.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Ophthalmic diseases present with a wide range of symptoms. The following table summarizes key symptomatology associated with major conditions encountered in ophthalmology:

| Condition | Key Symptoms |
|---|---|
| [[Cataract]] | Gradual painless decrease in [[Visual acuity]], [[Glare]], halos around lights, monocular diplopia, shift toward myopia (nuclear sclerosis), faded color perception |
| [[Glaucoma]] (Open-angle) | Typically asymptomatic until advanced; progressive peripheral [[Visual field]] loss, eventual central vision loss |
| [[Acute angle-closure glaucoma]] | Severe ocular pain, headache, nausea/vomiting, blurred vision, halos around lights, red eye, mid-dilated fixed [[Pupil]], markedly elevated [[Intraocular pressure|IOP]] |
| [[Diabetic retinopathy]] | Often asymptomatic in early stages; [[Floaters]], blurred vision, fluctuating vision, scotomas, sudden vision loss (vitreous hemorrhage) |
| [[Age-related macular degeneration]] | Central [[Scotoma]], [[Metamorphopsia]] (distortion of straight lines), difficulty reading, decreased contrast sensitivity |
| [[Retinal detachment]] | Sudden onset of [[Floaters]], [[Photopsia]] (flashes of light), curtain or shadow over visual field, painless vision loss |
| [[Anterior uveitis]] | Ocular pain, photophobia, redness ([[Ciliary flush]]), tearing, blurred vision |
| [[Keratoconus]] | Progressive blurring, distortion, frequent changes in spectacle prescription, monocular diplopia or ghosting |
| [[Optic neuritis]] | Acute unilateral vision loss, pain with eye movement, [[Dyschromatopsia]] (impaired color vision), [[Relative afferent pupillary defect]] (RAPD) |
| [[Central retinal artery occlusion]] (CRAO) | Sudden, painless, profound monocular vision loss; [[Cherry-red spot]] on fundoscopy |
| [[Central retinal vein occlusion]] (CRVO) | Sudden painless blurred vision, dilated tortuous retinal veins, widespread retinal hemorrhages ("blood and thunder" fundus) |

## Diagnostic Modalities in Ophthalmology

Modern ophthalmology employs an extensive array of diagnostic technologies to evaluate ocular structure and function:

### Clinical Examination Techniques

- **[[Visual acuity]] testing**: Snellen chart, ETDRS chart, or logMAR assessment to quantify central visual function.
- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: The cornerstone of ophthalmic examination, allowing magnified, illuminated evaluation of the [[Anterior segment]] (eyelids, conjunctiva, cornea, anterior chamber, iris, lens) and, with accessory lenses, the [[Posterior segment]].
- **[[Tonometry]]**: Measurement of [[Intraocular pressure]] (IOP). Methods include [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]] (gold standard), [[Non-contact tonometry]] (air-puff), [[Tono-Pen]], [[iCare tonometry]], and [[Dynamic contour tonometry]].
- **[[Gonioscopy]]**: Direct or indirect visualization of the [[Iridocorneal angle]] (anterior chamber angle) to assess for [[Angle closure]], [[Neovascularization of the angle]], [[Peripheral anterior synechiae]], and angle recession.
- **[[Dilated fundus examination]]**: Using [[Binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy]] (BIO) with a condensing lens or slit-lamp with a [[78D lens|78D]], [[90D lens|90D]], or contact lens to evaluate the [[Retina]], [[Optic disc]], [[Macula]], and [[Vitreous humor|vitreous]].

### Advanced Imaging

- **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)**: A non-invasive imaging modality providing high-resolution cross-sectional images of the retina, optic nerve head, and anterior segment. [[Spectral-domain OCT]] (SD-OCT) and [[Swept-source OCT]] (SS-OCT) enable detailed evaluation of retinal layer architecture, macular thickness, [[Retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL) thickness, and [[Ganglion cell complex]] (GCC) analysis. OCT is indispensable in the diagnosis and monitoring of [[Glaucoma]], [[AMD]], [[DME]], [[Epiretinal membrane]], [[Macular hole]], and [[Vitreomacular traction]].
- **[[OCT angiography]] (OCTA)**: A dye-free technique that visualizes retinal and choroidal vasculature by detecting motion contrast of red blood cells, useful in detecting [[Choroidal neovascularization]], retinal ischemia, and microvascular changes in [[Diabetic retinopathy]].
- **[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA)**: Intravenous injection of [[Fluorescein]] dye with sequential fundus photography to evaluate retinal vascular perfusion, leakage, and neovascularization. Essential in the workup of [[Diabetic retinopathy]], [[Retinal vein occlusion]], [[AMD]], and [[Uveitis]].
- **[[Indocyanine green angiography]] (ICGA)**: Uses [[Indocyanine green]] dye, which binds to plasma proteins and fluoresces in the near-infrared spectrum, allowing superior visualization of the [[Choroidal vasculature]]. Particularly useful in [[Polypoidal choroidal vasculopathy]], [[Central serous chorioretinopathy]], and occult CNV.
- **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]**: Ultrasound imaging of the posterior segment when direct visualization is precluded (e.g., dense cataract, vitreous hemorrhage). Used to detect [[Retinal detachment]], intraocular tumors, and posterior segment pathology.
- **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)**: High-frequency ultrasound providing detailed imaging of the [[Anterior segment]], including the [[Ciliary body]], [[Zonules of Zinn|zonules]], and posterior iris surface. Valuable in evaluating [[Angle closure]] mechanisms, [[Plateau iris syndrome]], and anterior segment tumors.
- **[[Corneal topography]] and [[Corneal tomography]]**: Mapping of the corneal surface curvature (topography, e.g., [[Placido disc]]-based systems) and full corneal thickness/elevation data (tomography, e.g., [[Scheimpflug imaging]] with [[Pentacam]]). Critical for diagnosing [[Keratoconus]], planning [[Refractive surgery]], and evaluating corneal ectasia.

### Functional Testing

- **[[Visual field testing]] (Perimetry)**: [[Standard automated perimetry]] (SAP), most commonly using the [[Humphrey visual field analyzer]], quantifies the sensitivity of the visual field. Essential for diagnosing and monitoring [[Glaucoma]], [[Optic neuropathy]], and neurological visual field defects. Patterns include [[Arcuate scotoma]], [[Nasal step]], [[Paracentral scotoma]], and [[Generalized depression]].
- **[[Electroretinography]] (ERG)**: Measures the electrical response of retinal cells (photoreceptors and inner retinal neurons) to light stimulation. Full-field ERG, multifocal ERG (mfERG), and pattern ERG (PERG) are used to evaluate [[Retinal dystrophy|retinal dystrophies]], [[Retinitis pigmentosa]], and retinal function in various diseases.
- **[[Visual evoked potential]] (VEP)**: Measures the electrical response of the [[Visual cortex]] to visual stimulation, useful in evaluating [[Optic nerve]] function, [[Optic neuritis]], and [[Demyelinating disease]].

## Surgical Approaches in Ophthalmology

Ophthalmic surgery has undergone revolutionary advances, with microsurgical and minimally invasive techniques now standard:

### Cataract Surgery

[[Phacoemulsification]] is the gold-standard technique for cataract extraction. Through a small (2.2–2.8 mm) clear corneal or scleral tunnel incision, an ultrasonic handpiece emulsifies and aspirates the opacified lens nucleus. A foldable [[Intraocular lens]] (IOL) is then implanted into the [[Capsular bag]]. IOL options include [[Monofocal IOL|monofocal]], [[Multifocal IOL|multifocal]], [[Extended depth of focus IOL|extended depth of focus (EDOF)]], [[Toric IOL|toric]] (astigmatism-correcting), and [[Accommodating IOL|accommodating]] lenses. [[Femtosecond laser-assisted cataract surgery]] (FLACS) uses a femtosecond laser for corneal incisions, capsulotomy, and lens fragmentation. Complications include [[Posterior capsule opacification]] (PCO), [[Cystoid macular edema]] (CME, Irvine-Gass syndrome), [[Endophthalmitis]], [[Retinal detachment]], and [[Intraocular lens dislocation]].

### Glaucoma Surgery

- **[[Trabeculectomy]]**: A guarded filtration procedure creating a fistula from the anterior chamber to the subconjunctival space, forming a [[Filtering bleb]] to lower IOP. Adjunctive use of [[Mitomycin C]] (MMC) or [[5-Fluorouracil]] (5-FU) modulates wound healing.
- **[[Glaucoma drainage device|Glaucoma drainage devices (tube shunts)]]**: [[Ahmed glaucoma valve]], [[Baerveldt implant]], [[Molteno implant]]—silicone tubes shunting aqueous to an equatorial plate.
- **[[Minimally invasive glaucoma surgery]] (MIGS)**: A rapidly expanding category including [[iStent]], [[Hydrus Microstent]], [[Kahook Dual Blade]] (goniotomy), [[Trabectome]], [[Xen Gel Stent]], and [[PRESERFLO MicroShunt]]. MIGS procedures offer a favorable safety profile with moderate IOP reduction, often combined with cataract surgery.
- **[[Laser trabeculoplasty]]**: [[Selective laser trabeculoplasty]] (SLT) applies low-energy, frequency-doubled Nd:YAG laser to the [[Trabecular meshwork]] to enhance aqueous outflow. It is repeatable and often used as a first-line or adjunctive treatment.
- **[[Cyclophotocoagulation]]**: [[Transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]] (TSCPC) or [[Endoscopic cyclophotocoagulation]] (ECP) destroys or modulates [[Ciliary body]] epithelium to reduce aqueous production. [[Micropulse transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]] (MP-TSCPC) offers a less destructive alternative.
- **[[Laser peripheral iridotomy]] (LPI)**: Nd:YAG laser creates an opening in the peripheral [[Iris (anatomy)|iris]] to relieve [[Pupillary block]] in [[Angle-closure glaucoma]].

### Vitreoretinal Surgery

- **[[Pars plana vitrectomy]] (PPV)**: Microsurgical removal of the [[Vitreous humor]] through small-gauge (23-, 25-, or 27-gauge) trocar-cannula systems. Indications include [[Retinal detachment]] repair, [[Vitreous hemorrhage]], [[Epiretinal membrane]] peeling, [[Macular hole]] surgery, [[Endophthalmitis]], and removal of dislocated lens material or IOLs. Internal tamponade agents include [[Sulfur hexafluoride]] (SF6) gas, [[Perfluoropropane]] (C3F8) gas, and [[Silicone oil]].
- **[[Scleral buckle]]**: An external silicone band or sponge is sutured to the sclera to indent the globe wall, apposing the RPE to the detached retina. Often combined with [[Cryopexy]] or laser retinopexy.
- **[[Pneumatic retinopexy]]**: An in-office procedure involving intravitreal injection of an expansile gas bubble combined with cryopexy or laser to repair select rhegmatogenous retinal detachments.
- **[[Intravitreal injection]]**: The most commonly performed ophthalmic procedure worldwide. Delivery of [[Anti-VEGF agent|anti-VEGF agents]], [[Corticosteroid|corticosteroids]], or [[Antibiotic|antibiotics]] directly into the [[Vitreous cavity]]. Strict aseptic technique is essential to minimize the risk of [[Endophthalmitis]].

### Corneal Surgery

- **[[Penetrating keratoplasty]] (PK)**: Full-thickness corneal transplantation for conditions such as advanced [[Keratoconus]], corneal scarring, and [[Corneal dystrophy|corneal dystrophies]].
- **[[Descemet's stripping automated endothelial keratoplasty]] (DSAEK)** and **[[Descemet's membrane endothelial keratoplasty]] (DMEK)**: Selective endothelial transplantation techniques for [[Fuchs' endothelial dystrophy]] and [[Pseudophakic bullous keratopathy]], offering faster visual recovery and lower rejection rates than PK.
- **[[Corneal collagen cross-linking]] (CXL)**: Riboflavin and ultraviolet-A (UVA) light treatment to strengthen corneal collagen bonds and halt progression of [[Keratoconus]] and post-refractive ectasia.
- **[[Refractive surgery]]**: [[LASIK]] (laser in situ keratomileusis), [[PRK]] (photorefractive keratectomy), and [[SMILE]] (small incision lenticule extraction) reshape the corneal stroma using [[Excimer laser]] or femtosecond laser technology to correct [[Myopia]], [[Hyperopia]], and [[Astigmatism]].

### Oculoplastic and Orbital Surgery

- **[[Blepharoplasty]]**: Surgical correction of dermatochalasis (excess eyelid skin) and orbital fat prolapse.
- **[[Ptosis repair]]**: Correction of [[Ptosis (eyelid)|ptosis]] via levator advancement/resection or Müller's muscle-conjunctival resection (MMCR).
- **[[Dacryocystorhinostomy]] (DCR)**: Surgical creation of a new drainage pathway from the [[Lacrimal sac]] to the nasal cavity for [[Nasolacrimal duct obstruction]].
- **[[Orbital decompression]]**: Removal of orbital bone and/or fat to treat [[Thyroid eye disease]] (Graves' orbitopathy) with proptosis and compressive optic neuropathy.
- **[[Enucleation]]** and **[[Evisceration (ophthalmology)|Evisceration]]**: Removal of the globe for end-stage painful blind eyes, intraocular malignancy, or severe trauma.

## The Ophthalmology-Optometry Interface

The relationship between [[Ophthalmology]] and [[Optometry]] is one of the most important collaborative dynamics in eye care. Optometrists serve as primary eye care providers, performing comprehensive eye examinations, prescribing corrective lenses, diagnosing ocular disease, and managing many conditions medically. Ophthalmologists provide advanced medical and surgical care. Effective co-management models are essential for conditions such as [[Glaucoma]], [[Diabetic retinopathy]], [[Cataract]], and [[AMD]], where optometrists may detect disease, initiate treatment, and monitor patients between ophthalmologic interventions. Timely referral from optometry to ophthalmology is critical in emergencies such as [[Acute angle-closure glaucoma]], [[Retinal detachment]], [[Central retinal artery occlusion]], [[Endophthalmitis]], [[Chemical eye injury]], and [[Open globe injury]].

## Emerging Technologies and Future Directions

Ophthalmology is at the forefront of medical innovation. Key emerging areas include:

- **[[Gene therapy]]**: [[Voretigene neparvovec]] (Luxturna) for [[RPE65]]-associated [[Leber congenital amaurosis]] and [[Retinitis pigmentosa]] represents the first FDA-approved ocular gene therapy. Numerous gene therapy trials are underway for other inherited retinal dystrophies.
- **[[Artificial intelligence]] (AI) in ophthalmology**: Deep learning algorithms for automated detection of [[Diabetic retinopathy]], [[Glaucoma]], and [[AMD]] from fundus photographs and OCT images are increasingly validated and deployed in screening programs.
- **[[Retinal prosthesis]]**: Devices such as the [[Argus II]] retinal prosthesis aim to restore rudimentary vision in patients with end-stage [[Retinitis pigmentosa]].
- **[[Stem cell therapy]]**: Research into RPE cell transplantation and photoreceptor regeneration for [[AMD]] and retinal degenerations is advancing through clinical trials.
- **Sustained-release drug delivery**: [[Port Delivery System]] (Susvimo) for sustained intravitreal [[Ranibizumab]] delivery in wet AMD, and biodegradable intravitreal implants, aim to reduce treatment burden.
- **[[Optogenetics]]**: Introduction of light-sensitive proteins into surviving retinal cells to restore light sensitivity in degenerative retinal diseases.

## Prognosis and Public Health Significance

Ophthalmic diseases represent a major global public health burden. According to the World Health Organization (WHO), at least 2.2 billion people worldwide have a near or distance vision impairment, with at least 1 billion cases being preventable or yet to be addressed. [[Cataract]] remains the leading cause of blindness globally, while [[Glaucoma]] is the leading cause of irreversible blindness. [[Diabetic retinopathy]] is the most common cause of blindness in working-age adults in developed nations. Early detection through comprehensive eye examinations—often performed by optometrists—and timely referral to ophthalmology for definitive management are the cornerstones of preventing avoidable vision loss.
