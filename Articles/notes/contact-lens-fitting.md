

# Contact Lens Fitting

**Contact Lens Fitting** is a specialized clinical procedure in [[Optometry]] and [[Ophthalmology]] involving the selection, evaluation, and optimization of [[contact lenses]] to achieve optimal visual correction, ocular health, and patient comfort. It encompasses a comprehensive assessment of [[ocular surface]] anatomy, [[refractive error]], [[tear film]] dynamics, and patient lifestyle factors to prescribe the most appropriate lens type, material, and parameters. Contact lens fitting is both an art and a science, requiring detailed knowledge of [[corneal topography]], lens design, material science, and the physiological response of the [[anterior segment]] to lens wear.

---

## Epidemiology and Clinical Significance

An estimated 140 million people worldwide wear contact lenses, with the majority being soft lens wearers. In the United States alone, approximately 45 million individuals use contact lenses. The clinical significance of proper contact lens fitting cannot be overstated: poorly fitted lenses are a leading cause of [[contact lens-related complications]], including [[microbial keratitis]], [[corneal neovascularization]], [[giant papillary conjunctivitis]] (GPC), [[corneal edema]], and [[dry eye disease]]. The annual incidence of [[microbial keratitis]] in contact lens wearers ranges from 2–4 per 10,000 daily wear soft lens users to 20 per 10,000 extended wear users, underscoring the importance of meticulous fitting and follow-up.

## Relevant Ocular Anatomy and Physiology

### Corneal Anatomy

The [[cornea]] is the primary refracting surface of the eye and the tissue most directly affected by contact lens wear. It is an avascular, transparent structure approximately 540 µm thick centrally and 670 µm peripherally, composed of five layers:

| Layer | Approximate Thickness | Clinical Relevance to Contact Lens Fitting |
|---|---|---|
| [[Epithelium]] | 50–60 µm | First barrier; susceptible to mechanical abrasion from lens edge or deposits |
| [[Bowman's Layer]] | 8–14 µm | Acellular; does not regenerate; scarring affects vision |
| [[Stroma]] | ~500 µm (90% of corneal thickness) | Primary determinant of corneal curvature; swells with hypoxia |
| [[Descemet's Membrane]] | 10–15 µm | Basement membrane of endothelium |
| [[Endothelium]] | 5 µm (single cell layer) | Maintains corneal deturgescence via active pump; does not regenerate in humans |

The corneal shape is typically prolate (steeper centrally, flatter peripherally), with an average central radius of curvature of approximately 7.80 mm (43.25 [[diopters]]). Understanding the [[corneal eccentricity]] (e-value, typically 0.4–0.6) is essential for fitting lenses that align with the corneal contour.

### Tear Film

The [[precorneal tear film]] is a trilaminar structure (lipid, aqueous, and mucin layers) approximately 3–7 µm thick that serves as the primary refracting surface, provides oxygen to the cornea, washes away debris, and lubricates the lens-cornea interface. The [[tear break-up time]] (TBUT), [[Schirmer's test]], and [[tear meniscus height]] are critical assessments during contact lens fitting. A stable tear film (TBUT > 10 seconds) is essential for successful lens wear.

### Conjunctiva and Lids

The [[palpebral conjunctiva]], particularly the upper tarsal plate, interacts with the lens surface during blinking. The [[meibomian glands]] contribute to the lipid layer of the tear film. [[Lid tension]], [[blink rate]], [[palpebral aperture]] size, and lid anatomy all influence lens centration, movement, and comfort.

## Pathophysiology of Contact Lens–Ocular Surface Interaction

Contact lens placement on the eye creates a complex interplay between the lens, tear film, cornea, and lids. Understanding the pathophysiology of this interaction is fundamental to successful fitting.

### Oxygen Transmissibility and Corneal Metabolism

The cornea derives most of its oxygen from the atmosphere via the tear film. A contact lens acts as a barrier to oxygen diffusion. The oxygen transmissibility of a lens is expressed as **Dk/t**, where **Dk** is the oxygen permeability of the material and **t** is the lens thickness. Insufficient oxygen transmissibility leads to:

- **[[Corneal hypoxia]]**: Triggers anaerobic glycolysis in the [[corneal epithelium]] and [[stroma]], producing [[lactic acid]], which draws water osmotically into the stroma, causing [[corneal edema]] (stromal swelling).
- **[[Corneal neovascularization]]**: Chronic hypoxia stimulates release of [[vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF), leading to ingrowth of limbal blood vessels into the normally avascular cornea.
- **[[Endothelial polymegethism]] and [[pleomorphism]]**: Long-term hypoxia causes morphological changes in the [[corneal endothelium]], reducing its functional reserve.

The Holden-Mertz criteria established that a minimum Dk/t of **24 × 10⁻⁹ (cm × mL O₂)/(sec × mL × mmHg)** is required to prevent corneal edema during daily wear, and **87 × 10⁻⁹** for overnight (extended) wear.

### Mechanical Effects

The lens exerts mechanical forces on the [[corneal epithelium]], [[limbus]], and [[conjunctiva]]. A lens that is too tight (steep) restricts tear exchange beneath the lens, leading to debris accumulation, metabolic waste buildup, and potential [[corneal staining]]. A lens that is too loose (flat) causes excessive movement, lens decentration, edge awareness, and mechanical irritation of the [[superior tarsal conjunctiva]], potentially inducing [[contact lens-induced papillary conjunctivitis]] (CLPC).

### Tear Film Disruption

Contact lenses divide the tear film into a pre-lens and post-lens tear film. This disruption can lead to:

- Increased [[tear evaporation]]
- Thinning of the [[lipid layer]]
- Reduced [[tear break-up time]]
- Symptoms of [[contact lens-related dry eye]] (CLDE)

Deposits of [[proteins]] (especially [[lysozyme]] and [[lactoferrin]]), [[lipids]], [[calcium]], and [[mucin]] accumulate on the lens surface, further destabilizing the tear film and potentially triggering inflammatory and immune responses.

### Inflammatory and Immune Responses

Chronic mechanical irritation and deposit accumulation can trigger:

- **[[Giant papillary conjunctivitis]]**: A Type I and Type IV hypersensitivity reaction characterized by papillae ≥1 mm on the upper tarsal conjunctiva.
- **[[Contact lens-induced acute red eye]] (CLARE)**: An acute inflammatory response, often associated with gram-negative bacterial colonization of the lens during overnight wear.
- **[[Contact lens peripheral ulcer]] (CLPU)**: A sterile, peripheral corneal infiltrate associated with bacterial toxins.
- **[[Superior epithelial arcuate lesion]] (SEAL)**: Arcuate corneal staining near the superior limbus, related to mechanical interaction between the lens edge and the superior lid.

## Pre-Fitting Clinical Assessment

A thorough pre-fitting evaluation is essential to determine candidacy, identify contraindications, and guide lens selection.

### Patient History

- **Refractive history**: Current [[spectacle prescription]], stability of refraction
- **Ocular history**: History of [[keratitis]], [[uveitis]], [[dry eye]], [[blepharitis]], [[corneal dystrophies]], prior [[refractive surgery]] (e.g., [[LASIK]], [[PRK]])
- **Medical history**: [[Diabetes mellitus]] (impairs corneal wound healing), [[autoimmune diseases]] (e.g., [[Sjögren's syndrome]], [[rheumatoid arthritis]]), immunosuppressive medications
- **Medications**: [[Antihistamines]], [[isotretinoin]], [[oral contraceptives]] (affect tear production)
- **Lifestyle and occupation**: Sports, screen time, dusty environments, cosmetic preferences
- **Motivation and expectations**

### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

A comprehensive [[slit-lamp examination]] of the [[anterior segment]] is mandatory:

| Structure | Assessment | Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| [[Eyelids]] | Lid position, tension, blink completeness, [[meibomian gland]] expression | Affects lens movement, comfort, and tear film stability |
| [[Conjunctiva]] | Injection, papillae, follicles, [[pinguecula]], [[pterygium]] | Identifies pre-existing inflammation or contraindications |
| [[Cornea]] | Clarity, scarring, [[neovascularization]], [[epithelial basement membrane dystrophy]], [[keratoconus]] signs | Determines lens type and material |
| [[Tear Film]] | TBUT, tear meniscus height, debris, foam | Assesses tear film adequacy for lens wear |
| [[Anterior Chamber]] | Depth, flare, cells | Rules out active inflammation |

### Corneal Topography and Keratometry

[[Keratometry]] measures the central corneal curvature (typically the central 3 mm zone) and provides the flat (K₁) and steep (K₂) meridian readings. This is the starting point for selecting the [[base curve]] (BC) of the lens.

[[Corneal topography]] (e.g., [[Placido disc]]-based or [[Scheimpflug imaging]]) provides a detailed map of the entire corneal surface, identifying:

- [[Regular astigmatism]] vs. [[irregular astigmatism]]
- [[Corneal eccentricity]]
- [[Keratoconus]] or [[pellucid marginal degeneration]]
- Post-surgical corneal irregularity

[[Anterior segment optical coherence tomography]] ([[AS-OCT]]) and [[scleral profilometry]] are increasingly used for specialty lens fitting, particularly for [[scleral lenses]].

### Pupillometry

[[Pupil]] size measurement under photopic and scotopic conditions is important for selecting the [[optical zone]] diameter, particularly in [[multifocal contact lenses]] and [[orthokeratology]] lenses.

### Refraction

A current [[manifest refraction]] and, when indicated, a [[cycloplegic refraction]] are performed. The [[vertex distance]] correction must be applied when converting spectacle prescriptions above ±4.00 D to contact lens power:

**F_CL = F_spectacle / (1 – d × F_spectacle)**

Where **d** is the vertex distance in meters (typically 0.012 m).

## Contact Lens Types and Materials

### Classification by Material

| Category | Material | Dk (Barrers) | Water Content | Key Properties |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Conventional [[Hydrogel]] | [[HEMA]]-based (Group I–IV) | 8–30 | 38–75% | Comfortable; limited O₂ transmissibility |
| [[Silicone Hydrogel]] | [[Lotrafilcon A]], [[Senofilcon A]], [[Comfilcon A]], [[Delefilcon A]] | 60–175 | 24–48% | High Dk; suitable for extended wear; may have higher modulus |
| [[Rigid Gas Permeable]] (RGP) | [[Fluorosilicone acrylate]], [[hexafocon A]] | 30–200+ | 0% | Excellent optics; durable; ideal for irregular corneas |
| [[Hybrid Lens]] | RGP center + soft skirt | Variable | Variable | Combines RGP optics with soft lens comfort |
| [[PMMA]] (historical) | [[Polymethyl methacrylate]] | 0 | 0% | Obsolete for daily wear; no oxygen permeability |

### Classification by Design

- **[[Spherical contact lenses]]**: Correct [[myopia]] and [[hyperopia]]
- **[[Toric contact lenses]]**: Correct [[astigmatism]]; incorporate prism ballast, peri-ballast, or accelerated stabilization design (ASD) for rotational stability
- **[[Multifocal contact lenses]]**: Correct [[presbyopia]]; designs include concentric ring, aspheric, and segmented translating
- **[[Monovision]]**: One eye corrected for distance, the other for near
- **[[Orthokeratology]] (Ortho-K) lenses**: Reverse-geometry RGP lenses worn overnight to temporarily reshape the cornea and reduce [[myopia]]
- **[[Scleral lenses]]**: Large-diameter RGP lenses (14.5–24 mm) that vault the entire cornea and rest on the [[sclera]]; indicated for [[keratoconus]], severe [[dry eye]], [[ocular surface disease]], and post-surgical irregularity
- **[[Cosmetic contact lenses]]**: Tinted or patterned lenses for cosmetic or prosthetic purposes

### Classification by Wear Schedule

| Schedule | Description | Typical Dk/t Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| Daily Wear (DW) | Removed nightly | ≥24 × 10⁻⁹ |
| Extended Wear (EW) | Worn continuously up to 6 nights | ≥87 × 10⁻⁹ |
| Continuous Wear (CW) | Worn continuously up to 30 nights | ≥87 × 10⁻⁹ (FDA-approved lenses only) |

### Classification by Replacement Schedule

- **Daily Disposable**: Single-use; lowest risk of complications; ideal for occasional wearers and allergy sufferers
- **Bi-weekly Disposable**: Replaced every 2 weeks
- **Monthly Disposable**: Replaced every 30 days
- **Conventional (Planned Replacement)**: Replaced every 3–12 months (less common today)

## The Fitting Process

### Soft Contact Lens Fitting

#### Initial Lens Selection

The initial trial lens is selected based on:

1. **Base Curve (BC)**: Typically 8.3–8.9 mm. Most manufacturers offer 1–2 base curves. A steeper BC (lower number) is chosen for steeper corneas (K > 44.00 D), and a flatter BC for flatter corneas (K < 42.00 D).
2. **Diameter**: Standard soft lens diameters range from 13.8–14.5 mm. The lens should extend 1–1.5 mm beyond the [[limbus]] in all directions.
3. **Power**: Based on the refracted prescription with vertex distance correction applied.
4. **Material**: Selected based on oxygen needs, wearing schedule, tear film quality, and deposit tendency.

#### Assessment of Fit

After allowing the lens to settle for 10–20 minutes, the following are evaluated:

| Parameter | Ideal Finding | Steep (Tight) Fit | Flat (Loose) Fit |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Centration** | Well-centered over cornea | Centered but immobile | Decentered, especially inferiorly |
| **Coverage** | Full corneal coverage with 1 mm overlap beyond limbus | Full coverage | Incomplete limbal coverage |
| **Movement on blink** | 0.25–0.50 mm vertical movement | <0.25 mm or no movement | >1.0 mm; excessive lag |
| **Push-up test** | Lens moves freely with gentle digital push, returns to center | Lens resists displacement; slow return | Lens moves excessively; does not return |
| **Patient comfort** | Comfortable after adaptation | Initial comfort may be good but worsens over time | Edge awareness, lens shifting |
| **Over-refraction** | Plano or minimal residual error | Possible minus over-refraction (lens vaulting) | Variable; unstable vision |

#### Fluorescein Assessment (Limited in Soft Lenses)

While [[sodium fluorescein]] is the gold standard for RGP fitting assessment, it is less useful with soft lenses due to absorption by hydrogel materials. High-molecular-weight fluorescein can be used with soft lenses without significant staining of the lens material.

### Rigid Gas Permeable (RGP) Lens Fitting

RGP fitting requires greater precision due to the rigid nature of the lens and its smaller diameter (typically 9.0–10.0 mm).

#### Initial Lens Selection

- **Base Curve**: Typically selected to align with the flattest keratometry reading ("on-K" fitting) or slightly steeper (0.50 D steeper than flat K for interpalpebral fitting).
- **Diameter**: 9.0–10.0 mm for corneal RGPs; larger for interlimbal designs.
- **Optical Zone Diameter (OZD)**: Typically 7.0–8.0 mm; must be larger than the pupil in mesopic conditions.
- **Edge Design**: Lenticular, CN bevel, or plus carrier for minus-powered lenses.

#### Fluorescein Pattern Assessment

This is the cornerstone of RGP fitting. After instilling [[sodium fluorescein]] and viewing with a [[cobalt blue filter]] and [[Wratten #12 yellow barrier filter]]:

| Fluorescein Pattern | Description | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **Alignment** | Thin, even layer of fluorescein across the cornea | Ideal fit |
| **Apical Clearance (Steep)** | Central pooling of fluorescein; dark (bearing) ring mid-peripherally | Lens is too steep; may cause central corneal edema |
| **Apical Bearing (Flat)** | Central dark area (touch); peripheral pooling | Lens is too flat; may cause central corneal staining and discomfort |
| **Edge Lift** | Adequate peripheral fluorescein band (0.5–1.0 mm) | Allows tear exchange; too much = lens instability; too little = seal-off |

#### Three-Point Touch (for Toric Corneas)

For corneas with >2.00 D of [[corneal astigmatism]], a spherical RGP may exhibit a "three-point touch" pattern: bearing at the apex and at two peripheral points along the flat meridian. A [[back-surface toric]] or [[bitoric]] RGP design may be required.

### Toric Soft Contact Lens Fitting

Toric soft lenses incorporate stabilization mechanisms to maintain the cylinder axis orientation:

- **Prism Ballast**: Thicker inferiorly; uses gravity
- **Peri-Ballast / Dual Thin Zones**: Thin zones at 3 and 9 o'clock; lid interaction stabilizes
- **Accelerated Stabilization Design (ASD)**: Four thin-thick zones for rapid stabilization

**LARS Rule** (Left Add, Right Subtract): If the lens rotates to the left (from the practitioner's perspective), add the degree of rotation to the prescribed axis. If it rotates to the right, subtract.

*Example*: Prescribed axis 180°, lens rotates 10° to the left → Order axis 190° (or equivalently, 10°).

### Multifocal Contact Lens Fitting

Multifocal lenses for [[presbyopia]] correction employ:

- **Center-near aspheric designs**: Central zone for near; peripheral for distance (most common in soft lenses)
- **Center-distance aspheric designs**: Central zone for distance; peripheral for near
- **Concentric ring designs**: Alternating distance and near zones
- **Segmented translating designs**: Primarily in RGP lenses; lower segment for near, upper for distance

Fitting considerations include:
- Dominant eye determination (typically fitted with distance-dominant lens)
- Add power selection based on age and near demand
- Pupil size (affects the balance of distance and near vision)
- Lighting conditions and patient expectations

### Scleral Lens Fitting

[[Scleral lenses]] are indicated for:
- [[Keratoconus]] and other [[corneal ectasias]]
- Severe [[ocular surface disease]] and [[dry eye]]
- Post-[[penetrating keratoplasty]] or post-[[refractive surgery]] irregularity
- [[Stevens-Johnson syndrome]], [[graft-versus-host disease]]

Fitting involves:
1. **Sagittal depth selection**: The lens must vault the entire cornea with 200–400 µm of clearance over the central cornea (assessed with [[AS-OCT]])
2. **Limbal clearance**: Adequate clearance over the [[limbus]] to avoid compression of [[limbal stem cells]]
3. **Landing zone alignment**: The haptic must align with the [[scleral]] contour; toric or quadrant-specific haptics may be needed for asymmetric scleral shapes
4. **Filling solution**: Lenses are filled with preservative-free [[saline]] (often with [[sodium fluorescein]] for initial assessment)
5. **Over-refraction**: Performed with the lens in situ

### Orthokeratology Fitting

[[Orthokeratology]] uses reverse-geometry RGP lenses worn overnight to temporarily flatten the central cornea, reducing [[myopia]] (typically up to –6.00 D) and mild [[astigmatism]]. It is also used for [[myopia control]] in children.

Key fitting parameters:
- **Base curve (treatment zone)**: Flatter than flat K by a calculated amount based on target myopia reduction
- **Reverse curve**: Steeper than BC to create the tear reservoir that drives corneal reshaping
- **Alignment curve**: Matches the mid-peripheral cornea
- **Peripheral curve**: Provides edge lift for tear exchange

Topographic assessment after overnight wear should show a well-centered "bull's-eye" pattern (central flattening with a mid-peripheral steepening ring).

## Post-Fitting Evaluation and Follow-Up

### Initial Follow-Up (1–2 Weeks)

- Assess visual acuity (distance and near)
- Over-refraction
- Slit-lamp evaluation: corneal staining with [[fluorescein]], conjunctival injection, lens deposits
- Lens fit assessment (centration, movement, rotation for torics)
- Patient-reported comfort and wearing time
- Review insertion, removal, and care regimen

### Routine Follow-Up (Every 6–12 Months)

- Comprehensive anterior segment examination
- [[Corneal topography]] to monitor for warpage
- Tear film assessment
- Lens condition evaluation
- Update prescription as needed
- Reinforce compliance with care regimen and replacement schedule

### Signs of Complications Requiring Intervention

| Complication | Clinical Signs | Management |
|---|---|---|
| [[Corneal neovascularization]] | Limbal vessel ingrowth >1 mm | Refit with higher Dk material; reduce wearing time |
| [[Microbial keratitis]] | Corneal infiltrate with overlying epithelial defect, pain, photophobia | Discontinue lens wear; corneal cultures; intensive topical [[fluoroquinolone]] therapy |
| [[Giant papillary conjunctivitis]] | Papillae ≥1 mm on upper tarsal conjunctiva, mucous discharge, itching | Discontinue or change lens type; topical [[mast cell stabilizers]]; daily disposables |
| [[Corneal edema]] | Stromal haze, Descemet's folds, microcysts | Refit with higher Dk/t lens; reduce wearing time |
| [[Contact lens-related dry eye]] | Reduced TBUT, corneal staining, discomfort | Rewetting drops; change to daily disposable; lower water content or silicone hydrogel material |
| [[CLARE]] | Acute red eye upon waking, minimal infiltrates | Discontinue overnight wear; topical [[NSAID]] or mild [[corticosteroid]] |
| [[Corneal warpage]] | Irregular topography, unstable refraction | Discontinue lens wear until topography normalizes |
| [[Superior epithelial arcuate lesion]] (SEAL) | Arcuate staining near superior limbus | Refit with lower modulus lens or different edge design |

## Special Considerations

### Pediatric Contact Lens Fitting

Contact lenses in children are indicated for:
- **[[Aphakia]]** following congenital [[cataract]] surgery (silicone elastomer or silicone hydrogel lenses)
- **[[Anisometropia]]** correction
- **[[Myopia control]]** (orthokeratology, soft multifocal lenses, or dual-focus lenses such as [[MiSight]])
- **Cosmetic/prosthetic** purposes

### Contact Lens Fitting After Refractive Surgery

Post-[[LASIK]], post-[[PRK]], and post-[[radial keratotomy]] corneas present unique challenges:
- Altered corneal shape (oblate post-myopic LASIK)
- Irregular astigmatism
- Reduced corneal sensitivity
- RGP, hybrid, or scleral lenses are often required

### Fitting in Keratoconus

[[Keratoconus]] management with contact lenses progresses through a hierarchy:
1. Spectacles (mild cases)
2. Soft toric or keratoconus-specific soft lenses (mild-moderate)
3. Corneal RGP lenses (moderate)
4. Piggyback systems (RGP over soft lens for comfort)
5. Hybrid lenses (e.g., [[SynergEyes]])
6. Scleral or mini-scleral lenses (moderate-advanced)
7. [[Corneal transplantation]] (when contact lenses fail)

### Therapeutic Contact Lenses

[[Bandage contact lenses]] (typically high-Dk silicone hydrogel, plano power) are used therapeutically for:
- [[Corneal erosion]] protection
- Post-surgical pain management (e.g., after [[photorefractive keratectomy]])
- [[Bullous keratopathy]] symptom relief
- Drug delivery enhancement
- Protection of healing [[corneal epithelium]]

## Patient Education and Compliance

Patient education is a critical component of contact lens fitting. Key topics include:

- **Hygiene**: Hand washing before lens handling; no water exposure (tap water, swimming, showering) to prevent [[Acanthamoeba keratitis]]
- **Care system**: Proper use of [[multipurpose solution]] or [[hydrogen peroxide]] disinfection systems; no topping off solution
- **Wearing schedule**: Adherence to prescribed daily wearing time; avoidance of sleeping in lenses unless specifically approved
- **Replacement schedule**: Strict adherence to replacement intervals
- **Warning signs**: Pain, redness, light sensitivity, blurred vision, tearing — "When in doubt, take them out" and seek immediate care
- **Follow-up compliance**: Regular examinations even in the absence of symptoms

## Emerging Technologies and Future Directions

- **[[Wavefront-guided contact lenses]]**: Custom lenses correcting higher-order [[aberrations]]
- **[[Smart contact lenses]]**: Embedded sensors for [[intraocular pressure]] monitoring (e.g., [[Sensimed Triggerfish]]) or glucose monitoring
- **[[Drug-eluting contact lenses]]**: Sustained release of medications (e.g., [[antihistamines]], [[antibiotics]], [[glaucoma]] medications)
- **[[3D-printed contact lenses]]**: Customized lens fabrication
- **[[Myopia control]] lenses**: Dual-focus and extended depth-of-focus designs (e.g., [[MiSight 1 day]], [[NaturalVue]])
- **Advanced scleral lens fitting**: Integration of [[scleral profilometry]] and [[impression-based]] fitting for highly customized scleral lenses
- **[[Biocompatible]] surface treatments**: Plasma surface modification and biomimetic coatings to improve wettability and reduce deposit formation

---

## Summary

Contact lens fitting is a multifaceted clinical skill requiring comprehensive knowledge of ocular anatomy, corneal physiology, lens materials, and optical principles. A systematic approach—encompassing thorough patient assessment, appropriate lens selection, meticulous fit evaluation, and diligent follow-up—is essential to maximize visual outcomes, maintain ocular health, and ensure long-term patient satisfaction. The field continues to evolve with advances in materials science, diagnostic imaging, and digital health technologies, expanding the scope of conditions amenable to contact lens correction and improving safety profiles for millions of wearers worldwide.
