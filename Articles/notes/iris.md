# Iris

The **iris** (plural: *irides*) is the thin, pigmented, contractile, annular structure of the [[Anterior Segment]] of the [[Eye]] that is responsible for controlling the diameter and size of the [[Pupil]], thereby regulating the amount of light reaching the [[Retina]]. Derived from the Greek word *ἶρις* (iris, "rainbow"), the iris is one of the most clinically significant structures in [[Optometry]] and [[Ophthalmology]], serving as a critical anatomical landmark, a site of numerous primary and secondary pathologies, and a key structure in both diagnostic and therapeutic ophthalmic procedures. The iris is the most anterior portion of the [[Uveal Tract]] (also known as the [[Uvea]]), which also includes the [[Ciliary Body]] and the [[Choroid]].

---

## Embryology and Development

The iris is a structure of dual embryological origin, a feature that underlies many of its unique clinical characteristics.

- **Neuroectodermal origin:** The posterior [[Iris Pigment Epithelium]] (IPE), a bilayered pigmented epithelium, and the [[Dilator Pupillae]] and [[Sphincter Pupillae]] muscles are derived from the [[Optic Cup]], which itself originates from the [[Neuroectoderm]]. This makes the iris sphincter and dilator muscles unique in the body, as they are smooth muscles of neuroectodermal rather than mesodermal origin.
- **Neural crest (mesenchymal) origin:** The [[Iris Stroma]], including its [[Melanocytes]], [[Fibroblasts]], collagen framework, and vasculature, is derived from [[Neural Crest Cells]] that migrate into the anterior segment during embryonic development.

Development of the iris begins during the third month of gestation and continues postnatally. The iris stroma is among the last anterior segment structures to fully differentiate. Notably, iris pigmentation is often incomplete at birth, which is why many neonates—particularly those of lighter-skinned ethnicities—are born with blue or gray irides that darken over the first 6–12 months of life as stromal [[Melanocytes]] produce and accumulate [[Melanin]].

Developmental anomalies of the iris include [[Aniridia]] (congenital absence or hypoplasia of the iris), [[Coloboma]] (a sector defect resulting from incomplete closure of the [[Embryonic Fissure]]), [[Persistent Pupillary Membrane]], and [[Heterochromia Iridis]].

## Gross and Microscopic Anatomy

### Gross Anatomy

The iris is a thin, disc-shaped diaphragm approximately 12 mm in diameter and 0.3–0.6 mm in thickness, with its thinnest region at the [[Iris Root]] (the peripheral attachment to the [[Ciliary Body]]) and its thickest region at the [[Collarette]] (the junction between the pupillary and ciliary zones). The central aperture of the iris is the [[Pupil]], which typically measures 2–8 mm in diameter depending on ambient illumination and autonomic tone.

The anterior surface of the iris is divided into two zones by the collarette:

| **Zone** | **Location** | **Features** |
|---|---|---|
| **Pupillary zone** | From the [[Pupillary Margin]] to the collarette | Smoother surface; contains the [[Sphincter Pupillae]] muscle; the [[Pupillary Ruff]] (pigmented frill) is visible at the pupillary margin |
| **Ciliary zone** | From the collarette to the [[Iris Root]] | Broader zone; contains radial [[Iris Crypts]] (of Fuchs), contraction furrows, and structural folds |

The posterior surface of the iris is uniformly covered by the heavily pigmented [[Iris Pigment Epithelium]], which is responsible for making the iris opaque to light (except through the pupil) regardless of iris color.

### Microscopic Anatomy (Histological Layers)

From anterior to posterior, the iris consists of the following layers:

| **Layer** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Anterior border layer** | A condensation of fibroblasts and melanocytes; NOT a true epithelium (lacks a basement membrane and tight junctions); discontinuous, forming the crypts of Fuchs |
| **Iris stroma** | Loose [[Connective Tissue]] containing melanocytes, fibroblasts, collagen fibrils, [[Mast Cells]], macrophages (clump cells of Koganei), and the vasculature; the [[Sphincter Pupillae]] muscle is embedded in the posterior stroma near the pupillary margin |
| **Iris dilator muscle (myoepithelium)** | The [[Dilator Pupillae]] muscle, a thin sheet of myoepithelial cells derived from the anterior layer of the iris pigment epithelium; extends from the iris root to the mid-iris region |
| **Posterior pigment epithelium** | A bilayered epithelium: the anterior pigmented epithelial layer (from which the dilator muscle derives) and the posterior pigmented epithelial layer, both heavily laden with melanin granules |

### Iris Color

Iris color is determined primarily by the density and distribution of [[Melanin]] within the stromal melanocytes, not by the pigment epithelium (which is uniformly darkly pigmented in all individuals except in [[Albinism]]).

| **Iris Color** | **Stromal Melanin Content** |
|---|---|
| Blue/Gray | Very low; light scattering ([[Tyndall Effect]] / [[Rayleigh Scattering]]) produces blue appearance |
| Green/Hazel | Moderate melanin; combination of scattering and pigment absorption |
| Brown/Dark brown | High melanin density; absorbs most incident light |

[[Heterochromia]] refers to a difference in iris color between the two eyes (heterochromia iridis) or within a single iris (heterochromia iridum/sectoral heterochromia) and may be congenital or acquired, with acquired forms warranting clinical investigation for conditions such as [[Fuchs Heterochromic Iridocyclitis]], [[Horner Syndrome]], or iris [[Melanoma]].

## Vascular Supply and Innervation

### Vascular Supply

The iris receives its blood supply from the [[Long Posterior Ciliary Arteries]] (two branches of the [[Ophthalmic Artery]]) and the [[Anterior Ciliary Arteries]] (derived from the muscular branches supplying the [[Extraocular Muscles]]). These vessels anastomose at the iris root to form the **[[Major Arterial Circle of the Iris]]** (circulus arteriosus iridis major), located in the ciliary body near the iris root. From this circle, radial arteries course centripetally through the iris stroma toward the pupil, where they may form an incomplete **[[Minor Arterial Circle of the Iris]]** (circulus arteriosus iridis minor) near the collarette.

The iris vasculature is unique in that the vessels are **non-fenestrated** and possess a robust [[Blood-Aqueous Barrier]] (BAB), maintained by tight junctions between the endothelial cells of iris capillaries and the non-pigmented epithelium of the [[Ciliary Body]]. Disruption of this barrier is a hallmark of [[Anterior Uveitis]] (iritis) and results in the clinical finding of [[Flare]] in the [[Anterior Chamber]].

Venous drainage of the iris occurs via the [[Vortex Veins]] through the ciliary body and choroid.

### Innervation

The iris receives both [[Sympathetic]] and [[Parasympathetic]] innervation, which governs pupillary function:

| **Muscle** | **Innervation** | **Action** | **Neurotransmitter** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Sphincter Pupillae]] | [[Parasympathetic]] via [[Oculomotor Nerve]] (CN III) → [[Edinger-Westphal Nucleus]] → [[Ciliary Ganglion]] → [[Short Ciliary Nerves]] | [[Miosis]] (pupil constriction) | [[Acetylcholine]] (muscarinic M3 receptors) |
| [[Dilator Pupillae]] | [[Sympathetic]] via hypothalamus → [[Ciliospinal Center of Budge]] (C8-T2) → superior cervical ganglion → [[Long Ciliary Nerves]] | [[Mydriasis]] (pupil dilation) | [[Norepinephrine]] (alpha-1 adrenergic receptors) |

Sensory innervation of the iris is provided by the [[Nasociliary Nerve]], a branch of the [[Ophthalmic Division]] (V1) of the [[Trigeminal Nerve]] (CN V), via the long and short ciliary nerves. This sensory innervation is clinically relevant in conditions such as [[Anterior Uveitis]], where iris inflammation causes significant [[Photophobia]] and deep, aching ocular pain.

## Physiology and Function

### Pupillary Light Reflex

The primary physiological function of the iris is to regulate the amount of light entering the eye through the [[Pupillary Light Reflex]]. When light is directed into one eye:

1. [[Photoreceptors]] (primarily [[Intrinsically Photosensitive Retinal Ganglion Cells]] (ipRGCs) containing [[Melanopsin]]) detect the stimulus.
2. Afferent signals travel via the [[Optic Nerve]] (CN II) to the [[Pretectal Nucleus]] in the [[Midbrain]].
3. Bilateral projections from the pretectal nucleus reach both [[Edinger-Westphal Nuclei]].
4. Efferent parasympathetic fibers travel via CN III to the [[Ciliary Ganglion]] and then via [[Short Ciliary Nerves]] to the [[Sphincter Pupillae]], causing bilateral miosis.

This reflex produces both a **direct response** (constriction of the ipsilateral pupil) and a **consensual response** (constriction of the contralateral pupil). Assessment of the pupillary light reflex is fundamental in clinical optometric and neurological examination, and abnormalities such as a [[Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect]] (RAPD or Marcus Gunn pupil) indicate asymmetric optic nerve or retinal disease.

### Near Response

The iris also participates in the [[Near Triad]] (also called the accommodation reflex), which consists of:
- [[Accommodation]] (increased [[Crystalline Lens]] curvature via [[Ciliary Muscle]] contraction)
- [[Convergence]] (medial rectus contraction)
- [[Miosis]] (sphincter pupillae contraction to increase depth of focus)

### Blood-Aqueous Barrier

The iris vasculature, along with the non-pigmented epithelium of the ciliary body, maintains the [[Blood-Aqueous Barrier]], which restricts the passage of proteins and cells from the blood into the [[Aqueous Humor]]. Breakdown of this barrier is a central pathophysiological event in [[Anterior Uveitis]] and can be quantified clinically using [[Laser Flare Photometry]] or graded on [[Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy]].

## Pathophysiology of Iris Diseases

The iris is subject to a wide spectrum of pathological processes, including inflammatory, neoplastic, degenerative, traumatic, iatrogenic, and congenital conditions. The following sections detail the major categories of iris pathology.

### Inflammatory Conditions

#### Anterior Uveitis (Iritis and Iridocyclitis)

[[Anterior Uveitis]] is the most common form of [[Uveitis]] and represents inflammation primarily involving the iris (**iritis**) or the iris and anterior [[Ciliary Body]] (**iridocyclitis**). It is the most frequently encountered iris pathology in clinical practice.

**Pathophysiology:** Anterior uveitis involves disruption of the [[Blood-Aqueous Barrier]], leading to leakage of proteins (flare) and inflammatory cells into the [[Anterior Chamber]]. The inflammatory cascade involves activation of [[T-Lymphocytes]], release of [[Cytokines]] (including [[TNF-alpha]], [[IL-6]], [[IL-1]]), and recruitment of inflammatory cells. The condition may be:

- **Infectious:** Associated with [[Herpes Simplex Virus]] (HSV), [[Herpes Zoster Virus]] (HZV/VZV), [[Toxoplasmosis]], [[Syphilis]], [[Tuberculosis]], [[Lyme Disease]]
- **Non-infectious/Autoimmune:** Associated with [[HLA-B27]]-related spondyloarthropathies (e.g., [[Ankylosing Spondylitis]], [[Reactive Arthritis]]), [[Sarcoidosis]], [[Behçet Disease]], [[Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis]] (JIA), [[Inflammatory Bowel Disease]]
- **Idiopathic:** No identifiable cause (approximately 50% of cases)

**Symptoms:**
- Ocular pain (deep, aching)
- [[Photophobia]] (often severe; consensual photophobia is pathognomonic)
- Blurred vision
- Tearing ([[Lacrimation]])
- [[Ciliary Flush]] (perilimbal injection)

**Clinical Signs:**

| **Sign** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| [[Cells]] in anterior chamber | White blood cells visible on slit-lamp; graded 0 to 4+ (SUN criteria) |
| [[Flare]] in anterior chamber | Protein in aqueous; Tyndall effect on slit-lamp beam; graded 0 to 4+ |
| [[Keratic Precipitates]] (KPs) | Inflammatory cell deposits on [[Corneal Endothelium]]; fine (non-granulomatous) or mutton-fat (granulomatous) |
| [[Posterior Synechiae]] | Adhesions between the iris and [[Anterior Lens Capsule]]; may cause irregular pupil |
| [[Fibrin]] in anterior chamber | Severe inflammation; may form a [[Fibrin Membrane]] |
| [[Hypopyon]] | Layered white blood cells in inferior anterior chamber |
| [[Iris Nodules]] | [[Koeppe Nodules]] (pupillary margin; granulomatous) and [[Busacca Nodules]] (iris stroma; granulomatous) |
| [[Iris Bombé]] | Forward bowing of the iris due to 360° posterior synechiae blocking aqueous flow; may cause secondary [[Angle-Closure Glaucoma]] |

**Diagnosis:**
- [[Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy]] is the primary diagnostic tool
- [[Intraocular Pressure]] (IOP) measurement (may be low, normal, or elevated)
- Laboratory workup guided by clinical presentation: [[HLA-B27]], [[ACE Level]], [[Chest X-Ray]], [[RPR]]/[[FTA-ABS]], [[QuantiFERON-TB Gold]], [[Lyme Titers]]
- [[Anterior Chamber Paracentesis]] for PCR or culture in suspected infectious cases

**Treatment:**
- **Topical [[Corticosteroids]]:** [[Prednisolone Acetate]] 1% is the gold standard; frequency titrated to severity (e.g., every 1–2 hours in severe cases, tapered gradually)
- **[[Cycloplegic]] agents:** [[Cyclopentolate]] 1%, [[Homatropine]] 2–5%, or [[Atropine]] 1% to relieve ciliary spasm (pain), prevent/break posterior synechiae, and stabilize the blood-aqueous barrier
- **Systemic therapy:** [[Oral Corticosteroids]], [[Methotrexate]], [[Azathioprine]], [[Mycophenolate Mofetil]], or [[Biologic Agents]] (e.g., [[Adalimumab]]) for chronic, recurrent, or bilateral disease
- **Treatment of underlying etiology** when identified

#### Fuchs Heterochromic Iridocyclitis (FHI)

[[Fuchs Heterochromic Iridocyclitis]] is a chronic, low-grade anterior uveitis characterized by:
- [[Heterochromia]] (the affected eye is typically lighter due to iris stromal atrophy)
- Diffuse, fine, stellate [[Keratic Precipitates]] scattered across the entire corneal endothelium
- Mild anterior chamber cells with minimal or no flare
- Absence of [[Posterior Synechiae]] (a distinguishing feature)
- Frequent development of [[Posterior Subcapsular Cataract]] and secondary [[Open-Angle Glaucoma]]
- Possible association with [[Rubella Virus]]

FHI typically does not respond well to topical corticosteroids, and treatment is generally directed at managing complications (cataract surgery, IOP-lowering therapy).

### Neoplastic Conditions

#### Iris Melanoma

[[Iris Melanoma]] is the most common primary malignant tumor of the iris, though it accounts for only 3–5% of all [[Uveal Melanoma]]. It arises from melanocytes within the iris stroma.

**Clinical Features:**
- Typically presents as a variably pigmented (brown, tan, or amelanotic) elevated mass in the iris stroma
- Most commonly located in the inferior iris
- May cause [[Corectopia]] (pupil displacement), [[Ectropion Uveae]], localized [[Cataract]], [[Hyphema]], or secondary [[Glaucoma]]
- Sentinel vessels may be present on the iris surface
- Iris melanomas generally have a better prognosis than [[Ciliary Body Melanoma]] or [[Choroidal Melanoma]] due to earlier detection and lower metastatic potential

**Diagnosis:**
- [[Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy]] with photographic documentation
- [[Gonioscopy]] to assess angle involvement
- [[Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography]] (AS-OCT)
- [[Ultrasound Biomicroscopy]] (UBM) to evaluate posterior extent and ciliary body involvement
- [[Iris Fluorescein Angiography]]
- [[Fine-Needle Aspiration Biopsy]] (FNAB) for cytology and genetic analysis (e.g., [[Gene Expression Profiling]], [[Chromosome 3]] status)

**Treatment:**
- Observation with serial photography for small, stable lesions
- [[Sector Iridectomy]] or [[Iridocyclectomy]] for localized tumors
- [[Plaque Brachytherapy]] (e.g., [[Iodine-125]] or [[Ruthenium-106]])
- [[Proton Beam Radiotherapy]]
- [[Enucleation]] in advanced cases with extensive involvement

#### Iris Nevus

An [[Iris Nevus]] is a benign melanocytic lesion of the iris stroma, present in approximately 4–6% of the general population. Nevi are typically flat or minimally elevated, well-circumscribed, and pigmented. Differentiation from early melanoma is critical and relies on documented growth, vascularity, ectropion uveae, seeding, and secondary effects (the mnemonic for suspicious features is sometimes adapted from the SHIELD criteria).

#### Iris Cysts

[[Iris Cysts]] may be:
- **Primary (epithelial):** Arising from the iris pigment epithelium; located at the pupillary margin or in the iridociliary sulcus; usually benign and asymptomatic
- **Primary (stromal):** Rare; may enlarge and require intervention
- **Secondary:** Due to trauma, surgery, or topical [[Miotic]] use (e.g., [[Pilocarpine]]); may also result from implantation of epithelial cells following penetrating injury

Diagnosis is aided by [[Ultrasound Biomicroscopy]] (UBM) and [[AS-OCT]], which can differentiate cystic from solid lesions.

### Traumatic Conditions

#### Traumatic Iridodialysis

[[Iridodialysis]] is a disinsertion (tearing) of the iris from its root at the [[Scleral Spur]] / ciliary body attachment, typically resulting from blunt [[Ocular Trauma]]. It presents as a D-shaped pupil with a visible dark crescent at the periphery. Patients may experience [[Monocular Diplopia]], [[Glare]], and [[Photophobia]]. Small iridodialyses may be observed; larger, symptomatic defects may require surgical repair (iridodialysis repair with suture fixation).

#### Traumatic Mydriasis and Sphincter Tears

Blunt trauma can cause damage to the [[Sphincter Pupillae]], resulting in [[Traumatic Mydriasis]] (a dilated, poorly reactive pupil) or visible iris sphincter tears (irregular pupil margin with radial notches). These may be permanent.

#### Hyphema

[[Hyphema]] (blood in the anterior chamber) frequently involves iris vascular disruption. It is graded by the percentage of anterior chamber filled with blood (Grade I: <33%, Grade II: 33–50%, Grade III: 50–100%, Grade IV: total/8-ball hyphema). Complications include [[Corneal Blood Staining]], secondary [[Glaucoma]], and [[Rebleeding]]. Management includes topical [[Corticosteroids]], [[Cycloplegics]], activity restriction, head elevation, and IOP monitoring. [[Aminocaproic Acid]] may be used to reduce rebleeding risk.

### Degenerative and Age-Related Conditions

#### Iris Atrophy

[[Iris Atrophy]] may be:
- **Age-related (senile):** Thinning and depigmentation of the iris stroma, particularly common in light-colored irides
- **Essential iris atrophy:** A variant of [[Iridocorneal Endothelial Syndrome]] (ICE syndrome), characterized by progressive iris atrophy, [[Corectopia]], [[Polycoria]] (hole formation), [[Peripheral Anterior Synechiae]] (PAS), and secondary glaucoma
- **Post-inflammatory:** Following severe or recurrent [[Anterior Uveitis]]
- **Post-infectious:** Notably following [[Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus]] or [[Herpes Simplex]] keratouveitis, which can cause sectoral iris atrophy

#### Pigment Dispersion Syndrome and Pigmentary Glaucoma

[[Pigment Dispersion Syndrome]] (PDS) involves mechanical rubbing of the posterior iris surface against the [[Zonular Fibers]] of the [[Crystalline Lens]], leading to liberation of pigment granules from the [[Iris Pigment Epithelium]]. These pigment granules deposit on anterior segment structures, including the [[Corneal Endothelium]] ([[Krukenberg Spindle]]), [[Trabecular Meshwork]], and lens surface. Characteristic findings include mid-peripheral iris transillumination defects (spoke-like pattern) and a concave iris configuration on [[AS-OCT]] or [[UBM]]. When pigment deposition in the trabecular meshwork causes elevated [[Intraocular Pressure]] and [[Glaucomatous Optic Neuropathy]], the condition is termed [[Pigmentary Glaucoma]].

#### Pseudoexfoliation Syndrome

[[Pseudoexfoliation Syndrome]] (PXF) involves the deposition of fibrillar [[Pseudoexfoliative Material]] on the anterior lens capsule, pupillary margin, iris surface, [[Zonules]], and trabecular meshwork. Iris findings include poor pupillary dilation, peripupillary iris transillumination defects, and pigment loss from the pupillary ruff. PXF is a major risk factor for [[Pseudoexfoliation Glaucoma]] and complications during [[Cataract Surgery]] (e.g., [[Zonular Weakness]], [[Intraoperative Floppy Iris Syndrome]]-like behavior).

### Congenital and Developmental Anomalies

| **Condition** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| [[Aniridia]] | Congenital absence or severe hypoplasia of the iris; associated with [[PAX6]] gene mutations; may be associated with [[Wilms Tumor]] (WAGR syndrome), [[Glaucoma]], [[Corneal Pannus]], [[Cataract]], and [[Foveal Hypoplasia]] |
| [[Iris Coloboma]] | Keyhole-shaped defect, typically inferonasally, due to incomplete closure of the [[Embryonic Fissure]]; may be isolated or part of a colobomatous spectrum involving the ciliary body, choroid, retina, and optic nerve |
| [[Persistent Pupillary Membrane]] | Remnants of the fetal pupillary membrane (tunica vasculosa lentis); usually benign; may rarely be dense enough to obstruct the visual axis |
| [[Corectopia]] | Displacement of the pupil from its normal central position; may be congenital or acquired |
| [[Polycoria]] | Presence of more than one pupillary opening; true polycoria (each opening has its own sphincter) is extremely rare |
| [[Albinism]] | Deficiency of melanin in the iris pigment epithelium and stroma; results in iris transillumination, photophobia, and reduced visual acuity due to foveal hypoplasia |

## Diagnostic Modalities for Iris Evaluation

| **Modality** | **Application** |
|---|---|
| [[Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy]] | Gold standard for iris examination; allows assessment of surface architecture, crypts, nodules, masses, synechiae, transillumination, and anterior chamber reaction |
| [[Gonioscopy]] | Visualization of the [[Iridocorneal Angle]] (angle structures including iris root, [[Ciliary Body Band]], [[Scleral Spur]], [[Trabecular Meshwork]], [[Schwalbe Line]]); essential for angle-closure assessment and iris/angle tumors |
| [[Iris Transillumination]] | Retroillumination through the pupil to detect areas of iris pigment epithelium loss (e.g., PDS, PXF, albinism, post-herpetic atrophy) |
| [[Anterior Segment OCT]] (AS-OCT) | High-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the iris; useful for measuring iris thickness, detecting cysts vs. solid masses, assessing iris configuration (plateau, bowing), and documenting angle anatomy |
| [[Ultrasound Biomicroscopy]] (UBM) | High-frequency (35–50 MHz) ultrasound providing cross-sectional imaging of the iris, ciliary body, and posterior chamber; superior to AS-OCT for imaging structures behind the iris (e.g., ciliary body tumors, iridociliary cysts, plateau iris configuration) |
| [[Iris Fluorescein Angiography]] (IFA) | Intravenous [[Fluorescein]] angiography focused on the iris vasculature; useful for evaluating iris tumors (neovascularization, leakage), [[Rubeosis Iridis]], and vascular anomalies |
| [[Anterior Segment Photography]] | Serial documentation of iris lesions (nevi, melanomas) for monitoring growth |
| [[Pupillometry]] | Quantitative measurement of pupil size and dynamics; useful in evaluating [[Anisocoria]], [[RAPD]], and pharmacological pupil testing |
| [[Pharmacological Pupil Testing]] | Use of topical agents (e.g., [[Cocaine]] 4–10%, [[Hydroxyamphetamine]] 1%, [[Pilocarpine]] 0.1%) to localize the lesion in [[Horner Syndrome]] or confirm [[Adie Tonic Pupil]] |

## Pharmacology Related to the Iris

The iris is a primary target of numerous ophthalmic pharmacological agents:

### Mydriatics and Cycloplegics

| **Agent** | **Mechanism** | **Onset** | **Duration** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Tropicamide]] 0.5–1% | Parasympatholytic (muscarinic antagonist) | 20–30 min | 4–6 hours |
| [[Cyclopentolate]] 0.5–2% | Parasympatholytic | 30–60 min | 12–24 hours |
| [[Homatropine]] 2–5% | Parasympatholytic | 30–60 min | 1–3 days |
| [[Atropine]] 0.5–1% | Parasympatholytic | 30–40 min | 7–14 days |
| [[Phenylephrine]] 2.5–10% | Sympathomimetic (alpha-1 agonist) | 15–30 min | 3–5 hours |

### Miotics

| **Agent** | **Mechanism** | **Clinical Use** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Pilocarpine]] 1–4% | Parasympathomimetic (direct muscarinic agonist) | [[Angle-Closure Glaucoma]] (pulls iris away from angle); also used in [[Presbyopia]] management (e.g., [[Vuity]]) |

### Intraoperative Floppy Iris Syndrome (IFIS)

[[Intraoperative Floppy Iris Syndrome]] is a triad of iris billowing, iris prolapse through surgical incisions, and progressive intraoperative miosis occurring during [[Cataract Surgery]]. It is strongly associated with current or prior use of systemic [[Alpha-1 Adrenergic Antagonists]], most notably [[Tamsulosin]] (Flomax), used for [[Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia]]. The mechanism involves atrophy of the [[Dilator Pupillae]] muscle. Management strategies include preoperative identification of at-risk patients, use of intracameral [[Phenylephrine]], [[Iris Hooks]], [[Pupil Expansion Rings]] (e.g., [[Malyugin Ring]]), and low-flow fluidic settings.

## Iris in Systemic Disease

The iris may manifest signs of systemic disease:

| **Systemic Condition** | **Iris Manifestation** |
|---|---|
| [[Diabetes Mellitus]] | [[Rubeosis Iridis]] (neovascularization of the iris) due to retinal ischemia; may lead to [[Neovascular Glaucoma]] |
| [[Neurofibromatosis Type 1]] (NF1) | [[Lisch Nodules]] (melanocytic iris hamartomas); present in >90% of adults with NF1; pathognomonic |
| [[Sarcoidosis]] | Granulomatous anterior uveitis with mutton-fat KPs, [[Busacca Nodules]], [[Koeppe Nodules]], and tent-shaped [[Posterior Synechiae]] |
| [[Ankylosing Spondylitis]] | Acute, recurrent, unilateral, non-granulomatous [[Anterior Uveitis]]; HLA-B27 positive |
| [[Wilson Disease]] | [[Kayser-Fleischer Ring]] (copper deposition in [[Descemet Membrane]], not the iris per se, but visible at the iris-cornea junction) |
| [[Down Syndrome]] | [[Brushfield Spots]] (small, white-yellow spots arranged circumferentially on the anterior iris surface; represent focal areas of stromal hyperplasia) |
| [[Horner Syndrome]] | [[Miosis]], [[Ptosis]], and [[Anhidrosis]]; iris heterochromia if congenital (affected side is lighter due to impaired melanocyte stimulation) |
| [[Marfan Syndrome]] | [[Iridodonesis]] (iris tremulousness) due to [[Zonular Weakness]] and [[Lens Subluxation]] |

|---|---|
| [[Laser Peripheral Iridotomy]] (LPI) | [[Primary Angle-Closure]] / [[Angle-Closure Glaucoma]] (creates a full-thickness hole in the peripheral iris to equalize pressure between the posterior and anterior chambers, relieving [[Pupillary Block]]) |

## Prognosis and Clinical Significance

The iris is a structure of immense clinical importance in optometric and ophthalmic practice. Its accessibility to direct visualization via [[Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy]] makes it one of the most readily examined internal structures of the body. Iris pathology ranges from benign and incidental (e.g., iris nevi, Brushfield spots) to sight-threatening (e.g., severe anterior uveitis with secondary glaucoma, iris melanoma) and life-threatening (e.g., iris melanoma with metastatic potential, aniridia associated with Wilms tumor in children). Comprehensive iris examination—including assessment of color, architecture, pupillary function, transillumination, and gonioscopic evaluation of the iridocorneal angle—is an essential component of every complete eye examination.
