

# Pseudoexfoliation Syndrome

---

**Pseudoexfoliation syndrome** (PEX, PXF, or **exfoliation syndrome**) is a systemic, age-related disorder of the [[extracellular matrix]] characterized by the progressive accumulation and deposition of abnormal fibrillar material on the [[anterior segment]] structures of the eye and in various visceral organs. First described by Finnish ophthalmologist John G. Lindberg in 1917, PEX is now recognized as the most common identifiable cause of [[open-angle glaucoma]] worldwide and a significant risk factor for complications during [[cataract surgery]]. The condition is of paramount clinical importance to optometrists and ophthalmologists alike, as its ocular manifestations can lead to severe, sight-threatening sequelae if not identified and managed in a timely fashion.

The term "pseudoexfoliation" was coined to distinguish this entity from **true exfoliation** (or **capsular delamination**), a condition historically associated with occupational exposure to infrared radiation (e.g., glassblower's cataract), in which the superficial [[lens capsule]] peels away in lamellar sheets. In pseudoexfoliation syndrome, the deposited material is a distinct, pathological fibrillar protein complex produced by abnormal cellular metabolism.

---

## Epidemiology

Pseudoexfoliation syndrome demonstrates a striking geographic and ethnic variation in prevalence. It is most commonly reported in Scandinavian countries, particularly Finland, Iceland, and Sweden, where prevalence rates in populations over 60 years of age can exceed 20–25%. High prevalence has also been documented in Greece, Saudi Arabia, the Indian subcontinent, Ethiopia, and among indigenous Australian populations. Conversely, PEX appears to be less common in populations of East Asian and African American descent, although it is by no means absent.

| **Population / Region** | **Estimated Prevalence (Age >60)** |
|---|---|
| Scandinavia (Finland, Iceland) | 20–25% |
| Greece | 11–20% |
| India | 5–15% |
| Saudi Arabia | 10–18% |
| United States (general) | 1–5% |
| Japan | 3–5% |
| Sub-Saharan Africa (variable) | 2–19% |

Key epidemiological features include:

- **Age**: PEX is overwhelmingly a disease of aging. It is rarely diagnosed before the age of 50, and its prevalence increases markedly with each decade of life. The mean age at diagnosis is typically in the seventh decade.
- **Sex**: While some studies suggest a slight female preponderance, this may reflect the longer life expectancy of women. Overall, the sex distribution is considered roughly equal.
- **Laterality**: PEX is frequently clinically unilateral at the time of initial diagnosis (approximately 50–65% of cases), but longitudinal studies have demonstrated that the fellow eye becomes involved in a significant proportion of patients over time (cumulative conversion rates of up to 40% over 10–15 years). Histological and electron microscopic studies have shown subclinical PEX material in the clinically "unaffected" fellow eye, suggesting the disease is fundamentally bilateral but asymmetric.
- **Genetics**: A strong genetic component has been established. Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified the [[lysyl oxidase-like 1]] (*LOXL1*) gene on chromosome 15q24.1 as the principal genetic risk factor. Specific single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in *LOXL1* (particularly rs1048661 and rs3825942) confer a markedly elevated risk. However, these risk alleles are extremely common in the general population, indicating that *LOXL1* variants are necessary but not sufficient for disease development, and additional genetic and environmental modifiers are involved. Other implicated loci include *CACNA1A*, *FP13736*, and regions near *TMEM136* and *AGPAT1*.
- **Environmental factors**: Increased ultraviolet (UV) light exposure, residence at higher latitudes (paradoxically, possibly related to lower ambient temperatures or dietary factors), and certain dietary deficiencies (e.g., folate, homocysteine metabolism) have been proposed as environmental modulators.

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of pseudoexfoliation syndrome is complex, multifactorial, and not yet fully elucidated. At its core, PEX represents a **systemic elastotic process**—a disorder of elastic fiber homeostasis—resulting in the overproduction and aberrant cross-linking of extracellular matrix components.

### Nature of Pseudoexfoliative Material

The hallmark of PEX is the deposition of a characteristic **amyloid-like, fibrillar material** (pseudoexfoliative material, or PEX material) on ocular and extraocular tissues. Ultrastructurally, PEX material consists of randomly oriented microfibrils approximately 25–50 nm in diameter, often with a characteristic cross-banded periodicity. Biochemical analysis has revealed that PEX material is composed of:

- **Elastic fiber components**: Including [[elastin]], [[fibrillin-1]], [[fibulin-2]], and latent transforming growth factor-β binding proteins (LTBPs).
- **Basement membrane components**: Such as [[laminin]], [[fibronectin]], [[nidogen]], and various [[collagen]] subtypes (types IV and VI).
- **Enzymatic cross-linking proteins**: Notably [[lysyl oxidase-like 1]] (LOXL1), which is critical for the cross-linking of elastin and collagen in connective tissues.
- **Glycosaminoglycans**: Including [[chondroitin sulfate]] and [[heparan sulfate]].
- **Clusterin (apolipoprotein J)**: An extracellular chaperone protein found abundantly in PEX deposits.
- **Tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases (TIMPs)** and **matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs)**: Reflecting a disturbed balance between matrix synthesis and degradation.

The material is highly resistant to enzymatic degradation, which accounts for its progressive accumulation over time.

### Cellular Sources

PEX material is produced by a variety of cell types within the eye, including:

- [[Lens epithelium|Lens epithelial cells]]
- [[Ciliary body|Non-pigmented ciliary epithelium]]
- [[Iris]] pigment epithelium and stromal cells
- [[Trabecular meshwork]] endothelial cells
- [[Corneal endothelium]]
- Vascular endothelial cells of [[iris]] blood vessels

Extraocularly, PEX material has been identified in the walls of blood vessels in the heart, lungs, liver, kidneys, gallbladder, and [[meninges]], confirming the systemic nature of the disease.

### Molecular Mechanisms

Several interconnected molecular pathways contribute to PEX pathogenesis:

1. **Oxidative stress**: There is substantial evidence of increased [[oxidative stress]] in the anterior segment of PEX eyes. Reduced levels of [[ascorbic acid]] (vitamin C) in the [[aqueous humor]], decreased activity of antioxidant enzymes (e.g., [[superoxide dismutase]], [[glutathione peroxidase]]), and elevated markers of oxidative damage (e.g., 8-hydroxy-2'-deoxyguanosine, malondialdehyde) have been documented. Oxidative stress is believed to trigger abnormal gene expression and protein aggregation.

2. **Transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-β) dysregulation**: Elevated levels of [[TGF-β1]] in the aqueous humor of PEX eyes promote excessive extracellular matrix production, fibrosis, and impaired matrix turnover. TGF-β1 upregulates the expression of matrix proteins and cross-linking enzymes while simultaneously downregulating matrix-degrading enzymes.

3. **Homocysteine metabolism**: Elevated [[homocysteine]] levels in the aqueous humor and plasma of PEX patients have been reported. Hyperhomocysteinemia may contribute to endothelial dysfunction, oxidative stress, and abnormal extracellular matrix metabolism.

4. **Impaired autophagy and proteostasis**: Dysfunctional cellular clearance mechanisms may contribute to the intracellular and extracellular accumulation of misfolded proteins and aggregated material.

5. **Elastin fiber dysregulation via LOXL1**: Dysregulation of LOXL1 expression—either overexpression leading to excessive cross-linking or underexpression leading to defective elastic fiber assembly—is central to the pathogenesis. The precise mechanism by which *LOXL1* variants confer risk remains an area of active investigation, with evidence suggesting epigenetic regulation and tissue-specific expression changes.

### Consequences of PEX Material Deposition in the Eye

The progressive accumulation of PEX material on anterior segment structures leads to several clinically significant consequences:

- **[[Trabecular meshwork]] obstruction**: PEX material, along with dispersed [[melanin]] pigment granules liberated from the [[iris]] pigment epithelium, progressively clogs the trabecular meshwork, increasing [[aqueous humor]] outflow resistance and elevating [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP). This is the primary mechanism underlying [[pseudoexfoliation glaucoma]] (PXG).
- **[[Zonular]] weakness and instability**: PEX material deposits on and between the [[zonules of Zinn]], leading to progressive zonular degeneration, fragility, and dehiscence. This results in [[phacodonesis]] (lens wobble), [[lens subluxation]], and significantly increased risk of intraoperative complications during [[cataract surgery]].
- **[[Blood-aqueous barrier]] breakdown**: Chronic, low-grade inflammation and vascular endothelial dysfunction contribute to increased protein concentration in the aqueous humor (aqueous flare).
- **Iris vasculopathy**: PEX material deposits in and around iris blood vessel walls, leading to perivascular fibrosis, vascular occlusion, iris [[ischemia]], and neovascularization. This contributes to poor [[mydriasis]] and iris rigidity.
- **[[Corneal endothelium|Corneal endothelial]] compromise**: PEX material deposition on the corneal endothelium accelerates endothelial cell loss, potentially contributing to [[corneal decompensation]], particularly after intraocular surgery.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

Pseudoexfoliation syndrome is typically **asymptomatic** in its early stages. Patients are most often identified during routine comprehensive eye examinations. When symptoms do occur, they are generally attributable to the secondary complications of PEX rather than the syndrome itself:

- **Gradual, painless visual decline**: Due to progressive [[cataract]] formation (often [[nuclear sclerosis]]) or [[glaucomatous optic neuropathy]].
- **Acute visual disturbance or pain**: In cases of acute [[angle-closure glaucoma]] secondary to lens subluxation or pupillary block, or in cases of markedly elevated IOP.
- **Reduced contrast sensitivity and glare**: Related to cataract or corneal endothelial changes.
- **Visual field loss**: Typically detected on [[perimetry]] in patients who have developed [[pseudoexfoliation glaucoma]].

It is critical for the examining clinician to recognize that the absence of symptoms does not equate to the absence of disease or risk.

### Signs

The clinical signs of PEX are best appreciated during careful [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]], ideally performed **before** and **after** pharmacological [[mydriasis]].

#### Classic Triad on the Anterior Lens Surface

The pathognomonic finding of PEX is the characteristic pattern of whitish-gray, dandruff-like fibrillar material on the anterior [[lens capsule]], classically described as a **three-zone pattern** (bull's-eye pattern):

| **Zone** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Central disc** | A translucent, homogeneous disc of PEX material in the central pupillary area, corresponding to the area of the lens capsule that is constantly exposed through the undilated pupil. |
| **Clear intermediate zone** | A relatively clear zone where the iris sphincter mechanically rubs against the lens surface during pupillary movement, effectively "wiping" away the deposited material. This zone corresponds to the area of iris-lens contact. |
| **Peripheral granular zone** | A granular, often scalloped or curled-edge ring of PEX material in the periphery, visible only upon pupillary dilation. This zone is protected from iris abrasion and thus accumulates material undisturbed. |

This three-zone pattern is considered **diagnostic** of PEX when present. However, early or subtle cases may show only one or two zones, or may present with diffuse, faint deposits.

#### Other Anterior Segment Signs

- **PEX material on the [[pupillary margin]]**: Fine, white, flaky deposits along the pupillary ruff are often one of the earliest detectable signs.
- **PEX material on the [[iris]] surface**: Deposits may be visible on the iris stroma, particularly near the sphincter region.
- **PEX material on the [[corneal endothelium]]**: Deposits may be seen as fine, scattered particles, sometimes in a pattern resembling [[Krukenberg spindle]] but typically more diffuse and less vertically oriented.
- **PEX material on the [[zonules]]**: Visible on dilated examination as whitish deposits coating the zonular fibers.
- **PEX material in the [[anterior chamber angle]]**: Visible on [[gonioscopy]] as a scalloped or wavy line anterior to [[Schwalbe's line]] (Sampaolesi's line) and on the [[trabecular meshwork]].
- **[[Sampaolesi's line]]**: An irregular, patchy line of pigment deposition anterior to or at [[Schwalbe's line]], seen on gonioscopy. While not exclusive to PEX, it is highly characteristic and often more prominent and irregular than the pigment line seen in [[pigment dispersion syndrome]].
- **Increased [[trabecular meshwork]] pigmentation**: Often dense and patchy (as opposed to the homogeneous, dense pigmentation of [[pigment dispersion syndrome]]).
- **Peripupillary iris transillumination defects**: Typically at the [[pupillary margin]] (moth-eaten appearance), resulting from mechanical rubbing of the iris pigment epithelium against the lens surface and PEX deposits. This contrasts with the mid-peripheral, spoke-like transillumination defects of [[pigment dispersion syndrome]].
- **Poor pupillary dilation ([[mydriasis]])**: Due to iris sphincter fibrosis, posterior [[synechiae]], and iris rigidity. Maximum dilation is often limited to 5–6 mm even with strong pharmacological mydriatics. This is a critically important clinical finding with significant surgical implications.
- **[[Phacodonesis]]**: Tremulousness or wobbling of the [[crystalline lens]] during eye movements, indicative of zonular weakness.
- **[[Lens subluxation]] or [[dislocation]]**: In advanced cases, progressive zonular dehiscence can lead to partial (subluxation) or complete (dislocation) displacement of the lens.
- **Anterior chamber depth asymmetry**: The affected eye may have a shallower [[anterior chamber]] due to anterior lens movement from zonular laxity.
- **Increased [[aqueous flare]]**: Detectable on slit-lamp examination or [[laser flare photometry]], reflecting blood-aqueous barrier compromise.

## Diagnosis

The diagnosis of pseudoexfoliation syndrome is primarily **clinical**, based on the identification of characteristic PEX material deposits during comprehensive [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] with [[mydriasis]].

### Essential Diagnostic Procedures

| **Procedure** | **Key Findings in PEX** |
|---|---|
| **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]** (undilated) | PEX material on pupillary margin, iris surface, corneal endothelium; peripupillary transillumination defects; poor dilation |
| **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]** (dilated) | Classic three-zone pattern on anterior lens capsule; PEX on zonules; phacodonesis; lens subluxation |
| **[[Gonioscopy]]** | Sampaolesi's line; patchy, dense trabecular meshwork pigmentation; PEX material in angle; open or narrow angle |
| **[[Intraocular pressure]] measurement ([[Tonometry]])** | May be normal or elevated; wide IOP fluctuations common; higher mean IOP than age-matched controls |
| **[[Optic nerve]] evaluation ([[Ophthalmoscopy]], [[OCT]])** | Assessment for [[glaucomatous optic neuropathy]]: increased [[cup-to-disc ratio]], neuroretinal rim thinning, disc hemorrhages, [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL) thinning |
| **[[Visual field testing]] ([[Perimetry]])** | Assessment for glaucomatous visual field loss; may show more advanced damage at presentation compared to [[primary open-angle glaucoma]] |
| **[[Anterior segment OCT]] (AS-OCT)** | Evaluation of anterior chamber depth, angle configuration, lens position |
| **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | Detailed imaging of zonular integrity, ciliary body, and posterior chamber; useful for assessing zonular dialysis preoperatively |

### Diagnostic Criteria

There is no single universally accepted grading system, but the diagnosis is established by the presence of:

1. **Definite PEX**: Characteristic white, fibrillar material visible on the anterior lens capsule (especially the classic three-zone pattern) and/or pupillary margin, confirmed by slit-lamp examination after dilation.
2. **Probable/Suspect PEX**: Presence of suggestive but non-pathognomonic signs such as poor mydriasis, peripupillary transillumination defects, Sampaolesi's line, or increased trabecular pigmentation without definitive PEX material on the lens capsule.

### Differential Diagnosis

It is important to distinguish PEX from other conditions that may present with similar anterior segment findings:

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| **True exfoliation (capsular delamination)** | Thin, scroll-like lamellar peeling of the lens capsule; associated with infrared/heat exposure; no systemic fibrillar deposits |
| **[[Pigment dispersion syndrome]]** | Mid-peripheral, radial iris transillumination defects; Krukenberg spindle; homogeneous dense trabecular pigmentation; concave iris configuration; younger patients |
| **[[Primary open-angle glaucoma]]** | No PEX material; less trabecular pigmentation; typically more symmetric IOP and damage |
| **[[Amyloidosis]]** | Systemic amyloid deposits; different fibrillar composition; Congo red positive with apple-green birefringence |
| **[[Uveitis]] (chronic)** | Inflammatory cells and flare; keratic precipitates; posterior synechiae; different clinical context |
| **Primary iris melanocytic proliferation** | Pigmented deposits; different morphology on slit-lamp |

### Ancillary and Emerging Diagnostic Technologies

- **[[Laser flare photometry]]**: Quantitative measurement of aqueous flare; elevated in PEX eyes, even without overt inflammation.
- **[[Specular microscopy]]**: Demonstrates reduced [[corneal endothelial cell]] density, increased polymegathism, and pleomorphism in PEX eyes.
- **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)**: [[RNFL]] and [[ganglion cell complex]] (GCC) analysis for early glaucoma detection. [[Anterior segment OCT]] for angle and lens position assessment.
- **[[Confocal microscopy]]**: In vivo confocal microscopy can reveal PEX deposits on the corneal endothelium and within corneal layers.
- **Genetic testing**: While *LOXL1* polymorphisms are strongly associated with PEX, genetic testing is not currently used in routine clinical practice due to the high prevalence of risk alleles in unaffected individuals and the multifactorial nature of the disease.

## Association with Glaucoma: Pseudoexfoliation Glaucoma (PXG)

[[Pseudoexfoliation glaucoma]] (PXG) is the most serious ocular complication of PEX and represents the most common form of identifiable (secondary) [[open-angle glaucoma]] globally. Approximately **25–50%** of patients with PEX will develop elevated IOP or glaucoma over their lifetime, though estimates vary by population.

### Key Features of PXG Compared to [[Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma]] (POAG)

| **Feature** | **PXG** | **POAG** |
|---|---|---|
| IOP at presentation | Often higher (mean 25–35 mmHg) | Typically lower (mean 20–28 mmHg) |
| IOP fluctuation | Greater diurnal and long-term fluctuation | More stable |
| Asymmetry | More frequently unilateral or markedly asymmetric | More often bilateral and symmetric |
| Optic nerve damage at diagnosis | Often more advanced | Variable |
| Visual field loss at diagnosis | Often more severe | Variable |
| Response to medical therapy | Often less responsive; more rapid progression | Generally more responsive |
| Need for surgical intervention | Higher rate | Lower rate |
| Prognosis | Generally worse if untreated | Variable |

The mechanism of IOP elevation in PXG involves the progressive obstruction of the [[trabecular meshwork]] by PEX material and liberated [[melanin]] granules, leading to increased [[aqueous humor]] outflow resistance. Additionally, there may be a component of trabecular endothelial cell dysfunction and collapse of [[Schlemm's canal]].

---

## Systemic Associations

Given the systemic nature of PEX, several extraocular associations have been reported, although the strength and clinical significance of these associations remain subjects of ongoing research:

- **Cardiovascular disease**: Associations with [[coronary artery disease]], [[aortic aneurysm]], [[cardiomyopathy]], and [[cerebrovascular disease]] have been reported. PEX material has been found in the walls of cardiac and cerebral blood vessels.
- **Sensorineural [[hearing loss]]**: Some studies have reported an increased prevalence of hearing impairment in PEX patients.
- **[[Alzheimer's disease]] and cognitive impairment**: Preliminary associations have been suggested, potentially related to shared mechanisms of protein aggregation and oxidative stress.
- **Elevated plasma [[homocysteine]]**: A potential systemic biomarker and contributor to vascular risk.
- **Pelvic organ prolapse**: Reported in some studies, consistent with the systemic connective tissue disorder hypothesis.

Clinicians should be aware of these potential systemic associations, although routine systemic screening based solely on a PEX diagnosis is not currently standard practice.

## Management and Treatment

There is currently **no treatment to prevent, halt, or reverse** the deposition of pseudoexfoliative material. Management is therefore directed at the **surveillance, prevention, and treatment of complications**, primarily [[glaucoma]] and cataract-related issues.

### Monitoring and Surveillance

All patients diagnosed with PEX, even those without elevated IOP or glaucoma, require **regular, lifelong ophthalmic monitoring** due to the progressive nature of the disease and the significant risk of developing glaucoma.

**Recommended monitoring schedule for PEX without glaucoma:**

| **Parameter** | **Frequency** |
|---|---|
| [[Intraocular pressure]] (IOP) | Every 6–12 months |
| [[Gonioscopy]] | Annually or as indicated |
| [[Optic nerve head]] evaluation (stereoscopic) | Every 6–12 months |
| [[OCT]] (RNFL / GCC) | Annually |
| [[Visual field testing]] | Annually or as indicated |
| Anterior segment evaluation (PEX progression, zonular status) | Every 6–12 months |
| Fellow eye assessment | At every visit |

### Pharmacological Management of Pseudoexfoliation Glaucoma

When IOP is elevated or glaucomatous damage is present, medical therapy is typically the first-line approach. The pharmacological agents used are the same as those for [[primary open-angle glaucoma]], but several important considerations apply:

#### First-Line Agents

- **[[Prostaglandin analogs]]** (e.g., [[latanoprost]], [[travoprost]], [[bimatoprost]], [[tafluprost]]): These are generally considered first-line therapy for PXG. They lower IOP by increasing [[uveoscleral outflow]], a pathway that is less affected by PEX material deposition than the conventional trabecular outflow pathway. They provide effective IOP reduction (25–35%) with once-daily dosing and minimal systemic side effects.

- **[[Beta-adrenergic antagonists]]** (e.g., [[timolol]], [[betaxolol]]): Reduce [[aqueous humor]] production. Effective as monotherapy or adjunctive therapy. Systemic side effects (bradycardia, bronchospasm, hypotension) must be considered, particularly in the elderly PEX population.

#### Second-Line and Adjunctive Agents

- **[[Alpha-adrenergic agonists]]** (e.g., [[brimonidine]]): Reduce aqueous production and may increase uveoscleral outflow. Useful as adjunctive therapy. Risk of allergic conjunctivitis with chronic use.

- **[[Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]]** (topical: [[dorzolamide]], [[brinzolamide]]; oral: [[acetazolamide]]): Reduce aqueous humor production. Topical agents are commonly used as adjunctive therapy. Oral agents are reserved for acute IOP elevation or refractory cases.

- **[[Rho kinase inhibitors]]** (e.g., [[netarsudil]]): A newer class that increases trabecular outflow by directly targeting the trabecular meshwork and [[Schlemm's canal]]. Theoretically attractive in PXG given the trabecular pathology, though specific large-scale PXG studies are limited.

- **[[Cholinergic agonists]]** (e.g., [[pilocarpine]]): Increase trabecular outflow by contracting the [[ciliary muscle]] and opening the trabecular meshwork. While effective, they are less commonly used as first-line agents due to side effects (miosis, brow ache, induced myopia, retinal detachment risk). However, low-dose pilocarpine may have a role in improving pupillary function preoperatively.

- **Fixed-dose combinations**: Combinations such as [[dorzolamide]]/[[timolol]], [[brimonidine]]/[[timolol]], or [[brinzolamide]]/[[brimonidine]] can improve adherence and efficacy when monotherapy is insufficient.

#### Important Pharmacological Considerations in PXG

- PXG often requires **more aggressive IOP lowering** than POAG, with lower target IOPs.
- PXG eyes may demonstrate **greater IOP fluctuation**, necessitating consideration of agents that provide consistent 24-hour IOP control.
- **Medical therapy alone is frequently insufficient** in PXG, and many patients will require laser or surgical intervention earlier in the disease course compared to POAG.
- **Adherence** is critical and should be assessed at every visit, particularly in elderly patients who may have difficulty with drop instillation.

### Laser Treatment

#### [[Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty]] (SLT)

[[SLT]] is an effective and well-established treatment option for PXG. It targets pigmented trabecular meshwork cells, stimulating biological remodeling and improving aqueous outflow. Key points:

- PXG eyes often respond **favorably** to SLT, potentially due to the increased trabecular pigmentation providing better laser energy absorption.
- IOP reduction of 20–30% can be expected in many patients.
- SLT can be used as a **primary treatment** (in lieu of or before initiating drops) or as an **adjunctive treatment**.
- The effect may wane over time (typically 1–5 years), but SLT is **repeatable**.
- There is a small risk of transient IOP spikes post-treatment, which should be monitored.

#### [[Argon Laser Trabeculoplasty]] (ALT)

ALT was historically used but has been largely supplanted by SLT due to SLT's comparable efficacy, better safety profile, and repeatability. ALT causes more structural damage to the trabecular meshwork and is generally not repeatable.

#### [[Laser Peripheral Iridotomy]] (LPI)

LPI may be indicated in PEX cases where there is a component of [[angle closure]] due to anterior lens displacement from zonular laxity, or in cases of [[pupillary block]].

### Surgical Management of Pseudoexfoliation Glaucoma

Surgical intervention is indicated when medical and laser therapies fail to achieve adequate IOP control or when the disease progresses despite treatment.

#### [[Trabeculectomy]]

[[Trabeculectomy]] remains a gold-standard filtering surgery for refractory PXG. It creates a guarded fistula allowing aqueous humor to drain from the anterior chamber to a subconjunctival bleb. Adjunctive use of antifibrotic agents ([[mitomycin C]] or [[5-fluorouracil]]) is standard to prevent bleb fibrosis. Success rates in PXG are generally comparable to those in POAG.

#### [[Glaucoma Drainage Devices]] (Tube Shunts)

Devices such as the [[Ahmed glaucoma valve]] or [[Baerveldt glaucoma implant]] are used in cases refractory to trabeculectomy or where trabeculectomy is deemed high-risk. They shunt aqueous humor to an equatorial plate, bypassing the trabecular meshwork entirely.

#### [[Minimally Invasive Glaucoma Surgery]] (MIGS)

MIGS procedures have gained increasing popularity and may be particularly useful in PXG, especially when combined with cataract surgery:

- **[[iStent]]** and **[[iStent inject]]**: Trabecular micro-bypass stents that enhance aqueous outflow through [[Schlemm's canal]].
- **[[Hydrus Microstent]]**: A scaffold placed in Schlemm's canal to maintain its patency.
- **[[Kahook Dual Blade]] (KDB) goniotomy**: Excision of a strip of trabecular meshwork to create a direct communication with Schlemm's canal.
- **[[Xen Gel Stent]]**: A subconjunctival drainage device creating a pathway similar to trabeculectomy but with a less invasive approach.
- **[[Endocyclophotocoagulation]] (ECP)**: Laser ablation of the [[ciliary processes]] to reduce aqueous production, often performed during cataract surgery.

The choice of MIGS procedure depends on the severity of glaucoma, target IOP, and the surgeon's experience.

#### [[Cyclodestructive Procedures]]

[[Transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]] (TSCPC) or micropulse cyclophotocoagulation (MP-CPC) may be considered in advanced or refractory PXG, particularly in eyes with limited visual potential. These procedures reduce aqueous production by ablating the [[ciliary body]].

### Cataract Surgery Considerations in PEX

[[Cataract surgery]] in patients with PEX presents unique challenges and carries a significantly higher risk of intraoperative and postoperative complications compared to routine cataract surgery. Awareness and meticulous preoperative planning are essential.

#### Preoperative Considerations

- **Thorough assessment of zonular integrity**: Look for phacodonesis, lens decentration, anterior chamber depth asymmetry, and iridodonesis.
- **Assessment of pupil dilation**: Anticipate poor mydriasis and plan accordingly (e.g., mechanical pupil expansion devices, intracameral mydriatics).
- **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)**: May be useful to assess zonular status in equivocal cases.
- **Informed consent**: Patients should be counseled about the increased risk of complications.

#### Intraoperative Risks and Management

| **Complication** | **Mechanism** | **Management Strategy** |
|---|---|---|
| **Poor mydriasis** | Iris sphincter fibrosis, posterior synechiae | Intracameral [[phenylephrine]]/[[lidocaine]]; [[iris hooks]]; [[Malyugin ring]]; pupil expansion devices |
| **Zonular dialysis/dehiscence** | Weakened, PEX-coated zonules | [[Capsular tension ring]] (CTR); modified CTR (e.g., [[Cionni ring]]); capsule hooks; gentle hydrodissection; low-flow phacoemulsification settings |
| **Posterior capsule rupture** | Zonular instability; lens movement | Anterior [[vitrectomy]]; careful IOL placement (sulcus, anterior chamber, or scleral fixation) |
| **Lens subluxation/dislocation** | Severe zonular loss | Intracapsular extraction; pars plana lentectomy; scleral-fixated or iris-fixated IOL |
| **Vitreous loss** | Posterior capsule rupture; zonular dialysis | Anterior vitrectomy; triamcinolone-assisted visualization |
| **Corneal endothelial damage** | Pre-existing endothelial compromise; prolonged surgery | Dispersive [[ophthalmic viscosurgical device]] (OVD); soft-shell technique; minimize ultrasound energy |

#### Postoperative Considerations

- **Increased risk of postoperative inflammation**: More aggressive anti-inflammatory regimen may be warranted (e.g., topical [[corticosteroids]], [[NSAIDs]]).
- **IOL decentration or dislocation**: Late in-the-bag IOL dislocation is a well-recognized complication of PEX, occurring years to decades after uneventful cataract surgery due to progressive zonular weakening. Reported rates are significantly higher in PEX eyes (5–10 times higher than non-PEX eyes). Prophylactic placement of a [[capsular tension ring]] at the time of initial surgery may reduce this risk.
- **Elevated IOP**: Postoperative IOP spikes are more common in PEX eyes.
- **[[Posterior capsule opacification]] (PCO)**: May occur, though some studies suggest a lower rate due to reduced lens epithelial cell proliferation in PEX.
- **Corneal decompensation**: Pre-existing endothelial compromise may be exacerbated by surgical trauma.

### Management of Late In-the-Bag IOL Dislocation

Late spontaneous dislocation of the IOL-capsular bag complex is an increasingly recognized complication in PEX patients. Management options include:

- **IOL repositioning and scleral fixation** of the existing capsular bag-IOL complex.
- **IOL exchange** with placement of a scleral-fixated, iris-fixated (e.g., iris-claw lens), or anterior chamber IOL.
- **Pars plana vitrectomy** may be required if the IOL-bag complex has dislocated posteriorly into the vitreous cavity.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of pseudoexfoliation syndrome is variable and depends largely on the development and severity of associated complications, particularly glaucoma:

- **PEX without glaucoma**: With regular monitoring, the prognosis for maintaining good vision is generally favorable, though lifelong surveillance is mandatory.
- **Pseudoexfoliation glaucoma**: PXG tends to be more aggressive than POAG, with higher presenting IOPs, greater IOP fluctuation, more advanced optic nerve damage at diagnosis, and a higher rate of progression to blindness if inadequately treated. However, with early detection and aggressive IOP management, visual outcomes can be significantly improved.
- **Cataract surgery outcomes**: While complication rates are higher, experienced surgeons using appropriate techniques and devices (CTRs, pupil expanders) can achieve excellent visual outcomes in the majority of PEX patients undergoing cataract surgery.

## Key Clinical Pearls for the Optometrist

1. **Always dilate**: The classic three-zone pattern on the lens capsule and zonular assessment require adequate mydriasis. PEX can be easily missed without dilation.
2. **Examine the pupillary margin carefully**: PEX deposits on the pupillary ruff and peripupillary transillumination defects may be the earliest detectable signs.
3. **Perform gonioscopy**: Sampaolesi's line and patchy trabecular pigmentation are important diagnostic clues and help assess angle status.
4. **Check IOP carefully and repeatedly**: PXG is characterized by wide IOP fluctuations. A single normal IOP reading does not exclude glaucoma. Consider multiple IOP measurements at different times of day.
5. **Assess the fellow eye**: Even if clinically unilateral, the fellow eye is at significant risk and should be monitored closely.
6. **Document zonular status**: Note any phacodonesis or lens decentration, as this information is critical for surgical planning.
7. **Communicate with the cataract surgeon**: If referring for cataract surgery, clearly document the presence of PEX, zonular status, pupil dilation quality, and any glaucoma history.
8. **Educate the patient**: Patients should understand the chronic, progressive nature of PEX and the importance of regular follow-up, even in the absence of symptoms.
9. **Consider systemic implications**: While routine systemic workup is not standard, be aware of potential cardiovascular associations, particularly in patients with other risk factors.
10. **Lifelong follow-up**: PEX is a progressive condition. Patients require ongoing monitoring indefinitely, even after successful cataract or glaucoma surgery.

---

## Summary

Pseudoexfoliation syndrome is a prevalent, age-related, systemic disorder of the extracellular matrix with profound ocular implications. Its hallmark is the deposition of characteristic fibrillar material throughout the anterior segment, leading to an increased risk of [[glaucoma]], [[cataract]] surgery complications, and [[lens dislocation]]. The condition is genetically influenced, with *LOXL1* as the principal susceptibility gene, and is modulated by oxidative stress, TGF-β dysregulation, and environmental factors. Diagnosis is clinical, relying on meticulous slit-lamp examination with dilation and gonioscopy. Management centers on vigilant monitoring, aggressive IOP control when glaucoma develops, and careful surgical planning for cataract extraction. Optometrists play a critical frontline role in the detection, monitoring, and co-management of this important condition.
