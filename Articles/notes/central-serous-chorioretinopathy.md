

# Central Serous Chorioretinopathy

---

**Central Serous Chorioretinopathy** (CSC or CSCR), also historically referred to as **central serous retinopathy** (CSR) or **idiopathic central serous choroidopathy**, is a chorioretinal disorder characterized by a localized serous [[neurosensory retinal detachment]] at the [[macula]], with or without an associated [[pigment epithelial detachment]] (PED). The condition arises from focal or multifocal defects in the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE), which allow fluid from a thickened, hyperpermeable [[choroid]] to accumulate in the subretinal space. CSC predominantly affects young to middle-aged adults, with a marked male predilection, and is strongly associated with endogenous or exogenous [[corticosteroid]] exposure, type A personality traits, and psychosocial stress. While the acute form is frequently self-limiting with spontaneous resolution within several months, chronic or recurrent CSC can lead to progressive [[photoreceptor]] damage, [[subretinal fibrosis]], and permanent visual impairment. CSC is now recognized as part of the broader spectrum of **pachychoroid disease**, a concept that has fundamentally reshaped understanding of its pathogenesis.

---

## Epidemiology

Central serous chorioretinopathy is one of the most common causes of macular disease in working-age adults. Its estimated annual incidence is approximately **5.8 per 100,000 in men** and **0.9 per 100,000 in women**, reflecting a male-to-female ratio of roughly 6:1, although some studies suggest the ratio may be closer to 2.7:1 when chronic forms are included. The peak age of onset is between **25 and 50 years**, with a mean age at presentation of approximately 40 years. Chronic CSC, however, may present in older individuals and is increasingly recognized in patients over 50 years of age.

### Demographic and Geographic Considerations

| Factor | Details |
|---|---|
| **Sex** | Male predominance (M:F ≈ 6:1 in acute CSC) |
| **Age of onset** | 25–50 years (peak ~40 years) |
| **Ethnicity** | Higher prevalence reported in Asian and Hispanic populations; also common in Caucasians |
| **Laterality** | Unilateral in most acute presentations; bilateral involvement in up to 40% of chronic cases |
| **Recurrence rate** | Approximately 30–50% within the first year |

## Etiology and Risk Factors

The precise etiology of CSC remains incompletely understood, but a multifactorial model involving choroidal vascular dysregulation, RPE dysfunction, and systemic influences is widely accepted. The following risk factors have been consistently identified in the literature:

### Primary Risk Factors

- **[[Corticosteroid]] use**: The single most well-established modifiable risk factor. Both exogenous (oral, inhaled, intranasal, topical dermatologic, intra-articular, epidural, and intravitreal) and endogenous (e.g., [[Cushing syndrome]]) corticosteroid exposure are strongly implicated. Corticosteroids are believed to increase choroidal vascular permeability, upregulate adrenergic receptors, and impair RPE ion transport mechanisms.
- **Psychological stress and type A personality**: Elevated levels of endogenous [[cortisol]] and [[catecholamines]] associated with chronic stress and competitive, time-urgent personality traits have been linked to CSC onset and recurrence.
- **Male sex**: Likely related to hormonal influences, including higher circulating [[testosterone]] levels and androgen receptor activity in the choroid.
- **[[Pregnancy]]**: CSC can occur during the third trimester, likely related to elevated endogenous cortisol levels. It typically resolves postpartum.

### Secondary and Associated Risk Factors

- **[[Obstructive sleep apnea]]** (OSA): Increasingly recognized as a significant risk factor, possibly through sympathetic nervous system activation and hypoxia-mediated choroidal changes.
- **[[Hypertension]]**
- **[[Helicobacter pylori]]** infection: Some studies have suggested an association, though the evidence remains debated.
- **Organ transplantation** (due to chronic immunosuppressive corticosteroid use)
- **Autoimmune diseases** requiring corticosteroid therapy (e.g., [[systemic lupus erythematosus]], [[rheumatoid arthritis]])
- **Use of sympathomimetic agents** and **phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitors** (e.g., [[sildenafil]])
- **Gastroesophageal reflux disease** ([[GERD]])
- **Alcohol use**

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of CSC has undergone a paradigm shift over the past two decades, evolving from an RPE-centric model to a choroid-centric model. CSC is now firmly classified within the **[[pachychoroid]] disease spectrum**, which includes [[pachychoroid pigment epitheliopathy]] (PPE), [[pachychoroid neovasculopathy]] (PNV), [[polypoidal choroidal vasculopathy]] (PCV), and focal choroidal excavation.

### Choroidal Dysfunction

The fundamental abnormality in CSC is a **thickened, congested, and hyperpermeable choroid** (pachychoroid). Key choroidal features include:

1. **Increased choroidal thickness**: Enhanced depth imaging [[optical coherence tomography]] (EDI-OCT) and swept-source [[OCT]] consistently demonstrate subfoveal choroidal thickness values exceeding 400 µm in CSC eyes (normal: ~250–300 µm), often with asymmetric thickening.
2. **Dilated choroidal vessels (pachyvessels)**: Enlargement of the outer choroidal vasculature ([[Haller's layer]]) with corresponding thinning or compression of the inner choroidal vasculature ([[Sattler's layer]]) and the [[choriocapillaris]].
3. **Choroidal vascular hyperpermeability**: [[Indocyanine green angiography]] (ICGA) reveals multifocal areas of choroidal hyperpermeability, often more extensive than the clinically apparent leakage on [[fluorescein angiography]] (FA). This hyperpermeability is thought to result from venous congestion, increased hydrostatic pressure, and breakdown of choroidal vascular endothelial barriers.
4. **Choriocapillaris ischemia and flow abnormalities**: [[OCT angiography]] (OCTA) studies have demonstrated focal areas of choriocapillaris flow voids and ischemia, which may contribute to overlying RPE stress and dysfunction.

### Retinal Pigment Epithelium Breakdown

The [[retinal pigment epithelium]] serves as the outer [[blood-retinal barrier]]. In CSC, the combination of increased choroidal hydrostatic pressure and focal RPE compromise leads to:

- **Focal RPE defects (leak points)**: These represent sites where the RPE tight junctions ([[zonulae occludentes]]) are disrupted, allowing transudation of protein-rich fluid from the choroid into the subretinal space.
- **RPE pump failure**: The RPE normally actively transports fluid from the subretinal space to the choroid via Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pumps. In CSC, the overwhelmed or dysfunctional RPE cannot adequately clear the accumulating subretinal fluid.
- **Pigment epithelial detachments (PEDs)**: Serous PEDs occur when fluid accumulates beneath the RPE, often at or near the site of the primary leak.

### Hormonal and Autonomic Mechanisms

Corticosteroids play a central role in CSC pathogenesis through multiple mechanisms:

- **Upregulation of adrenergic receptors** in choroidal vasculature, increasing sensitivity to circulating catecholamines
- **Increased expression of aquaporin channels** and altered ion transport across the RPE
- **Inhibition of collagen synthesis** in the RPE basement membrane and [[Bruch's membrane]]
- **Promotion of choroidal vascular permeability** through mineralocorticoid receptor (MR) activation in choroidal endothelial cells
- **Activation of the [[mineralocorticoid receptor]] (MR) pathway**: This has emerged as a critical pathogenic mechanism. Aldosterone and cortisol acting on MR in choroidal vessels upregulate the expression of the vasodilatory potassium channel KCa2.3, leading to choroidal vasodilation, increased blood flow, and vascular leakage.

### Proposed Pathogenic Sequence

1. Choroidal venous congestion and pachychoroid development → 2. Increased choroidal hydrostatic pressure → 3. Choriocapillaris ischemia and RPE stress → 4. Focal RPE barrier breakdown → 5. Subretinal fluid accumulation → 6. Neurosensory retinal detachment

## Classification

CSC is broadly classified into **acute** and **chronic** forms, though a continuum exists between these categories.

| Feature | Acute CSC | Chronic CSC |
|---|---|---|
| **Duration** | < 4–6 months | > 4–6 months (some define as > 3 months) |
| **Typical patient** | Young male, 25–45 years | Older patient, often > 50 years |
| **Leakage pattern (FA)** | Single focal leak point ("inkblot" or "smokestack") | Diffuse, multifocal, or gravitational tract leakage |
| **RPE changes** | Minimal | Widespread RPE atrophy, mottling, gravitational tracks |
| **Choroidal findings** | Focal pachychoroid | Diffuse pachychoroid, extensive hyperpermeability |
| **Subretinal fluid** | Well-circumscribed dome | Shallow, diffuse, may be bilateral |
| **Visual prognosis** | Generally favorable; spontaneous resolution in 80–90% | Guarded; progressive photoreceptor loss, RPE atrophy |
| **Associated findings** | Rare CNV | May develop secondary [[choroidal neovascularization]] (CNV) |
| **Recurrence** | 30–50% | Persistent or frequently recurrent |

A third category, **recurrent CSC**, is sometimes distinguished, referring to patients who experience repeated episodes of acute CSC with intervening periods of complete fluid resolution.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

Patients with CSC typically present with the following visual complaints, which are usually **unilateral** at onset:

- **Blurred or dimmed central vision**: The most common presenting symptom, typically mild to moderate (visual acuity often 20/20 to 20/40 in acute CSC)
- **Central [[scotoma]]** or relative central visual field depression
- **[[Metamorphopsia]]**: Distortion of straight lines, often described as a "warping" or "bending" of images, resulting from the physical displacement of photoreceptors by subretinal fluid
- **[[Micropsia]]**: Objects appear smaller than their actual size due to separation and stretching of photoreceptors by the serous detachment
- **[[Dyschromatopsia]]**: Subtle color vision changes, often a desaturation of colors in the affected eye
- **Relative [[hyperopia]]**: A hyperopic shift of +0.50 to +2.00 diopters caused by anterior displacement of the neurosensory retina by subretinal fluid, effectively shortening the eye's focal length at the macula
- **Reduced [[contrast sensitivity]]**
- **Prolonged [[photostress recovery time]]**: A hallmark clinical finding; recovery of visual acuity after exposure to bright light is significantly delayed (normal: < 50 seconds; CSC: often > 90 seconds)

### Signs on Clinical Examination

**[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]** with a fundus contact or non-contact lens (e.g., [[78D lens]], [[90D lens]], or [[Goldmann three-mirror lens]]) reveals:

- **Serous neurosensory retinal detachment**: A well-circumscribed, dome-shaped elevation of the transparent neurosensory retina at the macula, typically 1–5 disc diameters in size. The subretinal fluid is clear in acute cases.
- **Loss of the [[foveal reflex]]**
- **Pigment epithelial detachment (PED)**: A smaller, more sharply demarcated elevation of the RPE, often located at or near the edge of the neurosensory detachment. May appear as a yellowish or slightly elevated lesion.
- **RPE changes**: In chronic or recurrent cases, the RPE may show:
  - Mottled hyperpigmentation and hypopigmentation
  - **Gravitational tracks** (descending tracts): Linear streaks of RPE atrophy extending inferiorly from the macula, representing the path of gravitating subretinal fluid over time
  - Diffuse RPE atrophy
  - Fibrinous or lipid subretinal deposits (in severe or chronic cases)
- **Subretinal fibrin**: Occasionally seen in acute cases, appearing as a yellowish-white deposit on the posterior surface of the detached retina
- **Subretinal precipitates**: Small, punctate yellowish deposits on the RPE surface beneath the detachment

## Diagnostic Modalities

A comprehensive diagnostic evaluation is essential for confirming the diagnosis of CSC, characterizing the disease subtype, identifying leak points, and ruling out other causes of serous macular detachment.

### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

[[Optical coherence tomography]] is the primary and most frequently used diagnostic tool for CSC. **Spectral-domain OCT (SD-OCT)** and **swept-source OCT (SS-OCT)** provide high-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the retina and choroid.

**Key OCT findings in CSC:**

- **Subretinal fluid (SRF)**: A hyporeflective (dark) space between the neurosensory retina and the RPE, representing the serous detachment. The overlying retina is typically structurally intact in acute cases.
- **Pigment epithelial detachment (PED)**: Focal elevation of the RPE band from [[Bruch's membrane]], with a hyporeflective or moderately reflective space beneath.
- **Increased subfoveal choroidal thickness**: Best visualized with **enhanced depth imaging (EDI-OCT)** or SS-OCT. Choroidal thickness > 400 µm is highly suggestive.
- **Pachyvessels**: Dilated outer choroidal (Haller's layer) vessels with compression of Sattler's layer.
- **Elongation of photoreceptor outer segments**: In acute detachments, the photoreceptor outer segments may appear elongated within the subretinal fluid.
- **Subretinal fibrin or precipitates**: Hyperreflective material on the posterior surface of the detached retina or on the RPE surface.
- **Disruption of the [[ellipsoid zone]] (EZ) and [[external limiting membrane]] (ELM)**: Seen in chronic CSC, indicating photoreceptor damage and correlating with poorer visual outcomes.
- **RPE irregularities**: Thinning, thickening, or undulation of the RPE band in chronic disease.

### Fluorescein Angiography (FA)

[[Fluorescein angiography]] remains a critical diagnostic tool for identifying the site and pattern of RPE leakage.

**Classic FA patterns in CSC:**

| Pattern | Description | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| **Inkblot (expansile dot)** | A small, pinpoint hyperfluorescent spot that appears in the early arteriovenous phase and progressively expands concentrically in a uniform pattern throughout the angiogram | ~70–80% of acute cases |
| **Smokestack** | A pinpoint leak that ascends vertically like a plume of smoke before spreading laterally at the top of the detachment (mushroom or umbrella configuration) | ~10–20% of acute cases |
| **Diffuse RPE leak** | Multiple, ill-defined areas of granular hyperfluorescence without a single dominant leak point; characteristic of chronic CSC | Common in chronic CSC |
| **Gravitational track** | Linear hyperfluorescence extending inferiorly from the macula, representing chronic RPE atrophy along the path of dependent fluid | Chronic/recurrent CSC |
| **Window defect** | Transmission hyperfluorescence due to RPE atrophy without active leakage | Chronic CSC, resolved episodes |

### Indocyanine Green Angiography (ICGA)

[[Indocyanine green angiography]] is invaluable for evaluating the choroidal vasculature, as ICG dye is 98% protein-bound and remains largely intravascular, providing superior visualization of choroidal circulation compared to FA.

**Key ICGA findings:**

- **Choroidal vascular hyperpermeability**: Mid-to-late phase hyperfluorescent patches indicating leakage from congested choroidal vessels. These areas are often more extensive than the FA leak points and may be present bilaterally, even in clinically unilateral disease.
- **Dilated choroidal veins**
- **Focal areas of choriocapillaris hypoperfusion** (early-phase hypofluorescence)
- **Punctate hyperfluorescent spots**: Corresponding to active leak points

ICGA is particularly useful for:
- Identifying occult or multifocal leak points not visible on FA
- Guiding treatment (e.g., [[photodynamic therapy]] targeting areas of choroidal hyperpermeability)
- Differentiating CSC from other causes of serous detachment

### OCT Angiography (OCTA)

[[OCT angiography]] is a non-invasive, dye-free imaging modality that provides depth-resolved visualization of retinal and choroidal vasculature.

**OCTA findings in CSC:**

- **Choriocapillaris flow voids**: Focal areas of reduced or absent choriocapillaris perfusion, often corresponding to the area of RPE leakage
- **Dilated choroidal vessels** in deeper slabs
- **Detection of secondary [[choroidal neovascularization]] (CNV)**: OCTA is highly sensitive for identifying type 1 (sub-RPE) CNV, which can complicate chronic CSC and may be missed on FA alone
- **Absence of significant retinal vascular abnormalities** (helping to exclude other diagnoses)

### Fundus Autofluorescence (FAF)

[[Fundus autofluorescence]] imaging detects the distribution of [[lipofuscin]] in the RPE, providing information about RPE health and metabolic activity.

**FAF findings in CSC:**

- **Hypoautofluorescence**: Areas of RPE atrophy (lipofuscin loss) — seen in chronic CSC and resolved gravitational tracks
- **Hyperautofluorescence**: Areas of increased RPE metabolic stress or lipofuscin accumulation; may outline the area of current or prior subretinal fluid
- **Gravitational tracks**: Linear hypoautofluorescent streaks extending inferiorly
- **Granular or mottled pattern**: In chronic CSC, reflecting widespread RPE dysfunction

FAF is particularly useful for assessing the extent of RPE damage in chronic CSC and for monitoring disease progression over time.

### Additional Diagnostic Considerations

- **[[Visual field testing]]**: May reveal a relative central scotoma or paracentral depression corresponding to the area of detachment
- **[[Amsler grid]]**: Useful for patient self-monitoring; patients typically report central metamorphopsia
- **[[Multifocal electroretinography]] (mfERG)**: May show reduced central retinal function even after anatomic resolution of SRF, indicating subclinical photoreceptor dysfunction
- **B-scan [[ultrasonography]]**: Rarely needed but may demonstrate choroidal thickening and serous detachment in cases where media opacity precludes OCT imaging

## Differential Diagnosis

The differential diagnosis of CSC includes other conditions that can produce serous macular detachment or simulate its appearance:

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| **[[Choroidal neovascularization]] (e.g., from [[age-related macular degeneration]])** | Subretinal hemorrhage, lipid exudates, type 1 or type 2 neovascular membrane on OCTA/FA |
| **[[Polypoidal choroidal vasculopathy]]** (PCV) | Branching vascular network with polypoidal lesions on ICGA; may overlap with pachychoroid spectrum |
| **[[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]]** (VKH) | Bilateral, multifocal serous detachments; associated with vitiligo, poliosis, meningismus; diffuse choroidal thickening |
| **[[Posterior scleritis]]** | Pain, scleral thickening on B-scan, T-sign |
| **[[Optic disc pit maculopathy]]** | Optic disc pit visible on examination; schisis-like changes on OCT |
| **[[Choroidal tumor]]** (e.g., [[choroidal melanoma]], [[choroidal hemangioma]])** | Solid mass lesion on ultrasonography and OCT |
| **[[Uveal effusion syndrome]]** | Peripheral choroidal detachments, nanophthalmos |
| **[[Hypertensive choroidopathy]]** | Severe systemic hypertension, Elschnig spots, fibrinoid necrosis |
| **[[Dome-shaped macula]]** | Structural macular convexity in high myopia |

## Management and Treatment

### Observation (Acute CSC)

The initial management of **acute CSC** is typically **observation**, as spontaneous resolution occurs in approximately **80–90% of cases within 3–4 months**. During the observation period:

- **Discontinue or taper corticosteroids** whenever medically feasible (all routes of administration)
- **Stress reduction counseling** and lifestyle modification
- **Address modifiable risk factors**: Treat [[obstructive sleep apnea]], manage [[hypertension]], reduce alcohol consumption
- **Serial OCT monitoring**: Every 4–6 weeks to assess for fluid resolution
- **Patient education**: Amsler grid self-monitoring for metamorphopsia changes

### Indications for Active Treatment

Active intervention is considered when:

- Subretinal fluid persists beyond **3–4 months** (some clinicians intervene at 3 months)
- **Chronic CSC** with progressive RPE atrophy or photoreceptor damage
- **Recurrent CSC** with cumulative visual loss
- Occupational or functional visual requirements demand earlier intervention
- Development of secondary **[[choroidal neovascularization]]**
- Involvement of the **fellow eye** with prior visual loss in the first eye

### Photodynamic Therapy (PDT)

**[[Photodynamic therapy]]** with [[verteporfin]] (Visudyne®) is considered the **gold standard treatment** for chronic CSC and is the most extensively studied intervention.

#### Mechanism
Intravenous verteporfin is activated by a 689 nm diode laser, generating reactive oxygen species that cause selective vascular endothelial damage and thrombosis in the choriocapillaris, leading to choroidal vascular remodeling, reduced choroidal hyperpermeability, and decreased choroidal thickness.

#### Protocols

| Protocol | Verteporfin Dose | Laser Fluence | Laser Duration | Spot Size |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Standard (full-dose) PDT** | 6 mg/m² BSA | 50 J/cm² | 83 seconds | Covers area of hyperpermeability |
| **Half-dose PDT** | 3 mg/m² BSA | 50 J/cm² | 83 seconds | Covers area of hyperpermeability |
| **Half-fluence PDT** | 6 mg/m² BSA | 25 J/cm² | 83 seconds | Covers area of hyperpermeability |
| **Reduced settings PDT** | 3 mg/m² BSA | 25 J/cm² | 83 seconds | Covers area of hyperpermeability |

**Half-dose PDT** (the most commonly used protocol) has demonstrated:
- Complete SRF resolution in **67–100%** of eyes at 3–12 months
- Significant reduction in choroidal thickness
- Improvement in best-corrected [[visual acuity]] (BCVA)
- Lower risk of complications compared to full-dose PDT

**Complications of PDT** (more common with full-dose):
- Transient choriocapillaris hypoperfusion
- RPE atrophy (particularly with repeated full-dose treatments)
- Secondary CNV development
- Transient visual disturbance

The **PLACE trial** (2018), a landmark multicenter randomized controlled trial, demonstrated that half-dose PDT was superior to high-density subthreshold micropulse laser (SML) for chronic CSC, with significantly higher rates of complete SRF resolution at 6–8 weeks (67% vs. 29%).

### Subthreshold Micropulse Laser (SML)

**[[Subthreshold micropulse laser]]** therapy delivers repetitive, short-duration laser pulses (typically 810 nm diode or 577 nm yellow) with intervening "off" periods, allowing tissue cooling and minimizing thermal damage to the RPE and photoreceptors.

**Advantages:**
- Non-destructive to the RPE (no visible laser burn)
- Can be applied directly to the fovea
- Repeatable with minimal cumulative damage
- No need for intravenous dye injection

**Efficacy:**
- Complete SRF resolution reported in **40–75%** of cases at 3–6 months
- May require multiple treatment sessions
- Inferior to half-dose PDT in the PLACE trial for chronic CSC, but remains a viable option when PDT is unavailable or contraindicated

### Conventional Focal Laser Photocoagulation

**[[Focal laser photocoagulation]]** (typically 532 nm green or 577 nm yellow) can be applied to **extrafoveal leak points** identified on FA. This creates a controlled thermal burn at the RPE, sealing the leak point and accelerating fluid resorption.

**Limitations:**
- **Contraindicated for subfoveal or juxtafoveal leak points** due to risk of permanent scotoma
- Risk of [[choroidal neovascularization]] at the laser scar
- Risk of progressive scar enlargement
- Does not address the underlying choroidal pathology
- Largely supplanted by PDT and SML in modern practice

### Pharmacological Interventions

#### Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists (MRAs)

Based on the role of the [[mineralocorticoid receptor]] pathway in CSC pathogenesis, **MRAs** have emerged as a promising oral pharmacological treatment.

- **[[Eplerenone]]** (Inspra®): A selective MRA; typical dose 25–50 mg daily for 1–3 months
  - The **VICI trial** (2020), a large multicenter RCT, found that eplerenone was **not superior to placebo** for chronic CSC at 12 months in terms of BCVA improvement, tempering initial enthusiasm. However, some subgroups may benefit, and the drug showed a favorable safety profile.
  - Requires monitoring of serum [[potassium]] and [[renal function]] (risk of [[hyperkalemia]])
  - Contraindicated in patients with significant renal impairment or concomitant potassium-sparing diuretic use

- **[[Spironolactone]]**: A non-selective MRA; typical dose 25–50 mg daily
  - Similar mechanism to eplerenone but with additional anti-androgenic effects (risk of [[gynecomastia]], menstrual irregularities)
  - Some small studies have shown benefit in reducing SRF

#### Anti-VEGF Therapy

**[[Anti-VEGF]]** agents (e.g., [[ranibizumab]], [[bevacizumab]], [[aflibercept]]) are **not effective as primary treatment for CSC** without associated CNV, as CSC is not primarily a VEGF-driven disease. However, anti-VEGF therapy is indicated when:

- **Secondary type 1 CNV** complicates chronic CSC
- Combined with PDT for CNV associated with CSC

#### Other Pharmacological Agents Under Investigation

- **[[Rifampin]]** (rifampicin): An anti-tuberculosis agent that enhances hepatic metabolism of endogenous cortisol via cytochrome P450 induction. Some small studies have shown benefit, but evidence is limited.
- **[[Mifepristone]]** (RU-486): A glucocorticoid receptor antagonist; limited evidence
- **[[Ketoconazole]]**: Inhibits adrenal steroidogenesis; limited evidence
- **[[Finasteride]]**: A 5-alpha reductase inhibitor; investigated based on the hormonal hypothesis
- **[[Melatonin]]**: Investigated for its potential role in modulating cortisol levels
- **[[Aspirin]]** (low-dose): Some retrospective data suggest a possible protective effect

### Treatment of Secondary Choroidal Neovascularization

Chronic CSC may be complicated by the development of **type 1 (sub-RPE) [[choroidal neovascularization]]**, which can be subtle and difficult to detect. OCTA has significantly improved detection rates. Treatment options include:

- **Intravitreal anti-VEGF injections** (first-line for CNV)
- **Combination therapy**: Anti-VEGF + half-dose PDT
- **PDT alone** (if anti-VEGF response is inadequate)

## Prognosis and Visual Outcomes

### Acute CSC
- **Spontaneous resolution** in 80–90% of cases within 3–6 months
- Final BCVA is typically **20/20 to 20/30** after resolution
- Despite good Snellen acuity, patients may have persistent subtle visual complaints including:
  - Reduced contrast sensitivity
  - Mild metamorphopsia
  - Relative scotoma
  - Impaired color vision
  - Prolonged photostress recovery
- **Recurrence rate**: 30–50% within the first year; up to 50% over long-term follow-up

### Chronic CSC
- **Poorer visual prognosis** due to progressive RPE atrophy and photoreceptor degeneration
- BCVA may decline to **20/40 to 20/200** or worse in severe cases
- Risk of secondary CNV: estimated at **2–9%** in chronic CSC
- Bilateral involvement is more common
- May lead to **[[subretinal fibrosis]]** and permanent macular scarring

### Prognostic Factors

| Favorable Prognostic Factors | Unfavorable Prognostic Factors |
|---|---|
| Young age | Older age at onset |
| First episode | Multiple recurrences |
| Short duration of SRF | Prolonged SRF (> 4 months) |
| Intact ellipsoid zone on OCT | Disrupted EZ and ELM on OCT |
| Focal FA leak | Diffuse RPE atrophy |
| Prompt corticosteroid cessation | Ongoing corticosteroid exposure |
| Unilateral disease | Bilateral involvement |

## Special Considerations

### CSC in Pregnancy

CSC occurring during [[pregnancy]] (typically third trimester) is generally self-limiting and resolves spontaneously after delivery. Active intervention is rarely required. PDT and FA are relatively contraindicated during pregnancy. OCT monitoring is the preferred diagnostic and follow-up modality.

### CSC and the Pachychoroid Disease Spectrum

CSC is increasingly understood as one manifestation of the broader **[[pachychoroid]] disease spectrum**. The progression model suggests:

1. **Pachychoroid** (subclinical thickened choroid) →
2. **[[Pachychoroid pigment epitheliopathy]]** (PPE; RPE changes without SRF) →
3. **CSC** (SRF accumulation) →
4. **[[Pachychoroid neovasculopathy]]** (PNV; type 1 CNV development) →
5. **[[Polypoidal choroidal vasculopathy]]** (PCV; aneurysmal type 1 CNV)

This spectrum concept has important implications for long-term monitoring of CSC patients, who may be at risk for progression to neovascular complications.

### Bilateral and Subclinical Disease

Even in patients presenting with unilateral symptoms, the fellow eye frequently shows subclinical abnormalities on multimodal imaging, including:
- Increased choroidal thickness
- Choroidal hyperpermeability on ICGA
- RPE irregularities on FAF
- Prior subclinical episodes evidenced by RPE window defects on FA

## Summary of Management Algorithm

```
Acute CSC (< 3–4 months)
├── Discontinue corticosteroids
├── Address risk factors (stress, OSA, etc.)
├── Observation with serial OCT (every 4–6 weeks)
├── If resolved → Monitor for recurrence
└── If persistent (> 3–4 months) → Treat as chronic CSC

Chronic CSC (> 3–4 months) or Recurrent CSC
├── Half-dose PDT (preferred first-line for chronic CSC)
├── Subthreshold micropulse laser (alternative)
├── Oral MRA (eplerenone 25–50 mg) — consider as adjunct
├── Focal laser (extrafoveal leaks only)
└── If secondary CNV detected → Anti-VEGF ± PDT

Ongoing Management
├── Serial OCT and multimodal imaging
├── Amsler grid self-monitoring
├── Lifelong avoidance of corticosteroids when possible
└── Screen for pachychoroid spectrum progression
```
