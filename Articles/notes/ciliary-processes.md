

# Ciliary Processes

## Overview

The **ciliary processes** (Latin: *processus ciliares*) are approximately 70–80 finger-like or ridge-like projections that extend inward from the inner surface of the [[ciliary body]], specifically from its anterior portion known as the [[pars plicata]]. These highly vascularized and epithelium-covered structures represent one of the most physiologically critical microanatomical components of the [[uveal tract]], serving as the principal site of [[aqueous humor]] production in the [[human eye]]. Each ciliary process measures approximately 0.5–1.0 mm in height, 2.0 mm in length, and 0.5 mm in width, and collectively they form a circumferential crown (the *corona ciliaris*) posterior to the [[iris]] root and anterior to the [[pars plana]].

Understanding the anatomy, physiology, and pathology of the ciliary processes is indispensable for clinicians in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]], as dysfunction or structural alteration of these processes directly impacts [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP), [[accommodation]], and the metabolic homeostasis of avascular structures such as the [[cornea]] and [[crystalline lens]]. Pharmacological manipulation of ciliary process function forms the cornerstone of medical management of [[glaucoma]], the leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide.

---

## Embryology

The ciliary processes develop from a complex interaction between [[neural ectoderm]] and [[mesoderm]] (with contributions from [[neural crest]] cells) during the embryonic and fetal periods. The two epithelial layers—the [[pigmented ciliary epithelium]] (PE) and the [[non-pigmented ciliary epithelium]] (NPE)—are derived from the anterior rim of the [[optic cup]], which is of neuroectodermal origin. The PE is continuous with the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE), while the NPE is continuous with the [[neurosensory retina]].

The stromal core of each ciliary process, including its vasculature and connective tissue, is derived from [[neural crest]]-derived [[mesenchyme]]. Differentiation of the ciliary processes begins around the **third month of gestation** and continues postnatally, with the processes becoming more prominent and folded as the eye matures. The [[zonular fibers]] ([[Zonules of Zinn]]) that suspend the [[crystalline lens]] originate from the valleys between the ciliary processes and from the [[pars plana]] epithelium.

## Gross and Microscopic Anatomy

### Macroscopic Structure

The ciliary processes are arranged in a radial, crown-like configuration around the posterior chamber of the eye. They are divided into:

- **Major processes (plicae):** Approximately 70–80 large ridges, each about 2 mm long and 0.8–1.0 mm high.
- **Minor processes (pliculae):** Smaller, less prominent folds interspersed between the major processes.

The processes project into the [[posterior chamber]], and their tips are closely related to the [[lens equator]]. The [[zonular fibers]] attach in the valleys between the processes and along the [[pars plana]], transmitting the force of [[ciliary muscle]] contraction to the lens during [[accommodation]].

### Microscopic Structure

Each ciliary process consists of three principal layers:

| Layer | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Stromal core** | Highly vascularized connective tissue containing fenestrated capillaries, fibroblasts, collagen, and ground substance. The capillaries are derived from branches of the [[long posterior ciliary arteries]] and the [[anterior ciliary arteries]], forming the **major arterial circle of the iris** ([[Circle of Zinn-Haller]] at the ciliary body level). |
| **Pigmented ciliary epithelium (PE)** | The outer layer (facing the stroma), composed of cuboidal cells containing [[melanin]] granules. Continuous posteriorly with the [[retinal pigment epithelium]]. Basal surface faces the stroma; apical surface faces the NPE. |
| **Non-pigmented ciliary epithelium (NPE)** | The inner layer (facing the posterior chamber), composed of columnar-to-cuboidal cells that lack melanin. Continuous posteriorly with the [[neurosensory retina]]. The apical surfaces of the PE and NPE are apposed to each other, connected by [[gap junctions]] and [[desmosomes]]. The basal surface of the NPE faces the posterior chamber and is covered by the [[internal limiting membrane]] (ILM) of the ciliary body, from which zonular fibers originate. |

The unique **apex-to-apex** orientation of the bilayered epithelium is a hallmark of the ciliary processes and reflects their embryological derivation from the two layers of the optic cup. This arrangement is critical for the directional transport of ions and water from the stroma into the posterior chamber.

### Vascular Architecture

The ciliary processes possess one of the highest rates of blood flow per unit tissue weight of any structure in the body. The vascular supply is derived from:

1. **[[Long posterior ciliary arteries]]** (two, medial and lateral) — enter the eye through the sclera near the optic nerve and travel anteriorly within the suprachoroidal space.
2. **[[Anterior ciliary arteries]]** (seven, from the rectus muscles) — penetrate the sclera near the limbus.

These vessels anastomose to form the **[[major arterial circle of the iris]]** (circulus arteriosus iridis major), located in the ciliary body near the iris root. From this circle, arteriolar branches enter each ciliary process, forming an extensive capillary network. The capillaries within the ciliary processes are **fenestrated**, which is essential for the ultrafiltration step of aqueous humor formation. Venous drainage occurs via the [[vortex veins]] and [[anterior ciliary veins]].

### Innervation

The ciliary processes receive innervation from:

- **Parasympathetic fibers** via the [[short ciliary nerves]] (from the [[ciliary ganglion]], originating from [[cranial nerve III]]): primarily innervate the [[ciliary muscle]] but also influence blood flow.
- **Sympathetic fibers** from the [[superior cervical ganglion]] via the [[long ciliary nerves]] and the sympathetic root of the ciliary ganglion: regulate blood flow and directly influence aqueous humor secretion via β2-adrenergic receptors on the NPE.
- **Sensory fibers** from the [[nasociliary nerve]] (branch of [[cranial nerve V1]], the ophthalmic division of the [[trigeminal nerve]]).

## Physiology: Aqueous Humor Production

The primary physiological function of the ciliary processes is the **production of [[aqueous humor]]**, a clear, optically transparent fluid that fills the [[anterior chamber]] and [[posterior chamber]] of the eye. The normal rate of aqueous humor production is approximately **2.0–2.5 µL/min** (with a total volume of ~250 µL in the anterior chamber), and it exhibits a diurnal variation, being highest in the morning and lowest during sleep.

### Mechanisms of Aqueous Humor Formation

Aqueous humor formation involves three sequential processes:

| Mechanism | Description | Relative Contribution |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Ultrafiltration** | Passive movement of plasma through the fenestrated capillaries of the ciliary process stroma into the extracellular space, driven by hydrostatic pressure. The fenestrations allow passage of large molecules, but the tight junctions of the NPE (the [[blood-aqueous barrier]]) restrict further passive movement. | ~20% |
| **Diffusion** | Passive movement of lipid-soluble substances (e.g., O₂, CO₂) across cell membranes along concentration gradients. | Minor |
| **Active secretion** | Energy-dependent, enzyme-mediated transport of ions (primarily Na⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻) across the NPE into the posterior chamber, creating an osmotic gradient that draws water. This is the dominant mechanism and is dependent on [[Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase]] and [[carbonic anhydrase]] (isoenzyme II and IV) located in the NPE cells. | ~80% |

### Key Enzymes and Transporters

- **[[Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase]]**: Located on the basolateral (posterior chamber-facing) membrane of the NPE. Pumps 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in, creating an electrochemical gradient that drives water movement.
- **[[Carbonic anhydrase]] (CA-II and CA-IV)**: Catalyzes the reaction CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. The bicarbonate ion is transported into the posterior chamber, contributing to the osmotic gradient. This enzyme is the target of [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]] such as [[dorzolamide]] and [[acetazolamide]].
- **Aquaporins**: [[Aquaporin-1]] (AQP1) and [[Aquaporin-4]] (AQP4) are water channel proteins expressed in the NPE and PE, respectively, facilitating transcellular water transport.
- **Cl⁻ channels and Na⁺/K⁺/2Cl⁻ cotransporters**: Located on the PE (stromal side), these facilitate Cl⁻ uptake from the stroma, which is then passed to the NPE via gap junctions and secreted into the posterior chamber.

### The Blood-Aqueous Barrier (BAB)

The [[blood-aqueous barrier]] at the level of the ciliary processes is formed by the **tight junctions (zonulae occludentes)** between adjacent NPE cells. This barrier restricts the paracellular movement of plasma proteins and large molecules from the stromal ultrafiltrate into the aqueous humor, maintaining the aqueous as a protein-poor fluid (protein concentration ~0.02 g/dL compared to ~7 g/dL in plasma). Disruption of this barrier, as occurs in [[anterior uveitis]] or surgical trauma, leads to **[[flare]]** (protein leakage) and **[[cells]]** (inflammatory cell infiltration) visible on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]].

### Composition of Aqueous Humor

| Component | Concentration (Aqueous) | Concentration (Plasma) | Notes |
|-----------|------------------------|----------------------|-------|
| Na⁺ | ~144 mEq/L | ~140 mEq/L | Slightly hypertonic |
| K⁺ | ~4.5 mEq/L | ~4.5 mEq/L | Similar |
| Cl⁻ | ~110 mEq/L | ~103 mEq/L | Higher in aqueous |
| HCO₃⁻ | ~20 mEq/L | ~24 mEq/L | Slightly lower |
| Ascorbate | ~1.06 mM | ~0.04 mM | 15–25× higher; antioxidant role |
| Glucose | ~2.7–3.9 mM | ~5.6 mM | Lower; consumed by lens and cornea |
| Protein | ~0.02 g/dL | ~7 g/dL | 350× lower; maintained by BAB |
| Lactate | ~4.5 mM | ~0.9 mM | Higher; anaerobic metabolism of lens |

## Pathophysiology

Pathological conditions involving the ciliary processes can be broadly categorized into those affecting aqueous humor dynamics, structural abnormalities, inflammatory conditions, and neoplastic processes.

### 1. Hypersecretion and Hyposecretion of Aqueous Humor

- **Hypersecretion glaucoma**: An exceedingly rare condition in which excessive aqueous production by the ciliary processes leads to elevated [[intraocular pressure]]. This must be differentiated from outflow obstruction, which accounts for the vast majority of glaucoma cases.
- **Hyposecretion / Ciliary body shutdown**: Occurs in severe [[anterior uveitis]], [[cyclitic membrane]] formation, [[phthisis bulbi]], or following [[cyclodestructive procedures]]. Reduced aqueous production leads to [[ocular hypotony]] (IOP < 5 mmHg), which can cause [[maculopathy]], [[choroidal folds]], [[optic disc edema]], and ultimately [[phthisis bulbi]].

### 2. Inflammation: Ciliary Process Involvement in Uveitis

In [[intermediate uveitis]] and [[panuveitis]], the ciliary processes may become directly inflamed. Inflammatory mediators (prostaglandins, cytokines) disrupt the [[blood-aqueous barrier]], leading to:

- Increased protein in the aqueous (flare)
- Inflammatory cell infiltration
- Formation of [[cyclitic membranes]] (fibrovascular membranes spanning the posterior chamber, potentially causing [[tractional retinal detachment]])
- [[Hypotony]] due to reduced aqueous secretion by damaged NPE cells

### 3. Ciliary Process Cysts and Tumors

| Condition | Description | Clinical Significance |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| **[[Ciliary body cysts]]** (epithelial cysts) | Benign, fluid-filled cysts arising from the PE or NPE of the ciliary processes. Common, often incidental findings on [[ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM). | Usually asymptomatic. May cause angle narrowing or [[plateau iris configuration]] if large or multiple. |
| **[[Ciliary body melanoma]]** | Malignant melanocytic tumor arising from the uveal stroma of the ciliary body, including the processes. | Most common primary malignant intraocular tumor in adults (uveal melanoma). May present with lens subluxation, sector cataract, sentinel vessel, or secondary glaucoma. |
| **[[Medulloepithelioma]] (diktyoma)** | Rare embryonal tumor arising from the NPE of the ciliary processes. Most common in children. | Can be benign or malignant. Presents with leukocoria, lens notching, or a visible mass. |
| **[[Ciliary body adenoma]] / [[Adenocarcinoma]]** | Rare tumors of the NPE or PE. | May mimic melanoma clinically. Diagnosed by histopathology. |
| **[[Metastatic tumors]]** | Metastases to the ciliary body from breast, lung, or other primary sites. | Rare compared to choroidal metastases. |

### 4. Plateau Iris Syndrome / Configuration

In [[plateau iris configuration]], the ciliary processes are anteriorly positioned and/or enlarged, pushing the peripheral iris forward against the [[trabecular meshwork]], predisposing to [[angle-closure glaucoma]]. This is a critical anatomical variant diagnosed by [[ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM) or [[anterior segment optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT). Unlike [[pupillary block]], the anterior chamber depth may be relatively normal centrally, but the angle is occluded by the anteriorly rotated ciliary processes.

### 5. Ciliary Process Detachment and Effusion

[[Ciliary body detachment]] (cyclodialysis or supraciliary effusion) can occur following trauma, surgery, or in association with [[uveal effusion syndrome]]. Detachment of the ciliary processes from the scleral spur disrupts aqueous production and outflow, leading to profound [[hypotony]].

## Symptoms

Pathology of the ciliary processes rarely produces symptoms directly attributable to the processes themselves. Instead, symptoms arise from the downstream consequences of ciliary process dysfunction:

| Symptom | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| **Blurred vision** | Altered aqueous humor composition, hypotony maculopathy, lens subluxation from tumor, or secondary cataract |
| **Eye pain / aching** | Elevated IOP (glaucoma), inflammation (uveitis), or tumor expansion |
| **Photophobia** | Anterior uveitis with ciliary process involvement; breakdown of BAB |
| **Floaters** | Inflammatory cells or pigment dispersion into the vitreous |
| **Visual field loss** | Secondary glaucoma from angle closure (plateau iris) or tumor-related mechanisms |
| **Leukocoria** | Medulloepithelioma or cyclitic membrane in children |
| **Asymptomatic** | Many ciliary body cysts and early tumors are discovered incidentally |

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: Assessment of anterior chamber depth, flare, cells, and any visible mass behind the iris. [[Gonioscopy]] is essential to evaluate the [[iridocorneal angle]] and detect plateau iris configuration, angle closure, or tumor extension.
- **[[Dilated fundus examination]]**: May reveal a ciliary body mass extending posteriorly, or signs of hypotony (disc edema, choroidal folds).
- **[[Transillumination]]**: A focused light source placed on the sclera can reveal a shadow cast by a ciliary body mass (positive transillumination defect is absent over a solid tumor).

### Imaging Modalities

| Modality | Application in Ciliary Process Evaluation |
|----------|------------------------------------------|
| **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | Gold standard for imaging the ciliary processes. Uses high-frequency ultrasound (35–50 MHz) to provide cross-sectional images of the ciliary body, processes, and posterior chamber with ~50 µm resolution. Essential for diagnosing plateau iris, ciliary body cysts, tumors, cyclodialysis, and ciliary body detachment. |
| **[[Anterior segment OCT]] (AS-OCT)** | Non-contact imaging of the anterior segment. Useful for angle assessment and detecting plateau iris configuration, though penetration behind the iris is limited compared to UBM. Swept-source OCT has improved depth penetration. |
| **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** | Standard ophthalmic ultrasound (10 MHz) for detecting larger ciliary body masses, retinal detachment, and vitreous opacities. Lower resolution than UBM for ciliary process detail. |
| **[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA)** | Can demonstrate leakage from ciliary processes in cases of BAB breakdown (e.g., uveitis). Rarely used specifically for ciliary process evaluation. |
| **[[Indocyanine green angiography]] (ICGA)** | Better penetration through pigmented tissues; can visualize ciliary body vasculature and tumor vascularity. |
| **[[MRI]] of the orbit** | Useful for characterizing ciliary body tumors (melanoma vs. metastasis vs. other). Melanoma typically shows hyperintensity on T1 and hypointensity on T2 due to melanin content. |
| **[[CT scan]]** | Less useful than MRI for soft tissue characterization but may detect calcification (e.g., in medulloepithelioma). |
| **[[Optical coherence tomography angiography]] (OCTA)** | Emerging role in evaluating anterior segment vasculature, including ciliary body tumors. |

### Laboratory and Ancillary Testing

- **[[Aqueous humor analysis]]**: Paracentesis with cytology, flow cytometry, or PCR may be performed in cases of suspected intraocular lymphoma, infection, or to differentiate inflammatory from neoplastic processes.
- **[[Fine-needle aspiration biopsy]] (FNAB)**: May be performed for cytological diagnosis of ciliary body tumors when clinical and imaging findings are equivocal.
- **IOP measurement ([[tonometry]])**: Essential in all cases. Elevated IOP suggests secondary glaucoma; low IOP suggests hyposecretion or cyclodialysis.

## Treatment and Management

Management of ciliary process pathology depends on the underlying condition and its impact on intraocular pressure, vision, and ocular integrity.

### Pharmacological Interventions Targeting Ciliary Processes

The ciliary processes are the primary pharmacological target in the medical management of [[glaucoma]] and [[ocular hypertension]]. Drugs that reduce aqueous humor production by acting on the ciliary processes include:

| Drug Class | Mechanism of Action | Examples | IOP Reduction |
|------------|-------------------|----------|---------------|
| **[[Beta-adrenergic antagonists]] (β-blockers)** | Block β2-adrenergic receptors on the NPE, reducing cAMP-mediated active secretion of aqueous humor. | [[Timolol]], [[betaxolol]], [[levobunolol]], [[carteolol]] | 20–27% |
| **[[Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]] (CAIs)** | Inhibit carbonic anhydrase (CA-II and CA-IV) in the NPE, reducing HCO₃⁻ and Na⁺ transport, thereby decreasing aqueous production. | Topical: [[dorzolamide]], [[brinzolamide]]; Systemic: [[acetazolamide]], [[methazolamide]] | 15–20% (topical); 25–30% (systemic) |
| **[[Alpha-2 adrenergic agonists]]** | Stimulate α2-receptors on the NPE, reducing cAMP and decreasing aqueous secretion. Also increase uveoscleral outflow. | [[Brimonidine]], [[apraclonidine]] | 20–27% |
| **Combination agents** | Fixed-dose combinations targeting ciliary process secretion and/or outflow. | [[Dorzolamide/timolol]] (Cosopt), [[brimonidine/timolol]] (Combigan), [[brinzolamide/brimonidine]] (Simbrinza) | Additive effects |

> **Note:** [[Prostaglandin analogs]] (e.g., [[latanoprost]], [[travoprost]], [[bimatoprost]], [[tafluprost]]) primarily reduce IOP by increasing [[uveoscleral outflow]] rather than by acting directly on the ciliary processes, though they may have minor effects on ciliary body permeability.

### Management of Inflammatory Conditions

When ciliary processes are involved in [[uveitis]]:

- **Topical [[corticosteroids]]** (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate]] 1%, [[difluprednate]] 0.05%): Reduce inflammation and restore BAB integrity.
- **[[Cycloplegic agents]]** (e.g., [[atropine]] 1%, [[cyclopentolate]] 1%): Relax the [[ciliary muscle]], reduce pain from ciliary spasm, prevent [[posterior synechiae]], and stabilize the BAB.
- **Systemic immunosuppression**: In severe or recurrent cases (e.g., [[sarcoidosis]], [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]]), agents such as [[methotrexate]], [[mycophenolate mofetil]], or [[adalimumab]] may be required.

### Surgical and Procedural Interventions

#### Cyclodestructive Procedures

These procedures intentionally destroy ciliary process tissue to reduce aqueous humor production and are reserved for **refractory glaucoma** that has failed medical and conventional surgical therapy:

| Procedure | Technique | Indications |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| **[[Transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]] (TSCPC)** | Diode laser (810 nm) applied through the sclera to ablate ciliary processes. Can be performed in continuous-wave or micropulse mode. | Refractory glaucoma, neovascular glaucoma, painful blind eyes |
| **[[Micropulse transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]] (MP-TSCPC)** | Subthreshold, repetitive short pulses of diode laser energy that reduce aqueous production with less tissue destruction than continuous-wave TSCPC. | Refractory glaucoma with better visual potential; increasingly used earlier in treatment algorithm |
| **[[Endoscopic cyclophotocoagulation]] (ECP)** | Direct visualization and laser ablation of ciliary processes using an intraocular endoscope. Often combined with [[cataract surgery]]. | Moderate-to-advanced glaucoma, often combined with phacoemulsification |
| **[[Cyclocryotherapy]]** | Cryoprobe applied to the sclera overlying the ciliary body to freeze and destroy ciliary processes. Largely replaced by laser techniques due to higher complication rates. | Rarely used; historical significance |

**Complications of cyclodestructive procedures** include [[hypotony]], [[phthisis bulbi]], [[sympathetic ophthalmia]] (rare), chronic inflammation, [[hyphema]], and vision loss.

#### Tumor Management

- **[[Plaque brachytherapy]]** (e.g., iodine-125, ruthenium-106): Radioactive plaque sutured to the sclera overlying the ciliary body tumor. Standard treatment for small-to-medium [[ciliary body melanoma]].
- **[[Proton beam radiotherapy]]**: External beam charged-particle radiation for ciliary body tumors, particularly when plaque brachytherapy is not feasible.
- **[[Local resection]] (iridocyclectomy / partial lamellar sclerouvectomy)**: Surgical excision of the tumor with surrounding tissue. Reserved for select cases.
- **[[Enucleation]]**: Removal of the eye. Indicated for large tumors, tumors with extraocular extension, or when vision is unsalvageable.
- **Observation**: Small, indeterminate lesions may be monitored with serial UBM, photography, and ultrasound.

#### Management of Plateau Iris

- **[[Laser peripheral iridotomy]] (LPI)**: First-line treatment to eliminate any pupillary block component.
- **[[Argon laser peripheral iridoplasty]] (ALPI)**: Contracts the peripheral iris stroma, pulling it away from the angle. Specifically useful when the ciliary processes are pushing the iris forward (plateau iris mechanism).
- **[[Lens extraction]]**: In older patients, removal of the crystalline lens can deepen the anterior chamber and relieve the mechanical effect of anteriorly positioned ciliary processes.

#### Management of Ciliary Body Cysts

- Most ciliary body cysts are benign and require **observation** only.
- Symptomatic or angle-occluding cysts may be treated with **[[Nd:YAG laser]] cystotomy** or **[[argon laser]] photocoagulation** to collapse the cyst.
- Rarely, surgical excision is necessary.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of conditions involving the ciliary processes varies widely:

- **Glaucoma managed with aqueous suppressants**: Generally good with appropriate medication adherence and monitoring.
- **Plateau iris syndrome**: Good prognosis with early diagnosis and appropriate laser/surgical intervention.
- **Ciliary body cysts**: Excellent prognosis; most are benign and stable.
- **Ciliary body melanoma**: Five-year mortality rates range from 20–50% depending on tumor size, cell type, and presence of metastasis. Liver metastasis is the most common cause of death.
- **Medulloepithelioma**: Variable; benign forms have excellent prognosis after excision, while malignant forms may require enucleation.
- **Cyclodestructive procedures**: IOP control is achieved in 60–80% of cases, but repeated treatments may be necessary, and the risk of hypotony and phthisis is significant.

## Summary of Key Clinical Points

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Number** | ~70–80 major processes |
| **Location** | [[Pars plicata]] of the [[ciliary body]] |
| **Epithelium** | Bilayered: outer [[pigmented ciliary epithelium]] (PE) and inner [[non-pigmented ciliary epithelium]] (NPE), apex-to-apex |
| **Primary function** | [[Aqueous humor]] production (~2.0–2.5 µL/min) |
| **Dominant mechanism** | Active secretion (~80%) via [[Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase]] and [[carbonic anhydrase]] in NPE |
| **Barrier function** | [[Blood-aqueous barrier]] formed by tight junctions of NPE |
| **Key imaging modality** | [[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM) |
| **Pharmacological targets** | β2-receptors, carbonic anhydrase, α2-receptors on NPE |
| **Common pathology** | Ciliary body cysts, plateau iris, involvement in uveitis, melanoma |
| **Destructive procedures** | TSCPC, MP-TSCPC, ECP, cyclocryotherapy (for refractory glaucoma) |
