# Suppression (Visual)

**Suppression** (also referred to as **cortical suppression**, **visual suppression**, or **sensory suppression**) is a neuroadaptive mechanism in which the [[visual cortex]] actively inhibits the conscious perception of the image formed by one eye during [[binocular vision]]. This phenomenon represents one of the most clinically significant [[sensory adaptations]] encountered in optometric and ophthalmologic practice, serving as the brain's primary defense against [[diplopia]] (double vision) and [[visual confusion]] arising from misaligned or optically dissimilar ocular images. Suppression is not a disease per se but rather a cortical response to abnormal binocular input; however, its presence has profound implications for [[stereopsis]], [[amblyopia]] development, and the overall quality of [[binocular vision]].

Suppression is most commonly associated with [[strabismus]], [[anisometropia]], and other conditions that disrupt normal [[binocular correspondence]]. While it is predominantly a phenomenon of the developing visual system—occurring most readily during the [[critical period]] of visual development (birth to approximately 6–8 years of age)—subclinical and intermittent forms of suppression can also be identified in adults, particularly those with [[decompensated phorias]], [[intermittent exotropia]], or uncorrected [[refractive error]].

Understanding suppression is essential for every eye care practitioner, as its detection, characterization, and management form the cornerstone of [[binocular vision]] assessment and [[vision therapy]] planning.

---

## Epidemiology

The true prevalence of suppression in the general population is difficult to ascertain because it frequently exists as a subclinical finding. However, the following epidemiological observations are well established:

- Suppression is present in the vast majority (>90%) of patients with constant [[strabismus]], particularly those with onset during early childhood.
- Among patients with [[intermittent exotropia]], suppression is demonstrable during the tropic phase in approximately 60–85% of cases.
- Suppression is a near-universal finding in [[strabismic amblyopia]] and is present in a significant proportion of patients with [[anisometropic amblyopia]].
- Subclinical suppression (detectable only under dissociated testing conditions) may be present in 5–8% of the general adult population, often associated with high [[heterophoria]] or mild [[anisometropia]].
- The incidence of clinically significant suppression is higher in younger populations due to the greater neuroplasticity of the developing [[visual cortex]].

| Population | Estimated Prevalence of Suppression |
|---|---|
| Constant [[strabismus]] (childhood onset) | >90% |
| [[Intermittent exotropia]] (during tropic phase) | 60–85% |
| [[Strabismic amblyopia]] | ~95% |
| [[Anisometropic amblyopia]] | 50–70% |
| General adult population (subclinical) | 5–8% |
| [[Convergence insufficiency]] | 15–30% (intermittent) |

## Historical Background

The concept of suppression has been recognized since the early investigations of [[binocular vision]] in the 19th century. **Charles Wheatstone**, who invented the [[stereoscope]] in 1838, was among the first to observe that when dissimilar images were presented to the two eyes, one image could be perceptually eliminated. **Helmholtz** further elaborated on the phenomenon of [[binocular rivalry]], describing the alternating dominance of one eye's image over the other when incompatible stimuli were presented.

In the clinical realm, **Louis Émile Javal** (1896) and later **Claude Worth** (early 1900s) made seminal contributions to the understanding and clinical detection of suppression. Worth's classification of binocular vision into three grades—**simultaneous perception**, **fusion**, and **stereopsis**—provided a framework in which suppression could be understood as a failure at the most fundamental level of binocular integration. Worth's development of the [[Worth 4-Dot test]] remains one of the most widely used clinical tools for suppression detection to this day.

The 20th century brought neurophysiological insights, particularly through the work of **David Hubel** and **Torsten Wiesel**, whose Nobel Prize–winning research on [[ocular dominance columns]] and the [[critical period]] of visual development provided the neural substrate for understanding how suppression develops and becomes entrenched. Their monocular deprivation experiments in kittens demonstrated that disruption of binocular input during the critical period leads to permanent reorganization of cortical connections—a finding directly relevant to the pathophysiology of suppression and [[amblyopia]].

## Classification

Suppression can be classified along several dimensions, each of which carries clinical significance for diagnosis and management.

### By Depth

| Classification | Description |
|---|---|
| **Superficial (light) suppression** | Easily disrupted; patient can achieve [[binocular vision]] with minimal intervention or under favorable conditions |
| **Moderate suppression** | Requires significant effort or therapeutic intervention to overcome |
| **Deep (dense) suppression** | Resistant to disruption; often associated with constant [[strabismus]] and [[amblyopia]] |

### By Extent (Spatial)

| Classification | Description |
|---|---|
| **Foveal suppression** | Suppression limited to the [[fovea]] of the deviating eye; most common in small-angle [[strabismus]] |
| **Regional suppression** | Suppression of a defined area (suppression [[scotoma]]) corresponding to the area of the deviating eye's retina that receives the image of the fixation target |
| **Hemiretinal suppression** | Suppression of the nasal or temporal [[hemiretina]]; commonly seen in [[exotropia]] (temporal hemiretinal suppression of the deviating eye) and [[esotropia]] (nasal hemiretinal suppression) |
| **Total (global) suppression** | Complete suppression of the entire monocular image; rare, typically seen only in dense [[amblyopia]] |

### By Constancy

| Classification | Description |
|---|---|
| **Constant suppression** | Present at all times during binocular viewing; associated with constant [[strabismus]] |
| **Intermittent suppression** | Present only under certain conditions (e.g., fatigue, specific gaze positions, distance vs. near); associated with [[intermittent strabismus]] or decompensated [[phoria]] |
| **Facultative suppression** | Occurs only when the deviation is manifest; binocular vision is normal when eyes are aligned |

### By Etiology

| Classification | Description |
|---|---|
| **Strabismic suppression** | Secondary to ocular misalignment |
| **Anisometropic suppression** | Secondary to unequal refractive error causing unequal image clarity |
| **Stimulus deprivation suppression** | Secondary to media opacity or other causes of degraded retinal image |
| **Rivalry suppression** | Normal physiological phenomenon occurring when incompatible images are presented to the two eyes |

## Pathophysiology

### Neural Basis of Normal Binocular Vision

Under normal conditions, the images formed on the [[retina]] of each eye are transmitted via the [[optic nerve]], [[optic chiasm]], and [[optic tract]] to the [[lateral geniculate nucleus]] (LGN) of the [[thalamus]], and subsequently to the [[primary visual cortex]] (area [[V1]], Brodmann area 17) in the [[occipital lobe]]. In V1, inputs from the two eyes converge on [[binocular neurons]]—cells that receive excitatory input from corresponding retinal points in both eyes. These binocular neurons are organized within [[ocular dominance columns]] and are essential for the computation of [[retinal disparity]], which underlies [[stereopsis]].

Normal binocular vision requires:
1. **Clear retinal images** in both eyes (adequate [[visual acuity]])
2. **Proper ocular alignment** ([[orthophoria]] or well-compensated [[heterophoria]])
3. **Comparable image size** (absence of significant [[aniseikonia]])
4. **Normal cortical integration** (intact binocular neurons and [[fusion]] mechanisms)

### Mechanism of Suppression

When any of the above prerequisites are violated, the brain receives conflicting binocular input. This creates two potential perceptual disturbances:

1. **[[Diplopia]]** — The same object is perceived at two different spatial locations because its image falls on non-corresponding retinal points.
2. **[[Visual confusion]]** — Two different objects are perceived at the same spatial location because different images fall on corresponding retinal points (particularly the two [[fovea]]e).

Suppression is the cortical mechanism by which one of these conflicting inputs is actively inhibited to eliminate diplopia and confusion. The process involves **active inhibition** mediated by GABAergic ([[gamma-aminobutyric acid]]) interneurons within the [[visual cortex]]. Specifically:

- **Interocular inhibition** occurs at the level of [[V1]] and possibly higher cortical areas (e.g., [[V2]], [[V3]], and extrastriate cortex).
- The suppressed eye's signal is attenuated or gated before it reaches conscious perception, while the fixating eye's signal is preferentially processed.
- This inhibition is **active**, not passive—it requires ongoing cortical energy expenditure and can be modulated by attention, stimulus characteristics, and therapeutic interventions.

### The Role of the Critical Period

The [[critical period]] of visual development (approximately birth to 6–8 years, with a sensitive period extending to the early teens) is the window during which the visual cortex exhibits maximal [[neuroplasticity]]. During this period:

- Abnormal binocular input (from strabismus, anisometropia, or deprivation) leads to **competitive reorganization** of [[ocular dominance columns]].
- The cortical territory devoted to the suppressed eye shrinks, while that of the fixating (dominant) eye expands.
- If suppression is prolonged and uninterrupted, the cortical changes become increasingly difficult to reverse, leading to [[amblyopia]]—a permanent reduction in [[best-corrected visual acuity]] in the suppressed eye.
- After the critical period closes, the capacity for both suppression development and its reversal diminishes significantly, though recent research suggests that some degree of cortical plasticity persists into adulthood.

### Suppression Scotoma

In strabismic suppression, the area of the deviating eye's retina that is suppressed is not random but corresponds to specific functional zones:

- **Foveal suppression scotoma**: The [[fovea]] of the deviating eye is suppressed to eliminate [[visual confusion]] (since both foveae would otherwise perceive different objects at the same spatial location).
- **Diplopia point suppression scotoma**: The area of the deviating eye's retina on which the fixation target's image falls (the point that would cause diplopia) is also suppressed.

In [[esotropia]], the suppression scotoma typically involves the **nasal retina** of the deviating eye (corresponding to the temporal visual field). In [[exotropia]], the **temporal retina** of the deviating eye is suppressed (corresponding to the nasal visual field). These patterns reflect the direction of the deviation and the specific retinal areas receiving conflicting input.

### Neurochemical Substrates

Contemporary neuroscience research has identified several key neurochemical pathways involved in suppression:

| Neurotransmitter/Pathway | Role in Suppression |
|---|---|
| **[[GABA]]** (gamma-aminobutyric acid) | Primary inhibitory neurotransmitter mediating interocular suppression in [[V1]]; GABAergic interneurons actively inhibit input from the suppressed eye |
| **[[Glutamate]]** | Excitatory neurotransmitter; imbalance between glutamatergic excitation and GABAergic inhibition contributes to suppression |
| **[[Acetylcholine]]** | Modulates cortical plasticity; cholinergic mechanisms may influence the depth and reversibility of suppression |
| **[[Brain-derived neurotrophic factor]] (BDNF)** | Involved in experience-dependent plasticity; reduced BDNF signaling in the suppressed eye's cortical territory may contribute to amblyopia development |
| **[[Dopamine]]** | Retinal and cortical dopamine pathways may modulate suppression; levodopa has been investigated as an adjunct to amblyopia treatment |

### Binocular Rivalry vs. Clinical Suppression

It is important to distinguish **[[binocular rivalry]]**—a normal physiological phenomenon—from **clinical suppression**:

| Feature | Binocular Rivalry | Clinical Suppression |
|---|---|---|
| Occurrence | Normal individuals viewing incompatible stimuli | Patients with strabismus, anisometropia, or amblyopia |
| Alternation | Rapid, involuntary alternation between eyes | Stable, consistent suppression of one eye |
| Reversibility | Immediate upon removal of conflicting stimuli | May persist even after correction of underlying cause |
| Neural mechanism | Competitive inhibition between equally matched inputs | Asymmetric inhibition favoring the dominant/fixating eye |
| Clinical significance | None (normal phenomenon) | Indicates disrupted binocular vision; risk factor for amblyopia |

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Symptoms

Suppression itself is, by definition, an **asymptomatic** adaptation—the patient does not perceive the suppressed image and therefore does not experience diplopia or confusion. This is precisely what makes suppression clinically insidious: the patient is unaware of the loss of binocular input. However, the conditions associated with suppression and the consequences of suppression may produce the following symptoms and signs:

**In children:**
- Absence of [[diplopia]] despite manifest [[strabismus]] (a key clinical clue)
- Reduced or absent [[stereopsis]] (difficulty with depth perception tasks)
- Clumsiness, difficulty with hand-eye coordination, or avoidance of activities requiring depth judgment
- Head turn or tilt (if suppression is associated with an [[incomitant strabismus]] or [[anomalous head posture]])
- Closing or covering one eye in bright light (particularly in [[intermittent exotropia]])
- Poor academic performance (if associated with [[amblyopia]] or binocular vision dysfunction)

**In adults (with acquired or decompensated suppression):**
- Intermittent [[diplopia]] (when suppression breaks down)
- Asthenopia ([[eye strain]]), headaches, or difficulty with prolonged near work
- Difficulty with tasks requiring [[stereopsis]] (e.g., driving, sports, threading a needle)
- Visual fatigue or discomfort in environments with complex visual stimuli

### Signs

| Clinical Sign | Description |
|---|---|
| Absence of diplopia in the presence of [[strabismus]] | The hallmark of suppression; indicates active cortical inhibition |
| Reduced [[stereoacuity]] | Measured with [[Titmus test]], [[Randot test]], [[TNO test]], or [[Frisby test]] |
| Failure to perceive targets on suppression-specific tests | e.g., missing dots on [[Worth 4-Dot test]], absent arm on [[Bagolini striated lenses]] |
| [[Amblyopia]] in the suppressed eye | Reduced [[best-corrected visual acuity]] (BCVA) |
| [[Anomalous retinal correspondence]] (ARC) | Cortical remapping of binocular correspondence; often coexists with suppression in strabismus |
| Reduced contrast sensitivity in the suppressed eye | Detectable with [[contrast sensitivity testing]] |

## Diagnosis

The diagnosis of suppression requires the use of **dissociation techniques** that separate the visual input to the two eyes while maintaining binocular viewing conditions. A comprehensive suppression evaluation should characterize the **presence**, **depth**, **extent**, **constancy**, and **eye** involved.

### Clinical Tests for Suppression

#### 1. Worth 4-Dot Test (W4D)

The [[Worth 4-Dot test]] is one of the most widely used clinical tests for suppression. The patient wears [[red-green glasses]] (red over the right eye, green over the left eye by convention) and views a target consisting of four illuminated dots: one red, two green, and one white.

| Response | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| 4 dots seen | Normal [[binocular fusion]] (or [[anomalous retinal correspondence]]) |
| 5 dots seen | [[Diplopia]] (no fusion, no suppression) |
| 3 green dots seen | Right eye suppression |
| 2 red dots seen | Left eye suppression |
| Alternating between 2 and 3 dots | Alternating suppression |

The W4D can be performed at distance (6 meters/20 feet) and near (33 cm/13 inches). The distance test subtends a smaller angle and is more sensitive to **central (foveal)** suppression, while the near test subtends a larger angle and assesses a broader area of the retina.

#### 2. Bagolini Striated Lenses

[[Bagolini striated lenses]] are plano lenses with fine striations oriented at 45° and 135° for the right and left eyes, respectively. When viewing a point light source, each eye perceives a streak of light. Under normal binocular conditions, the patient reports an **X** pattern.

| Response | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Full X seen | Normal binocular vision or harmonious [[ARC]] |
| One streak missing entirely | Complete suppression of that eye |
| One streak with a gap through the center | Foveal suppression (central suppression scotoma) |
| Both streaks seen but not crossing | [[Diplopia]] |

Bagolini lenses are considered a **near-natural** test of binocular vision because they minimally disrupt normal viewing conditions.

#### 3. 4-Prism Diopter Base-Out Prism Test

The **4Δ base-out prism test** (also known as the **Irvine prism test**) is used to detect **foveal suppression** in patients with small-angle strabismus or [[microtropia]]. A 4Δ base-out prism is placed before one eye:

- **Normal response**: The eye behind the prism adducts (version movement), followed by a fusional vergence movement of both eyes.
- **Suppression response**: No movement is observed when the prism is placed before the suppressed eye, because the foveal image displacement is not perceived.

This test is particularly valuable in detecting [[monofixation syndrome]] and [[microtropia]].

#### 4. Red Filter Test (Red Lens Test)

A red filter is placed before one eye while the patient fixates a white light. The patient is asked what they see:

| Response | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| One pink light | Normal fusion |
| One red and one white light | Diplopia |
| One white light only | Suppression of the eye with the red filter |
| One red light only | Suppression of the eye without the red filter |

Increasing the density of the red filter increases the dissociation and can help grade the depth of suppression.

#### 5. Sbisa Bar (Suppression Bar)

The [[Sbisa bar]] is a graded density filter bar used to quantify the **depth of suppression**. Filters of increasing density are placed before the fixating (non-suppressed) eye until the patient reports awareness of the suppressed eye's image. The density at which suppression breaks indicates the depth:

- **Low-density filter breaks suppression** → Shallow suppression
- **High-density filter required** → Deep suppression
- **Suppression not broken even with maximum density** → Very deep (dense) suppression

#### 6. Synoptophore (Major Amblyoscope)

The [[synoptophore]] (also known as the [[major amblyoscope]]) is a haploscopic instrument that allows independent presentation of targets to each eye. It is used to:

- Detect the presence and extent of suppression
- Map the suppression scotoma
- Assess [[simultaneous perception]], [[fusion]], and [[stereopsis]] (Worth's three grades of binocular vision)
- Measure the angle of [[anomalous retinal correspondence]]

The synoptophore is considered the gold standard for detailed suppression assessment in a clinical setting.

#### 7. Polarized and Anaglyph Vectograms

Polarized vectograms (e.g., [[Quoits vectogram]], [[Clown vectogram]], [[Bernell Aperture Rule]]) and red-green anaglyphs are used in both diagnosis and treatment. Suppression is indicated when the patient fails to perceive the target presented to one eye.

#### 8. Dichoptic Testing and Computer-Based Assessment

Modern approaches to suppression assessment include **dichoptic** (separate-eye) stimulus presentation using:

- **[[Dichoptic letter charts]]** (e.g., the Dichoptic Letter e-Chart)
- **Virtual reality (VR) headsets** presenting different images to each eye
- **iPad/tablet-based applications** using red-green or polarized glasses (e.g., the **Balanced Binocular Viewing** paradigm)
- **Binocular rivalry paradigms** with quantitative measurement of suppression duration and dominance

These technologies allow precise quantification of suppression depth and are increasingly used in research and clinical practice.

### Diagnostic Algorithm

A systematic approach to suppression evaluation should include:

1. **History**: Age of onset of strabismus, prior treatment, symptoms of diplopia or asthenopia
2. **Visual acuity**: Monocular and binocular [[BCVA]]; interocular acuity difference suggests amblyopia
3. **Cover test**: [[Cover-uncover test]] and [[alternating cover test]] to detect strabismus and phoria
4. **Stereoacuity testing**: [[Titmus test]], [[Randot test]], [[TNO test]]
5. **Suppression testing**: W4D (distance and near), Bagolini lenses, 4Δ BO prism test
6. **Suppression mapping**: Synoptophore or dichoptic methods to delineate scotoma
7. **Depth assessment**: Sbisa bar or graded red filter
8. **Assessment of retinal correspondence**: Synoptophore, after-image test, Bagolini lenses

## Relationship to Other Conditions

### Suppression and Amblyopia

Suppression and [[amblyopia]] are intimately related but distinct entities. Suppression is a **binocular** phenomenon (occurring only during binocular viewing), while amblyopia is a **monocular** deficit (reduced acuity persists even during monocular viewing). However, chronic suppression during the [[critical period]] is believed to be a primary driver of amblyopia development:

1. Suppression reduces cortical representation of the suppressed eye
2. Reduced cortical stimulation leads to structural and functional changes in [[ocular dominance columns]]
3. These changes manifest as reduced [[visual acuity]], [[contrast sensitivity]], and [[spatial resolution]] in the suppressed eye—i.e., [[amblyopia]]

Contemporary models of amblyopia increasingly emphasize the **binocular nature** of the disorder, viewing suppression as both a cause and a maintaining factor of amblyopia. This has led to the development of **anti-suppression** and **dichoptic** treatment approaches.

### Suppression and Anomalous Retinal Correspondence (ARC)

[[Anomalous retinal correspondence]] (ARC) is another sensory adaptation to strabismus in which the cortex remaps the binocular correspondence so that the fovea of the fixating eye corresponds with an extrafoveal point in the deviating eye. Suppression and ARC frequently coexist:

- In some patients, suppression is the dominant adaptation (no ARC develops)
- In others, ARC develops in conjunction with suppression of the fovea of the deviating eye
- The relationship between suppression and ARC has implications for treatment: patients with deep ARC may be more resistant to restoration of normal binocular vision

### Suppression and Strabismus

Suppression is both a **consequence** and a **perpetuating factor** of [[strabismus]]:

- Strabismus creates the sensory conflict that triggers suppression
- Suppression eliminates the diplopia that would otherwise motivate fusional effort
- Without the drive to fuse, the strabismus may worsen or become constant
- This creates a self-reinforcing cycle: strabismus → suppression → reduced fusional drive → worsening strabismus

## Management and Treatment

The management of suppression is multifaceted and depends on the underlying etiology, the patient's age, the depth and constancy of suppression, and the treatment goals. The overarching objectives are:

1. **Eliminate or reduce the underlying cause** (correct refractive error, align the eyes)
2. **Break suppression** (make the patient aware of the suppressed eye's input)
3. **Build binocular integration** (develop simultaneous perception, fusion, and stereopsis)
4. **Maintain gains** (prevent recurrence of suppression)

### Optical Correction

The first step in managing suppression is always to ensure optimal [[refractive correction]]:

- Full correction of [[hyperopia]], [[myopia]], and [[astigmatism]]
- Correction of [[anisometropia]] (with spectacles, [[contact lenses]], or [[refractive surgery]] as appropriate)
- Consideration of [[aniseikonia]] management if significant image size difference exists
- Prescription of [[prism]] to neutralize small-angle deviations and promote fusion

### Occlusion Therapy (Patching)

[[Occlusion therapy]] (patching the dominant/fixating eye) is the traditional mainstay of [[amblyopia]] treatment and indirectly addresses suppression by forcing use of the suppressed eye. However, patching is a **monocular** treatment and does not directly train binocular integration. Modern approaches increasingly combine patching with binocular (anti-suppression) therapy.

| Patching Regimen | Indication |
|---|---|
| Full-time occlusion | Dense amblyopia with deep suppression (used cautiously to avoid reverse amblyopia) |
| Part-time occlusion (2–6 hours/day) | Moderate amblyopia; maintenance phase |
| Minimal occlusion (1–2 hours/day) | Mild amblyopia; used in conjunction with near activities |

### Penalization

[[Penalization]] (pharmacological or optical) is an alternative to patching:

- **Pharmacological penalization**: Instillation of [[atropine]] 1% in the dominant eye to blur near vision, forcing use of the amblyopic/suppressed eye
- **Optical penalization**: Deliberate over-plus or under-correction of the dominant eye
- **Bangerter foils**: Graded translucent filters placed on the dominant eye's spectacle lens to reduce image quality and encourage use of the suppressed eye

### Anti-Suppression Therapy (Vision Therapy)

**Anti-suppression therapy** is the direct treatment of suppression and forms a critical component of [[vision therapy]] (orthoptics). The goal is to make the patient aware of the suppressed eye's input and gradually build binocular integration.

#### Techniques and Instruments

| Technique | Description |
|---|---|
| **Red-green (anaglyph) activities** | Patient wears red-green glasses; targets are designed so that each eye sees different elements; suppression is evident when elements are missing |
| **Polarized vectograms** | Polarized targets (e.g., [[Quoits vectogram]]) present different images to each eye; used to train simultaneous perception and fusion |
| **Bar reader / red-green bar reader** | Alternating red and green bars placed over reading material; suppression causes lines to disappear |
| **Brock string** | A string with beads at various distances; the patient should perceive physiological diplopia of non-fixated beads; absence indicates suppression |
| **Aperture rule** | A handheld device presenting different targets to each eye through apertures; trains simultaneous perception and fusion |
| **Synoptophore exercises** | Graded exercises progressing from simultaneous perception to fusion to stereopsis |
| **TV trainer / septum** | A vertical septum divides the visual field so each eye sees different portions of a display; suppression causes loss of part of the image |
| **Flicker/flash stimulation** | Rapid alternating stimulation of the suppressed eye to "wake up" cortical pathways |

#### Dichoptic Therapy (Binocular Treatment)

A paradigm shift in suppression and amblyopia treatment has occurred with the development of **dichoptic therapy**, which presents different but complementary images to the two eyes simultaneously. The key innovation is **contrast balancing**: the image presented to the dominant eye is reduced in contrast while the image to the amblyopic/suppressed eye is presented at full contrast. This reduces the interocular inhibition that drives suppression and allows binocular integration to occur.

Dichoptic therapy platforms include:

- **[[Dig Rush]]** and **[[Falling Blocks]]** (iPad-based dichoptic games)
- **[[Vivid Vision]]** (virtual reality-based dichoptic therapy)
- **[[RevitalVision]]** (computer-based perceptual learning)
- **Custom dichoptic setups** using VR headsets, anaglyphic glasses, or polarized displays

Clinical trials (e.g., the **Pediatric Eye Disease Investigator Group [PEDIG]** studies) have shown that dichoptic therapy can improve both visual acuity and binocular function, though results have been variable and the optimal protocols are still being refined.

### Pharmacological Interventions

While no medication directly "treats" suppression, several pharmacological agents have been investigated as adjuncts:

| Agent | Mechanism | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Levodopa]]/carbidopa** | Dopaminergic enhancement of cortical plasticity | Some evidence of transient improvement in amblyopic eye acuity; effects may not persist after discontinuation |
| **[[Citicoline]]** | Enhances dopaminergic and cholinergic neurotransmission | Limited evidence of benefit as adjunct to patching in amblyopia |
| **[[Fluoxetine]]** (and other SSRIs) | May reopen critical period plasticity via serotonergic mechanisms | Animal model evidence; human trials limited |
| **[[Donepezil]]** | Cholinesterase inhibitor; enhances cholinergic cortical plasticity | Preliminary human studies show some promise |
| **[[Atropine]]** (penalization) | Cycloplegia of dominant eye; forces use of suppressed eye | Well-established for amblyopia treatment (PEDIG ATS studies) |

### Surgical Management

Surgery does not directly treat suppression but addresses the underlying [[strabismus]] that causes it. [[Strabismus surgery]] (e.g., [[recession]], [[resection]], [[adjustable sutures]]) aims to restore ocular alignment, which may:

- Reduce the sensory conflict driving suppression
- Create conditions favorable for binocular fusion
- Improve cosmesis and psychosocial function

However, surgery alone rarely eliminates suppression, particularly in patients with long-standing strabismus and deep suppression. **Pre-operative and post-operative vision therapy** is often recommended to maximize binocular outcomes.

### Prism Therapy

[[Prism]] lenses can be used to:

- Neutralize small residual deviations after surgery
- Provide immediate alignment in patients with decompensated phorias
- Serve as a diagnostic tool (Fresnel prisms) to assess the potential for binocular fusion before surgery
- Reduce suppression by bringing the deviated eye's image onto corresponding retinal points

### Treatment Algorithm

A general treatment algorithm for suppression management:

1. **Correct refractive error** fully (spectacles or contact lenses)
2. **Treat amblyopia** if present (patching, penalization, or dichoptic therapy)
3. **Anti-suppression therapy** (vision therapy techniques as described above)
4. **Address strabismus** (prism, surgery, or both)
5. **Build binocular vision** (fusion and stereopsis training)
6. **Maintenance therapy** (periodic reinforcement to prevent regression)

## Prognosis

The prognosis for suppression reversal depends on several factors:

| Factor | Favorable Prognosis | Unfavorable Prognosis |
|---|---|---|
| **Age** | Younger patients (within critical period) | Older patients (beyond critical period) |
| **Duration** | Recent onset | Long-standing (years) |
| **Constancy** | Intermittent suppression | Constant suppression |
| **Depth** | Shallow suppression | Deep/dense suppression |
| **Underlying cause** | Correctable (e.g., refractive error) | Structural or long-standing strabismus |
| **Amblyopia severity** | Mild (20/30–20/40) | Severe (20/200 or worse) |
| **Retinal correspondence** | Normal retinal correspondence (NRC) | Deep anomalous retinal correspondence (ARC) |
| **Patient compliance** | Good compliance with therapy | Poor compliance |

In general:
- Children with intermittent suppression and mild amblyopia have an excellent prognosis with appropriate treatment.
- Adults with long-standing constant suppression and deep amblyopia have a guarded prognosis, though recent evidence suggests that some improvement is possible even in adulthood with intensive dichoptic therapy and perceptual learning.

## Special Considerations

### Suppression in Intermittent Exotropia

[[Intermittent exotropia]] (X(T)) is a common condition in which suppression plays a central role. During the exotropic phase, the patient suppresses the deviating eye; during the orthotropic phase, normal binocular vision is present. The progression from intermittent to constant exotropia is often accompanied by deepening suppression. Monitoring suppression depth (e.g., with the W4D at distance) is an important component of the decision to intervene surgically.

### Suppression in Microtropia (Monofixation Syndrome)

[[Monofixation syndrome]] (Lang, 1969) is characterized by a small-angle strabismus (<10Δ), foveal suppression scotoma in the deviating eye, peripheral fusion, and reduced but present [[stereopsis]] (typically 60–3000 arc seconds). Suppression in monofixation syndrome is typically shallow and limited to the foveal region. The 4Δ base-out prism test is the key diagnostic tool.

### Suppression and Driving/Occupational Implications

Patients with suppression may have reduced [[stereopsis]] and impaired depth perception, which can affect:
- Driving safety (particularly judging distances)
- Occupational performance (surgery, piloting, sports)
- Quality of life

Counseling patients about these implications is an important aspect of clinical management.

### Post-Surgical Diplopia and Suppression

In adults undergoing [[strabismus surgery]], the restoration of ocular alignment may unmask previously suppressed diplopia—a phenomenon known as **intractable post-operative diplopia**. Pre-operative assessment of suppression and fusional potential (using prism adaptation or diagnostic Fresnel prisms) is essential to predict this risk.

---

## Summary

Suppression is a fundamental concept in [[binocular vision]] and a cornerstone of clinical optometry and ophthalmology. It represents the visual cortex's adaptive response to conflicting binocular input, serving to eliminate [[diplopia]] and [[visual confusion]] at the cost of reduced [[stereopsis]] and, potentially, the development of [[amblyopia]]. A thorough understanding of the pathophysiology, classification, diagnosis, and management of suppression is essential for every eye care practitioner. Modern advances in dichoptic therapy, neuroplasticity research, and computer-based assessment are expanding the therapeutic options and improving outcomes for patients with suppression across all age groups.
