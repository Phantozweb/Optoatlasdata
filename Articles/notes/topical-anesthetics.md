

# Topical Anesthetics in Ophthalmic Practice

---

## Overview

**Topical anesthetics** are pharmacological agents applied directly to the [[ocular surface]] to produce temporary, reversible loss of sensation by blocking [[nerve conduction]] in sensory nerve endings of the [[cornea]] and [[conjunctiva]]. They are among the most frequently used drugs in clinical [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]], serving as indispensable tools for diagnostic procedures, minor therapeutic interventions, and emergency management of acute ocular pain. Topical ophthalmic anesthetics belong to two major chemical classes: **amino esters** (e.g., [[proparacaine]], [[tetracaine]], [[benoxinate]]) and **amino amides** (e.g., [[lidocaine]]). Their mechanism of action centers on the reversible blockade of voltage-gated [[sodium channels]] in the [[corneal nerves]], thereby preventing the generation and propagation of [[action potentials]] along [[trigeminal nerve]] (cranial nerve V, ophthalmic division) afferent fibers.

While topical anesthetics are essential for clinical practice, their misuse—particularly chronic or unsupervised patient self-administration—represents a significant and well-documented cause of severe [[keratopathy]], [[corneal ulceration]], [[corneal perforation]], and even permanent [[vision loss]]. Understanding the pharmacology, clinical applications, adverse effects, and toxicological profile of these agents is therefore critical for every eye care practitioner.

---

## History and Development

The history of topical ocular anesthesia dates to **1884**, when the Viennese ophthalmologist **Carl Koller** first demonstrated the local anesthetic properties of [[cocaine]] applied to the [[cornea]], revolutionizing ophthalmic surgery. Cocaine, an amino ester derived from the coca plant (*Erythroxylum coca*), was the first drug used for topical ocular anesthesia, but its significant toxicity, potential for abuse, and sympathomimetic side effects (including [[mydriasis]] and [[corneal epithelium]] damage) prompted the search for safer alternatives.

In the mid-20th century, synthetic derivatives were developed. **[[Tetracaine]]** (amethocaine) was introduced in 1932, followed by **[[proparacaine]]** (proxymetacaine) in 1953 and **[[benoxinate]]** (oxybuprocaine) shortly thereafter. These agents offered improved safety profiles, reduced stinging upon instillation, and minimal systemic absorption. Today, **proparacaine hydrochloride 0.5%** and **tetracaine hydrochloride 0.5%** remain the two most widely used topical ophthalmic anesthetics worldwide.

## Pharmacology

### Chemical Classification

Topical ophthalmic anesthetics are classified based on their chemical structure into two primary groups:

| **Class** | **Agent** | **Concentration** | **Onset** | **Duration** | **Key Characteristics** |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Amino Esters** | [[Proparacaine]] HCl | 0.5% | 10–30 seconds | 10–20 minutes | Least stinging; most commonly used in clinical optometry |
| | [[Tetracaine]] HCl | 0.5% | 10–30 seconds | 15–20 minutes | More potent; greater epithelial toxicity; more stinging |
| | [[Benoxinate]] HCl (Oxybuprocaine) | 0.4% | 10–30 seconds | 10–20 minutes | Often combined with [[fluorescein]] sodium (Fluress®) |
| | [[Cocaine]] HCl | 1–4% | 1–5 minutes | 15–30 minutes | Rarely used; sympathomimetic effects; used in [[Horner syndrome]] testing |
| **Amino Amides** | [[Lidocaine]] HCl | 2–4% (gel or solution) | 1–5 minutes | 5–30 minutes | Used primarily in surgical settings; preservative-free formulations available |

### Structure-Activity Relationships

All local anesthetics share a common molecular architecture consisting of three components:

1. **Lipophilic aromatic ring** – Determines lipid solubility and ability to penetrate nerve membranes
2. **Intermediate chain** – Either an **ester linkage** (amino esters) or an **amide linkage** (amino amides); this determines the metabolic pathway
3. **Hydrophilic amine group** – Determines water solubility and ionization

The **ester linkage** in amino esters is hydrolyzed by plasma [[cholinesterase]] (pseudocholinesterase) and tissue esterases, resulting in rapid metabolism and a lower risk of systemic toxicity. The **amide linkage** in amino amides is metabolized hepatically by [[cytochrome P450]] enzymes, resulting in a longer half-life. The distinction is clinically relevant because patients with [[pseudocholinesterase deficiency]] may have prolonged effects from ester-type anesthetics, and cross-reactivity between the two classes for allergic reactions is exceedingly rare.

## Mechanism of Action (Pathophysiology of Anesthetic Effect)

### Molecular Mechanism

Topical anesthetics exert their effect by **reversibly blocking voltage-gated sodium channels (Nav channels)** in the membranes of sensory nerve fibers. The detailed mechanism proceeds as follows:

1. **Penetration**: The anesthetic molecule, which exists in equilibrium between an ionized (charged, hydrophilic) form and a non-ionized (uncharged, lipophilic) form, must first penetrate the [[corneal epithelium]]. The **non-ionized (base) form** is lipid-soluble and crosses the cell membrane.

2. **Ionization at intracellular pH**: Once inside the nerve cell, the molecule re-equilibrates, and the **ionized (cationic) form** predominates at physiological intracellular pH. This cationic form is the pharmacologically active species.

3. **Sodium channel blockade**: The ionized form binds to the **intracellular aspect of the alpha subunit** of the voltage-gated sodium channel, specifically at the **local anesthetic binding site** within the channel pore (segment S6 of domains I, III, and IV). This binding stabilizes the channel in its **inactivated (closed) conformation**, preventing the conformational change necessary for sodium ion influx.

4. **Blockade of action potential propagation**: Without sodium influx, the nerve fiber cannot reach the threshold for [[depolarization]], and [[action potential]] generation and propagation are inhibited. Sensory transmission along the [[ophthalmic division]] of the [[trigeminal nerve]] (V1) is thereby interrupted.

5. **Reversibility**: The binding is non-covalent and fully reversible. As the drug diffuses away from the nerve and is metabolized, normal sodium channel function is restored.

### Differential Nerve Fiber Blockade

Topical anesthetics preferentially block **small-diameter, unmyelinated C fibers** (which carry dull pain and temperature sensation) and **small myelinated Aδ fibers** (which carry sharp pain) before affecting larger myelinated fibers. This is because smaller fibers have a higher surface-area-to-volume ratio and require fewer sodium channels to be blocked to prevent conduction. In the [[cornea]]—which is the most densely innervated tissue in the human body, with approximately **7,000 nociceptors per square millimeter**—topical anesthetics effectively abolish pain, touch, and temperature sensation within seconds of application.

### Corneal Nerve Anatomy

The [[cornea]] receives its sensory innervation from the **long ciliary nerves**, which are branches of the [[nasociliary nerve]], itself a branch of the [[ophthalmic nerve]] (V1). These nerves enter the cornea at the [[limbus]] in the middle and anterior [[stroma]], lose their [[myelin sheaths]] within approximately 1 mm of entry, and then branch extensively to form the **sub-basal nerve plexus** beneath [[Bowman's layer]]. Terminal nerve endings penetrate through Bowman's layer into the [[corneal epithelium]], where they terminate as free nerve endings among the epithelial cells. This dense innervation is the basis for the cornea's exquisite sensitivity and the effectiveness of topical anesthetics.

## Clinical Applications

Topical anesthetics are used extensively in both diagnostic and therapeutic settings in optometric and ophthalmic practice.

### Diagnostic Applications

| **Procedure** | **Purpose** | **Preferred Agent** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]] | Measurement of [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) | Proparacaine 0.5% or Benoxinate/Fluorescein (Fluress®) |
| [[Gonioscopy]] | Evaluation of the [[anterior chamber angle]] | Proparacaine 0.5% |
| [[Schirmer test]] (with anesthesia) | Measurement of reflex [[tear production]] (Schirmer II) vs. basal secretion | Proparacaine 0.5% (note: Schirmer I is performed without anesthesia) |
| [[Corneal sensitivity testing]] (post-procedure comparison) | Assessment of [[corneal nerves]] function | Proparacaine 0.5% |
| [[Foreign body]] removal | Facilitation of [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] examination and removal | Proparacaine 0.5% or Tetracaine 0.5% |
| [[Corneal scraping]] / cultures | Collection of specimens for microbiological analysis in suspected [[microbial keratitis]] | Proparacaine 0.5% (preferred; less bacteriostatic than tetracaine) |
| [[Electroretinography]] (ERG) with contact lens electrodes | Placement of [[Burian-Allen]] or similar contact lens electrodes | Proparacaine 0.5% |
| [[Pachymetry]] (contact ultrasound) | Measurement of [[corneal thickness]] | Proparacaine 0.5% |
| [[B-scan ultrasonography]] (contact method) | Imaging of posterior segment | Proparacaine 0.5% |
| Diagnostic impression [[cytology]] | Collection of conjunctival epithelial cells | Proparacaine 0.5% |
| [[Lacrimal irrigation]] and probing | Assessment of [[nasolacrimal duct]] patency | Proparacaine 0.5% |

### Therapeutic Applications

| **Procedure** | **Purpose** |
|---|---|
| [[Corneal foreign body]] removal (including rust ring removal) | Pain control during removal with spud or needle |
| [[Subconjunctival injection]] (pre-anesthesia) | Surface anesthesia prior to injection |
| [[Punctal plug]] insertion | Comfort during insertion of [[punctal plugs]] for [[dry eye disease]] |
| Emergency pain relief | Immediate relief for patients with [[corneal abrasion]], [[chemical burn]] (during irrigation), or acute [[ocular trauma]] |
| [[Pterygium]] surgery (adjunctive) | Pre-surgical surface anesthesia |
| [[Intravitreal injection]] (adjunctive) | Surface anesthesia prior to injection of anti-[[VEGF]] agents |
| [[Photorefractive keratectomy]] (PRK) and [[LASIK]] (adjunctive) | Perioperative surface anesthesia |
| Minor conjunctival procedures | Incision and drainage of [[chalazion]] (adjunctive), suture removal |

### Special Diagnostic Use: Cocaine Test for Horner Syndrome

[[Cocaine]] 4–10% ophthalmic solution has a unique diagnostic application in the evaluation of suspected [[Horner syndrome]]. Cocaine blocks the reuptake of [[norepinephrine]] at the [[sympathetic]] nerve terminal at the [[iris dilator muscle]]. In a normal eye, this results in [[mydriasis]]. In Horner syndrome, the sympathetic pathway is disrupted, resulting in decreased norepinephrine release; therefore, cocaine fails to dilate the affected pupil, confirming the diagnosis. The resulting **anisocoria** (increased difference in pupil size) after cocaine instillation is diagnostic. Note: Due to regulatory restrictions on cocaine, **[[apraclonidine]]** 0.5–1% (an alpha-2 adrenergic agonist) has largely replaced cocaine for this purpose in many clinical settings, though the pharmacological principle differs (denervation supersensitivity).

## Pharmacokinetics

### Absorption

Topical ophthalmic anesthetics are absorbed through the [[corneal epithelium]] primarily via **transcellular diffusion** of the non-ionized form. The rate of absorption depends on:

- **Lipophilicity** of the agent
- **pH** of the formulation (closer to physiological pH = more non-ionized form = faster penetration but also more stinging)
- **Concentration** of the drug
- **Integrity of the corneal epithelium** (disrupted epithelium allows faster and deeper penetration)
- **Contact time** with the ocular surface

### Distribution

After penetrating the epithelium, the drug distributes into the [[corneal stroma]], [[aqueous humor]], and surrounding tissues. Systemic absorption via the [[conjunctival vasculature]] and [[nasolacrimal drainage]] is minimal but can occur, particularly with repeated instillation.

### Metabolism and Elimination

- **Amino esters** (proparacaine, tetracaine, benoxinate): Rapidly hydrolyzed by tissue esterases and plasma [[pseudocholinesterase]] to inactive metabolites, including [[para-aminobenzoic acid]] (PABA). This rapid metabolism contributes to their short duration of action and low systemic toxicity.
- **Amino amides** (lidocaine): Metabolized by hepatic [[microsomal enzymes]] (CYP1A2, CYP3A4). Systemic levels after topical ophthalmic use are negligible.

## Adverse Effects and Toxicity

### Immediate/Acute Adverse Effects

Upon instillation, topical anesthetics may cause:

- **Stinging and burning**: Transient discomfort lasting seconds; more pronounced with tetracaine than proparacaine
- **Transient [[conjunctival hyperemia]]**: Due to vasodilatory effects
- **[[Chemosis]]**: Mild conjunctival edema
- **Transient blurred vision**: Due to disruption of the [[tear film]]
- **[[Allergic contact dermatitis]]** or **[[allergic conjunctivitis]]**: Rare; more common with ester-type agents due to the PABA metabolite; cross-reactivity with other PABA-containing compounds (e.g., certain sunscreens, sulfonamides) is possible

### Epithelial Toxicity (Acute and Chronic)

All topical anesthetics are inherently **toxic to the [[corneal epithelium]]**. Even a single application causes measurable disruption of epithelial cell membranes and intercellular junctions. The mechanisms of epithelial toxicity include:

1. **Disruption of cell membrane integrity**: Anesthetics are amphiphilic molecules that intercalate into lipid bilayers, destabilizing cell membranes
2. **Inhibition of epithelial cell mitosis**: Topical anesthetics suppress the normal mitotic activity of [[corneal epithelial stem cells]] at the [[limbus]] and [[transient amplifying cells]] in the peripheral and central cornea
3. **Inhibition of cell migration**: Wound healing requires coordinated epithelial cell migration; anesthetics impair this process
4. **Desquamation of superficial epithelial cells**: Leading to punctate epithelial erosions visible with [[fluorescein]] staining
5. **Disruption of [[hemidesmosomes]] and [[basement membrane]] adhesion**: Weakening epithelial attachment
6. **Decreased [[tear film]] stability**: Anesthetics reduce [[goblet cell]] mucin secretion and alter the lipid layer, destabilizing the [[precorneal tear film]]

### Topical Anesthetic Abuse Keratopathy

**Topical anesthetic abuse keratopathy** (also termed **anesthetic keratopathy** or **anesthetic abuse keratitis**) is a severe, sight-threatening condition resulting from repeated, unsupervised self-administration of topical anesthetic drops. This is one of the most important clinical entities associated with topical anesthetics and warrants detailed discussion.

#### Pathophysiology of Anesthetic Abuse Keratopathy

Chronic exposure to topical anesthetics produces a cascade of pathological changes:

1. **Persistent epithelial defect (PED)**: Continuous inhibition of epithelial mitosis and migration prevents normal wound healing, resulting in a non-healing [[epithelial defect]]
2. **Stromal edema and infiltration**: Loss of the epithelial barrier allows stromal hydration and inflammatory cell infiltration
3. **Ring infiltrate**: A characteristic **stromal ring infiltrate** (immune ring) may develop, often mimicking [[Acanthamoeba keratitis]] or [[herpetic stromal keratitis]]
4. **Stromal melting ([[keratolysis]])**: Activation of [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs) and collagenases leads to progressive stromal thinning
5. **[[Descemetocele]] formation**: Advanced stromal loss may expose [[Descemet's membrane]]
6. **[[Corneal perforation]]**: The most devastating complication; may require emergency [[corneal transplantation]] ([[penetrating keratoplasty]])
7. **Secondary [[microbial keratitis]]**: The compromised epithelial barrier and impaired immune response predispose to bacterial, fungal, or [[Acanthamoeba]] superinfection
8. **[[Corneal scarring]] and [[neovascularization]]**: Even with treatment, permanent scarring and [[vision loss]] are common

#### Clinical Presentation of Anesthetic Abuse Keratopathy

Patients with anesthetic abuse keratopathy typically present with:

- **Severe ocular pain** (paradoxically worsening despite continued anesthetic use, creating a vicious cycle)
- **Decreased [[visual acuity]]**
- **[[Photophobia]]**
- **[[Epiphora]] (tearing)**
- **History of access to topical anesthetics** (healthcare workers, patients who obtained drops from emergency departments, or those who acquired them from pharmacies in countries where they are available over the counter)

#### Slit-Lamp Findings

| **Finding** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| [[Punctate epithelial erosions]] | Diffuse superficial punctate keratopathy (SPK) |
| Persistent [[epithelial defect]] | Non-healing epithelial defect, often with gray, heaped-up, or rolled edges |
| Stromal ring infiltrate | Dense, white, ring-shaped stromal opacity; may be mistaken for infectious keratitis |
| [[Stromal edema]] | Corneal thickening and haze |
| Stromal thinning/melting | Progressive loss of stromal tissue |
| [[Descemetocele]] | Bulging of Descemet's membrane through thinned stroma |
| [[Anterior chamber]] reaction | Cells and flare indicating secondary [[anterior uveitis]] |
| [[Hypopyon]] | Layered white blood cells in the anterior chamber (may indicate superinfection) |
| [[Corneal neovascularization]] | Ingrowth of blood vessels from the limbus |
| Lid edema and [[periorbital]] swelling | Secondary inflammatory changes |

#### Diagnosis

Diagnosis of topical anesthetic abuse keratopathy is primarily **clinical** and requires a **high index of suspicion**. Key diagnostic considerations include:

- **History**: Direct questioning about topical anesthetic use; patients may be reluctant to disclose abuse
- **Clinical pattern**: The combination of a persistent epithelial defect with a ring infiltrate in a patient with access to anesthetic drops is highly suggestive
- **Failure to respond to standard therapy**: Persistent epithelial defects that worsen despite appropriate treatment for presumed infectious or inflammatory keratitis should raise suspicion
- **Corneal cultures**: Should be obtained to rule out or identify superinfection; cultures may be negative if the keratopathy is purely toxic
- **[[Confocal microscopy]]**: May reveal loss of the sub-basal nerve plexus, epithelial cell irregularity, and stromal inflammatory infiltrates
- **Toxicology**: In rare cases, analysis of tear fluid or confiscated drops may confirm the presence of anesthetic agents

#### Differential Diagnosis

Anesthetic abuse keratopathy must be differentiated from:

- [[Acanthamoeba keratitis]] (ring infiltrate, severe pain)
- [[Herpes simplex keratitis]] (dendritic/geographic ulcer)
- [[Fungal keratitis]] (feathery infiltrate, satellite lesions)
- [[Bacterial keratitis]] (purulent infiltrate, rapid progression)
- [[Neurotrophic keratopathy]] (reduced corneal sensation from other causes, such as [[diabetes mellitus]], post-[[LASIK]], or [[herpes zoster ophthalmicus]])
- [[Factitious disorder]] (self-inflicted ocular injury)

## Management of Topical Anesthetic Abuse Keratopathy

### Principles of Treatment

1. **Immediate and complete cessation of topical anesthetic use**: This is the single most critical intervention. The patient must be counseled firmly, and all access to anesthetic drops must be eliminated.

2. **Pain management with non-anesthetic modalities**:
   - **Oral analgesics**: [[Acetaminophen]], [[NSAIDs]] (e.g., [[ibuprofen]]), or in severe cases, oral [[opioid]] analgesics
   - **Oral [[gabapentin]] or [[pregabalin]]**: For neuropathic pain component
   - **Cycloplegic agents**: [[Cyclopentolate]] 1% or [[homatropine]] 5% to relieve [[ciliary spasm]] and reduce pain from secondary [[anterior uveitis]]
   - **Preservative-free artificial tears**: Frequent lubrication to support epithelial healing
   - **[[Bandage contact lens]]**: A therapeutic soft contact lens (e.g., [[silicone hydrogel]]) may reduce pain from lid-cornea interaction and promote epithelial healing
   - **Cold compresses**: For symptomatic relief

3. **Promotion of epithelial healing**:
   - **Preservative-free [[artificial tears]]** and **[[lubricating ointments]]** (e.g., preservative-free [[hyaluronic acid]], [[carboxymethylcellulose]])
   - **[[Autologous serum tears]]**: Contain [[epidermal growth factor]] (EGF), [[nerve growth factor]] (NGF), [[fibronectin]], and [[vitamin A]], which promote epithelial healing and nerve regeneration
   - **[[Amniotic membrane transplantation]]**: Cryopreserved or dehydrated [[amniotic membrane]] (e.g., ProKera®, AmbioDisk®) provides a biological scaffold, anti-inflammatory cytokines, and growth factors to promote healing
   - **[[Tarsorrhaphy]]**: Partial or complete closure of the [[eyelids]] to protect the corneal surface in refractory cases
   - **[[Punctal occlusion]]**: To maximize tear retention on the ocular surface

4. **Infection prophylaxis and treatment**:
   - **Broad-spectrum topical antibiotics**: [[Fluoroquinolone]] drops (e.g., [[moxifloxacin]] 0.5%, [[gatifloxacin]] 0.3%) to prevent or treat secondary bacterial infection
   - **Corneal cultures** should be obtained before initiating antibiotic therapy if infection is suspected
   - **Fortified antibiotics** (e.g., fortified [[tobramycin]] and fortified [[vancomycin]]) may be necessary for severe or culture-positive infections

5. **Anti-inflammatory therapy**:
   - **Topical [[corticosteroids]]**: Used cautiously and only after infection is controlled; may reduce stromal inflammation and neovascularization (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate]] 1%, [[loteprednol etabonate]] 0.5%)
   - **Topical [[cyclosporine A]]** or **[[tacrolimus]]**: For chronic inflammation
   - **Oral [[doxycycline]]** or **[[tetracycline]]**: MMP inhibitors that reduce collagenase activity and stromal melting (e.g., doxycycline 50–100 mg BID)
   - **Topical [[medroxyprogesterone]]** 1%: Anti-collagenase properties

6. **Surgical intervention** (for advanced cases):
   - **[[Corneal gluing]]** (cyanoacrylate tissue adhesive): For impending or micro-perforations
   - **[[Conjunctival flap]]**: Gundersen flap for refractory cases
   - **[[Penetrating keratoplasty]]** or **[[deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]]** (DALK): For corneal perforation or severe scarring after stabilization
   - **[[Boston keratoprosthesis]]** or other [[keratoprosthesis]]: In cases of repeated graft failure

7. **Psychological and psychiatric support**:
   - Referral for counseling or psychiatric evaluation, particularly if factitious disorder or substance abuse is suspected
   - Behavioral modification strategies to prevent relapse

## Proper Clinical Use and Safety Guidelines

### Best Practices for Practitioners

To minimize the risk of adverse effects and prevent abuse, practitioners should adhere to the following guidelines:

1. **Never prescribe or dispense topical anesthetics for home use**: This is the most important rule. Topical anesthetics should only be administered under direct clinical supervision.

2. **Use the minimum effective dose**: Typically, 1–2 drops of proparacaine 0.5% is sufficient for most diagnostic procedures. Avoid repeated instillation unless absolutely necessary.

3. **Prefer proparacaine over tetracaine** for routine diagnostic use: Proparacaine causes less stinging, less epithelial toxicity, and is better tolerated by patients.

4. **Use preservative-free formulations when available**: Preservatives such as [[benzalkonium chloride]] (BAK) add additional epithelial toxicity.

5. **Inform patients** that the anesthetic effect is temporary (10–20 minutes) and that they should avoid rubbing their eyes during this period to prevent inadvertent [[corneal abrasion]].

6. **Document anesthetic use** in the medical record.

7. **Secure storage**: Keep topical anesthetics in locked cabinets to prevent unauthorized access.

8. **Educate emergency department colleagues**: Emergency physicians and nurses should be counseled against prescribing topical anesthetics for home use in patients presenting with corneal abrasions or other painful ocular conditions.

### Contraindications

| **Contraindication** | **Rationale** |
|---|---|
| Known hypersensitivity to the specific agent or class | Risk of [[anaphylaxis]] or severe [[allergic reaction]] |
| Chronic/repeated unsupervised use | Risk of anesthetic abuse keratopathy |
| Use as a long-term analgesic | Inhibits epithelial healing; masks progression of underlying disease |
| Patients with [[pseudocholinesterase deficiency]] (relative, for ester-type agents) | Prolonged duration of action and potential toxicity |

## Effects on Diagnostic Testing

Practitioners must be aware that topical anesthetics can influence certain diagnostic measurements:

- **[[Intraocular pressure]] (IOP)**: Topical anesthetics may cause a small, transient decrease in IOP (approximately 1–3 mmHg) due to increased [[aqueous humor]] outflow through the [[trabecular meshwork]] and mild corneal thinning from epithelial edema. This effect is generally clinically insignificant but should be considered in borderline cases.

- **[[Schirmer test]]**: The Schirmer I test (without anesthesia) measures total tear production (basal + reflex). The Schirmer test with anesthesia (Schirmer II or basal secretion test) uses topical anesthetic to eliminate reflex tearing, measuring only basal tear secretion. Normal values differ: Schirmer I ≥ 15 mm in 5 minutes; Schirmer with anesthesia ≥ 10 mm in 5 minutes.

- **[[Corneal sensitivity]]**: Topical anesthetics abolish corneal sensitivity, making [[esthesiometry]] (e.g., [[Cochet-Bonnet esthesiometer]]) impossible for the duration of the anesthetic effect.

- **[[Corneal pachymetry]]**: Topical anesthetics may cause mild corneal epithelial edema, potentially affecting ultrasound pachymetry readings by a few micrometers.

- **Microbiological cultures**: Tetracaine has significant **bacteriostatic properties** and may inhibit the growth of organisms in culture. **Proparacaine is preferred** when corneal cultures are to be obtained, as it has minimal bacteriostatic effect.

## Emerging Developments and Future Directions

### Novel Anesthetic Delivery Systems

Research is ongoing into sustained-release formulations and novel delivery systems for topical ocular anesthetics, including:

- **[[Nanoparticle]]-based delivery**: Encapsulation of anesthetic agents in biodegradable nanoparticles (e.g., PLGA, chitosan) to prolong duration of action and reduce toxicity
- **[[Liposomal]] formulations**: Liposome-encapsulated lidocaine or tetracaine for extended release
- **[[Hydrogel]]-based systems**: In situ gelling formulations that provide prolonged contact time with the ocular surface
- **[[Mucoadhesive]] formulations**: Enhanced retention on the ocular surface

### Nerve Growth Factor and Corneal Nerve Regeneration

**Recombinant human [[nerve growth factor]]** (rhNGF, cenegermin, Oxervate®) has been approved for the treatment of [[neurotrophic keratopathy]] and may have a role in promoting corneal nerve regeneration in patients recovering from anesthetic abuse keratopathy. By stimulating [[corneal nerve]] regrowth, NGF therapy may help restore corneal sensation and the neurotrophic support essential for epithelial health.

### Topical NSAIDs as Adjunctive Analgesics

Topical [[NSAIDs]] (e.g., [[ketorolac]] 0.5%, [[diclofenac]] 0.1%, [[nepafenac]] 0.1%) have been investigated as alternatives to topical anesthetics for post-procedural pain management. While they do not provide the immediate, profound anesthesia of traditional agents, they offer analgesic and anti-inflammatory effects without the epithelial toxicity of anesthetics and may reduce the demand for anesthetic drops in post-operative settings.

---

## Key Clinical Pearls

- **Proparacaine 0.5%** is the preferred agent for routine diagnostic procedures due to its rapid onset, minimal stinging, low epithelial toxicity, and minimal bacteriostatic effect.
- **Never prescribe topical anesthetics for patient self-administration**—this cannot be overemphasized.
- A **ring infiltrate** in a patient with a persistent epithelial defect and access to topical anesthetics should immediately raise suspicion for **anesthetic abuse keratopathy**.
- **Autologous serum tears** and **amniotic membrane transplantation** are valuable adjuncts in managing severe anesthetic keratopathy.
- Topical anesthetics are **not appropriate for long-term pain management** of any ocular condition, including [[corneal abrasion]], [[recurrent corneal erosion]], or post-surgical pain.
- When performing **corneal cultures**, use **proparacaine** rather than tetracaine to avoid bacteriostatic interference.
