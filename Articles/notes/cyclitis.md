# Cyclitis

**Cyclitis** is an [[intraocular inflammation]] characterized by inflammation of the [[ciliary body]], one of the principal components of the [[uveal tract]] (also known as the [[uvea]]). As a subset of [[anterior uveitis]], cyclitis specifically targets the [[pars plicata]] and/or [[pars plana]] of the ciliary body, distinguishing it from [[iritis]] (inflammation of the [[iris]]) and [[iridocyclitis]] (combined inflammation of the iris and ciliary body). Cyclitis may present as an acute, chronic, or recurrent condition and can be associated with significant ocular morbidity if not promptly diagnosed and appropriately managed. It is classified under the Standardization of Uveitis Nomenclature (SUN) Working Group framework as a form of [[anterior uveitis]] or [[intermediate uveitis]], depending on the precise anatomical focus of inflammation.

Cyclitis demands careful clinical attention from [[optometrists]], [[ophthalmologists]], and other eye care professionals due to its potential to cause vision-threatening complications including [[posterior synechiae]], [[cataract]], [[secondary glaucoma]], [[cystoid macular edema]] (CME), and [[hypotony]].

---

## Etymology and Classification

The term "cyclitis" derives from the Greek *kyklos* (κύκλος), meaning "circle" or "ring," referencing the circular anatomical configuration of the [[ciliary body]], combined with the suffix *-itis*, denoting inflammation. Within the anatomical classification system of [[uveitis]], cyclitis occupies a specific niche:

| **Classification** | **Primary Site of Inflammation** | **SUN Category** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Iritis]] | [[Iris]] | [[Anterior uveitis]] |
| **Cyclitis** | **[[Ciliary body]]** | **[[Anterior uveitis]] / [[Intermediate uveitis]]** |
| [[Iridocyclitis]] | [[Iris]] and [[ciliary body]] | [[Anterior uveitis]] |
| [[Pars planitis]] | [[Pars plana]] of ciliary body (with snowbanking/snowballs) | [[Intermediate uveitis]] |
| [[Choroiditis]] | [[Choroid]] | [[Posterior uveitis]] |
| [[Panuveitis]] | All uveal structures | [[Panuveitis]] |

When inflammation is predominantly localized to the [[pars plana]] region of the ciliary body, the condition is more precisely termed **[[pars planitis]]**, a specific subtype of [[intermediate uveitis]]. However, when the [[pars plicata]] is the primary site, the term cyclitis in its classical sense is most applicable. In clinical practice, the terms cyclitis and iridocyclitis are frequently used in close association, as isolated inflammation of the ciliary body without any iris involvement is relatively uncommon due to the shared vascular supply via the [[long posterior ciliary arteries]] and [[anterior ciliary arteries]].

## Anatomy of the Ciliary Body

A thorough understanding of cyclitis requires detailed knowledge of the [[ciliary body]] anatomy. The ciliary body is a ring-shaped structure approximately 6 mm wide that extends from the [[scleral spur]] anteriorly to the [[ora serrata]] posteriorly. It is composed of two principal regions:

### Pars Plicata
The **[[pars plicata]]** (corona ciliaris) is the anterior portion, approximately 2 mm wide, consisting of 70–80 radially oriented [[ciliary processes]]. These processes are highly vascularized and are the primary site of [[aqueous humor]] production through active secretion, ultrafiltration, and diffusion. The [[ciliary epithelium]] lining these processes consists of two layers: an outer [[pigmented ciliary epithelium]] (continuous with the [[retinal pigment epithelium]]) and an inner [[non-pigmented ciliary epithelium]] (continuous with the [[neurosensory retina]]). The non-pigmented epithelium is the principal site of active aqueous secretion and forms part of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]] (BAB).

### Pars Plana
The **[[pars plana]]** (orbiculus ciliaris) is the posterior, flatter portion, approximately 4 mm wide, extending to the [[ora serrata]]. It is relatively avascular compared to the pars plicata and serves as a common surgical entry point for [[pars plana vitrectomy]] (PPV) procedures.

### Ciliary Muscle
The **[[ciliary muscle]]**, a smooth muscle embedded within the ciliary body, is responsible for [[accommodation]] through its action on the [[zonular fibers]] (zonules of Zinn) attached to the [[crystalline lens]]. It consists of three fiber orientations: longitudinal (Brücke's muscle), radial, and circular (Müller's muscle). Inflammation of the ciliary muscle during cyclitis contributes to [[ciliary spasm]], which manifests clinically as deep, aching [[ocular pain]] and [[pseudomyopia]].

### Vascular Supply
The ciliary body receives its blood supply from the [[long posterior ciliary arteries]] (two branches of the [[ophthalmic artery]]) and the [[anterior ciliary arteries]] (branches of the muscular arteries supplying the [[extraocular muscles]]). These vessels form the **[[major arterial circle of the iris]]** (circulus arteriosus iridis major) at the root of the iris, from which branches supply both the iris and ciliary body. This shared vascular network explains why isolated cyclitis without concurrent iritis is uncommon.

## Epidemiology

Cyclitis, as a component of [[anterior uveitis]] and [[intermediate uveitis]], shares epidemiological characteristics with these broader categories. Key epidemiological features include:

- **Incidence**: Anterior uveitis (including iritis, cyclitis, and iridocyclitis) accounts for approximately 50–60% of all uveitis cases in tertiary referral centers and 90% in community-based settings. Isolated cyclitis or intermediate uveitis accounts for approximately 7–15% of all uveitis cases.
- **Age**: Cyclitis can occur at any age but demonstrates a bimodal distribution, with peaks in young adults (20–40 years) and a secondary peak in children and adolescents (particularly [[pars planitis]]).
- **Sex**: No consistent sex predilection has been established, though some studies suggest a slight male predominance in [[HLA-B27]]-associated anterior uveitis.
- **Geographic and Ethnic Variation**: The prevalence of specific etiologies varies geographically. [[HLA-B27]]-associated disease is more common in Caucasian populations, while infectious etiologies such as [[tuberculosis]] and [[syphilis]] are more prevalent in endemic regions.
- **Laterality**: Cyclitis may be unilateral or bilateral. Bilateral involvement is more common in systemic autoimmune conditions, [[sarcoidosis]], and certain infectious etiologies.

## Etiology

The etiology of cyclitis is diverse and can be broadly categorized into infectious, non-infectious (autoimmune/autoinflammatory), and idiopathic causes. A systematic etiological workup is essential for guiding targeted therapy.

### Non-Infectious (Autoimmune/Autoinflammatory) Causes

| **Condition** | **Key Associations** |
|---|---|
| [[HLA-B27]]-associated uveitis | [[Ankylosing spondylitis]], [[reactive arthritis]], [[psoriatic arthritis]], [[inflammatory bowel disease]] |
| [[Sarcoidosis]] | Bilateral granulomatous inflammation, [[mutton-fat KPs]], [[Busacca nodules]], [[Berlin nodules]] |
| [[Juvenile idiopathic arthritis]] (JIA) | Chronic anterior uveitis in children, often asymptomatic, ANA-positive |
| [[Behçet disease]] | Recurrent oral/genital ulcers, hypopyon uveitis |
| [[Multiple sclerosis]] | Associated with [[pars planitis]] / intermediate uveitis |
| [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]] | Bilateral granulomatous panuveitis with exudative retinal detachments |
| [[Tubulointerstitial nephritis and uveitis]] (TINU) | Anterior uveitis with renal disease, predominantly in adolescents |
| [[Fuchs heterochromic iridocyclitis]] | Chronic low-grade cyclitis, [[iris heterochromia]], diffuse stellate KPs, absence of synechiae |
| Drug-induced | [[Rifabutin]], [[cidofovir]], [[bisphosphonates]], [[checkpoint inhibitors]], [[sulfonamides]] |

### Infectious Causes

| **Pathogen/Condition** | **Key Features** |
|---|---|
| [[Herpes simplex virus]] (HSV) | Unilateral, elevated IOP, sectoral iris atrophy |
| [[Varicella-zoster virus]] (VZV) | [[Herpes zoster ophthalmicus]], sectoral iris atrophy |
| [[Cytomegalovirus]] (CMV) | [[Posner-Schlossman syndrome]] (glaucomatocyclitic crisis), coin-shaped KPs |
| [[Toxoplasma gondii]] | Retinochoroiditis with spillover anterior uveitis/cyclitis |
| [[Treponema pallidum]] ([[Syphilis]]) | "Great imitator," granulomatous or non-granulomatous |
| [[Mycobacterium tuberculosis]] | Granulomatous, broad-based synechiae, [[Koeppe nodules]] |
| [[Lyme disease]] ([[Borrelia burgdorferi]]) | Intermediate uveitis, vitritis |
| [[Propionibacterium acnes]] | Chronic postoperative endophthalmitis mimicking cyclitis |
| Endogenous [[endophthalmitis]] | Fungal (Candida) or bacterial seeding |

### Idiopathic
In a significant proportion of cases (up to 30–50% in some series), no identifiable systemic or infectious etiology is found despite thorough investigation, and the cyclitis is classified as **idiopathic**.

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of cyclitis involves a complex interplay of immune-mediated mechanisms leading to disruption of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]] (BAB) and subsequent intraocular inflammation.

### Blood-Aqueous Barrier Breakdown
The [[blood-aqueous barrier]] is maintained by tight junctions between the [[non-pigmented ciliary epithelium]] cells and the [[endothelial cells]] of the iris vasculature. In cyclitis, inflammatory mediators cause disruption of these tight junctions, leading to:

1. **Protein leakage** (flare) into the [[anterior chamber]] — detected clinically as [[aqueous flare]] on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]
2. **Cellular infiltration** — inflammatory cells (predominantly [[lymphocytes]], [[macrophages]], and [[plasma cells]]) migrate into the aqueous humor and vitreous cavity
3. **Fibrin exudation** — in severe cases, fibrin strands or membranes may form in the anterior chamber

### Immunological Mechanisms
The inflammatory cascade in non-infectious cyclitis is primarily driven by:

- **T-cell mediated immunity**: CD4+ [[T-helper cells]] (both Th1 and Th17 subsets) play a central role. Th1 cells produce [[interferon-gamma]] (IFN-γ) and [[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] (TNF-α), promoting macrophage activation and granuloma formation. Th17 cells produce [[interleukin-17]] (IL-17), which recruits [[neutrophils]] and amplifies tissue inflammation.
- **Molecular mimicry**: In [[HLA-B27]]-associated cyclitis, microbial antigens may share structural homology with self-antigens expressed on ciliary body tissues, triggering cross-reactive immune responses.
- **Cytokine and chemokine cascades**: Elevated levels of [[IL-1β]], [[IL-6]], [[IL-8]], [[TNF-α]], [[VEGF]], and [[monocyte chemoattractant protein-1]] (MCP-1) have been documented in the aqueous humor of patients with cyclitis. These mediators perpetuate the inflammatory cycle and contribute to complications such as [[cystoid macular edema]].
- **Complement activation**: Both classical and alternative complement pathways may be activated, contributing to tissue damage and amplification of inflammation.

### Granulomatous vs. Non-Granulomatous Inflammation
Cyclitis can be pathologically classified as:

- **Granulomatous**: Characterized by aggregates of [[epithelioid cells]] and [[multinucleated giant cells]] (Langhans-type), surrounded by lymphocytes. Clinically associated with large, greasy [[mutton-fat keratic precipitates]] (KPs), [[Busacca nodules]] (on iris stroma), and [[Koeppe nodules]] (at pupillary margin). Common in [[sarcoidosis]], [[tuberculosis]], [[syphilis]], and [[VKH disease]].
- **Non-granulomatous**: Characterized by diffuse lymphocytic and plasma cell infiltration without organized granuloma formation. Clinically associated with fine, white [[keratic precipitates]], and is more common in [[HLA-B27]]-associated disease and idiopathic cases.

### Consequences of Chronic Inflammation
Persistent or recurrent cyclitis leads to several pathological sequelae:

- **[[Posterior synechiae]]**: Adhesions between the posterior iris surface and the anterior [[lens capsule]], caused by fibrin deposition and organization. Extensive synechiae can lead to [[seclusio pupillae]] (360° synechiae) and [[iris bombé]], causing secondary [[angle-closure glaucoma]].
- **[[Cataract]] formation**: Chronic inflammation and prolonged [[corticosteroid]] use both contribute to [[posterior subcapsular cataract]] development. Inflammatory mediators directly damage the [[lens epithelium]] and alter lens metabolism.
- **[[Cystoid macular edema]] (CME)**: The most common cause of visual loss in cyclitis. Inflammatory mediators, particularly [[VEGF]] and [[prostaglandins]], increase retinal vascular permeability, leading to fluid accumulation in the outer plexiform (Henle's) and inner nuclear layers of the [[macula]].
- **[[Secondary glaucoma]]**: May result from multiple mechanisms including trabecular meshwork obstruction by inflammatory cells and debris ([[trabeculitis]]), peripheral anterior synechiae (PAS), pupillary block from posterior synechiae, and corticosteroid-induced IOP elevation.
- **[[Hypotony]]**: Chronic inflammation of the ciliary body can impair aqueous humor production by the [[ciliary epithelium]], leading to low [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP < 5 mmHg), [[choroidal effusion]], and [[phthisis bulbi]] in end-stage disease.
- **[[Band keratopathy]]**: Calcium deposition in [[Bowman's layer]] of the [[cornea]], particularly in chronic or juvenile-onset cyclitis.
- **[[Vitreous opacities]]**: Inflammatory cells, protein aggregates, and snowball opacities in the [[vitreous humor]], particularly in intermediate uveitis/pars planitis variants.
- **[[Epiretinal membrane]] (ERM) formation**: Chronic inflammation stimulates glial cell proliferation on the [[internal limiting membrane]] of the retina.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

The symptomatology of cyclitis varies depending on the acuity, severity, and underlying etiology. Key symptoms include:

| **Symptom** | **Mechanism** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|---|
| **Deep, aching ocular pain** | [[Ciliary spasm]], trigeminal nerve (V1) irritation | Distinguishes from superficial pain; may radiate to brow/temple |
| **Photophobia** | Ciliary spasm, iris sphincter irritation | Often severe in acute presentations |
| **Blurred vision** | Aqueous flare, cells, CME, posterior synechiae | Degree correlates with inflammation severity |
| **Lacrimation** | Reflex tearing from trigeminal irritation | Non-specific but common |
| **Floaters** | Vitreous cells, snowball opacities | More prominent in intermediate uveitis/pars planitis |
| **Redness** | [[Ciliary flush]] (circumcorneal/perilimbal injection) | Deep injection pattern, distinct from conjunctival injection |
| **Brow ache** | Referred pain via trigeminal nerve | Common in acute cyclitis |

**Important**: In chronic cyclitis, particularly in children with [[juvenile idiopathic arthritis]]-associated uveitis, the condition may be **entirely asymptomatic** despite significant anterior chamber inflammation. This underscores the critical importance of routine [[slit-lamp]] screening in at-risk pediatric populations.

### Signs

#### External Examination
- **[[Ciliary flush]]** (circumcorneal injection): A violaceous ring of deep episcleral and conjunctival vessel dilation surrounding the [[limbus]], reflecting inflammation of the ciliary body and iris root vasculature. This is distinct from the bright red, superficial injection seen in [[conjunctivitis]].
- **Mild [[eyelid edema]]**: May be present in severe cases.

#### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy Findings

##### Cornea
- **[[Keratic precipitates]] (KPs)**: Inflammatory cell deposits on the [[corneal endothelium]], typically distributed in a triangular pattern (Arlt's triangle) on the inferior cornea due to aqueous convection currents.
  - *Fine, white, non-granulomatous KPs*: Small, round, composed predominantly of lymphocytes and macrophages.
  - *Mutton-fat KPs*: Large, greasy, yellowish-white deposits composed of epithelioid cells and macrophages; indicative of granulomatous inflammation.
  - *Stellate KPs*: Distributed diffusely across the endothelium; characteristic of [[Fuchs heterochromic iridocyclitis]] and [[CMV anterior uveitis]].
  - *Old KPs*: Pigmented, shrunken, and hyalinized; indicate prior episodes of inflammation.
- **[[Band keratopathy]]**: Interpalpebral calcium deposition in Bowman's layer in chronic cases.
- **Corneal edema**: May occur secondary to endothelial dysfunction from KP deposition or elevated IOP.

##### Anterior Chamber
- **[[Aqueous cells]]**: Inflammatory cells suspended in the aqueous humor, graded on the SUN scale (0 to 4+) based on the number of cells visible in a 1 mm × 1 mm slit beam at 45–60° angle:

| **SUN Grade** | **Cells per Field** |
|---|---|
| 0 | < 1 |
| 0.5+ | 1–5 |
| 1+ | 6–15 |
| 2+ | 16–25 |
| 3+ | 26–50 |
| 4+ | > 50 |

- **[[Aqueous flare]]**: Protein in the aqueous humor causing a Tyndall effect (light scattering), graded 0 to 4+:

| **SUN Grade** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| 0 | None |
| 1+ | Faint (barely detectable) |
| 2+ | Moderate (iris and lens details clear) |
| 3+ | Marked (iris and lens details hazy) |
| 4+ | Intense (fibrin or plastic aqueous) |

- **[[Hypopyon]]**: Layered collection of white blood cells (predominantly neutrophils) in the inferior anterior chamber angle; seen in severe or [[HLA-B27]]-associated cyclitis, [[Behçet disease]], and infectious etiologies.
- **[[Fibrin]]**: Strands or membranes in the anterior chamber, indicating severe BAB breakdown.

##### Iris
- **[[Posterior synechiae]]**: Adhesions between the posterior iris and anterior lens capsule. May be focal or extensive (360° = [[seclusio pupillae]]).
- **[[Iris nodules]]**:
  - [[Koeppe nodules]]: Small, white nodules at the pupillary margin.
  - [[Busacca nodules]]: Larger nodules on the iris stroma surface; more specific for granulomatous inflammation.
  - [[Berlin nodules]]: Nodules at the anterior chamber angle (visible on [[gonioscopy]]).
- **[[Iris heterochromia]]**: Characteristic of [[Fuchs heterochromic iridocyclitis]]; the affected eye is typically lighter (hypochromic).
- **Iris atrophy**: Sectoral atrophy may suggest herpetic (HSV/VZV) etiology.

##### Lens
- **[[Posterior subcapsular cataract]]**: Common complication of chronic cyclitis and/or prolonged corticosteroid therapy.
- **Pigment deposits on anterior lens capsule**: Remnants of broken posterior synechiae (pigmented Vossius ring pattern in some cases).

##### Intraocular Pressure
- **Elevated IOP**: Due to trabeculitis, trabecular meshwork obstruction by inflammatory debris, peripheral anterior synechiae, pupillary block, or corticosteroid response.
- **Low IOP (hypotony)**: Due to ciliary body shutdown (reduced aqueous production) from severe or chronic inflammation; IOP < 5 mmHg is concerning for impending [[phthisis bulbi]].

#### Posterior Segment Findings
While cyclitis primarily involves the anterior segment, posterior segment involvement may occur, particularly in intermediate uveitis variants:

- **Vitreous cells and haze**: Graded 0 to 4+ using the Nussenblatt/NEI scale.
- **Snowball opacities**: White, round, inflammatory aggregates in the inferior vitreous; characteristic of [[pars planitis]].
- **Snowbanking**: White, fibrovascular exudative material overlying the [[pars plana]] and inferior peripheral retina; pathognomonic for [[pars planitis]].
- **[[Cystoid macular edema]]**: Best detected with [[optical coherence tomography]] (OCT); the most common cause of vision loss.
- **[[Retinal vasculitis]]**: Perivascular sheathing, particularly of peripheral retinal veins.
- **[[Epiretinal membrane]]**: Glial proliferation on the macular surface.
- **[[Optic disc edema]]**: May occur in severe or chronic inflammation.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis
The diagnosis of cyclitis is primarily clinical, based on a comprehensive ophthalmic examination including:

1. **Detailed history**: Onset, duration, laterality, recurrence pattern, associated systemic symptoms (joint pain, skin lesions, oral/genital ulcers, respiratory symptoms, neurological symptoms), medication history, sexual history, travel history, and exposure to animals/cats ([[toxoplasmosis]]).
2. **Complete ophthalmic examination**: Visual acuity, pupillary examination (relative afferent pupillary defect assessment), [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]], [[intraocular pressure]] measurement ([[Goldmann applanation tonometry]]), [[gonioscopy]] (to assess angle for PAS, nodules, neovascularization), and dilated [[fundoscopy]] with [[binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy]] (BIO).

### Laboratory Investigations
Targeted laboratory testing is guided by clinical suspicion. A "shotgun" approach to laboratory testing is generally discouraged; instead, investigations should be tailored to the clinical presentation.

| **Suspected Etiology** | **Recommended Investigations** |
|---|---|
| [[HLA-B27]]-associated | [[HLA-B27]] typing, sacroiliac joint radiography, ESR/CRP |
| [[Sarcoidosis]] | Chest X-ray or CT, serum [[ACE]] level, serum [[lysozyme]], [[gallium scan]] or PET-CT, tissue biopsy |
| [[Tuberculosis]] | [[Tuberculin skin test]] (Mantoux/PPD), [[QuantiFERON-TB Gold]] (IGRA), chest X-ray |
| [[Syphilis]] | [[RPR]]/[[VDRL]] (screening), [[FTA-ABS]]/[[TP-PA]] (confirmatory) |
| [[Lyme disease]] | [[ELISA]] for Borrelia antibodies, Western blot confirmation |
| Herpetic (HSV/VZV/CMV) | [[Aqueous humor PCR]] (polymerase chain reaction), [[Goldmann-Witmer coefficient]] |
| [[Toxoplasmosis]] | Serum IgG/IgM antibodies, aqueous humor PCR, Goldmann-Witmer coefficient |
| [[Juvenile idiopathic arthritis]] | [[ANA]] (antinuclear antibody), [[RF]] (rheumatoid factor), [[ESR]], [[CRP]] |
| [[Behçet disease]] | Clinical criteria (International Study Group), [[pathergy test]], HLA-B51 |
| General screening | CBC with differential, ESR, CRP, BUN/creatinine, urinalysis, chest X-ray |

### Imaging Modalities

| **Modality** | **Application in Cyclitis** |
|---|---|
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)** | Detection and quantification of [[cystoid macular edema]], [[epiretinal membrane]], subretinal fluid; monitoring treatment response |
| **[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA)** | Detection of CME (petaloid hyperfluorescence), retinal vasculitis (vessel wall staining/leakage), optic disc leakage, capillary non-perfusion |
| **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | High-resolution imaging of the [[ciliary body]], detection of ciliary body edema/thickening, cyclitic membranes, assessment of [[pars plana]] exudates, evaluation of hypotony etiology |
| **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** | Assessment of vitreous opacities, snowball opacities, retinal detachment, choroidal thickening when media opacity precludes fundoscopy |
| **[[OCT angiography]] (OCTA)** | Non-invasive assessment of macular and peripapillary vasculature, detection of macular ischemia |
| **[[Laser flare photometry]]** | Objective, quantitative measurement of aqueous flare; useful for monitoring subclinical inflammation and treatment response |
| **Chest X-ray / CT chest** | Screening for [[sarcoidosis]] (bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy) and [[tuberculosis]] |
| **MRI brain/orbits** | Evaluation for [[multiple sclerosis]] in patients with intermediate uveitis/pars planitis |

### Anterior Chamber Paracentesis
**[[Aqueous humor analysis]]** via anterior chamber paracentesis (aqueous tap) is indicated when infectious etiology is suspected and cannot be confirmed by serological testing alone. Aqueous humor can be analyzed for:
- **[[PCR]]**: Detection of viral DNA (HSV, VZV, CMV, EBV), *Toxoplasma gondii* DNA, *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* DNA
- **[[Goldmann-Witmer coefficient]]**: Ratio of specific antibody in aqueous humor to serum, corrected for total IgG; a coefficient > 3 indicates local intraocular antibody production
- **Cytology**: Evaluation for malignant cells ([[masquerade syndrome]], e.g., [[primary intraocular lymphoma]], [[retinoblastoma]])
- **Culture**: Bacterial and fungal cultures in suspected infectious endophthalmitis

## Differential Diagnosis

It is critical to differentiate cyclitis from conditions that may mimic its presentation (**[[masquerade syndromes]]**):

| **Category** | **Conditions** |
|---|---|
| **Neoplastic (masquerade)** | [[Primary intraocular lymphoma]] (PIOL/PVRL), [[retinoblastoma]] (pediatric), [[leukemia]], [[uveal melanoma]], [[metastatic carcinoma]] |
| **Non-neoplastic (masquerade)** | [[Retinal detachment]] (rhegmatogenous), [[retained intraocular foreign body]], [[pigment dispersion syndrome]], [[UGH syndrome]] (uveitis-glaucoma-hyphema) |
| **Other inflammatory** | [[Scleritis]] (posterior), [[endophthalmitis]], [[sympathetic ophthalmia]], [[lens-induced uveitis]] (phacoantigenic/phacolytic) |

## Management

### Principles of Treatment
The management of cyclitis follows a stepwise approach guided by the following principles:

1. **Suppress active inflammation** rapidly and effectively
2. **Prevent and manage complications** (synechiae, cataract, glaucoma, CME)
3. **Identify and treat underlying etiology** (infectious vs. non-infectious)
4. **Achieve steroid-free remission** using steroid-sparing immunosuppressive therapy when indicated
5. **Minimize treatment-related side effects**

### Pharmacological Interventions

#### 1. Topical Corticosteroids
Topical [[corticosteroids]] are the first-line treatment for acute cyclitis:

| **Agent** | **Potency** | **Typical Dosing** | **Notes** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Prednisolone acetate]] 1% | High | Q1-2h initially, taper over weeks | Gold standard; shake well before use |
| [[Difluprednate]] 0.05% | Very high (emulsion) | Q2-4h initially | More potent; higher risk of IOP elevation |
| [[Dexamethasone]] 0.1% | High | Q2-4h | Good penetration |
| [[Loteprednol etabonate]] 0.5% | Moderate | QID | Lower risk of IOP elevation; "soft steroid" |
| [[Fluorometholone]] 0.1% | Low-moderate | QID | Limited anterior chamber penetration; better for surface inflammation |

**Tapering protocol**: Aggressive initial dosing (e.g., prednisolone acetate 1% every 1–2 hours while awake) is gradually tapered based on clinical response, typically reducing by one drop per day every 1–2 weeks. Premature or rapid tapering is a common cause of recurrence.

#### 2. Cycloplegic/Mydriatic Agents
[[Cycloplegic]] agents are essential adjunctive therapy in cyclitis:

| **Agent** | **Duration of Action** | **Typical Dosing** | **Purpose** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Atropine]] 1% | 7–14 days | BID-TID | Severe inflammation; strongest cycloplegia |
| [[Homatropine]] 2–5% | 1–3 days | BID-TID | Moderate inflammation |
| [[Cyclopentolate]] 1% | 6–24 hours | BID-TID | Mild-moderate inflammation |

**Rationale**: Cycloplegics relieve pain by relaxing the inflamed [[ciliary muscle]] (reducing ciliary spasm), prevent and break [[posterior synechiae]] by dilating the pupil, and stabilize the [[blood-aqueous barrier]].

#### 3. Periocular Corticosteroid Injections
For cyclitis refractory to topical therapy or with significant posterior segment involvement (CME):

- **[[Sub-Tenon's injection]]** of [[triamcinolone acetonide]] (40 mg/mL): Posterior sub-Tenon's (trans-septal) injection provides sustained local drug delivery. Risks include IOP elevation, ptosis, and rarely globe perforation.
- **Orbital floor injection** of triamcinolone acetonide

#### 4. Intravitreal Corticosteroid Therapy
For refractory CME or significant vitritis:

- **Intravitreal [[triamcinolone acetonide]]** (IVTA, 2–4 mg): Provides potent anti-inflammatory effect but carries risks of cataract progression and IOP elevation.
- **[[Dexamethasone intravitreal implant]]** (Ozurdex®, 0.7 mg): Sustained-release biodegradable implant providing approximately 3–6 months of drug delivery. FDA-approved for non-infectious intermediate and posterior uveitis.
- **[[Fluocinolone acetonide intravitreal implant]]** (Retisert®, 0.59 mg): Non-biodegradable surgically implanted device providing up to 30 months of sustained corticosteroid release. FDA-approved for chronic non-infectious posterior uveitis. High rates of cataract (nearly 100%) and glaucoma requiring surgery (~30–40%).
- **[[Fluocinolone acetonide intravitreal implant]]** (Yutiq®, 0.18 mg): Injectable, non-biodegradable micro-insert providing up to 36 months of low-dose corticosteroid delivery with a more favorable side effect profile than Retisert.

#### 5. Systemic Corticosteroids
Indicated for severe bilateral cyclitis, cyclitis with systemic disease, or cases refractory to local therapy:

- **[[Prednisone]]** (oral): Initial dose of 1–1.5 mg/kg/day (typically 40–80 mg/day), tapered over weeks to months. Long-term use (>7.5 mg/day for >3 months) necessitates steroid-sparing immunosuppressive therapy.
- **Intravenous [[methylprednisolone]]** pulse therapy (1 g/day for 3 days): Reserved for sight-threatening inflammation.

#### 6. Steroid-Sparing Immunosuppressive Therapy (IMT)
Indicated when: (a) corticosteroids cannot be tapered below 7.5 mg/day prednisone equivalent without recurrence, (b) corticosteroid side effects are intolerable, or (c) the underlying disease requires long-term immunosuppression. The approach follows the guidelines established by the Standardization of Uveitis Nomenclature (SUN) Working Group and expert consensus.

| **Drug Class** | **Agent** | **Mechanism** | **Monitoring** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Antimetabolites** | [[Methotrexate]] (7.5–25 mg/week) | Dihydrofolate reductase inhibitor | CBC, LFTs, renal function; supplement with folic acid |
| | [[Mycophenolate mofetil]] (1–3 g/day) | Inosine monophosphate dehydrogenase inhibitor | CBC, LFTs |
| | [[Azathioprine]] (1–3 mg/kg/day) | Purine synthesis inhibitor | CBC, LFTs; check [[TPMT]] genotype before initiation |
| **T-cell inhibitors** | [[Cyclosporine A]] (2.5–5 mg/kg/day) | Calcineurin inhibitor | Blood pressure, renal function, drug levels |
| | [[Tacrolimus]] (0.05–0.15 mg/kg/day) | Calcineurin inhibitor | Blood pressure, renal function, drug levels, glucose |
| **Alkylating agents** | [[Cyclophosphamide]] | DNA cross-linking | CBC, urinalysis (hemorrhagic cystitis risk); reserved for severe/refractory cases |
| | [[Chlorambucil]] | DNA alkylation | CBC; reserved for severe/refractory cases |

#### 7. Biologic Agents
For refractory cyclitis or specific disease indications:

| **Agent** | **Target** | **Indication** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Adalimumab]] (Humira®) | [[TNF-α]] | FDA-approved for non-infectious intermediate, posterior, and panuveitis; first-line biologic |

#### 8. Treatment of Specific Infectious Etiologies

| **Etiology** | **Treatment** |
|---|---|
| HSV/VZV cyclitis | Oral [[valacyclovir]] (1 g TID for VZV; 500 mg TID for HSV) + topical corticosteroids + cycloplegics |
| CMV cyclitis | Oral [[valganciclovir]] (900 mg BID induction, 450 mg BID maintenance) or intravitreal [[ganciclovir]] |
| Syphilitic cyclitis | IV [[penicillin G]] (18–24 million units/day × 10–14 days) per neurosyphilis protocol |
| Tubercular cyclitis | Standard anti-TB therapy ([[RIPE]]: rifampin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide, ethambutol) + systemic corticosteroids |
| Toxoplasmic cyclitis | [[Pyrimethamine]] + [[sulfadiazine]] + folinic acid (classic triple therapy); or [[trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole]]; + corticosteroids (after 24–48h of antimicrobial coverage) |
| Lyme-associated cyclitis | Oral [[doxycycline]] (100 mg BID × 14–28 days) or IV [[ceftriaxone]] |

### Non-Pharmacological and Surgical Management

#### Laser Therapy
- **Peripheral retinal [[laser photocoagulation]]**: Indicated in pars planitis with peripheral retinal neovascularization or non-perfusion. Cryotherapy to the pars plana region (peripheral [[retinal cryopexy]]) has also been used historically but is less favored due to increased inflammation.

#### Surgical Interventions

| **Procedure** | **Indication** |
|---|---|
| **[[Pars plana vitrectomy]] (PPV)** | Refractory vitreous opacities, non-clearing vitreous hemorrhage, epiretinal membrane removal, diagnostic vitreous biopsy, tractional retinal detachment |
| **[[Cataract surgery]]** ([[phacoemulsification]] + IOL) | Visually significant cataract; requires quiescence of inflammation for ≥3 months preoperatively; aggressive perioperative anti-inflammatory prophylaxis |
| **[[Glaucoma surgery]]** | IOP uncontrolled with maximum medical therapy; options include [[trabeculectomy]] with [[mitomycin C]], [[glaucoma drainage device]] (Ahmed, Baerveldt), [[minimally invasive glaucoma surgery]] (MIGS) |
| **[[Band keratopathy]] removal** | Visually significant calcium deposits; [[EDTA chelation]] (disodium edetate 1.5–3%) or [[excimer laser]] [[phototherapeutic keratectomy]] (PTK) |
| **Synechiolysis** | Lysis of posterior synechiae during surgery or with pharmacological mydriasis |

## Prognosis

The prognosis of cyclitis depends on the underlying etiology, chronicity, severity, and promptness of treatment:

- **Acute, non-infectious cyclitis** (e.g., HLA-B27-associated): Generally favorable with appropriate treatment; most episodes resolve within weeks to months. However, recurrences are common (up to 50% within 5 years).
- **Chronic cyclitis** (e.g., sarcoidosis, JIA-associated, pars planitis): Requires long-term management; higher risk of complications including cataract (up to 50–70%), glaucoma (15–30%), and CME (20–40%).
- **Infectious cyclitis**: Prognosis depends on timely identification and treatment of the causative organism. Delayed treatment of herpetic or syphilitic cyclitis can result in permanent structural damage.
- **Vision-threatening complications**: CME remains the leading cause of visual morbidity. Early detection with OCT and aggressive treatment significantly improve visual outcomes.

## Special Considerations

### Pediatric Cyclitis
- [[Juvenile idiopathic arthritis]] (JIA) is the most common systemic association with chronic anterior uveitis/cyclitis in children.
- Often **asymptomatic** ("white eye" uveitis) — screening guidelines recommend slit-lamp examination every 3–4 months for high-risk children (ANA-positive, oligoarticular JIA, age of onset < 7 years).
- Higher risk of [[amblyopia]], [[band keratopathy]], and [[posterior synechiae]] due to delayed diagnosis.
- [[Methotrexate]] is the first-line steroid-sparing agent; [[adalimumab]] is the first-line biologic.

### Cyclitis in Pregnancy
- Many immunosuppressive agents are contraindicated (methotrexate [Category X], mycophenolate [Category D]).
- Topical and periocular corticosteroids are generally considered safe.
- [[Cyclosporine]] and [[azathioprine]] may be used with caution under specialist guidance.
- Disease activity may fluctuate during pregnancy and postpartum.

### Fuchs Heterochromic Iridocyclitis (FHI)
- A distinct clinical entity characterized by chronic, low-grade cyclitis with characteristic features: [[iris heterochromia]], diffuse stellate KPs, absence of posterior synechiae, and fine angle vessels (Amsler sign on gonioscopy).
- Typically does not require aggressive anti-inflammatory therapy.
- Complications include cataract (common) and glaucoma; CME is rare.
- Recent evidence suggests an association with [[rubella virus]] (or CMV in some populations) infection of the eye.

---

## Summary

Cyclitis represents a clinically significant form of [[intraocular inflammation]] centered on the [[ciliary body]]. Its diverse etiological spectrum—ranging from autoimmune and autoinflammatory conditions to infectious agents—demands a systematic diagnostic approach. The cornerstone of management involves rapid suppression of inflammation with [[corticosteroids]] and [[cycloplegics]], identification and treatment of underlying causes, and judicious use of steroid-sparing [[immunosuppressive therapy]] and [[biologic agents]] for chronic or refractory disease. Vigilant monitoring for complications, particularly [[cystoid macular edema]], [[cataract]], [[glaucoma]], and [[hypotony]], is essential to preserving visual function. Collaborative care between [[optometrists]], [[ophthalmologists]], and relevant medical specialists (rheumatology, infectious disease, pulmonology) optimizes patient outcomes.
