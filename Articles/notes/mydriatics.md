

# Mydriatics

---

**Mydriatics** are a class of pharmacological agents that induce **[[mydriasis]]** — the dilation of the [[pupil]] — by acting on the [[iris]] musculature. They represent one of the most frequently utilized categories of ophthalmic drugs in clinical [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]], serving indispensable roles in diagnostic examination, therapeutic intervention, and surgical preparation. Mydriatic agents achieve pupillary dilation either by stimulating the [[dilator pupillae muscle]] (sympathomimetic mechanism) or by inhibiting the [[sphincter pupillae muscle]] (parasympatholytic/anticholinergic mechanism). Many parasympatholytic mydriatics simultaneously induce **[[cycloplegia]]** — paralysis of the [[ciliary muscle]] — making them invaluable for accurate [[refraction]] in specific patient populations. A thorough understanding of the pharmacology, clinical indications, contraindications, adverse effects, and patient-specific considerations of mydriatic agents is essential for every eye care practitioner.

---

## History and Development

The use of substances to dilate the pupil dates back centuries. Extracts of the plant *[[Atropa belladonna]]* (meaning "beautiful lady" in Italian) were used by women during the Renaissance to dilate their pupils for cosmetic purposes, lending the plant its name. The active compound, [[atropine]], was isolated in the early 19th century and became the first widely used mydriatic and [[cycloplegic]] agent in clinical medicine. Over the subsequent decades, shorter-acting derivatives such as [[homatropine]], [[cyclopentolate]], and [[tropicamide]] were developed to provide clinicians with agents of varying onset, duration, and cycloplegic potency. On the sympathomimetic side, [[phenylephrine]] emerged as the primary adrenergic mydriatic, offering pupil dilation without significant cycloplegia. The evolution of mydriatic pharmacology has been driven by the clinical need for agents that balance effective dilation with minimal systemic side effects and rapid recovery of normal pupillary and accommodative function.

## Classification of Mydriatic Agents

Mydriatics are broadly classified into two pharmacological categories based on their mechanism of action:

### 1. Parasympatholytic (Anticholinergic / Muscarinic Antagonist) Mydriatics

These agents block [[muscarinic receptors]] (specifically **M3 subtype**) on the [[sphincter pupillae muscle]] and the [[ciliary muscle]], resulting in both mydriasis and cycloplegia.

### 2. Sympathomimetic (Adrenergic Agonist) Mydriatics

These agents stimulate **[[alpha-1 adrenergic receptors]]** on the [[dilator pupillae muscle]], causing active pupillary dilation without significant cycloplegia.

| **Agent** | **Class** | **Concentration(s)** | **Onset** | **Peak Mydriasis** | **Duration of Mydriasis** | **Cycloplegia** | **Primary Clinical Use** |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| [[Tropicamide]] | Anticholinergic | 0.5%, 1.0% | 15–30 min | 20–40 min | 4–6 hours | Mild/Minimal | Routine fundus examination |
| [[Cyclopentolate]] | Anticholinergic | 0.5%, 1.0%, 2.0% | 15–45 min | 30–60 min | 6–24 hours | Moderate–Strong | Cycloplegic refraction |
| [[Atropine]] | Anticholinergic | 0.5%, 1.0% | 30–40 min | 60–180 min | 7–14 days | Maximum | Cycloplegic refraction (pediatric), penalization therapy, uveitis |
| [[Homatropine]] | Anticholinergic | 2.0%, 5.0% | 15–30 min | 30–90 min | 1–3 days | Moderate | Therapeutic mydriasis/cycloplegia, uveitis |
| [[Scopolamine]] (Hyoscine) | Anticholinergic | 0.25% | 15–30 min | 20–60 min | 3–7 days | Strong | Uveitis, cycloplegic refraction |
| [[Phenylephrine]] | Sympathomimetic (α1-agonist) | 2.5%, 10% | 10–20 min | 15–60 min | 3–5 hours | None | Diagnostic dilation, combination with anticholinergics |
| [[Hydroxyamphetamine]] | Sympathomimetic (indirect) | 1.0% | 15–45 min | 30–60 min | 4–6 hours | None | Pharmacological testing ([[Horner syndrome]]) |

## Pathophysiology and Mechanism of Action

### Anatomy of the Iris and Pupillary Control

The [[pupil]] size is regulated by two smooth muscles within the [[iris]]:

1. **[[Sphincter pupillae muscle]] (pupillary sphincter):** A ring of smooth muscle fibers arranged circumferentially around the pupillary margin. It is innervated by **[[parasympathetic nerve fibers]]** originating from the [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]], traveling via the [[oculomotor nerve]] (cranial nerve III), synapsing in the [[ciliary ganglion]], and reaching the iris via the **[[short posterior ciliary nerves]]**. Activation of this muscle causes **[[miosis]]** (pupillary constriction). The neurotransmitter is [[acetylcholine]], acting on **[[muscarinic receptors]]** (M3 subtype).

2. **[[Dilator pupillae muscle]] (pupillary dilator):** Radially oriented myoepithelial fibers extending from the iris root to the pupillary margin. It is innervated by **[[sympathetic nerve fibers]]** originating from the [[hypothalamus]], descending to the [[ciliospinal center of Budge]] (C8–T2), ascending via the [[superior cervical ganglion]], and reaching the iris via the **[[long posterior ciliary nerves]]**. Activation causes **[[mydriasis]]** (pupillary dilation). The neurotransmitter is [[norepinephrine]], acting on **[[alpha-1 adrenergic receptors]]**.

The resting pupil size represents a dynamic equilibrium between parasympathetic (constricting) and sympathetic (dilating) tone.

### Parasympatholytic Mechanism

Anticholinergic mydriatics competitively block [[acetylcholine]] at the **M3 muscarinic receptors** on the sphincter pupillae muscle. By removing parasympathetic tone, the unopposed sympathetic input to the dilator pupillae causes the pupil to dilate. Simultaneously, blockade of muscarinic receptors on the [[ciliary muscle]] inhibits its contraction, resulting in **[[cycloplegia]]** — the paralysis of [[accommodation]]. The degree and duration of cycloplegia vary among agents depending on their receptor binding affinity and tissue penetration:

- **[[Atropine]]** has the highest binding affinity and longest duration, producing complete cycloplegia lasting up to 2 weeks.
- **[[Tropicamide]]** has the lowest binding affinity, producing only mild, transient cycloplegia.

### Sympathomimetic Mechanism

**[[Phenylephrine]]**, a direct-acting **α1-adrenergic agonist**, stimulates the dilator pupillae muscle, causing active radial contraction and pupillary dilation. Because the ciliary muscle is primarily under parasympathetic control and lacks significant adrenergic innervation relevant to accommodation, phenylephrine produces **mydriasis without clinically significant cycloplegia**. This distinction is critically important when the clinician desires dilation for fundus examination without disrupting the patient's accommodative function.

**[[Hydroxyamphetamine]]** acts indirectly by stimulating the release of endogenous [[norepinephrine]] from intact postganglionic sympathetic nerve terminals. Its primary clinical utility is in the pharmacological localization of lesions in [[Horner syndrome]] rather than routine dilation.

### Synergistic Combination Therapy

In clinical practice, a **parasympatholytic agent** (e.g., tropicamide 1%) is frequently combined with a **sympathomimetic agent** (e.g., phenylephrine 2.5%) to achieve maximal mydriasis. This combination exploits both mechanisms simultaneously — relaxation of the sphincter and active contraction of the dilator — producing more rapid, more complete, and more reliable dilation than either agent alone. This is particularly important in patients with darkly pigmented irides, where melanin binding in the [[iris stroma]] can reduce the bioavailability of topical agents and result in slower or incomplete dilation with a single agent.

## Clinical Indications

Mydriatic agents are employed across a wide spectrum of diagnostic and therapeutic scenarios in eye care:

### Diagnostic Indications

- **[[Dilated fundus examination]] (DFE):** The most common indication. Mydriasis allows comprehensive visualization of the [[retina]], [[optic nerve head]], [[macula]], [[retinal vasculature]], and peripheral retina using [[binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy]] (BIO), [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] with condensing lenses (e.g., [[Volk 90D]], [[78D]]), or [[fundus photography]].
- **[[Cycloplegic refraction]]:** Anticholinergic mydriatics with strong cycloplegic properties (atropine, cyclopentolate) are used to temporarily paralyze the ciliary muscle, eliminating [[accommodative spasm]] and revealing the true [[refractive error]]. This is especially critical in:
  - **Pediatric patients** (children tend to over-accommodate, masking [[hyperopia]])
  - **[[Accommodative esotropia]]** evaluation
  - **Suspected [[latent hyperopia]]** in young adults
  - **Post-[[refractive surgery]]** assessment
- **[[Fundus fluorescein angiography]] (FFA)** and **[[Indocyanine green angiography]] (ICGA):** Adequate mydriasis is essential for high-quality retinal imaging.
- **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT):** While not always mandatory, dilation improves signal quality and allows wider field scanning, particularly for peripheral pathology.
- **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]:** Mydriasis may facilitate correlation with clinical findings.
- **Pharmacological pupil testing:** Specific mydriatic agents are used diagnostically:
  - **[[Cocaine]] test (4–10%):** Blocks norepinephrine reuptake; failure to dilate confirms [[Horner syndrome]].
  - **[[Hydroxyamphetamine]] test (1%):** Differentiates preganglionic from postganglionic Horner syndrome.
  - **Dilute [[pilocarpine]] (0.1%):** Tests for [[cholinergic supersensitivity]] in [[Adie tonic pupil]].

### Therapeutic Indications

- **[[Anterior uveitis]] / [[Iritis]] / [[Iridocyclitis]]:** Mydriatic-cycloplegic agents (atropine, homatropine, cyclopentolate) are cornerstone therapy to:
  - **Prevent and break [[posterior synechiae]]** (adhesions between the [[iris]] and [[anterior lens capsule]])
  - **Relieve ciliary spasm** and associated deep aching pain
  - **Stabilize the [[blood-aqueous barrier]]**
  - **Improve patient comfort**
- **[[Amblyopia]] treatment (penalization therapy):** [[Atropine]] 1% instilled in the sound eye blurs near vision, forcing use of the amblyopic eye. This is an evidence-based alternative to [[occlusion therapy]] (patching), supported by the **[[Pediatric Eye Disease Investigator Group]] (PEDIG)** studies.
- **[[Myopia]] control:** Low-concentration [[atropine]] (0.01%–0.05%) has emerged as a pharmacological strategy to slow [[axial length]] elongation and [[myopia progression]] in children, supported by landmark studies including the **[[ATOM]]** and **[[LAMP]]** trials.
- **Pre-operative and intra-operative dilation:** Adequate mydriasis is essential for [[cataract surgery]], [[vitreoretinal surgery]], and [[laser photocoagulation]] procedures. Intracameral mydriatics (e.g., [[phenylephrine]]-[[ketorolac]] intraocular solution, marketed as **Omidria®**) are used to maintain intraoperative mydriasis and prevent [[intraoperative floppy iris syndrome]] (IFIS).

## Symptoms and Clinical Effects of Mydriasis

### Expected Pharmacological Effects

Patients undergoing pharmacological mydriasis will experience the following predictable effects:

- **Pupillary dilation:** The pupil enlarges, typically to 6–8 mm or greater depending on the agent, concentration, and iris pigmentation.
- **Photophobia / Light sensitivity:** The dilated pupil allows excessive light entry, causing significant discomfort in bright environments. Patients should be advised to wear [[sunglasses]] or tinted lenses after dilation.
- **Blurred near vision:** Particularly with cycloplegic agents, patients experience difficulty with near tasks (reading, phone use) due to paralysis of [[accommodation]]. The duration depends on the agent used.
- **Reduced depth of field:** Related to the larger pupil aperture.
- **Glare and halos:** Increased light scatter through the dilated pupil and peripheral [[crystalline lens]] opacities.

### Duration of Effects by Agent

| **Agent** | **Mydriasis Recovery** | **Cycloplegia Recovery** | **Patient Advisory** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tropicamide 1% | 4–6 hours | 2–4 hours (minimal) | Avoid driving for ~4 hours; wear sunglasses |
| Phenylephrine 2.5% | 3–5 hours | N/A | Sunglasses recommended |
| Cyclopentolate 1% | 6–24 hours | 12–24 hours | Avoid near work/driving for ~24 hours |
| Homatropine 5% | 1–3 days | 1–3 days | Prolonged near blur; plan accordingly |
| Atropine 1% | 7–14 days | 7–14 days | Significant lifestyle impact; full counseling required |

## Adverse Effects and Complications

### Ocular Adverse Effects

- **Acute [[angle-closure glaucoma]]:** The most serious ocular complication. Mydriasis causes the peripheral iris to bunch up, potentially occluding the [[trabecular meshwork]] in eyes with anatomically narrow [[anterior chamber angles]]. This is more common in:
  - [[Hyperopic]] eyes (shorter [[axial length]], shallower anterior chamber)
  - Elderly patients (increased [[lens]] thickness)
  - Asian and Inuit populations (higher prevalence of narrow angles)
  - Eyes with [[plateau iris configuration]]
  
  **Risk mitigation:** Perform [[gonioscopy]] or [[anterior segment OCT]] angle assessment before dilation in at-risk patients. The [[Van Herick technique]] at the [[slit lamp]] provides a rapid screening estimate of angle depth.

- **Allergic [[contact dermatitis]]** and **[[conjunctivitis]]:** Particularly with repeated use. [[Tropicamide]] and [[phenylephrine]] preservatives (e.g., [[benzalkonium chloride]]) may contribute.
- **Corneal epithelial toxicity:** Rare with standard use but possible with frequent instillation.
- **Elevation of [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP):** Transient IOP elevation may occur even in open-angle eyes due to changes in aqueous dynamics.
- **Rebound [[miosis]]:** Occasionally observed after phenylephrine wears off.

### Systemic Adverse Effects

Topical ophthalmic mydriatics can be absorbed systemically via the **[[nasolacrimal duct]]** and nasal mucosa, potentially causing significant systemic effects, particularly in pediatric and elderly patients:

#### Anticholinergic Systemic Toxicity

Systemic absorption of anticholinergic mydriatics can produce a classic **anticholinergic syndrome** (mnemonic: *"Hot as a hare, dry as a bone, red as a beet, mad as a hatter, blind as a bat"*):

| **System** | **Manifestation** |
|---|---|
| Cardiovascular | [[Tachycardia]], [[arrhythmia]], flushing |
| Central Nervous System | Confusion, hallucinations, agitation, seizures (especially cyclopentolate in children), drowsiness, ataxia |
| Gastrointestinal | Dry mouth, decreased bowel motility, nausea |
| Genitourinary | Urinary retention |
| Dermatologic | Dry skin, flushing, fever |
| Respiratory | Dry mucous membranes |

**Cyclopentolate** is particularly notorious for CNS toxicity in neonates and young children, including psychotic reactions, seizures, and feeding intolerance. **Atropine** toxicity can be life-threatening in infants.

#### Sympathomimetic Systemic Toxicity

**[[Phenylephrine]] 10%** carries significant cardiovascular risk:

- **Severe [[hypertension]]**
- **[[Tachycardia]] or reflex [[bradycardia]]**
- **[[Myocardial infarction]]** (reported cases)
- **[[Subarachnoid hemorrhage]]** (reported cases)
- **[[Cardiac arrhythmia]]**

For this reason, **phenylephrine 2.5%** is strongly preferred over the 10% concentration, particularly in:
- Neonates and infants
- Elderly patients
- Patients with [[hypertension]], [[coronary artery disease]], [[aneurysm]], or [[hyperthyroidism]]
- Patients taking [[monoamine oxidase inhibitors]] (MAOIs), [[tricyclic antidepressants]], or [[beta-blockers]]

### Minimizing Systemic Absorption

Practitioners should employ the following techniques to reduce systemic absorption:

1. **[[Punctal occlusion]]:** Firm digital pressure over the [[lacrimal puncta]] and [[nasolacrimal sac]] for 1–2 minutes after instillation.
2. **Eyelid closure:** Gentle lid closure for 1–2 minutes reduces drainage.
3. **Minimal dosing:** Use the lowest effective concentration and fewest drops necessary.
4. **Blotting excess:** Wiping away overflow from the periocular skin.
5. **Appropriate concentration selection:** Use phenylephrine 2.5% (not 10%) in at-risk populations; use cyclopentolate 0.5% in neonates.

## Contraindications and Precautions

### Absolute Contraindications

- **Known hypersensitivity** to the specific mydriatic agent or its preservatives
- **Untreated [[narrow-angle glaucoma]]** or documented **[[primary angle-closure glaucoma]]** (for all mydriatics)
- **Phenylephrine 10%** in neonates, low-birth-weight infants, and elderly patients with severe cardiovascular disease

### Relative Contraindications and Precautions

| **Condition** | **Concern** | **Recommendation** |
|---|---|---|
| Shallow [[anterior chamber]] / narrow angles | Risk of [[acute angle closure]] | Perform [[gonioscopy]]; consider [[laser peripheral iridotomy]] prior to dilation |
| [[Down syndrome]] | Increased sensitivity to atropine and other anticholinergics | Use lower concentrations; monitor closely |
| Premature neonates | Immature metabolism; risk of systemic toxicity | Use lowest concentrations (cyclopentolate 0.5%, phenylephrine 2.5%); single drop; punctal occlusion |
| Elderly patients | Cardiovascular risk (phenylephrine); anticholinergic burden | Avoid phenylephrine 10%; assess polypharmacy |
| [[Pregnancy]] and [[lactation]] | Limited safety data; potential fetal/neonatal effects | Use only when clearly indicated; tropicamide preferred |
| Patients on [[MAOIs]] | Potentiation of sympathomimetic effects | Avoid phenylephrine or use with extreme caution |
| [[Iris]]-supported [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) | Dilation may dislocate the IOL | Avoid mydriatics or use with extreme caution |
| History of [[IFIS]] / [[tamsulosin]] use | Poor dilation; intraoperative complications | Alert surgeon; consider intracameral agents |

## Diagnostic Considerations Related to Mydriatics

### Pre-Dilation Assessment

Before instilling any mydriatic agent, the clinician must perform:

1. **[[Case history]]:** Inquire about prior adverse reactions to dilating drops, systemic medications (especially MAOIs, tricyclic antidepressants, alpha-blockers like [[tamsulosin]]), cardiovascular history, and history of [[angle-closure glaucoma]].
2. **[[Anterior chamber depth]] assessment:** Using the [[Van Herick technique]] (slit-lamp estimation of the peripheral anterior chamber depth relative to corneal thickness). A grade ≤2 (AC depth < ½ corneal thickness) warrants further evaluation.
3. **[[Gonioscopy]]:** The gold standard for angle assessment. Should be performed in any patient with suspected narrow angles before dilation.
4. **[[Anterior segment OCT]]:** Provides objective, quantitative angle measurements and can identify [[plateau iris configuration]].
5. **Baseline [[pupil]] assessment:** Document resting pupil size, shape, symmetry, and reactivity. Note any pre-existing [[anisocoria]], [[afferent pupillary defect]] ([[RAPD]]), or iris abnormalities (e.g., [[posterior synechiae]], [[iris atrophy]], [[pseudoexfoliation]]).
6. **Baseline [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP):** Particularly important in patients with known [[glaucoma]] or ocular hypertension.

### Post-Dilation Assessment

After adequate dilation is achieved (typically 20–30 minutes post-instillation):

- **Verify adequate mydriasis:** Pupil diameter ≥6 mm is generally considered adequate for comprehensive fundus examination.
- **Perform the indicated examination:** [[Dilated fundus examination]], [[cycloplegic refraction]], imaging, etc.
- **Re-check IOP** if clinically indicated (e.g., narrow angle suspects, glaucoma patients).
- **Document** the agent(s) used, concentration(s), number of drops, time of instillation, and any adverse reactions.

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Patients

- **Neonates and infants (<1 year):** Use **cyclopentolate 0.5%** and/or **phenylephrine 2.5%** — single drop per eye with punctal occlusion. Avoid atropine 1% unless specifically indicated (use 0.5% if needed). Monitor for systemic toxicity (feeding intolerance, abdominal distension, tachycardia, flushing, CNS depression).
- **Children (1–12 years):** **Cyclopentolate 1%** is the standard cycloplegic agent for refraction. For maximum cycloplegia (e.g., accommodative esotropia workup), **atropine 1%** administered at home for 3 days prior to examination remains the gold standard.
- **Myopia control:** **Low-dose atropine (0.01%–0.05%)** is administered nightly for years. Side effects at these concentrations are minimal (slight mydriasis, minimal cycloplegia, mild photophobia).

### Geriatric Patients

- Increased risk of **systemic cardiovascular effects** from phenylephrine.
- Higher prevalence of **narrow angles** and **[[pseudoexfoliation syndrome]]**.
- Greater **anticholinergic burden** when combined with systemic medications (e.g., antihistamines, antidepressants, antipsychotics, bladder antimuscarinics).
- Slower drug metabolism may prolong effects.

### Patients with Darkly Pigmented Irides

- [[Melanin]] in the iris stroma binds mydriatic agents, reducing bioavailability and slowing onset.
- May require **higher concentrations**, **additional drops**, or **combination therapy** (tropicamide + phenylephrine) to achieve adequate dilation.
- Allow **additional time** (30–45 minutes) before examination.

## Emerging Developments

### Microdose and Spray Delivery Systems

**Mydcombi®** (tropicamide 1% / phenylephrine 2.5% ophthalmic spray) received FDA approval and represents a paradigm shift in mydriatic delivery. The microdose spray delivers a precisely metered dose to the ocular surface, significantly reducing the volume of drug administered compared to traditional eyedrops. This results in:
- Reduced systemic absorption
- Improved patient comfort (no drop sensation)
- More consistent dosing
- Reduced preservative exposure

### Pharmacogenomics

Research into genetic polymorphisms affecting [[muscarinic receptor]] expression, [[melanin]] content, and drug-metabolizing enzymes may eventually allow personalized mydriatic regimens tailored to individual patients.

### Low-Dose Atropine for Myopia Control

The use of **low-concentration atropine** for [[myopia]] control continues to be one of the most active areas of research in pediatric ophthalmology and optometry. The **[[LAMP study]]** (Low-Concentration Atropine for Myopia Progression) demonstrated that **atropine 0.05%** provided the best balance of efficacy and minimal side effects, though optimal concentration, duration of treatment, and rebound effects remain subjects of ongoing investigation.

## Medicolegal and Informed Consent Considerations

Practitioners should be aware of the following medicolegal aspects of mydriatic use:

- **Informed consent:** Patients (or guardians) should be informed of the purpose of dilation, expected effects (blurred vision, photophobia), duration, driving restrictions, and potential risks (including the rare risk of angle closure).
- **Documentation:** Record the agent(s), concentration(s), time of instillation, and any adverse events.
- **Driving advisory:** Patients should be advised that driving may be impaired after dilation, particularly with cycloplegic agents. Some jurisdictions have specific legal standards regarding visual acuity and driving after pharmacological dilation.
- **Occupational considerations:** Patients whose occupations require fine near vision or precise visual acuity should be counseled about the duration of effects and may need to plan accordingly.

---

## Summary

Mydriatic agents are foundational tools in the practice of [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]]. Their rational use requires a comprehensive understanding of [[ocular pharmacology]], [[iris]] anatomy, [[pupillary pathways]], and the clinical context in which they are employed. The choice of agent, concentration, and delivery method must be individualized based on the clinical indication, patient age, iris pigmentation, systemic health, concurrent medications, and anterior chamber anatomy. Vigilance for both ocular and systemic adverse effects — particularly in vulnerable populations such as neonates, the elderly, and patients with cardiovascular disease — is paramount. Advances in drug delivery (microdose sprays, intracameral formulations) and novel applications (low-dose atropine for myopia control) continue to expand the clinical utility and safety profile of this essential drug class.
