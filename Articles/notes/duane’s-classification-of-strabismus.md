

# Duane's Classification of Strabismus

---

**Duane's Classification of Strabismus** refers to the systematic categorization framework originally proposed by **Alexander Duane** in 1896 for the clinical classification of [[strabismus]] (ocular misalignment). This classification system organized the various forms of strabismus into coherent groups based on the direction of deviation, comitancy, and underlying etiology, and it served as a foundational schema in the clinical evaluation of [[binocular vision]] disorders for over a century. Duane's work represented one of the earliest rigorous attempts to bring order to the complex and heterogeneous spectrum of ocular motility disturbances, and its influence persists in modern [[orthoptics]] and [[pediatric ophthalmology]].

> **Note:** Duane's Classification of Strabismus should not be confused with **[[Duane Retraction Syndrome]]** (also known as Duane's Syndrome or Stilling-Türk-Duane Syndrome), which is a specific congenital cranial dysinnervation disorder. While Alexander Duane also described the retraction syndrome that bears his name, the classification system discussed in this article pertains to his broader taxonomic framework for all forms of strabismus.

---

## Historical Background

Alexander Duane (1858–1926) was an American [[ophthalmologist]] who made seminal contributions to the understanding of [[ocular motility]], [[accommodation]], and [[binocular vision]]. Practicing primarily in New York, Duane published extensively on disorders of eye movement and alignment. His 1896 classification, elaborated upon in subsequent editions of his textbook and clinical writings, sought to organize strabismus into categories that were clinically meaningful and diagnostically useful.

Prior to Duane's work, the classification of strabismus was inconsistent and often confusing. Various authors used overlapping and contradictory terminology. Duane synthesized the existing knowledge of his era—drawing upon the work of predecessors such as [[Albrecht von Graefe]], [[Louis Émile Javal]], [[Frans Cornelis Donders]], and [[Hermann von Helmholtz]]—and proposed a system that distinguished between different forms of [[esotropia]], [[exotropia]], and [[vertical deviations]] based on clinical features, age of onset, refractive status, and the behavior of the deviation under different conditions of fixation and accommodation.

Duane's classification was widely adopted in clinical practice and formed the basis upon which later classification systems—including those of **Costenbader**, **von Noorden**, and the modern **International Strabismological Association** frameworks—were built. Understanding Duane's original schema remains essential for appreciating the evolution of strabismus taxonomy and for interpreting historical ophthalmic literature.

---

## Fundamental Principles of Duane's Classification

Duane's classification rested on several key clinical principles:

1. **Direction of Deviation**: The primary axis of misalignment—horizontal ([[esotropia]] or [[exotropia]]), vertical ([[hypertropia]] or [[hypotropia]]), or cyclotorsional ([[cyclotropia]])—served as the first-order division.

2. **Comitancy vs. Incomitancy**: Duane distinguished between **[[comitant strabismus]]** (also termed concomitant strabismus), in which the angle of deviation remains relatively constant across all positions of gaze, and **[[incomitant strabismus]]** (also termed noncomitant or paralytic strabismus), in which the angle of deviation varies with gaze direction, typically due to [[extraocular muscle]] paresis or restriction.

3. **Age of Onset**: Duane recognized the critical importance of whether strabismus was **congenital** (or infantile, presenting within the first six months of life) versus **acquired** (developing later in childhood or adulthood), as this distinction carried profound implications for [[sensory adaptation]], [[amblyopia]] risk, and prognosis for [[stereopsis]] recovery.

4. **Relationship to Accommodation and Refraction**: Duane placed particular emphasis on the role of the [[accommodation]]-[[convergence]] relationship, distinguishing forms of esotropia that were driven by uncorrected [[hyperopia]] and excessive accommodative convergence from those that were not.

5. **Laterality**: Whether the strabismus was **unilateral** (one eye consistently deviating) or **alternating** (either eye capable of fixating while the other deviates) was an important feature in Duane's system, particularly regarding [[amblyopia]] risk.

## Classification Schema

### I. Comitant (Concomitant) Strabismus

Duane's classification of [[comitant strabismus]] encompassed deviations in which the angle of misalignment was approximately equal in all directions of gaze, implying that the [[extraocular muscles]] themselves were functioning normally but that the supranuclear control of binocular alignment was disrupted.

#### A. Comitant Esotropia (Convergent Strabismus)

Duane subdivided [[esotropia]]—the inward turning of one or both eyes—into several clinically distinct categories:

| **Subtype** | **Key Features** | **Typical Age of Onset** | **Refractive Association** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Infantile (Congenital) Esotropia** | Large, constant angle; cross-fixation pattern; associated with [[latent nystagmus]], [[dissociated vertical deviation]] (DVD), and [[inferior oblique overaction]] | Birth to 6 months | Variable; often low to moderate hyperopia |
| **Accommodative Esotropia** | Deviation driven by accommodative effort; improves or resolves with appropriate [[hyperopic]] correction | 6 months to 7 years (peak 2–3 years) | Moderate to high [[hyperopia]] (typically ≥ +2.00 to +6.00 D) |
| **Partially Accommodative Esotropia** | Accommodative component present but residual deviation persists even with full hyperopic correction | Variable | Moderate hyperopia with residual non-accommodative component |
| **Non-Accommodative (Essential) Esotropia** | Constant deviation unrelated to accommodative effort; does not improve with refractive correction | Variable | Variable; may be [[emmetropic]] or mildly hyperopic |
| **Acquired Non-Accommodative Esotropia** | Sudden or gradual onset of constant esotropia in older children or adults; may follow illness, trauma, or decompensation of a pre-existing [[esophoria]] | Childhood to adulthood | Variable |
| **Cyclic Esotropia** | Esotropia present on alternate days (typically 48-hour cycle); rare | Childhood | Variable |
| **Sensory Deprivation Esotropia** | Secondary to unilateral visual loss (e.g., from [[cataract]], [[optic nerve]] disease, or [[retinal]] pathology); the poorly seeing eye drifts inward | Any age | N/A |

##### Accommodative Esotropia: Detailed Subclassification

Duane's emphasis on the [[accommodation]]-[[convergence]] linkage led to further refinement of accommodative esotropia:

- **Refractive Accommodative Esotropia**: The deviation is entirely attributable to the accommodative effort required to overcome uncorrected [[hyperopia]]. The [[AC/A ratio]] (accommodative convergence to accommodation ratio) is normal. Full hyperopic correction with [[spectacles]] or [[contact lenses]] eliminates the deviation.

- **Non-Refractive Accommodative Esotropia (High AC/A Ratio Esotropia)**: The deviation is greater at near fixation than at distance, reflecting an abnormally high [[AC/A ratio]]. These patients generate an excessive convergence response per unit of accommodation. Management may require [[bifocal lenses]] or [[progressive addition lenses]] to reduce accommodative demand at near.

- **Mixed Mechanism Accommodative Esotropia**: A combination of significant hyperopia and an elevated AC/A ratio contributes to the deviation.

#### B. Comitant Exotropia (Divergent Strabismus)

Duane classified [[exotropia]]—the outward turning of one or both eyes—into the following categories:

| **Subtype** | **Key Features** | **Typical Presentation** |
|---|---|---|
| **Intermittent Exotropia** | The most common form; deviation is intermittent, often manifest during fatigue, illness, inattention, or distance fixation; [[fusional convergence]] maintains alignment at other times | Childhood; often noticed by parents during daydreaming or bright sunlight (reflexive closure of one eye) |
| **Constant Exotropia** | Persistent outward deviation; may evolve from longstanding intermittent exotropia (decompensation) or arise de novo | Variable |
| **Infantile (Congenital) Exotropia** | Large, constant exotropia present from birth or early infancy; rare compared to infantile esotropia; must exclude neurological causes | Birth to 6 months |
| **Sensory Deprivation Exotropia** | Secondary to unilateral visual loss; the poorly seeing eye drifts outward (more common in older children and adults with acquired visual loss) | Any age |

Duane further recognized the clinical subclassification of [[intermittent exotropia]] based on the distance-near relationship of the deviation:

- **Basic Type**: Deviation approximately equal at distance and near.
- **Divergence Excess Type**: Deviation significantly greater at distance than at near.
- **Convergence Insufficiency Type**: Deviation greater at near than at distance.
- **Pseudo-Divergence Excess Type**: Appears as divergence excess, but after monocular occlusion (to dissipate [[tenacious proximal fusion]]), the near deviation increases to approximate the distance deviation.

#### C. Comitant Vertical Deviations

While Duane's system primarily emphasized horizontal deviations (reflecting their far greater prevalence), he acknowledged comitant vertical deviations including:

- **[[Dissociated Vertical Deviation]] (DVD)**: An upward drifting of the non-fixating eye, often bilateral and asymmetric, commonly associated with [[infantile esotropia]]. DVD is unique in that it does not obey [[Hering's law]] of equal innervation.
- **Comitant [[hypertropia]]**: Relatively uncommon in isolation; when present, must be distinguished from incomitant vertical deviations due to [[cranial nerve]] palsies or [[extraocular muscle]] pathology.

### II. Incomitant (Noncomitant / Paralytic) Strabismus

Duane classified [[incomitant strabismus]] as deviations in which the angle of misalignment varies with the direction of gaze, typically increasing when the eyes move into the field of action of the affected muscle. This category encompasses:

#### A. Paralytic Strabismus (Cranial Nerve Palsies)

| **Cranial Nerve** | **Muscle(s) Affected** | **Primary Deviation Pattern** | **Key Clinical Features** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Oculomotor nerve]] (CN III) Palsy** | [[Medial rectus]], [[superior rectus]], [[inferior rectus]], [[inferior oblique]], [[levator palpebrae superioris]]; also carries [[parasympathetic]] fibers to [[pupillary sphincter]] and [[ciliary muscle]] | Exotropia and hypotropia ("down and out" position); [[ptosis]]; [[mydriasis]] (if pupil involved) | May be complete or partial; pupil involvement suggests compressive etiology (e.g., [[posterior communicating artery aneurysm]]); pupil-sparing palsy more commonly ischemic |
| **[[Trochlear nerve]] (CN IV) Palsy** | [[Superior oblique]] | Hypertropia of the affected eye, worse in contralateral gaze and ipsilateral head tilt ([[Bielschowsky head tilt test]] positive) | Most common isolated [[cranial nerve]] palsy affecting ocular motility; may be congenital or acquired (trauma is the most common acquired cause) |
| **[[Abducens nerve]] (CN VI) Palsy** | [[Lateral rectus]] | Esotropia, worse on ipsilateral gaze | Most common acquired ocular motor nerve palsy; etiologies include ischemia (in adults with [[diabetes mellitus]] or [[hypertension]]), increased [[intracranial pressure]], trauma, and neoplasm |

Duane emphasized the importance of distinguishing **primary deviation** (the deviation measured when the non-paretic eye fixates) from **secondary deviation** (the deviation measured when the paretic eye fixates), noting that the secondary deviation is always larger than the primary deviation in paralytic strabismus, in accordance with [[Hering's law of equal innervation]].

#### B. Restrictive Strabismus

Duane recognized that incomitant strabismus could also result from mechanical restriction of eye movement rather than neural paresis. Key examples include:

- **[[Thyroid eye disease]]** ([[Graves' ophthalmopathy]]): Fibrotic enlargement of [[extraocular muscles]], most commonly the [[inferior rectus]] (causing restriction of elevation) and [[medial rectus]] (causing restriction of abduction).
- **[[Orbital floor fracture]]** with entrapment of the [[inferior rectus]] or orbital soft tissues, causing restriction of elevation.
- **Post-surgical restriction**: Scarring or excessive tightening following previous [[strabismus surgery]].
- **[[Brown syndrome]]**: Restriction of elevation in adduction due to a tight or inelastic [[superior oblique]] tendon-trochlea complex.

#### C. Neuromuscular Junction Disorders

- **[[Myasthenia gravis]]**: Fatigable weakness of [[extraocular muscles]] that can mimic virtually any pattern of ocular motility disturbance. Duane and subsequent authors recognized the importance of considering [[myasthenia gravis]] in any patient with variable, incomitant strabismus, particularly when accompanied by [[ptosis]] and fatigability.

---

### III. Special Forms and Mixed Categories

Duane's classification also acknowledged several special forms of strabismus that did not fit neatly into the comitant/incomitant dichotomy:

- **Consecutive Strabismus**: A change in the direction of deviation, most commonly [[consecutive exotropia]] following surgical overcorrection of esotropia, or less commonly, consecutive esotropia following exotropia surgery.
- **Microtropia (Monofixation Syndrome)**: A small-angle strabismus (typically ≤ 8 [[prism diopters]]) with [[peripheral fusion]] but absent [[central fusion]] and reduced [[stereoacuity]]. While fully characterized later by Parks and others, the concept of small-angle deviations with anomalous sensory adaptations was recognized in Duane's era.
- **Nystagmus-Associated Strabismus**: Strabismus occurring in the context of [[nystagmus]], including [[infantile nystagmus syndrome]], where an anomalous head posture may be adopted to place the eyes in the [[null zone]].

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of strabismus, as organized within Duane's classification, varies by category:

### Comitant Strabismus

The precise pathophysiology of comitant strabismus remains incompletely understood, but Duane and subsequent investigators identified several contributing mechanisms:

1. **Accommodative-Convergence Imbalance**: In [[accommodative esotropia]], the fundamental mechanism is an excessive convergence response driven by the effort to accommodate for uncorrected [[hyperopia]]. The neural linkage between the [[accommodation]] center and the [[convergence]] center in the [[brainstem]] ([[near reflex]] triad) means that any increase in accommodative demand produces a proportional increase in convergence. When this convergence exceeds the patient's [[fusional divergence]] amplitude, manifest esotropia results.

2. **Innervational Imbalance**: In non-accommodative forms, the deviation is thought to arise from a supranuclear imbalance in the tonic innervation to the [[extraocular muscles]], possibly involving the [[vergence]] control centers in the [[midbrain]] and [[cerebellum]].

3. **Anatomical Factors**: Variations in [[orbital anatomy]], [[extraocular muscle]] insertion positions, muscle [[elasticity]], and [[pulley system]] geometry (the connective tissue pulleys through which the extraocular muscles pass) may predispose to comitant strabismus.

4. **Sensory Deprivation**: When one eye has significantly reduced visual acuity (from [[amblyopia]], [[cataract]], [[corneal opacity]], [[optic nerve]] disease, or [[retinal]] pathology), the absence of a clear retinal image eliminates the stimulus for [[bifoveal fixation]], and the deprived eye drifts into a position of rest—typically esotropia in young children and exotropia in older children and adults.

5. **Genetic Factors**: Family studies have demonstrated a significant hereditary component to comitant strabismus, with [[multifactorial inheritance]] patterns. First-degree relatives of patients with strabismus have a significantly elevated risk of developing strabismus themselves.

### Incomitant Strabismus

The pathophysiology of incomitant strabismus is more directly attributable to identifiable lesions:

- **Neural**: Damage to [[cranial nerves]] III, IV, or VI at any point along their course—from the [[brainstem]] nuclei to the [[extraocular muscles]]—produces characteristic patterns of weakness. Etiologies include ischemia (particularly in patients with [[diabetes mellitus]], [[hypertension]], and [[atherosclerosis]]), compression (by [[aneurysm]], [[tumor]], or [[herniation]]), inflammation, trauma, and demyelination.

- **Muscular**: Primary [[myopathy]] of the extraocular muscles, as seen in [[chronic progressive external ophthalmoplegia]] (CPEO), [[mitochondrial myopathy]], or inflammatory [[orbital myositis]].

- **Neuromuscular Junction**: Impaired [[acetylcholine]] transmission at the [[neuromuscular junction]], as in [[myasthenia gravis]], produces fatigable weakness.

- **Mechanical/Restrictive**: Physical restriction of globe rotation by fibrotic muscles (as in [[thyroid eye disease]]), entrapped tissue (as in [[orbital fracture]]), or tight tendons (as in [[Brown syndrome]]).

## Symptoms and Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

The symptoms of strabismus vary depending on the age of onset, type, and magnitude of the deviation:

| **Symptom** | **Typical Association** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Diplopia]] (Double Vision)** | Acquired strabismus in older children and adults | Simultaneous stimulation of non-corresponding retinal points in the two eyes |
| **[[Asthenopia]] (Eye Strain)** | Intermittent deviations; decompensating [[phoria]] | Excessive effort to maintain [[fusion]] |
| **Blurred Vision** | Accommodative esotropia (if uncorrected hyperopia); amblyopic eye | Refractive error; cortical suppression |
| **Head Turn or Tilt** | Incomitant strabismus (especially CN IV palsy); nystagmus | Adoption of a gaze position that minimizes the deviation or places eyes in the null zone |
| **Closure of One Eye** | Intermittent exotropia in bright light | Elimination of diplopia or visual confusion |
| **Cosmetic Concern** | All forms of manifest strabismus | Visible misalignment of the eyes |
| **Loss of Depth Perception** | All forms, especially constant unilateral strabismus | Disruption of [[stereopsis]] |

### Sensory Adaptations in Childhood-Onset Strabismus

Duane and his contemporaries recognized that children with early-onset strabismus develop [[sensory adaptations]] that are not available to adults:

- **[[Suppression]]**: The cortical inhibition of the image from the deviating eye, preventing [[diplopia]] and [[visual confusion]]. Suppression is gaze-dependent in incomitant strabismus and may be constant in comitant strabismus.
- **[[Amblyopia]]**: Reduced [[best-corrected visual acuity]] in the habitually deviating eye, resulting from chronic suppression and abnormal visual development during the [[critical period]].
- **[[Anomalous Retinal Correspondence]] (ARC)**: A remapping of the directional values of retinal points such that the [[fovea]] of the fixating eye corresponds with an extrafoveal point in the deviating eye, providing a rudimentary form of binocular cooperation.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

The diagnostic evaluation of strabismus, following Duane's principles, involves a systematic assessment:

1. **Visual Acuity Assessment**: Measurement of [[best-corrected visual acuity]] in each eye to detect [[amblyopia]]. Age-appropriate methods include [[Teller acuity cards]] (infants), [[Lea symbols]] or [[HOTV]] (preschool children), and [[Snellen]] or [[ETDRS]] charts (older children and adults).

2. **[[Cover Test]] and [[Cover-Uncover Test]]**:
   - The **cover-uncover test** detects manifest deviations ([[tropia]]): covering the fixating eye forces the deviating eye to take up fixation, producing a visible movement.
   - The **alternating cover test** reveals the total deviation ([[tropia]] + [[phoria]]) by repeatedly alternating the occluder between the two eyes, preventing fusion.
   - The direction and magnitude of the deviation are quantified using [[prism bars]] or loose [[prisms]] (prism and alternate cover test, or PACT).

3. **Assessment of Comitancy**: The deviation is measured in multiple positions of gaze (typically the nine diagnostic positions of gaze) to determine whether the angle is constant (comitant) or variable (incomitant). This is critical for classification within Duane's framework.

4. **[[Hirschberg Test]] and [[Krimsky Test]]**: In uncooperative patients (especially infants), the position of the [[corneal light reflex]] provides an estimate of the deviation. Each millimeter of decentration of the reflex corresponds to approximately 22 [[prism diopters]] of deviation (Hirschberg). The Krimsky test uses prisms to center the reflex.

5. **[[Cycloplegic Refraction]]**: Essential in all children with strabismus, particularly esotropia, to quantify the full magnitude of [[hyperopia]] (or other [[refractive error]]). [[Cycloplegic]] agents such as [[cyclopentolate]] or [[atropine]] are used to paralyze the [[ciliary muscle]] and reveal the latent refractive error. This step is critical for identifying [[accommodative esotropia]].

6. **Assessment of [[Stereopsis]]**: Tests such as the [[Titmus stereo test]], [[Randot stereo test]], [[Lang stereo test]], or [[TNO stereo test]] quantify the level of [[binocular vision]]. Reduced or absent stereopsis indicates disrupted binocularity.

7. **Assessment of [[Ductions]] and [[Versions]]**: Monocular (ductions) and binocular (versions) eye movements are evaluated to identify limitations of movement suggestive of paresis or restriction.

8. **[[Forced Duction Test]]**: Performed under topical or general [[anesthesia]], this test distinguishes restrictive from paralytic causes of limited eye movement. If the globe cannot be passively rotated into the limited field, restriction is present.

9. **[[Parks Three-Step Test]]**: Specifically used for vertical deviations, this test identifies the paretic [[cyclovertical muscle]] by determining (1) which eye is hypertropic, (2) whether the hypertropia increases in right or left gaze, and (3) whether it increases on right or left head tilt.

10. **Measurement of [[AC/A Ratio]]**: The ratio of accommodative convergence to accommodation is measured using the **heterophoria method** or the **gradient method** (measuring the deviation with and without plus lenses at near). An elevated AC/A ratio (>6:1) suggests non-refractive accommodative esotropia.

### Ancillary Diagnostic Modalities

- **[[Neuroimaging]]** ([[MRI]] or [[CT]]): Indicated in acquired incomitant strabismus to rule out intracranial pathology (e.g., [[tumor]], [[aneurysm]], [[demyelination]], [[hemorrhage]]).
- **[[Hess screen test]] / [[Lancaster red-green test]]**: Graphically maps the deviation in multiple gaze positions, useful for documenting and monitoring incomitant deviations.
- **[[Electromyography]] (EMG)**: Rarely used clinically but can assess extraocular muscle innervation patterns.
- **Laboratory studies**: [[Anti-acetylcholine receptor antibodies]], [[thyroid function tests]], [[erythrocyte sedimentation rate]], [[blood glucose]], and other tests as indicated by the clinical picture.

## Treatment and Management

### General Principles

The goals of strabismus treatment, consistent with Duane's clinical philosophy, include:

1. **Elimination or reduction of the deviation** (achieving ocular alignment)
2. **Treatment of [[amblyopia]]** (restoring equal visual acuity)
3. **Restoration or preservation of [[binocular vision]]** and [[stereopsis]]
4. **Elimination of abnormal head posture**
5. **Improvement of cosmetic appearance**

### Optical Treatment

- **Full [[hyperopic]] correction**: The cornerstone of management for [[accommodative esotropia]]. Prescribing the full cycloplegic refraction (or near-full correction) reduces the accommodative demand and thereby reduces the convergence drive. In purely refractive accommodative esotropia, spectacles alone may fully correct the deviation.
- **[[Bifocal lenses]] or [[progressive addition lenses]]**: Indicated for non-refractive accommodative esotropia (high AC/A ratio type), where the deviation is controlled at distance with hyperopic correction but persists at near. An executive-style bifocal with the segment line bisecting the [[pupil]] is traditionally prescribed, with a typical add of +2.50 to +3.00 D.
- **[[Myopic]] correction**: In exotropia, full correction of [[myopia]] (or slight overcorrection) may help stimulate accommodative convergence and reduce the exodeviation.
- **[[Prism]] therapy**: [[Fresnel prisms]] or ground-in prisms can be incorporated into spectacles to optically compensate for small to moderate deviations, particularly in patients with diplopia who are not surgical candidates or as a temporizing measure.

### Amblyopia Treatment

- **[[Occlusion therapy]] (Patching)**: The gold standard for [[amblyopia]] treatment. The sound eye is patched for a prescribed number of hours per day to force use of the amblyopic eye. Dosing is guided by the severity of amblyopia and the patient's age.
- **[[Atropine penalization]]**: Instillation of [[atropine sulfate]] 1% in the sound eye blurs near vision, encouraging use of the amblyopic eye. An alternative to patching, particularly in patients with poor compliance.
- **Optical penalization**: Deliberate undercorrection or overcorrection of the sound eye's refractive error to blur its vision.

### Orthoptic / Vision Therapy

- **[[Fusional vergence]] training**: Exercises to improve [[fusional convergence]] (for exotropia) or [[fusional divergence]] (for esotropia) amplitudes. Most effective for [[convergence insufficiency]] type intermittent exotropia.
- **Anti-suppression therapy**: Techniques to reduce cortical [[suppression]] and promote [[binocular vision]].
- **Accommodative facility training**: Exercises to improve the speed and accuracy of the [[accommodative response]].

### Pharmacological Interventions

- **[[Botulinum toxin]] (Botox) injection**: Injection of [[botulinum toxin type A]] into an [[extraocular muscle]] produces temporary chemodenervation and weakening of the injected muscle. Applications include:
  - Acute [[sixth nerve palsy]] (injection into the ipsilateral [[medial rectus]] to prevent contracture while awaiting recovery)
  - Small-angle residual deviations after surgery
  - Diagnostic or therapeutic trial before definitive surgery
  - Patients who are poor surgical candidates

- **[[Miotics]] (e.g., [[echothiophate iodide]])**: Rarely used today, these [[anticholinesterase]] agents were historically employed to reduce the accommodative effort required for near focus, thereby reducing accommodative convergence in accommodative esotropia. Their use has largely been supplanted by bifocal spectacles due to side effects including [[iris cysts]], [[cataract]], and systemic cholinergic effects.

### Surgical Treatment

[[Strabismus surgery]] is indicated when optical, pharmacological, and orthoptic measures fail to achieve satisfactory alignment, or when the deviation is of a type not amenable to non-surgical management.

#### Surgical Principles

- **[[Recession]]**: The muscle insertion is detached from the globe and reattached at a more posterior position, effectively weakening the muscle's action.
- **[[Resection]]**: A segment of the muscle is excised and the shortened muscle is reattached at its original insertion, effectively strengthening the muscle's action.
- **[[Plication]]**: The muscle is folded upon itself and sutured, achieving a strengthening effect similar to resection but without excising tissue.
- **Adjustable sutures**: The muscle is reattached with a suture that can be adjusted postoperatively (typically within 24 hours) to fine-tune alignment. Particularly useful in adults and cooperative adolescents.
- **[[Inferior oblique]] weakening procedures**: Recession, myectomy, or anteriorization of the [[inferior oblique muscle]], indicated for [[inferior oblique overaction]] commonly associated with [[infantile esotropia]].
- **[[Superior oblique]] tuck or advancement**: Strengthening procedures for the [[superior oblique muscle]] in cases of [[superior oblique]] palsy.

#### Surgical Approaches by Strabismus Type

| **Strabismus Type** | **Typical Surgical Approach** |
|---|---|
| **Infantile Esotropia** | Bilateral [[medial rectus]] recession (most common); may require additional surgery for [[inferior oblique overaction]] or [[dissociated vertical deviation]] |
| **Residual/Non-Accommodative Esotropia** | Bilateral medial rectus recession or unilateral recession-resection (medial rectus recession + [[lateral rectus]] resection) |
| **Intermittent Exotropia** | Bilateral [[lateral rectus]] recession or unilateral recession-resection (lateral rectus recession + medial rectus resection) |
| **Constant Exotropia** | Similar to intermittent exotropia; may require larger surgical doses |
| **CN IV Palsy (Superior Oblique Palsy)** | [[Inferior oblique]] weakening of the ipsilateral eye; [[superior oblique]] tuck; or [[inferior rectus]] recession of the contralateral eye (depending on the pattern) |
| **CN VI Palsy (Lateral Rectus Palsy)** | Medial rectus recession ± lateral rectus resection; in complete palsy, transposition procedures (e.g., [[Hummelsheim procedure]], [[Jensen procedure]], or full tendon transposition with augmentation suture) |

### Prognosis and Follow-Up

- **Accommodative esotropia**: Excellent prognosis with appropriate optical correction; many patients eventually outgrow the need for bifocals as the AC/A ratio normalizes with age. Some patients may eventually outgrow the need for hyperopic correction entirely.
- **Infantile esotropia**: Early surgical alignment (ideally before age 2 years, and some advocate before 6 months) improves the prognosis for developing at least gross [[stereopsis]] ([[peripheral fusion]]). However, fine stereoacuity is rarely achieved.
- **Intermittent exotropia**: Variable natural history; some patients remain stable for years, while others progress to constant exotropia. Surgical success rates are generally good (70–85% alignment within 10 prism diopters at long-term follow-up), though reoperation rates are not insignificant.
- **Paralytic strabismus**: Prognosis depends on the underlying etiology. Ischemic cranial nerve palsies (e.g., in [[diabetes mellitus]]) typically recover spontaneously within 3–6 months. Compressive or traumatic palsies may require surgical intervention if recovery does not occur.

## Clinical Significance and Legacy

Duane's classification of strabismus established a systematic, clinically oriented framework that transformed the evaluation and management of ocular misalignment. Its emphasis on distinguishing accommodative from non-accommodative esotropia directly influenced treatment paradigms that remain in use today—specifically, the primacy of [[cycloplegic refraction]] and optical correction as the first step in managing childhood esotropia.

While modern classification systems have refined and expanded upon Duane's original schema—incorporating advances in [[neuroimaging]], [[genetics]], [[orbital imaging]], and understanding of [[cranial dysinnervation disorders]]—the fundamental organizational principles he established remain embedded in contemporary clinical practice. The distinction between comitant and incomitant strabismus, the recognition of accommodative factors, and the systematic assessment of deviation in multiple gaze positions all trace their clinical lineage to Duane's foundational work.
