

# Vitreous Base

---

## Definition

The **vitreous base** is a three-dimensional, annular (ring-shaped) zone of dense [[vitreous humor]] attachment that straddles the [[ora serrata]] of the eye, extending approximately 2 mm anterior to the ora serrata over the [[pars plana]] of the [[ciliary body]] and approximately 1–3 mm posterior to the ora serrata onto the peripheral [[retina]]. It represents the strongest and most clinically significant site of [[vitreoretinal adhesion]] in the human eye. Unlike other regions of vitreoretinal attachment—such as the [[optic disc]], the [[fovea]], and along major [[retinal blood vessels]]—the vitreous base is characterized by an interdigitation of [[collagen]] fibrils from the [[vitreous cortex]] that penetrate directly into the [[internal limiting membrane]] (ILM) and the [[basement membrane]] of the non-pigmented [[ciliary epithelium]], creating an essentially inseparable bond. This unique structural relationship makes the vitreous base the last region to separate during [[posterior vitreous detachment]] (PVD) and the most common site of pathological traction leading to [[retinal tears]], [[retinal detachment]], and [[vitreous base avulsion]].

---

## Embryology and Development

The vitreous base begins to form during the secondary and tertiary stages of [[vitreous development]]. During [[embryogenesis]], the primary vitreous (also known as the [[vasa hyaloidea propria]]) is gradually replaced by the secondary (definitive) vitreous, which is an avascular, transparent gel composed predominantly of [[type II collagen]] fibrils and [[hyaluronic acid]] ([[hyaluronan]]). The tertiary vitreous contributes to the formation of the [[zonular fibers]] of the [[crystalline lens]].

During fetal development, the vitreous base is established as collagen fibrils from the developing vitreous cortex become firmly anchored into the basal lamina of the peripheral retina and the non-pigmented epithelium of the [[pars plana]]. At birth, the vitreous base extends from approximately 1.5 mm anterior to the ora serrata to approximately 1–2 mm posterior to it. With aging, the posterior border of the vitreous base migrates posteriorly, a process that has significant clinical implications for the development of peripheral retinal pathology in older adults. By the seventh and eighth decades of life, the posterior extent of the vitreous base may reach 3 mm or more behind the ora serrata.

## Anatomy and Histology

### Gross Anatomy

The vitreous base is a circumferential band approximately 3–6 mm in total width (anteroposterior dimension) that encircles the interior of the globe at the junction of the [[pars plana]] and the peripheral retina. It is not a discrete membrane but rather a zone of transition where the density of vitreous collagen fibrils increases dramatically and where these fibrils become physically interwoven with the underlying tissue.

| **Anatomical Feature** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Location** | Straddles the [[ora serrata]], extending anteriorly over the [[pars plana]] and posteriorly over the peripheral [[retina]] |
| **Anterior extent** | ~1.5–2.0 mm anterior to the ora serrata |
| **Posterior extent** | ~1–3 mm posterior to the ora serrata (increases with age) |
| **Width** | ~3–6 mm total anteroposterior dimension |
| **Shape** | Annular (ring-shaped), circumferential |
| **Attachment strength** | Strongest vitreoretinal adhesion in the eye; essentially inseparable |

### Histological Architecture

At the microscopic level, the vitreous base is distinguished by the following features:

1. **Collagen Fibril Interdigitation**: [[Type II collagen]] fibrils from the vitreous cortex pass perpendicularly through the [[internal limiting membrane]] (ILM) of the retina and insert directly into the basement membrane of the non-pigmented [[ciliary epithelium]] anteriorly. This creates a Velcro-like interdigitation that is far stronger than the tangential orientation of collagen fibrils found in other regions of the vitreous cortex.

2. **Increased Fibril Density**: The concentration of collagen fibrils per unit area is markedly higher in the vitreous base compared to the central vitreous or even other areas of the vitreous cortex. This dense packing contributes to the mechanical strength of the adhesion.

3. **Fibrillar Splaying**: As collagen fibrils approach the vitreous base, they splay out and bifurcate, creating a branching network that anchors into the underlying tissue at multiple points. This splaying pattern has been demonstrated by [[scanning electron microscopy]] (SEM) and [[transmission electron microscopy]] (TEM).

4. **Absence of a Cleavage Plane**: In most regions of the vitreous cortex, a natural cleavage plane exists between the posterior vitreous cortex and the ILM, which allows for [[posterior vitreous detachment]]. At the vitreous base, no such cleavage plane exists because the collagen fibrils are physically embedded within the ILM and the underlying basal lamina.

5. **Relationship to the [[Ora Serrata]]**: The ora serrata itself is a scalloped border with approximately 16–18 [[ora bays]] (rounded indentations) and corresponding [[ora teeth]] (dentate processes) in the average adult eye. The vitreous base attachment is particularly strong at the ora teeth and at the [[meridional folds]] and [[meridional complexes]] that are sometimes present in this region.

### Relationship to Adjacent Structures

- **Anteriorly**: The vitreous base blends with the attachment of the vitreous to the [[pars plana]] and, more anteriorly, with the [[anterior hyaloid face]], which is adherent to the posterior surface of the [[crystalline lens]] via the [[ligamentum hyaloideocapsulare]] (Wieger's ligament).
- **Posteriorly**: The vitreous base transitions into the less firmly adherent vitreous cortex overlying the peripheral and equatorial retina.
- **Laterally**: The vitreous base is continuous around the full 360° circumference of the globe.

## Pathophysiology

The vitreous base is central to the pathophysiology of numerous vitreoretinal disorders. Its extraordinary adhesive strength means that when tractional forces are applied—whether from [[vitreous syneresis]], [[posterior vitreous detachment]], or [[ocular trauma]]—the vitreous base does not simply separate from the underlying tissue. Instead, the traction is transmitted directly to the peripheral retina and pars plana, with potentially sight-threatening consequences.

### Age-Related Changes

With aging, the vitreous undergoes two concurrent degenerative processes:

1. **[[Vitreous Syneresis]]** (Liquefaction): The gel structure of the vitreous progressively breaks down, with dissociation of [[hyaluronic acid]] from the collagen fibril network. This leads to the formation of fluid-filled lacunae within the vitreous cavity. By the eighth decade of life, more than 50% of the vitreous volume may be liquefied.

2. **[[Vitreous Synchysis]]**: Aggregation of collagen fibrils into visible bundles, perceived by the patient as [[floaters]] ([[muscae volitantes]]).

These processes lead to progressive collapse of the vitreous gel ([[vitreous syneresis]]) and eventual separation of the posterior vitreous cortex from the retinal surface—a [[posterior vitreous detachment]] (PVD). During PVD, the vitreous separates sequentially from the posterior pole, the equator, and finally approaches the vitreous base. Because the vitreous base adhesion is inseparable, the PVD terminates at the posterior border of the vitreous base. If the separation is incomplete or if anomalous adhesions exist at the posterior border of the vitreous base, significant tractional forces can be generated.

### Posterior Migration of the Vitreous Base

A critically important age-related change is the gradual posterior migration of the posterior border of the vitreous base. This means that in older individuals, the zone of firm vitreoretinal adhesion extends further posteriorly onto the peripheral retina. This posterior migration increases the area of retina subject to tractional forces during PVD and may explain the increased incidence of peripheral [[retinal tears]] and [[retinal detachment]] in elderly patients.

### Mechanisms of Retinal Tear Formation

Retinal tears related to the vitreous base occur through several mechanisms:

| **Mechanism** | **Description** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|---|
| **Traction at the posterior border** | During PVD, anteroposterior and tangential traction at the posterior margin of the vitreous base can create a [[horseshoe tear]] (flap tear) | Most common mechanism of [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]] |
| **Avulsion of the vitreous base** | Severe blunt [[ocular trauma]] can avulse the vitreous base from the underlying tissue, tearing the retina and/or pars plana epithelium | Pathognomonic of blunt ocular trauma; may lead to [[retinal dialysis]] |
| **Traction on [[lattice degeneration]]** | [[Lattice degeneration]] near the posterior border of the vitreous base is subject to increased traction during PVD | Significant risk factor for retinal detachment |
| **Operculated holes** | A small piece of retina may be completely avulsed by vitreous traction, creating a free-floating [[operculum]] | Generally lower risk of retinal detachment than horseshoe tears |

### Vitreous Base Avulsion

**Vitreous base avulsion** is a pathognomonic finding of blunt [[ocular trauma]] (e.g., from a fist, ball, or airbag deployment). In this condition, the extreme forces generated by sudden globe deformation cause the vitreous base—along with its attached retinal and/or pars plana tissue—to be torn away from the underlying structures. The avulsed vitreous base appears as a grayish-white, irregular ring or arc of tissue suspended in the vitreous cavity. This finding is virtually diagnostic of prior blunt trauma and is often associated with:

- [[Retinal dialysis]] (a circumferential tear at the ora serrata)
- [[Giant retinal tear]] (a tear extending ≥90° of the circumference)
- [[Cyclodialysis]] (separation of the [[ciliary body]] from the [[scleral spur]])
- [[Angle recession]] ([[traumatic recession]] of the [[anterior chamber angle]])
- [[Hyphema]]
- [[Lens subluxation]] or [[lens dislocation]]

### Role in Proliferative Vitreoretinopathy (PVR)

The vitreous base serves as a scaffold for cellular proliferation in [[proliferative vitreoretinopathy]] (PVR), a condition in which [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE) cells, [[glial cells]], [[fibroblasts]], and [[macrophages]] migrate onto the vitreous base and form contractile [[epiretinal membranes]]. These membranes exert tractional forces on the peripheral retina, leading to recurrent or persistent [[retinal detachment]]. The vitreous base is the most common site of anterior PVR membrane formation, and surgical management of PVR often requires meticulous dissection and removal of these membranes from the vitreous base—a technically challenging procedure.

### Role in Peripheral Retinal Degenerations

Several peripheral retinal degenerations are anatomically related to the vitreous base:

- **[[Lattice Degeneration]]**: Typically located just posterior to the vitreous base, lattice degeneration is subject to tractional forces during PVD. The combination of lattice degeneration and vitreous base traction significantly increases the risk of retinal tear formation.
- **[[Cystoid Degeneration]]**: Peripheral [[cystoid degeneration]] is nearly universal in adults and is located at the ora serrata within the vitreous base region.
- **[[Retinoschisis]]**: [[Degenerative retinoschisis]] typically begins in areas of peripheral cystoid degeneration near the vitreous base.
- **[[Retinal Tufts]]**: [[Cystic retinal tufts]] and [[non-cystic retinal tufts]] are small elevations of the peripheral retina within or near the vitreous base that may serve as sites of anomalous vitreoretinal adhesion and subsequent tear formation.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

The vitreous base itself is an anatomical structure and does not produce symptoms in its normal state. However, pathological processes involving the vitreous base may produce the following symptoms:

### Symptoms of Vitreous Base-Related Pathology

| **Symptom** | **Mechanism** | **Associated Condition** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Photopsia]]** (flashes of light) | Mechanical stimulation of [[photoreceptors]] by vitreous traction at the vitreous base | [[Posterior vitreous detachment]], [[retinal tear]] |
| **[[Floaters]]** (muscae volitantes) | Vitreous opacities, hemorrhage, or avulsed tissue in the visual axis | PVD, [[vitreous hemorrhage]], vitreous base avulsion |
| **Sudden increase in floaters** | Acute vitreous hemorrhage from a torn retinal vessel at the vitreous base | [[Retinal tear]] with associated vessel rupture |
| **Curtain or shadow in peripheral vision** | Subretinal fluid accumulation from a retinal break at the vitreous base | [[Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]] |
| **Decreased visual acuity** | Vitreous hemorrhage, retinal detachment involving the [[macula]] | Advanced vitreous base-related pathology |
| **Pain** | Typically absent unless associated with trauma or inflammation | [[Ocular trauma]], [[endophthalmitis]] |

### Asymptomatic Findings

Many vitreous base-related findings are discovered incidentally during routine [[dilated fundus examination]]:

- Vitreous base avulsion (in patients with a history of remote trauma)
- Peripheral retinal tears without detachment
- [[Retinal dialysis]] without significant subretinal fluid
- Lattice degeneration near the vitreous base

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

The vitreous base cannot be directly visualized with standard [[direct ophthalmoscopy]] due to its extreme peripheral location. Specialized examination techniques are required:

1. **[[Binocular Indirect Ophthalmoscopy]] (BIO)**: The gold standard for examining the vitreous base and peripheral retina. Using a condensing lens (typically a [[20D lens]] or [[28D lens]]) and [[scleral indentation]] (scleral depression), the examiner can bring the vitreous base into view. Scleral indentation is performed by gently pressing on the globe through the eyelid with a scleral depressor, which rotates the peripheral retina and vitreous base into the examiner's line of sight.

2. **[[Scleral Indentation]]**: An essential adjunct to BIO for complete visualization of the vitreous base. It allows the examiner to:
   - Visualize the ora serrata and pars plana
   - Identify retinal tears, dialyses, and holes at the vitreous base
   - Assess the height and mobility of retinal detachments
   - Detect vitreous base avulsion
   - Evaluate the relationship of peripheral retinal lesions to the vitreous base

3. **[[Goldmann Three-Mirror Lens]] Examination**: A contact lens with angled mirrors that allows [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] of the peripheral retina and vitreous base. The most peripheral mirror (the bullet-shaped or D-shaped mirror) provides visualization of the ora serrata region.

4. **[[Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy]] with Non-Contact Lenses**: Wide-field non-contact lenses (e.g., [[90D lens]], [[78D lens]], [[SuperField lens]]) can provide views of the peripheral retina, though they are generally less effective than BIO with scleral indentation for visualizing the extreme periphery.

### Imaging Modalities

| **Modality** | **Application to Vitreous Base** | **Advantages** | **Limitations** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Ultrasound B-scan]]** | Visualization of vitreous base in eyes with opaque media (e.g., [[vitreous hemorrhage]], dense [[cataract]]) | Can detect retinal detachment, vitreous base avulsion, and intraocular foreign bodies | Operator-dependent; limited resolution |
| **[[Ultrasound Biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | High-resolution imaging of the vitreous base, pars plana, and ciliary body | Excellent resolution of anterior vitreous base structures; can image through opaque media | Limited depth of penetration (~5 mm); requires immersion technique |
| **[[Optical Coherence Tomography]] (OCT)** | Peripheral retinal OCT can image the posterior vitreous base | Non-invasive; high axial resolution | Limited field of view; difficult to image extreme periphery |
| **[[Wide-Field Fundus Photography]]** (e.g., [[Optos]]) | Documentation of peripheral retinal pathology near the vitreous base | Wide field of view (up to 200°); useful for documentation and monitoring | May not capture the most anterior extent of the vitreous base; image distortion at periphery |
| **[[Wide-Field Fluorescein Angiography]]** | Assessment of peripheral retinal vascular pathology near the vitreous base | Can identify [[neovascularization]], [[ischemia]], and vascular leakage | Invasive (requires dye injection); limited to vascular pathology |
| **[[Anterior Segment OCT]] (AS-OCT)** | Imaging of the anterior vitreous base and pars plana region | Non-contact; high resolution | Limited penetration depth; cannot image posterior vitreous base |

### Diagnostic Criteria for Vitreous Base Avulsion

Vitreous base avulsion is diagnosed clinically by the presence of:

- A grayish-white, irregular, arc-shaped or ring-shaped band of tissue visible in the vitreous cavity
- The tissue is typically located in the peripheral vitreous, near the ora serrata
- Associated findings of blunt ocular trauma (angle recession, cyclodialysis, retinal dialysis, lens subluxation)
- The avulsed tissue may be associated with attached retinal pigment epithelium, giving it a pigmented appearance

## Management and Treatment

### General Principles

Management of vitreous base-related pathology depends on the specific condition and its severity. The vitreous base itself is a normal anatomical structure that does not require treatment. However, pathological processes involving the vitreous base—particularly retinal tears, retinal detachment, and vitreous base avulsion—require prompt recognition and appropriate intervention.

### Observation and Monitoring

The following vitreous base-related findings may be managed with observation and periodic monitoring:

- **Asymptomatic retinal holes** without surrounding subretinal fluid
- **Lattice degeneration** without tears or symptoms
- **Cystic retinal tufts** without traction or symptoms
- **Stable, chronic retinal dialysis** with limited subretinal fluid and a demarcation line
- **Old vitreous base avulsion** without active retinal detachment

Patients with these findings should be counseled about the symptoms of retinal detachment (new floaters, photopsia, visual field loss) and instructed to seek immediate evaluation if such symptoms develop.

### Laser Photocoagulation

**[[Laser photocoagulation]]** (typically [[argon laser]] or [[diode laser]]) is the primary treatment for retinal tears and selected retinal breaks at or near the vitreous base:

- **Indications**: Symptomatic horseshoe tears, retinal tears with subclinical subretinal fluid, retinal dialysis with limited subretinal fluid, prophylactic treatment of high-risk lattice degeneration near the vitreous base
- **Technique**: Two to three rows of confluent laser burns are applied around the retinal break, creating a [[chorioretinal adhesion]] that prevents subretinal fluid from extending through the break
- **Delivery methods**: [[Slit-lamp delivery]] with a contact lens, [[indirect ophthalmoscope delivery]] (laser indirect ophthalmoscopy, or LIO), or [[endolaser]] during vitreoretinal surgery
- **Laser indirect ophthalmoscopy (LIO)** is particularly useful for treating peripheral retinal breaks near the vitreous base because it allows treatment with the patient in a supine position and provides excellent visualization of the peripheral retina with scleral indentation

### Cryotherapy

**[[Cryotherapy]]** (cryopexy) is an alternative to laser photocoagulation for treating retinal breaks at the vitreous base:

- **Indications**: Retinal tears or dialyses at the vitreous base, particularly when laser delivery is difficult (e.g., due to media opacity, poor pupil dilation, or anterior location of the break)
- **Technique**: A [[cryoprobe]] is applied to the scleral surface overlying the retinal break, and the tissue is frozen until an ice ball is visible ophthalmoscopically. The freeze-thaw cycle creates an inflammatory adhesion between the retina and the underlying [[retinal pigment epithelium]]/[[choroid]]
- **Advantages**: Can be applied through opaque media; effective for anterior breaks near the ora serrata
- **Disadvantages**: Greater inflammatory response than laser; may promote [[proliferative vitreoretinopathy]] (PVR) if excessive treatment is applied; less precise than laser

### Surgical Approaches

#### Scleral Buckling

**[[Scleral buckling]]** is a surgical technique used to treat [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]] caused by retinal breaks at or near the vitreous base:

- **Principle**: An [[explant]] (silicone sponge or solid silicone band) is sutured to the scleral surface overlying the retinal break, creating an indentation (buckle) that apposes the detached retina to the underlying RPE and relieves vitreous traction on the break
- **Encircling band**: A 360° [[encircling band]] (cerclage) is often placed at the equator to support the vitreous base circumferentially, particularly in cases of multiple breaks, retinal dialysis, or when PVR is present
- **Drainage of subretinal fluid**: May be performed to facilitate retinal reattachment
- **Cryotherapy or laser**: Applied to the retinal break(s) to create a permanent chorioretinal adhesion
- **Scleral buckling is particularly effective** for retinal detachments caused by breaks at the vitreous base in young, phakic patients

#### Pars Plana Vitrectomy (PPV)

**[[Pars plana vitrectomy]]** (PPV) is a microsurgical technique that involves removal of the vitreous gel from the eye. It is used in the management of several vitreous base-related conditions:

- **Indications**: Complex retinal detachment with PVR, giant retinal tear, vitreous hemorrhage obscuring the view of the vitreous base, retained intraocular foreign body near the vitreous base, and cases where scleral buckling alone is insufficient
- **Vitreous base shaving**: During PPV, the surgeon may perform meticulous shaving of the vitreous base using a high-speed vitreous cutter with scleral indentation. This is a critical step in the management of PVR, as residual vitreous at the vitreous base serves as a scaffold for membrane reproliferation. The surgeon must exercise extreme caution during vitreous base shaving to avoid iatrogenic retinal tears.
- **Endolaser**: Applied to retinal breaks and areas of traction at the vitreous base under direct visualization
- **Tamponade**: [[Sulfur hexafluoride]] (SF₆) gas, [[perfluoropropane]] (C₃F₈) gas, or [[silicone oil]] may be used as internal tamponade agents to maintain retinal apposition while the chorioretinal adhesion matures

#### Combined Scleral Buckle and Vitrectomy

In complex cases—particularly those involving PVR, giant retinal tears, or multiple retinal breaks at the vitreous base—a **combined scleral buckle and vitrectomy** approach may be employed. The encircling band provides support to the vitreous base while the vitrectomy allows for membrane peeling, vitreous base shaving, and endolaser treatment.

### Pharmacological Considerations

While there are no pharmacological agents that directly target the vitreous base, several agents are relevant to the management of vitreous base-related pathology:

| **Agent** | **Role** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Ocriplasmin]]** (Jetrea) | Pharmacologic vitreolysis | Recombinant [[protease]] that cleaves [[laminin]] and [[fibronectin]] at the vitreoretinal interface; primarily used for [[vitreomacular traction]], not effective at the vitreous base due to the strength of adhesion |
| **Intravitreal [[corticosteroids]]** | Anti-inflammatory adjunct in PVR management | Reduces inflammation and may inhibit cellular proliferation |
| **Intravitreal [[anti-VEGF]] agents** | Treatment of [[neovascularization]] near the vitreous base | Inhibits [[vascular endothelial growth factor]]; used in proliferative diabetic retinopathy and other neovascular conditions affecting the peripheral retina |
| **[[5-Fluorouracil]] (5-FU)** | Adjunct in PVR prevention/treatment | [[Antimetabolite]] that inhibits fibroblast proliferation |
| **[[Daunorubicin]]** | Adjunct in PVR management | Antiproliferative agent used intraoperatively |

## Complications of Vitreous Base Pathology

| **Complication** | **Mechanism** | **Prognosis** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Rhegmatogenous Retinal Detachment]]** | Retinal tear at the vitreous base allows liquefied vitreous to enter the subretinal space | Good if treated promptly; poor if macula detaches or PVR develops |
| **[[Proliferative Vitreoretinopathy]]** | Cellular proliferation on the vitreous base scaffold | Leading cause of surgical failure in retinal detachment repair |
| **[[Vitreous Hemorrhage]]** | Rupture of retinal vessels at the site of a retinal tear at the vitreous base | Usually resolves spontaneously; may require vitrectomy if persistent |
| **[[Giant Retinal Tear]]** | Extensive tear (≥90°) at the posterior border of the vitreous base | Requires urgent surgical intervention; guarded prognosis |
| **Chronic [[Hypotony]]** | Cyclodialysis associated with vitreous base avulsion | May lead to [[phthisis bulbi]] if untreated |

## Special Considerations

### Pediatric Considerations

In children and young adults, the vitreous gel is more formed and firmly adherent to the retina, and the vitreous base is narrower than in older adults. Posterior vitreous detachment is rare in this age group. However, traumatic vitreous base avulsion and retinal dialysis are relatively more common in young patients due to the higher incidence of blunt ocular trauma in this population. [[Retinal dialysis]] is the most common cause of rhegmatogenous retinal detachment in children and young adults, and it typically occurs at the vitreous base, most commonly in the inferotemporal quadrant.

### Myopia

Patients with high [[myopia]] (axial length >26 mm) have an increased risk of vitreous base-related pathology due to:

- Earlier onset of vitreous syneresis and PVD
- Thinner peripheral retina
- Higher prevalence of [[lattice degeneration]] and other peripheral retinal degenerations
- Greater mechanical stress on the vitreous base due to increased globe size

### Pseudophakia and Aphakia

Patients who have undergone [[cataract surgery]] ([[pseudophakia]]) or lens removal ([[aphakia]]) have an increased risk of retinal detachment, partly due to alterations in vitreous dynamics and an increased rate of PVD following surgery. The vitreous base remains a critical site of traction in these patients.

### Intraoperative Considerations

During [[pars plana vitrectomy]], the surgical instruments (vitreous cutter, light pipe, infusion cannula) are inserted through the [[pars plana]]—the region immediately anterior to the vitreous base. The standard sclerotomy sites are placed 3.5–4.0 mm posterior to the [[limbus]] in phakic eyes and 3.0–3.5 mm in pseudophakic/aphakic eyes to avoid damage to the [[crystalline lens]] and the [[retina]] at the vitreous base. Inadvertent entry through the vitreous base or retina can cause iatrogenic retinal tears or detachment.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of vitreous base-related pathology varies widely depending on the specific condition:

- **Retinal tears treated promptly with laser or cryotherapy**: Excellent prognosis; >95% success rate in preventing retinal detachment
- **Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment from vitreous base tears**: Good prognosis with modern surgical techniques (scleral buckling, vitrectomy); anatomic success rates of 85–95% with a single procedure
- **Retinal detachment complicated by PVR**: Guarded prognosis; may require multiple surgeries; final anatomic reattachment rates of 60–80%, but visual outcomes are often limited
- **Giant retinal tear**: Guarded prognosis; requires specialized surgical techniques; anatomic success rates of 70–90% with modern vitrectomy and tamponade
- **Traumatic vitreous base avulsion with retinal dialysis**: Good prognosis if detected and treated early; scleral buckling is highly effective for retinal dialysis

---

## Summary

The vitreous base is the most clinically significant site of vitreoretinal adhesion in the human eye. Its unique histological architecture—characterized by the interdigitation of vitreous collagen fibrils with the internal limiting membrane and the basement membrane of the ciliary epithelium—creates an essentially inseparable bond that has profound implications for the pathogenesis of retinal tears, retinal detachment, and proliferative vitreoretinopathy. A thorough understanding of the anatomy, age-related changes, and pathological processes involving the vitreous base is essential for all eye care professionals, particularly those involved in the diagnosis and management of vitreoretinal disease. Comprehensive examination of the vitreous base using binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy with scleral indentation remains the gold standard for detecting pathology in this critical region.
