

# Pupillary Block

---

**Pupillary block** is a pathological condition in which the flow of [[aqueous humor]] from the [[posterior chamber]] to the [[anterior chamber]] of the eye is impeded at the level of the [[pupil]], due to apposition or adhesion between the [[iris]] and the [[crystalline lens]], [[intraocular lens]] (IOL), [[vitreous humor]], or other structures. This obstruction creates a pressure differential between the posterior and anterior chambers, causing the peripheral iris to bow anteriorly ([[iris bombé]]), which in turn occludes the [[trabecular meshwork]] and precipitates a sudden and often dramatic rise in [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP). Pupillary block is the most common mechanism underlying [[acute angle-closure glaucoma]] (AACG), accounting for approximately 75–90% of all angle-closure cases worldwide. It represents an ophthalmic emergency that demands prompt recognition and intervention to prevent irreversible damage to the [[optic nerve]] and permanent vision loss.

---

## Epidemiology

Pupillary block and its sequela, [[angle-closure glaucoma]], demonstrate significant demographic and geographic variation. The condition is more prevalent among individuals of East Asian, Southeast Asian, and Inuit descent, populations in which anatomical predispositions such as a shallow [[anterior chamber depth]] (ACD), thick [[iris]], and anteriorly positioned [[crystalline lens]] are more common. Women are affected two to four times more frequently than men, a disparity attributed to shorter [[axial length]], shallower anterior chambers, and thicker lenses. The incidence increases markedly with age, peaking in the sixth and seventh decades of life, correlating with progressive lens thickening and anterior lens migration.

| **Risk Factor** | **Mechanism / Association** |
|---|---|
| Age > 50 years | Progressive [[lens]] thickening and anterior displacement |
| Female sex | Shorter [[axial length]], shallower [[anterior chamber]] |
| East Asian / Inuit ethnicity | Anatomically narrow [[iridocorneal angle]] |
| [[Hyperopia]] | Shorter axial length, crowded anterior segment |
| Family history of [[angle closure]] | Heritable anatomical traits |
| Shallow anterior chamber (< 2.5 mm) | Reduced space for aqueous flow |
| Short axial length (< 21.5 mm) | Crowded anterior segment anatomy |
| Thick crystalline lens | Increased lens–iris contact |
| Anterior lens position | Increased pupillary block force |
| Pharmacological [[mydriasis]] | Mid-dilated pupil maximizes iris–lens contact |

Globally, angle-closure glaucoma is responsible for approximately 50% of glaucoma-related blindness despite being less prevalent than [[open-angle glaucoma]], underscoring the severity and urgency of pupillary block as a clinical entity.

## Anatomy and Aqueous Humor Dynamics

A thorough understanding of pupillary block requires detailed knowledge of the anatomy governing [[aqueous humor]] production and outflow.

### Aqueous Humor Production

Aqueous humor is produced by the [[ciliary body]], specifically the [[ciliary epithelium]] of the [[ciliary processes]], located in the [[posterior chamber]]. Production occurs through three mechanisms: active secretion (the dominant mechanism, involving Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase and carbonic anhydrase), ultrafiltration, and diffusion. The normal rate of aqueous production is approximately 2.0–2.5 µL/min during waking hours, with a significant circadian reduction during sleep.

### Normal Aqueous Flow Pathway

Under physiological conditions, aqueous humor flows from the [[posterior chamber]], through the [[pupil]], into the [[anterior chamber]], and exits primarily via the [[trabecular meshwork]] into [[Schlemm's canal]] (the conventional or pressure-dependent pathway), with a smaller proportion draining through the [[uveoscleral pathway]] (the unconventional or pressure-independent pathway). The passage of aqueous through the pupil is facilitated by a slight physiological pressure gradient between the posterior and anterior chambers (approximately 0.5–1.0 mmHg), which maintains a mildly convex iris configuration in most eyes.

### The Pupillary Block Mechanism

In pupillary block, the normal flow of aqueous through the pupil is obstructed. The degree of contact between the posterior iris surface and the anterior lens surface (or other obstructing structure) determines the resistance to aqueous flow. When this contact becomes sufficiently extensive or firm, aqueous accumulates in the posterior chamber, elevating posterior chamber pressure relative to the anterior chamber. This pressure differential forces the peripheral iris anteriorly, creating the characteristic configuration known as [[iris bombé]]. The anteriorly displaced peripheral iris apposes the [[trabecular meshwork]] and [[peripheral anterior synechiae]] (PAS) may form, obstructing aqueous outflow and causing a rapid, often severe elevation in IOP.

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of pupillary block is a cascade of events that begins with anatomical predisposition and culminates in acute or chronic angle closure.

### Relative Pupillary Block

The term **relative pupillary block** describes the physiological state in which there is some degree of resistance to aqueous flow at the pupillary margin due to normal iris–lens contact. In most individuals, this resistance is minimal and clinically insignificant. However, in anatomically predisposed eyes—those with shallow anterior chambers, thick lenses, or anteriorly positioned lenses—relative pupillary block is exaggerated. The force of pupillary block is maximal when the pupil is in a mid-dilated position (approximately 3.0–4.5 mm diameter), because in this configuration, the iris sphincter is relaxed enough to allow significant lens contact but not so dilated that the iris is pulled taut and away from the lens surface.

### Absolute Pupillary Block

When the resistance to aqueous flow at the pupil becomes complete, **absolute pupillary block** occurs. No aqueous can pass from the posterior to the anterior chamber. Posterior chamber pressure rises rapidly, the iris balloons forward (iris bombé), and the [[iridocorneal angle]] is mechanically closed by the anteriorly displaced peripheral iris. This results in acute obstruction of the [[trabecular meshwork]] and a precipitous rise in IOP, often exceeding 50–70 mmHg.

### Phacomorphic Component

With aging, the [[crystalline lens]] undergoes continuous growth, increasing in anteroposterior thickness at a rate of approximately 0.02 mm per year. This progressive thickening, combined with anterior migration of the lens, narrows the anterior chamber and increases iris–lens contact. In eyes with pre-existing anatomical narrowness, this age-related lens change can convert a subclinical relative pupillary block into an acute absolute pupillary block. An intumescent or [[cataract]]ous lens that swells significantly (phacomorphic mechanism) can dramatically exacerbate pupillary block.

### Inflammatory and Post-Surgical Pupillary Block

Pupillary block may also occur secondary to [[posterior synechiae]]—adhesions between the posterior iris surface and the anterior lens capsule—which develop as a consequence of [[anterior uveitis]] or intraocular inflammation. When posterior synechiae extend 360° around the pupillary margin (seclusio pupillae), aqueous is completely trapped in the posterior chamber, producing iris bombé and secondary angle closure.

In the postoperative setting, pupillary block can occur after [[cataract surgery]] (with or without IOL implantation), [[vitrectomy]], or [[glaucoma surgery]]. Mechanisms include:

- **Aphakic pupillary block**: Vitreous face or residual lens material obstructs the pupil in the absence of a lens.
- **Pseudophakic pupillary block**: The IOL (particularly [[anterior chamber intraocular lens|anterior chamber IOLs]] or posteriorly dislocated IOLs) or the IOL–capsular bag complex creates a seal at the pupillary margin.
- **Aqueous misdirection** (malignant glaucoma): Although a distinct entity, it must be differentiated from pupillary block; in aqueous misdirection, aqueous is directed posteriorly into the [[vitreous cavity]] rather than being blocked at the pupil.

### Molecular and Cellular Considerations

At the cellular level, prolonged elevation of IOP from pupillary block causes mechanical compression and ischemia of the [[retinal ganglion cells]] and their axons at the [[lamina cribrosa]] of the [[optic nerve head]]. Ischemic injury to the [[trabecular meshwork]] endothelium and the [[iris]] sphincter muscle may occur during acute attacks. The [[corneal endothelium]] is also vulnerable, with acute IOP elevation causing endothelial cell dysfunction, stromal edema, and [[Descemet's membrane]] folds. Inflammatory mediators released during the acute episode, including [[prostaglandins]] and cytokines, contribute to breakdown of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]], further complicating the clinical picture.

## Classification

Pupillary block can be classified along several axes:

| **Classification Axis** | **Categories** |
|---|---|
| **Onset** | Acute, subacute (intermittent), chronic |
| **Etiology** | Primary (anatomical), secondary (inflammatory, post-surgical, lens-related) |
| **Lens status** | Phakic, pseudophakic, aphakic |
| **Completeness** | Relative (partial obstruction), absolute (complete obstruction) |
| **Laterality** | Unilateral, bilateral (fellow eye at high risk) |

### Acute Pupillary Block

Characterized by sudden, complete obstruction of aqueous flow through the pupil, resulting in rapid IOP elevation, severe symptoms, and an ophthalmic emergency.

### Subacute (Intermittent) Pupillary Block

Episodes of transient pupillary block that resolve spontaneously (e.g., when the pupil constricts or dilates sufficiently to break the iris–lens seal). Patients may report recurrent episodes of blurred vision, halos around lights, and mild ocular discomfort, often in the evening or in dim illumination.

### Chronic Pupillary Block

Persistent, low-grade pupillary block leading to gradual formation of [[peripheral anterior synechiae]] (PAS), progressive angle closure, and chronic elevation of IOP. This form may be insidious and asymptomatic until significant [[optic nerve]] damage has occurred.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

The symptomatology of pupillary block varies with the acuity and severity of the episode.

| **Symptom** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|
| Severe ocular pain | Acute IOP elevation, corneal epithelial edema, iris ischemia |
| Blurred vision | [[Corneal edema]], microcystic epithelial changes |
| Halos around lights | Corneal epithelial and stromal edema causing light diffraction |
| Frontal headache | Referred pain via [[trigeminal nerve]] (V1) |
| Nausea and vomiting | Vagal response to severe pain and IOP elevation |
| Redness of the eye | [[Ciliary injection]], conjunctival hyperemia |
| Tearing (lacrimation) | Reflex tearing from corneal irritation |
| Fixed, mid-dilated pupil | Iris sphincter ischemia and paralysis |

In subacute or intermittent episodes, symptoms may be milder and self-limited, with patients describing transient visual blurring and colored halos, particularly in low-light conditions.

### Signs

On clinical examination, the following signs are characteristic of acute pupillary block:

- **Elevated IOP**: Typically 40–80 mmHg, measured by [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]] or other tonometric methods.
- **Corneal edema**: Diffuse epithelial and stromal edema with [[Descemet's folds]]; microcystic epithelial changes may be present.
- **Shallow anterior chamber**: Uniformly shallow centrally and peripherally, with the iris in a convex (bombé) configuration.
- **Iris bombé**: Forward bowing of the peripheral iris due to posterior chamber pressure buildup.
- **Mid-dilated, fixed pupil**: The pupil is typically 4–6 mm in diameter, vertically oval, and poorly reactive to light due to iris sphincter ischemia.
- **Ciliary injection**: Circumcorneal (perilimbal) injection reflecting anterior segment inflammation and congestion.
- **Anterior chamber flare and cells**: Breakdown of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]] with aqueous flare and inflammatory cells.
- **Glaukomflecken**: Small, white, anterior subcapsular lens opacities representing focal areas of lens epithelial necrosis from ischemia during the acute attack. These are pathognomonic of a prior acute angle-closure episode.
- **Iris atrophy**: Sectoral or diffuse iris stromal atrophy, particularly of the [[iris sphincter]], may be observed after resolution of an acute attack (sector iris atrophy in a spiral or patchy pattern).
- **Optic disc changes**: In prolonged or recurrent attacks, [[optic disc]] pallor, cupping, or edema may be observed.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Assessment

Diagnosis of pupillary block is primarily clinical, based on the constellation of symptoms, signs, and anatomical findings described above. A systematic approach includes:

1. **History**: Onset, duration, and character of symptoms; precipitating factors (dim lighting, pharmacological mydriasis, prone positioning); prior episodes; medication history (especially [[anticholinergics]], [[sympathomimetics]], [[sulfonamides]], [[topiramate]]); family history of [[glaucoma]] or angle closure.

2. **Visual acuity**: Typically markedly reduced during an acute attack due to corneal edema.

3. **IOP measurement**: [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]] is the gold standard. IOP is markedly elevated (often > 40 mmHg) in acute pupillary block.

4. **Slit-lamp biomicroscopy**: Detailed examination of the [[cornea]] (edema, Descemet's folds), [[anterior chamber]] (depth, cells, flare), [[iris]] (bombé configuration, atrophy, posterior synechiae), [[pupil]] (size, shape, reactivity), and [[lens]] (glaukomflecken, cataract, position).

5. **Gonioscopy**: [[Gonioscopy]] is the definitive method for assessing the [[iridocorneal angle]]. In pupillary block, the angle is appositionally closed (360° or near-360°), with the peripheral iris in contact with the trabecular meshwork. Indentation (dynamic or compression) gonioscopy using a [[Zeiss four-mirror goniolens]] or [[Posner lens]] can differentiate appositional closure (which opens with indentation) from synechial closure (which does not). Gonioscopy should be performed on both eyes, as the fellow eye is at high risk.

### Imaging Modalities

| **Modality** | **Utility in Pupillary Block** |
|---|---|
| [[Anterior segment optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT) | Quantitative measurement of ACD, angle opening distance (AOD), trabecular-iris space area (TISA), iris thickness, lens vault; visualization of iris bombé and angle closure |
| [[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM) | High-resolution imaging of the angle, ciliary body, lens position, and posterior chamber; particularly useful when corneal edema precludes optical imaging |
| [[A-scan ultrasonography]] | Measurement of axial length, anterior chamber depth, and lens thickness |
| [[B-scan ultrasonography]] | Assessment of posterior segment (e.g., to rule out choroidal effusion, posterior segment mass) |
| [[Scheimpflug imaging]] ([[Pentacam]]) | Anterior chamber depth, volume, and angle measurements; lens density assessment |

#### Anterior Segment OCT (AS-OCT)

[[AS-OCT]] provides non-contact, high-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the anterior segment. It is invaluable for quantifying anterior chamber depth, angle width, and iris configuration. In pupillary block, AS-OCT demonstrates a convex iris profile, shallow anterior chamber, and narrow or closed iridocorneal angle. Quantitative parameters such as **angle opening distance at 500 µm (AOD500)**, **trabecular-iris space area (TISA)**, and **lens vault** (the perpendicular distance between the anterior lens pole and the horizontal line connecting the two scleral spurs) are useful for risk stratification and monitoring.

#### Ultrasound Biomicroscopy (UBM)

[[UBM]] uses high-frequency ultrasound (35–50 MHz) to image the anterior segment with a resolution of approximately 25–50 µm. It is particularly useful when corneal edema prevents adequate optical imaging. UBM can visualize the [[ciliary body]], [[zonules]], and posterior chamber, helping to differentiate pupillary block from other mechanisms of angle closure such as [[plateau iris syndrome]], [[aqueous misdirection]], or [[ciliary body]] tumors.

### Provocative Testing

Historically, provocative tests such as the **dark room prone provocative test** and **pharmacological mydriatic provocative test** were used to identify eyes at risk for pupillary block. In these tests, the patient is placed in conditions that promote pupillary dilation (dark room, prone position, or topical mydriatic agents), and IOP is measured before and after. A rise in IOP of ≥ 8 mmHg with concurrent angle narrowing was considered a positive result. However, these tests have fallen out of favor due to low sensitivity and specificity and the risk of precipitating an acute attack. Modern imaging modalities (AS-OCT, UBM) have largely supplanted provocative testing.

### Differential Diagnosis

It is critical to differentiate pupillary block from other causes of acute IOP elevation and angle closure:

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| [[Plateau iris syndrome]] | Flat iris plane (no bombé), anteriorly positioned ciliary processes on UBM, angle closure persists after laser peripheral iridotomy |
| [[Aqueous misdirection]] (malignant glaucoma) | Uniformly shallow anterior chamber (central and peripheral), does not respond to iridotomy, often post-surgical |
| [[Phacomorphic glaucoma]] | Intumescent/swollen cataractous lens pushing iris forward; lens extraction is definitive treatment |
| [[Neovascular glaucoma]] | [[Rubeosis iridis]] (neovascularization of iris), fibrovascular membrane over angle, associated with [[diabetic retinopathy]] or [[central retinal vein occlusion]] |
| [[Suprachoroidal hemorrhage]] | Acute onset post-surgery, shallow anterior chamber, elevated IOP, B-scan shows choroidal hemorrhage |
| [[Lens subluxation/dislocation]] | Asymmetric anterior chamber, [[phacodonesis]], visible zonular dehiscence |

## Management

Management of pupillary block is directed at three goals: (1) acute reduction of IOP, (2) elimination of the pupillary block mechanism, and (3) prevention of recurrence and fellow eye involvement.

### Acute Medical Management

The initial management of an acute pupillary block episode is pharmacological, aimed at rapidly lowering IOP and reducing inflammation to facilitate definitive treatment.

| **Medication** | **Class** | **Mechanism** | **Dose/Route** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Timolol]] 0.5% | [[Beta-adrenergic antagonist]] | Reduces aqueous production | 1 drop topically, may repeat ×1 |
| [[Apraclonidine]] 1% or [[Brimonidine]] 0.2% | [[Alpha-2 adrenergic agonist]] | Reduces aqueous production, increases uveoscleral outflow | 1 drop topically |
| [[Dorzolamide]] 2% or [[Brinzolamide]] 1% | Topical [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitor]] | Reduces aqueous production | 1 drop topically q8h |
| [[Acetazolamide]] 500 mg | Systemic [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitor]] | Reduces aqueous production | Oral or IV |
| [[Mannitol]] 20% (1–2 g/kg) | [[Osmotic agent]] | Reduces vitreous volume, lowers IOP | IV infusion over 30–45 min |
| [[Pilocarpine]] 1–2% | [[Miotic]] ([[muscarinic agonist]]) | Constricts pupil, pulls iris away from lens, opens angle | 1 drop q15 min ×2–4 (after IOP begins to decrease) |
| [[Prednisolone acetate]] 1% | Topical [[corticosteroid]] | Reduces anterior segment inflammation | 1 drop q15–60 min initially |
| Topical [[glycerin]] | Hyperosmotic (topical) | Clears corneal edema to facilitate examination and laser treatment | Applied to corneal surface |

**Important clinical note regarding pilocarpine**: In the setting of extremely high IOP (> 50 mmHg), the iris sphincter muscle is ischemic and paralyzed, rendering pilocarpine ineffective and potentially paradoxically worsening the block by increasing iris–lens contact via the forward movement of the lens–iris diaphragm. Pilocarpine should therefore be administered only after IOP has been partially reduced by other agents. Intensive pilocarpine dosing (e.g., every minute) is contraindicated as it may exacerbate inflammation and cause further anterior rotation of the ciliary body.

### Definitive Treatment: Laser Peripheral Iridotomy (LPI)

**[[Laser peripheral iridotomy]]** (LPI) is the definitive first-line treatment for pupillary block. By creating a full-thickness opening in the peripheral iris, LPI establishes an alternative pathway for aqueous humor to flow from the posterior chamber to the anterior chamber, bypassing the pupillary obstruction and equalizing the pressure between the two chambers. This eliminates the iris bombé and allows the peripheral iris to fall posteriorly, reopening the iridocorneal angle.

#### Technique

- **Laser type**: [[Nd:YAG laser]] (most commonly used; photodisruptive mechanism) or [[argon laser]] (photocoagulative mechanism; may be used as a pre-treatment to thin the iris stroma before Nd:YAG application, particularly in thick, dark irides).
- **Location**: The iridotomy is typically placed in the superior peripheral iris (between 11 and 1 o'clock), where it will be covered by the upper eyelid to minimize visual symptoms such as glare, ghost images, or linear dysphotopsia. An alternative location in the temporal periphery may be chosen in specific circumstances.
- **Lens**: An [[Abraham iridotomy lens]] or [[Wise iridotomy lens]] is used to focus the laser beam, stabilize the eye, and provide magnification.
- **Energy settings (Nd:YAG)**: Typically 2–8 mJ per pulse, with 1–3 pulses per application. The goal is a patent, full-thickness iridotomy of at least 150–200 µm in diameter.
- **Endpoint**: Visualization of a gush of aqueous and pigment from the posterior chamber through the iridotomy site, deepening of the anterior chamber, and a visible opening through which the anterior lens capsule or red reflex can be seen.

#### Post-LPI Management

- Topical [[corticosteroid]] (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate]] 1%) four times daily for 4–7 days to control post-laser inflammation.
- Topical [[apraclonidine]] 1% or [[brimonidine]] 0.2% immediately before and after the procedure to prevent post-laser IOP spikes.
- IOP check at 1 hour post-procedure and follow-up at 1 week and 4–6 weeks.
- Gonioscopy after LPI to assess angle opening and rule out residual appositional or synechial closure.

#### Complications of LPI

| **Complication** | **Incidence / Notes** |
|---|---|
| Transient IOP spike | Common (10–30%); usually self-limited; managed with topical alpha-agonists |
| Bleeding from iris vessels | Usually minor; controlled with gentle pressure on the contact lens |
| Anterior uveitis | Mild; managed with topical corticosteroids |
| Closure of iridotomy | May occur in 5–10% of cases; requires retreatment |
| Dysphotopsia (glare, ghost images, linear streaks) | More common with inferiorly or temporally placed iridotomies not covered by the lid |
| Corneal or lens damage | Rare with proper technique |
| Posterior synechiae formation | Rare |
| Focal cataract | Rare; from inadvertent lens capsule damage |

### Surgical Peripheral Iridectomy

When laser iridotomy is not feasible (e.g., due to persistent corneal edema, inability to cooperate, or failed laser attempts), a **surgical (incisional) [[peripheral iridectomy]]** may be performed. This involves creating a small, full-thickness excision of peripheral iris tissue through a limbal or corneal incision under local or general anesthesia. The procedure achieves the same physiological goal as LPI—creating an alternative aqueous pathway—but carries the additional risks of intraocular surgery, including infection, hemorrhage, and cataract formation.

### Lens Extraction

In cases where pupillary block is driven primarily by a large, anteriorly positioned, or intumescent [[crystalline lens]] (phacomorphic component), **[[lens extraction]]** ([[phacoemulsification]] with or without [[intraocular lens]] implantation) may be the most appropriate definitive treatment. Lens extraction deepens the anterior chamber, eliminates iris–lens contact, and permanently resolves the pupillary block mechanism. Several landmark studies, including the **EAGLE (Effectiveness of Early Lens Extraction for the Treatment of Primary Angle-Closure Glaucoma) trial**, have demonstrated that clear lens extraction is at least as effective as LPI in managing primary angle closure and may offer superior long-term IOP control and quality of life outcomes, particularly in patients with coexisting cataract.

### Management of the Fellow Eye

The fellow eye of a patient who has experienced acute pupillary block is at extremely high risk (approximately 40–80% lifetime risk) of developing a similar attack. **Prophylactic LPI** of the fellow eye is therefore strongly recommended and should be performed as soon as practically possible, ideally during the same clinical encounter or within days of the acute episode.

### Management of Pseudophakic and Aphakic Pupillary Block

In pseudophakic or aphakic eyes, pupillary block may occur due to:

- Vitreous plugging the pupil or iridotomy
- IOL–iris adhesion
- Inflammatory membranes (seclusio pupillae)
- Anterior hyaloid face apposition to the iris or IOL

Management includes:

- **Nd:YAG laser iridotomy** (if not already patent)
- **Nd:YAG laser vitreolysis** or **anterior hyaloidotomy** (to disrupt vitreous or hyaloid face obstruction)
- **Surgical intervention**: Anterior vitrectomy, IOL repositioning, or synechiolysis as indicated
- **Intensive anti-inflammatory therapy** for inflammatory pupillary block

### Long-Term Follow-Up

Patients who have experienced pupillary block require lifelong monitoring, including:

- Regular IOP measurement
- Gonioscopy to assess for progressive [[peripheral anterior synechiae]] (PAS) formation
- [[Optic nerve head]] evaluation (clinical examination, [[optical coherence tomography]] of the [[retinal nerve fiber layer]], and [[visual field testing]])
- Assessment of iridotomy patency
- Monitoring for cataract progression (which may be accelerated after an acute attack or LPI)

Even after successful LPI, some patients may develop chronic angle closure due to pre-existing PAS or ongoing non-pupillary block mechanisms (e.g., [[plateau iris configuration]]). These patients may require additional interventions, including [[argon laser peripheral iridoplasty]] (ALPI), long-term topical [[antiglaucoma medications]], or [[glaucoma filtering surgery]] ([[trabeculectomy]], [[glaucoma drainage device]] implantation).

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis of pupillary block depends on the duration and severity of the IOP elevation, the promptness of treatment, and the extent of pre-existing damage.

- **Acute attacks treated within hours**: Generally excellent visual prognosis if IOP is rapidly controlled and LPI is performed. Most patients recover good visual acuity, though some may have residual optic nerve damage.
- **Delayed treatment (> 24–72 hours)**: Significant risk of permanent optic nerve damage, chronic IOP elevation, and irreversible vision loss. Prolonged ischemia may cause iris sphincter atrophy, lens opacification (glaukomflecken), and trabecular meshwork damage.
- **Chronic angle closure from pupillary block**: May result in progressive, insidious optic neuropathy similar to [[primary open-angle glaucoma]], with gradual visual field loss.

---

## Prevention

Prevention strategies for pupillary block include:

1. **Identification of at-risk eyes**: Routine screening with slit-lamp assessment of anterior chamber depth (van Herick technique), gonioscopy, and anterior segment imaging in high-risk populations.
2. **Prophylactic LPI**: In eyes with occludable angles (defined as ≥ 180° of iridotrabecular contact on gonioscopy) or anatomical features suggestive of high risk.
3. **Patient education**: Advising at-risk patients about symptoms of acute angle closure and the importance of seeking immediate care.
4. **Medication awareness**: Caution with medications that may precipitate pupillary block in predisposed individuals, including [[anticholinergics]], [[adrenergic agents]], [[sulfonamides]] (e.g., [[topiramate]]), and [[selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors]] (SSRIs).
