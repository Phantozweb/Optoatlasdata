

# Optic Cup

The **optic cup** (Latin: *excavatio disci*) is the central, pale depression within the [[optic disc]] (also known as the [[optic nerve head]]) visible upon [[fundoscopy]]. It represents the area of the disc devoid of [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL) axons, where the [[lamina cribrosa]] is most directly visible. The optic cup is a critically important anatomical landmark in clinical [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]], as its size, shape, depth, and ratio relative to the overall disc diameter — the [[cup-to-disc ratio]] (CDR) — serve as primary indicators for the detection, monitoring, and management of [[glaucoma]] and other [[optic neuropathies]].

Understanding the optic cup requires a thorough appreciation of its embryological origins, normal anatomical variation, biomechanical properties, and the pathological processes that lead to its enlargement. This article provides an exhaustive clinical reference on the optic cup for eye care professionals.

---

## Embryology and Development

The optic cup in the context of ocular embryology has a dual meaning. During embryogenesis, the **embryonic optic cup** is a bilayered invagination of the [[optic vesicle]] that forms during the fourth to fifth week of gestation. This embryonic structure gives rise to the [[retina]], the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE), the [[ciliary body]] epithelium, and the [[iris]] epithelium. The invagination process creates the **choroidal fissure** (optic fissure), which closes by approximately the seventh week of gestation. Failure of this closure results in [[coloboma]], which can affect the optic disc and the clinical appearance of the optic cup.

The **clinical optic cup** — the central excavation of the optic disc observed postnatally — develops as [[retinal ganglion cell]] (RGC) axons converge at the [[scleral canal]] and exit the eye through the [[lamina cribrosa]]. The central region of the disc, where fewer axons traverse, remains relatively depressed, forming the physiological cup. The size of the physiological cup is largely determined by:

- The diameter of the [[scleral canal]]
- The total number of RGC axons (approximately 1.0–1.2 million in healthy eyes)
- The connective tissue architecture of the [[lamina cribrosa]]
- Genetic factors influencing disc size

Larger optic discs tend to have larger physiological cups, while smaller discs may have little to no visible cupping. This relationship is essential for clinicians to understand, as a large cup in a large disc may be entirely physiological, whereas a moderate cup in a small disc may represent pathological loss of neural tissue.

## Anatomy and Morphology

### Gross Anatomy

The optic cup is situated centrally or slightly temporally within the [[optic disc]]. The disc itself is approximately 1.5 mm in vertical diameter and 1.75 mm in horizontal diameter in the average adult eye. The cup is bounded by the **neuroretinal rim** — the tissue between the cup margin and the disc margin — which contains the bulk of RGC axons.

Key anatomical features of the optic cup include:

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| **Cup floor** | The deepest portion of the excavation, where the [[lamina cribrosa]] pores are most visible |
| **Cup walls** | The sloping transition from the neuroretinal rim to the cup floor; may be gradual or steep |
| **Cup margin** | The line of inflection where the retinal surface begins to descend into the cup |
| **Laminar dots** | Visible pores of the [[lamina cribrosa]] at the cup floor, representing passage points for RGC axon bundles |
| **Central retinal vessels** | The [[central retinal artery]] and [[central retinal vein]] typically emerge from the nasal aspect of the cup |

### Neuroretinal Rim

The **[[neuroretinal rim]]** is the tissue surrounding the cup and is composed of RGC axons, [[astrocytes]], capillaries, and connective tissue. In healthy eyes, the rim follows the **ISNT rule**: the rim is thickest **I**nferiorly, then **S**uperiorly, then **N**asally, and thinnest **T**emporally. Violation of the ISNT rule is a clinical red flag for [[glaucomatous optic neuropathy]].

### Cup-to-Disc Ratio (CDR)

The **[[cup-to-disc ratio]]** is the most widely used clinical metric for describing the optic cup. It is expressed as a decimal (e.g., 0.3, 0.5, 0.7) representing the ratio of the cup diameter to the disc diameter, measured either vertically or horizontally.

| CDR Range | Clinical Interpretation |
|---|---|
| 0.0 – 0.3 | Small cup; common in small-to-average discs; generally physiological |
| 0.3 – 0.5 | Average cup; physiological in most individuals |
| 0.5 – 0.7 | Moderate cup; may be physiological in large discs but warrants careful evaluation |
| > 0.7 | Large cup; suspicious for [[glaucoma]] unless disc is correspondingly large |
| Asymmetry > 0.2 between eyes | Highly suspicious for unilateral or asymmetric glaucomatous damage |

It is critical to note that CDR alone is insufficient for diagnosis. It must be interpreted in the context of disc size, rim configuration, RNFL appearance, [[visual field]] findings, and [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP).

## Pathophysiology

### Physiological Cupping

Physiological cupping refers to the normal excavation of the optic disc present in healthy eyes. It is determined primarily by the size of the scleral canal and the number of nerve fibers. Physiological cups are typically:

- Round or horizontally oval
- Centrally located
- Symmetric between the two eyes (CDR asymmetry < 0.2)
- Associated with an intact neuroretinal rim following the ISNT rule
- Stable over time

### Pathological Cupping (Glaucomatous Optic Neuropathy)

Pathological enlargement of the optic cup is the hallmark structural change in [[glaucoma]]. The pathophysiology of glaucomatous cupping involves a complex interplay of mechanical, vascular, and biological factors:

#### 1. Mechanical Theory

Elevated [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) exerts direct mechanical stress on the [[lamina cribrosa]] and the RGC axons passing through it. The lamina cribrosa is a mesh-like connective tissue structure composed of [[collagen]] types I, III, and IV, [[elastin]], and [[glycosaminoglycans]]. Under elevated IOP:

- The lamina cribrosa undergoes **posterior bowing** (posterior displacement)
- Laminar pores become compressed and distorted, particularly in the superior and inferior poles where the pores are larger and the connective tissue is thinner
- Axonal transport (both anterograde and retrograde) is disrupted at the level of the lamina, leading to accumulation of [[neurotrophins]] (e.g., [[brain-derived neurotrophic factor]], BDNF) and organelles
- RGC axons undergo [[Wallerian degeneration]] and eventual apoptosis
- Loss of axonal volume results in deepening and widening of the optic cup

#### 2. Vascular Theory

Impaired blood flow to the optic nerve head contributes to RGC damage. The optic nerve head is supplied by the **short posterior ciliary arteries** (SPCAs) via the [[circle of Zinn-Haller]]. Factors contributing to vascular insufficiency include:

- Elevated IOP reducing [[ocular perfusion pressure]] (OPP = mean arterial pressure – IOP)
- Systemic [[hypotension]], particularly nocturnal dips
- [[Vasospasm]] (as seen in [[normal-tension glaucoma]])
- [[Atherosclerosis]] and other systemic vascular diseases
- Impaired autoregulation of optic nerve head blood flow

Ischemia leads to [[oxidative stress]], release of [[reactive oxygen species]] (ROS), [[glutamate]] excitotoxicity, mitochondrial dysfunction, and activation of apoptotic cascades in RGCs.

#### 3. Biomechanical Susceptibility

Individual susceptibility to cupping at any given IOP level depends on the biomechanical properties of the [[lamina cribrosa]] and [[peripapillary sclera]]:

- **Thinner lamina cribrosa** — more susceptible to posterior displacement
- **Larger scleral canal** — less structural support
- **Reduced connective tissue density** — as seen in [[myopia]] and aging
- **Racial differences** — individuals of African descent tend to have thinner laminae and larger discs, contributing to higher glaucoma prevalence and severity

#### 4. Cellular and Molecular Mechanisms

At the cellular level, glaucomatous cupping involves:

- **RGC apoptosis** mediated by caspase-3 and caspase-9 activation
- **Astrocyte remodeling** — reactive [[astrocytes]] in the optic nerve head undergo hypertrophy and alter extracellular matrix composition
- **Microglial activation** — release of pro-inflammatory cytokines ([[TNF-α]], [[IL-1β]], [[IL-6]])
- **Matrix metalloproteinase (MMP)** upregulation leading to degradation of laminar connective tissue
- **Mitochondrial dysfunction** in RGCs, particularly in the unmyelinated prelaminar and laminar segments where energy demand is highest
- **Excitotoxicity** from elevated extracellular [[glutamate]] levels activating [[NMDA receptors]]

### Non-Glaucomatous Causes of Optic Cup Enlargement

While [[glaucoma]] is the most common cause of pathological cupping, other conditions can produce optic cup enlargement:

| Condition | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| [[Anterior ischemic optic neuropathy]] (AION) | Post-ischemic atrophy of RGC axons; cupping may develop weeks to months after the acute event |
| [[Compressive optic neuropathy]] | Tumors (e.g., [[pituitary adenoma]], [[meningioma]]) causing chronic axonal loss |
| [[Optic neuritis]] (severe/recurrent) | Demyelination and axonal loss leading to secondary cupping |
| [[Hereditary optic neuropathies]] (e.g., [[Leber hereditary optic neuropathy]]) | Mitochondrial dysfunction causing RGC death |
| [[Methanol]] toxicity | Selective toxicity to RGCs and optic nerve |
| [[Traumatic optic neuropathy]] | Direct or indirect injury to the optic nerve |
| Congenital anomalies (e.g., [[optic disc coloboma]], [[morning glory anomaly]]) | Structural disc abnormalities mimicking or producing cupping |

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Asymptomatic Nature

The optic cup itself does not produce symptoms. Physiological cupping is entirely asymptomatic. Even pathological enlargement of the optic cup in early-to-moderate [[glaucoma]] is typically **asymptomatic**, as peripheral [[visual field]] loss progresses insidiously and is compensated by the fellow eye.

### Symptoms Associated with Advanced Cupping

When optic cup enlargement is associated with advanced glaucomatous damage, patients may report:

- **Peripheral vision loss** — often described as "tunnel vision" in late stages
- **Difficulty with dark adaptation** — due to RGC and RNFL loss
- **Contrast sensitivity reduction**
- **Difficulty navigating stairs or curbs** — reflecting inferior visual field loss (from superior rim thinning)
- **Central vision loss** — only in end-stage disease when the remaining central island of vision is threatened

In non-glaucomatous optic neuropathies causing cupping, symptoms may include:

- **Acute vision loss** (e.g., [[AION]], [[optic neuritis]])
- **Color vision deficiency** ([[dyschromatopsia]])
- **Relative afferent pupillary defect** ([[RAPD]])
- **Pain with eye movement** (in [[optic neuritis]])

## Diagnosis and Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical Examination

#### Direct Ophthalmoscopy

[[Direct ophthalmoscopy]] provides a magnified (approximately 15×), monocular view of the optic disc. While useful for screening, it does not provide stereoscopic depth perception, making accurate cup assessment challenging.

#### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy with Fundus Lenses

[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] combined with a high-powered condensing lens (e.g., [[78D lens]], [[90D lens]], or [[Volk Super Field lens]]) provides a stereoscopic, magnified view of the optic disc. This is the gold standard for clinical optic cup assessment. Key observations include:

- **CDR estimation** (vertical and horizontal)
- **Neuroretinal rim width and configuration** (ISNT rule compliance)
- **Cup depth and contour**
- **Laminar dot sign** — visibility of lamina cribrosa pores, which increases with cupping
- **Bayoneting of vessels** — vessels disappearing at the cup rim and reappearing on the cup floor, indicating steep cup walls
- **Nasalization of vessels** — central retinal vessels displaced nasally due to temporal rim loss
- **Disc hemorrhages** ([[Drance hemorrhages]]) — flame-shaped hemorrhages at the disc margin, a strong risk factor for glaucoma progression
- **Peripapillary atrophy** — [[beta-zone peripapillary atrophy]] (absence of RPE) is associated with glaucoma
- **RNFL defects** — wedge-shaped or diffuse RNFL thinning visible as dark striations in the peripapillary retina

#### Stereoscopic Disc Photography

[[Stereo disc photography]] provides a permanent, three-dimensional record of the optic disc and cup. Serial photographs allow detection of progressive cupping over time. This remains an important tool for longitudinal monitoring.

### Imaging Technologies

#### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT) is the most widely used imaging technology for objective assessment of the optic cup and related structures. Modern [[spectral-domain OCT]] (SD-OCT) and [[swept-source OCT]] (SS-OCT) provide:

| OCT Parameter | Clinical Relevance |
|---|---|
| **RNFL thickness** | Quantitative measurement of peripapillary RNFL; thinning correlates with cup enlargement and glaucoma severity |
| **Optic nerve head (ONH) analysis** | Automated measurement of disc area, cup area, rim area, cup volume, CDR |
| **Ganglion cell complex (GCC)** or **ganglion cell–inner plexiform layer (GCIPL)** | Macular RGC loss detection; may precede detectable RNFL thinning |
| **Lamina cribrosa depth** | Posterior displacement of the lamina correlates with glaucomatous damage |
| **Minimum rim width (BMO-MRW)** | Bruch's membrane opening-based minimum rim width; more anatomically accurate than conventional rim measurements |
| **RNFL deviation and trend analysis** | Progression detection over serial visits |

OCT normative databases allow comparison of a patient's measurements to age- and ethnicity-matched controls, flagging values outside normal limits.

#### Heidelberg Retinal Tomography (HRT)

The [[Heidelberg Retinal Tomograph]] (HRT) uses [[confocal scanning laser ophthalmoscopy]] to create a topographic map of the optic disc surface. It provides measurements of cup area, cup volume, rim area, rim volume, cup shape measure, and the **Moorfields Regression Analysis (MRA)**, which classifies sectors as within normal limits, borderline, or outside normal limits. While largely supplanted by OCT, HRT remains valuable in certain clinical and research settings.

#### GDx Nerve Fiber Analyzer

The [[GDx]] uses [[scanning laser polarimetry]] to measure RNFL thickness based on the birefringent properties of the nerve fiber layer. It has been largely replaced by OCT but contributed significantly to understanding the relationship between RNFL loss and cup enlargement.

### Visual Field Testing

[[Visual field]] testing ([[perimetry]]) is essential for correlating structural optic cup changes with functional vision loss. Standard automated perimetry (SAP) using the [[Humphrey Visual Field Analyzer]] (e.g., 24-2 or 30-2 SITA Standard/Fast protocols) is the clinical standard.

Key visual field indices include:

- **Mean Deviation (MD)** — overall depression of the visual field
- **Pattern Standard Deviation (PSD)** — localized visual field loss
- **Visual Field Index (VFI)** — percentage of remaining visual function
- **Glaucoma Hemifield Test (GHT)** — compares superior and inferior hemifields

Structure-function correlation: Superior optic cup enlargement (inferior rim thinning) corresponds to **inferior visual field loss**, and vice versa, following the anatomical arrangement of RGC axons.

### Tonometry

[[Intraocular pressure]] measurement via [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]] (GAT), [[non-contact tonometry]], [[iCare rebound tonometry]], or [[dynamic contour tonometry]] is essential in the evaluation of optic cup enlargement, as elevated IOP is the primary modifiable risk factor for [[glaucoma]].

### Pachymetry

[[Central corneal thickness]] (CCT) measurement via [[ultrasonic pachymetry]] or [[anterior segment OCT]] is important because thin corneas are an independent risk factor for glaucoma progression (as demonstrated in the [[Ocular Hypertension Treatment Study]], OHTS) and can lead to underestimation of IOP by GAT.

### Gonioscopy

[[Gonioscopy]] is performed to evaluate the [[anterior chamber angle]] and classify glaucoma as [[open-angle glaucoma|open-angle]] or [[angle-closure glaucoma|angle-closure]], which directly influences management decisions.

## Differential Diagnosis

When evaluating an enlarged optic cup, clinicians must differentiate between:

| Diagnosis | Key Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| **Physiological large cup** | Large disc, intact ISNT rule, symmetric CDR, normal IOP, normal visual fields, stable over time |
| **[[Primary open-angle glaucoma]]** | Progressive cupping, rim thinning (especially inferior/superior), elevated or normal IOP, corresponding visual field loss, disc hemorrhages |
| **[[Normal-tension glaucoma]]** | Cupping with IOP consistently ≤ 21 mmHg, disc hemorrhages common, may have vasospastic history |
| **[[Optic disc pit]]** | Focal, round, gray depression usually in the temporal disc; may be associated with [[serous macular detachment]] |
| **[[Optic disc coloboma]]** | Inferonasal excavation, often with peripapillary pigmentary changes; congenital |
| **[[Myopic disc]]** | Tilted disc, peripapillary atrophy, temporal crescent; may mimic cupping |
| **Non-glaucomatous optic atrophy with cupping** | History of ischemic, compressive, or inflammatory optic neuropathy; pallor exceeding cupping |

A critical clinical pearl: in **glaucoma**, cupping typically **precedes or is proportional to pallor**, whereas in non-glaucomatous optic atrophy, **pallor often exceeds cupping**.

## Management and Treatment

Management of the optic cup itself is directed at the underlying condition causing pathological enlargement. In the vast majority of cases, this means management of [[glaucoma]].

### Goals of Treatment

- **Preserve remaining visual function** by halting or slowing progressive optic cup enlargement and RNFL loss
- **Reduce IOP** to a target level individualized for each patient (typically 20–50% reduction from baseline, depending on disease severity and risk factors)
- **Monitor for progression** using serial OCT, visual fields, and clinical examination

### Pharmacological Interventions

IOP-lowering medications are the first-line treatment for most forms of glaucoma:

| Drug Class | Examples | Mechanism | IOP Reduction |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Prostaglandin analogs]]** | [[Latanoprost]], [[Travoprost]], [[Bimatoprost]], [[Tafluprost]] | Increase uveoscleral outflow | 25–35% |
| **[[Beta-adrenergic antagonists]]** | [[Timolol]], [[Betaxolol]], [[Levobunolol]] | Decrease aqueous humor production | 20–25% |
| **[[Alpha-adrenergic agonists]]** | [[Brimonidine]], [[Apraclonidine]] | Decrease aqueous production; increase uveoscleral outflow | 20–25% |
| **[[Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]]** (topical) | [[Dorzolamide]], [[Brinzolamide]] | Decrease aqueous production | 15–20% |
| **[[Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]]** (oral) | [[Acetazolamide]], [[Methazolamide]] | Decrease aqueous production | 25–40% |
| **[[Rho kinase inhibitors]]** | [[Netarsudil]] | Increase trabecular meshwork outflow; decrease episcleral venous pressure | 15–20% |
| **[[Nitric oxide-donating prostaglandin analogs]]** | [[Latanoprostene bunod]] | Increase uveoscleral and trabecular outflow | 30–36% |
| **[[Cholinergic agonists]]** | [[Pilocarpine]] | Increase trabecular outflow via ciliary muscle contraction | 20–25% |

Fixed-dose combinations (e.g., [[Dorzolamide-Timolol]], [[Brimonidine-Timolol]], [[Netarsudil-Latanoprost]]) improve adherence by reducing drop burden.

### Laser Procedures

| Procedure | Indication | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Selective laser trabeculoplasty]]** (SLT) | Open-angle glaucoma; first-line or adjunctive | Stimulates trabecular meshwork remodeling to improve aqueous outflow |
| **[[Argon laser trabeculoplasty]]** (ALT) | Open-angle glaucoma | Thermal coagulation of trabecular meshwork to improve outflow |
| **[[Laser peripheral iridotomy]]** (LPI) | Angle-closure glaucoma | Creates a full-thickness opening in the peripheral iris to relieve pupillary block |
| **[[Cyclophotocoagulation]]** (CPC) | Refractory glaucoma | Destroys ciliary body epithelium to reduce aqueous production |

### Surgical Approaches

When medical and laser therapy are insufficient to prevent progressive cupping:

| Surgery | Description |
|---|---|
| **[[Trabeculectomy]]** | Creation of a guarded fistula from the anterior chamber to the subconjunctival space, forming a filtering bleb |
| **[[Glaucoma drainage devices]]** (e.g., [[Ahmed valve]], [[Baerveldt implant]]) | Tube shunt directing aqueous to an equatorial plate reservoir |
| **[[Minimally invasive glaucoma surgery]]** (MIGS) | Includes [[iStent]], [[Hydrus Microstent]], [[Kahook Dual Blade]] goniotomy, [[XEN Gel Stent]], [[OMNI Surgical System]], [[ab interno canaloplasty]] (ABiC); lower risk profile than traditional surgery |
| **[[Goniotomy]] / [[Trabeculotomy]]** | Incision or ablation of the trabecular meshwork; used in congenital/juvenile glaucoma and increasingly in adults |

### Neuroprotection (Emerging Therapies)

Given that glaucomatous cupping results from RGC death, neuroprotective strategies aim to protect RGCs independent of IOP reduction:

- **[[Memantine]]** — NMDA receptor antagonist; showed promise in animal models but failed to demonstrate significant benefit in the large-scale clinical trial (Allergan Memantine Study)
- **[[Brimonidine]]** — has demonstrated neuroprotective properties in the [[Low-Pressure Glaucoma Treatment Study]] (LoGTS), with visual field preservation beyond its IOP-lowering effect
- **[[Citicoline]]** — a neuroprotective agent showing potential in improving RGC function in glaucoma patients
- **[[Neurotrophic factors]]** (e.g., [[BDNF]], [[CNTF]]) — under investigation for intravitreal or gene therapy delivery
- **Stem cell therapy** — experimental approaches to replace lost RGCs
- **Gene therapy** — targeting mitochondrial dysfunction and apoptotic pathways

### Monitoring and Follow-Up

Patients with pathological or suspicious optic cups require regular monitoring:

| Parameter | Frequency |
|---|---|
| **IOP measurement** | Every 3–6 months (more frequently if unstable) |
| **OCT (RNFL and ONH)** | Every 6–12 months; trend analysis over ≥3 visits |
| **Visual field testing** | Every 6–12 months; progression analysis (GPA, VFI trend) |
| **Stereo disc photography** | Annually or as needed for comparison |
| **Gonioscopy** | Annually or when angle status is uncertain |
| **Pachymetry** | Baseline measurement; repeat if corneal changes suspected |

## Special Considerations

### Optic Cup in Pediatric Populations

In neonates and infants, the optic cup is typically small. Rapid enlargement of the cup in an infant is a hallmark of **[[congenital glaucoma]]** (also known as [[primary infantile glaucoma]]) and is often accompanied by:

- [[Buphthalmos]] (enlarged globe)
- [[Haab striae]] (breaks in [[Descemet's membrane]])
- [[Corneal edema]]
- [[Epiphora]] and [[photophobia]]

Notably, cupping in congenital glaucoma may be **reversible** with successful IOP reduction, a phenomenon rarely observed in adults. This reversibility is attributed to the greater elasticity of the infant lamina cribrosa.

### Optic Cup in Myopia

[[Myopia]], particularly [[high myopia]] (> -6.00 D), presents unique challenges in optic cup assessment:

- Myopic discs are often tilted and oval, with prominent [[peripapillary atrophy]]
- The lamina cribrosa may be thinner and more posteriorly displaced
- OCT normative databases may not accurately represent myopic eyes
- Distinguishing physiological myopic cupping from glaucomatous cupping requires careful clinical judgment and longitudinal follow-up

### Racial and Ethnic Variations

Optic cup size and CDR vary significantly across racial and ethnic groups:

- Individuals of **African descent** tend to have larger discs and larger cups, with higher baseline CDRs
- **Asian** populations have a higher prevalence of [[normal-tension glaucoma]] and [[angle-closure glaucoma]]
- OCT normative databases must be race-matched for accurate interpretation

### Optic Cup and Systemic Disease

Systemic conditions that may influence optic cup morphology include:

- **[[Diabetes mellitus]]** — may affect optic nerve head blood flow
- **[[Systemic hypertension]]** — influences ocular perfusion pressure
- **[[Obstructive sleep apnea]]** — associated with glaucoma and optic neuropathy
- **[[Alzheimer's disease]]** and other neurodegenerative conditions — associated with RNFL thinning and potential cup changes

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis for eyes with pathological optic cup enlargement depends on:

- **Stage at diagnosis** — earlier detection allows more effective intervention
- **Rate of progression** — rapid progressors require more aggressive treatment
- **IOP control** — achieving and maintaining target IOP is the strongest predictor of stability
- **Patient adherence** — medication compliance is a major challenge in glaucoma management
- **Life expectancy** — the clinical significance of slow progression must be weighed against the patient's expected lifespan

With appropriate management, the majority of patients with glaucomatous cupping can maintain functional vision throughout their lifetime. However, late-stage disease with advanced cupping (CDR > 0.9) and severe visual field loss carries a guarded prognosis.

---

## Summary

The optic cup is a fundamental anatomical structure whose clinical evaluation is central to the practice of [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]]. Its assessment — through careful stereoscopic examination, advanced imaging, and functional testing — enables the detection and monitoring of [[glaucoma]], the leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide. A thorough understanding of the optic cup's anatomy, the pathophysiology of its enlargement, and the full spectrum of diagnostic and therapeutic options is essential for every eye care professional committed to preserving vision.
