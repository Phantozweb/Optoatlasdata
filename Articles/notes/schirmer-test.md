

# Schirmer Test

---

The **Schirmer Test** is a standardized, semi-quantitative clinical diagnostic procedure used to measure [[aqueous tear production]] and assess the functional status of the [[lacrimal gland]] apparatus. First described by the German ophthalmologist **Otto Schirmer** in 1903, the test remains one of the most widely employed and clinically significant methods for evaluating [[tear film]] secretion in the diagnosis and management of [[dry eye disease]] (DED), [[keratoconjunctivitis sicca]] (KCS), and [[Sjögren syndrome]]. Despite its simplicity, the Schirmer Test provides invaluable information about the aqueous component of the [[tear film]], guiding clinicians in differential diagnosis, disease staging, treatment planning, and longitudinal monitoring of lacrimal function.

The test involves the placement of a standardized strip of filter paper into the [[inferior conjunctival fornix]] and measuring the length of wetting over a defined period, typically five minutes. Variations of the test—including Schirmer I (with and without [[topical anesthesia]]) and Schirmer II—allow clinicians to differentiate between basal tear secretion and reflex tear secretion, providing a nuanced understanding of the underlying pathophysiology of tear deficiency.

---

## Historical Background

Otto Schirmer introduced his eponymous test in 1903 as a practical, office-based method for quantifying tear production. Prior to Schirmer's contribution, assessment of lacrimal function was largely subjective and lacked standardization. Schirmer's innovation was the use of a calibrated strip of [[Whatman filter paper]] (No. 41), which provided a reproducible and inexpensive tool for clinical measurement. Over the subsequent century, the test has undergone refinements in technique, interpretation, and clinical application, but its fundamental principle remains unchanged. The Schirmer Test is now endorsed by major clinical guidelines, including those from the [[Tear Film and Ocular Surface Society]] (TFOS) [[Dry Eye Workshop]] (DEWS) reports, the [[American Academy of Ophthalmology]] (AAO), and the [[International Dry Eye Workshop]] (IDEW).

## Anatomy and Physiology of the Lacrimal System

A thorough understanding of the Schirmer Test requires knowledge of the anatomy and physiology of the [[lacrimal functional unit]] (LFU), which comprises the [[lacrimal gland]], [[accessory lacrimal glands]] (glands of [[Krause]] and [[Wolfring]]), the [[ocular surface]] (including the [[corneal epithelium]] and [[conjunctival epithelium]]), the [[meibomian glands]], the [[nasolacrimal drainage system]], and the interconnecting sensory and autonomic neural pathways.

### Aqueous Tear Production

The aqueous layer of the [[precorneal tear film]] is primarily produced by the main [[lacrimal gland]], located in the [[lacrimal fossa]] of the [[frontal bone]] within the superotemporal orbit. The lacrimal gland is innervated by:

- **Parasympathetic fibers** from the [[lacrimal nucleus]] of the [[facial nerve]] (CN VII), relayed via the [[pterygopalatine ganglion]] and the [[zygomatic nerve]] to the [[lacrimal nerve]] (a branch of the [[ophthalmic division]] of the [[trigeminal nerve]], CN V₁).
- **Sympathetic fibers** from the [[superior cervical ganglion]].
- **Sensory afferents** from the [[trigeminal nerve]] (CN V), which mediate the [[corneal reflex arc]] and drive reflex tearing.

The accessory lacrimal glands of [[Krause]] (located in the [[conjunctival fornix]]) and [[Wolfring]] (located near the superior tarsal border) contribute to **basal tear secretion**, which maintains the ocular surface in the absence of reflex stimulation.

### Tear Film Layers

The [[tear film]] is a trilaminar structure consisting of:

| Layer | Thickness | Source | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lipid layer** | ~0.1 µm | [[Meibomian glands]], glands of [[Zeis]] and [[Moll]] | Retards evaporation, stabilizes tear film |
| **Aqueous layer** | ~7 µm | Main and accessory [[lacrimal glands]] | Provides nutrients, oxygen, antimicrobial factors |
| **Mucin layer** | ~0.02–0.05 µm | [[Goblet cells]], corneal and conjunctival epithelium | Promotes wettability, anchors tear film to epithelium |

The Schirmer Test specifically evaluates the **aqueous component** of the tear film, though it may be indirectly influenced by reflex stimulation and neural integrity.

## Pathophysiology Relevant to the Schirmer Test

The Schirmer Test is most directly relevant to the evaluation of **aqueous-deficient dry eye** (ADDE), one of the two major subtypes of [[dry eye disease]] as classified by the TFOS DEWS II report (the other being [[evaporative dry eye]], EDE).

### Aqueous-Deficient Dry Eye (ADDE)

ADDE results from a reduction in the volume and/or flow rate of aqueous tear secretion. It is further subdivided into:

1. **[[Sjögren syndrome]] dry eye (SSDE):** An [[autoimmune]] [[exocrinopathy]] characterized by lymphocytic infiltration and destruction of the lacrimal and [[salivary glands]], leading to severe aqueous tear deficiency and [[xerostomia]]. SSDE may be primary or secondary (associated with other autoimmune diseases such as [[rheumatoid arthritis]], [[systemic lupus erythematosus]], or [[scleroderma]]).

2. **Non-Sjögren syndrome dry eye (NSSDE):** Encompasses a heterogeneous group of conditions causing lacrimal gland dysfunction without the systemic autoimmune features of Sjögren syndrome. Causes include:
   - Age-related lacrimal gland atrophy
   - [[Lacrimal gland]] infiltration (e.g., [[sarcoidosis]], [[lymphoma]], [[amyloidosis]])
   - [[Lacrimal gland]] ablation or surgical removal
   - Sensory denervation of the [[cornea]] (e.g., post-[[LASIK]], post-[[herpes zoster ophthalmicus]])
   - Pharmacological suppression of tear secretion (e.g., [[anticholinergic]] medications, [[antihistamines]], [[beta-blockers]], [[diuretics]])
   - [[Radiation therapy]] to the orbit
   - [[Graft-versus-host disease]] (GVHD)
   - Congenital [[alacrima]]

### Neural Regulation and Reflex Arc

The Schirmer Test, particularly when performed without anesthesia, assesses the integrity of the **lacrimal reflex arc**:

1. **Afferent limb:** Sensory stimulation of the [[cornea]] and [[conjunctiva]] via the [[trigeminal nerve]] (CN V₁).
2. **Central processing:** Integration in the [[lacrimal nucleus]] of the [[pons]].
3. **Efferent limb:** Parasympathetic stimulation of the lacrimal gland via CN VII.

Disruption at any point along this arc—whether due to corneal hypoesthesia (e.g., post-refractive surgery, [[neurotrophic keratopathy]]), central neurological disease, or efferent autonomic dysfunction—can result in diminished reflex tearing and a reduced Schirmer score.

### Inflammatory Cascade

In chronic [[dry eye disease]], reduced aqueous tear production leads to [[tear film hyperosmolarity]], which triggers an inflammatory cascade on the [[ocular surface]]. This involves activation of [[mitogen-activated protein kinase]] (MAPK) and [[nuclear factor kappa B]] (NF-κB) signaling pathways, release of pro-inflammatory [[cytokines]] (including [[interleukin-1]] (IL-1), [[interleukin-6]] (IL-6), [[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] (TNF-α)), and upregulation of [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs), particularly [[MMP-9]]. This inflammation further damages the lacrimal gland and ocular surface, perpetuating a vicious cycle of tear deficiency and surface disease. The Schirmer Test serves as a key metric for identifying and monitoring this aqueous-deficient component.

## Clinical Indications

The Schirmer Test is indicated in the following clinical scenarios:

- Evaluation of patients presenting with symptoms of [[dry eye disease]] (ocular dryness, foreign body sensation, burning, tearing, photophobia, blurred vision)
- Screening for [[Sjögren syndrome]] (as part of the American-European Consensus Group classification criteria and the 2016 ACR/EULAR classification criteria)
- Preoperative assessment before [[cataract surgery]], [[refractive surgery]] ([[LASIK]], [[PRK]], [[SMILE]]), or [[corneal transplantation]]
- Monitoring lacrimal function in patients on medications known to reduce tear production
- Assessment of lacrimal gland function in patients with [[facial nerve palsy]], [[trigeminal neuropathy]], or post-surgical sensory loss
- Evaluation of [[contact lens]]-related dry eye
- Longitudinal monitoring of treatment efficacy in dry eye management
- Differential diagnosis of tearing disorders (to distinguish reflex tearing from [[nasolacrimal duct obstruction]])

## Test Methodology

### Equipment

The Schirmer Test requires:

- **Schirmer test strips:** Standardized strips of Whatman No. 41 filter paper, typically 5 mm wide × 35 mm long, with a rounded or notched end for placement over the [[lower eyelid]] margin. Commercially available strips are individually packaged and sterile.
- **Timer:** A stopwatch or timer set for 5 minutes.
- **Topical anesthetic** (for Schirmer I with anesthesia): Typically [[proparacaine]] 0.5% or [[tetracaine]] 0.5%.
- **Millimeter ruler** (if strips are not pre-graduated): Most modern strips are pre-marked with millimeter gradations.

### Variants of the Schirmer Test

| Variant | Anesthesia | What It Measures | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Schirmer I (without anesthesia)** | None | Basal + reflex tear secretion (total tear production) | Screens for overall aqueous tear deficiency |
| **Schirmer I (with anesthesia)** | Topical anesthetic applied | Basal tear secretion only (reflex component eliminated) | Isolates basal secretion; more specific for lacrimal gland function |
| **Schirmer II (Jones Test)** | Topical anesthetic applied, then nasal stimulation | Reflex tear secretion | Assesses integrity of the reflex arc; used in neurological evaluation |

### Step-by-Step Procedure

#### Schirmer I Test (Without Anesthesia)

1. **Patient preparation:** The patient is seated comfortably in a well-lit room. The test should ideally be performed before any other ocular surface tests (e.g., [[fluorescein staining]], [[tear break-up time]]) to avoid reflex tearing from prior manipulation.
2. **Strip placement:** The clinician folds the rounded or notched end of the Schirmer strip at the pre-scored mark (typically 5 mm from the end) and hooks it over the temporal one-third of the [[lower eyelid]] margin, ensuring the folded portion rests in the [[inferior conjunctival fornix]] without touching the [[cornea]].
3. **Patient instructions:** The patient is instructed to look slightly upward and blink normally. Some protocols recommend gentle eye closure; however, the TFOS DEWS II report suggests that eyes remain open with normal blinking to simulate physiological conditions.
4. **Timing:** A timer is started immediately upon strip placement. The strip remains in situ for exactly **5 minutes**.
5. **Reading:** After 5 minutes, the strip is carefully removed and the length of wetting (in millimeters) from the fold/notch is measured immediately, as the wet front may advance slightly after removal.
6. **Documentation:** Results are recorded for each eye separately.

#### Schirmer I Test (With Anesthesia — Basal Secretion Test)

1. One to two drops of [[topical anesthetic]] (e.g., [[proparacaine]] 0.5%) are instilled into the [[inferior conjunctival fornix]] of each eye.
2. After waiting 1–2 minutes for the anesthetic to take full effect, excess fluid is gently blotted from the inferior fornix using a tissue or cotton-tipped applicator, taking care not to stimulate the ocular surface.
3. The Schirmer strip is placed as described above.
4. The test proceeds for 5 minutes, and the wetting length is measured.

#### Schirmer II Test (Reflex Secretion Test)

1. Topical anesthetic is instilled as in the basal secretion test.
2. The Schirmer strip is placed in the usual manner.
3. **Nasal stimulation** is applied using a cotton-tipped applicator inserted into the [[nasal mucosa]] (typically the inferior turbinate) on the ipsilateral side, stimulating the [[nasolacrimal reflex]] via the [[trigeminal nerve]].
4. The wetting length is measured after 5 minutes.
5. This variant specifically evaluates the **reflex secretory capacity** of the lacrimal gland and the integrity of the afferent-efferent reflex arc.

## Interpretation of Results

### Normal and Abnormal Values

| Test Variant | Normal | Borderline/Suspicious | Abnormal (Dry Eye) | Severe Deficiency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Schirmer I (no anesthesia)** | ≥15 mm/5 min | 10–14 mm/5 min | <10 mm/5 min | ≤5 mm/5 min |
| **Schirmer I (with anesthesia)** | ≥10 mm/5 min | 6–9 mm/5 min | ≤5 mm/5 min | ≤2 mm/5 min |
| **Schirmer II** | ≥15 mm/5 min | — | <15 mm/5 min | — |

**Key interpretive points:**

- A Schirmer I (without anesthesia) value of **<10 mm/5 min** is widely accepted as indicative of **aqueous tear deficiency** and is used as a diagnostic criterion for [[dry eye disease]].
- A Schirmer I (without anesthesia) value of **≤5 mm/5 min** is considered a hallmark of **severe aqueous-deficient dry eye** and is one of the ocular criteria in the classification of [[Sjögren syndrome]] (per the 2016 ACR/EULAR criteria and the American-European Consensus Group criteria).
- The **basal secretion test** (Schirmer I with anesthesia) value of **≤5 mm/5 min** suggests significant impairment of basal lacrimal gland function.
- A **discrepancy** between Schirmer I without anesthesia (high) and Schirmer I with anesthesia (low) suggests that reflex tearing is compensating for poor basal secretion—a common finding in early dry eye.
- A low Schirmer II result in the presence of adequate basal secretion may indicate a **neurological deficit** in the reflex arc.

### Age-Related Considerations

Tear production physiologically declines with age. Studies have demonstrated that mean Schirmer I values decrease from approximately 20–30 mm/5 min in young adults to 10–15 mm/5 min in individuals over 60 years of age. Clinicians should interpret results in the context of the patient's age, sex (females, particularly postmenopausal women, tend to have lower values), and systemic health status.

## Diagnostic Role and Integration with Other Tests

The Schirmer Test is rarely used in isolation. It is most informative when integrated into a comprehensive [[dry eye]] diagnostic workup, which may include:

| Diagnostic Test | Parameter Assessed | Complementary Role with Schirmer Test |
|---|---|---|
| [[Tear break-up time]] (TBUT) | Tear film stability | Identifies evaporative component; low TBUT with normal Schirmer suggests EDE |
| [[Fluorescein staining]] | Corneal epithelial damage | Correlates severity of surface damage with degree of aqueous deficiency |
| [[Lissamine green staining]] | Conjunctival epithelial damage | Detects conjunctival damage in dry eye; part of Sjögren criteria |
| [[Rose bengal staining]] | Devitalized epithelial cells | Historical stain for dry eye; largely replaced by lissamine green |
| [[Tear osmolarity]] | Tear film hyperosmolarity | Elevated osmolarity (>308 mOsm/L) confirms dry eye; complements Schirmer |
| [[MMP-9]] point-of-care testing (e.g., InflammaDry) | Ocular surface inflammation | Identifies inflammatory component even with borderline Schirmer values |
| [[Meibography]] | Meibomian gland morphology | Differentiates evaporative from aqueous-deficient dry eye |
| [[Tear meniscus height]] (TMH) | Tear volume | Non-invasive measure of tear volume; correlates with Schirmer results |
| [[Phenol red thread test]] | Tear volume (residual) | Less invasive alternative to Schirmer; measures residual tear volume over 15 seconds |
| [[Ocular Surface Disease Index]] (OSDI) | Symptom severity | Subjective symptom score; poor correlation with Schirmer highlights symptom-sign discordance |

### Role in Sjögren Syndrome Diagnosis

The Schirmer Test holds a pivotal role in the classification and diagnosis of [[Sjögren syndrome]]. According to the **2016 ACR/EULAR classification criteria**, a Schirmer Test result of **≤5 mm/5 min** (without anesthesia) is one of the weighted items contributing to the classification score. The test is also included in the **American-European Consensus Group (AECG) criteria** as an objective measure of ocular dryness. In suspected Sjögren syndrome, the Schirmer Test is typically performed alongside:

- [[Ocular surface staining]] (fluorescein and lissamine green)
- [[Salivary flow rate]] measurement
- [[Labial salivary gland biopsy]] (focus score ≥1)
- Serological testing for [[anti-SSA/Ro]] and [[anti-SSB/La]] antibodies

## Advantages and Limitations

### Advantages

- **Simplicity:** Requires minimal equipment and can be performed in any clinical setting.
- **Low cost:** Schirmer strips are inexpensive and widely available.
- **Non-invasive:** The test is well-tolerated by most patients.
- **Standardized:** Widely recognized and included in major diagnostic criteria.
- **Quantitative:** Provides a numerical value for documentation and longitudinal comparison.
- **Differentiates tear components:** Variants (with/without anesthesia) allow separation of basal and reflex secretion.

### Limitations

- **Poor reproducibility:** Significant intra-individual and inter-visit variability has been documented. Coefficients of variation can exceed 25–40%, limiting the test's reliability for detecting small changes over time.
- **Reflex tearing artifact:** The strip itself may stimulate reflex tearing (even with anesthesia), leading to falsely elevated results.
- **Environmental sensitivity:** Results may be influenced by ambient temperature, humidity, air currents, and lighting conditions.
- **Poor correlation with symptoms:** Multiple studies have demonstrated a weak correlation between Schirmer values and patient-reported symptom scores (e.g., [[OSDI]], [[DEQ-5]]).
- **Does not assess tear quality:** The test measures only the aqueous volume component and does not evaluate [[lipid layer]] integrity, [[mucin]] production, or [[tear film osmolarity]].
- **Patient discomfort:** Some patients, particularly those with severe dry eye or ocular surface sensitivity, may find the strip uncomfortable, potentially affecting compliance and results.
- **Influence of medications:** Topical and systemic medications (e.g., [[anticholinergics]], [[antihistamines]], [[antidepressants]], [[topical NSAIDs]]) can significantly alter results.

## Special Populations and Considerations

### Pediatric Patients

Performing the Schirmer Test in children can be challenging due to poor cooperation. Modified techniques, including shorter test durations or the use of the [[phenol red thread test]] (which requires only 15 seconds), may be more practical. Normal values in children tend to be higher than in adults.

### Contact Lens Wearers

[[Contact lens]] wear can alter tear dynamics and ocular surface sensitivity. The Schirmer Test should be performed after lens removal, with an adequate washout period (at least 2 hours, ideally longer) to minimize the confounding effects of lens-induced reflex tearing or corneal hypoesthesia.

### Post-Refractive Surgery Patients

Patients who have undergone [[LASIK]], [[PRK]], or other corneal refractive procedures may have reduced [[corneal sensitivity]] due to transection of corneal nerves. This can result in diminished reflex tearing and lower Schirmer I (without anesthesia) values, even if basal secretion is preserved. The Schirmer Test is an important component of the preoperative dry eye assessment for refractive surgery candidates.

### Systemic Disease Associations

Reduced Schirmer values may be observed in a variety of systemic conditions beyond [[Sjögren syndrome]], including:

- [[Rheumatoid arthritis]]
- [[Systemic lupus erythematosus]]
- [[Scleroderma]] / [[systemic sclerosis]]
- [[Sarcoidosis]]
- [[Graft-versus-host disease]]
- [[Hepatitis C]]
- [[HIV/AIDS]]
- [[Diabetes mellitus]] (via autonomic neuropathy)
- [[Parkinson disease]]

## Management Implications Based on Schirmer Test Results

The Schirmer Test result directly informs the management strategy for patients with [[aqueous-deficient dry eye]]. Treatment is typically stepwise, guided by disease severity as reflected by Schirmer values, symptom burden, and ocular surface findings.

### Mild Aqueous Deficiency (Schirmer I: 10–14 mm/5 min)

- **[[Artificial tears]]:** Preservative-free formulations are preferred for frequent use. Options include [[carboxymethylcellulose]], [[hyaluronic acid]], [[hydroxypropyl methylcellulose]], and [[polyethylene glycol]]-based drops.
- **Environmental modifications:** Humidifiers, avoidance of air drafts, and screen hygiene (regular blinking exercises, the 20-20-20 rule).
- **Lid hygiene:** Warm compresses and lid scrubs to optimize [[meibomian gland]] function and reduce any coexistent evaporative component.

### Moderate Aqueous Deficiency (Schirmer I: 5–9 mm/5 min)

- **Anti-inflammatory therapy:**
  - [[Cyclosporine A]] 0.05%–0.1% ophthalmic emulsion (e.g., Restasis®, Cequa®): Reduces T-cell–mediated inflammation of the lacrimal gland and ocular surface; has been shown to increase Schirmer scores in clinical trials.
  - [[Lifitegrast]] 5% ophthalmic solution (Xiidra®): An [[LFA-1]] antagonist that inhibits T-cell activation and cytokine release.
  - Short-term [[topical corticosteroids]] (e.g., [[loteprednol etabonate]] 0.5%, [[fluorometholone]] 0.1%) for acute flares.
- **[[Punctal plugs]]:** Temporary (collagen) or semi-permanent (silicone, hydrogel) [[punctal occlusion]] to conserve existing tears on the ocular surface. Punctal plugs are particularly effective when Schirmer values indicate reduced but not absent tear production.
- **Omega-3 fatty acid supplementation:** Although evidence is mixed (the DREAM study showed limited benefit over placebo), some patients report symptomatic improvement.
- **[[Autologous serum tears]]:** 20% concentration; considered when conventional therapies are insufficient.

### Severe Aqueous Deficiency (Schirmer I: ≤5 mm/5 min)

- **Intensified lubrication:** Frequent preservative-free artificial tears, gel formulations (e.g., [[carbomer]] gels), and nighttime ointments (e.g., [[mineral oil]]/[[white petrolatum]]-based).
- **[[Autologous serum tears]]** or **[[platelet-rich plasma]] (PRP) tears:** Provide growth factors, fibronectin, and other bioactive components that promote epithelial healing.
- **[[Punctal cautery]]:** Permanent punctal occlusion via thermal or electrical cautery when plugs are effective but repeatedly lost.
- **[[Moisture chamber spectacles]]** or **[[scleral contact lenses]]:** Scleral lenses vault over the cornea and maintain a fluid reservoir, providing continuous hydration in severe aqueous deficiency.
- **[[Secretagogues]]:** Oral [[pilocarpine]] (5 mg TID) or [[cevimeline]] (30 mg TID) may stimulate residual lacrimal gland function via [[muscarinic receptor]] agonism. These are particularly useful in [[Sjögren syndrome]].
- **Systemic immunomodulation:** In Sjögren syndrome or other autoimmune-related ADDE, systemic agents such as [[hydroxychloroquine]], [[methotrexate]], [[rituximab]], or [[mycophenolate mofetil]] may be considered in collaboration with [[rheumatology]].
- **Surgical options:**
  - **[[Salivary gland transplantation]]** (submandibular gland autotransplantation): Reserved for the most severe, refractory cases.
  - **[[Tarsorrhaphy]]:** Partial or complete eyelid closure to reduce evaporative surface area in extreme cases.
  - **[[Amniotic membrane transplantation]]:** For management of persistent [[corneal epithelial defects]] secondary to severe aqueous deficiency.

## Emerging Technologies and Alternatives

While the Schirmer Test remains a clinical mainstay, several newer technologies offer complementary or alternative assessments of tear production:

- **[[Tear osmolarity]] measurement** (e.g., TearLab®): Provides a quantitative measure of tear film osmolarity; values >308 mOsm/L are diagnostic for dry eye.
- **[[Phenol red thread test]]** (Zone-Quick®): A less invasive, faster (15-second) alternative that measures residual tear volume using a pH-sensitive thread. Normal values are ≥10 mm/15 sec.
- **[[Anterior segment optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT):** Allows non-invasive measurement of [[tear meniscus height]] and cross-sectional area as surrogate markers of tear volume.
- **[[Tear film interferometry]]** (e.g., LipiView®): Evaluates lipid layer thickness and blink dynamics, complementing the aqueous assessment provided by the Schirmer Test.
- **[[Fluorophotometry]]:** A research-grade technique that measures tear flow rate and turnover using fluorescein dilution; more precise than Schirmer but not practical for routine clinical use.
- **Point-of-care [[biomarker]] testing:** [[MMP-9]] (InflammaDry®), [[lactoferrin]] (TearScan™), and [[immunoglobulin E]] (IgE) testing provide molecular-level information about ocular surface inflammation and tear composition.

## Clinical Pearls and Best Practices

1. **Perform the Schirmer Test early in the examination sequence**, before instillation of any drops or manipulation of the ocular surface, to minimize reflex tearing artifacts.
2. **Standardize environmental conditions** as much as possible (consistent room temperature, humidity, and lighting).
3. **Test both eyes simultaneously** to improve efficiency and reduce the influence of time-dependent variability.
4. **Use preservative-free anesthetic** for the basal secretion test to avoid epithelial toxicity that could confound results.
5. **Interpret results in clinical context:** A single Schirmer value should never be used as the sole diagnostic criterion for dry eye. Always correlate with symptoms, ocular surface staining, TBUT, and other objective measures.
6. **Repeat testing on separate visits** when results are borderline or discordant with clinical findings, given the known variability of the test.
7. **Document strip placement location** (temporal third of the lower lid) to ensure consistency across visits.
8. **Consider the basal secretion test** (with anesthesia) when reflex tearing is suspected of masking true aqueous deficiency—a common scenario in patients with paradoxical tearing (epiphora) due to ocular surface irritation.

## Coding and Documentation

In clinical practice, proper documentation and coding of the Schirmer Test are essential for medical records and reimbursement:

- **CPT Code:** The Schirmer Test is typically billed under **CPT 68801** ("Dilation of lacrimal puncta, with or without irrigation") in some jurisdictions, though coding practices vary. In many settings, it is considered part of the comprehensive eye examination and not separately billable.
- **ICD-10 Codes** commonly associated with Schirmer Test indications include:
  - **H04.12** – Dry eye syndrome of lacrimal gland
  - **H04.121–H04.129** – Dry eye syndrome, right/left/bilateral/unspecified
  - **M35.00–M35.09** – Sjögren syndrome

---

## Summary

The **Schirmer Test** remains an indispensable tool in the clinical assessment of [[tear production]] and the diagnosis of [[aqueous-deficient dry eye]]. Its simplicity, low cost, and widespread availability make it a cornerstone of the dry eye diagnostic workup in both optometric and ophthalmologic practice. While the test has well-recognized limitations—including variability, susceptibility to reflex tearing, and poor symptom correlation—its clinical utility is maximized when interpreted in conjunction with other objective and subjective measures of ocular surface health. The Schirmer Test continues to play a critical role in the classification of [[Sjögren syndrome]], preoperative assessment for ocular surgery, and longitudinal monitoring of lacrimal function, ensuring its enduring relevance in modern eye care.
