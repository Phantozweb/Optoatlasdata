

# Snell's Law

---

**Snell's Law** (also known as the **Law of Refraction** or **Snell–Descartes Law**) is a fundamental principle of [[geometric optics]] that quantitatively describes the relationship between the [[angle of incidence]] and the [[angle of refraction]] when a ray of [[light]] passes from one transparent optical medium into another of differing [[refractive index]]. Named after the Dutch astronomer and mathematician **Willebrord Snellius** (1580–1626), and independently derived by **René Descartes**, this law constitutes one of the most essential physical principles underpinning the entire discipline of [[clinical optometry]], [[ophthalmology]], [[optical dispensing]], and [[visual optics]].

Snell's Law is not a disease or pathological condition; rather, it is a **biophysical law** that governs how light is redirected at every refractive interface within the [[human eye]]—from the [[tear film]] to the [[cornea]], [[aqueous humor]], [[crystalline lens]], and [[vitreous humor]]. A thorough understanding of Snell's Law is indispensable for the clinician seeking to comprehend [[refractive errors]], design [[corrective lenses]], interpret [[diagnostic imaging]], plan [[refractive surgery]], and understand the optical basis of virtually every instrument used in eye care.

---

## Mathematical Formulation

Snell's Law is expressed mathematically as:

> **n₁ sin(θ₁) = n₂ sin(θ₂)**

Where:

| Symbol | Definition |
|--------|-----------|
| **n₁** | [[Refractive index]] of the first (incident) medium |
| **n₂** | [[Refractive index]] of the second (refracted) medium |
| **θ₁** | [[Angle of incidence]]—the angle between the incident ray and the [[surface normal]] |
| **θ₂** | [[Angle of refraction]]—the angle between the refracted ray and the surface normal |

The **surface normal** is an imaginary line drawn perpendicular to the refractive interface at the point where the light ray strikes the surface. All angles in Snell's Law are measured with respect to this normal, not to the surface itself.

### Key Corollaries

1. **When light passes from a medium of lower refractive index to one of higher refractive index** (e.g., air to [[cornea]]), the refracted ray bends **toward** the normal (θ₂ < θ₁).
2. **When light passes from a medium of higher refractive index to one of lower refractive index** (e.g., [[glass]] to air), the refracted ray bends **away from** the normal (θ₂ > θ₁).
3. **When light strikes perpendicular to the interface** (θ₁ = 0°), it passes through without deviation regardless of the refractive indices (sin 0° = 0, so θ₂ = 0°).
4. The law applies at **each individual refractive interface**; in a multi-element optical system such as the eye, it must be applied sequentially at every surface.

## Historical Background and Discovery

The empirical observation that light bends when entering a denser medium dates to antiquity. **Claudius Ptolemy** (circa 100–170 AD) attempted to tabulate angles of incidence and refraction but failed to identify the precise mathematical relationship. The Arab scholar **Ibn Sahl** (circa 984 AD) is now credited with the earliest known correct formulation of the law of refraction in his treatise *On Burning Mirrors and Lenses*, predating Snellius by over six centuries.

**Willebrord Snellius** rediscovered the law experimentally around 1621 but never published it. **René Descartes** published the law in his *Dioptrique* (1637), deriving it from theoretical principles—though his derivation contained a flawed assumption about the speed of light. **Pierre de Fermat** later provided a rigorous derivation based on his [[principle of least time]], demonstrating that light follows the path that minimizes travel time between two points, which naturally yields Snell's Law.

The law was further validated by the [[wave theory of light]] developed by **Christiaan Huygens** and later by **James Clerk Maxwell's** [[electromagnetic theory]], which showed that the refractive index is related to the electromagnetic properties of the medium (n = c/v, where c is the speed of light in vacuum and v is the speed in the medium).

## Pathophysiology: Snell's Law in the Refractive System of the Eye

While Snell's Law itself is not a pathological process, its principles directly govern the **pathophysiology of all [[refractive errors]]** ([[ametropia]]). The human eye functions as a compound optical system with multiple refractive interfaces, and any deviation from the ideal application of Snell's Law at these interfaces results in optical aberrations and visual dysfunction.

### Refractive Interfaces of the Human Eye

The following table summarizes the major refractive interfaces of the [[schematic eye]] and their associated refractive indices:

| Interface | Medium Before | Refractive Index (n₁) | Medium After | Refractive Index (n₂) | Approximate Dioptric Power |
|-----------|--------------|----------------------|--------------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| **Air–[[Tear Film]]** | Air | 1.000 | Tear film | 1.336 | — |
| **[[Tear Film]]–[[Corneal Epithelium]]** | Tear film | 1.336 | Corneal epithelium | 1.376 | Combined anterior corneal surface: **+48.83 D** |
| **[[Corneal Stroma]]–[[Aqueous Humor]]** | Corneal stroma | 1.376 | Aqueous humor | 1.336 | Posterior corneal surface: **−5.88 D** |
| **[[Aqueous Humor]]–[[Crystalline Lens]] (anterior)** | Aqueous humor | 1.336 | Lens cortex | 1.386 | Anterior lens surface: **+8.00 to +10.00 D** |
| **[[Crystalline Lens]] (posterior)–[[Vitreous Humor]]** | Lens cortex/nucleus | 1.406 (avg.) | Vitreous humor | 1.336 | Posterior lens surface: **+12.00 to +14.00 D** |

At **each** of these interfaces, Snell's Law dictates the precise angle at which light is refracted. The total refractive power of the relaxed [[emmetropic]] eye is approximately **+58 to +60 diopters (D)**, with the [[anterior corneal surface]] contributing roughly two-thirds of this power due to the large difference in refractive index between air (n = 1.000) and the cornea (n = 1.376).

### Pathophysiological Consequences of Altered Snell's Law Parameters

#### 1. Myopia ([[Nearsightedness]])
In [[myopia]], the axial length of the eye is too long relative to its total refractive power, or the refractive power is excessive. Snell's Law operates normally at each interface, but the cumulative refraction causes light from distant objects to converge **anterior to** the [[retina]]. The condition can also arise from increased corneal curvature (steeper radius), which, per the [[lensmaker's equation]] derived from Snell's Law, increases the dioptric power of the anterior corneal surface.

#### 2. Hyperopia ([[Farsightedness]])
In [[hyperopia]], the eye is too short or the refractive power is insufficient. Light from distant objects is focused **posterior to** the retina. A flatter corneal curvature reduces the refractive power at the air–cornea interface as predicted by Snell's Law.

#### 3. Astigmatism
[[Astigmatism]] arises when the refractive surfaces—most commonly the [[anterior corneal surface]]—are not spherical but [[toric]], possessing different radii of curvature in different meridians. Snell's Law applied independently along each meridian yields different amounts of refraction, producing two distinct [[focal lines]] rather than a single [[focal point]]. This is the direct optical basis for the formation of the [[Sturm's conoid]] (the [[conoid of Sturm]]) and the [[circle of least confusion]].

#### 4. Keratoconus
In [[keratoconus]], progressive thinning and ectasia of the [[corneal stroma]] produce an irregular, cone-shaped cornea. The altered surface geometry means that Snell's Law is applied at a surface with highly irregular and locally variable curvature, resulting in severe [[irregular astigmatism]], [[higher-order aberrations]] (particularly [[coma]]), and significant visual distortion that cannot be fully corrected with [[spectacle lenses]].

#### 5. Lenticular Changes (Cataracts)
In [[cataract]] formation, the refractive index of the [[crystalline lens]] changes non-uniformly due to protein aggregation and water content alterations. This disrupts the orderly application of Snell's Law within the [[gradient-index (GRIN) lens]] structure, producing [[light scatter]], [[glare]], and a shift in refractive error (often a [[myopic shift]] in [[nuclear sclerotic cataract]] due to increased nuclear refractive index).

#### 6. Total Internal Reflection and the Critical Angle
A critically important corollary of Snell's Law is [[total internal reflection]] (TIR). When light travels from a medium of higher refractive index to one of lower refractive index, there exists a **[[critical angle]]** (θc) beyond which all light is reflected back into the denser medium rather than being refracted:

> **θc = arcsin(n₂ / n₁)** (where n₁ > n₂)

This phenomenon is clinically exploited in [[gonioscopy]]. The critical angle at the cornea–air interface (approximately 46.5°) prevents direct visualization of the [[anterior chamber angle]] because light from the angle undergoes total internal reflection at the corneal surface. A [[goniolens]] (e.g., [[Goldmann three-mirror lens]], [[Zeiss four-mirror lens]]) eliminates TIR by replacing the air interface with a contact lens of similar refractive index, coupled with a viscous medium or tear film, thereby allowing the clinician to visualize the [[trabecular meshwork]], [[Schlemm's canal]], and other angle structures.

Total internal reflection is also the operating principle behind [[fiber optics]] used in [[endoilluminators]] during [[vitreoretinal surgery]] and in various [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] illumination systems.

## Clinical Symptoms Related to Disrupted Refraction (Snell's Law Deviations)

Since Snell's Law governs all ocular refraction, any pathological or iatrogenic alteration of the parameters in the equation (refractive index, surface curvature, angle of incidence) can produce the following symptoms:

| Symptom | Mechanism (Snell's Law Basis) | Associated Conditions |
|---------|------------------------------|----------------------|
| **[[Blurred vision]]** | Incorrect focal point position due to altered cumulative refraction | [[Myopia]], [[hyperopia]], [[astigmatism]], [[presbyopia]], [[cataract]] |
| **[[Diplopia]] (monocular)** | Multiple refractive zones within a single optical element | [[Cataract]], [[keratoconus]], post-[[LASIK]] ectasia |
| **[[Glare]] and [[halos]]** | Light scatter from irregular refractive index changes or surface irregularities | [[Cataract]], [[corneal edema]], [[Fuchs' endothelial dystrophy]] |
| **[[Asthenopia]]** | Sustained accommodative effort to compensate for refractive imbalance | Uncorrected [[hyperopia]], [[accommodative dysfunction]] |
| **[[Metamorphopsia]]** | Irregular refraction at a distorted corneal or lenticular surface | [[Keratoconus]], [[corneal scarring]], [[macular pathology]] |
| **[[Chromatic aberration]]** | Wavelength-dependent refractive index ([[dispersion]]) per Snell's Law | Inherent in all refractive media; exacerbated by [[IOL]] design |
| **Night vision disturbances** | Peripheral rays refracted differently through dilated [[pupil]] ([[spherical aberration]]) | Post-[[refractive surgery]], [[keratoconus]] |

## Diagnostic Modalities Based on Snell's Law

Virtually every diagnostic instrument in optometric and ophthalmic practice relies on Snell's Law for its operation. The following are key diagnostic applications:

### 1. [[Retinoscopy]]
[[Retinoscopy]] (skiascopy) is the objective technique for determining [[refractive error]]. The clinician projects a streak or spot of light into the patient's eye and observes the [[retinal reflex]] movement. The light undergoes refraction at the cornea and lens (governed by Snell's Law) on both entry and exit. The [[neutralization point]]—where the reflex fills the pupil without movement—indicates the refractive correction needed. The mathematical basis for converting retinoscopic findings to a spectacle prescription relies entirely on Snell's Law and the [[vergence equation]].

### 2. [[Keratometry]] and [[Corneal Topography]]
[[Keratometry]] measures the radius of curvature of the [[anterior corneal surface]] by analyzing the size and position of reflected images ([[Purkinje images]]). The conversion from radius of curvature to dioptric power uses the formula:

> **P = (n₂ − n₁) / r**

This is a direct derivation from Snell's Law applied to a spherical refracting surface (the [[paraxial approximation]]). Modern [[corneal topography]] systems (e.g., [[Placido disc]]-based, [[Scheimpflug imaging]], [[optical coherence tomography]]-based) map thousands of points on the corneal surface, applying Snell's Law at each point to generate detailed power maps, elevation maps, and aberration profiles.

### 3. [[Optical Coherence Tomography]] (OCT)
[[OCT]] uses [[low-coherence interferometry]] to produce cross-sectional images of ocular structures. The light beam undergoes refraction at each tissue interface according to Snell's Law, and the system must account for the refractive indices of the [[cornea]], [[aqueous humor]], [[crystalline lens]], and [[vitreous humor]] to accurately calculate tissue thicknesses and distances.

### 4. [[Refraction]] (Subjective and Objective)
Both [[subjective refraction]] (using a [[phoropter]] or [[trial frame]]) and [[objective refraction]] (using [[autorefractors]] or [[wavefront aberrometers]]) are fundamentally exercises in applying Snell's Law. The clinician determines the lens power needed to redirect light so that it focuses precisely on the [[fovea]], compensating for the eye's refractive error.

### 5. [[Gonioscopy]]
As described above, [[gonioscopy]] directly exploits the phenomenon of [[total internal reflection]]—a consequence of Snell's Law—to visualize the [[iridocorneal angle]]. Without a goniolens, the critical angle at the cornea–air interface prevents observation of angle structures.

### 6. [[Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy]]
The [[slit-lamp]] uses focused beams of light that undergo refraction at corneal and lenticular surfaces. Techniques such as [[optical section]], [[specular reflection]], and [[retroillumination]] all depend on Snell's Law to create contrast and reveal pathology within transparent ocular media.

### 7. [[Intraocular Lens]] (IOL) Power Calculation
Preoperative [[IOL power calculation]] for [[cataract surgery]] relies on formulas (e.g., [[SRK/T]], [[Holladay]], [[Haigis]], [[Barrett Universal II]]) that incorporate corneal power, axial length, and anterior chamber depth. The corneal power component is derived from Snell's Law applied at the anterior and posterior corneal surfaces. Modern formulas using [[ray tracing]] apply Snell's Law iteratively at each refractive surface to predict postoperative refraction with high accuracy.

## Management and Treatment: Clinical Applications of Snell's Law

### 1. Spectacle Lens Design and Dispensing

[[Spectacle lenses]] correct refractive errors by introducing an additional refractive interface that modifies the vergence of light entering the eye. Snell's Law governs refraction at both surfaces of the spectacle lens. Key considerations include:

- **[[Lens index]]**: Higher-index lens materials (n = 1.60, 1.67, 1.74) bend light more per unit thickness, allowing thinner lenses for high prescriptions. The choice of index directly affects the application of Snell's Law at the lens surfaces.
- **[[Abbe number]] and [[chromatic dispersion]]**: The refractive index varies with wavelength ([[dispersion]]), as described by the [[Cauchy equation]] or [[Sellmeier equation]]. Lower Abbe numbers indicate greater dispersion, producing more [[chromatic aberration]]. This is a direct consequence of Snell's Law being wavelength-dependent.
- **[[Prism]] and decentration**: [[Prismatic effect]] in lenses is governed by Snell's Law. [[Prentice's rule]] (Δ = cF, where Δ is prism diopters, c is decentration in cm, and F is lens power) is derived from the application of Snell's Law to a lens at points away from the optical center.
- **[[Aspheric lens]] design**: Aspheric surfaces are designed to minimize [[oblique astigmatism]] and other peripheral aberrations by optimizing the angle of incidence at each point on the lens surface, ensuring Snell's Law produces the desired refraction across the entire lens aperture.

| Lens Material | Refractive Index (n) | Abbe Number (V) | Clinical Consideration |
|--------------|---------------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| [[CR-39]] | 1.498 | 58 | Standard; low dispersion |
| [[Crown glass]] | 1.523 | 59 | Excellent optics; heavy, fragile |
| [[Polycarbonate]] | 1.586 | 30 | Impact-resistant; high dispersion |
| [[Trivex]] | 1.532 | 43 | Lightweight; good optics |
| High-index plastic | 1.60–1.74 | 32–42 | Thin; increased dispersion |

### 2. Contact Lens Optics

[[Contact lenses]] sit directly on the [[tear film]], effectively replacing the air–cornea interface with a lens–tear film–cornea system. Snell's Law must be applied at the anterior contact lens surface (air–lens), the posterior contact lens surface (lens–tear film), and the anterior corneal surface (tear film–cornea). The [[tear lens]] formed between the posterior surface of a [[rigid gas-permeable (RGP) lens]] and the anterior cornea can itself have refractive power, which is clinically significant in correcting [[astigmatism]] (the "tear lens effect" or "lacrimal lens").

In [[orthokeratology]] ([[ortho-K]]), specially designed RGP lenses are worn overnight to temporarily reshape the [[corneal epithelium]], altering the anterior corneal curvature and thus changing the application of Snell's Law at the primary refractive surface. This produces a temporary reduction in [[myopia]].

### 3. Refractive Surgery

All forms of [[refractive surgery]] achieve their effect by altering the parameters in Snell's Law at the corneal surface:

- **[[LASIK]] (Laser-Assisted In Situ Keratomileusis)**: An [[excimer laser]] ablates [[corneal stroma]] beneath a flap, changing the anterior corneal curvature. For myopia correction, the central cornea is flattened (increased radius of curvature), reducing the dioptric power at the air–cornea interface as predicted by Snell's Law. For hyperopia, the peripheral cornea is ablated to steepen the center.

- **[[PRK]] (Photorefractive Keratectomy)**: Similar to LASIK but the ablation is performed on the corneal surface after epithelial removal. The optical principle is identical—reshaping the corneal curvature to alter refraction per Snell's Law.

- **[[SMILE]] (Small Incision Lenticule Extraction)**: A [[femtosecond laser]] creates a lenticule within the stroma, which is extracted through a small incision. The resulting change in corneal curvature modifies the Snell's Law refraction at the anterior surface.

- **[[Phakic IOL]] implantation**: An additional refractive element is placed inside the eye (e.g., [[Visian ICL]]), introducing new refractive interfaces where Snell's Law applies.

- **[[Refractive lens exchange]] (RLE)**: The natural crystalline lens is replaced with an [[IOL]] of calculated power, altering the refractive indices and surface curvatures within the eye.

### 4. Intraocular Lens Selection

Modern [[IOL]] designs—including [[monofocal]], [[multifocal]], [[extended depth of focus (EDOF)]], and [[toric]] lenses—are engineered using Snell's Law and advanced ray-tracing algorithms. The refractive index of common IOL materials includes:

| IOL Material | Refractive Index | Example |
|-------------|-----------------|---------|
| [[PMMA]] | 1.49 | Historical rigid IOLs |
| [[Silicone]] | 1.41–1.46 | Foldable IOLs |
| [[Hydrophobic acrylic]] | 1.47–1.55 | Most commonly used today |
| [[Hydrophilic acrylic]] | 1.43–1.47 | Select foldable IOLs |

Higher refractive index IOL materials allow thinner optic profiles, facilitating smaller incision surgery. The IOL power calculation formulas incorporate Snell's Law (either explicitly through ray tracing or implicitly through vergence-based thin-lens approximations) to determine the optimal lens power for achieving the target postoperative refraction.

### 5. Prism Therapy

[[Prism lenses]] are prescribed to manage [[binocular vision disorders]] such as [[strabismus]], [[convergence insufficiency]], and [[diplopia]]. A prism deviates light by applying Snell's Law at its two non-parallel surfaces. The amount of deviation depends on the prism's [[apical angle]], refractive index, and the angle of incidence. [[Fresnel prisms]]—thin, lightweight press-on prisms—use the same principle with a series of small prism elements.

### 6. Low Vision Aids and Magnification

[[Magnification]] systems used in [[low vision rehabilitation]]—including [[high-plus spectacles]], [[stand magnifiers]], [[loupes]], and [[telescopic systems]]—all rely on Snell's Law to redirect light and produce enlarged retinal images. The design of these devices requires precise application of refraction principles at multiple lens surfaces.

## Snell's Law and Optical Aberrations

In the [[paraxial approximation]] (small angles near the optical axis), Snell's Law simplifies to:

> **n₁ θ₁ ≈ n₂ θ₂**

This linear approximation is the basis of [[first-order optics]] (Gaussian optics) and works well for rays close to the optical axis. However, for rays at larger angles (marginal rays), the full sinusoidal form of Snell's Law must be used, and deviations from the paraxial prediction produce **[[optical aberrations]]**:

| Aberration | Snell's Law Basis | Clinical Impact |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------|
| **[[Spherical aberration]]** | Marginal rays refracted more than paraxial rays at a spherical surface | [[Halos]], reduced contrast sensitivity; addressed by [[aspheric IOLs]] and [[wavefront-guided LASIK]] |
| **[[Coma]]** | Off-axis rays refracted asymmetrically | Comet-shaped image blur; prominent in [[keratoconus]] |
| **[[Chromatic aberration]]** | Wavelength-dependent refractive index (dispersion) | Color fringing; minimized by [[achromatic lens]] design |
| **[[Oblique astigmatism]]** | Different refraction in tangential vs. sagittal planes for off-axis rays | Peripheral blur in spectacle lenses; corrected by [[best-form lens]] design |
| **[[Curvature of field]]** | Image surface is curved rather than flat | Relevant to retinal image quality and [[wide-field imaging]] |
| **[[Distortion]]** | Magnification varies with distance from optical axis | Pincushion/barrel distortion in high-power lenses |

Modern [[wavefront aberrometry]] (e.g., [[Hartmann-Shack sensor]]) measures the total wavefront error of the eye, which represents the cumulative effect of Snell's Law applied at all ocular surfaces for all ray heights and angles. This data drives [[wavefront-guided]] and [[wavefront-optimized]] refractive surgery and [[customized contact lens]] design.

## Snell's Law in Immersion Media and Surgical Optics

During [[vitreoretinal surgery]], the surgeon views the retina through the cornea, aqueous, lens (or IOL), and vitreous—each interface governed by Snell's Law. The use of [[wide-angle viewing systems]] (e.g., [[BIOM]], [[Resight]]) and [[contact lenses]] (e.g., [[flat contact lens]], [[prism contact lens]]) during surgery is designed to manage the refraction at these interfaces and overcome total internal reflection at the retinal–vitreous interface for peripheral retinal visualization.

In [[immersion ultrasonography]] (e.g., [[immersion A-scan biometry]]), the ultrasound probe is coupled to the eye through a fluid medium, and the speed of sound (analogous to the speed of light in Snell's Law) differs in each ocular tissue. The same mathematical framework of Snell's Law applies to acoustic waves, enabling accurate measurement of [[axial length]] for IOL power calculation.

## Snell's Law and Dispersion: The Basis of Chromatic Effects

The refractive index of any transparent medium is not a single value but varies with the [[wavelength]] of light—a phenomenon known as [[dispersion]]. Snell's Law, when applied with wavelength-dependent refractive indices, predicts that different colors of light will be refracted by different amounts. This produces:

- **[[Longitudinal chromatic aberration]] (LCA)**: Different wavelengths focus at different distances along the optical axis. The human eye has approximately **+1.50 to +2.00 D** of LCA across the visible spectrum, meaning blue light focuses approximately 1.5 D anterior to red light.
- **[[Transverse chromatic aberration]] (TCA)**: Different wavelengths produce images of different sizes, causing color fringing at the edges of the visual field.

The [[Abbe number]] (V-number) quantifies dispersion:

> **V = (nD − 1) / (nF − nC)**

Where nD, nF, and nC are the refractive indices at the sodium D-line (589 nm), hydrogen F-line (486 nm), and hydrogen C-line (656 nm), respectively. Lower Abbe numbers indicate greater dispersion and more chromatic aberration—a critical consideration in spectacle lens material selection.

## Snell's Window: A Clinical and Physical Phenomenon

**[[Snell's window]]** (also called the **optical manhole**) is the circular cone of light visible to an observer submerged in a medium of higher refractive index (e.g., water) looking upward toward a medium of lower refractive index (e.g., air). Due to total internal reflection, only light entering within the critical angle is transmitted; all other light is reflected. The half-angle of Snell's window equals the critical angle.

While primarily a physical optics phenomenon, Snell's window is conceptually analogous to the limited field of view encountered in [[gonioscopy]] and [[fundoscopy]] when viewing through the curved corneal surface, and it illustrates why specialized lenses are required to overcome total internal reflection in clinical examination.

## Clinical Pearls

1. **The cornea is the most powerful refractive element** of the eye (~+43 D) because of the large refractive index difference at the air–cornea interface (Δn ≈ 0.376). This is why even small changes in corneal curvature (e.g., 1 mm change in radius ≈ 6 D change in power) have profound refractive consequences.

2. **Immersion of the cornea in water** (n = 1.333) nearly eliminates its refractive power (Δn ≈ 0.043), which is why unaided underwater vision is severely blurred and why [[swimming goggles]] restore the air–cornea interface.

3. **Post-[[LASIK]] keratometry readings are inaccurate** when standard keratometric indices are used because the relationship between anterior and posterior corneal curvature has been altered. Adjusted formulas or direct measurement of both surfaces (e.g., with [[Scheimpflug imaging]] or [[OCT]]) are required for accurate IOL power calculation in post-refractive surgery eyes.

4. **The [[gradient refractive index]]** of the crystalline lens means that Snell's Law must be applied continuously throughout the lens substance, not just at its surfaces. This GRIN structure contributes approximately +20 D to the lens's total power and partially compensates for the eye's spherical aberration.

5. **[[Chromatic aberration]] of the eye** can be exploited diagnostically using the [[duochrome test]] (red-green test), which relies on the differential refraction of red and green light (per Snell's Law with wavelength-dependent n) to refine the spherical component of the refraction.
