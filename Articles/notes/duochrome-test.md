

# Duochrome Test

---

The **Duochrome Test** (also known as the **Bichrome Test**, **Red-Green Test**, or **Duo-chrome Refraction Test**) is a widely employed clinical technique in [[Optometry]] and [[Ophthalmology]] used to refine the [[Spherical Equivalent]] component of a patient's [[Refractive Error]] during [[Subjective Refraction]]. The test exploits the principle of [[Chromatic Aberration]]—specifically, [[Longitudinal Chromatic Aberration]] (LCA)—inherent to the human [[Eye]] to determine whether a patient is optimally corrected, slightly [[Myopia|myopic]] (over-minused), or slightly [[Hyperopia|hyperopic]] (under-minused). It is considered a fundamental endpoint test in clinical refraction and is taught universally in optometric and ophthalmic training programs worldwide.

The test is typically presented as a split-field illuminated chart with one half displaying optotypes (usually [[Snellen Letters]] or numbers) on a **red background** and the other half on a **green background**. The patient is asked to compare the clarity or "blackness" of the letters on each side. Based on the patient's response, the clinician adjusts the spherical power of the correcting lens to achieve optimal refraction.

---

## Historical Background

The duochrome test has its origins in the understanding of [[Chromatic Aberration]] that dates back to Sir [[Isaac Newton]]'s experiments with [[Prism (optics)|prisms]] in the 17th century. Newton demonstrated that white light is composed of a spectrum of wavelengths, each refracted to a different degree by optical media. The clinical application of this principle to refraction was developed in the early-to-mid 20th century as optometric instrumentation became more sophisticated. The test became a standard component of the [[Subjective Refraction]] routine by the mid-1900s and remains a staple of modern clinical practice, incorporated into both manual [[Phoropter]] examinations and automated [[Autorefractor]]-assisted protocols.

---

## Underlying Optical Principle: Chromatic Aberration

### Longitudinal Chromatic Aberration (LCA)

The duochrome test is grounded entirely in the phenomenon of [[Longitudinal Chromatic Aberration]] (LCA). LCA occurs because the [[Refractive Index]] of any optical medium—including the [[Cornea]], [[Aqueous Humor]], [[Crystalline Lens]], and [[Vitreous Humor]]—varies with the [[Wavelength]] of light. Shorter wavelengths (toward the blue-violet end of the [[Visible Spectrum]]) are refracted more strongly than longer wavelengths (toward the red end). This means that different wavelengths of light come to focus at different points along the [[Optical Axis]] of the eye.

In the human eye, the total LCA across the visible spectrum (approximately 400–700 nm) is approximately **1.50 to 2.00 [[Diopter|diopters]]**. The specific wavelengths used in the duochrome test are:

| Parameter | Red Filter | Green Filter |
|---|---|---|
| **Approximate Wavelength** | ~620–700 nm (peak ~620 nm) | ~500–565 nm (peak ~535 nm) |
| **Focal Point Relative to Retina (Emmetropic Eye)** | Slightly **behind** the [[Retina]] | Slightly **in front of** the [[Retina]] |
| **Chromatic Interval** | ~0.50 D difference between red and green foci | |

In an optimally corrected (emmetropic or fully corrected ametropic) eye, the [[Circle of Least Confusion]] for white light falls precisely on the [[Retina]]. Under this condition, the red wavelengths focus approximately **0.25 D behind** the retina and the green wavelengths focus approximately **0.25 D in front of** the retina. Because the defocus is equal and opposite for both colors, the patient perceives the optotypes on both the red and green backgrounds as **equally clear** (or equally blurred).

### Relationship to the Interval of Sturm

In the context of [[Astigmatism]], the [[Interval of Sturm]] describes the distance between the two focal lines produced by a toric optical system. The duochrome test is analogous in concept: the red and green focal points create a small "chromatic interval" along the optical axis. The goal of the test is to position the [[Circle of Least Confusion]] of this chromatic interval on the retinal plane, thereby achieving the best spherical balance.

## Pathophysiology and Physiological Basis

### Chromatic Dispersion in Ocular Media

The duochrome test does not assess a pathological condition per se; rather, it leverages normal ocular physiology. The [[Cornea]] contributes approximately two-thirds of the eye's total [[Refractive Power]] (~43 D), and the [[Crystalline Lens]] contributes the remaining one-third (~15–20 D). Both structures exhibit chromatic dispersion, meaning their refractive power varies with wavelength. The combined effect produces the LCA exploited by the duochrome test.

The [[Abbe Number]] (V-number) is a measure of a material's dispersion. Lower Abbe numbers indicate greater dispersion. The ocular media have an effective Abbe number of approximately **45–55**, which produces the clinically significant LCA of ~1.50–2.00 D across the full visible spectrum. The duochrome test uses only the red and green portions of the spectrum, narrowing the effective chromatic interval to approximately **0.50 D**.

### Neural Processing of Chromatic Information

The [[Retina]] contains three types of [[Cone Cell|cone photoreceptors]]: [[S-cone|S-cones]] (short-wavelength, blue-sensitive), [[M-cone|M-cones]] (medium-wavelength, green-sensitive), and [[L-cone|L-cones]] (long-wavelength, red-sensitive). The duochrome test primarily stimulates the [[L-cone|L-cones]] (red background) and [[M-cone|M-cones]] (green background). The patient's subjective comparison of clarity on each side depends on the relative sharpness of the retinal image formed by each wavelength band, which is processed through the [[Parvocellular Pathway]] responsible for high-spatial-frequency detail and color opponency.

### Accommodation and Its Influence

[[Accommodation]] is a critical confounding variable in the duochrome test. The [[Ciliary Muscle]] can alter the power of the [[Crystalline Lens]], shifting the focal point of all wavelengths simultaneously. If the patient accommodates during the test, the focal points of both red and green light shift anteriorly (toward the front of the eye), potentially causing the green letters to appear clearer even when the patient is optimally corrected or slightly under-corrected. This is why the duochrome test is ideally performed under conditions that minimize accommodative effort, and why the clinician typically **fogs** the patient (adds plus power) before initiating the test.

## Clinical Indications

The duochrome test is indicated in the following clinical scenarios:

1. **Refinement of spherical refraction** during routine [[Subjective Refraction]] — the most common indication.
2. **Verification of endpoint** after [[Retinoscopy]] or [[Autorefraction]] to confirm that the spherical component is neither over-minused nor under-minused.
3. **Binocular balancing** — a modified duochrome technique can be used to balance accommodation between the two eyes.
4. **Screening for residual spherical error** in patients with [[Contact Lens]] prescriptions or post-[[Refractive Surgery]] (e.g., [[LASIK]], [[PRK]]).
5. **Pediatric and low-literacy refraction** — because the test requires only a simple comparison judgment ("Which side is clearer, red or green?"), it can be used with patients who have difficulty reading standard [[Snellen Chart|Snellen charts]].
6. **Confirmation of monocular endpoint** before proceeding to [[Binocular Vision]] testing.

---

## Contraindications and Limitations

| Contraindication / Limitation | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **[[Color Vision Deficiency]]** (e.g., [[Protanopia]], [[Deuteranopia]]) | Patients with red-green color blindness cannot reliably distinguish or compare the two backgrounds. The test is **unreliable** in these patients. |
| **Significant [[Media Opacity]]** | [[Cataract]], [[Corneal Opacity]], or [[Vitreous Hemorrhage]] may differentially scatter or absorb wavelengths, invalidating the chromatic comparison. |
| **Very low [[Visual Acuity]]** | Patients who cannot resolve the optotypes on either side (e.g., VA worse than ~20/60 to 20/80) may not provide meaningful responses. |
| **Uncontrolled [[Accommodation]]** | Young patients with high accommodative tone may consistently prefer the green side, leading to over-minusing. Adequate fogging is essential. |
| **[[Amblyopia]]** | The amblyopic eye may not provide reliable subjective responses due to reduced cortical processing of spatial detail. |
| **Significant uncorrected [[Astigmatism]]** | The duochrome test refines only the spherical component. Uncorrected astigmatism introduces a [[Conoid of Sturm]] that confounds the chromatic comparison. Astigmatism should be corrected **before** performing the duochrome test. |

## Clinical Procedure

### Equipment Required

- [[Phoropter]] or [[Trial Frame]] with [[Trial Lens|trial lenses]]
- Duochrome chart (projected or internally illuminated within a [[Projector Chart]] system or [[Digital Acuity Chart]])
- Appropriate room illumination (dimmed to enhance contrast on the duochrome chart)

### Step-by-Step Protocol

1. **Correct astigmatism first.** Ensure that the [[Cylinder]] axis and power have been determined and placed in the phoropter or trial frame. The duochrome test is used **only** to refine the [[Sphere]] component.

2. **Fog the patient.** Add approximately **+0.50 to +0.75 D** of sphere power over the current best sphere to relax [[Accommodation]] and ensure the patient begins the test in a slightly myopic (over-plussed) state. This is critical because the test is designed to approach the endpoint from the "red-clearer" (myopic) side.

3. **Present the duochrome chart.** Display the red-green split chart at the standard testing distance (typically 6 meters / 20 feet or calibrated equivalent for the chart system).

4. **Ask the patient:** *"Looking at the chart, are the letters clearer and blacker on the red side, the green side, or are they about the same?"*

5. **Interpret the response and adjust:**

| Patient Response | Interpretation | Clinical Action |
|---|---|---|
| **"Red is clearer"** | The eye is **fogged** (relatively myopic); the [[Circle of Least Confusion]] is anterior to the retina, so longer wavelengths (red) are closer to the retinal plane. | Reduce plus power (or add minus power) in **−0.25 D steps**. |
| **"Green is clearer"** | The eye is **under-plussed** (relatively hyperopic or over-minused); the circle of least confusion is posterior to the retina, so shorter wavelengths (green) are closer to the retinal plane. | Add plus power (or reduce minus power) in **+0.25 D steps**. |
| **"Both sides are equal"** | The eye is **optimally corrected**; the circle of least confusion lies on the retina, and the chromatic defocus is balanced. | **Endpoint reached.** No further adjustment needed. |

6. **Repeat until balanced.** Continue adjusting in 0.25 D steps until the patient reports equality or, if equality cannot be achieved, **leave the patient slightly fogged** (preferring red) rather than over-minused (preferring green). This is the universally accepted clinical convention: **"Leave the patient in the red"** to avoid stimulating unnecessary accommodation and to provide a more comfortable prescription.

7. **Perform binocularly if desired.** Some clinicians perform a binocular duochrome balance by alternately occluding each eye and comparing the duochrome responses, then equalizing the fog between the two eyes.

### Mnemonic Aids

Several mnemonics are commonly taught to optometry students:

- **"Red is Retarded"** — Red wavelengths are refracted less (retarded), focusing behind the retina in an emmetropic eye.
- **"Green is Grabby"** — Green wavelengths are refracted more, focusing in front of the retina.
- **"R.A.M. / G.A.P."** — **R**ed = **A**dd **M**inus; **G**reen = **A**dd **P**lus.
- **"If in doubt, leave in the red and get out"** — Always err on the side of slight fogging (myopic shift) rather than over-correction.

## Interpretation in Specific Refractive Conditions

### Myopia

In a [[Myopia|myopic]] patient who is **under-corrected** (insufficient minus), the focal point of all wavelengths lies in front of the retina. Green wavelengths, being refracted more, are even further in front. The patient will report **green clearer**. Adding minus power moves the focal points posteriorly toward the retina.

### Hyperopia

In a [[Hyperopia|hyperopic]] patient who is **under-corrected** (insufficient plus), the focal point lies behind the retina. Red wavelengths, being refracted less, are even further behind. The patient will report **red clearer**. Adding plus power moves the focal points anteriorly toward the retina.

### Emmetropia / Optimal Correction

When the [[Spherical Equivalent]] is correctly placed on the retina, the red and green defocus are symmetric, and the patient reports **equal clarity** on both sides.

### Post-Cycloplegic Refraction

When [[Cycloplegia]] has been induced (e.g., with [[Cyclopentolate]] or [[Tropicamide]]), accommodation is paralyzed, and the duochrome test can be performed without concern for accommodative interference. This is particularly useful in [[Pediatric Optometry|pediatric patients]] and young adults with suspected [[Latent Hyperopia]] or [[Accommodative Spasm]].

## Modifications and Variants

### Binocular Duochrome Balancing

In this technique, the duochrome test is performed binocularly with a [[Polarized Filter]] or [[Septum]] to dissociate the two eyes. Each eye views the duochrome chart independently, and the clinician adjusts the sphere for each eye until both report equal red-green balance. This ensures that the accommodative demand is equalized between the two eyes, which is important for [[Binocular Vision]] comfort.

### Duochrome with Reduced Illumination

Some clinicians perform the duochrome test under reduced ambient illumination to increase [[Pupil]] size, which amplifies the effect of chromatic aberration and may make the test more sensitive. However, larger pupils also increase the influence of [[Spherical Aberration]] and [[Higher-Order Aberrations]], which can confound results.

### Digital and Automated Duochrome

Modern [[Digital Acuity Chart|digital acuity systems]] (e.g., those by M&S Technologies, Topcon, or Reichert) include built-in duochrome slides with precisely calibrated red and green filters. Some [[Autorefractor|autorefractors]] and [[Aberrometer|aberrometers]] incorporate a chromatic aberration analysis that provides an objective analog to the subjective duochrome test.

### Modified Duochrome for Low Vision

For patients with [[Low Vision]], larger optotypes or high-contrast symbols can be used on the duochrome background. Alternatively, the patient can be asked to compare the overall brightness or "sharpness" of the colored backgrounds rather than individual letters.

## Relationship to Other Refraction Techniques

| Technique | Relationship to Duochrome Test |
|---|---|
| **[[Retinoscopy]]** | Provides an objective starting point; duochrome refines the subjective endpoint. |
| **[[Jackson Cross-Cylinder]] (JCC)** | Refines [[Cylinder]] axis and power; duochrome refines [[Sphere]] only. The JCC should be performed **before** the duochrome test. |
| **[[Maximum Plus to Maximum Visual Acuity]] (MPMVA)** | An alternative endpoint technique; some clinicians use MPMVA first and confirm with duochrome. |
| **[[Binocular Balancing]]** | Duochrome can be used as a binocular balancing method (see above). Other methods include prism dissociation and alternate occlusion. |
| **[[Autorefraction]]** | Provides an objective measurement; duochrome serves as a subjective verification. |
| **[[Aberrometry]]** | Measures [[Higher-Order Aberrations]] and total wavefront error; duochrome addresses only defocus (sphere). |

## Sources of Error and Clinical Pearls

### Common Sources of Error

1. **Failure to fog adequately.** If the patient is not fogged before beginning the test, accommodation may cause a false "green clearer" response, leading to over-minusing.

2. **Uncorrected astigmatism.** Residual [[Astigmatism]] creates a [[Conoid of Sturm]] that interacts unpredictably with the chromatic interval, producing unreliable responses.

3. **Color vision deficiency.** Approximately **8% of males** and **0.5% of females** of Northern European descent have some form of [[Color Vision Deficiency]], most commonly [[Deuteranomaly]] or [[Protanomaly]]. These patients may not perceive the red and green backgrounds equivalently, invalidating the test. A [[Color Vision Test]] (e.g., [[Ishihara Test]], [[Hardy-Rand-Rittler Test]]) should be performed before relying on the duochrome test.

4. **Chromatic filters of inconsistent quality.** Poorly calibrated or faded filters may not isolate the intended wavelength bands, reducing test accuracy.

5. **Patient misunderstanding.** Some patients compare the **brightness** of the backgrounds rather than the **clarity of the letters**. The clinician must clearly instruct the patient to compare the sharpness and blackness of the optotypes, not the vividness of the colors.

6. **Instrument myopia.** When using a [[Phoropter]], the enclosed viewing environment can induce [[Instrument Myopia]], causing the patient to accommodate and falsely prefer the green side.

### Clinical Pearls

- Always approach the endpoint from the **fogged (red-clearer) side** by adding plus before beginning the test.
- If the patient **immediately reports green clearer** without fogging, add +0.75 to +1.00 D and restart.
- The duochrome test is most reliable at visual acuity levels of **[[20/30]] or better**. Below this level, the blur circles for both colors overlap significantly, reducing discriminability.
- In **presbyopic patients**, the duochrome test can be performed at near through the proposed [[Bifocal]] or [[Progressive Addition Lens|progressive]] add to verify the near spherical endpoint.
- The test is **not a substitute** for careful [[Jackson Cross-Cylinder]] refinement of astigmatism. It should be used **after** cylinder has been finalized.
- In patients with **[[Pseudophakia]]** (intraocular lens implants), the chromatic aberration characteristics may differ from those of the natural crystalline lens, potentially altering duochrome responses. Some [[Intraocular Lens|IOLs]], particularly those made of high-Abbe-number materials, have reduced chromatic aberration.

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Patients

Children, particularly those under age 10, may have difficulty understanding the duochrome comparison task. Simplified instructions ("Which side has the darker, sharper letters—the red side or the green side?") and the use of familiar symbols (e.g., [[LEA Symbols]] or [[HOTV Test|HOTV letters]]) on the duochrome background can improve reliability. [[Cycloplegic Refraction]] is often preferred in young children to eliminate accommodative variability, making the duochrome test more reliable when performed post-cycloplegia.

### Geriatric Patients

Older patients frequently have [[Age-Related Nuclear Sclerosis]] or early [[Cataract]], which increases the absorption of shorter wavelengths (blue and green). This can cause a systematic bias toward **red clearer** responses even when the refraction is optimal. Clinicians should be aware of this potential artifact and correlate duochrome findings with other endpoint techniques (e.g., MPMVA, [[Best Vision Sphere]]).

### Patients with Macular Disease

Patients with [[Age-Related Macular Degeneration]], [[Macular Edema]], or other [[Maculopathy|maculopathies]] may have distorted or reduced central vision that impairs their ability to compare letter clarity on the two backgrounds. The duochrome test may be unreliable in these patients, and alternative refraction endpoints should be used.

## Pharmacological Considerations

While the duochrome test itself is not a pharmacological intervention, certain ophthalmic medications directly affect its performance:

| Medication | Effect on Duochrome Test |
|---|---|
| **[[Cyclopentolate]] (1%)** | Induces [[Cycloplegia]], eliminating accommodative interference. Improves reliability of duochrome in young patients. |
| **[[Tropicamide]] (1%)** | Primarily [[Mydriasis|mydriatic]] with mild cycloplegic effect. May partially reduce accommodative interference. |
| **[[Atropine]] (1%)** | Potent, long-lasting cycloplegic. Eliminates accommodation completely. Ideal for duochrome testing in children with high accommodative tone. |
| **[[Phenylephrine]] (2.5%)** | Mydriatic only (no cycloplegia). Dilates pupil, which may increase chromatic aberration effect but does not control accommodation. |
| **[[Pilocarpine]]** | [[Miotic]]; constricts pupil and stimulates accommodation. **Contraindicated** before duochrome testing as it induces a myopic shift. |

## Evidence Base and Reliability

Studies evaluating the **test-retest reliability** of the duochrome test have generally found it to be a **moderately reliable** endpoint technique with a precision of approximately **±0.25 to ±0.50 D** in cooperative patients. Its reliability is enhanced when:

- The patient has good visual acuity (20/30 or better).
- Astigmatism has been fully corrected.
- Accommodation is controlled (via fogging or cycloplegia).
- The patient has normal color vision.

Comparative studies have shown that the duochrome test produces endpoints that are **clinically equivalent** to those obtained by the [[Maximum Plus to Maximum Visual Acuity]] (MPMVA) technique in most patients, though slight systematic differences may exist in young patients with active accommodation.

Research by **Borish (1970)**, **Bennett and Rabbetts (1998)**, and **Grosvenor (2007)** has extensively documented the optical principles and clinical application of the duochrome test, establishing it as a validated component of the standard refraction protocol.

---

## Summary

The duochrome test is an elegant, simple, and clinically valuable tool that exploits the natural [[Longitudinal Chromatic Aberration]] of the human eye to refine the spherical component of a patient's refractive correction. When performed correctly—with adequate fogging, prior correction of astigmatism, and clear patient instructions—it provides a reliable endpoint that helps ensure optimal visual comfort and acuity. Its limitations in patients with [[Color Vision Deficiency]], significant [[Media Opacity]], or uncontrolled [[Accommodation]] must be recognized, and alternative techniques should be employed in these populations. The duochrome test remains an indispensable component of the optometric and ophthalmic examination.
