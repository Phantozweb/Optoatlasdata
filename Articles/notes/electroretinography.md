

# Electroretinography

**Electroretinography (ERG)** is a specialized, non-invasive electrophysiological diagnostic technique that measures the electrical responses of the various cell types in the [[retina]], including the [[photoreceptors]] ([[rod cells]] and [[cone cells]]), [[inner retinal cells]] ([[bipolar cells]] and [[amacrine cells]]), and [[retinal ganglion cells]], in response to calibrated light stimulation. The resulting waveform, known as the **electroretinogram**, provides an objective, quantitative assessment of retinal function that is indispensable in the diagnosis, monitoring, and prognostication of a wide spectrum of inherited and acquired retinal diseases. Electroretinography remains one of the cornerstone investigations in clinical [[electrophysiology]] of vision and is standardized internationally by the **International Society for Clinical Electrophysiology of Vision (ISCEV)**.

---

## History and Development

The electrical activity of the retina in response to light was first described by the Swedish physiologist **Alarik Frithiof Holmgren** in 1865, who recorded electrical potentials from the eyes of frogs. Subsequent foundational work by **Dewar and McKendrick** (1873) confirmed these findings in humans. The modern clinical application of ERG was significantly advanced by **Ragnar Granit**, who in the 1930s and 1940s dissected the ERG waveform into its component processes (PI, PII, and PIII), work for which he was awarded the **Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine** in 1967. The development of contact lens electrodes by **Karpe** in 1945 made clinical ERG practical, and the subsequent establishment of ISCEV standards (first published in 1989, with regular updates) has ensured reproducibility and comparability of ERG recordings across institutions worldwide.

## Physiological Basis and Pathophysiology

### Retinal Electrophysiology

The [[retina]] is a highly organized, multilayered neural tissue that transduces light energy into electrical signals. Understanding the cellular origins of the ERG waveform is essential for clinical interpretation.

#### Photoreceptor Layer

The initial event in visual transduction occurs in the [[outer segments]] of [[rod cells]] and [[cone cells]]. In darkness, a steady inward current (the "dark current") flows through cyclic guanosine monophosphate (cGMP)-gated channels in the outer segment membrane. Light absorption by [[rhodopsin]] (in rods) or [[cone opsins]] (in cones) activates a G-protein cascade ([[transducin]] → [[phosphodiesterase]]) that hydrolyzes cGMP, leading to closure of these channels, hyperpolarization of the photoreceptor, and a reduction in [[glutamate]] release at the synaptic terminal. This photoreceptor hyperpolarization is the primary generator of the **a-wave** of the ERG.

#### Inner Retinal Layers

The reduction in glutamate release from photoreceptors is detected by second-order neurons. **ON-bipolar cells** (which express [[mGluR6]] receptors) depolarize in response to decreased glutamate, while **OFF-bipolar cells** (which express ionotropic glutamate receptors) hyperpolarize. The depolarization of ON-bipolar cells, mediated by a slow potassium (K⁺) current in [[Müller cells]] (the principal retinal glial cells), generates the **b-wave** of the ERG. Müller cells play a critical role by siphoning extracellular K⁺ released during bipolar cell activity, producing transretinal currents that contribute substantially to the b-wave amplitude.

#### Oscillatory Potentials

Superimposed on the ascending limb of the b-wave are a series of small, rapid, rhythmic wavelets known as **oscillatory potentials (OPs)**. These are believed to originate from feedback interactions among [[amacrine cells]], [[bipolar cells]], and [[retinal ganglion cells]] in the [[inner plexiform layer]]. OPs are particularly sensitive to inner retinal ischemia and are clinically significant in conditions such as [[diabetic retinopathy]].

#### Retinal Pigment Epithelium

The **c-wave**, a slow positive potential following the b-wave, arises from the hyperpolarization of the apical membrane of the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE) in response to the light-induced decrease in subretinal K⁺ concentration. While the c-wave is not routinely measured in standard clinical ERG, it is assessed in the **electro-oculogram (EOG)**, which evaluates RPE function.

### Summary of Waveform Generators

| ERG Component | Primary Cellular Origin | Ionic Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **a-wave** | [[Photoreceptors]] (rods and cones) | Closure of cGMP-gated Na⁺/Ca²⁺ channels; photoreceptor hyperpolarization |
| **b-wave** | [[ON-bipolar cells]] / [[Müller cells]] | K⁺ currents generated by ON-bipolar cell depolarization and Müller cell K⁺ siphoning |
| **Oscillatory Potentials** | [[Amacrine cells]], inner retinal circuitry | Feedback inhibitory circuits in the [[inner plexiform layer]] |
| **c-wave** | [[Retinal pigment epithelium]] | RPE apical membrane hyperpolarization due to decreased subretinal [K⁺] |
| **d-wave (OFF response)** | [[OFF-bipolar cells]], cone photoreceptors | Depolarization of OFF-pathway neurons at light offset |

## Types of Electroretinography

### Full-Field (Ganzfeld) ERG (ffERG)

The **full-field ERG** is the most widely used form and is the basis of the ISCEV standard. It records a mass electrical response from the entire retina using a Ganzfeld (uniform field) stimulator. The ISCEV standard protocol (updated 2022) includes the following six responses:

| Protocol Step | Adaptation State | Stimulus | Primary Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Dark-adapted 0.01** | Scotopic (≥20 min dark adaptation) | Dim flash (0.01 cd·s/m²) | [[Rod cells]] (rod-driven b-wave only) |
| **Dark-adapted 3.0** | Scotopic | Standard flash (3.0 cd·s/m²) | Combined rod and cone response (a-wave and b-wave) |
| **Dark-adapted 10.0** | Scotopic | Strong flash (10.0 cd·s/m²) | Enhanced a-wave for photoreceptor assessment |
| **Dark-adapted 3.0 OPs** | Scotopic | Standard flash with bandpass filter (75–300 Hz) | [[Oscillatory potentials]]; inner retinal function |
| **Light-adapted 3.0** | Photopic (≥10 min light adaptation, 30 cd/m² background) | Standard flash (3.0 cd·s/m²) | [[Cone cells]] (cone-driven response) |
| **Light-adapted 30 Hz flicker** | Photopic | 30 Hz flickering stimulus (3.0 cd·s/m²) | Cone pathway temporal resolution; cone system isolation |

### Multifocal ERG (mfERG)

The **multifocal ERG (mfERG)**, developed by **Erich Sutter** in the early 1990s, uses a pseudorandom binary m-sequence stimulus (typically a hexagonal array of 61 or 103 elements) to simultaneously record local ERG responses from multiple discrete retinal areas. This technique provides a topographic map of retinal function, with the highest spatial resolution in the [[macula]] and [[fovea]]. The mfERG is particularly valuable for detecting localized retinal dysfunction that may be missed by the full-field ERG, such as in:

- [[Hydroxychloroquine]] (Plaquenil) retinal toxicity screening
- [[Macular dystrophies]]
- Localized [[retinal detachment]]
- [[Stargardt disease]] (early stages)
- Unexplained visual acuity loss with normal fundoscopy

The first-order kernel of the mfERG waveform consists of an initial negative deflection (N1), a positive peak (P1), and a second negative trough (N2). The N1 component is analogous to the a-wave and reflects outer retinal (photoreceptor and OFF-bipolar cell) activity, while the P1 component is analogous to the b-wave and reflects ON- and OFF-bipolar cell activity.

### Pattern ERG (PERG)

The **pattern ERG (PERG)** is elicited by a reversing checkerboard or grating pattern stimulus presented on a monitor, without a change in overall luminance. Because the stimulus is isoluminant, the PERG primarily reflects the function of the [[retinal ganglion cells]] and their axons, with contributions from the inner retina. The PERG waveform consists of:

- **P50**: A positive component at approximately 50 ms, generated primarily by macular retinal ganglion cells with contributions from outer retinal elements.
- **N95**: A negative component at approximately 95 ms, generated predominantly by retinal ganglion cells.

The PERG is critically important in:

- [[Glaucoma]] (early detection of ganglion cell dysfunction)
- [[Optic neuropathies]] (distinguishing retinal from optic nerve disease)
- [[Multiple sclerosis]] with [[optic neuritis]]
- Differentiating macular disease from optic nerve disease in patients with reduced [[visual acuity]]

### Electroretinography Variants and Extended Protocols

| ERG Variant | Stimulus Type | Primary Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Full-field ERG (ffERG)** | Ganzfeld flash | Generalized retinal disease |
| **Multifocal ERG (mfERG)** | Pseudorandom hexagonal array | Localized macular/retinal dysfunction |
| **Pattern ERG (PERG)** | Reversing checkerboard | [[Retinal ganglion cell]] and macular function |
| **Photopic Negative Response (PhNR)** | Full-field flash (photopic) | Retinal ganglion cell function; [[glaucoma]] |
| **ON-OFF ERG** | Long-duration stimulus (≥150 ms) | Separation of ON and OFF pathway dysfunction |
| **Chromatic ERG (S-cone ERG)** | Blue flash on orange background | [[S-cone]] (short-wavelength) pathway; [[enhanced S-cone syndrome]] |
| **Extended Protocol ERG** | Variable intensity series | Detailed photoreceptor sensitivity profiling |

## Clinical Indications

Electroretinography is indicated in a broad range of clinical scenarios. The following represents the major categories of indications:

### Inherited Retinal Dystrophies

ERG is the gold standard for the functional diagnosis and classification of inherited retinal diseases, including:

- **[[Retinitis pigmentosa]] (RP)**: The most common inherited retinal dystrophy. The scotopic (dark-adapted) ERG is characteristically reduced or extinguished early in the disease, often before significant fundoscopic changes. The photopic ERG becomes progressively reduced as cone function deteriorates. ERG is essential for confirming the diagnosis, classifying the subtype (rod-cone vs. cone-rod dystrophy), and monitoring progression.
- **[[Cone dystrophy]] and [[cone-rod dystrophy]]**: Photopic ERG and 30 Hz flicker are preferentially reduced or absent, with relative preservation of scotopic responses in early stages.
- **[[Congenital stationary night blindness]] (CSNB)**: Characterized by a normal a-wave but absent or severely reduced b-wave (negative ERG or "Schubert-Bornschein" type), reflecting dysfunction at the photoreceptor-to-bipolar cell synapse. Subtypes include complete CSNB (Riggs type with absent rod ERG) and incomplete CSNB.
- **[[Leber congenital amaurosis]] (LCA)**: Severely reduced or non-recordable ERG from birth or early infancy, critical for diagnosis and now for eligibility assessment for [[gene therapy]] (e.g., [[voretigene neparvovec]] for RPE65-associated LCA).
- **[[Stargardt disease]]**: mfERG shows localized macular dysfunction; ffERG may be normal early but can show progressive cone and rod dysfunction in advanced stages.
- **[[Choroideremia]]**: Progressive reduction in scotopic and photopic ERG.
- **[[X-linked retinoschisis]]**: Characteristic electronegative ERG (b-wave amplitude less than a-wave amplitude) due to inner retinal dysfunction.
- **[[Achromatopsia]]**: Absent or severely reduced photopic ERG and 30 Hz flicker with normal scotopic responses.
- **[[Enhanced S-cone syndrome]] / [[Goldmann-Favre syndrome]]**: Characteristic supernormal S-cone ERG response with absent rod-specific ERG.
- **[[Usher syndrome]]**: ERG findings consistent with RP, combined with sensorineural hearing loss.

### Acquired Retinal Conditions

- **[[Diabetic retinopathy]]**: Oscillatory potentials are reduced early in diabetic retinopathy, even before clinically visible retinopathy, reflecting inner retinal ischemia. ERG can be used to predict progression to proliferative disease.
- **[[Central retinal artery occlusion]] (CRAO)**: Reduced b-wave with relatively preserved a-wave (negative ERG pattern), reflecting inner retinal ischemia with preserved photoreceptor function.
- **[[Central retinal vein occlusion]] (CRVO)**: Reduced b-wave amplitude and prolonged implicit times; the b/a ratio and 30 Hz flicker amplitude are prognostic for neovascular complications (ischemic vs. non-ischemic CRVO).
- **[[Autoimmune retinopathy]] (AIR) / [[Cancer-associated retinopathy]] (CAR) / [[Melanoma-associated retinopathy]] (MAR)**: ERG is essential for diagnosis. CAR typically shows reduced a-wave and b-wave (photoreceptor-level dysfunction), while MAR characteristically shows a negative ERG (ON-bipolar cell dysfunction with preserved a-wave).
- **[[Retinal detachment]]**: ERG amplitude reduction proportional to the area of detached retina; useful for assessing retinal function when media opacities preclude fundoscopy.
- **Drug toxicity screening**: Most notably [[hydroxychloroquine]] (Plaquenil) toxicity, where mfERG can detect early parafoveal cone dysfunction before structural changes are visible on [[optical coherence tomography]] (OCT) or [[fundus autofluorescence]] (FAF).
- **[[Siderosis bulbi]]**: Progressive reduction in b-wave amplitude due to iron toxicity to the retina from retained intraocular foreign body.
- **[[Vitamin A deficiency]]**: Reduced scotopic ERG with relatively preserved photopic responses, reflecting rod photoreceptor dysfunction.

### Preoperative and Prognostic Assessment

- Assessment of retinal function in eyes with dense media opacities (e.g., [[cataract]], [[vitreous hemorrhage]], [[corneal opacity]]) where fundoscopy is not possible, to predict visual potential after surgery.
- Monitoring retinal function during and after [[vitreoretinal surgery]].
- Intraoperative ERG monitoring during retinal surgery.

### Pediatric Applications

- Diagnosis of inherited retinal dystrophies in preverbal children (e.g., LCA, CSNB, achromatopsia).
- Evaluation of unexplained [[nystagmus]] or visual inattention in infants.
- Assessment of retinal function in children with [[developmental delay]] and suspected retinal disease.
- ERG under sedation or general anesthesia may be required in young children.

## Technical Methodology

### Patient Preparation

1. **Pupil dilation**: Maximal [[mydriasis]] is achieved using topical [[tropicamide]] (0.5–1%) and/or [[phenylephrine]] (2.5%) to ensure maximal retinal illumination.
2. **Dark adaptation**: For scotopic protocols, a minimum of 20 minutes of complete dark adaptation is required (ISCEV standard). Longer adaptation (30–45 minutes) may be used for research protocols.
3. **Light adaptation**: For photopic protocols, at least 10 minutes of adaptation to a standard background luminance (30 cd/m²) is required.
4. **Topical anesthesia**: A topical anesthetic (e.g., [[proparacaine]] 0.5% or [[tetracaine]] 0.5%) is instilled before placement of corneal contact lens electrodes.

### Electrode Types

| Electrode Type | Description | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Burian-Allen** | Contact lens with built-in speculum and lid electrodes | Stable recordings, built-in reference | May be uncomfortable; requires anesthesia |
| **DTL (Dawson-Trick-Litzkow)** | Fine silver-nylon fiber placed in the lower fornix | Well-tolerated, minimal corneal contact | Lower amplitude signals, potential movement artifact |
| **ERG-Jet** | Disposable contact lens electrode | Sterile, single-use, good signal | Cost per use |
| **Gold foil** | Thin gold foil hooked over lower lid | Comfortable, good for PERG | May shift position |
| **Skin electrodes** | Adhesive electrodes placed on lower lid skin | Non-invasive, well-tolerated in children | Significantly reduced amplitude (50–70% lower); not ISCEV standard for most protocols |
| **HK-loop** | Wire loop electrode in lower fornix | Comfortable, reusable | Requires careful positioning |

The **active electrode** is placed on or near the [[cornea]]. A **reference electrode** is placed at the ipsilateral outer canthus or forehead, and a **ground electrode** is placed on the forehead or earlobe.

### Recording Parameters

- **Amplifier bandwidth**: Typically 0.3–300 Hz for standard ERG; 75–300 Hz for oscillatory potentials.
- **Sampling rate**: ≥1,000 Hz (typically 2,000–4,000 Hz).
- **Signal averaging**: Multiple responses are averaged to improve the signal-to-noise ratio (typically 2–5 averages for scotopic, 10–20 for photopic, and more for mfERG).
- **Impedance**: Electrode impedance should be <5 kΩ.
- **Artifact rejection**: Automated rejection of responses contaminated by blinks or eye movements.

### Stimulus Parameters

The Ganzfeld stimulator provides a uniform, full-field illumination. Flash intensity is measured in candela-seconds per square meter (cd·s/m²). The ISCEV standard specifies precise flash strengths and background luminances for each protocol step.

## Interpretation of ERG Waveforms

### Key Measurement Parameters

For each ERG waveform, the following parameters are measured:

- **a-wave amplitude**: Measured from the pre-stimulus baseline to the trough of the a-wave (in microvolts, μV).
- **a-wave implicit time**: Time from stimulus onset to the a-wave trough (in milliseconds, ms).
- **b-wave amplitude**: Measured from the trough of the a-wave to the peak of the b-wave (μV).
- **b-wave implicit time**: Time from stimulus onset to the b-wave peak (ms).
- **b/a ratio**: The ratio of b-wave amplitude to a-wave amplitude. A ratio <1.0 indicates a "negative ERG."
- **30 Hz flicker amplitude and implicit time**: Measured peak-to-trough and from stimulus to peak.
- **Oscillatory potential amplitudes and implicit times**: Measured individually or as a summed OP index.

### Normal Values

Normal values are laboratory-specific and depend on electrode type, stimulator characteristics, and patient demographics (age, axial length, pupil size). Each laboratory must establish its own normative database. Generally:

| Parameter | Approximate Normal Range (Burian-Allen electrode) |
|---|---|
| Dark-adapted 0.01 b-wave amplitude | 150–350 μV |
| Dark-adapted 3.0 a-wave amplitude | 200–400 μV |
| Dark-adapted 3.0 b-wave amplitude | 350–600 μV |
| Light-adapted 3.0 a-wave amplitude | 20–50 μV |
| Light-adapted 3.0 b-wave amplitude | 100–250 μV |
| 30 Hz flicker amplitude | 50–150 μV |
| 30 Hz flicker implicit time | 26–33 ms |

### Characteristic ERG Patterns in Disease

| ERG Pattern | Description | Associated Conditions |
|---|---|---|
| **Non-recordable (extinguished) ERG** | No detectable a-wave or b-wave | Advanced [[retinitis pigmentosa]], [[Leber congenital amaurosis]], complete retinal detachment, end-stage retinal disease |
| **Reduced scotopic with preserved photopic** | Decreased dark-adapted responses, normal light-adapted responses | Early [[retinitis pigmentosa]], [[vitamin A deficiency]], [[congenital stationary night blindness]] (some forms) |
| **Reduced photopic with preserved scotopic** | Decreased light-adapted responses, normal dark-adapted responses | [[Cone dystrophy]], [[achromatopsia]], early [[cone-rod dystrophy]] |
| **Negative ERG** (b/a ratio <1.0) | b-wave amplitude less than a-wave amplitude | [[X-linked retinoschisis]], [[congenital stationary night blindness]] (complete type), [[central retinal artery occlusion]], [[melanoma-associated retinopathy]], [[juvenile retinoschisis]] |
| **Reduced oscillatory potentials** | Decreased OP amplitudes with relatively preserved a- and b-waves | [[Diabetic retinopathy]], retinal ischemia, [[central retinal vein occlusion]] |
| **Supernormal ERG** | Abnormally large b-wave amplitude | [[Enhanced S-cone syndrome]] (with blue stimulus), early stages of some storage diseases |
| **Delayed implicit times** | Prolonged time to peak with relatively preserved amplitudes | Early [[retinitis pigmentosa]], [[hydroxychloroquine]] toxicity, retinal ischemia |
| **Electronegative with normal a-wave** | Preserved photoreceptor function with inner retinal dysfunction | [[Melanoma-associated retinopathy]], [[CRAO]], [[CSNB]], [[X-linked retinoschisis]] |

## Role in Diagnosis

### Diagnostic Algorithm

ERG is typically performed as part of a comprehensive electrophysiological assessment that may include:

1. **Full-field ERG (ffERG)**: To assess generalized retinal function.
2. **Multifocal ERG (mfERG)**: To map localized retinal dysfunction.
3. **Pattern ERG (PERG)**: To assess macular and retinal ganglion cell function.
4. **[[Electro-oculogram]] (EOG)**: To assess RPE function (e.g., in [[Best vitelliform macular dystrophy]]).
5. **[[Visual evoked potential]] (VEP)**: To assess the entire visual pathway from retina to [[visual cortex]].

The combination of these tests allows precise localization of dysfunction within the visual pathway and differentiation between retinal and post-retinal disease.

### Differential Diagnosis Using ERG

ERG findings must always be interpreted in the context of the full clinical picture, including:

- Patient history and symptoms
- [[Visual acuity]] and [[visual field]] testing
- [[Fundoscopy]] and retinal imaging ([[OCT]], [[fundus autofluorescence]], [[fluorescein angiography]])
- Genetic testing (increasingly important in the era of gene therapy)
- Family history and inheritance pattern

## Relationship to Symptoms

While electroretinography is a diagnostic test rather than a disease, the clinical conditions for which it is indicated present with characteristic symptoms that prompt ERG testing:

| Symptom | Possible Condition | Expected ERG Finding |
|---|---|---|
| [[Nyctalopia]] (night blindness) | [[Retinitis pigmentosa]], [[CSNB]], [[vitamin A deficiency]] | Reduced or absent scotopic ERG |
| Progressive peripheral visual field loss | [[Retinitis pigmentosa]], [[choroideremia]] | Reduced scotopic and photopic ERG |
| Central vision loss | [[Cone dystrophy]], [[Stargardt disease]], macular disease | Reduced photopic ERG; abnormal mfERG |
| [[Photophobia]] and color vision deficiency | [[Achromatopsia]], [[cone dystrophy]] | Absent or reduced photopic ERG; normal scotopic ERG |
| Unexplained vision loss | [[Autoimmune retinopathy]], [[paraneoplastic syndrome]] | Variable; often reduced a-wave and b-wave |
| Visual disturbance in a patient on [[hydroxychloroquine]] | Drug toxicity | Abnormal mfERG (paracentral ring depression) |
| Nystagmus in infancy | [[Leber congenital amaurosis]], [[achromatopsia]], [[CSNB]] | Non-recordable or severely abnormal ERG |

## Management and Treatment Implications

### ERG-Guided Therapeutic Decisions

While ERG itself is a diagnostic modality, its results directly influence management decisions:

1. **Gene therapy eligibility**: ERG is a key inclusion criterion for gene therapy clinical trials and approved treatments. For example, [[voretigene neparvovec]] (Luxturna) for [[RPE65]]-associated retinal dystrophy requires demonstration of viable retinal cells, which ERG can help assess. Residual ERG responses suggest the presence of functional photoreceptors amenable to gene therapy rescue.

2. **Monitoring disease progression**: Serial ERG recordings over time provide objective documentation of disease progression in inherited retinal dystrophies, informing prognosis and counseling.

3. **Drug toxicity monitoring**: The American Academy of Ophthalmology (AAO) guidelines for [[hydroxychloroquine]] screening recommend mfERG as an ancillary test (alongside [[OCT]] and [[visual field]] testing) for detecting early retinal toxicity. Detection of toxicity prompts drug discontinuation or dose modification.

4. **Prognostication in vascular disease**: In [[central retinal vein occlusion]], the ERG b/a ratio and 30 Hz flicker implicit time are strong predictors of ischemic vs. non-ischemic type, guiding decisions regarding [[anti-VEGF]] therapy, [[panretinal photocoagulation]], and follow-up frequency.

5. **Surgical planning**: In eyes with opaque media, ERG helps predict visual potential after [[cataract surgery]], [[vitrectomy]], or [[corneal transplantation]]. A non-recordable ERG in such cases suggests poor visual prognosis.

6. **Nutritional supplementation**: In [[vitamin A deficiency]]-related retinal dysfunction, ERG can document improvement following supplementation with [[vitamin A]] (retinol).

7. **Neuroprotective and pharmacological trials**: ERG serves as a primary or secondary outcome measure in clinical trials of neuroprotective agents (e.g., [[ciliary neurotrophic factor]], [[valproic acid]]), [[retinal prostheses]], and [[stem cell therapies]] for retinal degenerative diseases.

### Emerging Therapeutic Applications

The advent of precision medicine has elevated the importance of ERG in several emerging therapeutic areas:

- **[[Gene therapy]]**: ERG is used to assess baseline retinal function, select candidates, and monitor therapeutic efficacy post-treatment.
- **[[Optogenetics]]**: ERG-like recordings may be used to assess the function of optogenetically transduced retinal cells.
- **[[Retinal prostheses]] (e.g., [[Argus II]])**: ERG confirms the absence of native retinal function, supporting candidacy for prosthetic implantation.
- **[[Stem cell therapy]]**: ERG is a critical outcome measure in trials of retinal progenitor cell and RPE cell transplantation.

## Limitations and Pitfalls

### Technical Limitations

- **Signal variability**: ERG amplitudes can be affected by electrode type, electrode position, pupil size, refractive error, axial length, media clarity, patient cooperation, and ambient light contamination.
- **Age-related changes**: ERG amplitudes decrease with age (approximately 1–2% per year after age 20), necessitating age-matched normative data.
- **High myopia**: Eyes with high [[myopia]] and increased [[axial length]] may have reduced ERG amplitudes due to retinal stretching and lower photoreceptor density, which must be distinguished from pathological reduction.
- **Media opacities**: Dense [[cataract]], [[vitreous hemorrhage]], or [[corneal edema]] can attenuate the stimulus reaching the retina, reducing ERG amplitudes artifactually.
- **Patient cooperation**: Movement artifacts, poor fixation, and excessive blinking can degrade recordings, particularly in children and uncooperative patients.
- **Sedation effects**: General anesthesia and sedation agents (e.g., [[ketamine]], [[propofol]], volatile anesthetics) can alter ERG amplitudes and implicit times, requiring awareness during pediatric testing.

### Interpretive Pitfalls

- A normal full-field ERG does not exclude localized macular disease (mfERG is required).
- A normal ERG does not exclude optic nerve disease (PERG and VEP are needed).
- ERG cannot distinguish between different genetic causes of the same phenotype (genetic testing is required).
- Mild or early disease may produce ERG changes within the normal range, requiring serial testing to detect progression.

## Quality Assurance and Standards

### ISCEV Standards

The **International Society for Clinical Electrophysiology of Vision (ISCEV)** publishes and regularly updates standards for:

- Full-field ERG (most recent update: 2022)
- Multifocal ERG (most recent update: 2021)
- Pattern ERG (most recent update: 2012, with revisions)
- Electro-oculogram (EOG)
- Visual evoked potential (VEP)

Adherence to ISCEV standards is essential for:

- Reproducibility of results within and between laboratories
- Comparability of data across institutions and clinical trials
- Medicolegal defensibility of test results
- Accreditation of electrophysiology laboratories

### Calibration and Maintenance

- Ganzfeld stimulators must be regularly calibrated for luminance output using a photometer.
- Electrodes must be inspected for damage and impedance checked before each session.
- Amplifier calibration and noise floor testing should be performed regularly.
- Environmental light contamination in the testing room must be minimized.

## Advances and Future Directions

### Portable and Handheld ERG

Recent technological advances have led to the development of portable, handheld ERG devices (e.g., **RETeval**, **LKC UTAS**) that use skin electrodes and do not require pupil dilation or corneal contact. These devices are expanding access to ERG testing in primary care, pediatric, and resource-limited settings. While they do not replace full ISCEV-standard ERG for comprehensive diagnosis, they are valuable for screening applications, particularly in [[diabetic retinopathy]] screening programs.

### Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning

AI-based algorithms are being developed to:

- Automate ERG waveform analysis and component identification
- Detect subtle abnormalities that may be missed by human interpretation
- Predict disease progression based on ERG waveform features
- Integrate ERG data with imaging (OCT, FAF) and genetic data for multimodal diagnostic models

### ERG in Gene Therapy Trials

As the number of gene therapy trials for inherited retinal diseases expands, ERG is increasingly used as a primary efficacy endpoint. Standardization of ERG protocols across multicenter trials is a current area of active development.

### Full-Field Stimulus Testing (FST)

**Full-field stimulus testing** is a psychophysical test that measures light sensitivity thresholds using a Ganzfeld stimulus. While not an ERG per se, it is often performed alongside ERG and provides complementary information about residual retinal sensitivity in patients with severely reduced or non-recordable ERG, such as those with advanced RP or LCA being considered for gene therapy.

---

## Summary

Electroretinography is an indispensable diagnostic tool in the armamentarium of the eye care professional. It provides objective, quantitative assessment of retinal function at the cellular level, enabling the diagnosis, classification, monitoring, and prognostication of a vast array of inherited and acquired retinal diseases. With the advent of gene therapy, stem cell therapy, and other precision medicine approaches, the clinical importance of ERG continues to grow. Mastery of ERG interpretation, awareness of its limitations, and adherence to ISCEV standards are essential competencies for optometrists and ophthalmologists involved in the care of patients with retinal disease.
