

# Angle-Closure Glaucoma

**Angle-Closure Glaucoma** (ACG), also referred to as **angle-closure disease** or **closed-angle glaucoma**, is a group of ocular conditions characterized by the apposition or adhesion of the peripheral [[iris]] to the [[trabecular meshwork]], resulting in obstruction of [[aqueous humor]] outflow and, frequently, elevation of [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP). It represents one of the leading causes of irreversible [[blindness]] worldwide and constitutes an ophthalmic emergency in its acute presentation. Unlike [[primary open-angle glaucoma]] (POAG), in which the drainage angle remains anatomically open, angle-closure glaucoma involves a mechanical impediment to aqueous outflow at the level of the [[anterior chamber angle]]. The disease disproportionately affects individuals of East Asian and Southeast Asian descent, women, hyperopes, and older adults. Globally, angle-closure glaucoma accounts for approximately 50% of glaucoma-related blindness despite representing a smaller proportion of total glaucoma cases, underscoring its aggressive nature and the critical importance of early detection and intervention.

---

## Epidemiology

Angle-closure glaucoma affects an estimated 20 million people worldwide, with approximately 3.9 million bilaterally blind from the disease. The prevalence varies significantly by ethnicity and geography:

| **Population** | **Estimated Prevalence (age >40)** |
|---|---|
| East Asian (Chinese, Mongolian) | 1.0–1.5% |
| Southeast Asian | 0.4–1.4% |
| Inuit | 2.0–3.5% |
| South Asian (Indian subcontinent) | 0.5–1.0% |
| European Caucasian | 0.1–0.6% |
| African descent | 0.1–0.5% |

Women are affected two to four times more frequently than men, attributable in part to shallower [[anterior chamber]] depths, shorter [[axial length]], and thicker [[crystalline lens]] dimensions. The incidence increases markedly after the age of 60, correlating with progressive lens thickening and anterior lens migration.

## Classification

Angle-closure disease exists on a clinical spectrum and is classified according to the International Society of Geographical and Epidemiological Ophthalmology (ISGEO) system and the World Glaucoma Association (WGA) consensus:

### 1. Primary Angle-Closure Suspect (PACS)
An eye in which appositional contact between the peripheral iris and the posterior [[trabecular meshwork]] is present or deemed likely on [[gonioscopy]] (≥180° of iridotrabecular contact), but without [[peripheral anterior synechiae]] (PAS), elevated IOP, or [[optic nerve]] damage.

### 2. Primary Angle Closure (PAC)
An eye with an occludable angle (≥180° iridotrabecular contact) and features indicating obstruction of aqueous outflow, such as elevated IOP, PAS, or evidence of prior acute episodes (e.g., [[glaukomflecken]], iris atrophy), but **without** glaucomatous optic neuropathy.

### 3. Primary Angle-Closure Glaucoma (PACG)
An eye meeting the criteria for PAC with the addition of glaucomatous [[optic disc]] damage (increased [[cup-to-disc ratio]], [[neuroretinal rim]] thinning, [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] defects) and/or reproducible [[visual field]] loss consistent with glaucoma.

### 4. Acute Angle-Closure Crisis (AACC)
A symptomatic, sudden-onset elevation of IOP due to complete or near-complete closure of the anterior chamber angle. This is an **ophthalmic emergency** requiring immediate intervention.

### Secondary Angle Closure
Angle closure may also occur secondary to other pathological processes, including:
- **[[Neovascular glaucoma]]** (fibrovascular membrane contraction)
- **[[Uveitis]]** with posterior synechiae and iris bombé
- **Lens-related mechanisms** ([[phacomorphic glaucoma]], lens subluxation)
- **[[Ciliary body]] tumors or cysts** (plateau iris-like configuration)
- **Pharmacological** (topiramate-induced supraciliary effusion, sulfonamides)
- **Post-surgical** (aqueous misdirection / [[malignant glaucoma]], scleral buckle)
- **[[Iridocorneal endothelial syndrome]]** (ICE syndrome)

## Anatomy and Predisposing Factors

Understanding the anatomy of the [[anterior segment]] is essential for comprehending the pathogenesis of angle-closure glaucoma.

### Anatomical Risk Factors

| **Anatomical Feature** | **Relationship to Angle Closure** |
|---|---|
| Shallow [[anterior chamber]] depth (ACD) | ACD < 2.5 mm significantly increases risk |
| Short [[axial length]] | Eyes < 22 mm are predisposed ([[hyperopia]]) |
| Thick, anteriorly positioned [[crystalline lens]] | Increases relative [[pupillary block]] |
| Large [[lens vault]] (LV) | Anterior protrusion of the lens past the scleral spur plane |
| Small [[corneal]] diameter | Crowded anterior segment |
| Plateau [[iris]] configuration | Anteriorly positioned [[ciliary body]] and iris root |
| Narrow [[anterior chamber angle]] | Reduced space for aqueous drainage |
| Increased iris thickness and curvature | Greater propensity for iridotrabecular contact |

The concept of **[[lens vault]]** — the perpendicular distance between the anterior pole of the crystalline lens and the horizontal line connecting the two [[scleral spurs]] — has emerged as one of the strongest single biometric predictors of angle closure, often surpassing ACD alone.

### Demographic and Systemic Risk Factors
- **Age >60 years**: Progressive lens growth and anterior migration
- **Female sex**: Anatomically smaller eyes, shallower chambers
- **Family history** of angle closure
- **East Asian or Inuit ethnicity**
- **[[Hyperopia]]**: Shorter axial length, crowded anterior segment

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of angle-closure glaucoma is multifactorial and involves several distinct mechanisms, often coexisting within the same eye.

### 1. Pupillary Block (Most Common Mechanism)

**[[Pupillary block]]** is the predominant mechanism in the majority (approximately 75–90%) of angle-closure cases. It occurs when the flow of [[aqueous humor]] from the [[posterior chamber]] to the [[anterior chamber]] through the [[pupil]] is impeded by apposition of the posterior iris surface to the anterior lens surface. This resistance creates a pressure gradient, with the posterior chamber pressure exceeding that of the anterior chamber. The resulting forward bowing of the peripheral iris (iris bombé) causes the iris root to appose the trabecular meshwork, obstructing aqueous outflow.

Pupillary block is maximal at mid-dilation (approximately 3.0–4.5 mm pupil diameter), which explains why acute attacks are frequently precipitated by dim illumination, emotional stress, pharmacological [[mydriasis]], or systemic anticholinergic medications.

### 2. Plateau Iris Mechanism

In **[[plateau iris]] configuration**, the [[ciliary body]] is anteriorly rotated and positioned, pushing the peripheral iris root forward against the trabecular meshwork. The central anterior chamber depth may appear relatively normal, but the angle is critically narrow due to the abnormal iris root insertion and ciliary body anatomy. **Plateau iris syndrome** is diagnosed when angle closure persists or recurs after a patent [[laser peripheral iridotomy]] (LPI) has eliminated the pupillary block component.

### 3. Lens-Induced Mechanisms

The [[crystalline lens]] plays a central role in angle-closure pathogenesis:
- **Phacomorphic mechanism**: An intumescent or cataractous lens increases in anteroposterior diameter, pushing the iris forward and narrowing the angle.
- **Anterior lens subluxation**: Zonular weakness (e.g., in [[pseudoexfoliation syndrome]], [[Marfan syndrome]], or trauma) allows the lens to shift anteriorly.

### 4. Retrolenticular Mechanisms

- **Aqueous misdirection ([[malignant glaucoma]])**: Aqueous humor is directed posteriorly into the [[vitreous cavity]], causing anterior displacement of the lens-iris diaphragm.
- **Supraciliary/suprachoroidal effusion**: Fluid accumulation posterior to the ciliary body pushes the lens-iris diaphragm forward (e.g., drug-induced with [[topiramate]] or [[sulfonamides]], or post-surgical).

### 5. Peripheral Iris Crowding (Non-Pupillary Block)

Thick peripheral iris stroma, particularly in eyes with a large iris cross-sectional area, can contribute to angle crowding upon dilation even in the absence of classic pupillary block or plateau iris anatomy. This mechanism has been increasingly recognized in Asian populations.

### Consequences of Angle Closure

Regardless of the mechanism, the final common pathway involves:

1. **Obstruction of the [[trabecular meshwork]]** → reduced aqueous outflow
2. **Elevation of [[intraocular pressure]]** → may be acute (>40–80 mmHg) or chronic (mildly to moderately elevated)
3. **Formation of [[peripheral anterior synechiae]] (PAS)** → permanent adhesion of the iris to the trabecular meshwork and/or [[corneal endothelium]], leading to irreversible angle closure
4. **Trabecular meshwork damage** → even after angle reopening, chronic IOP elevation may persist due to trabecular dysfunction
5. **[[Optic nerve]] damage** → [[retinal ganglion cell]] apoptosis, axonal loss, and progressive [[visual field]] loss characteristic of glaucomatous optic neuropathy
6. **[[Ischemic]] damage** → in acute attacks, extremely high IOP can cause ischemic injury to the iris (sector atrophy), lens epithelium ([[glaukomflecken]]), corneal endothelium, retina, and optic nerve

## Clinical Presentation

### Acute Angle-Closure Crisis (AACC)

Acute angle-closure crisis is a dramatic, symptomatic presentation that constitutes an **ophthalmic emergency**. Patients typically present with:

**Symptoms:**
- Severe, unilateral [[ocular pain]] (may radiate to the ipsilateral brow or temple)
- Sudden onset of blurred or markedly decreased [[visual acuity]]
- Perception of colored halos around lights (due to [[corneal edema]])
- [[Headache]] (often severe, may mimic migraine or intracranial pathology)
- Nausea and vomiting (vagal response to severe pain and elevated IOP)
- Redness of the affected eye

**Signs:**
| **Clinical Sign** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| Elevated IOP | Typically 40–80 mmHg (may exceed 80 mmHg) |
| [[Corneal edema]] | Microcystic epithelial edema, stromal haze, Descemet folds |
| Conjunctival injection | Diffuse or ciliary flush |
| Mid-dilated, fixed pupil | Oval or vertically oval, poorly reactive to light |
| Shallow [[anterior chamber]] | Peripheral and often central shallowing |
| [[Anterior chamber]] cells and flare | Secondary to iris ischemia and blood-aqueous barrier breakdown |
| Iris changes | Sector atrophy, posterior synechiae, sphincter damage |
| [[Glaukomflecken]] | Small, anterior subcapsular lens opacities (pathognomonic of prior acute attack) |
| [[Optic disc]] edema or hyperemia | In acute phase |
| Fellow eye | Typically demonstrates a narrow, occludable angle |

**Precipitating Factors:**
- Dim illumination (movie theaters, nighttime)
- Emotional stress or anxiety
- Pharmacological mydriasis (tropicamide, phenylephrine, cyclopentolate)
- Systemic medications with anticholinergic or adrenergic properties (antihistamines, decongestants, antidepressants, antipsychotics, anti-Parkinson agents, inhaled [[ipratropium]])
- Prone or head-down positioning
- Rapid fluid intake (water-drinking provocative test)

### Chronic Angle-Closure Glaucoma

Chronic angle-closure glaucoma is often **asymptomatic** or minimally symptomatic until advanced stages, similar to POAG. Patients may present with:
- Gradual, painless loss of peripheral [[visual field]]
- Mildly to moderately elevated IOP (or even normal IOP with extensive PAS)
- Progressive [[optic disc]] cupping
- Extensive PAS on gonioscopy
- History of intermittent subacute episodes (mild headache, brow ache, transient blurring, halos) that resolved spontaneously

### Intermittent (Subacute) Angle Closure

Patients experience recurrent, self-limited episodes of angle closure characterized by:
- Intermittent blurred vision
- Mild ocular discomfort or brow ache
- Halos around lights
- Symptoms often occur in the evening and resolve with sleep (pupillary [[miosis]] during sleep breaks the pupillary block)

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

#### 1. Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy
- Assessment of [[anterior chamber]] depth (Van Herick technique: peripheral ACD < 1/4 corneal thickness suggests a narrow angle)
- Evaluation for corneal edema, anterior chamber cells/flare, iris atrophy, posterior synechiae, and lens changes
- Assessment of pupil shape and reactivity

#### 2. Gonioscopy

**[[Gonioscopy]]** is the **gold standard** for evaluating the anterior chamber angle and is indispensable in the diagnosis and classification of angle-closure disease.

**Grading Systems:**

| **Shaffer Grade** | **Angle Width (degrees)** | **Structures Visible** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Grade 4 | 35–45° | [[Ciliary body]] band | Wide open |
| Grade 3 | 25–35° | [[Scleral spur]] | Open, no risk of closure |
| Grade 2 | 20° | [[Trabecular meshwork]] | Narrow, possible closure |
| Grade 1 | 10° | [[Schwalbe's line]] only | Very narrow, high risk |
| Grade 0 (Slit) | 0° | No structures visible | Closed |

**Key gonioscopic findings in angle closure:**
- Iridotrabecular contact (ITC) ≥180° (appositional or synechial)
- [[Peripheral anterior synechiae]] (PAS): permanent adhesions
- Distinction between appositional and synechial closure using indentation (compression/dynamic) gonioscopy — appositional closure opens with indentation; synechial closure does not
- Iris configuration: convex (pupillary block), plateau (flat iris approach with steep peripheral drop-off)
- Pigment distribution patterns

#### 3. Intraocular Pressure Measurement
- **[[Goldmann applanation tonometry]]** (GAT) is the gold standard
- IOP may be markedly elevated (acute), mildly elevated (chronic), or even normal (in early PACS or after spontaneous resolution of an acute episode)
- Diurnal IOP variation should be considered

#### 4. Optic Nerve and Retinal Nerve Fiber Layer Assessment
- **[[Fundoscopy]]**: Stereoscopic evaluation of the [[optic disc]] for cupping, rim thinning, disc hemorrhages, and RNFL defects
- Asymmetry of cup-to-disc ratio between eyes is significant

#### 5. Visual Field Testing
- **[[Standard automated perimetry]]** (SAP), typically [[Humphrey visual field]] 24-2 or 30-2 SITA strategy
- Characteristic patterns include arcuate scotomas, nasal steps, paracentral defects, and generalized depression
- In acute attacks, diffuse depression may be present

### Imaging Modalities

#### 1. Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography (AS-OCT)

**[[Anterior segment OCT]]** provides high-resolution, cross-sectional imaging of the anterior chamber angle and is invaluable for:
- Quantitative measurement of anterior chamber depth (ACD), anterior chamber width (ACW), anterior chamber area (ACA), and lens vault (LV)
- Assessment of angle opening distance (AOD), trabecular-iris space area (TISA), and angle recess area (ARA)
- Identification of iridotrabecular contact
- Differentiation between appositional and synechial closure (with limitations)
- Evaluation of iris configuration and thickness
- Pre- and post-treatment angle assessment (e.g., after LPI or lens extraction)

#### 2. Ultrasound Biomicroscopy (UBM)

**[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]]** (typically 35–50 MHz) provides imaging of structures posterior to the iris that are not accessible by AS-OCT:
- [[Ciliary body]] position and morphology (critical for diagnosing [[plateau iris]])
- Ciliary processes and their relationship to the iris
- Zonular apparatus
- Supraciliary and suprachoroidal space (effusions)
- Lens position and subluxation
- Posterior chamber anatomy

UBM is particularly valuable for:
- Confirming plateau iris mechanism
- Evaluating secondary causes of angle closure (tumors, cysts, effusions)
- Assessing aqueous misdirection

#### 3. Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) of the Posterior Segment

- **[[Spectral-domain OCT]]** or **[[Swept-source OCT]]** for [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL) thickness, [[ganglion cell complex]] (GCC) analysis, and optic nerve head parameters
- Useful for detecting structural damage before functional loss on perimetry
- Monitoring progression over time

#### 4. Optical Biometry / A-Scan Ultrasonography

- Measurement of [[axial length]], ACD, and lens thickness
- Useful for identifying biometric risk factors and surgical planning (IOL calculation for lens extraction)

#### 5. Scheimpflug Imaging (Pentacam)

- Provides anterior segment tomography including ACD, anterior chamber volume, and corneal parameters
- Useful as a screening adjunct but does not replace gonioscopy

## Differential Diagnosis

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| [[Neovascular glaucoma]] | Neovascularization of the iris (NVI) and angle (NVA); history of [[diabetic retinopathy]], [[central retinal vein occlusion]] |
| [[Phacomorphic glaucoma]] | Intumescent cataract, unilateral, lens-induced |
| [[Uveitic glaucoma]] | Significant anterior chamber inflammation, posterior synechiae, keratic precipitates |
| [[Aqueous misdirection]] (malignant glaucoma) | Uniformly shallow anterior chamber (axial and peripheral), typically post-surgical |
| [[Suprachoroidal hemorrhage]] | Post-surgical or traumatic, severe pain, very high IOP |
| Drug-induced angle closure | History of topiramate, sulfonamides; bilateral; myopic shift |
| [[ICE syndrome]] | Unilateral, corneal endothelial abnormalities, corectopia, iris atrophy |

## Management

### Acute Angle-Closure Crisis: Emergency Treatment

The goals of acute management are to: (1) lower IOP rapidly, (2) relieve pain, (3) clear corneal edema, (4) constrict the pupil, and (5) perform definitive treatment (LPI) as soon as feasible.

#### Pharmacological Treatment Protocol

| **Medication** | **Class** | **Route** | **Dose** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Timolol]] 0.5%** | [[Beta-blocker]] | Topical | 1 drop, may repeat ×1 | Decreases aqueous production |
| **[[Apraclonidine]] 1%** or **[[Brimonidine]] 0.2%** | [[Alpha-2 agonist]] | Topical | 1 drop | Decreases aqueous production, increases uveoscleral outflow |
| **[[Pilocarpine]] 1–2%** | [[Miotic]] (parasympathomimetic) | Topical | 1 drop q15min ×2–3, then q4–6h | Constricts pupil, pulls iris away from trabecular meshwork; **Note:** Ineffective if IOP >40–50 mmHg due to iris sphincter ischemia; administer after IOP begins to decrease |
| **[[Dorzolamide]] 2%** or **[[Brinzolamide]] 1%** | Topical [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitor]] | Topical | 1 drop q8h | Decreases aqueous production |
| **[[Acetazolamide]]** | Systemic [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitor]] | Oral (500 mg) or IV (500 mg) | Stat dose | Potent reduction of aqueous production |
| **[[Mannitol]] 20%** | [[Osmotic agent]] | IV | 1–2 g/kg over 45 min | Reduces vitreous volume, lowers IOP; use if medical therapy insufficient |
| **[[Glycerol]] 50%** | Osmotic agent | Oral | 1–1.5 g/kg | Alternative to IV mannitol |
| **[[Prednisolone acetate]] 1%** | [[Corticosteroid]] | Topical | q15min ×4, then q1h | Reduces inflammation from ischemic iris damage |
| **Analgesics/Antiemetics** | Supportive | Oral/IV | As needed | Symptom management |

**Important Clinical Pearls:**
- Pilocarpine is **contraindicated at very high IOP** (>50 mmHg) because the ischemic iris sphincter cannot respond; it should be administered after IOP has been partially lowered by other agents.
- **Pilocarpine 4%** should be **avoided** as it can paradoxically worsen pupillary block by increasing lens-iris contact and can cause ciliary spasm.
- The **fellow eye** should receive prophylactic pilocarpine 1% and be scheduled for prophylactic LPI.
- Supine positioning may worsen pupillary block; consider having the patient sit upright.
- If IOP does not respond within 1–2 hours, consider **anterior chamber paracentesis** as a temporizing measure.

#### Anterior Chamber Paracentesis

If medical therapy fails to adequately lower IOP within 1–2 hours, **anterior chamber paracentesis** can provide immediate IOP reduction. Using a 27–30 gauge needle or a paracentesis blade under topical anesthesia and slit-lamp visualization, a small volume (0.05–0.1 mL) of aqueous humor is withdrawn from the anterior chamber. This is a temporizing measure to facilitate corneal clearing and enable definitive laser treatment.

### Definitive Treatment: Laser Peripheral Iridotomy (LPI)

**[[Laser peripheral iridotomy]]** is the **first-line definitive treatment** for angle closure caused by pupillary block.

**Mechanism:** Creates a full-thickness opening in the peripheral iris, equalizing pressure between the posterior and anterior chambers, thereby eliminating the pupillary block component and allowing the iris to fall posteriorly away from the trabecular meshwork.

**Technique:**
- Typically performed with a **[[Nd:YAG laser]]** (photodisruptive), often preceded by an **[[argon laser]]** (photocoagulative) to thin the iris stroma (sequential technique)
- Preferred location: Superior peripheral iris (10–2 o'clock position), covered by the upper eyelid to minimize dysphotopsia; temporal or nasal locations may be used if superior iris is inaccessible
- An **Abraham iridotomy lens** or **Wise iridotomy lens** is used for contact delivery
- Pre-treatment with **pilocarpine 1–2%** to stretch and thin the peripheral iris
- Pre-treatment with **apraclonidine 1%** to prevent post-laser IOP spike

**Post-LPI Management:**
- Topical corticosteroid (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate]] 1%) QID for 5–7 days
- IOP check at 1 hour post-procedure and at 1 week
- Gonioscopy at 2–4 weeks to assess angle opening
- If the angle remains significantly closed despite a patent LPI, consider **plateau iris mechanism** or other non-pupillary block etiologies

**Complications of LPI:**
- Transient IOP spike (usually within 1 hour)
- Anterior uveitis (mild, self-limited)
- Hyphema (usually minor)
- Dysphotopsia (glare, ghost images, linear streaks) — more common with inferiorly or temporally placed iridotomies
- Closure of the iridotomy (may require retreatment)
- Corneal or lens damage (rare with proper technique)
- Focal lens opacity at the iridotomy site

### Laser Peripheral Iridoplasty (ALPI)

**[[Argon laser peripheral iridoplasty]]** (ALPI) involves placing contraction burns on the peripheral iris stroma to physically pull the iris root away from the trabecular meshwork.

**Indications:**
- Acute angle closure unresponsive to medical therapy (as a bridge to LPI)
- **Plateau iris syndrome** (persistent angle closure after patent LPI)
- Angle closure with a non-pupillary block mechanism

**Technique:**
- Argon laser, 200–500 μm spot size, 200–400 mW power, 0.3–0.5 second duration
- 20–30 burns placed circumferentially on the far peripheral iris
- Abraham lens for contact delivery

### Surgical Management

#### 1. Lens Extraction (Phacoemulsification / Clear Lens Extraction)

**[[Phacoemulsification]]** with [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) implantation has emerged as a highly effective treatment for angle-closure glaucoma and is increasingly considered as a **primary intervention**, particularly in eyes with coexisting [[cataract]].

**Rationale:** Removal of the crystalline lens (the thickest structure in the anterior segment) dramatically deepens the anterior chamber, widens the drainage angle, eliminates pupillary block, and reduces lens vault to zero.

**Evidence:** The landmark **EAGLE study** (Effectiveness of Early Lens Extraction for the Treatment of Primary Angle-Closure Glaucoma) demonstrated that clear lens extraction was superior to LPI in terms of IOP control, quality of life, and cost-effectiveness in patients with PAC (with IOP >30 mmHg) or PACG.

**Indications for lens extraction in angle closure:**
- PACG with coexisting visually significant cataract
- PAC or PACG with IOP inadequately controlled after LPI
- Acute angle-closure crisis (after initial medical stabilization)
- Phacomorphic angle closure
- Large lens vault as the primary mechanism
- Patient preference (combined cataract and glaucoma management)

#### 2. Trabeculectomy

**[[Trabeculectomy]]** (guarded filtration surgery) may be indicated in PACG with:
- IOP uncontrolled despite LPI, medications, and/or lens extraction
- Advanced glaucomatous damage requiring very low target IOP
- Extensive PAS (>270°) with irreversible trabecular damage

Trabeculectomy in angle-closure eyes carries a higher risk of complications (shallow anterior chamber, malignant glaucoma, choroidal effusion) compared to open-angle eyes.

#### 3. Glaucoma Drainage Devices (Tube Shunts)

**[[Glaucoma drainage devices]]** (e.g., [[Ahmed glaucoma valve]], [[Baerveldt implant]]) are considered in:
- Failed trabeculectomy
- Refractory PACG
- Eyes with extensive conjunctival scarring
- Neovascular or uveitic secondary angle closure

#### 4. Goniosynechialysis

**[[Goniosynechialysis]]** involves the surgical stripping of PAS from the trabecular meshwork, typically performed in conjunction with phacoemulsification. It is most effective when PAS are of relatively recent onset (< 6–12 months) and the underlying trabecular meshwork retains function.

#### 5. Endocyclophotocoagulation (ECP) and Cyclophotocoagulation

**[[Endocyclophotocoagulation]]** can be performed concurrently with phacoemulsification to reduce aqueous production by ablating [[ciliary processes]]. **Transscleral [[cyclophotocoagulation]]** (diode laser) is reserved for refractory cases or eyes with poor visual potential.

#### 6. Minimally Invasive Glaucoma Surgery (MIGS)

Certain **[[MIGS]]** procedures may have a role in angle-closure glaucoma, particularly after angle opening has been achieved:
- **[[iStent]]** or **[[iStent inject]]**: Trabecular micro-bypass stents placed during phacoemulsification
- **[[Kahook Dual Blade]]** or **[[goniotomy]]**: Excision of trabecular meshwork
- **[[Hydrus Microstent]]**: Trabecular scaffold

The evidence base for MIGS in angle-closure glaucoma is less robust than in POAG, and careful patient selection is essential.

### Long-Term Medical Therapy

Patients with PACG who have residual IOP elevation after laser and/or surgical intervention may require chronic topical [[antiglaucoma medications]]:

| **Drug Class** | **Examples** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Prostaglandin analogs]] | [[Latanoprost]], [[Travoprost]], [[Bimatoprost]], [[Tafluprost]] | Increase uveoscleral outflow |
| [[Beta-blockers]] | [[Timolol]], [[Betaxolol]] | Decrease aqueous production |
| [[Alpha-2 agonists]] | [[Brimonidine]] | Decrease aqueous production, increase uveoscleral outflow |
| [[Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]] (topical) | [[Dorzolamide]], [[Brinzolamide]] | Decrease aqueous production |
| [[Rho kinase inhibitors]] | [[Netarsudil]] | Increase trabecular outflow, decrease aqueous production |
| Fixed combinations | [[Dorzolamide-timolol]], [[Brimonidine-timolol]], [[Latanoprost-timolol]] | Combination mechanisms |

**Note:** [[Miotics]] (e.g., pilocarpine) are generally **avoided for chronic use** in angle-closure glaucoma due to side effects (induced myopia, brow ache, dim vision, increased risk of [[retinal detachment]], and paradoxical worsening of pupillary block in some cases).

## Management of the Fellow Eye

The fellow eye of a patient presenting with acute angle-closure crisis has a **40–80% risk** of developing an acute attack within 5–10 years if left untreated. **Prophylactic LPI** of the fellow eye is therefore **strongly recommended** and should be performed as soon as practical, ideally during the same visit or within days of the acute presentation.

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis of angle-closure glaucoma depends on the stage at diagnosis, the duration and severity of IOP elevation, and the timeliness of intervention:

- **PACS**: Excellent prognosis with monitoring; only 0.5–5% progress to PAC or PACG over 5 years
- **PAC without optic nerve damage**: Good prognosis with appropriate treatment (LPI ± medications ± lens extraction)
- **PACG (chronic)**: Variable; depends on the extent of optic nerve damage at diagnosis and adequacy of IOP control
- **Acute angle-closure crisis**: Prognosis depends on the duration of the attack and the peak IOP; prompt treatment (within hours) generally preserves vision, but delays can result in permanent optic nerve damage, ischemic retinal injury, and severe visual loss
- **Advanced PACG with extensive PAS**: Guarded prognosis; may require multiple interventions and aggressive IOP lowering

---

## Screening and Prevention

Given the asymptomatic nature of chronic angle closure and the devastating consequences of acute attacks, screening is of paramount importance:

- **Routine gonioscopy** should be performed on all patients over age 40, particularly those with risk factors (hyperopia, shallow anterior chamber, Asian ethnicity, family history)
- **Van Herick assessment** at every slit-lamp examination as a rapid screening tool
- **AS-OCT** may serve as a screening adjunct in high-risk populations
- **Prophylactic LPI** for PACS remains controversial; current evidence suggests observation with regular monitoring is appropriate for most PACS, with LPI reserved for those with additional risk factors or progression to PAC
- **Patient education** regarding symptoms of acute angle closure and the need for emergency care
- **Medication awareness**: Clinicians prescribing anticholinergic, adrenergic, or sulfonamide-class medications should be aware of the risk of precipitating angle closure in predisposed individuals

## Special Considerations

### Angle Closure in Myopic Eyes
While angle closure is classically associated with hyperopia and short axial length, it can occur in myopic eyes, particularly in the setting of plateau iris configuration, large lens vault relative to anterior segment dimensions, or secondary mechanisms.

### Nanophthalmos
**[[Nanophthalmos]]** (axial length < 20 mm) represents an extreme form of anatomical predisposition to angle closure, with very shallow anterior chambers, high lens-to-eye volume ratios, and thickened sclera that impedes uveal outflow. These eyes are at very high risk for angle closure and are challenging to manage surgically due to the risk of uveal effusion.

### Pregnancy
Angle-closure management during pregnancy requires careful medication selection. [[Brimonidine]] is generally avoided near term due to neonatal CNS depression risk. [[Acetazolamide]] is category C. [[Beta-blockers]] may be used with caution. LPI can be safely performed during pregnancy.

---

## Summary

Angle-closure glaucoma is a sight-threatening condition that demands a thorough understanding of anterior segment anatomy, multiple pathogenic mechanisms, and a systematic approach to diagnosis and management. Gonioscopy remains the cornerstone of diagnosis, supplemented by advanced imaging modalities including AS-OCT and UBM. Treatment ranges from emergency pharmacological intervention and laser peripheral iridotomy to lens extraction and filtration surgery, tailored to the specific mechanism and stage of disease. Early detection through routine screening, prompt treatment of acute episodes, and prophylactic management of fellow eyes are essential to preventing irreversible vision loss from this aggressive form of [[glaucoma]].
