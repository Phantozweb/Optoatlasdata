

# Tear Break-Up Time (TBUT)

---

**Tear Break-Up Time (TBUT)** is a critical clinical measurement used in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] to assess the stability of the [[precorneal tear film]]. It is defined as the interval between a complete blink and the first appearance of a dry spot or disruption in the [[tear film]] as observed under [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] with [[sodium fluorescein]] instillation and a [[cobalt blue filter]]. TBUT is one of the most widely employed diagnostic tests in the evaluation of [[dry eye disease]] (DED), formally classified as [[dry eye syndrome]] or [[keratoconjunctivitis sicca]], and serves as a cornerstone metric for characterizing the [[evaporative dry eye]] subtype. A reduced TBUT is indicative of tear film instability, which is now recognized as a central mechanism in the pathophysiology of [[ocular surface disease]].

---

## Historical Background

The concept of tear film break-up was first described by **Norn** in 1969, who introduced the fluorescein break-up time test as a clinical method for evaluating tear film stability. Norn's original work established the foundational methodology that remains largely unchanged in contemporary clinical practice. Prior to this, assessment of the tear film was limited to subjective patient reports and rudimentary observation. The introduction of TBUT represented a paradigm shift, providing clinicians with a semi-quantitative, reproducible measure of tear film integrity.

Over subsequent decades, the test was refined and incorporated into the diagnostic criteria established by major consensus panels, including the **Tear Film and Ocular Surface Society Dry Eye Workshop (TFOS DEWS)** reports of 2007 and the updated **TFOS DEWS II** report of 2017. These landmark publications cemented TBUT as an essential component of the dry eye diagnostic battery alongside [[Schirmer's test]], [[ocular surface staining]], [[tear osmolarity]], and validated symptom questionnaires such as the [[Ocular Surface Disease Index]] (OSDI).

## Anatomy and Physiology of the Tear Film

A thorough understanding of TBUT requires detailed knowledge of the [[tear film]] structure and the physiological mechanisms that maintain its stability.

### Trilaminar Model vs. Gel Model

The classical **trilaminar model** describes the tear film as consisting of three distinct layers:

| Layer | Thickness | Source | Primary Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Lipid layer]]** | ~0.1 µm (outer) | [[Meibomian glands]], [[glands of Zeis]], [[glands of Moll]] | Retards evaporation; provides smooth optical surface |
| **[[Aqueous layer]]** | ~7 µm (middle) | [[Lacrimal gland]] (reflex and basal), [[accessory lacrimal glands]] ([[glands of Krause]], [[glands of Wolfring]]) | Provides nutrients, oxygen, antimicrobial factors; washes debris |
| **[[Mucin layer]]** | ~0.02–0.05 µm (inner) | [[Conjunctival goblet cells]], [[corneal epithelium]], [[conjunctival epithelium]] | Converts hydrophobic corneal surface to hydrophilic; anchors tear film |

The more contemporary **gel model** (proposed by Wolff and refined by others) suggests that the aqueous and mucin layers are not sharply demarcated but rather exist as a **mucin concentration gradient**, with the highest mucin concentration at the [[corneal epithelium]] surface and decreasing concentration toward the lipid layer. Membrane-associated mucins, particularly **[[MUC1]]**, **[[MUC4]]**, and **[[MUC16]]**, form the [[glycocalyx]] on the apical surface of corneal and conjunctival epithelial cells, while secretory mucins such as **[[MUC5AC]]** (produced by [[goblet cells]]) contribute to the gel-like aqueous-mucin phase.

### Tear Film Dynamics

The tear film is continuously maintained through the **blink mechanism**. Each complete blink redistributes the tear film across the [[ocular surface]], re-establishing a uniform layer. The average interblink interval in healthy individuals is approximately **4–6 seconds** under normal conditions, though this varies with activities such as reading or screen use, where blink rate may decrease by up to 60%. The stability of the tear film between blinks is the precise parameter that TBUT measures.

Tear film stability depends on multiple interacting factors:

- **Lipid layer integrity**: A deficient or disorganized lipid layer (as seen in [[meibomian gland dysfunction]]) accelerates evaporation.
- **Aqueous volume**: Insufficient aqueous production (as in [[aqueous-deficient dry eye]]) reduces the bulk of the tear film.
- **Mucin quality and distribution**: Deficient or altered mucins impair wetting of the epithelial surface.
- **Corneal surface regularity**: Epithelial irregularities (e.g., from [[punctate epithelial erosions]]) create focal areas of poor wettability.
- **Blinking completeness**: Incomplete blinks fail to fully resurface the tear film.

## Pathophysiology of Tear Film Instability

Tear film instability, as quantified by a reduced TBUT, is now recognized as the **core mechanism** of dry eye disease according to the **TFOS DEWS II** definition:

> *"Dry eye is a multifactorial disease of the ocular surface characterized by a loss of homeostasis of the tear film, and accompanied by ocular symptoms, in which tear film instability and hyperosmolarity, ocular surface inflammation and damage, and neurosensory abnormalities play etiological roles."*

### Mechanisms Leading to Reduced TBUT

#### 1. Evaporative Mechanisms

The most common cause of reduced TBUT is **excessive evaporation** of the tear film, predominantly due to [[meibomian gland dysfunction]] (MGD). MGD leads to qualitative and/or quantitative deficiency of the [[lipid layer]], which normally retards evaporative loss. Without an adequate lipid layer, the aqueous phase evaporates rapidly, leading to:

- **Localized hyperosmolarity** at the site of tear film thinning
- Activation of [[inflammatory cascades]] involving [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs), particularly **[[MMP-9]]**
- Release of pro-inflammatory cytokines including **[[interleukin-1]]** (IL-1), **[[interleukin-6]]** (IL-6), **[[interleukin-8]]** (IL-8), and **[[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]]** (TNF-α)
- Apoptosis of [[corneal epithelial cells]] and [[conjunctival epithelial cells]]
- Loss of [[goblet cells]], further reducing mucin production and perpetuating instability

This creates the well-described **"vicious circle of dry eye"**, wherein tear film instability begets inflammation, which begets further instability.

#### 2. Aqueous Deficiency

Reduced aqueous tear production, as seen in [[Sjögren syndrome]]-associated and non-Sjögren aqueous-deficient dry eye, decreases the overall volume of the tear film. A thinner aqueous layer breaks up more rapidly, reducing TBUT. Aqueous deficiency may result from:

- Autoimmune destruction of [[lacrimal gland]] tissue ([[Sjögren syndrome]])
- Age-related lacrimal gland atrophy
- Medications with [[anticholinergic]] effects (e.g., [[antihistamines]], [[tricyclic antidepressants]], [[antipsychotics]])
- Post-[[LASIK]] or post-[[photorefractive keratectomy]] (PRK) neurotrophic changes
- [[Radiation therapy]] to the head and neck

#### 3. Mucin Deficiency

Conditions that reduce goblet cell density or alter mucin expression impair the wettability of the ocular surface, leading to rapid tear film break-up. These include:

- [[Stevens-Johnson syndrome]] / [[toxic epidermal necrolysis]]
- [[Ocular cicatricial pemphigoid]]
- Chemical burns
- Chronic [[allergic conjunctivitis]]
- [[Vitamin A deficiency]] ([[xerophthalmia]])
- Chronic use of preserved [[topical ophthalmic medications]] (particularly those containing [[benzalkonium chloride]])

#### 4. Epithelial Surface Irregularity

Any condition that disrupts the smooth, regular surface of the [[corneal epithelium]] creates focal areas where the tear film cannot maintain uniform thickness. These include:

- [[Epithelial basement membrane dystrophy]] (EBMD / [[map-dot-fingerprint dystrophy]])
- [[Recurrent corneal erosion syndrome]]
- [[Superior limbic keratoconjunctivitis]]
- Post-surgical surface irregularity

#### 5. Lid-Related Factors

Abnormalities of the [[eyelids]] that impair the blink mechanism or alter the lid-globe interface contribute to tear film instability:

- [[Lagophthalmos]] (incomplete lid closure)
- [[Floppy eyelid syndrome]]
- Lid malposition ([[ectropion]], [[entropion]])
- Reduced blink rate or incomplete blink (common with prolonged [[visual display terminal]] use)
- [[Conjunctivochalasis]] (redundant conjunctival folds that disrupt tear meniscus)

### The Hyperosmolarity-Inflammation Cascade

When the tear film breaks up prematurely, the exposed areas of the ocular surface experience **localized hyperosmolarity**. Normal tear osmolarity ranges from approximately **296–302 mOsm/L**; in dry eye, values may exceed **316 mOsm/L** or demonstrate increased inter-eye variability. Hyperosmolar stress activates **[[mitogen-activated protein kinase]]** (MAPK) signaling pathways, particularly the **[[c-Jun N-terminal kinase]]** (JNK) and **[[p38 MAPK]]** pathways, in corneal and conjunctival epithelial cells. This leads to:

- Upregulation of **[[MMP-9]]**, which degrades [[tight junction]] proteins and [[basement membrane]] components
- Increased expression of **[[intercellular adhesion molecule-1]]** (ICAM-1), facilitating [[T-cell]] recruitment
- Activation of **[[nuclear factor kappa-B]]** (NF-κB) transcription factor
- Goblet cell apoptosis and reduced **[[MUC5AC]]** secretion
- Dendritic cell maturation and antigen presentation, potentially initiating adaptive immune responses

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Patients with reduced TBUT may present with a constellation of symptoms that overlap significantly with the broader clinical picture of [[dry eye disease]]. However, it is important to note that **symptom severity does not always correlate with TBUT values**, a phenomenon well-documented in the literature and attributed to variations in [[corneal nerve]] sensitivity.

### Common Symptoms Associated with Reduced TBUT

| Symptom | Description |
|---|---|
| **Ocular discomfort** | Burning, stinging, foreign body sensation, grittiness |
| **Visual disturbance** | Fluctuating or blurred vision, especially with sustained visual tasks; improvement with blinking |
| **Photophobia** | Increased sensitivity to light due to irregular tear film optics and corneal nerve stimulation |
| **Tearing (paradoxical)** | Reflex [[lacrimation]] triggered by ocular surface irritation despite underlying tear film instability |
| **Redness** | [[Conjunctival hyperemia]], particularly in the [[interpalpebral zone]] |
| **Lid heaviness / fatigue** | Sensation of tired eyes, particularly toward end of day |
| **Contact lens intolerance** | Reduced comfortable wearing time in [[contact lens]] users |

### Symptom Modifiers

Symptoms are typically **exacerbated** by:

- Low humidity environments (air conditioning, airplane cabins, heated rooms)
- Prolonged screen use ([[computer vision syndrome]])
- Wind exposure
- Smoke or air pollution
- Sustained reading or driving
- Contact lens wear

Symptoms may be **ameliorated** by:

- Blinking consciously
- Use of [[artificial tears]]
- Humidified environments
- Rest from visual tasks

### Short TBUT Dry Eye (sBUT-DE)

A distinct clinical entity, **short TBUT dry eye** (also termed **short break-up time type dry eye** or **tear film instability dry eye**), has been described particularly in the Japanese and Asian ophthalmic literature. This subtype is characterized by:

- TBUT ≤ 5 seconds
- Minimal or absent [[corneal staining]]
- Normal [[Schirmer's test]] values
- Significant subjective symptoms

This entity highlights that tear film instability can be the **primary and sole abnormality** driving symptomatic dry eye, even in the absence of aqueous deficiency or overt ocular surface damage.

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Fluorescein Tear Break-Up Time (FL-TBUT)

#### Standard Technique

The **fluorescein TBUT** (FL-TBUT) is the traditional and most widely used method:

1. **Instillation**: A small amount of **[[sodium fluorescein]]** is instilled into the [[inferior conjunctival fornix]]. This may be achieved using:
   - A **fluorescein-impregnated strip** (e.g., Flu-Glo®) wetted with a single drop of **non-preserved [[saline]]** — this is the preferred method as it minimizes reflex tearing and avoids the preservatives found in fluorescein solution
   - A drop of **2% fluorescein sodium solution** (less preferred due to volume-induced reflex tearing and potential preservative effects)

2. **Distribution**: The patient is instructed to blink several times to distribute the fluorescein evenly across the ocular surface.

3. **Observation**: The patient is then asked to **stare straight ahead without blinking**. The examiner observes the tear film through the [[slit-lamp biomicroscope]] using a **wide, diffuse beam** with the **[[cobalt blue filter]]** engaged. A **Wratten #12 yellow barrier filter** (or equivalent) placed over the observation system enhances fluorescence contrast.

4. **Timing**: A stopwatch or the slit-lamp timer is used to measure the interval from the **last complete blink** to the **first appearance of a dark spot, streak, or hole** in the otherwise uniform fluorescent tear film.

5. **Repetition**: The measurement should be repeated **at least three times**, and the **average** is recorded as the TBUT value.

#### Interpretation

| TBUT Value | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| **> 10 seconds** | Normal tear film stability |
| **5–10 seconds** | Marginal / borderline; may be clinically significant in symptomatic patients |
| **< 5 seconds** | Abnormal; indicative of significant tear film instability |

**Note**: Some authorities use a cutoff of **< 10 seconds** as abnormal, while others, particularly in the context of the **TFOS DEWS II** diagnostic methodology, use **< 10 seconds** as the threshold. The **Asia Dry Eye Society** has proposed **≤ 5 seconds** as the diagnostic cutoff for short TBUT dry eye.

#### Sources of Variability

FL-TBUT is subject to several sources of variability that clinicians must be aware of:

- **Volume of fluorescein instilled**: Larger volumes artificially prolong TBUT by increasing tear volume; smaller, controlled volumes (as with wetted strips) are preferred.
- **Concentration of fluorescein**: Higher concentrations may destabilize the tear film.
- **Preservatives**: [[Benzalkonium chloride]] (BAK) in fluorescein solutions can disrupt the lipid layer and shorten TBUT.
- **Ambient conditions**: Room humidity, temperature, and airflow affect evaporation rates.
- **Patient factors**: Anxiety, forced staring, and incomplete initial blinks affect results.
- **Examiner technique**: Variability in beam width, illumination intensity, and timing precision.
- **Time of day**: TBUT may be lower in the morning due to overnight tear film changes.

### Non-Invasive Tear Break-Up Time (NIBUT)

To overcome the limitations of fluorescein instillation, **non-invasive break-up time (NIBUT)** methods have been developed. These techniques assess tear film stability without instilling any substance, thereby avoiding reflex tearing and fluorescein-induced destabilization.

#### Methods of NIBUT Measurement

| Instrument / Method | Principle |
|---|---|
| **[[Tearscope Plus]]** | Projects a grid pattern onto the tear film; break-up is detected when the reflected grid distorts |
| **[[Keratograph 5M]]** (Oculus) | Infrared ring-based [[topography]] system that detects tear film surface irregularity; provides automated NIBUT measurement |
| **[[DR-1α]]** (Kowa) | Interference pattern analysis of the tear film lipid layer; detects break-up as disruption of interference fringes |
| **[[Medmont E300]]** | Placido disc-based topographer adapted for tear film analysis |
| **Smartphone-based devices** | Emerging technologies using reflected light patterns |

#### NIBUT Normal Values

NIBUT values are generally **longer** than FL-TBUT values because the tear film is not disturbed by fluorescein instillation. Typical normal NIBUT values are:

- **> 10–15 seconds**: Normal
- **< 10 seconds**: Suggestive of tear film instability
- **< 5 seconds**: Clearly abnormal

The **Keratograph 5M** provides two automated measurements:
- **NIKBUT-first**: Time to first break-up (analogous to standard TBUT)
- **NIKBUT-average**: Average break-up time across the entire corneal surface

### Adjunctive Diagnostic Tests

TBUT should not be interpreted in isolation. A comprehensive dry eye evaluation includes:

| Test | Parameter Assessed |
|---|---|
| **[[Schirmer's test]]** (I and II) | Aqueous tear production |
| **[[Tear osmolarity]]** (e.g., TearLab®) | Tear hyperosmolarity (≥ 308 mOsm/L or inter-eye difference ≥ 8 mOsm/L) |
| **[[Ocular surface staining]]** (fluorescein, [[lissamine green]], [[rose bengal]]) | Epithelial damage to [[cornea]] and [[conjunctiva]] |
| **[[Meibography]]** | Meibomian gland morphology (dropout, truncation, tortuosity) |
| **[[Lipid layer interferometry]]** (e.g., LipiView®) | Lipid layer thickness |
| **[[MMP-9 testing]]** (e.g., InflammaDry®) | Ocular surface inflammation |
| **[[Tear meniscus height]]** measurement | Tear volume estimation |
| **[[Phenol red thread test]]** | Tear volume (alternative to Schirmer's) |
| **Symptom questionnaires** ([[OSDI]], [[DEQ-5]], [[SPEED]]) | Subjective symptom severity |

### Diagnostic Algorithm

The **TFOS DEWS II Diagnostic Methodology** report recommends a staged approach:

1. **Screening**: Symptom questionnaire (OSDI or DEQ-5) + risk factor assessment
2. **Triaging**: Rule out mimickers (e.g., [[allergic conjunctivitis]], [[infectious keratitis]])
3. **Diagnostic testing**: Tear osmolarity → TBUT → ocular surface staining → (additional tests as indicated)
4. **Subtype classification**: Evaporative vs. aqueous-deficient vs. mixed (guided by meibography, Schirmer's, lipid layer assessment)

## Management and Treatment

Management of reduced TBUT is directed at **restoring tear film stability** through a multimodal approach that addresses the underlying etiology. Treatment is typically stepwise, escalating from conservative measures to pharmacological and procedural interventions.

### Step 1: Environmental and Behavioral Modifications

- **Humidity optimization**: Use of room humidifiers; avoidance of direct airflow from fans, heaters, and air conditioners
- **Blink exercises**: Conscious, complete blinking exercises, particularly during screen use; the **20-20-20 rule** (every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds)
- **Screen ergonomics**: Lowering monitor position to reduce [[palpebral aperture]] and exposed ocular surface area
- **Protective eyewear**: Moisture chamber glasses or wraparound frames to reduce evaporation
- **Dietary supplementation**: **[[Omega-3 essential fatty acids]]** (EPA and DHA) — evidence is mixed (the DREAM study showed no significant benefit over placebo for moderate-to-severe DED, though some subgroups may benefit); anti-inflammatory properties may theoretically support tear film stability
- **Medication review**: Identify and, where possible, reduce or substitute medications with drying side effects ([[anticholinergics]], [[antihistamines]], [[beta-blockers]], [[diuretics]], [[isotretinoin]])

### Step 2: Tear Supplementation and Lid Hygiene

#### Artificial Tears

[[Artificial tears]] are the first-line pharmacological intervention for tear film instability:

| Type | Examples | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **Low-viscosity drops** | [[Carboxymethylcellulose]] (CMC), [[polyvinyl alcohol]] | Minimal visual blurring; frequent dosing needed |
| **Medium-viscosity drops** | [[Hydroxypropyl methylcellulose]] (HPMC), [[hyaluronic acid]] (HA) | Longer retention; mild transient blurring |
| **High-viscosity gels** | [[Carbomer]] gels (e.g., Viscotears®), [[polyethylene glycol]]/[[propylene glycol]] gels | Extended retention; significant blurring; best for nighttime use |
| **Lipid-containing drops** | Lipid emulsion drops (e.g., Systane Balance®, Retaine MGD®) | Specifically target lipid layer deficiency; stabilize tear film |
| **Ointments** | [[White petrolatum]]/[[mineral oil]] (e.g., Lacri-Lube®) | Maximum retention; significant blurring; nighttime use |

**Preservative-free formulations** are strongly preferred, especially for patients using drops more than **4 times daily**, as preservatives (particularly [[benzalkonium chloride]]) are toxic to the [[corneal epithelium]] and [[conjunctival goblet cells]], paradoxically worsening tear film instability.

**[[Hyaluronic acid]]**-based tears deserve special mention: HA is a naturally occurring [[glycosaminoglycan]] with exceptional water-binding capacity and viscoelastic properties. Studies have demonstrated that HA-based artificial tears significantly improve TBUT compared to other formulations.

#### Lid Hygiene and Warm Compresses

For patients with concurrent [[meibomian gland dysfunction]] (the most common cause of evaporative tear film instability):

- **Warm compresses**: Applied to closed eyelids for **10–15 minutes** at approximately **40–45°C** to melt inspissated meibum. Commercially available heated eye masks (e.g., Bruder mask, MGDRx EyeBag®) provide more consistent and sustained heat delivery than warm washcloths.
- **Lid massage**: Gentle digital expression of meibomian glands following warm compress application.
- **Lid scrubs**: Cleaning of the lid margins with dilute baby shampoo, commercially available lid wipes (e.g., OCuSOFT®), or [[hypochlorous acid]] (HOCl) spray (e.g., Avenova®) to reduce bacterial biofilm and [[Demodex]] burden.

### Step 3: Prescription Pharmacological Therapy

#### Anti-Inflammatory Agents

| Agent | Mechanism | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Cyclosporine A]] 0.05%** (Restasis®) or **0.09%** (Cequa®) | [[Calcineurin inhibitor]]; suppresses T-cell activation; increases goblet cell density; reduces inflammatory cytokines | Onset of action: 3–6 months; initial stinging common |
| **[[Cyclosporine A]] 0.1% cationic emulsion** (Ikervis® / Verkazia®) | Same mechanism; cationic emulsion enhances ocular surface retention | Available in Europe; approved for severe keratitis in DED |
| **[[Lifitegrast]] 5%** (Xiidra®) | [[Lymphocyte function-associated antigen-1]] (LFA-1) antagonist; blocks LFA-1/ICAM-1 interaction; reduces T-cell-mediated inflammation | Faster onset than cyclosporine (some improvement by 2 weeks); dysgeusia is common side effect |
| **[[Loteprednol etabonate]] 0.25%** (Eysuvis®) | [[Corticosteroid]]; potent anti-inflammatory; approved for short-term (2-week) treatment of DED flares | Site-specific corticosteroid with reduced risk of [[intraocular pressure]] elevation |
| **Short-term pulse corticosteroids** | Various (e.g., [[fluorometholone]] 0.1%, [[prednisolone acetate]] 1%) | Used as induction therapy for 2–4 weeks to rapidly suppress inflammation before transitioning to steroid-sparing agents; requires [[IOP]] monitoring |
| **[[Perfluorohexyloctane]]** (Miebo®) | Non-aqueous, semifluorinated alkane; stabilizes lipid layer; reduces evaporation | FDA-approved 2023 for DED signs and symptoms; novel mechanism targeting evaporative component |

#### Secretagogues

- **[[Diquafosol tetrasodium]] 3%** (Diquas®): A [[P2Y2 receptor]] agonist that stimulates aqueous and mucin secretion from the conjunctival epithelium and goblet cells. Widely used in Japan and Asia; has demonstrated significant improvement in TBUT in clinical trials.
- **[[Rebamipide]] 2%** (Mucosta®): A [[mucin secretagogue]] that increases MUC5AC and MUC16 expression, improving tear film stability. Also used primarily in Asia.

### Step 4: Procedural and Device-Based Interventions

| Procedure | Mechanism | Indication |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Punctal plugs]]** (collagen or silicone) | Occlude [[puncta]] to reduce tear drainage and increase tear film volume and residence time | Aqueous-deficient or mixed dry eye; should address inflammation first to avoid retaining inflammatory mediators |
| **[[Punctal cautery]]** | Permanent punctal occlusion | Refractory cases after successful plug trial |
| **[[LipiFlow®]]** (thermal pulsation) | Applies controlled heat (42.5°C) to inner eyelid surface with simultaneous pulsatile pressure to express meibomian glands | Obstructive [[meibomian gland dysfunction]] |
| **[[iLux®]]** | LED-based heat with compression for meibomian gland expression | Obstructive MGD |
| **[[TearCare®]]** | Open-eye, wearable thermal device with manual expression | Obstructive MGD |
| **[[Intense pulsed light]]** (IPL) therapy | Broad-spectrum light (500–1200 nm) applied to periocular skin; reduces inflammation, eradicates [[Demodex]], improves meibomian gland function, reduces abnormal vasculature | MGD-related evaporative dry eye; particularly effective in patients with [[rosacea]]-associated MGD |
| **[[Intranasal tear neurostimulation]]** (TrueTear® / iTear100®) | Electrical or mechanical stimulation of the [[anterior ethmoidal nerve]] to activate the [[nasolacrimal reflex]] and stimulate natural tear production | Aqueous-deficient dry eye |
| **[[Amniotic membrane]]** placement (e.g., Prokera®, BioDOptix®) | Cryopreserved [[amniotic membrane]] placed on ocular surface; provides anti-inflammatory, anti-scarring, and regenerative factors | Severe ocular surface disease with persistent epithelial defects |

### Step 5: Biological Tear Substitutes

For refractory cases:

- **[[Autologous serum tears]]** (typically 20% or 50% concentration): Contain [[epidermal growth factor]] (EGF), [[transforming growth factor-beta]] (TGF-β), [[fibronectin]], [[vitamin A]], and other factors that support epithelial healing and tear film stability.
- **[[Platelet-rich plasma]]** (PRP) tears: Similar to autologous serum but with higher concentrations of growth factors.
- **[[Allogeneic serum tears]]** (e.g., from umbilical cord blood): Alternative when autologous collection is impractical.

### Step 6: Surgical Interventions

Surgical approaches are reserved for the most severe, refractory cases:

- **[[Tarsorrhaphy]]** (partial or complete): Reduces palpebral aperture and exposed ocular surface area, decreasing evaporation.
- **[[Salivary gland transplantation]]** (e.g., submandibular gland autotransplantation): Provides an alternative source of fluid to the ocular surface in cases of total lacrimal gland failure.
- **[[Mucous membrane grafting]]**: For severe cicatrizing disease with goblet cell loss.

## Special Populations and Considerations

### Contact Lens Wearers

[[Contact lens]] wear is a significant risk factor for reduced TBUT. The lens divides the tear film into a **pre-lens tear film** (PLTF) and a **post-lens tear film** (PoLTF), both of which are thinner and less stable than the native tear film. Pre-lens TBUT is typically shorter than TBUT over the bare cornea. Strategies include:

- Fitting with **high water content** or **silicone hydrogel** lenses with surface treatments
- Use of **daily disposable** lenses to minimize deposit accumulation
- Rewetting drops compatible with contact lenses
- Reducing wearing time

### Post-Refractive Surgery

[[LASIK]], [[PRK]], and [[SMILE]] procedures sever [[corneal nerves]], reducing reflex tear secretion and blink rate. TBUT is commonly reduced for **3–12 months** postoperatively and may remain permanently reduced in some patients. Preoperative TBUT assessment is essential for surgical candidacy evaluation.

### Aging

TBUT decreases with age due to cumulative meibomian gland dropout, reduced aqueous production, and hormonal changes (particularly postmenopausal [[estrogen]] and [[androgen]] decline affecting meibomian gland function).

### Systemic Disease Associations

Reduced TBUT is associated with numerous systemic conditions:

- [[Sjögren syndrome]]
- [[Rheumatoid arthritis]]
- [[Systemic lupus erythematosus]]
- [[Rosacea]]
- [[Diabetes mellitus]] (via [[diabetic neuropathy]] affecting corneal nerves)
- [[Thyroid eye disease]]
- [[Graft-versus-host disease]]
- [[Hepatitis C]]
- [[Parkinson disease]] (reduced blink rate)

## Emerging Technologies and Future Directions

- **[[Optical coherence tomography]]** (OCT)-based tear film analysis: Ultra-high-resolution OCT can measure tear film thickness in real time and detect break-up with micron-level precision.
- **[[Artificial intelligence]]** (AI)-assisted TBUT measurement: Machine learning algorithms applied to video recordings of the tear film can provide automated, objective, and highly reproducible TBUT measurements.
- **[[Tear film biomarkers]]**: Proteomic and lipidomic analysis of tears may identify specific molecular signatures associated with tear film instability subtypes.
- **Novel therapeutics**: [[KPI-121]] (loteprednol etabonate nanoparticle), [[NOV03]] (perfluorohexyloctane, now approved as Miebo®), [[CyclASol®]] (cyclosporine A in a semifluorinated alkane vehicle), [[reproxalap]] (RASP inhibitor), and [[tanfanercept]] (TNF-α inhibitor) represent the expanding pharmacological pipeline.
- **[[Gene therapy]]** and **[[regenerative medicine]]** approaches targeting goblet cell regeneration and meibomian gland restoration are in preclinical stages.

---

## Summary

Tear Break-Up Time remains an indispensable clinical measurement in the diagnosis and management of [[dry eye disease]] and [[ocular surface disease]]. Its simplicity, accessibility, and clinical relevance have ensured its enduring place in the optometric and ophthalmic examination. However, clinicians must be aware of its limitations, including variability related to technique, fluorescein volume, and environmental conditions. The advent of non-invasive technologies and AI-assisted analysis promises to enhance the precision and objectivity of tear film stability assessment. A reduced TBUT should always be interpreted within the broader clinical context, incorporating symptom assessment, tear osmolarity, ocular surface staining, meibomian gland evaluation, and systemic health considerations to guide individualized, evidence-based management.
