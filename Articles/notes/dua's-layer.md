

# Dua's Layer (Pre-Descemet's Layer)

---

**Dua's layer**, also referred to as the **pre-Descemet's layer (PDL)**, is a well-defined, acellular, strong layer of the [[cornea]] situated between the posterior [[corneal stroma]] and [[Descemet's membrane]]. First described and characterized in 2013 by Professor Harminder Singh Dua and colleagues at the University of Nottingham, United Kingdom, this discovery fundamentally challenged the long-held classical teaching that the human [[cornea]] consists of only five distinct layers. The identification of Dua's layer effectively proposed a six-layer model of corneal anatomy and has had significant implications for understanding corneal biomechanics, [[corneal surgery]], and the pathophysiology of several posterior corneal disorders.

Dua's layer is approximately **10.15 ± 3.6 micrometers (μm)** in thickness and is composed predominantly of **5 to 8 lamellae** of tightly packed [[type I collagen]] fibers. Despite its thinness, it demonstrates remarkable mechanical strength, being capable of withstanding pressures of up to **1.5 to 2 bars (approximately 1,500–2,000 mmHg)** of air pressure, far exceeding normal [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP). This extraordinary tensile strength has profound clinical relevance, particularly in the context of [[deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]] (DALK), [[Descemet's membrane endothelial keratoplasty]] (DMEK), and the understanding of conditions such as [[acute hydrops]] and [[descemetocele]] formation.

---

## Historical Background and Discovery

The existence of a pre-Descemet's layer had been speculated upon for decades. Historical references to a possible distinct posterior stromal layer date back to the work of **Dua, Faraj, Said, Gray, and Lowe** who, in a landmark study published in **Ophthalmology** (the journal of the [[American Academy of Ophthalmology]]) in June 2013, provided definitive evidence for its existence through a series of meticulous experiments.

The discovery was made using a technique of simulated [[big-bubble]] formation in cadaveric human corneas. During the big-bubble technique—a procedure commonly employed during [[DALK]]—air is injected into the deep [[corneal stroma]] to separate the stroma from [[Descemet's membrane]]. Dua and colleagues observed that two distinct types of bubbles could form:

| Bubble Type | Description | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Type 1 (Big Bubble)** | Large, round, central bubble formed between Dua's layer and the posterior stroma | Separates stroma from Dua's layer; Dua's layer remains attached to Descemet's membrane |
| **Type 2 (Small Bubble)** | Smaller, irregular, more peripheral bubble formed between Dua's layer and [[Descemet's membrane]] | Separates Dua's layer from Descemet's membrane; higher risk of perforation |
| **Mixed Bubble** | Combination of Type 1 and Type 2 occurring simultaneously | Complex surgical scenario requiring careful management |

The observation that these two bubble types formed at different anatomical planes provided the first direct evidence that a distinct layer existed between the deep stroma and Descemet's membrane. Subsequent [[electron microscopy]], [[immunohistochemistry]], and biomechanical testing confirmed the layer's unique structural and functional properties.

Prior to this discovery, the classical model of the cornea described five layers: the [[corneal epithelium]], [[Bowman's layer]] (anterior limiting lamina), the [[corneal stroma]], [[Descemet's membrane]] (posterior limiting lamina), and the [[corneal endothelium]]. The identification of Dua's layer introduced a sixth layer, positioned between the stroma and Descemet's membrane, prompting a re-evaluation of corneal anatomy in ophthalmic textbooks worldwide.

## Anatomy and Ultrastructure

### Gross Anatomy

Dua's layer is located in the **posterior-most region of the cornea**, immediately anterior to [[Descemet's membrane]] and posterior to the deepest lamellae of the [[corneal stroma]]. It extends across the full diameter of the cornea and is continuous with the peripheral corneal architecture, blending into the region of the [[trabecular meshwork]] and [[Schwalbe's line]] at the [[limbus]].

### Dimensions

| Parameter | Measurement |
|---|---|
| **Thickness** | 10.15 ± 3.6 μm (range: ~6–15 μm) |
| **Number of collagen lamellae** | 5–8 lamellae |
| **Collagen fiber diameter** | Similar to posterior stromal collagen (~30 nm) |
| **Burst pressure tolerance** | 1.5–2.0 bars (~1,500–2,000 mmHg) |

### Ultrastructural Composition

Dua's layer is an **acellular structure**, meaning it does not contain [[keratocytes]] or any other cellular elements. It is composed almost exclusively of:

- **[[Type I collagen]] fibers**: These are arranged in tightly packed, highly organized lamellae. The collagen fibers run in multiple directions, contributing to the layer's exceptional tensile strength.
- **[[Type IV collagen]]**: Present in trace amounts, particularly at the interface with [[Descemet's membrane]], suggesting a transitional zone.
- **[[Proteoglycans]]**: Including [[keratan sulfate]] proteoglycans and [[dermatan sulfate]] proteoglycans, which contribute to the interfibrillar matrix and help maintain the regular spacing of collagen fibrils essential for [[corneal transparency]].

Under **[[transmission electron microscopy]] (TEM)**, Dua's layer appears as a distinct zone of compact, well-organized collagen lamellae that are clearly distinguishable from the more loosely arranged posterior stromal lamellae above and the non-banded, basement membrane-like structure of [[Descemet's membrane]] below. The collagen fibrils within Dua's layer demonstrate a **uniform diameter** and **regular interfibrillar spacing**, which is critical for maintaining optical clarity.

Under **[[scanning electron microscopy]] (SEM)**, the layer can be visualized as a smooth, sheet-like structure when separated from adjacent layers during big-bubble dissection, further confirming its identity as a discrete anatomical entity.

### Relationship to Adjacent Structures

| Adjacent Structure | Relationship to Dua's Layer |
|---|---|
| **Posterior [[corneal stroma]]** | Dua's layer lies immediately posterior; separated during Type 1 big-bubble formation |
| **[[Descemet's membrane]]** | Dua's layer lies immediately anterior; separated during Type 2 big-bubble formation |
| **[[Corneal endothelium]]** | Indirectly related; endothelium lines the posterior surface of Descemet's membrane |
| **[[Schwalbe's line]] / [[Trabecular meshwork]]** | Dua's layer extends peripherally and merges with these limbal structures |

## Pathophysiology

The identification of Dua's layer has provided new pathophysiological explanations for several well-known but previously poorly understood corneal conditions. Understanding the biomechanical role of this layer is essential for modern corneal practice.

### Role in Corneal Biomechanics

Dua's layer contributes significantly to the overall structural integrity of the [[cornea]]. Its remarkable ability to withstand pressures far exceeding physiological [[intraocular pressure]] suggests that it serves as a critical load-bearing element in the posterior cornea. The layer's high tensile strength is attributed to the tight packing and multi-directional orientation of its [[type I collagen]] lamellae, which distribute mechanical stress efficiently.

In the context of corneal biomechanics, Dua's layer may function as a **posterior structural scaffold**, helping to maintain corneal curvature and resist deformation under fluctuations in IOP. This has implications for understanding conditions involving corneal ectasia, such as [[keratoconus]], where progressive thinning and weakening of the corneal stroma may eventually compromise Dua's layer.

### Pathophysiological Implications in Specific Conditions

#### 1. Acute Hydrops in [[Keratoconus]]

[[Acute hydrops]] is a well-recognized complication of advanced [[keratoconus]] and other corneal ectasias (e.g., [[pellucid marginal degeneration]]). It is characterized by the sudden onset of corneal edema due to a break in [[Descemet's membrane]], allowing [[aqueous humor]] to flood into the [[corneal stroma]].

The discovery of Dua's layer has refined the understanding of acute hydrops. It is now hypothesized that in many cases of acute hydrops, the initial rupture occurs in **Descemet's membrane alone**, while **Dua's layer may remain intact** for a period, acting as a partial barrier. When Dua's layer subsequently ruptures, the full clinical picture of acute hydrops manifests. This two-stage rupture model explains the variable clinical presentations and degrees of severity observed in acute hydrops:

| Stage | Anatomical Event | Clinical Presentation |
|---|---|---|
| **Pre-hydrops / Early** | Micro-tears in Descemet's membrane; Dua's layer intact | Minimal or localized edema; may be subclinical |
| **Partial hydrops** | Descemet's membrane ruptured; Dua's layer partially intact | Localized stromal edema; limited aqueous influx |
| **Full hydrops** | Both Descemet's membrane and Dua's layer ruptured | Massive stromal edema; significant visual impairment; pain |

#### 2. [[Descemetocele]] Formation

A [[descemetocele]] occurs when progressive corneal thinning (e.g., from infection, ulceration, or [[corneal melting]]) leads to loss of stromal tissue, leaving only [[Descemet's membrane]] (and now, by extension, Dua's layer) as the remaining barrier against perforation. The recognition of Dua's layer explains why descemetoceles can sometimes withstand surprisingly high pressures without perforating—the combined strength of Dua's layer and Descemet's membrane provides a more robust barrier than Descemet's membrane alone would.

Clinically, this means that what was previously thought to be a single-layer barrier (Descemet's membrane) in a descemetocele is actually a **dual-layer barrier** (Dua's layer + Descemet's membrane), which has implications for surgical planning and prognosis.

#### 3. Pre-Descemet's Dystrophies

Several [[corneal dystrophies]] have been described that affect the pre-Descemet's region, and the existence of Dua's layer provides an anatomical substrate for these conditions. **Pre-Descemet's corneal dystrophies** (also known as **deep stromal dystrophies**) include conditions where abnormal deposits accumulate in the region corresponding to Dua's layer. Examples include:

- **[[Pre-Descemet's corneal dystrophy]]** (also called **deep filiform dystrophy** or **Grayson-Wilbrandt dystrophy** variant): Characterized by fine, punctate, or filiform opacities in the pre-Descemet's zone.
- **[[Fleck dystrophy]]**: May involve deposits at or near the level of Dua's layer.

The identification of Dua's layer allows for more precise anatomical localization of these deposits using modern imaging techniques such as [[anterior segment optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT) and [[confocal microscopy]].

#### 4. Corneal Edema and Endothelial Dysfunction

In conditions such as [[Fuchs' endothelial dystrophy]] and [[pseudophakic bullous keratopathy]], dysfunction of the [[corneal endothelium]] leads to failure of the endothelial pump mechanism and subsequent corneal edema. Dua's layer, being acellular and composed of tightly packed collagen, may act as a **partial diffusion barrier** that modulates the rate and pattern of fluid movement from the [[anterior chamber]] into the [[corneal stroma]]. This could explain regional variations in edema distribution observed clinically.

#### 5. Implications for [[Corneal Perforation]]

Understanding the sequential failure of corneal layers during progressive thinning is critical in emergency ophthalmology. The recognition that Dua's layer provides an additional barrier between the stroma and the anterior chamber means that in cases of progressive [[corneal ulceration]] or [[corneal melting]] (e.g., from [[Pseudomonas]] keratitis or autoimmune [[peripheral ulcerative keratitis]]), there may be an additional "safety margin" before frank perforation occurs.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Dua's layer itself, being a normal anatomical structure, does not produce symptoms in its healthy state. However, pathological involvement of Dua's layer—whether through rupture, separation, or disease deposition—can contribute to a range of clinical presentations:

### Symptoms Associated with Dua's Layer Pathology

| Condition Involving Dua's Layer | Symptoms |
|---|---|
| **[[Acute hydrops]]** (rupture of Dua's layer + Descemet's membrane) | Sudden onset of blurred vision, ocular pain, tearing ([[epiphora]]), [[photophobia]], foreign body sensation |
| **[[Descemetocele]]** (Dua's layer as residual barrier) | Progressive visual deterioration, pain (if associated with infection/inflammation), sensation of ocular fragility |
| **Pre-Descemet's dystrophy** (deposits in Dua's layer region) | Usually asymptomatic or mild visual disturbance; may cause subtle [[glare]] or [[halos]] |
| **Iatrogenic Dua's layer detachment** (during [[DALK]] or other surgery) | Postoperative [[double anterior chamber]], corneal edema, visual disturbance |
| **Type 2 big-bubble during [[DALK]]** | Intraoperative complication; may lead to Descemet's membrane detachment, [[aqueous humor]] leak, or need for conversion to [[penetrating keratoplasty]] |

### Signs on Clinical Examination

On [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]], pathology involving Dua's layer may manifest as:

- **Stromal edema** with a clear demarcation at the level of Dua's layer (in cases of partial hydrops)
- **Descemet's folds** or **Descemet's detachment** visible as a membrane floating in the [[anterior chamber]]
- **Pre-Descemet's opacities**: Fine, punctate, or filiform deposits visible in the deep stroma/pre-Descemet's zone
- **Double anterior chamber**: A rare but recognized complication following [[DALK]] where fluid accumulates between Dua's layer and [[Descemet's membrane]]
- **Intact bulging membrane** in descemetocele (representing Dua's layer + Descemet's membrane)

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

The primary tool for initial assessment of Dua's layer pathology is **[[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**. Using a narrow slit beam with high magnification, the clinician can evaluate the deep corneal layers and identify:

- The depth and extent of corneal opacities or deposits
- The presence of Descemet's folds or detachment
- The integrity of the posterior corneal surface
- Signs of acute hydrops or descemetocele

**[[Retroillumination]]** techniques during slit-lamp examination can enhance visualization of pre-Descemet's deposits and subtle posterior corneal abnormalities.

### Advanced Diagnostic Modalities

#### 1. [[Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography]] (AS-OCT)

AS-OCT is the most valuable non-invasive imaging modality for evaluating Dua's layer in vivo. High-resolution AS-OCT systems (e.g., spectral-domain OCT, swept-source OCT) can provide cross-sectional images of the cornea with axial resolutions of **5–7 μm or better**, which approaches the thickness of Dua's layer itself.

AS-OCT can demonstrate:

- **Separation between the posterior stroma and Descemet's membrane** (Type 1 big-bubble plane)
- **Separation between Dua's layer and Descemet's membrane** (Type 2 big-bubble plane)
- **Double anterior chamber** formation after [[DALK]]
- **Descemet's membrane detachment** with or without Dua's layer involvement
- **Residual corneal thickness** in descemetocele, helping to determine whether Dua's layer is intact
- **Pre-Descemet's deposits** in corneal dystrophies

| AS-OCT Finding | Clinical Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Hyper-reflective line posterior to stroma, anterior to Descemet's | Dua's layer visualized as distinct structure |
| Fluid space between stroma and Descemet's | Type 1 big-bubble or stromal-Dua's layer separation |
| Fluid space between Dua's layer and Descemet's | Type 2 big-bubble or Dua's layer-Descemet's separation |
| Thin residual posterior membrane in corneal thinning | Dua's layer + Descemet's membrane intact (descemetocele) |
| Focal disruption of posterior layers | Rupture of Dua's layer ± Descemet's membrane (hydrops) |

#### 2. [[In Vivo Confocal Microscopy]] (IVCM)

[[Confocal microscopy]] (e.g., Heidelberg Retina Tomograph with Rostock Cornea Module, or Nidek ConfoScan) provides **cellular-level resolution** of the cornea in vivo. While Dua's layer is acellular, IVCM can identify:

- The **transition zone** between the posterior keratocyte-containing stroma and the acellular pre-Descemet's zone
- **Abnormal deposits** or inclusions within the pre-Descemet's region
- The **posterior keratocyte density** adjacent to Dua's layer
- **Endothelial cell morphology** on the other side of Descemet's membrane

IVCM is particularly useful in the diagnosis and monitoring of **pre-Descemet's corneal dystrophies**, where it can characterize the morphology, density, and distribution of abnormal deposits.

#### 3. [[Ultrasound Biomicroscopy]] (UBM)

[[UBM]] uses high-frequency ultrasound (typically 35–50 MHz) to image the anterior segment. While its resolution is inferior to AS-OCT for corneal layer differentiation, UBM can be useful in cases where corneal opacification prevents adequate OCT imaging (e.g., dense corneal edema in acute hydrops). UBM can demonstrate:

- Gross separation of posterior corneal layers
- Anterior chamber depth and configuration
- Peripheral corneal and limbal anatomy relevant to Dua's layer's peripheral extent

#### 4. [[Specular Microscopy]]

[[Specular microscopy]] primarily evaluates the [[corneal endothelium]] but can provide indirect information about the posterior corneal layers. In cases of Descemet's membrane pathology adjacent to Dua's layer, specular microscopy may reveal:

- **Endothelial cell density** changes
- **Guttae** or other Descemet's membrane abnormalities
- **Polymegethism** and **pleomorphism** of endothelial cells

#### 5. Histopathological and Laboratory Diagnosis

Definitive identification of Dua's layer requires **ex vivo** analysis, which is typically performed on:

- **Corneal buttons** obtained during [[penetrating keratoplasty]] or [[therapeutic keratoplasty]]
- **Cadaveric corneal tissue** in research settings

Techniques include:

- **[[Light microscopy]]** with [[hematoxylin and eosin]] (H&E) staining
- **[[Transmission electron microscopy]] (TEM)**: Gold standard for ultrastructural characterization
- **[[Scanning electron microscopy]] (SEM)**: For surface topography
- **[[Immunohistochemistry]]**: Using antibodies against [[type I collagen]], [[type IV collagen]], and other extracellular matrix components
- **[[Periodic acid-Schiff]] (PAS) staining**: To differentiate Dua's layer (PAS-negative) from Descemet's membrane (PAS-positive)

## Management and Treatment

Since Dua's layer is a normal anatomical structure, "treatment" pertains to the management of conditions that involve or affect this layer, as well as surgical techniques that exploit knowledge of its existence.

### Surgical Relevance

#### 1. [[Deep Anterior Lamellar Keratoplasty]] (DALK)

The discovery of Dua's layer has had its most profound impact on the technique and understanding of [[DALK]]. In DALK, the goal is to remove the diseased anterior corneal stroma while preserving the patient's own [[Descemet's membrane]] and [[corneal endothelium]], thereby reducing the risk of [[endothelial rejection]].

The **big-bubble technique** (originally described by Anwar and Teichmann in 2002) involves injecting air into the deep stroma to create a separation plane. With the recognition of Dua's layer, surgeons now understand that:

- A **Type 1 big-bubble** separates the stroma from Dua's layer, leaving Dua's layer attached to Descemet's membrane. This is the **preferred surgical plane** as it provides a smooth, strong recipient bed (Dua's layer + Descemet's membrane + endothelium) for the donor graft.
- A **Type 2 big-bubble** separates Dua's layer from Descemet's membrane, exposing bare Descemet's membrane. This plane is more fragile and carries a higher risk of **Descemet's membrane perforation** and conversion to [[penetrating keratoplasty]] (PK).

| DALK Dissection Plane | Recipient Bed Composition | Advantages | Risks |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Type 1 (Stromal-Dua's layer)** | Dua's layer + Descemet's membrane + Endothelium | Stronger bed; lower perforation risk; smoother interface | Slightly thicker residual bed |
| **Type 2 (Dua's layer-Descemet's)** | Descemet's membrane + Endothelium only | Thinnest possible residual bed; potentially better visual outcomes | Higher perforation risk; more fragile bed |
| **Manual deep dissection** | Variable stromal remnant + Dua's layer + Descemet's + Endothelium | Applicable when big-bubble fails | Irregular interface; potential for residual stroma |

**Surgical tip**: When performing DALK, achieving a Type 1 big-bubble is generally considered safer and is recommended, especially for less experienced surgeons. The presence of Dua's layer provides an additional protective barrier against inadvertent perforation into the [[anterior chamber]].

#### 2. [[Descemet's Membrane Endothelial Keratoplasty]] (DMEK) and [[Descemet's Stripping Automated Endothelial Keratoplasty]] (DSAEK)

In [[DMEK]], the surgeon strips the patient's diseased [[Descemet's membrane]] and [[corneal endothelium]] and replaces them with a donor Descemet's membrane-endothelium complex. Knowledge of Dua's layer is relevant because:

- During **Descemet's membrane stripping**, the surgeon must ensure that the stripping plane is between Dua's layer and Descemet's membrane (the Type 2 plane), leaving Dua's layer intact on the recipient cornea.
- An intact Dua's layer on the recipient bed may facilitate **donor graft adhesion** and provide a smoother surface for the transplanted tissue.
- In [[DSAEK]], where a thicker donor lenticule (including posterior stroma, Descemet's membrane, and endothelium) is transplanted, the interface between the donor tissue and the recipient's Dua's layer may influence visual outcomes and interface haze.

#### 3. Management of Intraoperative Complications

If a **Type 2 big-bubble** forms inadvertently during DALK, or if Descemet's membrane is perforated:

- **Small perforations** (< 1–2 mm): May be managed conservatively with air tamponade ([[intracameral air injection]]) and careful suturing of the donor graft. Dua's layer, if intact around the perforation, may help contain the air bubble and support healing.
- **Large perforations**: May necessitate conversion to [[penetrating keratoplasty]].
- **Double anterior chamber**: If fluid accumulates between Dua's layer and Descemet's membrane postoperatively, it may require **air or [[SF6 gas]] injection** into the anterior chamber to tamponade the layers together, or in refractory cases, **surgical intervention**.

### Pharmacological Considerations

There are no pharmacological agents specifically targeting Dua's layer. However, medications used in the management of conditions involving Dua's layer include:

| Medication Class | Examples | Indication Related to Dua's Layer |
|---|---|---|
| **Hypertonic saline** | [[Sodium chloride 5% drops/ointment]] | Reduce corneal edema in acute hydrops or post-DALK edema |
| **Topical corticosteroids** | [[Prednisolone acetate 1%]], [[Dexamethasone 0.1%]] | Control inflammation post-DALK/DMEK; manage acute hydrops |
| **Topical cycloplegics** | [[Atropine 1%]], [[Cyclopentolate 1%]] | Pain relief and stabilization of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]] in acute hydrops |
| **Topical antibiotics** | [[Moxifloxacin 0.5%]], [[Gatifloxacin 0.3%]] | Prophylaxis against secondary infection in descemetocele or post-surgical |
| **Intracameral air/gas** | Air, [[SF6]], [[C3F8]] | Tamponade for Descemet's membrane reattachment; management of double anterior chamber |
| **Topical [[IOP]]-lowering agents** | [[Timolol 0.5%]], [[Brimonidine 0.2%]] | Reduce IOP to minimize stress on compromised Dua's layer/Descemet's membrane |

### Conservative Management of Acute Hydrops

In cases of [[acute hydrops]] where Dua's layer and [[Descemet's membrane]] have ruptured:

1. **Observation**: Acute hydrops is often self-limiting, resolving over **6–10 weeks** as the endothelium migrates to cover the defect and Descemet's membrane edges re-approximate.
2. **Hypertonic saline** (5% NaCl drops and ointment): To draw fluid out of the edematous stroma.
3. **Topical corticosteroids**: To reduce associated inflammation.
4. **Cycloplegics**: For pain management.
5. **Intracameral air or [[SF6 gas]] injection**: In severe or non-resolving cases, injection of air or expansile gas into the anterior chamber can tamponade the Descemet's membrane break and accelerate resolution. This technique has been shown to reduce the duration of hydrops and may result in better final visual acuity.
6. **[[Compressive sutures]]**: Placement of corneal compressive sutures over the area of hydrops to mechanically appose the ruptured edges.

### Emerging Research and Future Directions

- **Bioengineering of Dua's layer**: Research is ongoing into the development of synthetic or bioengineered scaffolds that mimic the properties of Dua's layer for use in corneal reconstruction and [[tissue engineering]].
- **[[Femtosecond laser]]-assisted DALK**: Femtosecond lasers may allow more precise dissection planes, potentially enabling consistent separation at the Type 1 (stromal-Dua's layer) plane.
- **Role in [[corneal cross-linking]] (CXL)**: Understanding how [[riboflavin]]/UVA cross-linking affects Dua's layer may be important for optimizing CXL protocols in [[keratoconus]] management.
- **Pharmacological targeting**: Future drug delivery systems may exploit the barrier properties of Dua's layer for sustained-release intraocular drug delivery.
- **Advanced imaging**: Development of ultra-high-resolution OCT systems (with axial resolution < 3 μm) may allow routine in vivo visualization and measurement of Dua's layer in clinical practice.

## Controversies and Ongoing Debate

The designation of Dua's layer as a truly "new" and "distinct" corneal layer has not been universally accepted without debate. Some corneal anatomists and surgeons have raised the following points:

1. **Is it a distinct layer or a zone?** Critics argue that Dua's layer may represent the deepest, most compact zone of the [[corneal stroma]] rather than a truly separate anatomical layer. The absence of a clear biochemical or cellular boundary (as exists, for example, between the epithelium and Bowman's layer) has been cited as evidence against its classification as a distinct layer.

2. **Nomenclature concerns**: Some authors prefer the term **"pre-Descemet's layer"** or **"posterior stromal lamellae"** rather than eponymously naming it "Dua's layer," arguing that the structure was not entirely unknown before 2013 and that earlier investigators had noted the existence of a compact posterior stromal zone.

3. **Clinical relevance debate**: While the surgical implications (particularly for DALK) are widely acknowledged, some clinicians question whether the distinction between Dua's layer and the deep stroma has meaningful clinical impact beyond the operating room.

4. **Reproducibility**: Some studies have had difficulty consistently reproducing the Type 1/Type 2 big-bubble distinction, suggesting that the separation planes may be more variable than initially described.

Despite these controversies, the majority of the ophthalmic community acknowledges that the work of Dua and colleagues has significantly advanced the understanding of posterior corneal anatomy and has practical implications for corneal surgery.
