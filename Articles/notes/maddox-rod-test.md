# Maddox Rod Test

---

## Overview

The **Maddox Rod Test** is a fundamental, widely employed clinical procedure in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] used to detect, measure, and characterize [[heterophoria]] (latent ocular misalignment) and small-angle [[heterotropia]] (manifest ocular deviation). Named after the British ophthalmologist **Ernest Edmund Maddox (1860–1933)**, who introduced the device in the late 19th century, the test exploits the optical properties of a series of fused, high-powered cylindrical lenses (rods) to dissociate the visual input of one eye from the other. By converting a point source of white light into a perceived streak (line) in one eye while the fellow eye continues to perceive the point source, the Maddox rod effectively disrupts [[binocular fusion]] and reveals the underlying resting position of the eyes—the [[phoria]]. The test is indispensable in the clinical evaluation of patients presenting with [[diplopia]], [[asthenopia]], and suspected [[strabismus]], and it remains a cornerstone of the [[binocular vision]] assessment battery.

The Maddox rod is available as a handheld lens or is incorporated into the [[trial frame]] and [[phoropter]] (refractor head). It is typically fabricated from red-tinted glass or plastic, though clear (white) variants also exist. The red tint serves a dual purpose: it further aids dissociation by introducing a chromatic difference between the two eyes' percepts, and it makes the streak more easily identifiable against a background illumination.

---

## Historical Background

Ernest Edmund Maddox, a prominent figure in British ophthalmology, developed the Maddox rod as part of a broader effort to create practical, office-based instruments for the assessment of ocular motility and binocular alignment. Prior to the Maddox rod, clinicians relied heavily on cover testing and subjective questioning, which, while valuable, did not always provide a precise, quantifiable measure of the direction and magnitude of a phoria under fully dissociated conditions. Maddox's innovation was to use the optical principle of a strong plus cylinder to create a maximally dissociative stimulus that was nonetheless simple enough for routine clinical use. His contributions also include the [[Maddox Wing Test]], a related but distinct instrument for measuring phorias at near fixation, and the [[Maddox double rod test]], a modification used specifically for the detection and measurement of [[cyclophoria]] (torsional misalignment).

The Maddox rod test was first described in Maddox's seminal text, *The Clinical Use of Prisms; and the Decentering of Lenses* (1893), and has since undergone minimal modification, a testament to its elegant simplicity and enduring clinical utility.

## Optical Principles and Instrumentation

### Construction of the Maddox Rod

A Maddox rod is not a single rod but rather a series of **parallel, high-powered convex cylindrical lenses** (typically plano-convex) fused together side by side within a circular disc that fits into a standard trial frame cell or phoropter aperture. Each individual rod functions as a strong plus cylinder (approximately +20.00 to +30.00 diopters). When a point source of light passes through these parallel cylinders, the light is refracted powerfully in the meridian perpendicular to the long axis of the rods, while it is essentially unaffected in the meridian parallel to the rods. The net optical effect is that a point source of light is transformed into a **linear streak** oriented at 90 degrees to the axis of the rods.

| Property | Description |
|---|---|
| **Lens Type** | Multiple parallel plano-convex cylinders (high plus power) |
| **Typical Power** | +20.00 D to +30.00 D per rod element |
| **Material** | Red-tinted glass or plastic (also available in clear/white) |
| **Mounting** | Circular disc for trial frame or phoropter |
| **Optical Effect** | Converts a point light source into a linear streak perpendicular to the rod axis |
| **Dissociation Mechanism** | The streak image is so optically dissimilar to the point source seen by the fellow eye that [[sensory fusion]] is disrupted |

### Mechanism of Dissociation

The principle underlying the Maddox rod test is **total dissociation of binocular fusion**. Under normal binocular viewing conditions, the brain fuses the slightly disparate images from the two eyes into a single percept via [[sensory fusion]] and maintains alignment of the visual axes via [[motor fusion]]. When fusion is disrupted—by occluding one eye, by introducing a sufficiently different image to one eye, or by other means—the eyes are free to assume their **tonic resting position**, revealing any latent misalignment (phoria).

The Maddox rod achieves dissociation by presenting one eye with a stimulus (a streak of light) that is so fundamentally different in form and often in color from the stimulus seen by the fellow eye (a point of light) that the brain cannot fuse the two images. Because the images are unfusable, [[motor fusion]] is suspended, and the eyes drift to their phoric posture. The patient then reports the spatial relationship between the streak and the point light, which directly indicates the direction and approximate magnitude of the deviation.

This method of dissociation is considered **subjective** because it relies on the patient's report of the relative positions of the streak and the point light. It contrasts with **objective** methods such as the [[cover test]] and [[cover-uncover test]], which rely on the examiner's observation of eye movements.

## Clinical Indications

The Maddox rod test is indicated in a wide range of clinical scenarios involving the assessment of ocular alignment and binocular vision:

- **Detection and measurement of [[horizontal phoria]]** ([[esophoria]], [[exophoria]])
- **Detection and measurement of [[vertical phoria]]** ([[hyperphoria]], [[hypophoria]])
- **Assessment of small-angle [[heterotropia]]** where the deviation is too small to be reliably detected by cover testing alone
- **Evaluation of patients with [[diplopia]]** (double vision) to determine the direction and magnitude of misalignment
- **Monitoring of [[strabismus]]** before and after surgical or prismatic intervention
- **Assessment of [[cyclophoria]]** (torsional deviation) when the double Maddox rod test is employed
- **Differentiation between [[comitant]] and [[incomitant strabismus]]** when performed in multiple gaze positions
- **Pre-operative and post-operative evaluation** in [[strabismus surgery]]
- **Assessment of [[decompensating phoria]]** in patients with [[asthenopia]] or intermittent diplopia
- **Evaluation of [[cranial nerve palsy]]** (e.g., [[third nerve palsy]], [[fourth nerve palsy]], [[sixth nerve palsy]]) affecting [[extraocular muscles]]

## Pathophysiology of Conditions Assessed by the Maddox Rod Test

The Maddox rod test does not assess a disease per se; rather, it measures the physiological or pathological state of ocular alignment. Understanding the pathophysiology of the conditions it detects is essential for clinical interpretation.

### Heterophoria

A [[heterophoria]] is a latent deviation of the visual axes that is normally controlled by [[fusional vergence]] mechanisms. When fusion is disrupted (as by the Maddox rod), the eyes assume their tonic resting position, revealing the phoria. Phorias arise from a combination of anatomical, innervational, and mechanical factors:

- **Anatomical factors**: Variations in [[orbital]] geometry, [[extraocular muscle]] insertion sites, and [[globe]] size and position.
- **Innervational factors**: Imbalances in tonic innervation to the [[medial rectus]], [[lateral rectus]], [[superior rectus]], [[inferior rectus]], [[superior oblique]], and [[inferior oblique]] muscles.
- **Optical factors**: Uncorrected or undercorrected [[refractive error]], particularly [[anisometropia]] and high [[hyperopia]] (which drives [[accommodative convergence]] and may produce [[esophoria]]).
- **Mechanical factors**: Restrictive or paretic conditions affecting the extraocular muscles or their surrounding tissues (e.g., [[thyroid eye disease]], [[orbital fracture]]).

Small phorias are nearly universal in the general population and are typically well-compensated by fusional vergence reserves. When the phoria exceeds the compensatory capacity of the fusional vergence system, the patient may experience symptoms of [[asthenopia]], intermittent [[diplopia]], or the phoria may decompensate into a manifest [[tropia]].

### Heterotropia

A [[heterotropia]] (strabismus) is a manifest deviation of the visual axes that is present even under binocular viewing conditions. The Maddox rod test can detect and quantify small-angle tropias, particularly when combined with prisms (see below). The pathophysiology of strabismus is multifactorial and may involve:

- **Congenital or developmental** anomalies of the [[extraocular muscles]] or their innervation
- **Acquired** paresis or paralysis of [[cranial nerves]] III ([[oculomotor nerve]]), IV ([[trochlear nerve]]), or VI ([[abducens nerve]])
- **Mechanical restriction** (e.g., [[thyroid orbitopathy]], [[orbital floor fracture]])
- **Decompensation** of a pre-existing phoria
- **Central neurological** lesions affecting the [[supranuclear]] or [[internuclear]] pathways of ocular motility (e.g., [[internuclear ophthalmoplegia]])

### Cyclophoria

[[Cyclophoria]] (torsional phoria) refers to a latent rotational misalignment of the eyes around the [[anteroposterior axis]]. It is most commonly associated with [[superior oblique]] dysfunction (e.g., [[fourth nerve palsy]]). The **double Maddox rod test** is specifically designed to detect and measure cyclophoria (see below).

## Symptoms Prompting the Use of the Maddox Rod Test

Patients who are candidates for Maddox rod testing typically present with one or more of the following symptoms:

| Symptom | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|
| **[[Diplopia]]** (binocular) | Suggests manifest or intermittent ocular misalignment |
| **[[Asthenopia]]** (eye strain) | May indicate a decompensating phoria |
| **Headache** (especially frontal or periorbital) | Often associated with excessive fusional vergence effort |
| **Blurred vision** (intermittent) | May result from accommodative-vergence imbalance |
| **Difficulty with reading or near tasks** | Suggests near phoria or convergence insufficiency |
| **Sensation of "pulling" or "tugging" of the eyes** | Subjective awareness of fusional vergence effort |
| **Tilting or turning of the head** | Compensatory posture for vertical or torsional deviation (e.g., [[superior oblique palsy]]) |
| **Closing or covering one eye** | Voluntary suppression to eliminate diplopia |

## Diagnostic Procedure: Step-by-Step Clinical Technique

### Equipment Required

- **Maddox rod** (red-tinted, mounted in a trial frame cell or phoropter disc)
- **Point source of light** (a small, bright, isolated light such as a penlight, muscle light, or the fixation light on a [[Maddox rod and tangent scale]] setup)
- **[[Trial frame]]** or **[[phoropter]]**
- **[[Prism bar]]** or **loose prisms** (for quantification)
- **[[Tangent scale]]** (optional, for direct measurement in prism diopters)

### Patient Positioning

The patient is seated comfortably at the appropriate testing distance:
- **Distance (6 meters / 20 feet)**: For assessment of distance phoria
- **Near (40 cm / 16 inches)**: For assessment of near phoria

The room illumination is reduced to make the point light source clearly visible.

### Measurement of Horizontal Phoria

1. **Place the Maddox rod before one eye** (conventionally the right eye) in the trial frame or phoropter, with the **rods oriented horizontally** (axis at 180°). This produces a **vertical streak** in the eye behind the Maddox rod.
2. The fellow (left) eye views the point light source directly.
3. Instruct the patient to look at the point light and report the position of the red vertical streak relative to the white point light.

**Interpretation:**

| Patient's Report | Interpretation | Type of Phoria |
|---|---|---|
| The red streak passes **through** the white light | No horizontal phoria | **[[Orthophoria]]** |
| The red streak is to the **right** of the white light (uncrossed diplopia) | The right eye (behind the rod) is deviated **inward** | **[[Esophoria]]** |
| The red streak is to the **left** of the white light (crossed diplopia) | The right eye (behind the rod) is deviated **outward** | **[[Exophoria]]** |

> **Mnemonic**: With the Maddox rod before the right eye and rods horizontal, the streak is vertical. The streak appears on the **same side as the deviation** when considering the direction of the eye behind the rod. In esophoria, the eye turns in (nasally), so the image falls temporally—the streak appears on the opposite side of the light from the direction of the eye turn (uncrossed diplopia).

4. **Quantification**: Introduce prisms (base-out for esophoria, base-in for exophoria) before either eye using a [[prism bar]] or rotary prism until the patient reports that the red streak passes directly through the white light. The prism power required to achieve this alignment equals the magnitude of the phoria in **[[prism diopters]] (Δ)**.

### Measurement of Vertical Phoria

1. **Rotate the Maddox rod** so that the rods are oriented **vertically** (axis at 90°). This produces a **horizontal streak**.
2. The fellow eye views the point light source.
3. Instruct the patient to report the position of the red horizontal streak relative to the white point light.

**Interpretation:**

| Patient's Report | Interpretation | Type of Phoria |
|---|---|---|
| The red streak passes **through** the white light | No vertical phoria | **[[Orthophoria]]** |
| The red streak is **above** the white light | The eye behind the rod is deviated **downward** ([[hypophoria]] of that eye) | **[[Hyperphoria]]** of the fellow eye (or [[hypophoria]] of the tested eye) |
| The red streak is **below** the white light | The eye behind the rod is deviated **upward** ([[hyperphoria]] of that eye) | **[[Hyperphoria]]** of the tested eye |

> **Key Principle**: The streak is displaced in the **opposite direction** to the deviation of the eye behind the Maddox rod. If the right eye (behind the rod) is higher (right hyperphoria), the image falls lower on the retina, and the streak is perceived as being **below** the light.

4. **Quantification**: Introduce vertical prisms (base-down before the hyperphoric eye or base-up before the hypophoric eye) until alignment is reported. The prism power equals the vertical phoria magnitude.

### Measurement of Cyclophoria (Double Maddox Rod Test)

The **double Maddox rod test** is a modification used to detect and measure [[cyclophoria]] (torsional misalignment):

1. Place a **red Maddox rod** before one eye and a **white (clear) Maddox rod** before the other eye, both with rods oriented **vertically** (axis at 90°), producing horizontal streaks in both eyes.
2. The patient views a point light source.
3. Under normal conditions (no cyclophoria), the two horizontal streaks (one red, one white) should appear **parallel and superimposed**.
4. If cyclophoria is present, the patient will report that the two streaks are **tilted** relative to each other (i.e., not parallel).
5. The patient is asked to rotate one of the Maddox rods (using the trial frame's axis scale) until the two streaks appear parallel. The number of degrees of rotation required equals the magnitude of the cyclophoria.

**Interpretation:**

| Finding | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Streaks parallel, superimposed | No cyclophoria |
| Top of the streak tilted **nasally** (incyclotorsion of that eye) | **[[Incyclophoria]]** of the eye behind that rod |
| Top of the streak tilted **temporally** (excyclotorsion of that eye) | **[[Excyclophoria]]** of the eye behind that rod |

This test is particularly valuable in the diagnosis of [[superior oblique palsy]] ([[fourth nerve palsy]]), which characteristically produces **excyclotorsion** of the affected eye.

## Interpretation and Clinical Significance

### Normal Values

Small phorias are physiologically normal and well-compensated in most individuals. The following are generally accepted normative ranges:

| Type of Phoria | Distance (6 m) | Near (40 cm) |
|---|---|---|
| **Horizontal** | 1Δ [[esophoria]] (± 1Δ) | 3Δ [[exophoria]] (± 3Δ) |
| **Vertical** | 0Δ (± 0.5Δ) | 0Δ (± 0.5Δ) |
| **Cyclophoria** | 0° (± 0.5°) | 0° (± 0.5°) |

> **Note**: These values are approximate and may vary by source. The clinical significance of a phoria depends not only on its magnitude but also on the patient's [[fusional vergence reserves]], symptoms, and visual demands. [[Sheard's criterion]] and [[Percival's criterion]] are commonly used to assess whether a phoria is likely to be symptomatic.

### Relationship to Fusional Vergence Reserves

A phoria becomes clinically significant when it exceeds the patient's ability to compensate through fusional vergence. According to **[[Sheard's criterion]]**, the compensating fusional vergence reserve should be at least **twice** the magnitude of the phoria for comfortable binocular vision. For example, a patient with 6Δ of exophoria at near should have at least 12Δ of [[positive fusional vergence]] (base-out) at near to maintain comfortable fusion.

### Differentiating Phoria from Tropia

The Maddox rod test, as a dissociation test, measures the **total deviation** (phoria + tropia combined) when a tropia is present. It does not inherently distinguish between a phoria and a tropia. For this reason, the Maddox rod test should always be interpreted in conjunction with the [[cover test]] (which differentiates phoria from tropia) and other clinical findings.

## Comparison with Other Diagnostic Tests

| Test | Type | Measures | Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Maddox Rod Test** | Subjective, dissociation | Horizontal, vertical, torsional phoria/tropia | Quick, quantifiable with prisms, minimal equipment | Subjective (requires patient cooperation), does not differentiate phoria from tropia |
| **[[Cover Test]]** / **[[Cover-Uncover Test]]** | Objective | Tropia and phoria (direction) | Objective, differentiates phoria from tropia | Requires examiner skill, less precise for small deviations |
| **[[Alternate Prism Cover Test]]** | Objective | Total deviation (phoria + tropia) | Gold standard for quantification, objective | Requires patient fixation, examiner skill |
| **[[Maddox Wing Test]]** | Subjective, dissociation | Near phoria (horizontal and vertical) | Designed for near, easy to use | Near only, subjective |
| **[[Von Graefe Technique]]** | Subjective, dissociation (via prism) | Horizontal and vertical phoria | Performed in phoropter, quantifiable | Instrument-induced artifacts, subjective |
| **[[Hirschberg Test]]** | Objective | Gross estimation of tropia | Very quick, no patient cooperation needed | Imprecise (estimates only), does not detect phoria |
| **[[Synoptophore]]** (Major Amblyoscope) | Subjective/Objective | Deviation, fusion, stereopsis | Comprehensive, measures multiple parameters | Expensive, time-consuming, instrument-specific |

## Special Considerations and Clinical Pearls

### Use in Pediatric Populations

The Maddox rod test requires the patient to provide a reliable subjective response regarding the relative positions of the streak and the point light. This limits its utility in **very young children** and patients with **cognitive or communication impairments**. In pediatric populations, objective tests such as the [[cover test]], [[Hirschberg test]], and [[Krimsky test]] are generally preferred. However, cooperative children (typically age 7 and older) can often perform the Maddox rod test reliably.

### Use in Incomitant Deviations

When an [[incomitant strabismus]] (a deviation that varies with gaze direction) is suspected—as in [[cranial nerve palsies]] or [[restrictive strabismus]]—the Maddox rod test can be performed in **multiple gaze positions** (the nine cardinal positions of gaze) to map the pattern of deviation. This is particularly useful in identifying the **paretic muscle** in cases of isolated [[extraocular muscle]] palsy. The results can be recorded on a **[[Hess chart]]** or **[[Lancaster red-green test]]** grid for documentation and comparison over time.

### Torsional Assessment in Fourth Nerve Palsy

The **double Maddox rod test** is considered one of the most clinically accessible methods for detecting and quantifying [[cyclotorsion]]. In [[fourth nerve palsy]] (the most common isolated [[cranial nerve palsy]] affecting ocular motility), the affected eye demonstrates **excyclotorsion** due to unopposed action of the [[inferior oblique muscle]] and loss of the intorting action of the [[superior oblique muscle]]. The double Maddox rod test can detect excyclotorsion as small as 1–2 degrees, making it a sensitive tool for this diagnosis.

### Limitations and Sources of Error

- **Subjectivity**: The test relies entirely on the patient's report, which may be unreliable in patients with poor attention, cognitive impairment, or malingering.
- **Proximal convergence**: When performed at near in a phoropter, the awareness of instrument proximity may induce [[proximal convergence]], artificially increasing esophoria or decreasing exophoria.
- **Accommodative state**: The patient's [[accommodative]] effort during testing can influence the measured phoria through the [[accommodative convergence/accommodation (AC/A) ratio]].
- **Suppression**: Patients with deep [[suppression]] (as in long-standing strabismus with [[amblyopia]]) may not perceive the streak or the point light, rendering the test uninterpretable.
- **Head tilt**: Inadvertent head tilt can introduce artifactual cyclophoria measurements in the double Maddox rod test.

## Management of Conditions Identified by the Maddox Rod Test

The Maddox rod test is a diagnostic tool; management depends on the underlying condition identified.

### Non-Pharmacological Management

| Intervention | Indication | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Prismatic correction]]** (incorporated into spectacles) | Symptomatic phoria or small-angle tropia | Optically realigns images to reduce fusional demand |
| **[[Vision therapy]] / [[Orthoptic exercises]]** | Decompensating phoria, [[convergence insufficiency]], inadequate fusional vergence reserves | Strengthens fusional vergence amplitude and facility |
| **Correction of [[refractive error]]** | Phoria secondary to uncorrected refractive error (e.g., accommodative esophoria from uncorrected hyperopia) | Reduces accommodative demand and associated convergence |
| **Occlusion (patching)** | Intractable diplopia not amenable to prism or surgery | Eliminates diplopia by removing one eye's input |

### Pharmacological Interventions

Pharmacological treatment is rarely directed at the phoria itself but may be relevant in specific etiologies:

| Agent | Indication | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Botulinum toxin]] (Botox) injection** into extraocular muscles | Acute or chronic strabismus, particularly [[sixth nerve palsy]] with contracture risk | Chemodenervation of the antagonist muscle to prevent contracture and allow recovery |
| **[[Phospholine iodide]] (echothiophate)** or **[[pilocarpine]]** | Accommodative esophoria (rarely used today) | Enhances accommodative response, reducing convergence demand |
| **Systemic corticosteroids** | [[Thyroid eye disease]] with restrictive strabismus | Reduces orbital inflammation and muscle swelling |

### Surgical Approaches

When conservative measures fail or the deviation is large and symptomatic, [[strabismus surgery]] may be indicated:

- **[[Recession]]**: Weakening procedure; the muscle insertion is moved posteriorly on the globe to reduce its effective pull.
- **[[Resection]]**: Strengthening procedure; a segment of the muscle is removed and the shortened muscle is reattached, increasing its effective pull.
- **[[Adjustable suture technique]]**: Allows post-operative fine-tuning of alignment under topical anesthesia.
- **[[Superior oblique tuck]]** or **[[inferior oblique weakening]]**: Specifically for vertical and torsional deviations, particularly in [[fourth nerve palsy]].
- **[[Harada-Ito procedure]]**: A specialized surgery for symptomatic excyclotorsion, involving anteriorization of the anterior fibers of the [[superior oblique]] tendon.

The Maddox rod test is frequently used in the **pre-operative** assessment to quantify the deviation and in the **post-operative** period to evaluate surgical outcomes and residual deviation.

## Documentation and Recording

The results of the Maddox rod test should be clearly documented in the clinical record. A standard notation format includes:

- **Eye tested** (which eye has the Maddox rod)
- **Testing distance** (distance or near)
- **Direction of deviation** (eso, exo, hyper, hypo, incyclo, excyclo)
- **Magnitude** (in prism diopters for horizontal/vertical; in degrees for torsional)

**Example documentation:**

> *Maddox rod (red) OD, distance: 4Δ esophoria. Near: 8Δ exophoria. Vertical: 1Δ R hyperphoria at distance. Double Maddox rod: 3° excyclotorsion OS.*

---

## Summary

The **Maddox Rod Test** remains one of the most elegant, efficient, and clinically valuable tools in the assessment of [[binocular vision]] and [[ocular alignment]]. Its ability to fully dissociate the eyes and provide a subjective, quantifiable measure of horizontal, vertical, and (with the double rod modification) torsional deviations makes it indispensable in the diagnostic armamentarium of every [[optometrist]] and [[ophthalmologist]]. When interpreted in the context of the full clinical examination—including [[cover testing]], [[fusional vergence]] assessment, and [[refraction]]—the Maddox rod test provides critical information that guides the management of patients with [[heterophoria]], [[heterotropia]], [[diplopia]], and [[asthenopia]].
