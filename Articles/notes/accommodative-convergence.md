

# Accommodative Convergence

**Accommodative convergence** is the reflexive, synkinetic convergence of the [[visual axes]] that occurs as a direct consequence of the activation of the [[accommodation]] reflex. It is one of the four principal components of total convergence described in [[Maddox's classification]] and represents a fundamental neurophysiological linkage between the [[accommodative system]] and the [[vergence system]]. The quantitative relationship between accommodative convergence and accommodation is expressed as the **AC/A ratio** (accommodative convergence to accommodation ratio), one of the most clinically significant measurements in [[binocular vision]] assessment. Abnormalities in accommodative convergence—whether excessive or deficient relative to the accommodative demand—are implicated in a wide spectrum of [[binocular vision disorders]], including [[convergence excess]], [[convergence insufficiency]], [[accommodative esotropia]], and various forms of [[asthenopia]]. A thorough understanding of accommodative convergence is indispensable for the practicing [[optometrist]] and [[ophthalmologist]] in the diagnosis and management of non-strabismic and strabismic binocular vision anomalies.

---

## Historical Background

The concept of a neurological linkage between accommodation and convergence was first systematically described by **[[Franciscus Cornelis Donders]]** in the mid-19th century. Donders recognized that the effort to focus on a near object was invariably accompanied by an inward rotation of the eyes. **[[Ernest Edmund Maddox]]** later formalized the components of convergence into four categories: **tonic convergence**, **proximal convergence**, **fusional (disparity) convergence**, and **accommodative convergence**. This classification remains the cornerstone of clinical vergence analysis. The quantification of the AC/A ratio was subsequently refined by **[[Morgan]]**, **[[Alpern]]**, and others throughout the 20th century, establishing normative data and clinical methodologies that are still in widespread use today.

## Anatomy and Neurophysiology

### The Near Triad

Accommodative convergence is one element of the **[[near triad]]** (also called the near response or near synkinesis), which comprises:

1. **[[Accommodation]]** – Increased [[crystalline lens]] convexity via contraction of the [[ciliary muscle]]
2. **[[Convergence]]** – Adduction of both eyes mediated primarily by the [[medial rectus muscles]]
3. **[[Pupillary miosis]]** – Constriction of the [[pupil]] via the [[sphincter pupillae muscle]]

These three responses are neurologically coupled but can be partially dissociated under certain clinical and experimental conditions.

### Neural Substrate

The neural control of accommodative convergence involves a complex network of cortical and subcortical structures:

- **[[Visual cortex]] (Area V1, V2):** Processes retinal blur signals that initiate the accommodative response.
- **[[Frontal eye fields]] (FEF):** Contribute to voluntary and reflexive vergence commands.
- **[[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]]:** Provides parasympathetic innervation to the [[ciliary muscle]] and [[sphincter pupillae]] via the [[oculomotor nerve]] (CN III) and the [[ciliary ganglion]].
- **[[Supraoculomotor area]] and [[mesencephalic reticular formation]]:** Serve as critical integration centers where accommodative signals are cross-linked to vergence motor neurons.
- **[[Oculomotor nucleus]]:** Contains motor neurons innervating the [[medial rectus muscle]], which executes the convergence movement.

The cross-link between accommodation and convergence is believed to occur at the level of the **[[supraoculomotor area]]** in the [[midbrain]], where premotor neurons for accommodation project collaterals to vergence premotor neurons. This anatomical arrangement provides the structural basis for the synkinetic relationship. Importantly, the coupling is **bidirectional**: just as accommodation drives convergence (accommodative convergence), convergence can drive accommodation (**[[convergence accommodation]]**, quantified by the **CA/C ratio**).

### Maddox's Components of Convergence

To contextualize accommodative convergence within the broader vergence system:

| Component | Stimulus | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Tonic convergence** | Baseline neural tone | Resting vergence position in the absence of visual stimuli; typically ~1–2 [[prism diopters]] of convergence |
| **Proximal convergence** | Awareness of nearness | Psychic or cognitive awareness that a target is near |
| **Accommodative convergence** | [[Retinal blur]] → accommodation | Convergence driven reflexively by the accommodative effort |
| **Fusional (disparity) convergence** | [[Retinal disparity]] | Motor fusion response to eliminate [[diplopia]]; fine-tunes alignment |

Total convergence at any given moment is the algebraic sum of these four components. Accommodative convergence typically constitutes the **largest single component** of the total convergence response at near, making it the dominant driver of binocular alignment during near-vision tasks.

## Pathophysiology

### The AC/A Ratio: Definition and Significance

The **AC/A ratio** quantifies the amount of accommodative convergence (in [[prism diopters]], Δ) elicited per unit of accommodation (in [[diopters]], D). It is expressed in units of **Δ/D**.

$$\text{AC/A ratio} = \frac{\text{Accommodative Convergence (Δ)}}{\text{Accommodation (D)}}$$

The **population mean** AC/A ratio is approximately **3–5 Δ/D**, though normal values range from approximately 2 to 7 Δ/D depending on the measurement method and population studied.

#### Clinical Significance of Abnormal AC/A Ratios

| AC/A Ratio | Classification | Clinical Associations |
|---|---|---|
| **< 3 Δ/D** | Low AC/A ratio | [[Convergence insufficiency]], [[divergence excess]] (simulated type), near [[exophoria]] |
| **3–5 Δ/D** | Normal AC/A ratio | Normal binocular vision; basic [[esophoria]]/[[exophoria]] if present |
| **> 5 Δ/D** (some authorities use > 6 or > 7) | High AC/A ratio | [[Convergence excess]], [[accommodative esotropia]] (high AC/A type), near [[esophoria]] |

### Mechanisms of Abnormal Accommodative Convergence

#### High AC/A Ratio

A **high AC/A ratio** indicates that each diopter of accommodative effort generates an excessive amount of convergence. This leads to a greater [[esophoric]] or [[esotropic]] posture at near than at distance. The pathophysiology may involve:

- **Increased gain of the cross-link** between the accommodative controller and the vergence controller at the [[supraoculomotor area]] level.
- **Structural or innervational factors** affecting the [[medial rectus muscle]] sensitivity to vergence commands.
- **Uncorrected [[hyperopia]]**: In hyperopic individuals, the additional accommodative effort required to compensate for the refractive error amplifies accommodative convergence, potentially unmasking a functionally high AC/A response. This is the classic mechanism of **[[accommodative esotropia]]** with a high AC/A ratio.

In children, a high AC/A ratio is the hallmark of **high AC/A accommodative esotropia**, where the patient may be orthophoric or nearly so at distance but demonstrates a significant [[esotropia]] at near. This condition is distinct from **refractive accommodative esotropia**, where the deviation is similar at distance and near and is driven primarily by uncorrected hyperopia.

#### Low AC/A Ratio

A **low AC/A ratio** indicates that accommodative effort generates an insufficient convergence response. This results in a tendency toward [[exophoria]] or [[exotropia]] that is greater at near than at distance. Mechanisms may include:

- **Reduced gain of the accommodative-vergence cross-link.**
- **Weakness or reduced responsiveness of the [[medial rectus muscles]]** to vergence innervation.
- **High [[accommodative facility]]** with relatively decoupled vergence response.

Low AC/A ratios are commonly associated with **[[convergence insufficiency]]**, the most prevalent binocular vision disorder in the general population.

### Interaction with Fusional Vergence

When accommodative convergence does not precisely match the convergence demand for a given fixation distance, the residual misalignment (a [[fixation disparity]] or [[heterophoria]]) must be compensated by **[[fusional vergence]]** (positive or negative). If the fusional vergence reserves are insufficient to compensate for the error introduced by an abnormal AC/A ratio, the patient may experience symptoms of [[asthenopia]], [[diplopia]], or may develop a manifest [[strabismus]].

The relationship can be summarized:

> **Total Convergence Demand = Tonic + Proximal + Accommodative Convergence + Fusional Vergence**

If accommodative convergence overshoots (high AC/A), positive fusional vergence demand decreases but negative fusional vergence (divergence) must increase. If accommodative convergence undershoots (low AC/A), positive fusional vergence must increase to maintain single binocular vision.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Patients with abnormal accommodative convergence relationships may present with a wide range of symptoms, depending on the direction and magnitude of the anomaly and the adequacy of compensatory fusional vergence reserves.

### Common Symptoms

| Symptom | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **[[Asthenopia]]** (eye strain) | Excessive fusional vergence effort to compensate for accommodative convergence mismatch |
| **[[Headache]]** (frontal or temporal) | Sustained contraction of extraocular and periorbital muscles; autonomic referral |
| **Blurred vision at near** | Avoidance of accommodation to reduce excessive convergence (in high AC/A); or accommodative fatigue |
| **[[Diplopia]]** (intermittent or constant) | Decompensation of fusional vergence reserves |
| **Difficulty reading / loss of place** | Unstable binocular fixation during sustained near tasks |
| **Words "moving" or "swimming" on the page** | Intermittent vergence instability |
| **Closing or covering one eye** | Suppression strategy to eliminate diplopia or confusion |
| **Reduced attention span for near tasks** | Avoidance behavior, especially in children |
| **Print appears to "come closer" then recede** | Fluctuating accommodative-vergence interaction |

### Symptom Patterns by AC/A Anomaly

- **High AC/A ratio patients** tend to report symptoms predominantly at **near**, including esotropia at near, diplopia with near tasks, and frontal headaches after reading. Children may adopt a characteristic **chin-down posture** to reduce the near demand or may close one eye.

- **Low AC/A ratio patients** report symptoms of **convergence insufficiency** at near: difficulty sustaining near focus, words running together, asthenopia after prolonged reading, and sometimes exotropia at near.

## Diagnosis

### History and Symptom Assessment

A thorough case history is essential. Clinicians should inquire about:

- Duration and frequency of symptoms
- Relationship to near work (reading, computer use, mobile devices)
- Time of day symptoms worsen (typically worse later in the day with fatigue)
- History of [[strabismus]], [[amblyopia]], or prior [[vision therapy]]
- Current [[refractive correction]] and compliance

Validated questionnaires such as the **[[Convergence Insufficiency Symptom Survey]] (CISS)** can be useful for quantifying symptom severity.

### Clinical Measurements

#### 1. Cover Test (Distance and Near)

The **[[cover test]]** (unilateral and alternating) is performed at both distance (6 meters or 20 feet) and near (40 cm or 16 inches) to identify the presence and magnitude of any [[heterophoria]] or [[heterotropia]]. A significant difference between the distance and near deviation is a hallmark of an abnormal AC/A ratio:

- **Near deviation significantly more esophoric/esotropic than distance** → suggests **high AC/A ratio**
- **Near deviation significantly more exophoric/exotropic than distance** → suggests **low AC/A ratio**

#### 2. Measurement of the AC/A Ratio

Two primary clinical methods exist:

##### a. Calculated (Heterophoria) Method

$$\text{AC/A} = \text{IPD (cm)} + \frac{H_n - H_d}{D_n}$$

Where:
- **IPD** = [[interpupillary distance]] in centimeters
- **H_n** = near [[heterophoria]] (eso is positive, exo is negative)
- **H_d** = distance heterophoria (eso is positive, exo is negative)
- **D_n** = dioptric demand of the near target (e.g., 2.5 D for 40 cm)

This method incorporates all components of convergence (tonic, proximal, accommodative, and fusional) and therefore yields a **response AC/A ratio** that tends to be higher than the gradient method.

##### b. Gradient Method

The gradient method isolates accommodative convergence by measuring the change in heterophoria at a **fixed distance** when lenses of known power are introduced:

$$\text{AC/A} = \frac{\Delta \text{phoria}}{\Delta \text{lens power}}$$

Typically, the near phoria is measured through the habitual correction, then remeasured with an additional **+1.00 D** or **−1.00 D** lens. The change in phoria divided by the lens power change gives the gradient AC/A ratio.

| Method | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|
| **Calculated (Heterophoria)** | Accounts for all convergence components; clinically practical | Influenced by proximal convergence; tends to overestimate |
| **Gradient** | Isolates accommodative convergence; eliminates proximal cue changes | May underestimate if accommodative response is not fully stimulated; requires accurate phoria measurement |

**Clinical Pearl:** The gradient AC/A ratio is generally considered more accurate for isolating the true accommodative convergence component, while the calculated method provides a more ecologically valid measure of the total near vergence response.

#### 3. Near Point of Convergence (NPC)

The **[[near point of convergence]]** is measured by advancing a target toward the patient until one eye loses fixation (break) and then withdrawing until bifoveal fixation is regained (recovery). While not a direct measure of accommodative convergence, a receded NPC (> 10 cm break) is commonly associated with **low AC/A ratio** conditions such as [[convergence insufficiency]].

#### 4. Fusional Vergence Ranges

**Positive fusional vergence (PFV)** and **negative fusional vergence (NFV)** are measured at distance and near using a [[prism bar]] or [[rotary prisms]] in a [[phoropter]]. These measurements assess the patient's ability to compensate for any misalignment introduced by abnormal accommodative convergence.

| Measurement | Expected Normal (Near) | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **PFV (Base-out)** | Break: ~15–25 Δ; Recovery: ~10–18 Δ | Reduced PFV with low AC/A → symptomatic convergence insufficiency |
| **NFV (Base-in)** | Break: ~12–16 Δ; Recovery: ~8–12 Δ | Reduced NFV with high AC/A → symptomatic convergence excess |

#### 5. Accommodative Testing

Since accommodative convergence is driven by accommodation, a complete assessment of the [[accommodative system]] is essential:

- **[[Accommodative amplitude]]** (push-up, pull-away, or minus lens method)
- **[[Accommodative facility]]** (±2.00 D flipper lenses, monocular and binocular)
- **[[Monocular estimation method]] (MEM) [[retinoscopy]]** or **[[Nott retinoscopy]]** to assess the [[accommodative response]] (lag or lead)

Abnormalities in accommodation (e.g., [[accommodative insufficiency]], [[accommodative excess]], [[accommodative infacility]]) will directly affect accommodative convergence output and must be identified and addressed.

#### 6. Fixation Disparity Analysis

**[[Fixation disparity]]** testing using instruments such as the **[[Sheedy disparometer]]**, **[[Wesson fixation disparity card]]**, or **[[forced duction curve]] (Ogle's curve)** can reveal the stress on the vergence system imposed by abnormal accommodative convergence. The **associated phoria** derived from fixation disparity analysis may guide prescribing of prism or lens corrections.

## Differential Diagnosis

When evaluating a patient with suspected accommodative convergence anomaly, the clinician must differentiate from or consider co-existing conditions:

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| **[[Accommodative esotropia]]** (refractive type) | Similar deviation at distance and near; normal AC/A; significant uncorrected [[hyperopia]] |
| **[[Accommodative esotropia]]** (high AC/A type) | Greater esotropia at near than distance; high AC/A ratio |
| **[[Non-accommodative esotropia]]** | Deviation present with full refractive correction; not influenced by accommodative manipulation |
| **[[Convergence insufficiency]]** | Exophoria/exotropia greater at near; low AC/A; receded [[NPC]] |
| **[[Convergence excess]]** | Esophoria/esotropia greater at near; high AC/A; normal NPC |
| **[[Divergence insufficiency]]** | Esophoria greater at distance; normal AC/A |
| **[[Divergence excess]]** | Exophoria/exotropia greater at distance; may have pseudo-high AC/A if tenacious proximal fusion |
| **[[Sixth nerve palsy]]** | Unilateral or bilateral abduction deficit; acute onset; neurological evaluation required |

## Management and Treatment

Management of accommodative convergence anomalies is directed at restoring a functional balance between the accommodative and vergence systems. The approach is tailored to the specific anomaly (high vs. low AC/A ratio) and the patient's age, refractive status, and symptom severity.

### 1. Optical Correction of Refractive Error

The **first and most fundamental step** is accurate correction of any [[refractive error]], particularly [[hyperopia]]:

- In **hyperopic patients with high AC/A**, full cycloplegic correction reduces the accommodative demand and consequently reduces accommodative convergence. This is the cornerstone of management for **[[refractive accommodative esotropia]]**.
- In **myopic patients**, ensuring appropriate correction prevents over-accommodation at near.

### 2. Plus Lens Additions (Near Add / Bifocals / Progressive Lenses)

For patients with a **high AC/A ratio**, the addition of plus power for near tasks reduces the accommodative demand and thereby reduces accommodative convergence:

- **[[Bifocal]] lenses** (typically executive/flat-top style in children to ensure consistent use of the near segment) with adds ranging from **+1.00 to +3.00 D** depending on the magnitude of the near esotropia.
- **[[Progressive addition lenses]] (PALs)** may be used in older children and adults for cosmetic reasons, though the narrower near zone may be less effective in young children.
- The goal is to reduce the near deviation to within the range compensable by fusional vergence.

**Clinical Pearl:** In **high AC/A accommodative esotropia**, the near add is titrated to the minimum plus power that eliminates the near esotropia while maintaining clear near vision.

### 3. Minus Lens Therapy

In select cases of **low AC/A ratio** with convergence insufficiency, small amounts of **minus lens over-correction** at near can be used to stimulate additional accommodation and thereby increase accommodative convergence. However, this approach is less commonly employed than vision therapy and must be used cautiously to avoid inducing accommodative spasm.

### 4. Prism Therapy

**[[Prism]]** correction (base-in for esophoria/esotropia; base-out for exophoria/exotropia) can be prescribed to reduce the fusional vergence demand:

- **Relieving prisms** are typically prescribed at a fraction (usually one-third to one-half) of the measured phoria.
- Prism may be incorporated into spectacle lenses (**[[Fresnel prism]]** for temporary use or ground-in prism for long-term wear).
- Prism addresses the **symptom** (misalignment) rather than the **cause** (abnormal AC/A ratio) and is therefore often used as an adjunct rather than a primary treatment.

### 5. Vision Therapy / Orthoptics

**[[Vision therapy]]** (also known as [[orthoptic exercises]]) is a structured program of neurosensory and neuromuscular activities designed to improve vergence and accommodative function. It is considered the **gold standard treatment** for many accommodative convergence anomalies, particularly [[convergence insufficiency]].

#### Key Vision Therapy Techniques

| Technique | Target | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Pencil push-ups]] (HBPP)** | Convergence amplitude | Patient converges on a near target advanced toward the nose; trains positive fusional vergence |
| **[[Brock string]]** | Vergence awareness and control | String with beads at varying distances; trains convergence, divergence, and anti-suppression |
| **[[Vectograms]] / [[Tranaglyphs]]** | Fusional vergence ranges | Polarized or red-green targets requiring vergence changes to maintain fusion |
| **[[Aperture rule]]** | Flat and stereoscopic fusion | Trains convergence and divergence in a controlled setting |
| **[[Computer-based vergence therapy]]** (e.g., [[HTS]], [[VividVision]])| Vergence and accommodation | Interactive software programs for home and office-based therapy |
| **[[Accommodative facility training]]** (±2.00 flipper lenses) | Accommodative flexibility | Improves speed and accuracy of accommodative response, indirectly normalizing accommodative convergence |
| **[[Loose prism jumps]]** | Fusional vergence step facility | Alternating base-in and base-out prisms to train rapid vergence responses |

#### Evidence Base

The **[[Convergence Insufficiency Treatment Trial]] (CITT)** — a landmark multicenter, randomized clinical trial — demonstrated that **office-based vergence/accommodative therapy** was significantly superior to home-based pencil push-ups, home-based computer vergence therapy, and office-based placebo therapy for the treatment of symptomatic convergence insufficiency in children and adults. This provides Level I evidence supporting vision therapy for low AC/A-related convergence disorders.

### 6. Pharmacological Interventions

Pharmacological agents play a limited but specific role in the management of accommodative convergence anomalies:

- **[[Miotics]]** (e.g., **[[echothiophate iodide]]** 0.125%, **[[pilocarpine]]**): These [[cholinesterase inhibitors]] or direct [[parasympathomimetics]] stimulate the [[ciliary muscle]] peripherally, reducing the central accommodative innervation required for a given near focus. This in turn reduces accommodative convergence. Historically used in **high AC/A accommodative esotropia** as an alternative to bifocals, particularly in very young children who cannot reliably wear spectacles. However, their use has declined due to side effects (including [[iris cysts]], [[cataract]] risk with long-term use, and systemic cholinergic effects) and the availability of effective optical and surgical alternatives.

- **[[Cycloplegic agents]]** (e.g., **[[atropine]]**): Used diagnostically (cycloplegic refraction) to reveal the full hyperopic refractive error. Therapeutic low-dose atropine (0.01%–0.05%) is primarily studied for [[myopia control]] but does not have a primary role in managing accommodative convergence anomalies.

### 7. Surgical Management

Surgery is considered when optical and therapeutic interventions fail to adequately control the deviation, particularly in cases of **high AC/A accommodative esotropia** with a residual near esotropia despite full hyperopic correction:

- **[[Bilateral medial rectus recession]]**: Weakens the medial recti to reduce convergence. The amount of recession is calibrated to the near deviation.
- **[[Posterior fixation suture]] ([[Faden procedure]])**: A suture is placed through the medial rectus muscle and the sclera posterior to the muscle's equatorial insertion. This effectively weakens the muscle's action at near (where the eye is converged and the muscle's arc of contact changes) without significantly affecting distance alignment. The Faden procedure is particularly well-suited for **high AC/A esotropia** because it selectively reduces the near deviation.
- **[[Augmented bilateral medial rectus recession]]**: Combining standard recession with a posterior fixation suture for large near-distance disparities.
- **[[Slanted recession]]**: Differential recession of the superior and inferior poles of the medial rectus to address pattern deviations if present.

| Surgical Procedure | Indication | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Bilateral MR recession** | Residual esotropia at near and distance despite optical correction | Weakens medial recti globally |
| **Faden procedure (posterior fixation suture)** | High AC/A esotropia with orthophoria at distance but esotropia at near | Selectively weakens medial rectus at near gaze |
| **Combined recession + Faden** | Large near-distance disparity | Addresses both distance and near components |

## Special Considerations

### Pediatric Patients

- **[[Accommodative esotropia]]** typically presents between ages **2–4 years** and is the most common form of childhood esotropia.
- Early and full correction of [[hyperopia]] is critical to prevent [[amblyopia]] and loss of [[stereopsis]].
- The AC/A ratio should be measured once the child is compliant with spectacle wear and the refractive esotropia has been addressed.
- Bifocal adds should be prescribed with a **flat-top (executive) segment** set at the **lower pupillary margin** to ensure the child looks through the near segment during close tasks.

### Presbyopic Patients

- As [[presbyopia]] develops, the accommodative system becomes progressively less responsive. Since accommodative convergence is driven by accommodative effort, the AC/A relationship may change with age.
- Presbyopic patients wearing progressive or bifocal lenses have their accommodative demand reduced, which also reduces accommodative convergence. This can unmask a latent [[exophoria]] at near (previously compensated by accommodative convergence), leading to new-onset symptoms of [[asthenopia]] or [[diplopia]] at near.
- Careful assessment of the near phoria through the reading add is essential in symptomatic presbyopic patients.

### Digital Device Use and Accommodative Convergence

The increasing prevalence of prolonged near work on digital devices has heightened the clinical relevance of accommodative convergence anomalies. Sustained near focus at short working distances (often < 33 cm with smartphones) places high demands on both accommodation and convergence, potentially exacerbating symptoms in patients with borderline AC/A abnormalities. This contributes to the growing prevalence of **[[digital eye strain]]** (also known as [[computer vision syndrome]]).

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis for patients with accommodative convergence anomalies is generally **favorable** with appropriate management:

- **High AC/A accommodative esotropia** in children often responds well to spectacle correction with bifocal adds; many children experience a reduction in hyperopia over time, and some may eventually discontinue bifocals. However, a subset will require surgical intervention.
- **Convergence insufficiency** (low AC/A) has an excellent prognosis with office-based vision therapy, with success rates exceeding **70–80%** in clinical trials.
- Long-term monitoring is essential, as the accommodative-vergence relationship can change with age, refractive changes, and systemic health factors.

---

## Summary

Accommodative convergence is a cornerstone concept in [[binocular vision]] and [[clinical optometry]]. The AC/A ratio serves as the critical quantitative metric linking the [[accommodative system]] to the [[vergence system]], and its accurate measurement is essential for the diagnosis and management of numerous binocular vision disorders. A systematic approach—encompassing thorough history-taking, precise measurement of phorias and tropias at distance and near, determination of the AC/A ratio by both calculated and gradient methods, assessment of fusional vergence reserves, and evaluation of accommodative function—enables the clinician to formulate an evidence-based management plan. Treatment options span the full spectrum from optical correction and lens additions, through vision therapy, to pharmacological and surgical interventions, and should be individualized based on the patient's age, clinical findings, and functional needs.
