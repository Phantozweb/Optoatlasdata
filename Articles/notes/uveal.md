

# Uveal Tract (Uvea)

---

The **uveal tract**, commonly referred to as the **[[uvea]]**, is the highly vascularized, pigmented middle coat of the eye situated between the outer fibrous tunic ([[sclera]] and [[cornea]]) and the inner neural tunic ([[retina]]). The term "uvea" derives from the Latin word *uva*, meaning "grape," owing to the structure's resemblance to a dark grape when the sclera is removed. The uveal tract is composed of three anatomically continuous yet functionally distinct structures: the **[[iris]]**, the **[[ciliary body]]**, and the **[[choroid]]**. Collectively, these structures are responsible for critical ocular functions including regulation of light entry, [[accommodation]], [[aqueous humor]] production, and nourishment of the outer retinal layers. Pathology of the uveal tract—broadly termed **[[uveitis]]** when inflammatory, or **uveal neoplasia** when neoplastic—represents a significant cause of ocular morbidity and vision loss worldwide. This article provides an exhaustive clinical overview of the uveal tract, encompassing its anatomy, embryology, physiology, pathophysiology, clinical presentation, diagnostic modalities, and management strategies relevant to the practicing [[optometrist]] and [[ophthalmologist]].

---

## Anatomy of the Uveal Tract

### Iris

The **[[iris]]** is the most anterior component of the uveal tract, forming a thin, contractile diaphragm situated between the [[anterior chamber]] and the [[posterior chamber]] of the eye. It is perforated centrally by the **[[pupil]]**, whose diameter is dynamically regulated by two smooth muscles:

| Muscle | Innervation | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Sphincter pupillae]]** (pupillary sphincter) | [[Parasympathetic nervous system]] via [[cranial nerve III]] (short ciliary nerves) | [[Miosis]] (pupil constriction) |
| **[[Dilator pupillae]]** (pupillary dilator) | [[Sympathetic nervous system]] via long ciliary nerves | [[Mydriasis]] (pupil dilation) |

The iris stroma is a loose connective tissue matrix containing [[melanocytes]], [[fibroblasts]], collagen fibrils, and a rich vascular network. The density and distribution of stromal melanocytes determine **iris color**: eyes with dense melanocyte populations appear brown, while those with sparse melanocytes appear blue or green due to [[Rayleigh scattering]] of light. The posterior surface of the iris is lined by a bilayered [[pigmented epithelium]] continuous with the non-pigmented epithelium of the [[ciliary body]].

The iris receives its blood supply primarily from the **[[major arterial circle of the iris]]** (circulus arteriosus iridis major), formed by anastomoses of the [[long posterior ciliary arteries]] and the [[anterior ciliary arteries]].

### Ciliary Body

The **[[ciliary body]]** is a ring-shaped structure extending from the [[scleral spur]] anteriorly to the [[ora serrata]] posteriorly. It is divided into two regions:

- **[[Pars plicata]]** (corona ciliaris): The anterior, ridged portion containing approximately 70–80 **[[ciliary processes]]** responsible for the active secretion of [[aqueous humor]].
- **[[Pars plana]]** (orbiculus ciliaris): The posterior, flat portion that serves as a common surgical entry site for [[pars plana vitrectomy]] and intravitreal injections.

The ciliary body contains the **[[ciliary muscle]]**, a smooth muscle with longitudinal (Brücke's), radial, and circular (Müller's) fibers. Contraction of the ciliary muscle relaxes tension on the **[[zonules of Zinn]]** (zonular fibers), allowing the [[crystalline lens]] to assume a more convex shape—the basis of **[[accommodation]]**.

The ciliary epithelium is bilayered: an inner **non-pigmented epithelium** (facing the posterior chamber) and an outer **pigmented epithelium** (continuous with the [[retinal pigment epithelium]]). The non-pigmented epithelium is the primary site of aqueous humor production through a combination of active secretion (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase dependent), ultrafiltration, and diffusion.

### Choroid

The **[[choroid]]** is the posterior and most extensive component of the uveal tract, extending from the ora serrata anteriorly to the [[optic nerve]] posteriorly. It is the most vascular tissue in the body per unit weight and is responsible for supplying oxygen and nutrients to the outer layers of the [[retina]], including the [[photoreceptors]] and the [[retinal pigment epithelium (RPE)]].

The choroid is organized into several layers (from external to internal):

| Layer | Description |
|---|---|
| **[[Suprachoroidal lamina]]** (lamina fusca) | Transitional zone between sclera and choroid; contains melanocytes and loose connective tissue; potential space for [[suprachoroidal hemorrhage]] or [[choroidal effusion]] |
| **[[Haller's layer]]** (outer vascular layer) | Contains large-caliber choroidal vessels (arteries and veins) |
| **[[Sattler's layer]]** (inner vascular layer) | Contains medium-caliber vessels |
| **[[Choriocapillaris]]** | Dense capillary network immediately external to [[Bruch's membrane]]; fenestrated endothelium allows high-volume perfusion of the RPE and outer retina |
| **[[Bruch's membrane]]** | Pentalaminar structure (basement membrane of RPE, inner collagenous zone, elastic layer, outer collagenous zone, basement membrane of choriocapillaris) serving as a selective barrier between the choroid and RPE |

The choroid receives its blood supply from the **[[short posterior ciliary arteries]]** (SPCAs), which branch from the [[ophthalmic artery]]. Venous drainage occurs via the **[[vortex veins]]** (venae vorticosae), typically four in number, which exit the globe posterior to the equator and drain into the [[superior and inferior ophthalmic veins]].

## Embryology

The uveal tract has a dual embryological origin:

- **[[Neural crest]]–derived mesenchyme**: Gives rise to the stromal components of the iris, ciliary body, and choroid, including melanocytes, fibroblasts, and vascular endothelium.
- **[[Neuroectoderm]]** (optic cup): Gives rise to the pigmented and non-pigmented epithelium of the ciliary body, the posterior pigmented epithelium of the iris, the [[sphincter pupillae]], and the [[dilator pupillae]] muscles.

This dual origin is clinically significant because developmental anomalies can arise from defects in either cell lineage, leading to conditions such as **[[aniridia]]**, **[[coloboma]]** (failure of the [[embryonic fissure]] to close), and **[[persistent hyperplastic primary vitreous (PHPV)]]**.

## Physiology of the Uveal Tract

### Light Regulation

The iris functions as a dynamic aperture controlling the amount of light reaching the [[retina]]. The [[pupillary light reflex]] is mediated by the [[afferent pathway]] (via [[cranial nerve II]]) and the [[efferent pathway]] (via [[cranial nerve III]]). Abnormalities in this reflex, such as a **[[relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD)]]**, can indicate asymmetric optic nerve or retinal disease.

### Aqueous Humor Dynamics

The ciliary body produces approximately **2.0–2.5 µL/min** of [[aqueous humor]], which flows from the [[posterior chamber]] through the [[pupil]] into the [[anterior chamber]], and exits primarily via the **[[trabecular meshwork]]** and **[[Schlemm's canal]]** (conventional outflow, ~80–90%) and secondarily via the **[[uveoscleral pathway]]** (unconventional outflow, ~10–20%). Disruption of this balance is central to the pathogenesis of [[glaucoma]].

### Choroidal Blood Flow and Thermoregulation

The choroid accounts for approximately **85% of total ocular blood flow**. This high perfusion rate serves not only to nourish the outer retina but also to dissipate heat generated by light absorption in the RPE and photoreceptors, thereby maintaining thermal homeostasis of the posterior segment.

## Pathophysiology of Uveal Diseases

### Uveal Inflammation (Uveitis)

**[[Uveitis]]** refers to inflammation of the uveal tract and is a leading cause of preventable blindness, accounting for approximately **10–15% of total blindness** in developed countries. The Standardization of Uveitis Nomenclature (SUN) Working Group classifies uveitis anatomically:

| Classification | Primary Site of Inflammation | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Anterior uveitis]]** | Anterior chamber (iris, ciliary body) | [[Iritis]], [[iridocyclitis]], [[HLA-B27]]-associated uveitis, [[herpetic anterior uveitis]] |
| **[[Intermediate uveitis]]** | Vitreous cavity, peripheral retina | [[Pars planitis]], [[multiple sclerosis]]-associated uveitis |
| **[[Posterior uveitis]]** | Choroid, retina | [[Choroiditis]], [[chorioretinitis]], [[toxoplasmosis]], [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada (VKH) disease]] |
| **[[Panuveitis]]** | All segments | [[Behçet disease]], [[sarcoidosis]], [[sympathetic ophthalmia]] |

#### Immunopathogenesis

The uveal tract is an immunologically privileged site, maintained by the **[[blood-ocular barrier]]** (comprising the [[blood-aqueous barrier]] and the [[blood-retinal barrier]]) and by active immunosuppressive mechanisms collectively termed **[[anterior chamber-associated immune deviation (ACAID)]]**. Breakdown of these barriers—whether by infectious agents, autoimmune processes, or trauma—triggers an inflammatory cascade involving:

1. **Innate immunity**: Activation of resident [[macrophages]], [[dendritic cells]], and [[mast cells]]; release of [[cytokines]] (e.g., [[TNF-α]], [[IL-1β]], [[IL-6]]) and [[chemokines]].
2. **Adaptive immunity**: Recruitment and activation of [[T lymphocytes]] (particularly [[Th1]] and [[Th17]] subsets) and, in some cases, [[B lymphocytes]] with autoantibody production.
3. **Tissue damage**: Fibrin deposition, [[posterior synechiae]] formation, [[cystoid macular edema (CME)]], [[secondary glaucoma]], and [[cataract]] formation.

#### Infectious Etiologies

Numerous infectious agents can cause uveal inflammation:

| Pathogen Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Viral** | [[Herpes simplex virus (HSV)]], [[Varicella-zoster virus (VZV)]], [[Cytomegalovirus (CMV)]], [[Rubella virus]] |
| **Bacterial** | [[Treponema pallidum]] ([[syphilis]]), [[Mycobacterium tuberculosis]], [[Borrelia burgdorferi]] ([[Lyme disease]]), [[Bartonella henselae]] |
| **Parasitic** | [[Toxoplasma gondii]], [[Toxocara canis]], [[Onchocerca volvulus]] |
| **Fungal** | [[Candida albicans]], [[Histoplasma capsulatum]] (presumed [[ocular histoplasmosis syndrome]]) |

#### Non-Infectious / Autoimmune Etiologies

- **[[HLA-B27]]-associated anterior uveitis**: The most common identifiable cause of acute anterior uveitis; associated with [[ankylosing spondylitis]], [[reactive arthritis]], [[psoriatic arthritis]], and [[inflammatory bowel disease]].
- **[[Sarcoidosis]]**: Granulomatous uveitis with characteristic [[mutton-fat keratic precipitates]], [[iris nodules]] (Koeppe and Busacca), and [[snowball opacities]].
- **[[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada (VKH) disease]]**: Bilateral granulomatous panuveitis with exudative [[retinal detachment]], targeting melanocyte-containing tissues.
- **[[Behçet disease]]**: Occlusive vasculitis with [[hypopyon]] uveitis, retinal vasculitis, and risk of severe vision loss.
- **[[Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA)]]**: Chronic, often asymptomatic anterior uveitis in children, particularly those with oligoarticular JIA and positive [[antinuclear antibody (ANA)]].
- **[[Sympathetic ophthalmia]]**: Bilateral granulomatous panuveitis following penetrating trauma or surgery to one eye, mediated by autoimmunity against uveal melanocyte antigens.

### Uveal Neoplasia

#### Benign Uveal Tumors

- **[[Choroidal nevus]]**: The most common intraocular tumor, found in approximately 5–10% of the adult population. Typically flat, pigmented, and less than 2 mm in thickness. Monitored for transformation to melanoma using the mnemonic **"To Find Small Ocular Melanoma Using Helpful Hints Daily"** (Thickness >2 mm, Fluid subretinal, Symptoms, Orange pigment, Margin ≤3 mm from disc, Ultrasonographic Hollowness, Halo absent, Drusen absent).
- **[[Iris nevus]]**: Pigmented lesion of the iris stroma; requires differentiation from [[iris melanoma]].
- **[[Choroidal hemangioma]]**: Benign vascular tumor; may be circumscribed (sporadic) or diffuse (associated with [[Sturge-Weber syndrome]]).

#### Malignant Uveal Tumors

- **[[Uveal melanoma]]**: The most common primary intraocular malignancy in adults, with an incidence of approximately 5–7 per million per year in Caucasian populations. Arises most commonly in the choroid (~85%), followed by the ciliary body (~10%) and iris (~5%). Risk factors include light skin color, light iris color, [[ocular melanocytosis]], [[BAP1 tumor predisposition syndrome]], and the presence of a pre-existing choroidal nevus.

  **Molecular genetics**: Uveal melanoma is characterized by distinct chromosomal aberrations:
  - **[[Monosomy 3]]**: Strongly associated with metastatic disease and poor prognosis.
  - **[[Gain of chromosome 8q]]**: Associated with increased metastatic risk.
  - **[[BAP1 gene]]** mutations: Loss of BAP1 expression correlates with class 2 gene expression profile and high metastatic risk.
  - **[[GNAQ]]** and **[[GNA11]]** mutations: Found in >80% of uveal melanomas; activating mutations in these G-protein alpha subunits drive the [[MAPK pathway]].

  Gene expression profiling (GEP) classifies uveal melanomas into:
  - **Class 1A**: Low metastatic risk (~2% at 5 years)
  - **Class 1B**: Intermediate metastatic risk (~21% at 5 years)
  - **Class 2**: High metastatic risk (~72% at 5 years)

- **[[Choroidal metastasis]]**: The most common intraocular malignancy overall (more common than primary uveal melanoma). Most frequently from breast carcinoma (in women) and lung carcinoma (in men). Typically presents as a creamy-yellow, flat or dome-shaped choroidal lesion with associated [[exudative retinal detachment]].

- **[[Medulloepithelioma]]** (diktyoma): Rare embryonal tumor of the non-pigmented ciliary epithelium, typically presenting in childhood.

### Other Uveal Pathologies

- **[[Uveal effusion syndrome]]**: Idiopathic serous detachment of the choroid and ciliary body, often associated with abnormal scleral thickness or composition (nanophthalmos, Hunter syndrome).
- **[[Choroidal rupture]]**: Traumatic disruption of the choroid, Bruch's membrane, and RPE, often concentric to the optic disc; may lead to [[choroidal neovascularization (CNV)]].
- **[[Choroidal detachment]]**: Separation of the choroid from the sclera, which may be serous (transudative) or hemorrhagic (suprachoroidal hemorrhage); commonly occurs post-operatively or in the setting of hypotony.

## Clinical Presentation (Symptoms and Signs)

### Symptoms of Uveal Disease

The symptomatology of uveal disease varies significantly depending on the anatomical location and nature of the pathology:

| Symptom | Anterior Uveal Disease | Posterior Uveal Disease | Uveal Neoplasia |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pain** | Moderate to severe, deep aching; photophobia | Usually painless | Usually painless (unless secondary glaucoma) |
| **Redness** | [[Ciliary flush]] (circumcorneal injection) | Absent or minimal | Absent or minimal |
| **Vision loss** | Mild to moderate (from cells/flare, CME) | Moderate to severe (from vitritis, CME, retinal involvement) | Variable; may be asymptomatic or present with visual field defect, metamorphopsia |
| **Floaters** | Mild | Prominent (vitreous cells, snowballs) | May occur with vitreous seeding |
| **Photopsia** | Uncommon | May occur with retinal involvement | May occur with retinal detachment |
| **Scotoma** | Uncommon | Common with chorioretinal lesions | Common with macular or peripapillary tumors |

### Signs of Uveal Disease

#### Anterior Segment Signs

- **[[Keratic precipitates (KPs)]]**: Inflammatory cell deposits on the [[corneal endothelium]]. Fine, non-granulomatous KPs suggest non-granulomatous uveitis (e.g., HLA-B27). Large, greasy **[[mutton-fat KPs]]** suggest granulomatous uveitis (e.g., sarcoidosis, VKH, tuberculosis, syphilis).
- **[[Anterior chamber cells and flare]]**: Graded 0 to 4+ per the SUN criteria. Cells represent inflammatory infiltrate; flare represents protein leakage from breakdown of the blood-aqueous barrier.
- **[[Posterior synechiae]]**: Adhesions between the iris and the anterior lens capsule; may lead to [[iris bombé]] and [[secondary angle-closure glaucoma]] if 360° (seclusio pupillae).
- **[[Hypopyon]]**: Layered collection of white blood cells in the inferior anterior chamber; seen in severe anterior uveitis (e.g., Behçet disease, [[HLA-B27]] uveitis, endophthalmitis) or masquerade syndromes (e.g., retinoblastoma, lymphoma).
- **[[Iris nodules]]**: **Koeppe nodules** (at the pupillary margin) and **Busacca nodules** (on the iris stroma) are characteristic of granulomatous uveitis.
- **[[Heterochromia]]**: Iris color asymmetry; seen in [[Fuchs heterochromic iridocyclitis]].
- **[[Rubeosis iridis]]** ([[neovascularization of the iris]]): May occur secondary to chronic posterior segment ischemia or intraocular tumors.

#### Posterior Segment Signs

- **[[Vitritis]]**: Vitreous haze and cells; graded 0 to 4+ using the Nussenblatt/NEI scale.
- **[[Snowbanking]]**: Exudative material over the pars plana, characteristic of [[pars planitis]].
- **[[Choroidal lesions]]**: Focal or multifocal areas of choroiditis, choroidal granulomas, or choroidal infiltrates.
- **[[Retinal vasculitis]]**: Perivascular sheathing, vascular leakage, or occlusion.
- **[[Cystoid macular edema (CME)]]**: The most common cause of vision loss in uveitis.
- **[[Exudative retinal detachment]]**: Seen in VKH, posterior scleritis, uveal effusion, and choroidal tumors.
- **Tumor-specific signs**: Dome-shaped or mushroom-shaped choroidal mass (melanoma), orange pigment ([[lipofuscin]]) overlying a choroidal lesion, sentinel vessels on the episclera (ciliary body melanoma).

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical Examination

- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: Essential for evaluating anterior segment inflammation, KPs, cells, flare, synechiae, and iris lesions.
- **[[Gonioscopy]]**: Assessment of the [[anterior chamber angle]] for peripheral anterior synechiae (PAS), angle neovascularization, or tumor extension.
- **[[Dilated fundus examination]]** with **[[binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy (BIO)]]** and **[[slit-lamp fundoscopy]]** (e.g., 78D, 90D lenses): Critical for evaluating posterior segment pathology.
- **[[Intraocular pressure (IOP)]]** measurement: IOP may be elevated (trabeculitis, trabecular meshwork obstruction by cells/debris, steroid response) or decreased (ciliary body shutdown from inflammation).

### Imaging

| Modality | Application in Uveal Disease |
|---|---|
| **[[Optical coherence tomography (OCT)]]** | Detection and monitoring of CME, subretinal fluid, epiretinal membrane, choroidal thickening, and subfoveal pathology |
| **[[Enhanced depth imaging OCT (EDI-OCT)]]** | Measurement of choroidal thickness; useful in VKH, central serous chorioretinopathy, and choroidal tumors |
| **[[OCT angiography (OCTA)]]** | Non-invasive visualization of choroidal neovascularization, choriocapillaris flow voids, and retinal vascular changes |
| **[[Fundus fluorescein angiography (FFA)]]** | Detection of retinal vasculitis, CME (petaloid pattern), disc leakage, capillary non-perfusion, and choroidal neovascularization |
| **[[Indocyanine green angiography (ICGA)]]** | Superior visualization of choroidal vasculature; essential for diagnosing choroidal inflammatory conditions (e.g., [[multifocal choroiditis]], [[birdshot chorioretinopathy]], VKH) and differentiating choroidal tumors |
| **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** | Evaluation of choroidal masses (acoustic hollowness in melanoma, high internal reflectivity in hemangioma), choroidal detachment, and posterior segment assessment when media opacity precludes fundoscopy |
| **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy (UBM)]]** | High-resolution imaging of the iris, ciliary body, and anterior uveal structures; essential for ciliary body tumors and anterior segment pathology |
| **[[Fundus autofluorescence (FAF)]]** | Detection of lipofuscin accumulation (orange pigment) overlying choroidal nevi/melanomas; RPE changes in inflammatory conditions |
| **[[MRI of the orbit]]** | Characterization of uveal melanoma (hyperintense on T1, hypointense on T2 due to melanin content); assessment of extraocular extension |
| **[[PET/CT]]** | Systemic staging for metastatic uveal melanoma or identification of primary malignancy in choroidal metastasis |

### Laboratory Investigations

A targeted laboratory workup is essential in uveitis, guided by clinical presentation:

| Suspected Etiology | Recommended Tests |
|---|---|
| **HLA-B27-associated** | [[HLA-B27]] typing, sacroiliac joint radiography |
| **Sarcoidosis** | [[Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE)]], [[lysozyme]], chest X-ray or CT, [[gallium scan]] or [[PET/CT]] |
| **Tuberculosis** | [[Tuberculin skin test (PPD)]], [[QuantiFERON-TB Gold]], chest X-ray |
| **Syphilis** | [[RPR]]/[[VDRL]] (screening), [[FTA-ABS]]/[[TP-PA]] (confirmatory) |
| **Toxoplasmosis** | [[Toxoplasma IgG/IgM]] serology; aqueous humor [[PCR]] |
| **Herpetic uveitis** | Aqueous humor [[PCR]] for HSV, VZV, CMV |
| **Behçet disease** | Clinical criteria (International Study Group); [[pathergy test]] |
| **Lymphoma (masquerade)** | Vitreous biopsy with [[cytology]], [[flow cytometry]], [[IL-10/IL-6 ratio]] |

### Biopsy and Cytogenetics

- **[[Fine-needle aspiration biopsy (FNAB)]]**: Used for diagnostic confirmation of intraocular tumors when clinical diagnosis is uncertain; also used for prognostic cytogenetic and molecular testing in uveal melanoma (monosomy 3, GEP).
- **[[Vitreous biopsy]]**: Indicated in suspected intraocular lymphoma or atypical uveitis unresponsive to therapy.
- **[[Aqueous humor analysis]]**: PCR for infectious agents; Goldmann-Witmer coefficient for local antibody production.

## Management and Treatment

### Pharmacological Treatment of Uveitis

#### Corticosteroids

[[Corticosteroids]] remain the first-line treatment for non-infectious uveitis due to their potent anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive properties:

| Route | Examples | Indications |
|---|---|---|
| **Topical** | [[Prednisolone acetate 1%]], [[difluprednate 0.05%]] | Anterior uveitis |
| **Periocular injection** | [[Sub-Tenon's triamcinolone acetonide]], [[orbital floor triamcinolone]] | Intermediate/posterior uveitis, CME unresponsive to topical therapy |
| **Intravitreal injection** | [[Intravitreal triamcinolone acetonide]], [[dexamethasone intravitreal implant (Ozurdex)]] | Posterior uveitis, refractory CME |
| **Sustained-release implant** | [[Fluocinolone acetonide intravitreal implant (Retisert, Yutiq)]] | Chronic non-infectious posterior uveitis |
| **Systemic** | [[Oral prednisone]] (typically 1 mg/kg/day with taper) | Severe bilateral uveitis, systemic inflammatory disease |

**Side effects** of corticosteroids include [[posterior subcapsular cataract]], [[steroid-induced glaucoma]], [[central serous chorioretinopathy]], and systemic effects (hyperglycemia, osteoporosis, adrenal suppression) with prolonged oral use.

#### Cycloplegic/Mydriatic Agents

- **[[Cyclopentolate 1%]]**, **[[homatropine 2–5%]]**, or **[[atropine 1%]]** are used in anterior uveitis to relieve ciliary spasm (pain), prevent and break posterior synechiae, and stabilize the blood-aqueous barrier.

#### Immunomodulatory Therapy (Steroid-Sparing Agents)

Indicated when corticosteroids cannot be tapered below 7.5 mg/day of prednisone equivalent, or when side effects are unacceptable:

| Drug Class | Examples | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Antimetabolites** | [[Methotrexate]], [[mycophenolate mofetil]], [[azathioprine]] | Inhibition of purine/pyrimidine synthesis; suppression of lymphocyte proliferation |
| **T-cell inhibitors** | [[Cyclosporine A]], [[tacrolimus]] | Calcineurin inhibition; suppression of IL-2 and T-cell activation |
| **Alkylating agents** | [[Cyclophosphamide]], [[chlorambucil]] | DNA cross-linking; reserved for severe, sight-threatening disease (e.g., Behçet) |
| **Biologic agents (anti-TNF)** | [[Adalimumab (Humira)]], [[infliximab]] | TNF-α inhibition; adalimumab is FDA-approved for non-infectious intermediate, posterior, and panuveitis |
| **Other biologics** | [[Rituximab]] (anti-CD20), [[tocilizumab]] (anti-IL-6R), [[secukinumab]] (anti-IL-17A) | Targeted immunosuppression for refractory cases |

#### Antimicrobial Therapy

For infectious uveitis, targeted antimicrobial therapy is essential:

- **[[Toxoplasmosis]]**: Classic triple therapy with [[pyrimethamine]], [[sulfadiazine]], and [[folinic acid]]; alternatives include [[trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole]] or [[azithromycin]] combined with pyrimethamine.
- **[[Syphilitic uveitis]]**: [[Intravenous penicillin G]] (neurosyphilis protocol).
- **[[Tubercular uveitis]]**: Standard anti-tubercular therapy ([[isoniazid]], [[rifampicin]], [[pyrazinamide]], [[ethambutol]]) with concurrent corticosteroids.
- **[[Herpetic uveitis]]**: [[Oral valacyclovir]] or [[acyclovir]]; [[intravenous acyclovir]] for [[acute retinal necrosis (ARN)]].
- **[[CMV retinitis]]**: [[Intravenous or intravitreal ganciclovir]], [[valganciclovir]], or [[foscarnet]].

### Management of Uveal Melanoma

Treatment of [[uveal melanoma]] depends on tumor size, location, and extent, as well as patient factors:

| Treatment Modality | Indications | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Plaque brachytherapy]]** (e.g., [[Iodine-125]], [[Ruthenium-106]]) | Small to medium choroidal melanomas (≤18 mm basal diameter, ≤12 mm thickness) | Most common globe-preserving treatment; radioactive plaque sutured to sclera overlying the tumor |
| **[[Proton beam radiotherapy]]** | Medium to large tumors, peripapillary or juxtapapillary tumors | Charged particle radiation with sharp dose fall-off |
| **[[Transpupillary thermotherapy (TTT)]]** | Small choroidal melanomas; adjunct to brachytherapy | Infrared laser (810 nm) applied to tumor surface |
| **[[Enucleation]]** | Large tumors (>18 mm basal diameter or >12 mm thickness), tumors with extraocular extension, blind painful eyes | Removal of the globe; orbital implant placed |
| **[[Local resection]]** (endoresection or exoresection) | Select cases, particularly ciliary body or iridociliary tumors | Technically demanding; risk of recurrence and complications |
| **Observation** | Small, indeterminate choroidal melanocytic lesions (suspicious nevi) | Serial photography, OCT, ultrasonography at 3–6 month intervals |

**Systemic surveillance** for metastatic disease is critical, as uveal melanoma has a propensity for **hepatic metastasis** (>90% of metastases involve the liver). Surveillance protocols typically include liver function tests and hepatic imaging (ultrasound, MRI, or CT) every 6–12 months. Emerging systemic therapies for metastatic uveal melanoma include **[[tebentafusp]]** (a bispecific gp100-directed T-cell engager, FDA-approved for HLA-A*02:01-positive metastatic uveal melanoma) and clinical trials involving [[immune checkpoint inhibitors]], [[MEK inhibitors]], and [[hepatic-directed therapies]] (e.g., [[immunoembolization]], [[percutaneous hepatic perfusion]]).

### Management of Choroidal Metastasis

- **Systemic chemotherapy** directed at the primary malignancy.
- **[[External beam radiation therapy (EBRT)]]** or **plaque brachytherapy** for local tumor control.
- **[[Intravitreal anti-VEGF]]** agents for associated exudative retinal detachment.
- **Observation** if the patient is already receiving effective systemic therapy.

### Surgical Interventions for Uveal Complications

- **[[Cataract extraction]]**: For visually significant [[posterior subcapsular cataract]] secondary to chronic uveitis or corticosteroid use; requires meticulous perioperative inflammation control (quiescence for ≥3 months recommended).
- **[[Glaucoma surgery]]**: [[Trabeculectomy]], [[glaucoma drainage device]] implantation, or [[minimally invasive glaucoma surgery (MIGS)]] for uveitic glaucoma refractory to medical therapy.
- **[[Pars plana vitrectomy (PPV)]]**: For vitreous opacification, epiretinal membrane, tractional retinal detachment, or diagnostic vitreous biopsy.
- **[[Scleral windows/sclerectomy]]**: For uveal effusion syndrome associated with abnormal scleral thickness.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of uveal disease is highly variable:

- **Acute anterior uveitis** (e.g., HLA-B27-associated): Generally favorable with prompt treatment; recurrences are common but typically responsive to therapy.
- **Chronic uveitis** (e.g., JIA-associated, sarcoidosis): Risk of cumulative structural damage (synechiae, cataract, glaucoma, band keratopathy, CME) leading to permanent vision loss.
- **Uveal melanoma**: 5-year mortality from metastatic disease ranges from ~1% (class 1A GEP) to ~50% (class 2 GEP with monosomy 3). Even after successful local tumor control, metastatic risk persists for decades.
- **Choroidal metastasis**: Prognosis is primarily determined by the systemic oncological status; median survival after diagnosis of choroidal metastasis is approximately 6–12 months depending on the primary tumor.

## Complications of Uveal Disease

| Complication | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **[[Secondary glaucoma]]** | Trabecular meshwork obstruction by inflammatory cells/debris, peripheral anterior synechiae, pupillary block from posterior synechiae, steroid response, neovascular glaucoma |
| **[[Cataract]]** | Chronic inflammation, posterior synechiae, corticosteroid use |
| **[[Cystoid macular edema]]** | Breakdown of blood-retinal barrier; most common cause of vision loss in uveitis |
| **[[Band keratopathy]]** | Calcium deposition in Bowman's layer; associated with chronic anterior uveitis (especially JIA) |
| **[[Epiretinal membrane]]** | Chronic vitreous inflammation and cellular proliferation |
| **[[Retinal detachment]]** | Exudative (from choroiditis, tumors) or tractional (from vitreoretinal proliferation) |
| **[[Phthisis bulbi]]** | End-stage chronic inflammation with ciliary body destruction, hypotony, and globe shrinkage |
| **[[Optic disc edema]]** | Peripapillary inflammation or elevated intracranial pressure in systemic disease |

## Special Considerations in Optometric Practice

Optometrists play a critical role in the detection, co-management, and long-term monitoring of uveal diseases:

1. **Early detection**: Recognition of anterior uveitis signs (ciliary flush, cells, flare, KPs) during routine examination.
2. **Referral**: Timely referral to [[uveitis specialists]] or [[ocular oncologists]] for posterior uveitis, recurrent/chronic uveitis, or suspected intraocular tumors.
3. **Monitoring**: Serial documentation of choroidal nevi with fundus photography, OCT, and autofluorescence to detect transformation to melanoma.
4. **Co-management**: Monitoring IOP in patients on topical or periocular corticosteroids; managing dry eye and ocular surface disease in patients on systemic immunosuppression.
5. **Patient education**: Counseling on the importance of adherence to immunosuppressive therapy, recognition of recurrence symptoms, and the need for systemic surveillance in uveal melanoma.
