# Van Herick Technique

---

The **Van Herick technique** (also referred to as the **Van Herick, Shaffer, and Schwartz method**) is a rapid, non-invasive, [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]-based clinical method used to estimate the depth of the peripheral [[anterior chamber angle]]. First described by Warwick Van Herick, Ross A. Shaffer, and Arnold Schwartz in 1969, this technique provides a semi-quantitative assessment of the [[anterior chamber]] depth at the limbus, serving as a critical screening tool for identifying eyes at risk of [[angle-closure glaucoma]]. It remains one of the most widely taught and clinically utilized preliminary angle assessment methods in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] practice worldwide, owing to its simplicity, speed, and lack of requirement for specialized contact lenses or additional equipment beyond a standard [[slit lamp]].

While [[gonioscopy]] remains the gold standard for direct visualization and grading of the [[iridocorneal angle]], the Van Herick technique occupies an indispensable role as a screening procedure that can be performed during routine slit-lamp examination, alerting the clinician to the need for more comprehensive angle evaluation. Its clinical significance has only grown with the increasing global prevalence of [[primary angle-closure glaucoma]] (PACG), particularly in populations of Asian descent where narrow angles are more common.

---

## Historical Background

The technique was formally introduced in the landmark 1969 publication by Van Herick, Shaffer, and Schwartz in the *American Journal of Ophthalmology*. The authors sought to develop a simple, rapid method for estimating the anterior chamber angle width that could be incorporated into routine slit-lamp examination without the need for a [[goniolens]] or topical anesthetic. Their original study correlated the peripheral anterior chamber depth (as estimated by their slit-lamp method) with [[gonioscopic]] angle grades, establishing the grading system that remains in clinical use today. The technique was conceived as a screening tool—not a replacement for gonioscopy—but one that could efficiently identify patients requiring further angle assessment.

Prior to the Van Herick technique, estimation of anterior chamber depth relied on methods such as the **Smith method** (penlight oblique illumination test), the **Scheimpflug photography** technique, or direct gonioscopy. The Van Herick method offered a significant advantage in that it could be performed in seconds during a standard slit-lamp examination, required no patient contact, and had a high sensitivity for detecting narrow angles when performed correctly.

## Anatomical and Pathophysiological Basis

### Relevant Anatomy

Understanding the Van Herick technique requires a thorough knowledge of the anatomy of the [[anterior segment]] of the eye. The key structures involved include:

- **[[Cornea]]**: The transparent, avascular anterior structure of the eye. The Van Herick technique utilizes the corneal thickness at the temporal (or nasal) limbus as the internal reference standard against which the peripheral anterior chamber depth is compared.
- **[[Anterior Chamber]]**: The fluid-filled space between the posterior surface of the cornea ([[corneal endothelium]]) and the anterior surface of the [[iris]]. The depth of this chamber varies from its deepest point centrally (typically 2.5–3.5 mm in emmetropic adults) to its shallowest point peripherally at the [[angle recess]].
- **[[Iridocorneal Angle]] (Anterior Chamber Angle)**: The junction where the peripheral iris meets the inner surface of the [[corneoscleral limbus]]. This angle houses the [[trabecular meshwork]], [[Schlemm's canal]], and other structures critical for [[aqueous humor]] outflow. The width of this angle determines the ease with which aqueous humor can access the drainage apparatus.
- **[[Limbus]]**: The transitional zone between the [[cornea]] and [[sclera]], which serves as the anatomical landmark for positioning the slit beam in the Van Herick technique.
- **[[Iris]]**: The pigmented, contractile diaphragm whose peripheral configuration (convex, flat, or concave) significantly influences the anterior chamber angle width.

### Pathophysiology of Narrow Angles

The Van Herick technique is fundamentally a screening tool for detecting **narrow anterior chamber angles**, which represent the anatomical predisposition to [[angle-closure glaucoma]]. The pathophysiology of angle narrowing involves several mechanisms:

1. **[[Pupillary Block]]**: The most common mechanism, in which the flow of [[aqueous humor]] from the [[posterior chamber]] to the anterior chamber is impeded at the pupillary margin due to apposition between the [[lens]] and the posterior surface of the iris. This creates a pressure differential that bows the peripheral iris anteriorly ([[iris bombé]]), narrowing or closing the angle.

2. **[[Plateau Iris Configuration]]**: An anatomical variant in which the [[ciliary body]] is anteriorly positioned, pushing the peripheral iris root forward and narrowing the angle despite a relatively deep central anterior chamber. This condition may not always be detected by the Van Herick technique because the central and even mid-peripheral chamber depth may appear normal.

3. **[[Lens]]-Related Factors**: With aging, the [[crystalline lens]] increases in anteroposterior thickness and may shift anteriorly, progressively shallowing the anterior chamber. This is the primary reason why the prevalence of narrow angles increases with age, particularly after the fifth decade of life. [[Intumescent cataract]], [[lens subluxation]], and [[phacomorphic glaucoma]] represent extreme examples of lens-related angle narrowing.

4. **Anatomical Predisposition**: Eyes that are [[hyperopic]] (shorter [[axial length]]), have a relatively large lens, a shallow anterior chamber, or a thick, anteriorly inserted iris root are at increased risk for narrow angles. These anatomical features are more prevalent in certain ethnic groups, particularly East Asian and Inuit populations.

The Van Herick technique detects the downstream consequence of these mechanisms—a shallow peripheral anterior chamber—rather than the mechanism itself. This is why a narrow Van Herick grade mandates further evaluation with [[gonioscopy]], [[anterior segment optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT), or [[ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM) to determine the underlying cause and the true angle configuration.

## Clinical Indications

The Van Herick technique is indicated in the following clinical scenarios:

| **Indication** | **Rationale** |
|---|---|
| Routine comprehensive eye examination | Screening for occult narrow angles in all patients, particularly those over age 40 |
| Pre-[[mydriasis]] assessment | To assess the risk of precipitating [[acute angle-closure crisis]] before pharmacological pupil dilation |
| [[Hyperopia]] | Hyperopic eyes have shorter axial lengths and shallower anterior chambers, increasing angle-closure risk |
| Family history of [[angle-closure glaucoma]] | Genetic predisposition to narrow angles |
| Symptoms suggestive of intermittent angle closure | Episodic blurred vision, halos around lights, brow ache, eye pain |
| Asian, Inuit, or other high-risk ethnic background | Higher prevalence of narrow angles and PACG |
| Suspected [[plateau iris]] or [[pupillary block]] | Initial screening before gonioscopy |
| Pre-operative assessment | Before [[cataract surgery]], [[refractive surgery]], or other anterior segment procedures |
| Monitoring of known narrow angles | Serial assessment over time to detect progressive shallowing |

## Technique and Procedure

### Equipment Required

- Standard [[slit-lamp biomicroscope]] with adjustable beam width, height, and angle
- No additional lenses, topical anesthetics, or coupling agents are required

### Step-by-Step Procedure

The Van Herick technique is performed as follows:

1. **Patient Positioning**: The patient is seated comfortably at the slit lamp with the chin on the chin rest and forehead against the headband. The patient is instructed to look straight ahead at the fixation target.

2. **Slit Beam Configuration**:
   - Set the slit beam to a **very narrow optic section** (approximately 0.2–0.3 mm wide).
   - Set the beam height to approximately **2–4 mm** (a short, narrow beam).
   - Set the beam to **maximum brightness** for optimal visualization.
   - The illumination arm is set at an angle of approximately **60 degrees temporally** from the microscope (observation) axis. Some clinicians use angles between 40° and 60°; the original description specified approximately 60°.

3. **Beam Placement**: Direct the narrow slit beam to strike the **temporal limbus** as perpendicularly to the corneal surface as possible. The beam should be positioned at the very edge of the limbus—at the junction of the clear [[cornea]] and the opaque [[sclera]]. The goal is to create an **optical section** of the peripheral cornea at the limbus.

4. **Observation**: Using the slit-lamp microscope (typically at 10×–16× magnification), the clinician observes the optical section from directly in front of the patient (along the microscope axis). Three distinct structures should be visible:
   - **Corneal optical section (bright band)**: The cross-sectional illumination of the peripheral cornea, appearing as a bright, well-defined parallelogram.
   - **Dark interval (shadow gap)**: The space between the posterior corneal surface and the anterior iris surface, representing the **peripheral anterior chamber depth** at the limbus.
   - **Iris surface (bright band)**: The illuminated anterior surface of the peripheral iris.

5. **Comparison and Grading**: The clinician compares the **width of the dark interval** (peripheral anterior chamber depth) to the **thickness of the corneal optical section** (corneal thickness at the limbus). This ratio forms the basis of the Van Herick grading system.

6. **Repeat on the Nasal Side** (recommended): Although the temporal limbus is most commonly assessed, many clinicians recommend repeating the assessment at the **nasal limbus** (with the illumination arm angled nasally at approximately 60°). The nasal angle is often narrower than the temporal angle, and assessing both sides provides a more complete picture. Some authorities recommend that the **narrowest grade** obtained should be the one recorded.

### Important Technical Considerations

- The beam must be placed precisely at the **limbus**, not on the central or mid-peripheral cornea, as the anterior chamber is deepest centrally and the technique specifically assesses the **peripheral** depth.
- The illumination angle should be sufficiently oblique (approximately 60°) to create a clear separation between the corneal section and the iris reflection. If the angle is too small, the dark interval may be artificially obscured.
- The patient must maintain **primary gaze**. If the patient looks toward the light source, the angle assessment may be inaccurate due to changes in iris position.
- The technique assesses only the **temporal and/or nasal** angles. It does not evaluate the **superior or inferior** angles, which may have different configurations (the superior angle is often the narrowest). This is a recognized limitation.

## Grading System

The Van Herick grading system classifies the peripheral anterior chamber depth into grades based on the ratio of the dark interval (peripheral anterior chamber depth) to the corneal optical section thickness:

| **Van Herick Grade** | **Peripheral AC Depth : Corneal Thickness Ratio** | **Estimated Angle Width** | **Clinical Interpretation** | **Risk of Angle Closure** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Grade 4** | ≥ 1:1 (dark interval ≥ corneal thickness) | 35°–45° (wide open) | Wide open angle; no risk | Negligible |
| **Grade 3** | 1:4 to 1:2 (dark interval is ¼ to ½ of corneal thickness) | 20°–35° (open) | Open angle; unlikely to close | Very low |
| **Grade 2** | 1:4 (dark interval is ¼ of corneal thickness) | ~20° (moderately narrow) | Narrow angle; possible risk | Low to moderate |
| **Grade 1** | < 1:4 (dark interval is less than ¼ of corneal thickness) | ~10° (very narrow) | Very narrow angle; significant risk | High |
| **Grade 0 (Slit)** | Slit-like or no visible dark interval | 0°–10° (closed or near-closed) | Angle closure likely present or imminent | Very high / Closed |

### Clinical Decision-Making Based on Grade

- **Grade 4 and Grade 3**: These grades are considered **safe for mydriasis** and are not associated with significant angle-closure risk. Routine gonioscopy may still be performed as part of a comprehensive glaucoma evaluation but is not urgently indicated based on the Van Herick grade alone.

- **Grade 2**: This grade represents a **borderline or cautionary** finding. [[Gonioscopy]] is **recommended** to directly assess the angle structures and determine the true angle width. Mydriasis should be performed with caution, and the clinician should consider using shorter-acting mydriatic agents (e.g., [[tropicamide]] 0.5% or 1%) rather than longer-acting agents (e.g., [[phenylephrine]] 10% combined with [[cyclopentolate]]). Post-dilation [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) checks and angle reassessment are advisable.

- **Grade 1**: This grade indicates a **significantly narrow angle** with a high risk of angle closure. **Gonioscopy is mandatory**. Mydriasis should be **avoided or performed with extreme caution** only if clinically essential, with informed consent, immediate availability of [[pilocarpine]] or other [[miotic]] agents, and close post-dilation monitoring. Referral for consideration of prophylactic [[laser peripheral iridotomy]] (LPI) may be appropriate.

- **Grade 0**: This grade suggests **appositional or synechial angle closure** may already be present. **Gonioscopy is urgently required**. Mydriasis is **contraindicated** unless performed under controlled conditions by a specialist. Immediate referral to a [[glaucoma]] specialist or [[ophthalmologist]] is indicated. Assessment of [[intraocular pressure]], [[optic nerve]] status, and consideration of emergent treatment (including LPI or medical therapy) is necessary.

## Correlation with Gonioscopy

The Van Herick technique has been extensively studied for its correlation with [[gonioscopy]], the gold standard for angle assessment. The original study by Van Herick, Shaffer, and Schwartz (1969) demonstrated a strong correlation between their limbal chamber depth estimation and Shaffer gonioscopic angle grades. Subsequent studies have confirmed the following general correlations:

| **Van Herick Grade** | **Approximate Shaffer Gonioscopic Grade** | **Visible Angle Structures on Gonioscopy** |
|---|---|---|
| Grade 4 | Shaffer Grade 4 (wide open, 35°–45°) | [[Ciliary body band]], [[scleral spur]], [[trabecular meshwork]], [[Schwalbe's line]] all visible |
| Grade 3 | Shaffer Grade 3 (open, 20°–35°) | Scleral spur visible; ciliary body band may not be visible |
| Grade 2 | Shaffer Grade 2 (moderately narrow, ~20°) | Only anterior trabecular meshwork and Schwalbe's line visible |
| Grade 1 | Shaffer Grade 1 (very narrow, ~10°) | Only Schwalbe's line visible; angle closure possible |
| Grade 0 | Shaffer Grade 0 (closed, 0°) | No angle structures visible; [[peripheral anterior synechiae]] may be present |

### Sensitivity and Specificity

Multiple studies have evaluated the diagnostic accuracy of the Van Herick technique:

- **Sensitivity** for detecting gonioscopically narrow angles (Shaffer Grade ≤ 2) ranges from approximately **61% to 84%** depending on the study population and the threshold used.
- **Specificity** ranges from approximately **70% to 94%**.
- The technique has a **high negative predictive value**, meaning that a wide Van Herick grade (Grade 3 or 4) reliably excludes a narrow angle.
- The **positive predictive value** is more variable, particularly in populations with a low prevalence of narrow angles, meaning that some patients graded as narrow by Van Herick may have open angles on gonioscopy.

These findings reinforce the role of the Van Herick technique as a **screening tool** rather than a definitive diagnostic test. A narrow Van Herick grade should always prompt gonioscopy for confirmation.

## Limitations

Despite its widespread use and clinical utility, the Van Herick technique has several well-recognized limitations:

1. **Assessment of Only Two Quadrants**: The technique evaluates only the temporal and nasal angles. The **superior and inferior angles**, which are often the narrowest (particularly the superior angle), are not assessed. This can lead to underestimation of angle-closure risk in eyes with asymmetric angle configurations.

2. **Peripheral, Not Direct, Assessment**: The technique estimates the angle width indirectly by measuring the peripheral anterior chamber depth at the limbus. It does not directly visualize the angle structures ([[trabecular meshwork]], [[scleral spur]], [[Schlemm's canal]], etc.) as gonioscopy does.

3. **Subjectivity**: The grading is inherently subjective and depends on the clinician's experience and judgment. Inter-observer variability has been documented, particularly for borderline grades (Grade 2 vs. Grade 3).

4. **Inability to Detect Plateau Iris**: In [[plateau iris configuration]], the central and peripheral anterior chamber may appear relatively deep, but the angle recess is narrow due to anteriorly positioned [[ciliary processes]]. The Van Herick technique may yield a falsely reassuring grade in these cases.

5. **Corneal Thickness Variability**: The technique uses corneal thickness as the reference standard. In patients with abnormally thick or thin corneas (e.g., post-[[LASIK]], [[keratoconus]], [[corneal edema]]), the ratio may be misleading.

6. **Cannot Detect [[Peripheral Anterior Synechiae]] (PAS)**: The technique cannot distinguish between appositional angle closure (reversible) and synechial angle closure (irreversible). Only gonioscopy (particularly [[indentation gonioscopy]]) can make this distinction.

7. **Not Suitable for Grading the Angle in Detail**: The technique provides a gross estimate of angle width but cannot characterize the angle anatomy in the detail required for surgical planning or for distinguishing between different mechanisms of angle closure.

8. **Influence of Ambient Lighting**: The technique should ideally be performed in a dimly lit room to avoid pupillary miosis, which can artificially open the angle and lead to overestimation of angle width.

## Comparison with Other Angle Assessment Methods

| **Method** | **Type** | **Advantages** | **Disadvantages** | **Relationship to Van Herick** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Van Herick Technique** | Non-contact, slit-lamp screening | Rapid, non-invasive, no special equipment | Indirect, subjective, limited to 2 quadrants | — |
| **[[Gonioscopy]]** | Direct contact lens examination | Gold standard; direct visualization of all 4 quadrants and angle structures | Requires goniolens, topical anesthetic, skill; contact with eye | Confirmatory test when Van Herick is narrow |
| **[[Anterior Segment OCT]] (AS-OCT)** | Non-contact imaging | Objective, quantitative, reproducible; images all quadrants | Expensive equipment; may overestimate narrow angles; cannot visualize posterior to iris | Complementary; increasingly used as adjunct |
| **[[Ultrasound Biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | Contact ultrasound imaging | Visualizes structures behind iris (ciliary body, zonules); detects plateau iris | Requires immersion technique; contact; operator-dependent | Used when plateau iris or posterior pathology suspected |
| **Smith's Penlight Test** | Non-contact, penlight screening | Very simple; no equipment needed | Very low sensitivity and specificity; largely obsolete | Van Herick is superior and has replaced this in most settings |
| **[[Scheimpflug Imaging]] (e.g., [[Pentacam]])** | Non-contact imaging | Quantitative anterior chamber depth and volume measurements | Does not directly image the angle recess; expensive | Complementary; provides central ACD measurement |

## Role in Pre-Mydriasis Assessment

One of the most critical clinical applications of the Van Herick technique is the **pre-mydriasis safety assessment**. Before instilling [[mydriatic]] agents (e.g., [[tropicamide]], [[phenylephrine]], [[cyclopentolate]]) for dilated fundus examination, the clinician must assess the risk of precipitating an [[acute angle-closure crisis]]. The Van Herick technique provides a rapid, reliable method for this assessment.

### Pre-Mydriasis Protocol

1. Perform Van Herick assessment at both temporal and nasal limbus.
2. Record the grade for each side; use the **narrower grade** for clinical decision-making.
3. Apply the following guidelines:

| **Van Herick Grade** | **Mydriasis Decision** | **Precautions** |
|---|---|---|
| Grade 3–4 | Safe to dilate | Standard dilation protocol |
| Grade 2 | Dilate with caution | Use short-acting agents; check IOP 30–60 minutes post-dilation; have [[pilocarpine]] available |
| Grade 1 | Avoid dilation or dilate only if essential | Perform gonioscopy first; if dilation essential, use tropicamide 0.5% only; close monitoring; informed consent |
| Grade 0 | Do not dilate | Urgent gonioscopy and referral; dilation contraindicated |

## Management Implications

While the Van Herick technique itself is a diagnostic/screening procedure and not a treatment, its findings directly guide clinical management:

### Pharmacological Considerations

- **[[Pilocarpine]]**: A [[miotic]] agent that constricts the pupil and pulls the peripheral iris away from the angle, opening it. Used as prophylaxis or treatment in angle-closure scenarios.
- **[[Timolol]]**, **[[Brimonidine]]**, **[[Dorzolamide]]**: [[Aqueous suppressants]] used to lower [[intraocular pressure]] in acute or chronic angle closure.
- **Oral or intravenous [[Acetazolamide]]** or **[[Mannitol]]**: Used in [[acute angle-closure crisis]] to rapidly lower IOP.

### Laser and Surgical Interventions

- **[[Laser Peripheral Iridotomy]] (LPI)**: The definitive prophylactic and therapeutic procedure for eyes with narrow angles due to [[pupillary block]]. Creates a full-thickness opening in the peripheral iris, equalizing pressure between the posterior and anterior chambers and relieving iris bombé. Indicated when gonioscopy confirms a narrow, occludable angle (typically Shaffer Grade ≤ 2 or Van Herick Grade ≤ 1–2 with gonioscopic confirmation).

- **[[Laser Iridoplasty]] (Argon Laser Peripheral Iridoplasty, ALPI)**: Used in cases of [[plateau iris syndrome]] where LPI alone is insufficient to open the angle. Laser burns are applied to the peripheral iris stroma, causing contraction and pulling the iris root away from the angle.

- **[[Cataract Extraction]] ([[Phacoemulsification]])**: In eyes with narrow angles and coexisting [[cataract]], lens removal deepens the anterior chamber and widens the angle. Increasingly recognized as a primary treatment for angle closure, particularly in the EAGLE study (Effectiveness of Early Lens Extraction for the Treatment of Primary Angle-Closure Glaucoma).

- **[[Trabeculectomy]]** or **[[Glaucoma Drainage Device]]**: Reserved for cases of [[angle-closure glaucoma]] with uncontrolled IOP despite LPI and medical therapy.

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Patients

The Van Herick technique can be performed in cooperative children but may be challenging in very young patients. Pediatric narrow angles are rare but can occur in conditions such as [[retinopathy of prematurity]], [[persistent fetal vasculature]], or [[nanophthalmos]].

### Elderly Patients

The prevalence of narrow angles increases significantly with age due to progressive lens thickening and anterior lens shift. The Van Herick technique is particularly important in elderly patients presenting for routine examination or cataract evaluation.

### Asian Populations

Epidemiological studies have consistently demonstrated a higher prevalence of narrow angles and [[primary angle-closure glaucoma]] in East Asian and Southeast Asian populations. The Van Herick technique is an essential screening tool in these populations, and clinicians should maintain a lower threshold for performing gonioscopy when borderline grades are obtained.

## Documentation

Proper documentation of the Van Herick assessment should include:

- **Date and time** of assessment
- **Eye(s) assessed** (OD, OS, or OU)
- **Quadrant(s) assessed** (temporal, nasal, or both)
- **Grade** for each quadrant assessed
- **Clinical decision** based on the grade (e.g., "safe for mydriasis," "gonioscopy recommended")

Example documentation:
> *Van Herick: OD temporal Grade 3, nasal Grade 3; OS temporal Grade 2, nasal Grade 1. Gonioscopy recommended OS prior to dilation.*

## Teaching and Competency

The Van Herick technique is a core competency in optometric and ophthalmic education. It is typically taught in the first or second year of optometry programs as part of the slit-lamp biomicroscopy curriculum. Key teaching points include:

- Proper slit-lamp setup and beam configuration
- Accurate identification of the limbus
- Correct interpretation of the corneal section, dark interval, and iris reflection
- Consistent application of the grading system
- Understanding of the technique's limitations and the indications for gonioscopy

Standardized training with photographic or video examples, grading exercises, and supervised clinical practice is recommended to minimize inter-observer variability and ensure competency.

## Recent Advances and Future Directions

While the Van Herick technique has remained largely unchanged since its original description in 1969, several developments are relevant:

1. **Digital Slit-Lamp Photography**: Modern slit lamps equipped with digital cameras allow photographic documentation of the Van Herick assessment, facilitating telemedicine consultations, teaching, and longitudinal comparison.

2. **Artificial Intelligence (AI)**: Emerging research is exploring the use of AI algorithms to automatically grade Van Herick images captured by digital slit lamps, potentially reducing subjectivity and inter-observer variability.

3. **Integration with AS-OCT**: Some clinicians advocate combining the Van Herick technique with [[anterior segment OCT]] for a more comprehensive, objective angle assessment, particularly in screening programs for angle-closure glaucoma.

4. **Smartphone-Based Slit-Lamp Adapters**: Low-cost smartphone adapters for slit lamps may enable photographic Van Herick assessment in resource-limited settings, expanding access to angle screening in underserved populations.

---

## Summary

The Van Herick technique remains an indispensable clinical tool in optometric and ophthalmic practice for the rapid, non-invasive screening of anterior chamber angle width. Its simplicity, speed, and integration into routine slit-lamp examination make it an ideal first-line assessment for identifying patients at risk of [[angle-closure glaucoma]]. However, clinicians must be aware of its limitations—particularly its inability to assess all four quadrants, its subjectivity, and its indirect nature—and must use [[gonioscopy]] as the definitive confirmatory test whenever a narrow angle is suspected. Proper training, consistent technique, and appropriate clinical decision-making based on the Van Herick grade are essential for optimizing patient safety and outcomes.
