

# Fibrillin

---

**Fibrillin** is a large, cysteine-rich [[glycoprotein]] that serves as the principal structural component of [[extracellular matrix]] [[microfibrils]] in virtually all connective tissues throughout the human body. In the context of [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]], fibrillin is of paramount clinical significance because of its critical role in the structural integrity of the [[zonular fibers]] (also known as the [[zonules of Zinn]]) that suspend the [[crystalline lens]], as well as its contributions to the [[sclera]], [[cornea]], [[ciliary body]], and other ocular connective tissues. Mutations in the genes encoding fibrillin — most notably *FBN1* and *FBN2* — are responsible for a spectrum of heritable connective tissue disorders with profound ocular manifestations, the most clinically significant of which is [[Marfan syndrome]].

Understanding fibrillin at the molecular, histological, and clinical level is essential for eye care professionals, as fibrillin-related pathology frequently presents first in the eye and may be the initial diagnostic clue to a life-threatening systemic condition.

---

## Molecular Biology and Biochemistry

### Gene Structure

Fibrillin exists in three known isoforms encoded by three distinct genes:

| Isoform | Gene | Chromosomal Locus | Primary Clinical Association |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Fibrillin-1** | *FBN1* | 15q21.1 | [[Marfan syndrome]], [[ectopia lentis]] |
| **Fibrillin-2** | *FBN2* | 5q23.3 | [[Congenital contractural arachnodactyly]] (Beals syndrome) |
| **Fibrillin-3** | *FBN3* | 19p13.2 | Under investigation; possible role in [[polycystic ovary syndrome]] |

Of these, **fibrillin-1** is by far the most clinically relevant isoform in ophthalmic practice. The *FBN1* gene is one of the largest human genes, spanning approximately 235 kilobases of genomic DNA and containing 65 exons. It encodes a ~350 kDa profibrillin polypeptide that is proteolytically processed into the mature ~320 kDa fibrillin-1 monomer.

### Protein Structure

The fibrillin-1 monomer is a modular protein composed predominantly of:

- **47 epidermal growth factor-like (EGF-like) domains**, of which 43 are calcium-binding (cb-EGF domains). These calcium-binding domains are critical for maintaining the rigid, rod-like conformation of the molecule and for resistance to [[proteolysis]].
- **7 transforming growth factor-β binding protein-like (TB) domains**, which are unique to fibrillins and [[latent TGF-β binding proteins]] (LTBPs).
- **Hybrid domains** that share features of both EGF-like and TB domains.
- An **N-terminal** and **C-terminal** domain involved in multimerization and processing.

The abundance of cysteine residues (approximately 14% of total amino acids) allows for extensive intramolecular [[disulfide bonding]], which is essential for proper protein folding and structural stability.

### Microfibril Assembly

Fibrillin-1 monomers undergo a complex, hierarchical assembly process:

1. **Intracellular processing**: Profibrillin is synthesized in the [[rough endoplasmic reticulum]], undergoes [[glycosylation]], and is secreted into the extracellular space.
2. **Extracellular polymerization**: Monomers self-assemble in a head-to-tail fashion, forming beaded microfibrils with a characteristic periodicity of approximately 56 nm as observed by [[electron microscopy]].
3. **Cross-linking and stabilization**: [[Transglutaminase]]-mediated cross-links and disulfide bonds stabilize the mature microfibrillar scaffold.
4. **Interaction with other matrix components**: Microfibrils interact with [[elastin]], [[fibulins]], [[microfibril-associated glycoproteins]] (MAGPs), and [[proteoglycans]] to form the complete elastic fiber system or, in tissues such as the [[zonular fibers]], exist as elastin-free microfibrillar bundles.

### Functional Roles

Fibrillin-containing microfibrils serve multiple critical functions:

- **Structural scaffolding**: Providing tensile strength and elasticity to connective tissues.
- **TGF-β signaling regulation**: Fibrillin-1 sequesters [[latent TGF-β]] complexes in the extracellular matrix, thereby regulating the bioavailability of [[transforming growth factor-beta]] (TGF-β). Disruption of this regulatory function is now understood to be a central mechanism in the pathogenesis of fibrillin-related diseases.
- **BMP signaling modulation**: Fibrillin also modulates [[bone morphogenetic protein]] (BMP) signaling pathways.
- **Mechanotransduction**: Microfibrils transmit mechanical forces across tissues and may influence cellular behavior through integrin-mediated signaling.

## Ocular Distribution and Function

Fibrillin-containing microfibrils are widely distributed throughout the eye and are essential for the structural integrity and biomechanical function of multiple ocular tissues.

### Zonular Fibers (Zonules of Zinn)

The [[zonular fibers]] represent the most clinically significant fibrillin-rich structure in the eye. These fibers are composed almost entirely of fibrillin-1 microfibrils (with minimal or no elastin component) and are responsible for:

- Suspending the [[crystalline lens]] within the [[posterior chamber]].
- Transmitting the force of [[ciliary muscle]] contraction to the lens capsule during [[accommodation]].
- Maintaining the position of the lens relative to the [[optical axis]].

The zonules originate from the non-pigmented epithelium of the [[ciliary body]] (specifically the [[pars plana]] and [[pars plicata]]) and insert into the [[lens capsule]] at the equatorial region, both anteriorly and posteriorly. The zonular apparatus is organized into three groups:

| Zonular Group | Origin | Insertion |
|---|---|---|
| **Anterior zonules** | Pars plicata of ciliary body | Anterior lens capsule |
| **Equatorial zonules** | Valleys between ciliary processes | Lens equator |
| **Posterior zonules** | Pars plana of ciliary body | Posterior lens capsule |

Because the zonules are composed predominantly of fibrillin-1, they are exquisitely sensitive to mutations in *FBN1*. Defective fibrillin-1 leads to weakened, fragmented, or absent zonular fibers, resulting in [[ectopia lentis]] (lens subluxation or dislocation) — the hallmark ocular finding in [[Marfan syndrome]].

### Sclera and Cornea

Fibrillin microfibrils are present in the [[sclera]] and [[corneal stroma]], where they contribute to the structural framework of these tissues. In the cornea, microfibrils are found in [[Descemet's membrane]] and throughout the stroma, contributing to corneal biomechanical properties. Fibrillin abnormalities may contribute to:

- [[Cornea plana]] (in some fibrillin-2-related conditions)
- Altered [[corneal curvature]] and reduced [[central corneal thickness]] (observed in Marfan syndrome patients)
- [[Megalocornea]] in some fibrillinopathies

### Ciliary Body and Trabecular Meshwork

Fibrillin microfibrils are integral components of the [[ciliary body]] stroma and the [[trabecular meshwork]]. Their presence in the trabecular meshwork suggests a potential role in [[aqueous humor]] outflow regulation, and fibrillin dysfunction may contribute to the increased incidence of [[glaucoma]] observed in some fibrillinopathies.

### Vitreous Body

Fibrillin microfibrils have been identified in the [[vitreous body]], where they may contribute to the gel-like structural framework alongside [[collagen type II]], [[collagen type IX]], and [[hyaluronic acid]].

### Retinal and Choroidal Vasculature

As a component of the elastic laminae of blood vessels, fibrillin is present in the walls of the [[retinal vasculature]], [[choroidal vasculature]], and the [[ophthalmic artery]]. Fibrillin dysfunction may therefore have implications for vascular integrity within the eye.

## Pathophysiology of Fibrillin-Related Ocular Disease

### Dominant-Negative and Haploinsufficiency Mechanisms

Over 3,000 mutations in *FBN1* have been identified to date. The pathophysiology of fibrillin-1-related disease involves two principal mechanisms:

1. **Dominant-negative effect**: Mutant fibrillin-1 monomers are incorporated into microfibrils but disrupt the overall structure, leading to weakened, dysfunctional microfibrils. This is the predominant mechanism in classic [[Marfan syndrome]].

2. **Haploinsufficiency**: Reduced total quantity of normal fibrillin-1 due to nonsense mutations, frameshifts, or mRNA instability. This mechanism may produce milder phenotypes or contribute to the variable expressivity seen in fibrillinopathies.

### TGF-β Dysregulation

A paradigm shift in understanding fibrillin pathophysiology occurred with the discovery that fibrillin-1 deficiency leads to excessive [[TGF-β signaling]]. In the normal state, fibrillin-1 microfibrils sequester the [[large latent complex]] (LLC) of TGF-β in the extracellular matrix, keeping TGF-β in an inactive form. When fibrillin-1 is deficient or dysfunctional:

- Latent TGF-β is released prematurely from the matrix.
- Excessive active TGF-β drives pathological signaling through [[SMAD2/3]] and non-canonical pathways.
- This results in matrix metalloproteinase (MMP) upregulation, further matrix degradation, altered cell proliferation, and tissue remodeling.

In the eye, TGF-β dysregulation may contribute to:

- Progressive zonular fiber degradation and [[ectopia lentis]].
- Altered scleral and corneal remodeling leading to [[myopia]] and reduced corneal thickness.
- Potential contributions to [[retinal detachment]] risk through vitreous and retinal matrix changes.

### Genotype-Phenotype Correlations in Ocular Disease

| Mutation Type | Typical Ocular Phenotype | Severity |
|---|---|---|
| Cysteine substitution in cb-EGF domain | Classic ectopia lentis, often bilateral and superotemporal | Moderate to severe |
| Premature termination codon (haploinsufficiency) | Variable lens subluxation, may be milder | Mild to moderate |
| In-frame deletion/insertion in TB domain | Severe ectopia lentis, may present in childhood | Severe |
| Mutations in neonatal region (exons 24–32) | [[Neonatal Marfan syndrome]] with severe ocular involvement | Very severe |
| *FBN1* mutations causing isolated ectopia lentis | Lens subluxation without significant systemic features | Variable |

## Clinical Presentation and Ocular Symptoms

### Ectopia Lentis

[[Ectopia lentis]] is the cardinal ocular manifestation of fibrillin-1 dysfunction and is present in approximately **60–80%** of patients with [[Marfan syndrome]]. Key clinical features include:

- **Direction of subluxation**: Characteristically **superotemporal** in Marfan syndrome (in contrast to the inferonasal subluxation seen in [[homocystinuria]]).
- **Bilaterality**: Usually bilateral, though asymmetric involvement is common.
- **Progression**: Typically progressive, with increasing lens displacement over time due to ongoing zonular fiber degradation.
- **Age of onset**: May be present at birth or develop during childhood/adolescence. Lens subluxation may not be clinically apparent until the second or third decade of life in milder cases.

#### Visual Consequences of Ectopia Lentis

- **[[Lenticular astigmatism]]**: Irregular astigmatism from tilting of the subluxated lens.
- **[[Monocular diplopia]]**: The edge of the displaced lens may bisect the [[pupil]], creating a dual optical system (phakic and aphakic zones).
- **[[High myopia]]**: Due to increased lenticular curvature from relaxed zonules (lens spherophakia) or axial elongation.
- **[[Aphakic refractive error]]**: If the lens is completely dislocated out of the visual axis.
- **[[Amblyopia]]**: Particularly if ectopia lentis is present in early childhood and uncorrected.

### Myopia

[[Myopia]], often of high degree, is extremely common in fibrillin-related disorders and may result from:

- Increased [[axial length]] due to scleral weakening and elongation.
- Increased lenticular curvature (microspherophakia/spherophakia) from zonular laxity.
- Anterior displacement of the lens-iris diaphragm.

Myopia in Marfan syndrome is frequently progressive and may exceed -10.00 diopters.

### Flat Cornea

Reduced [[corneal curvature]] (flat cornea, or reduced [[keratometry]] readings) is a recognized feature of Marfan syndrome and is included in the revised [[Ghent nosology]] as a systemic feature. Mean keratometry values in Marfan patients are significantly lower than age-matched controls. Additionally, [[central corneal thickness]] is often reduced.

### Retinal Detachment

Patients with fibrillin-related disorders have an increased risk of [[retinal detachment]], estimated at **5–10 times** the general population risk. Contributing factors include:

- High axial myopia with associated [[lattice degeneration]] and peripheral retinal thinning.
- Vitreous abnormalities related to fibrillin microfibril dysfunction.
- Increased risk following [[lens extraction]] surgery.

### Glaucoma

Both [[open-angle glaucoma]] and [[angle-closure glaucoma]] may occur in fibrillinopathies:

- **Open-angle glaucoma**: Possibly related to trabecular meshwork microfibril dysfunction.
- **Angle-closure glaucoma**: May result from anterior displacement of the lens-iris diaphragm due to zonular laxity, leading to [[pupillary block]].
- **Secondary glaucoma**: Following lens dislocation into the [[anterior chamber]] or after surgical intervention.

### Other Ocular Findings

- **[[Iridodonesis]]**: Tremulousness of the [[iris]] due to lack of lens support, a clinical sign of zonular weakness.
- **[[Phacodonesis]]**: Tremulousness of the lens itself.
- **[[Increased axial length]]**: Measurable by [[A-scan ultrasonography]] or [[optical biometry]].
- **[[Iris transillumination defects]]**: Occasionally reported.
- **[[Strabismus]]**: May occur secondary to high refractive error or amblyopia.
- **[[Cataract]]**: Early-onset cataract formation may occur, possibly related to abnormal lens capsule-zonular interactions.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

The diagnosis of fibrillin-related ocular disease requires a comprehensive ophthalmic evaluation:

| Examination Technique | Findings |
|---|---|
| **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]** | Lens subluxation, iridodonesis, phacodonesis, zonular fiber visualization, anterior chamber depth assessment |
| **Dilated [[slit-lamp examination]]** | Lens edge visualization, direction and degree of subluxation, zonular fiber integrity |
| **[[Refraction]]** | High myopia, lenticular astigmatism, anisometropia |
| **[[Keratometry]]/[[Corneal topography]]** | Flat cornea (reduced K readings), irregular astigmatism |
| **[[A-scan ultrasonography]]** | Increased axial length |
| **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | Zonular fiber visualization, ciliary body assessment, anterior segment anatomy |
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)** | Retinal assessment, macular evaluation |
| **[[Gonioscopy]]** | Angle assessment for glaucoma risk |
| **[[Dilated fundus examination]]** | Peripheral retinal evaluation for lattice degeneration, retinal holes/tears, retinal detachment |
| **[[Intraocular pressure]] measurement** | Glaucoma screening (note: reduced CCT may lead to underestimation with [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]]) |

### Systemic Diagnostic Criteria: Revised Ghent Nosology (2010)

The diagnosis of [[Marfan syndrome]] — the most common fibrillinopathy — is based on the **revised Ghent nosology**, which emphasizes two cardinal features:

1. **[[Aortic root dilation]]/dissection** (Z-score ≥ 2)
2. **[[Ectopia lentis]]**

In the presence of both features, the diagnosis of Marfan syndrome is confirmed regardless of family history. In the absence of one feature, a **systemic score** ≥ 7 and/or identification of a pathogenic *FBN1* mutation is required.

**Ocular criteria contributing to the Ghent systemic score:**

| Feature | Points |
|---|---|
| Ectopia lentis | Major criterion (sufficient with aortic root dilation for diagnosis) |
| Myopia > 3 diopters | 1 point toward systemic score |
| Flat cornea (K < 43D) | 1 point toward systemic score |

### Genetic Testing

[[Molecular genetic testing]] for *FBN1* mutations is an important diagnostic tool:

- **Sanger sequencing** and **next-generation sequencing (NGS)** panels can identify point mutations, small insertions/deletions.
- **Multiplex ligation-dependent probe amplification (MLPA)** detects larger deletions/duplications.
- Genetic testing is particularly valuable in:
  - Patients with isolated ectopia lentis to determine if *FBN1* is causative.
  - Distinguishing Marfan syndrome from other conditions in the differential diagnosis.
  - Prenatal and preimplantation genetic diagnosis in affected families.
  - Guiding cardiovascular surveillance intensity.

### Differential Diagnosis of Fibrillin-Related Ectopia Lentis

| Condition | Gene | Direction of Subluxation | Key Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Marfan syndrome]]** | *FBN1* | Superotemporal | Aortic root dilation, skeletal features, positive family history |
| **[[Homocystinuria]]** | *CBS* | Inferonasal | Intellectual disability, thromboembolic events, elevated homocysteine |
| **[[Weill-Marchesani syndrome]]** | *FBN1*, *ADAMTS10*, *ADAMTS17*, *LTBP2* | Variable (often anterior) | Short stature, brachydactyly, microspherophakia |
| **[[Ehlers-Danlos syndrome]] (kyphoscoliotic type)** | *PLOD1* | Variable | Skin hyperextensibility, joint hypermobility, scoliosis |
| **[[Sulfite oxidase deficiency]]** | *SUOX* | Variable | Seizures, intellectual disability, elevated sulfite |
| **[[Isolated ectopia lentis]]** | *FBN1*, *ADAMTSL4* | Variable | No significant systemic features |
| **Trauma** | N/A | Variable | History of ocular trauma |

## Management and Treatment

### Optical Correction

Management of refractive error in fibrillin-related ectopia lentis is often challenging and requires individualized approaches:

- **[[Spectacle correction]]**: Appropriate for mild subluxation where the lens remains in the visual axis. High myopic corrections with astigmatic correction may be required. Frequent refraction updates are necessary due to progressive nature.
- **[[Contact lenses]]**: [[Rigid gas permeable]] (RGP) lenses may provide superior optical correction for irregular astigmatism. [[Scleral lenses]] may be considered in complex cases.
- **Aphakic correction**: If the lens is completely dislocated from the visual axis, aphakic spectacles or contact lenses may be prescribed.
- **[[Pinhole effect]]**: In cases of monocular diplopia from a bisected pupil, [[miotic agents]] (e.g., [[pilocarpine]]) may be used to constrict the pupil and eliminate the aphakic portion of the visual axis, though this approach has significant limitations.

### Pharmacological Interventions

#### Miotic Therapy

- **[[Pilocarpine]]** (1–2%): May be used to constrict the pupil in cases where the lens edge bisects the pupillary aperture, reducing monocular diplopia and improving visual quality. However, miotics may exacerbate angle-closure risk in some configurations and may induce accommodative spasm.

#### Cycloplegic/Mydriatic Caution

- **Mydriatic agents** should be used with caution in patients with known zonular weakness, as pupillary dilation may allow a subluxated lens to dislocate anteriorly into the [[anterior chamber]], precipitating acute [[pupillary block glaucoma]]. If dilation is necessary for examination, the patient should be monitored and [[pilocarpine]] should be available for reversal.

#### Glaucoma Management

- [[Intraocular pressure]]-lowering medications ([[beta-blockers]], [[prostaglandin analogs]], [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]], [[alpha-agonists]]) as indicated.
- Avoidance of [[miotics]] in cases where they may worsen pupillary block.
- [[Laser peripheral iridotomy]] (LPI) may be considered for angle-closure risk.

#### Systemic Pharmacotherapy (Relevant to Ocular Outcomes)

Emerging evidence supports the use of systemic medications that target the underlying TGF-β dysregulation in fibrillinopathies:

- **[[Losartan]]** (angiotensin II receptor blocker): Has been shown in animal models and some clinical studies to reduce TGF-β signaling and slow aortic root dilation. Its potential to slow ocular disease progression (e.g., progressive ectopia lentis, axial elongation) is under investigation.
- **[[Beta-blockers]]** (e.g., [[atenolol]]): Standard of care for aortic root protection; may have indirect benefits on ocular hemodynamics.
- **[[Doxycycline]]**: As a non-specific MMP inhibitor, has been explored experimentally for its potential to reduce matrix degradation in fibrillin-deficient tissues.

### Surgical Management

#### Indications for Lens Surgery

Surgical intervention for ectopia lentis is considered when:

- Best-corrected visual acuity cannot be adequately achieved with optical correction.
- The lens is dislocated into the anterior chamber or vitreous cavity.
- Lens-related complications occur (e.g., pupillary block glaucoma, [[phacomorphic glaucoma]], [[uveitis]]).
- Progressive subluxation threatens visual function.

#### Surgical Techniques

| Technique | Description | Considerations |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Lensectomy]] via [[pars plana vitrectomy]]** | Removal of the subluxated/dislocated lens via a posterior approach with vitrectomy instrumentation | Often preferred for posteriorly dislocated lenses; requires vitreoretinal surgical expertise |
| **[[Intracapsular cataract extraction]] (ICCE)** | Removal of the entire lens with capsule | Historically used; largely replaced by modern techniques |
| **[[Phacoemulsification]] with capsular tension ring (CTR)** | Standard cataract surgery technique with a [[capsular tension ring]] or [[modified CTR]] (e.g., [[Cionni ring]]) to redistribute zonular forces | Feasible in mild-moderate subluxation with sufficient residual zonular support |
| **[[Iris-fixated IOL]]** | [[Intraocular lens]] fixated to the iris (e.g., [[Artisan/Verisyse]] lens) | Option when capsular support is insufficient |
| **[[Scleral-fixated IOL]]** | IOL sutured or glued to the scleral wall | Provides stable IOL fixation in the absence of capsular support; techniques include [[Gore-Tex suture fixation]] and [[Yamane technique]] (flanged intrascleral fixation) |
| **Aphakia with contact lens correction** | Lens removal without IOL implantation | May be preferred in young children to allow for growth and refractive changes |

#### Surgical Considerations Specific to Fibrillinopathies

- **Zonular fragility**: Surgeons must anticipate extreme zonular weakness. Capsular tension rings, iris retractors, and gentle hydrodissection techniques are essential.
- **Capsular integrity**: The lens capsule may be thinner or more fragile than normal.
- **Increased risk of [[retinal detachment]]**: Post-surgical retinal detachment risk is elevated; thorough peripheral retinal examination and prophylactic [[laser retinopexy]] should be considered.
- **Anesthetic considerations**: Patients with Marfan syndrome may have cardiovascular complications requiring coordination with [[anesthesiology]] and [[cardiology]].
- **Long-term follow-up**: IOL decentration, late dislocation, and secondary glaucoma require lifelong monitoring.

### Retinal Detachment Management

- Prophylactic [[laser photocoagulation]] or [[cryopexy]] for lattice degeneration and retinal breaks.
- Prompt surgical repair ([[scleral buckle]], [[pars plana vitrectomy]], [[pneumatic retinopexy]]) for retinal detachment.
- Patient education regarding symptoms of retinal detachment (flashes, floaters, visual field curtain).

## Monitoring and Follow-Up

Patients with known or suspected fibrillin-related ocular disease require structured, long-term ophthalmic surveillance:

| Age Group | Recommended Frequency | Key Assessments |
|---|---|---|
| **Infants/Children (0–5 years)** | Every 6 months | Refraction (cycloplegic), lens position, amblyopia screening, axial length |
| **Children/Adolescents (5–18 years)** | Every 6–12 months | Refraction, lens position, IOP, dilated fundus exam, axial length |
| **Adults** | Annually (more frequently if progressive) | Refraction, lens position, IOP, gonioscopy, dilated fundus exam, OCT |
| **Post-surgical patients** | Per surgical protocol, then annually | IOL position, IOP, retinal status, visual acuity |

## Multidisciplinary Care

The management of patients with fibrillin-related disorders requires a coordinated multidisciplinary approach:

- **[[Optometrist]]/[[Ophthalmologist]]**: Primary ocular care, refractive management, surgical referral.
- **[[Cardiologist]]**: Aortic root monitoring, cardiovascular risk management (echocardiography, beta-blocker/losartan therapy).
- **[[Medical geneticist]]/[[Genetic counselor]]**: Genetic testing, family counseling, reproductive planning.
- **[[Orthopedic surgeon]]**: Skeletal manifestations (scoliosis, pectus deformity).
- **[[Primary care physician]]**: Coordination of care, systemic monitoring.

Eye care professionals play a crucial role in the initial identification of fibrillinopathies, as ectopia lentis or unexplained high myopia in a young patient may be the presenting sign that triggers systemic evaluation and potentially life-saving cardiovascular surveillance.

## Emerging Research and Future Directions

### Gene Therapy

Preclinical research is exploring [[gene therapy]] approaches to restore normal fibrillin-1 expression in affected tissues. Adeno-associated virus (AAV)-mediated delivery of functional *FBN1* to the ciliary epithelium could theoretically restore zonular fiber integrity, though significant challenges remain regarding the large size of the *FBN1* coding sequence (~8.6 kb) relative to AAV packaging capacity.

### Anti-TGF-β Therapies

Beyond losartan, more targeted inhibitors of the TGF-β signaling pathway are under investigation:

- **[[Fresolimumab]]** (anti-TGF-β antibody): Investigated in other fibrotic conditions; potential application in fibrillinopathies.
- **[[SMAD]] pathway modulators**: Small molecule inhibitors targeting downstream TGF-β signaling.

### Bioengineered Zonular Fibers

Research into synthetic or bioengineered zonular fiber replacements using recombinant fibrillin or fibrillin-mimetic polymers represents a long-term goal for restoring lens suspension in patients with severe zonular deficiency.

### Advanced Imaging

- **[[Anterior segment OCT]]** and **[[ultrasound biomicroscopy]]** continue to improve in resolution, allowing for more detailed assessment of zonular fiber integrity and early detection of subluxation.
- **[[Adaptive optics]]** imaging may enable visualization of microfibrillar structures in vivo in the future.

### Pharmacological Zonular Stabilization

Investigation into agents that could stabilize or strengthen existing zonular fibers — for example, [[collagen cross-linking]] agents adapted for microfibrillar structures — represents an area of active interest.

## Summary

Fibrillin, particularly fibrillin-1 encoded by the *FBN1* gene, is a fundamental structural glycoprotein of the extracellular matrix with critical importance in ocular anatomy and physiology. Its role as the primary component of the [[zonular fibers]] makes it indispensable for lens suspension and accommodation. Mutations in *FBN1* lead to a spectrum of fibrillinopathies — most notably [[Marfan syndrome]] — characterized by [[ectopia lentis]], [[high myopia]], [[flat cornea]], increased risk of [[retinal detachment]], and [[glaucoma]]. The discovery that fibrillin-1 regulates [[TGF-β]] bioavailability has transformed our understanding of disease pathogenesis and opened new therapeutic avenues. Eye care professionals must maintain a high index of suspicion for fibrillin-related disease, as ocular findings frequently precede and may prompt the diagnosis of potentially life-threatening systemic conditions. Comprehensive, lifelong ophthalmic monitoring and multidisciplinary collaboration are essential for optimizing outcomes in affected patients.
