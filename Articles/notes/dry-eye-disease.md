

# Dry Eye Disease

---

**Dry Eye Disease** (DED), also referred to as **[[Keratoconjunctivitis Sicca]]** (KCS), **dysfunctional tear syndrome**, or **ocular surface disease**, is a multifactorial disease of the [[ocular surface]] characterized by a loss of homeostasis of the [[tear film]], accompanied by ocular symptoms, in which tear film instability and hyperosmolarity, [[ocular surface]] inflammation and damage, and neurosensory abnormalities play etiological roles. It is one of the most frequently encountered conditions in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] clinical practice, affecting an estimated 5–50% of the global population depending on diagnostic criteria, geographic region, and demographic studied. DED represents a significant public health burden, impairing visual function, quality of life, workplace productivity, and psychological well-being.

The Tear Film & Ocular Surface Society (TFOS) Dry Eye Workshop II (DEWS II) report, published in 2017, established the current consensus definition and classification framework that guides contemporary clinical understanding and management of this complex disease.

---

## Epidemiology

The prevalence of Dry Eye Disease varies widely across studies due to differences in diagnostic criteria, survey instruments, and populations assessed. Large epidemiological studies have consistently demonstrated the following trends:

| **Factor** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| **Global Prevalence** | Ranges from 5% to 50%; symptomatic DED estimated at 5–15% in most Western populations |
| **Sex Predilection** | Females are disproportionately affected, with prevalence approximately 1.5–2× higher than males, attributed to hormonal influences ([[androgen]] deficiency, [[estrogen]] fluctuations) |
| **Age** | Prevalence increases significantly with age, particularly after age 50; however, increasing rates are observed in younger populations due to digital device use |
| **Geographic Variation** | Higher prevalence reported in Asian populations (e.g., studies from Japan, China, South Korea, Taiwan report rates of 20–52%) compared to Caucasian populations |
| **Environmental Factors** | Increased prevalence in arid climates, high-altitude regions, areas with high air pollution, and low-humidity indoor environments |
| **Digital Device Use** | Prolonged screen time is a significant modern risk factor, associated with reduced [[blink rate]] and incomplete blinks |

Key epidemiological studies include the **Beaver Dam Eye Study**, the **Women's Health Study**, the **Salisbury Eye Evaluation**, and the **Osaka Study**, all of which have contributed substantially to our understanding of DED prevalence and risk factors.

## Classification

The TFOS DEWS II classification system categorizes DED into two principal subtypes based on the predominant pathophysiological mechanism, though significant overlap exists:

### 1. Aqueous-Deficient Dry Eye (ADDE)

Aqueous-deficient dry eye results from reduced [[lacrimal gland]] secretion of the aqueous component of the [[tear film]]. This subtype is further divided into:

- **[[Sjögren Syndrome]]-Associated Dry Eye (SSDE):** An [[autoimmune]] exocrinopathy characterized by lymphocytic infiltration and destruction of the [[lacrimal gland]] and [[salivary gland]] tissue. It may be primary (isolated) or secondary (associated with other systemic autoimmune diseases such as [[rheumatoid arthritis]], [[systemic lupus erythematosus]], or [[systemic sclerosis]]).
- **Non-Sjögren Syndrome Aqueous-Deficient Dry Eye:** Encompasses lacrimal gland insufficiency from causes other than Sjögren syndrome, including age-related lacrimal gland dysfunction, congenital alacrima, lacrimal gland infiltration (e.g., [[sarcoidosis]], [[lymphoma]], [[graft-versus-host disease]]), lacrimal gland denervation, and systemic medication effects.

### 2. Evaporative Dry Eye (EDE)

Evaporative dry eye is the more prevalent subtype, accounting for the majority of DED cases (estimated at up to 86% of cases in some studies). It results from excessive evaporation of the tear film from the [[ocular surface]] despite normal lacrimal secretory function. Causes include:

- **Intrinsic Causes:**
  - **[[Meibomian Gland Dysfunction]] (MGD):** The single most common cause of evaporative dry eye. MGD involves terminal duct obstruction and/or qualitative/quantitative changes in [[meibum]] secretion, leading to deficiency of the [[lipid layer]] of the tear film.
  - Disorders of lid aperture and lid dynamics (e.g., incomplete blink, reduced blink rate)
  - Low [[blink rate]] associated with concentrated visual tasks (e.g., computer use, reading, driving)
  - [[Conjunctivochalasis]]

- **Extrinsic Causes:**
  - [[Contact lens]] wear
  - [[Ocular surface]] disease related to topical medication preservatives (e.g., [[benzalkonium chloride]])
  - [[Allergic conjunctivitis]]
  - Vitamin A deficiency

### Mixed Mechanism Dry Eye

In clinical practice, the majority of patients present with features of both aqueous deficiency and evaporative dysfunction. The TFOS DEWS II framework acknowledges this overlap and encourages clinicians to identify the predominant mechanism to guide targeted therapy.

## Anatomy and Physiology of the Tear Film

A thorough understanding of the [[tear film]] is essential for comprehending DED pathophysiology. The tear film is a complex, dynamic fluid structure approximately 3–4 μm thick that covers the [[ocular surface]], providing optical, protective, nutritive, and lubricating functions.

### Traditional Three-Layer Model

Historically, the tear film was described as consisting of three distinct layers:

| **Layer** | **Thickness** | **Source** | **Function** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Lipid Layer]]** | ~0.02–0.10 μm | [[Meibomian glands]] (modified sebaceous glands in the [[tarsal plate]]) | Retards evaporation, stabilizes tear film, provides smooth optical surface, prevents tear overflow |
| **[[Aqueous Layer]]** | ~3–4 μm (bulk of tear film) | Main and accessory [[lacrimal glands]] (glands of [[Krause]] and [[Wolfring]]) | Provides oxygen and nutrients to avascular [[corneal epithelium]], washes away debris, contains antimicrobial proteins ([[lysozyme]], [[lactoferrin]], [[immunoglobulin A]]) |
| **[[Mucin Layer]]** | ~0.02–0.05 μm | [[Conjunctival]] [[goblet cells]], corneal and conjunctival [[epithelial cells]] | Converts hydrophobic corneal epithelial surface to hydrophilic surface, anchors tear film, traps pathogens |

### Contemporary Gel Model

Current understanding favors a **two-compartment model** consisting of a superficial lipid layer overlying a mucoaqueous phase, in which soluble mucins (MUC5AC, MUC7) are distributed in a concentration gradient throughout the aqueous component, with membrane-associated mucins (MUC1, MUC4, MUC16) forming the [[glycocalyx]] at the epithelial surface.

### Tear Film Dynamics

The tear film is maintained by a coordinated system involving:

- **Tear production** by the lacrimal glands (basal and reflex secretion)
- **Tear distribution** via the blinking mechanism of the [[eyelids]]
- **Tear drainage** through the [[puncta]], [[canaliculi]], [[lacrimal sac]], and [[nasolacrimal duct]]
- **Tear evaporation** from the exposed ocular surface

Disruption at any point in this system can initiate or perpetuate DED.

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of Dry Eye Disease is complex, involving interconnected mechanisms that create self-perpetuating vicious cycles of ocular surface damage. The TFOS DEWS II Pathophysiology Report identified the following core mechanisms:

### 1. Tear Film Instability

Tear film instability—the inability of the tear film to remain intact between blinks—is a central initiating event. Instability may result from lipid layer deficiency (as in MGD), mucin deficiency, or aqueous deficiency. When the tear film breaks up prematurely, localized areas of the [[corneal]] and [[conjunctival]] [[epithelium]] are exposed to desiccating stress.

### 2. Tear Hyperosmolarity

[[Tear hyperosmolarity]] is considered a core mechanism and a key biomarker of DED. Normal tear osmolarity is approximately 296–302 mOsm/L. In DED, osmolarity rises (often >308 mOsm/L, with values >316 mOsm/L considered diagnostic) due to either reduced aqueous production or increased evaporation. Hyperosmolarity triggers a cascade of inflammatory events in [[ocular surface]] epithelial cells, including:

- Activation of [[mitogen-activated protein kinase]] (MAPK) signaling pathways (p38 MAPK, JNK, ERK)
- Release of pro-inflammatory cytokines: [[interleukin-1]] (IL-1α, IL-1β), [[interleukin-6]] (IL-6), [[interleukin-8]] (IL-8), [[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] (TNF-α)
- Increased expression of [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMP-9)
- Activation of [[nuclear factor kappa B]] (NF-κB) signaling

### 3. Ocular Surface Inflammation

Inflammation is both a consequence and a perpetuator of DED. The inflammatory cascade initiated by hyperosmolarity and tear film instability leads to:

- Recruitment and activation of [[T-helper cells]] (particularly Th1 and Th17 subsets) in the [[conjunctiva]]
- Dendritic cell maturation and antigen presentation
- Upregulation of [[intercellular adhesion molecule-1]] (ICAM-1) and other adhesion molecules
- [[Goblet cell]] apoptosis and loss, further destabilizing the mucin layer
- Epithelial cell apoptosis and desquamation
- Release of neuromediators that alter corneal nerve function

### 4. Neurosensory Abnormalities

The [[cornea]] is the most densely innervated tissue in the human body, supplied primarily by the ophthalmic division (V1) of the [[trigeminal nerve]]. In DED:

- Chronic inflammation can damage corneal nerves, leading to **[[neuropathic pain]]** and altered corneal sensitivity
- Some patients develop **neurotrophic changes** with reduced corneal sensitivity despite significant ocular surface damage
- A subset of patients exhibits **nociceptive sensitization**, experiencing disproportionate pain relative to clinical signs
- Disruption of the **[[lacrimal functional unit]]** (LFU)—the integrated system comprising the ocular surface, lacrimal glands, eyelids, and interconnecting sensory and motor nerves—impairs reflex tear secretion

### 5. The Vicious Circle of DED

These mechanisms interact in a self-amplifying vicious circle:

> **Tear film instability → Hyperosmolarity → Inflammation → Epithelial/goblet cell damage → Further tear film instability → Worsening hyperosmolarity → Amplified inflammation**

This vicious circle explains why DED is a chronic, progressive condition that often worsens without intervention and why early treatment aimed at breaking the cycle is critical.

### 6. Role of Meibomian Gland Dysfunction

[[Meibomian Gland Dysfunction]] deserves special emphasis given its central role in the majority of DED cases. The meibomian glands are holocrine glands embedded in the [[tarsal plates]] of the upper and lower eyelids (approximately 25–30 in the upper lid, 20–25 in the lower lid). In MGD:

- Terminal duct [[keratinization]] and obstruction lead to meibum stasis
- Altered meibum composition (increased melting point, altered lipid profiles) produces a deficient or abnormal lipid layer
- Chronic obstruction leads to gland atrophy (acinar dropout), which is **irreversible**
- Secondary bacterial colonization (particularly *[[Staphylococcus]]* species) may exacerbate inflammation through bacterial lipase activity, producing free fatty acids that are toxic to the ocular surface

## Risk Factors

Numerous risk factors have been identified for the development and progression of DED:

| **Category** | **Risk Factors** |
|---|---|
| **Demographic** | Advanced age, female sex, Asian ethnicity |
| **Hormonal** | [[Androgen]] deficiency, [[menopause]], anti-androgen therapy, hormone replacement therapy (variable effect) |
| **Environmental** | Low humidity, high wind exposure, air conditioning, central heating, high altitude, air pollution, prolonged visual display terminal (VDT) use |
| **Medications** | [[Antihistamines]], [[anticholinergics]], [[beta-blockers]], [[diuretics]], [[antidepressants]] (SSRIs, tricyclics), [[isotretinoin]], [[oral contraceptives]], systemic [[chemotherapy]] |
| **Ocular** | [[Contact lens]] wear, prior [[refractive surgery]] (especially [[LASIK]]), chronic topical medication use (especially with [[benzalkonium chloride]]), [[blepharitis]], [[Demodex]] infestation |
| **Systemic Disease** | [[Sjögren syndrome]], [[rheumatoid arthritis]], [[systemic lupus erythematosus]], [[diabetes mellitus]], [[thyroid disease]], [[rosacea]], [[graft-versus-host disease]], [[hepatitis C]], [[HIV/AIDS]], [[sarcoidosis]] |
| **Surgical** | [[LASIK]], [[PRK]], [[cataract surgery]], [[cosmetic blepharoplasty]] |
| **Dietary** | Low omega-3 fatty acid intake, dehydration |
| **Lifestyle** | Smoking, excessive alcohol consumption, poor sleep quality |

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Symptoms

DED symptoms are characteristically variable, often fluctuating throughout the day, and may be discordant with clinical signs. Common symptoms include:

- **Dryness** — the hallmark symptom
- **Burning** or stinging sensation
- **Foreign body sensation** (grittiness, sandiness)
- **Ocular fatigue**, particularly with sustained visual tasks
- **Photophobia** (light sensitivity)
- **Blurred vision** — often transient, improving with blinking (indicative of tear film irregularity)
- **Tearing** (paradoxical [[epiphora]]) — reflex tearing in response to ocular surface irritation
- **Redness** ([[conjunctival hyperemia]])
- **Mucous discharge** — particularly stringy or filamentary mucus
- **Difficulty wearing [[contact lenses]]**
- **Sensation of heavy eyelids**

Symptoms are typically **worse** in the following conditions:
- Late in the day (tear film depletion)
- In low-humidity environments (air-conditioned offices, airplane cabins)
- During prolonged reading or screen use (reduced blink rate)
- In windy or smoky environments
- Upon awakening (in certain subtypes, particularly with incomplete lid closure)

### Symptom Assessment Tools

Validated questionnaires are essential for standardized symptom assessment:

| **Questionnaire** | **Description** | **Scoring** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Ocular Surface Disease Index]] (OSDI)** | 12-item questionnaire assessing symptoms, visual function, and environmental triggers over the past week | 0–100; Normal (0–12), Mild (13–22), Moderate (23–32), Severe (33–100) |
| **[[Dry Eye Questionnaire-5]] (DEQ-5)** | 5-item rapid screening tool assessing frequency and intensity of dryness and watering | 0–22; ≥6 suggests DED |
| **[[Standard Patient Evaluation of Eye Dryness]] (SPEED)** | 8-item questionnaire evaluating symptom frequency and severity | 0–28 |
| **[[Symptom Assessment in Dry Eye]] (SANDE)** | Visual analog scale assessing frequency and severity | 0–100 mm VAS |
| **[[Impact of Dry Eye on Everyday Life]] (IDEEL)** | Comprehensive quality-of-life instrument | Multi-domain scoring |

## Diagnosis

Diagnosis of DED requires a systematic, multimodal approach integrating symptom assessment with objective clinical testing. No single test is pathognomonic; rather, a battery of tests is recommended.

### Diagnostic Algorithm (TFOS DEWS II Recommended Approach)

1. **Screening questionnaire** (OSDI or DEQ-5)
2. **Risk factor assessment** (history, medications, systemic disease)
3. **Homeostasis markers** (to confirm diagnosis):
   - Non-invasive tear breakup time (NIBUT)
   - Tear osmolarity
   - Ocular surface staining
4. **Subtype classification tests** (to guide treatment):
   - Lipid layer assessment
   - Meibography
   - Tear volume measurement (Schirmer test, meniscometry)
   - Meibomian gland expression and evaluation

### Diagnostic Tests in Detail

#### Tear Film Stability

- **[[Tear Breakup Time]] (TBUT):** Fluorescein is instilled, and the tear film is observed under [[cobalt blue filter]] illumination at the [[slit lamp]]. The time from the last complete blink to the first appearance of a dry spot or break in the fluorescein-stained tear film is recorded. **Normal: >10 seconds; Abnormal: <10 seconds; Diagnostic of DED: ≤5 seconds** (per some criteria).
- **[[Non-Invasive Tear Breakup Time]] (NIBUT):** Measured using instruments such as the [[Keratograph 5M]] (Oculus), [[Tearscope]], or other topographers that project patterns onto the tear film and detect distortion without instilling fluorescein. Considered more physiologically accurate. **Normal: >10–15 seconds.**

#### Tear Osmolarity

- Measured using the **[[TearLab Osmolarity System]]** or **I-PEN** device.
- A single reading **≥308 mOsm/L** or an **inter-eye difference ≥8 mOsm/L** is considered diagnostic.
- Tear osmolarity is considered by many experts to be the single best objective marker of DED and correlates with disease severity.

#### Ocular Surface Staining

Vital dye staining reveals epithelial damage on the cornea and conjunctiva:

| **Dye** | **Target** | **Grading System** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Fluorescein]]** | Corneal epithelial defects (stains areas of disrupted cell junctions) | [[Oxford Grading Scale]] (0–V), [[NEI/Industry Workshop Scale]] |
| **[[Lissamine Green]]** | Conjunctival and lid margin epithelial damage; dead/degenerated cells | Oxford Scale, van Bijsterveld Scale |
| **[[Rose Bengal]]** | Similar to lissamine green but causes significant stinging; less commonly used today | van Bijsterveld Scale (0–9) |

The **Oxford Grading Scale** uses a series of panels (0–V) with increasing density of punctate staining dots for standardized comparison.

#### Tear Volume Assessment

- **[[Schirmer Test]] I (without anesthesia):** A standardized filter paper strip (Whatman #41) is placed in the lower conjunctival fornix at the junction of the lateral and middle thirds. Wetting is measured after 5 minutes. **Normal: ≥10 mm; Suggestive of aqueous deficiency: ≤5 mm.**
- **[[Schirmer Test]] II (with anesthesia):** Performed after instillation of topical anesthetic to measure basal secretion only (eliminating reflex tearing). **Normal: ≥5 mm.**
- **[[Phenol Red Thread Test]]:** A cotton thread impregnated with phenol red pH indicator is placed in the lower fornix for 15 seconds. The thread changes color (yellow to red) upon contact with tears. **Normal: ≥10 mm; Abnormal: <10 mm.** Less invasive and faster than Schirmer testing.
- **[[Tear Meniscus Height]] (TMH):** Measured via [[anterior segment OCT]] or slit-lamp observation. **Normal: ≥0.2–0.3 mm; Reduced in aqueous-deficient DED.**

#### Meibomian Gland Assessment

- **Lid margin examination:** Assessment for telangiectasia, irregularity, plugging of meibomian gland orifices, anterior or posterior displacement of the [[mucocutaneous junction]] (Marx's line)
- **Meibomian gland expression:** Digital pressure or standardized devices (e.g., [[Meibomian Gland Evaluator]]) applied to the lower lid to assess meibum quality (clear/cloudy/granular/inspissated/toothpaste-like) and expressibility
- **[[Meibography]]:** Infrared imaging of the meibomian glands through the everted eyelid to assess gland morphology, dropout, truncation, and atrophy. Available on devices such as the [[Keratograph 5M]], [[LipiView II]], and [[Meibox]]. Gland dropout is graded using the **Meiboscore** (0–3 per lid).

#### Lipid Layer Assessment

- **[[LipiView II]] (Johnson & Johnson Vision):** Interferometric device that measures lipid layer thickness (LLT) in nanometers and captures blink dynamics. **Normal LLT: >75 nm; Thin: <60 nm.**
- **[[Tearscope]]:** Specular reflection instrument for qualitative assessment of lipid layer patterns.

#### Inflammatory Biomarkers

- **[[MMP-9]] (InflammaDry, Quidel):** Point-of-care immunoassay detecting elevated [[matrix metalloproteinase-9]] levels in tear fluid. **Positive result: ≥40 ng/mL.** Indicates ocular surface inflammation.
- **[[Lactoferrin]] assay:** Reduced lactoferrin levels indicate lacrimal gland hyposecretion.
- **[[Tear Immunoglobulin E]] (IgE):** Helps differentiate allergic from non-allergic ocular surface disease.

#### Advanced Diagnostic Technologies

- **[[In Vivo Confocal Microscopy]] (IVCM):** Enables cellular-level imaging of the [[corneal epithelium]], [[stroma]], [[corneal nerves]] (sub-basal nerve plexus), and inflammatory cell infiltration. Useful in research settings and for evaluating [[neuropathic pain]] components.
- **[[Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography]] (AS-OCT):** High-resolution imaging of tear meniscus height, tear film thickness, and corneal epithelial mapping.
- **Tear film proteomics and lipidomics:** Emerging research tools for biomarker discovery.

#### Systemic Workup

When [[Sjögren syndrome]] or other systemic autoimmune disease is suspected:

- **Serological testing:** [[Anti-SSA (Ro)]] and [[anti-SSB (La)]] antibodies, [[antinuclear antibody]] (ANA), [[rheumatoid factor]] (RF), [[erythrocyte sedimentation rate]] (ESR), [[C-reactive protein]] (CRP)
- **[[Sjö Test]]:** Early biomarker panel including antibodies to salivary gland protein-1 (SP-1), carbonic anhydrase VI (CA-6), and parotid secretory protein (PSP)
- **Salivary gland biopsy** (minor labial salivary gland)
- **Salivary flow rate measurement**
- Referral to [[rheumatology]]

## Severity Grading

The TFOS DEWS severity grading system provides a framework for staging disease severity and guiding stepped management:

| **Parameter** | **Level 1 (Mild)** | **Level 2 (Moderate)** | **Level 3 (Severe)** | **Level 4 (Very Severe)** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Symptoms** | Mild, episodic; environmental triggers | Moderate, episodic or chronic; may limit activities | Severe, frequent or constant; limiting activities | Severe and/or disabling; constant |
| **Visual Signs** | None or mild conjunctival injection | Mild conjunctival staining, mild corneal staining | Moderate-severe corneal staining, filamentary keratitis | Severe corneal staining, erosions, [[corneal ulceration]], [[corneal neovascularization]] |
| **Tear Film Signs** | Mild debris, reduced meniscus | Mild debris, reduced meniscus, mild MGD | Filamentary keratitis, mucus clumping, increased tear debris | [[Filamentary keratitis]], severe MGD, trichiasis |
| **TBUT** | Variable | ≤10 seconds | ≤5 seconds | Immediate breakup |
| **Schirmer** | Variable | ≤10 mm/5 min | ≤5 mm/5 min | ≤2 mm/5 min |
| **Corneal/Conjunctival Staining** | Minimal | Variable to mild | Marked central corneal staining | Severe erosions, filaments |

## Management and Treatment

Management of DED follows a **stepwise, evidence-based approach** tailored to disease severity, subtype (aqueous-deficient vs. evaporative vs. mixed), and individual patient factors. The TFOS DEWS II Management and Therapy Report provides a staged treatment algorithm.

### Step 1: Education, Environmental/Dietary Modification, and Lid Hygiene

Applicable to **all severity levels** as foundational therapy:

- **Patient education:** Explain the chronic nature of DED, set realistic expectations, emphasize treatment adherence
- **Environmental modifications:**
  - Use of humidifiers
  - Avoidance of direct airflow (fans, air conditioning vents, car heaters)
  - Protective eyewear (moisture chamber glasses, wraparound sunglasses)
  - Ergonomic workstation adjustments (lower screen position to reduce palpebral aperture)
- **Behavioral modifications:**
  - Conscious blinking exercises (especially during screen use)
  - Regular breaks during sustained visual tasks (20-20-20 rule: every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds)
- **Dietary supplementation:**
  - [[Omega-3 essential fatty acids]] (EPA and DHA from fish oil or flaxseed oil): Anti-inflammatory properties; evidence is mixed (the DREAM study showed no significant benefit over placebo for moderate-to-severe DED, though other studies support benefit)
  - Adequate systemic hydration
- **Lid hygiene (for MGD/[[blepharitis]]):**
  - Warm compresses (sustained heat at 40–45°C for 10–15 minutes to melt inspissated meibum)
  - Lid massage (gentle expression of meibomian glands after warming)
  - Lid scrubs (dilute baby shampoo, commercial lid wipes, or hypochlorous acid-based cleansers such as [[Avenova]] or [[HypoChlor]])
- **Modification or elimination of offending systemic medications** (in consultation with prescribing physician)
- **Preservative-free topical medications** when possible

### Step 2: Pharmacological and Non-Pharmacological Therapies

If Step 1 is insufficient:

#### Artificial Tears and Lubricants

The mainstay of DED management across all severity levels:

| **Type** | **Examples** | **Characteristics** |
|---|---|---|
| **Preservative-free aqueous-based drops** | Refresh Optive (CMC), Systane Ultra (PG/PEG), TheraTears | Preferred for frequent use (>4×/day); no preservative toxicity |
| **Preserved aqueous-based drops** | Various OTC formulations | Acceptable for mild DED with infrequent use; [[benzalkonium chloride]] (BAK) should be avoided |
| **Lipid-based drops** | Systane Balance, Refresh Optive Advanced, Retaine MGD | Supplement lipid layer; beneficial in evaporative DED/MGD |
| **Hyaluronic acid-based drops** | Hylo-Forte, Blink Intensive Tears | Excellent retention time, biocompatible, promotes epithelial healing |
| **Gels and ointments** | GenTeal Gel, Refresh PM, Lacri-Lube | Greater viscosity for prolonged relief; ointments preferred at bedtime due to visual blurring |
| **Autologous serum tears** | 20–100% concentration | Contains growth factors (EGF, TGF-β, fibronectin), vitamins, immunoglobulins; reserved for severe/refractory DED |
| **Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) drops** | Variable preparation | Similar rationale to autologous serum; emerging evidence |

#### Anti-Inflammatory Therapy

Inflammation is a central driver of DED; anti-inflammatory agents target the underlying disease process:

- **[[Cyclosporine A]] (CsA):**
  - **Restasis (0.05% cyclosporine emulsion, Allergan):** FDA-approved for DED; inhibits T-cell activation and cytokine release; increases goblet cell density; requires 3–6 months for full effect; twice-daily dosing
  - **Cequa (0.09% cyclosporine, Sun Pharma):** Nanomicellar formulation with enhanced bioavailability; twice-daily dosing
  - **Ikervis (0.1% cyclosporine, Santen):** Cationic emulsion approved in Europe; once-daily dosing at bedtime
  - **Vevye (0.1% cyclosporine, Novaliq):** Water-free, preservative-free formulation using EyeSol technology; FDA-approved 2023

- **[[Lifitegrast]] 5% (Xiidra, Novartis):**
  - [[Lymphocyte function-associated antigen-1]] (LFA-1) antagonist; blocks LFA-1/ICAM-1 interaction, inhibiting T-cell recruitment and activation
  - FDA-approved for signs and symptoms of DED; twice-daily dosing
  - Faster onset of symptom relief compared to cyclosporine (as early as 2 weeks)

- **[[Corticosteroids]] (short-term pulse therapy):**
  - Used for acute inflammatory flares or as induction therapy before initiating cyclosporine or lifitegrast
  - Options: [[loteprednol etabonate]] 0.5% (Lotemax), [[fluorometholone]] 0.1%, [[prednisolone acetate]] 1%
  - Loteprednol preferred due to lower risk of [[intraocular pressure]] elevation
  - Typical pulse: 2–4 weeks with taper; monitor [[IOP]]
  - **Eysuvis (loteprednol etabonate 0.25%, Kala Pharmaceuticals):** FDA-approved specifically for short-term (2-week) treatment of DED flares using mucus-penetrating particle (MPP) technology

- **[[Perfluorohexyloctane]] (Miebo, Bausch + Lomb):**
  - FDA-approved 2023 for signs and symptoms of DED associated with MGD
  - Novel non-aqueous, preservative-free eye drop that stabilizes the lipid layer and reduces evaporation
  - Dosed four times daily

- **[[Varenicline]] nasal spray 0.03 mg (Tyrvaya, Viatris):**
  - Cholinergic agonist (nicotinic acetylcholine receptor agonist) administered intranasally
  - Stimulates the trigeminal parasympathetic pathway to increase basal tear production (aqueous, lipid, and mucin components)
  - FDA-approved 2021; twice-daily nasal spray
  - Novel mechanism targeting the [[lacrimal functional unit]] neuroanatomically

#### Tear Conservation

- **[[Punctal plugs]]:**
  - Silicone or collagen plugs inserted into the upper and/or lower [[puncta]] to reduce tear drainage and increase tear film residence time
  - Collagen plugs: temporary (dissolve in days to weeks); useful as a diagnostic trial
  - Silicone/acrylic plugs: semi-permanent; removable
  - **Contraindication:** Active ocular surface inflammation should be controlled before insertion (to avoid retaining inflammatory mediators on the ocular surface)
  - Complications: spontaneous extrusion, migration into canaliculus, [[canaliculitis]], epiphora

- **[[Punctal cautery]]:** Permanent punctal occlusion via thermal cautery; reserved for severe aqueous-deficient DED refractory to other measures

#### In-Office Procedures for MGD

- **[[LipiFlow]] (Johnson & Johnson Vision):**
  - Vectored thermal pulsation device that applies controlled heat (42.5°C) to the inner eyelid surface while simultaneously applying pulsatile pressure to the outer lid
  - 12-minute single-session treatment
  - Evacuates obstructed meibomian glands
  - Evidence supports sustained improvement in meibomian gland function for 9–12 months

- **[[Intense Pulsed Light]] (IPL) Therapy:**
  - Broad-spectrum light (500–1200 nm) applied to the periocular skin
  - Mechanisms: reduces [[Demodex]] mite burden, decreases telangiectatic blood vessels (reducing inflammatory mediator delivery), liquefies meibum, modulates inflammatory cytokines
  - Typically requires 3–4 sessions at 2–4 week intervals with maintenance treatments
  - Devices: [[OptiLight]] (Lumenis, FDA-approved for DED management), various IPL platforms
  - Contraindicated in Fitzpatrick skin types V–VI

- **[[iLux]] (Alcon):**
  - Portable, handheld LED-based thermal pulsation device for in-office meibomian gland treatment
  - Provides direct visualization during treatment

- **[[TearCare]] (Sight Sciences):**
  - Open-eye, wearable thermal device applied to the external eyelid surface
  - Followed by manual meibomian gland expression

- **Meibomian gland probing:**
  - Intraductal probing of obstructed meibomian gland orifices using fine probes (Maskin probes)
  - Relieves fixed obstructions; may restore gland function

- **[[BlephEx]]:**
  - In-office micro-exfoliation device for mechanical debridement of the lid margin
  - Removes biofilm, collarettes, and debris associated with [[anterior blepharitis]] and [[Demodex]] infestation

- **[[Zest]] (Zocular Eyelid System Treatment):**
  - In-office okra-based gel lid debridement procedure
  - Targets biofilm and Demodex

### Step 3: Oral Therapeutics, Therapeutic Contact Lenses, and Advanced Interventions

For moderate-to-severe DED refractory to Steps 1–2:

- **Oral [[tetracycline]] derivatives:**
  - [[Doxycycline]] (40–100 mg daily) or [[minocycline]] (50–100 mg daily)
  - Anti-inflammatory (sub-antimicrobial dose), anti-MMP activity, lipase inhibition
  - Particularly useful in MGD associated with [[ocular rosacea]]
  - Duration: typically 6–12 weeks; may be extended at sub-antimicrobial doses

- **Oral [[pilocarpine]] or [[cevimeline]]:**
  - Muscarinic agonists that stimulate lacrimal and salivary gland secretion
  - Primarily used in [[Sjögren syndrome]]-associated DED
  - Side effects: sweating, gastrointestinal disturbance

- **Oral [[omega-3 fatty acid]] supplementation** (if not initiated in Step 1)

- **Therapeutic [[contact lenses]]:**
  - **[[Scleral lenses]]:** Large-diameter rigid gas-permeable lenses that vault over the entire [[cornea]], creating a fluid-filled reservoir that continuously bathes the ocular surface. Highly effective for severe DED, [[neurotrophic keratopathy]], and [[ocular surface disease]].
  - **[[PROSE]] (Prosthetic Replacement of the Ocular Surface Ecosystem):** Custom-designed scleral lens system for severe ocular surface disease
  - **Soft bandage contact lenses:** Provide mechanical protection of the corneal surface in cases of [[persistent epithelial defects]] or [[filamentary keratitis]]

### Step 4: Surgical and Advanced Interventions

Reserved for **severe, refractory DED**:

- **[[Tarsorrhaphy]]:** Partial or complete surgical closure of the [[palpebral fissure]] to reduce the exposed ocular surface area and decrease evaporation. May be temporary (suture) or permanent.
- **[[Conjunctivochalasis]] surgery:** Excision or cauterization of redundant conjunctival folds that disrupt tear film distribution
- **[[Amniotic membrane transplantation]]:** Cryopreserved or dehydrated [[amniotic membrane]] (e.g., [[Prokera]], [[AmbioDisk]]) placed on the ocular surface to promote epithelial healing, reduce inflammation, and suppress scarring. Contains growth factors, anti-inflammatory cytokines, and protease inhibitors.
- **[[Salivary gland autotransplantation]]:** Transplantation of submandibular salivary gland tissue to the temporal fossa with ductal anastomosis to the conjunctival fornix; reserved for absolute aqueous deficiency
- **[[Mucous membrane grafting]]:** For severe [[conjunctival]] scarring with [[fornix]] shortening
- **[[Lacrimal gland]] botulinum toxin injection:** Rarely used; for paradoxical reflex tearing

## Special Populations and Considerations

### Dry Eye and Contact Lens Wear

[[Contact lens]]-related dry eye (CLDE) affects 30–50% of contact lens wearers and is the leading cause of contact lens dropout. Mechanisms include increased tear evaporation, tear film compartmentalization, mechanical disruption of the lipid layer, and goblet cell loss. Management includes:

- Refitting with daily disposable lenses (reduced deposit accumulation)
- Switching to silicone hydrogel materials (higher oxygen transmissibility)
- Use of preservative-free rewetting drops
- Reducing wearing time
- Addressing underlying MGD

### Dry Eye After Refractive Surgery

[[LASIK]] severs corneal nerves during flap creation, disrupting the [[lacrimal functional unit]] reflex arc. Post-LASIK dry eye affects up to 95% of patients in the early postoperative period, with most cases resolving within 6–12 months as nerves regenerate. Risk factors for persistent post-LASIK DED include pre-existing DED, high myopic correction, female sex, and Asian ethnicity. [[PRK]] and [[SMILE]] may have lower rates of post-surgical dry eye due to less corneal nerve disruption.

### Dry Eye and Systemic Disease

Clinicians must maintain a high index of suspicion for underlying systemic disease, particularly [[Sjögren syndrome]], in patients presenting with severe aqueous-deficient DED, especially young to middle-aged women with concurrent dry mouth ([[xerostomia]]), fatigue, and arthralgias.

### Dry Eye in Pediatric Populations

Though less commonly diagnosed, DED in children is increasingly recognized, particularly in association with excessive digital device use, [[allergic conjunctivitis]], and [[juvenile idiopathic arthritis]].

## Prognosis

DED is a **chronic condition** that typically requires long-term management. While mild DED can often be well-controlled with environmental modifications and artificial tears, moderate-to-severe disease may require ongoing anti-inflammatory therapy and periodic in-office treatments. Early identification and treatment of MGD is critical, as meibomian gland atrophy is irreversible. Patient education regarding the chronic nature of the disease and the importance of treatment adherence is paramount to achieving optimal outcomes.

## Emerging Therapies and Future Directions

The DED therapeutic landscape is rapidly evolving. Areas of active research and development include:

- **[[Nerve growth factor]] (NGF) analogs:** [[Cenegermin]] (Oxervate), approved for [[neurotrophic keratitis]], is being investigated for DED with neurosensory dysfunction
- **[[RGN-259]] (Thymosin β4):** Regenerative peptide promoting corneal epithelial wound healing
- **[[NOV03]] (perfluorohexyloctane):** Now approved as Miebo
- **[[KPI-121]] (loteprednol etabonate submicron):** Now approved as Eysuvis
- **[[Reproxalap]] (Aldeyra Therapeutics):** Reactive aldehyde species (RASP) inhibitor targeting novel inflammatory pathway
- **[[Lacripep]]:** Synthetic lacritin-derived peptide promoting tear secretion
- **Gene therapy** for lacrimal gland aquaporin expression
- **Nanotechnology-based drug delivery systems**
- **Microbiome modulation** of the ocular surface
- **Bioengineered tear substitutes**
- **Wearable tear film monitoring devices**

## Summary

Dry Eye Disease is a highly prevalent, multifactorial condition of the [[ocular surface]] that significantly impacts patient quality of life and visual function. Its pathophysiology involves a self-perpetuating vicious circle of tear film instability, hyperosmolarity, inflammation, and neurosensory dysfunction. Accurate diagnosis requires integration of validated symptom questionnaires with a battery of objective clinical tests, including tear film stability assessment, osmolarity measurement, ocular surface staining, and meibomian gland evaluation. Management follows a stepwise approach, beginning with patient education, environmental modification, and lid hygiene, progressing through anti-inflammatory pharmacotherapy, in-office procedures, and advanced interventions as dictated by disease severity and subtype. The expanding therapeutic armamentarium—including novel anti-inflammatory agents, neurostimulatory approaches, thermal pulsation devices, and intense pulsed light therapy—offers increasingly effective options for managing this complex and chronic disease.
