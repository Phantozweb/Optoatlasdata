

# Cornea

The **[[cornea]]** is the transparent, avascular, dome-shaped anterior structure of the [[eye]] that serves as the primary refractive surface, contributing approximately **+43 diopters** (roughly two-thirds) of the eye's total refractive power of approximately +60 diopters. It functions simultaneously as a structural barrier protecting the intraocular contents and as a precise optical element whose transparency and curvature are essential for clear vision. The cornea measures approximately **11.5 mm horizontally** and **10.5 mm vertically** in adults, with a central thickness of approximately **540 µm (0.54 mm)** and a peripheral thickness of approximately **700 µm**. Its anterior radius of curvature averages **7.8 mm**, while the posterior radius averages **6.5 mm**. Diseases, injuries, and degenerations of the cornea represent a leading cause of visual impairment and blindness worldwide, making a thorough understanding of corneal anatomy, physiology, pathology, and therapeutics indispensable for every [[optometrist]] and [[ophthalmologist]].

---

## Embryology

The cornea derives from multiple embryological sources. The **[[corneal epithelium]]** originates from surface [[ectoderm]], while the **[[corneal stroma]]**, **[[corneal endothelium]]**, and **[[Descemet's membrane]]** are derived from [[neural crest]] cells (mesenchyme). During the fifth to sixth week of gestation, a wave of neural crest cells migrates between the surface ectoderm and the [[lens vesicle]] to form the corneal endothelium and, subsequently, the stroma. [[Bowman's layer]] is synthesized by stromal [[keratocytes]] and anterior epithelial cells during fetal development. The proper orchestration of these embryological events is critical; disruptions can lead to congenital anomalies such as [[Peter's anomaly]], [[sclerocornea]], and [[congenital hereditary endothelial dystrophy (CHED)]].

## Anatomy and Histology

The cornea is classically described as consisting of **five (or six) distinct layers**, from anterior to posterior:

### 1. Corneal Epithelium

The **[[corneal epithelium]]** is a non-keratinized, stratified squamous epithelium approximately **50–60 µm** thick, comprising 5–7 cell layers:

- **Superficial squamous cells (2–3 layers):** Flattened cells with microvilli and microplicae on their apical surface that anchor the [[tear film]]. Tight junctions ([[zonulae occludentes]]) between these cells form a critical barrier against pathogen entry.
- **Wing cells (2–3 layers):** Polygonal transitional cells.
- **Basal columnar cells (single layer):** The only mitotically active epithelial cells, attached to the [[basement membrane]] via [[hemidesmosomes]] and anchoring fibrils (type VII [[collagen]]).

The epithelium has a remarkable regenerative capacity, with a complete turnover cycle of approximately **7–10 days**. [[Limbal stem cells]], located at the [[limbus]] (the junction between the cornea and [[sclera]]) in the [[Palisades of Vogt]], are the ultimate source of epithelial renewal. Loss or dysfunction of these stem cells leads to **[[limbal stem cell deficiency (LSCD)]]**, resulting in [[conjunctivalization]] of the corneal surface.

### 2. Bowman's Layer (Bowman's Membrane)

**[[Bowman's layer]]** is an acellular, condensed layer of randomly arranged [[collagen]] fibrils (primarily types I and III) approximately **8–12 µm** thick. It lies immediately beneath the epithelial basement membrane. Bowman's layer does not regenerate after injury; damage results in scar formation. Its precise function remains debated, but it is thought to contribute to corneal structural integrity and resistance to trauma. Notably, Bowman's layer is absent in many animal species and is a feature relatively unique to primates.

### 3. Corneal Stroma (Substantia Propria)

The **[[corneal stroma]]** constitutes approximately **90%** of the total corneal thickness (~450–500 µm centrally). It is composed of approximately **200–250 lamellae** of highly organized [[collagen]] fibrils (predominantly type I, with types III, V, and VI also present). The uniform diameter (~31 nm) and regular spacing (~64 nm) of these fibrils, maintained by [[proteoglycans]] such as **[[keratan sulfate]]** proteoglycans ([[lumican]], [[keratocan]], [[mimecan]]) and **[[dermatan sulfate]]** proteoglycans ([[decorin]]), are essential for corneal transparency, as described by the **[[Maurice lattice theory]]** of transparency.

Interspersed between the lamellae are **[[keratocytes]]** (corneal fibroblasts), which are quiescent, flattened cells that maintain the extracellular matrix. Upon injury, keratocytes can transform into activated **[[fibroblasts]]** and **[[myofibroblasts]]**, contributing to wound healing but also to corneal scarring and haze.

The stroma is maintained in a state of relative **[[deturgescence]]** (partial dehydration at ~78% water content), which is critical for transparency. Disruption of this hydration balance—whether from endothelial failure, epithelial compromise, or elevated [[intraocular pressure]]—leads to stromal edema and loss of transparency.

### 4. Dua's Layer (Pre-Descemet's Layer)

In 2013, Dua and colleagues described a distinct, strong, acellular layer approximately **10–15 µm** thick located between the posterior stroma and [[Descemet's membrane]], termed **[[Dua's layer]]**. Composed primarily of type I collagen arranged in 5–8 lamellae, this layer has been implicated in the pathogenesis of **[[descemetocele]]** formation and certain types of corneal hydrops. Its recognition as a truly separate sixth layer remains a subject of ongoing academic discussion.

### 5. Descemet's Membrane

**[[Descemet's membrane]]** is the thick basement membrane of the corneal endothelium. It is composed primarily of type IV and type VIII [[collagen]]. It thickens throughout life, measuring approximately **3 µm at birth** and **10–12 µm in adulthood**. It has two zones:

- **Anterior banded zone (ABZ):** Produced during fetal development, with a characteristic banding pattern on electron microscopy.
- **Posterior non-banded zone (PNBZ):** Produced postnatally by the endothelium throughout life.

Descemet's membrane can be stripped and will regenerate, a property exploited in surgical procedures such as **[[Descemet's stripping automated endothelial keratoplasty (DSAEK)]]** and **[[Descemet's membrane endothelial keratoplasty (DMEK)]]**. Focal excrescences of Descemet's membrane are known as **[[Hassall-Henle bodies]]** (peripheral) and **[[corneal guttae]]** (central), the latter being the hallmark of **[[Fuchs' endothelial corneal dystrophy]]**.

### 6. Corneal Endothelium

The **[[corneal endothelium]]** is a single layer of hexagonal cells approximately **5 µm thick** on the posterior corneal surface. At birth, the endothelial cell density (ECD) is approximately **3,500–4,000 cells/mm²**; this declines with age to approximately **2,500 cells/mm²** in adults. Human corneal endothelial cells are largely **amitotic** in vivo, meaning they do not proliferate to replace lost cells. Instead, remaining cells enlarge (**[[polymegethism]]**) and become irregular in shape (**[[pleomorphism]]**) to cover the defect.

The endothelium maintains corneal transparency through two mechanisms:

1. **Barrier function:** Incomplete tight junctions ([[zonulae occludentes]] and [[maculae occludentes]]) allow controlled leakage of aqueous humor nutrients into the stroma.
2. **Active pump function:** Na⁺/K⁺-[[ATPase]] pumps and [[bicarbonate]]-dependent ion transport actively move fluid from the stroma back into the [[anterior chamber]], maintaining stromal deturgescence.

When ECD falls below a critical threshold (typically **~400–700 cells/mm²**), the endothelium can no longer maintain deturgescence, resulting in **[[corneal edema]]** and loss of vision.

| Layer | Thickness | Key Composition | Regenerative Capacity |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Corneal Epithelium]] | 50–60 µm | Stratified squamous cells, [[limbal stem cells]] | Excellent (7–10 day turnover) |
| [[Bowman's Layer]] | 8–12 µm | Acellular collagen (types I, III) | Does not regenerate; scars |
| [[Corneal Stroma]] | ~450–500 µm | Type I collagen lamellae, keratocytes, proteoglycans | Limited; heals with scarring |
| [[Dua's Layer]] | 10–15 µm | Type I collagen | Under investigation |
| [[Descemet's Membrane]] | 3–12 µm (age-dependent) | Type IV, VIII collagen | Can regenerate |
| [[Corneal Endothelium]] | ~5 µm | Monolayer of hexagonal cells | Very limited (amitotic in vivo) |

## Innervation

The cornea is the **most densely innervated tissue in the human body**, with a nerve density approximately 300–600 times that of skin. Sensory innervation is supplied by the **[[ophthalmic division (V1)]]** of the [[trigeminal nerve (CN V)]], primarily via the **[[long ciliary nerves]]** and, to a lesser extent, the **[[short ciliary nerves]]**. Nerve trunks enter the cornea radially at the mid-stromal level, lose their [[myelin]] sheaths within 1–2 mm of the limbus, and branch extensively. The **sub-basal nerve plexus**, located between Bowman's layer and the basal epithelium, is the densest nerve network and is clinically visualized using **[[in vivo confocal microscopy (IVCM)]]**.

Corneal nerves serve critical functions:

- **Sensation:** Protective blink reflex, tearing.
- **Trophic support:** Release of neurotrophic factors (e.g., **[[substance P]]**, **[[nerve growth factor (NGF)]]**, **[[calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP)]]**) essential for epithelial health and wound healing.

Damage to corneal nerves—from surgery (e.g., [[LASIK]], [[penetrating keratoplasty]]), infection (e.g., [[herpes simplex keratitis]], [[herpes zoster ophthalmicus]]), diabetes, or other causes—can lead to **[[neurotrophic keratopathy]]**, a degenerative condition characterized by impaired epithelial healing, persistent epithelial defects, stromal melting, and potential perforation.

## Physiology and Transparency

Corneal transparency is a remarkable biophysical phenomenon dependent on several factors:

1. **Regular collagen fibril spacing** (Maurice lattice theory): Destructive interference of scattered light from uniformly spaced fibrils results in transparency. Disruption of this spacing (e.g., edema, scarring) causes opacity.
2. **Stromal deturgescence:** Maintained by the endothelial pump-barrier mechanism and intact epithelial barrier.
3. **Avascularity:** The cornea is avascular, receiving nutrients from the [[tear film]] (oxygen), [[aqueous humor]] (glucose, amino acids), and [[limbal vasculature]]. The "[[angiogenic privilege]]" of the cornea is maintained by anti-angiogenic factors including **[[soluble VEGF receptor-1 (sVEGFR-1/sFlt-1)]]**, **[[thrombospondin-1]]**, **[[angiostatin]]**, and **[[endostatin]]**. Loss of this privilege leads to **[[corneal neovascularization]]**.
4. **Absence of myelinated nerves** within the cornea proper.
5. **Relative acellularity** of the stroma.
6. **Smooth optical surface** maintained by the tear film.

### Corneal Metabolism

The cornea derives most of its **oxygen** from the atmosphere via the tear film (open-eye) and from the [[palpebral conjunctiva]] (closed-eye). Glucose is supplied primarily from the aqueous humor. The corneal epithelium is metabolically active, utilizing both **aerobic glycolysis** (Krebs cycle) and **anaerobic glycolysis** (Embden-Meyerhof pathway). Under hypoxic conditions (e.g., extended [[contact lens]] wear), anaerobic metabolism predominates, leading to [[lactic acid]] accumulation, stromal acidosis, and edema.

## Pathophysiology of Corneal Disease

Corneal pathology can be broadly categorized by the layer(s) affected and the underlying mechanism:

### Infectious Keratitis

**[[Infectious keratitis]]** is a sight-threatening emergency. Causative organisms include:

| Organism Type | Common Pathogens | Key Risk Factors | Clinical Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Bacterial** | [[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]], [[Staphylococcus aureus]], [[Streptococcus pneumoniae]], [[Moraxella]] spp. | [[Contact lens]] wear, trauma, ocular surface disease | Rapidly progressive stromal infiltrate, suppurative, [[hypopyon]] |
| **Fungal** | [[Fusarium]], [[Aspergillus]] (filamentary); [[Candida]] (yeast) | Agricultural trauma, topical [[corticosteroid]] use, tropical climate | Feathery margins, satellite lesions, endothelial plaque, indolent course |
| **Viral** | [[Herpes simplex virus (HSV)]], [[Varicella-zoster virus (VZV)]], [[Adenovirus]] | Prior HSV infection, immunosuppression | Dendritic/geographic ulcer (HSV), pseudodendrites (VZV), subepithelial infiltrates (adenoviral) |
| **Parasitic** | [[Acanthamoeba]] spp. | Contact lens wear with water exposure, poor hygiene | Severe pain disproportionate to findings, [[perineural infiltrates]] (radial keratoneuritis), ring infiltrate |

The pathophysiology of infectious keratitis involves microbial invasion through a compromised epithelial barrier, followed by direct tissue destruction, release of microbial toxins and enzymes (e.g., [[matrix metalloproteinases]]), and a robust host [[inflammatory response]] with recruitment of [[neutrophils]] and other immune cells. The resulting stromal necrosis and scarring can cause permanent visual loss.

### Corneal Dystrophies

**[[Corneal dystrophies]]** are a group of inherited, bilateral, progressive, non-inflammatory corneal disorders classified by the **IC3D (International Committee for Classification of Corneal Dystrophies)** system. They are categorized by the primary layer affected:

| Category | Examples | Gene/Protein | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Epithelial/Subepithelial** | [[Epithelial basement membrane dystrophy (EBMD/Map-dot-fingerprint)]], [[Meesmann dystrophy]] | TGFBI, KRT3/KRT12 | Recurrent erosions, microcysts |
| **Bowman's Layer** | [[Reis-Bücklers dystrophy]], [[Thiel-Behnke dystrophy]] | TGFBI | Painful recurrent erosions, anterior corneal opacities |
| **Stromal** | [[Lattice dystrophy]], [[Granular dystrophy]], [[Macular dystrophy]], [[Schnyder dystrophy]] | TGFBI, CHST6, UBIAD1 | Characteristic stromal deposits (amyloid, hyaline, GAG, cholesterol) |
| **Descemet's/Endothelial** | [[Fuchs' endothelial corneal dystrophy (FECD)]], [[Posterior polymorphous corneal dystrophy (PPCD)]], [[Congenital hereditary endothelial dystrophy (CHED)]] | TCF4, COL8A2, ZEB1, SLC4A11 | Guttae, corneal edema, reduced ECD |

**[[Fuchs' endothelial corneal dystrophy]]** is the most common corneal dystrophy, characterized by progressive loss of endothelial cells, formation of central [[corneal guttae]] (excrescences of Descemet's membrane), and eventual corneal edema. Patients classically report **worse vision upon waking** (due to overnight reduction in tear evaporation and increased corneal hydration) that improves throughout the day.

### Corneal Ectasias

**[[Corneal ectasia]]** refers to progressive thinning and protrusion of the cornea, leading to irregular [[astigmatism]] and visual distortion. The most important ectatic conditions include:

- **[[Keratoconus]]:** The most common corneal ectasia (prevalence ~1:2,000, though recent studies suggest higher rates). Onset typically in puberty, with progressive bilateral (often asymmetric) central or paracentral corneal thinning and conical protrusion. Pathophysiology involves a complex interplay of genetic predisposition (e.g., VSX1, SOD1 gene associations), biomechanical weakening (reduced collagen cross-linking, altered proteoglycan distribution), oxidative stress (decreased [[superoxide dismutase]], [[aldehyde dehydrogenase]]), and environmental factors (eye rubbing, [[atopy]]). Clinical signs include [[Munson's sign]], [[Vogt's striae]], [[Fleischer ring]] (iron deposition), and [[corneal hydrops]] (acute rupture of Descemet's membrane).
- **[[Pellucid marginal degeneration]]:** Inferior peripheral corneal thinning with a band of clear cornea between the area of thinning and the limbus, producing a characteristic "crab-claw" or "butterfly" pattern on [[corneal topography]].
- **[[Keratoglobus]]:** Generalized, limbus-to-limbus corneal thinning and globular protrusion. Rare; may be associated with connective tissue disorders (e.g., [[Ehlers-Danlos syndrome type VI]]).
- **[[Post-LASIK ectasia]]:** Iatrogenic ectasia following [[refractive surgery]], particularly when insufficient residual stromal bed thickness remains or undiagnosed forme fruste keratoconus is present preoperatively.

### Corneal Degenerations

Unlike dystrophies, **[[corneal degenerations]]** are typically unilateral or asymmetric, non-hereditary, and age-related or secondary to other conditions:

- **[[Arcus senilis]] (corneal arcus):** Peripheral lipid deposition; benign in the elderly but warrants lipid panel investigation in patients under 40 (**[[arcus juvenilis]]**).
- **[[Band keratopathy]]:** Calcium deposition in Bowman's layer, associated with chronic [[uveitis]], [[hypercalcemia]], and chronic ocular surface disease. Treated with **[[chelation therapy]]** using [[EDTA (ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid)]].
- **[[Salzmann's nodular degeneration]]:** Bluish-white, elevated subepithelial nodules, often following chronic keratitis or contact lens wear.
- **[[Terrien's marginal degeneration]]:** Progressive peripheral corneal thinning, typically superior, with intact epithelium and lipid deposition at the leading edge.

### Corneal Edema

**[[Corneal edema]]** results from failure of the mechanisms maintaining stromal deturgescence. Causes include:

- **Endothelial dysfunction:** [[Fuchs' dystrophy]], surgical trauma (e.g., post-[[cataract surgery]] [[pseudophakic bullous keratopathy]]), [[iridocorneal endothelial (ICE) syndrome]].
- **Elevated [[intraocular pressure (IOP)]]:** Acute [[angle-closure glaucoma]].
- **Epithelial compromise:** Extensive epithelial defects.
- **Inflammation:** Severe [[anterior uveitis]], [[endotheliitis]].

Clinically, corneal edema manifests as stromal thickening, Descemet's folds, epithelial microcystic changes (**[[epithelial bullae]]** in advanced cases—**[[bullous keratopathy]]**), and reduced visual acuity. Patients may report halos around lights due to light scatter.

## Symptoms of Corneal Disease

Symptoms vary by the specific condition but commonly include:

- **Decreased visual acuity** (blurred vision, distortion)
- **Pain** (ranging from foreign body sensation to severe, deep aching pain)
- **[[Photophobia]]** (light sensitivity)
- **[[Epiphora]]** (tearing/lacrimation)
- **Redness** ([[ciliary injection]], [[conjunctival injection]])
- **Sensation of foreign body**
- **Halos around lights** (corneal edema)
- **Recurrent erosion symptoms** (sharp pain upon waking, often with a history of prior corneal abrasion)

It is important to note that some corneal conditions (e.g., early [[keratoconus]], early [[Fuchs' dystrophy]], corneal degenerations) may be **asymptomatic** in their initial stages and detected only on routine examination.

## Diagnostic Modalities

A comprehensive corneal evaluation employs a range of clinical and technological tools:

### Clinical Examination

- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]:** The cornerstone of corneal examination. Allows detailed evaluation of all corneal layers using various illumination techniques:
  - **Diffuse illumination:** Overview of corneal clarity.
  - **Direct focal illumination:** Localization of opacities and infiltrates.
  - **Retroillumination:** Detection of endothelial abnormalities (guttae, keratic precipitates), subtle edema, and ghost vessels.
  - **Specular reflection:** Assessment of endothelial cell mosaic.
  - **Sclerotic scatter:** Detection of subtle corneal edema or opacities.
  - **Indirect illumination:** Evaluation of areas adjacent to the light beam.
- **[[Fluorescein staining]]:** Sodium fluorescein dye highlights epithelial defects (stains exposed stroma green under cobalt blue light). Essential for detecting [[corneal abrasions]], [[dendritic ulcers]], and assessing [[contact lens]] fit.
- **[[Rose Bengal]] / [[Lissamine Green]] staining:** Stain devitalized or dead epithelial cells and mucus; useful in [[dry eye disease]] and [[exposure keratopathy]].
- **[[Corneal sensitivity testing]] (Cochet-Bonnet aesthesiometry):** Quantifies corneal nerve function; reduced in [[neurotrophic keratopathy]], [[herpes simplex keratitis]], [[diabetes]], and post-surgical states.

### Imaging and Measurement Technologies

| Modality | Primary Application | Key Information Provided |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Corneal topography]]** (Placido disc-based) | Surface curvature mapping | Anterior curvature, detection of [[keratoconus]], irregular [[astigmatism]], contact lens fitting |
| **[[Corneal tomography]]** (e.g., [[Pentacam]], [[Orbscan]], [[Galilei]]) | Full corneal characterization | Anterior and posterior elevation maps, pachymetry maps, corneal thickness spatial profile, detection of early ectasia |
| **[[Optical coherence tomography (OCT)]]** – Anterior Segment | Cross-sectional corneal imaging | Layer-by-layer analysis, epithelial thickness mapping, DMEK graft assessment, tumor evaluation |
| **[[Specular microscopy]]** | Endothelial cell analysis | Endothelial cell density (ECD), [[polymegethism]] (CV), [[pleomorphism]] (% hexagonality) |
| **[[In vivo confocal microscopy (IVCM)]]** (e.g., Heidelberg HRT/RCM) | Cellular-level corneal imaging | Detection of [[Acanthamoeba]] cysts, fungal hyphae, sub-basal nerve plexus evaluation, inflammatory cell quantification |
| **[[Pachymetry]]** (ultrasonic or optical) | Corneal thickness measurement | Central corneal thickness (CCT) for [[glaucoma]] risk assessment, pre-refractive surgery screening, monitoring edema |
| **[[Corneal biomechanical analysis]]** (e.g., [[Ocular Response Analyzer (ORA)]], [[Corvis ST]]) | Biomechanical properties | Corneal hysteresis (CH), corneal resistance factor (CRF), deformation amplitude; screening for ectasia susceptibility |
| **[[Corneal wavefront analysis]]** | Higher-order aberration measurement | Quantification of [[coma]], [[spherical aberration]], [[trefoil]]; useful in ectasia and post-surgical evaluation |

### Laboratory Investigations

For suspected **[[infectious keratitis]]**, corneal scraping for microbiological analysis is essential:

- **Smears:** [[Gram stain]], [[Giemsa stain]], [[KOH (potassium hydroxide) preparation]], [[Calcofluor white]] stain (for Acanthamoeba and fungi).
- **Cultures:** Blood agar, chocolate agar, Sabouraud dextrose agar (fungi), non-nutrient agar with E. coli overlay (Acanthamoeba), thioglycolate broth.
- **[[Polymerase chain reaction (PCR)]]:** Highly sensitive for HSV, VZV, Acanthamoeba, and atypical organisms.
- **Corneal biopsy:** Reserved for culture-negative, non-responsive cases.

## Treatment and Management

### Medical (Pharmacological) Management

#### Infectious Keratitis

- **Bacterial keratitis:** Empiric therapy with **fortified topical antibiotics** (e.g., fortified [[cefazolin]] 5% + fortified [[tobramycin]] 1.4%, or fortified [[vancomycin]] 2.5% + fortified [[ceftazidime]] 5%) for severe ulcers. **[[Fluoroquinolones]]** (e.g., [[moxifloxacin]] 0.5%, [[gatifloxacin]] 0.3%) are used as monotherapy for small-to-moderate ulcers. Therapy is adjusted based on culture and sensitivity results.
- **Fungal keratitis:** **[[Natamycin]] 5%** (first-line for filamentary fungi), **[[voriconazole]] 1%** (topical), **[[amphotericin B]] 0.15–0.25%** (for yeast). Prolonged treatment (weeks to months) is typically required.
- **Herpes simplex keratitis:**
  - *Epithelial keratitis:* Topical **[[ganciclovir]] 0.15% gel** or oral **[[acyclovir]]** (400 mg 5x/day) / **[[valacyclovir]]** (500 mg TID).
  - *Stromal keratitis (immune):* Topical **[[corticosteroids]]** (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate]] 1%) with concurrent antiviral prophylaxis.
  - *Endotheliitis:* Topical corticosteroids + systemic antivirals.
  - The **[[Herpetic Eye Disease Study (HEDS)]]** demonstrated that long-term oral [[acyclovir]] (400 mg BID) significantly reduces recurrence of HSV keratitis.
- **Acanthamoeba keratitis:** Dual therapy with **[[polyhexamethylene biguanide (PHMB)] 0.02%** or **[[chlorhexidine]] 0.02%** combined with a **[[diamidine]]** (e.g., [[propamidine isethionate]] 0.1% [Brolene]). Treatment is prolonged (months) and often challenging.

#### Anti-Inflammatory and Immunomodulatory Therapy

- **Topical [[corticosteroids]]:** Used judiciously for immune-mediated stromal keratitis, graft rejection, and post-surgical inflammation. Must be avoided in active epithelial infectious keratitis (especially fungal and Acanthamoeba). Risks include elevated [[IOP]], [[posterior subcapsular cataract]], and potentiation of infection.
- **Topical [[cyclosporine A]]** (0.05%–2%) and **[[tacrolimus]]** (0.03%–0.1%): Calcineurin inhibitors used for chronic ocular surface inflammation, [[vernal keratoconjunctivitis]], [[atopic keratoconjunctivitis]], and as steroid-sparing agents in corneal graft rejection prophylaxis.
- **Topical [[NSAIDs]]** (e.g., [[diclofenac]], [[ketorolac]]): Used for pain control and inflammation; caution regarding corneal melting with prolonged use.

#### Lubrication and Ocular Surface Optimization

- **Preservative-free [[artificial tears]]** and **[[lubricating ointments]]**: Mainstay for [[dry eye disease]], [[exposure keratopathy]], and supportive care in corneal healing.
- **[[Autologous serum tears]]** (20%): Rich in [[epidermal growth factor (EGF)]], [[fibronectin]], and [[vitamin A]]; used for severe dry eye, [[neurotrophic keratopathy]], and persistent epithelial defects.
- **[[Cenegermin]]** (recombinant human [[nerve growth factor]]): FDA-approved topical treatment for **[[neurotrophic keratitis]]** (stages 2 and 3).

#### Hyperosmotic Agents

- **Hypertonic [[sodium chloride]] (5%)** drops and ointment: Draw fluid from the edematous cornea osmotically; used in [[Fuchs' dystrophy]] and other causes of corneal edema as a temporizing measure.

### Surgical Management

#### Corneal Transplantation (Keratoplasty)

Corneal transplantation is one of the most successful forms of solid organ transplantation. Modern techniques have evolved from full-thickness to selective, layer-specific procedures:

| Procedure | Indication | Layers Replaced | Key Advantages |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Penetrating keratoplasty (PKP)]]** | Full-thickness corneal disease, severe scarring, failed grafts | All layers | Addresses all-layer pathology |
| **[[Deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty (DALK)]]** | Stromal pathology with healthy endothelium (e.g., [[keratoconus]], stromal dystrophies, scars) | Epithelium, Bowman's, stroma | Preserves host endothelium; lower rejection risk |
| **[[Descemet's stripping automated endothelial keratoplasty (DSAEK)]]** | Endothelial dysfunction ([[Fuchs' dystrophy]], [[pseudophakic bullous keratopathy]]) | Posterior stroma + Descemet's + endothelium (donor) | Faster visual recovery than PKP; less astigmatism |
| **[[Descemet's membrane endothelial keratoplasty (DMEK)]]** | Endothelial dysfunction | Descemet's membrane + endothelium only (donor) | Best visual outcomes for endothelial disease; lowest rejection rate |
| **[[Anterior lamellar keratoplasty (ALK)]]** | Superficial stromal opacities | Partial anterior stroma | Minimal structural disruption |

**Graft rejection** remains a significant concern, occurring in approximately 5–30% of PKP cases. It is mediated primarily by **[[T-cell]]**-driven immune responses against donor [[human leukocyte antigens (HLA)]]. Clinical signs include the classic mnemonic **"RSVP"**: Redness, Sensitivity (photophobia), Visual decline, Pain. Endothelial rejection (Khodadoust line) is the most visually threatening form. Treatment involves intensive topical corticosteroids (e.g., prednisolone acetate 1% hourly) ± systemic immunosuppression.

#### Corneal Collagen Cross-Linking (CXL)

**[[Corneal collagen cross-linking (CXL)]]** is the standard of care for halting progression of [[keratoconus]] and other corneal ectasias. The **Dresden protocol** (standard, epithelium-off) involves:

1. Epithelial debridement.
2. Application of **[[riboflavin]] (vitamin B2) 0.1%** for 30 minutes.
3. Irradiation with **[[ultraviolet-A (UVA)]] light (370 nm, 3 mW/cm²)** for 30 minutes (total energy: 5.4 J/cm²).

This induces covalent cross-links between collagen fibrils, increasing corneal rigidity and biomechanical strength. **Accelerated CXL** protocols (higher irradiance, shorter duration) and **transepithelial (epi-on) CXL** are also in use, though their equivalence to the standard protocol is still under investigation. CXL combined with other procedures (e.g., **CXL + [[photorefractive keratectomy (PRK)]]**, termed "Athens protocol" or "CXL Plus") is used to simultaneously strengthen and reshape the cornea.

#### Phototherapeutic Keratectomy (PTK)

**[[Phototherapeutic keratectomy (PTK)]]** uses the **[[excimer laser]]** (193 nm argon fluoride) to ablate superficial corneal pathology, including:

- Recurrent corneal erosions refractory to medical therapy.
- Anterior corneal dystrophies (e.g., [[EBMD]], [[Reis-Bücklers dystrophy]], [[granular dystrophy]]).
- Superficial corneal scars and irregularities.
- [[Band keratopathy]] (as an alternative to EDTA chelation).
- [[Salzmann's nodular degeneration]].

#### Keratoprosthesis

For patients with multiple failed grafts or severe ocular surface disease precluding standard keratoplasty, **[[keratoprosthesis]]** (artificial cornea) may be considered:

- **[[Boston Keratoprosthesis (KPro)]] Type I:** Most commonly used; requires lifelong topical antibiotic prophylaxis and monitoring for complications (glaucoma, retroprosthetic membrane, endophthalmitis, sterile keratolysis).
- **[[Osteo-odonto-keratoprosthesis (OOKP)]]:** Uses autologous tooth root and alveolar bone as a biological skirt; reserved for the most severe cases (e.g., [[Stevens-Johnson syndrome]], [[ocular cicatricial pemphigoid]], severe chemical burns).

#### Other Surgical Interventions

- **[[Amniotic membrane transplantation (AMT)]]:** Used as a biological bandage or graft for persistent epithelial defects, corneal ulcers, [[symblepharon]] lysis, and limbal stem cell deficiency. Amniotic membrane has anti-inflammatory, anti-scarring, and pro-epithelialization properties.
- **[[Limbal stem cell transplantation]]:** For [[limbal stem cell deficiency]]. Options include [[conjunctival-limbal autograft (CLAU)]], [[living-related conjunctival-limbal allograft (lr-CLAL)]], and **[[cultivated limbal epithelial transplantation (CLET)]]**.
- **[[Intrastromal corneal ring segments (ICRS)]]** (e.g., [[Intacs]], [[Ferrara rings]]): Implanted into the corneal stroma to flatten the cone and reduce irregular astigmatism in [[keratoconus]].
- **[[Conjunctival flap]] (Gundersen flap):** Used for chronic, non-healing corneal ulcers or perforations when visual rehabilitation is not the primary goal.
- **Tissue adhesives** (e.g., **[[cyanoacrylate glue]]**, **[[fibrin glue]]**): Used for small corneal perforations and descemetoceles as a temporizing measure.

## Contact Lens-Related Corneal Complications

Given the prevalence of [[contact lens]] wear, corneal complications related to contact lenses deserve special mention:

- **[[Contact lens-associated microbial keratitis]]:** Most commonly caused by [[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]] (soft lenses) and [[Acanthamoeba]] (poor hygiene, water exposure). Risk factors include overnight wear, poor hygiene, swimming in lenses, and use of tap water for lens care.
- **[[Contact lens-induced peripheral ulcer (CLPU)]]:** Sterile, small, peripheral infiltrate; typically self-limiting after lens discontinuation.
- **[[Contact lens acute red eye (CLARE)]]:** Acute inflammatory response, often associated with overnight wear.
- **[[Corneal neovascularization]]:** Chronic hypoxia from low-Dk lens materials stimulates limbal vessel ingrowth.
- **[[Giant papillary conjunctivitis (GPC)]]:** Immune-mediated response to lens deposits.
- **[[Corneal warpage]]:** Reversible topographic changes from rigid or soft lens wear; important to identify before [[refractive surgery]] screening.

## Emerging Therapies and Future Directions

The field of corneal therapeutics is rapidly evolving:

- **[[Rho kinase (ROCK) inhibitors]]** (e.g., [[netarsudil]], [[ripasudil]]): Under investigation for promoting corneal endothelial cell proliferation and potentially reducing the need for endothelial keratoplasty.
- **[[Cultured corneal endothelial cell injection therapy]]:** Pioneering work by Kinoshita and colleagues has demonstrated that injection of cultured human corneal endothelial cells combined with a ROCK inhibitor into the anterior chamber can restore endothelial function in [[bullous keratopathy]], potentially revolutionizing treatment of endothelial disease.
- **[[Bioengineered corneas]]** and **[[decellularized corneal scaffolds]]:** Under development as alternatives to donor tissue.
- **[[Gene therapy]]** for corneal dystrophies: Targeting specific mutations (e.g., TGFBI) to prevent or reverse dystrophic changes.
- **[[Femtosecond laser]]-assisted corneal surgery:** Increasingly used for precise lamellar dissection in keratoplasty and creation of intrastromal pockets for drug delivery or implant insertion.
- **[[Artificial intelligence (AI)]]** in corneal diagnostics: Machine learning algorithms for automated detection of keratoconus, grading of corneal opacities, and prediction of graft survival.

## Prognosis

The prognosis for corneal disease varies widely depending on the specific condition, severity, and timeliness of intervention:

- **Corneal abrasions:** Excellent prognosis; most heal within 24–72 hours.
- **Bacterial keratitis:** Good prognosis with early, appropriate treatment; central ulcers and virulent organisms (e.g., Pseudomonas) carry higher risk of scarring and visual loss.
- **Keratoconus:** Generally good with modern management (spectacles → contact lenses → CXL → ICRS → keratoplasty as needed). CXL has dramatically reduced the need for transplantation.
- **Fuchs' dystrophy:** Excellent visual outcomes with modern endothelial keratoplasty (DMEK achieves ≥20/25 in >75% of cases).
- **Acanthamoeba keratitis:** Guarded; often requires prolonged treatment and may result in significant scarring.
- **Chemical burns:** Prognosis depends on severity (Roper-Hall classification); severe alkali burns carry a poor prognosis due to limbal stem cell destruction and chronic inflammation.
