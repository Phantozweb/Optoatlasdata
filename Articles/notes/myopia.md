

# Myopia

**Myopia** (from Greek *myein*, "to close," and *ops*, "eye"), commonly referred to as **nearsightedness** or **short-sightedness**, is a highly prevalent [[refractive error]] in which parallel rays of light entering the eye are focused anterior to the [[retina]] when [[accommodation]] is relaxed, resulting in blurred distance vision. It is the most common ocular disorder worldwide and represents a significant and growing public health concern, with projections estimating that approximately 50% of the global population will be affected by 2050. Myopia ranges in severity from mild, easily correctable forms to high (pathological) myopia, which carries substantial risk of sight-threatening complications including [[myopic macular degeneration]], [[retinal detachment]], [[glaucoma]], and [[posterior staphyloma]].

---

## Epidemiology

Myopia prevalence varies dramatically by geographic region, ethnicity, age, and environmental exposure. East and Southeast Asian countries report the highest prevalence rates, with studies in urban centers of China, South Korea, Singapore, and Taiwan documenting rates exceeding 80–90% among young adults. In contrast, prevalence in sub-Saharan Africa and rural South America remains comparatively low (5–15%), though it is rising.

| **Region / Population** | **Estimated Prevalence** | **Trend** |
|---|---|---|
| East Asia (urban young adults) | 80–97% | Rapidly increasing |
| Europe (young adults) | 40–50% | Increasing |
| North America (young adults) | 35–45% | Increasing |
| Sub-Saharan Africa | 5–15% | Slowly increasing |
| Global (all ages, 2020 estimate) | ~34% (~2.6 billion) | Increasing |
| Global projection (2050) | ~50% (~4.8 billion) | — |

**High myopia** (≥ −6.00 D or axial length ≥ 26.0 mm) currently affects approximately 10% of myopic individuals globally, and this proportion is expected to rise to nearly 10% of the total world population by 2050. The economic burden of uncorrected and pathological myopia is enormous, with estimates exceeding $250 billion USD annually in lost productivity and healthcare costs.

The onset of myopia typically occurs during childhood, with peak progression between ages 8 and 15 years. Earlier onset is associated with greater ultimate myopic refractive error and higher risk of pathological sequelae. A secular trend toward earlier onset and higher final myopia has been documented across multiple populations over the past several decades.

## Classification

Myopia can be classified by several schema depending on the clinical or research context:

### By Degree of Refractive Error

| **Category** | **Spherical Equivalent** |
|---|---|
| Low myopia | −0.50 D to −3.00 D |
| Moderate myopia | −3.25 D to −6.00 D |
| High myopia | Greater than −6.00 D |

### By Etiology and Clinical Behavior

- **Simple (physiological) myopia**: The most common form; results from a mismatch between the optical power of the [[cornea]] and [[crystalline lens]] and the [[axial length]] of the eye. Typically stabilizes in early adulthood and does not cause degenerative changes.
- **Pathological (degenerative) myopia**: Characterized by progressive axial elongation, often exceeding 26.5 mm, with associated structural changes in the [[sclera]], [[choroid]], [[Bruch's membrane]], and [[retinal pigment epithelium (RPE)]]. Carries significant risk of vision loss.
- **Secondary myopia**: Myopia arising secondary to other ocular or systemic conditions, including [[nuclear sclerotic cataract]], [[keratoconus]], medication-induced myopia (e.g., topiramate, sulfonamides), or elevated [[blood glucose]] in uncontrolled [[diabetes mellitus]].
- **Pseudomyopia**: A transient myopic shift caused by excessive [[ciliary muscle]] spasm (accommodative spasm), often seen in young patients with prolonged near work. True refractive error is less myopic or even [[hyperopic]] when measured under [[cycloplegia]].

### By Optical Mechanism

- **Axial myopia**: Due to an excessively long axial length relative to the refractive power of the eye. This is by far the most common mechanism and the primary driver of pathological myopia.
- **Refractive myopia**: Due to excessive refractive power of the cornea and/or lens, with a normal axial length. Subtypes include:
  - **Curvature myopia**: Increased curvature (steepening) of the cornea or lens.
  - **Index myopia**: Increased refractive index of the crystalline lens, as seen in nuclear sclerotic cataract.
  - **Anterior chamber myopia**: Forward displacement of the lens.

## Pathophysiology

### Optical Basis

In the emmetropic eye, the combined refractive power of the [[cornea]] (approximately +43 D) and the [[crystalline lens]] (approximately +15–20 D) focuses parallel light rays precisely on the [[fovea]] when accommodation is relaxed. In myopia, this focal point falls anterior to the retina. The degree of defocus is quantified in [[diopters]] (D) of negative (concave) lens power required to shift the focal point back onto the retina.

Each millimeter of excess axial length corresponds to approximately −2.50 to −3.00 D of myopia, though this relationship varies with corneal power and lens position.

### Axial Elongation and Scleral Remodeling

The dominant mechanism underlying most myopia, particularly progressive and pathological forms, is excessive elongation of the [[globe]], driven primarily by expansion of the posterior segment. This process involves complex biochemical remodeling of the [[sclera]]:

- **Scleral thinning**: The posterior sclera in myopic eyes is significantly thinner than in emmetropic eyes, particularly at the posterior pole. In high myopia, scleral thickness may be reduced by 50% or more.
- **Extracellular matrix (ECM) degradation**: There is a net loss of [[collagen]] (particularly type I collagen), [[proteoglycans]], and [[glycosaminoglycans]] in the scleral ECM. Upregulation of [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs), especially MMP-2, and downregulation of tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases (TIMPs) have been demonstrated in animal models and human myopic scleral tissue.
- **Fibroblast transformation**: Scleral [[fibroblasts]] in myopic eyes show altered phenotype, reduced collagen synthesis, and increased expression of degradative enzymes.
- **Biomechanical weakening**: The cumulative effect of ECM loss and scleral thinning is a reduction in scleral rigidity and resistance to deformation under normal [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP), facilitating progressive elongation.

### Choroidal Involvement

The [[choroid]] plays a critical intermediary role in the signaling cascade that regulates eye growth:

- **Choroidal thinning**: Myopic eyes exhibit significantly thinner choroids, particularly subfoveally. Subfoveal choroidal thickness in high myopia may be less than 100 μm, compared to 250–350 μm in emmetropic eyes.
- **Choroidal blood flow**: Reduced choroidal perfusion has been documented in myopic eyes and may contribute to secondary degenerative changes in the [[RPE]] and outer retina.
- **Bidirectional thickness modulation**: Animal studies have demonstrated that the choroid can rapidly thicken or thin in response to optical defocus signals, acting as a "fine-tuning" mechanism for retinal position relative to the focal plane. Myopic defocus (image focused behind the retina) induces choroidal thickening and slows axial elongation, while hyperopic defocus (image focused in front of the retina) induces choroidal thinning and accelerates elongation.

### Retinal Signaling and Emmetropization

The process of [[emmetropization]]—the visually guided homeostatic mechanism by which the developing eye adjusts its axial length to match its optical power—is central to understanding myopia development:

- **Local retinal control**: Extensive evidence from animal models (chick, tree shrew, guinea pig, marmoset, rhesus macaque) demonstrates that eye growth is regulated locally by the retina, largely independent of central (brain-mediated) pathways. Sectioning the [[optic nerve]] does not prevent experimentally induced myopia.
- **Retinal neurotransmitter cascades**: Multiple retinal signaling molecules have been implicated in the regulation of eye growth, including:
  - **[[Dopamine]]**: A key inhibitory signal for axial elongation. Bright light exposure stimulates retinal dopamine release, which is believed to be one mechanism by which outdoor activity is protective against myopia. Dopamine agonists inhibit experimental myopia in animal models.
  - **[[Acetylcholine]] / Muscarinic pathways**: Muscarinic receptor antagonists (e.g., [[atropine]]) potently inhibit experimental myopia, though the precise receptor subtypes and downstream mechanisms remain under investigation. M1 and M4 receptors on the retina and sclera have been implicated.
  - **Retinoic acid**: Levels of [[retinoic acid]] in the retina and choroid change in response to defocus and are thought to modulate scleral ECM remodeling.
  - **Nitric oxide (NO)**, **glucagon**, **vasoactive intestinal peptide (VIP)**, and **GABA** have also been implicated in various animal models.

### Genetic Factors

Myopia has a strong hereditary component. Twin studies estimate heritability at 60–90%. Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified over 200 genetic loci associated with refractive error and myopia, including genes involved in:

- Extracellular matrix structure and remodeling (e.g., *COL1A1*, *COL2A1*, *LAMA2*)
- Retinal neurotransmission and signaling (e.g., *GJD2*, *RASGRF1*)
- Eye growth regulation (e.g., *PAX6*, *ZC3H11B*)
- Light-dependent pathways (e.g., *CHRNG*)

However, the rapid increase in myopia prevalence over recent decades cannot be explained by genetics alone and underscores the critical role of environmental and behavioral factors.

### Environmental and Behavioral Risk Factors

| **Risk Factor** | **Evidence Level** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|---|
| Reduced outdoor time | Strong (meta-analyses, RCTs) | Increased retinal [[dopamine]] release from bright light; greater depth of field from pupil constriction; spectral composition of sunlight |
| Prolonged near work | Moderate | Sustained [[accommodative]] effort; peripheral hyperopic defocus; reduced blink rate |
| Higher education level | Strong (epidemiological) | Proxy for near work and reduced outdoor time |
| Urbanization | Strong | Reduced outdoor exposure; increased near work demands |
| Parental myopia | Strong | Genetic predisposition + shared environment |
| Screen time | Emerging | Likely a proxy for near work and reduced outdoor time; direct effects under investigation |

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

The cardinal symptom of myopia is **blurred distance vision**. Patients may report:

- Difficulty reading road signs, whiteboards, or distant displays
- Squinting to improve distance clarity (the "pinhole effect" of narrowing the [[palpebral fissure]])
- [[Asthenopia]] (eye strain), particularly with prolonged visual tasks
- Frontal [[headache]], often associated with sustained squinting or accommodative effort
- In children, behavioral signs such as sitting close to screens, holding books very close, poor academic performance, or reluctance to participate in outdoor sports

Near vision is characteristically preserved and may be excellent, as the myopic [[far point]] is closer than infinity.

### Signs

- **Reduced [[visual acuity]]** at distance, correctable with negative (concave) lenses
- **Deeper [[anterior chamber]]** (in axial myopia)
- **Larger [[pupil]]** diameter (a subtle and inconsistent finding)
- In high/pathological myopia:
  - [[Posterior staphyloma]]: Outpouching of the posterior sclera, best visualized on [[optical coherence tomography]] (OCT) or [[B-scan ultrasonography]]
  - [[Lacquer cracks]]: Breaks in [[Bruch's membrane]]
  - [[Myopic choroidal neovascularization]] (mCNV): Subretinal neovascular membrane, a leading cause of central vision loss in pathological myopia
  - [[Chorioretinal atrophy]]: Patchy or diffuse atrophy of the [[RPE]] and [[choriocapillaris]]
  - [[Fuchs' spot]]: A pigmented macular lesion representing organized mCNV
  - [[Lattice degeneration]]: Peripheral retinal thinning predisposing to [[retinal tears]] and [[rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]]
  - [[Tilted optic disc]], [[peripapillary atrophy]], and [[optic disc crescent]]
  - [[Myopic tractional maculopathy]]: Including [[foveoschisis]], [[macular hole]], and [[macular detachment]]

## Diagnosis

### Refraction

- **[[Subjective refraction]]**: The gold standard for determining the corrective prescription. Performed using a [[phoropter]] or trial frame with progressive addition of minus (concave) spherical lenses until best-corrected distance [[visual acuity]] is achieved. The principle of "maximum plus / minimum minus" should be applied to avoid over-minusing, which can induce accommodative spasm and pseudomyopia.
- **[[Cycloplegic refraction]]**: Essential in children and young adults to eliminate [[accommodative]] tone and reveal the true refractive error. Agents include [[cyclopentolate]] 1% (most commonly used) and [[atropine]] 1% (gold standard for complete cycloplegia, particularly in heavily pigmented irides). Failure to perform cycloplegic refraction in pediatric patients may result in significant overestimation of myopia.
- **[[Autorefraction]]**: Objective measurement of refractive error using automated instruments. Useful for screening and as a starting point for subjective refraction. Cycloplegic autorefraction is widely used in epidemiological studies.
- **[[Retinoscopy]]**: An objective technique in which the examiner observes the movement of the retinal reflex (red reflex) while sweeping a streak or spot of light across the pupil and neutralizing the reflex with lenses. "With" movement indicates a need for more plus (or less minus) power; "against" movement indicates a need for more minus power. Retinoscopy under cycloplegia remains a fundamental skill in pediatric optometry and ophthalmology.

### Biometry and Axial Length Measurement

- **[[Optical biometry]]** (e.g., IOLMaster, Lenstar): Non-contact, highly precise measurement of axial length, anterior chamber depth, lens thickness, and corneal curvature. Axial length measurement is increasingly recognized as a critical parameter for monitoring myopia progression, as it directly correlates with the risk of pathological complications.
- **[[A-scan ultrasonography]]**: Contact ultrasound measurement of axial length. Less precise than optical biometry but useful when optical media are opaque.

| **Axial Length** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|
| < 22.0 mm | Likely hyperopic |
| 22.0–24.5 mm | Emmetropic range |
| 24.5–26.0 mm | Moderate myopia range |
| 26.0–28.0 mm | High myopia; increased risk of pathological changes |
| > 28.0 mm | Very high myopia; significant risk of degenerative complications |

### Corneal Topography and Tomography

- **[[Corneal topography]]** (e.g., Placido disc-based systems) and **[[corneal tomography]]** (e.g., [[Scheimpflug imaging]], [[Pentacam]]): Essential for evaluating corneal curvature, ruling out [[keratoconus]] or other ectatic disorders, and planning [[refractive surgery]]. Myopic corneas may show steeper central curvature.

### Fundus Examination

- **[[Dilated fundus examination]]**: Critical in all myopic patients, particularly those with high myopia, to evaluate for peripheral retinal pathology ([[lattice degeneration]], [[retinal holes]], [[retinal tears]]), posterior pole changes ([[staphyloma]], [[chorioretinal atrophy]], [[lacquer cracks]], [[mCNV]]), and [[optic disc]] abnormalities.
- **[[Optical coherence tomography (OCT)]]**: High-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the retina and choroid. Indispensable for detecting and monitoring:
  - [[Myopic tractional maculopathy]] (foveoschisis, lamellar or full-thickness macular hole)
  - [[Choroidal neovascularization]]
  - Subfoveal [[choroidal thickness]] (a biomarker for myopia progression and treatment response)
  - [[Dome-shaped macula]]
- **[[OCT angiography (OCTA)]]**: Non-invasive visualization of retinal and choroidal vasculature; useful for detecting and monitoring mCNV without the need for [[fluorescein angiography]].
- **[[Fundus photography]]** and **[[ultra-widefield imaging]]**: Documentation of peripheral and posterior pole pathology; essential for longitudinal monitoring.

### Additional Investigations

- **[[Visual field testing]]**: May be indicated in high myopia to evaluate for [[glaucomatous]] visual field loss, as myopic eyes are at increased risk for [[open-angle glaucoma]]. Interpretation of visual fields in highly myopic eyes requires caution due to potential artifacts from tilted discs and peripapillary atrophy.
- **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]**: Useful for evaluating posterior staphyloma and posterior segment pathology when fundus view is limited.

## Complications of Myopia

The risk of sight-threatening complications increases exponentially with increasing axial length and degree of myopia. There is **no safe level of myopia**; even low myopia carries a statistically increased risk compared to emmetropia.

| **Complication** | **Relative Risk (High Myopia vs. Emmetropia)** |
|---|---|
| [[Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]] | 5–15× |
| [[Myopic macular degeneration]] | 40–100× |
| [[Open-angle glaucoma]] | 2–3× |
| [[Posterior subcapsular cataract]] | 2–5× |
| [[Choroidal neovascularization]] | Significantly elevated |
| [[Myopic tractional maculopathy]] | Specific to high/pathological myopia |

Myopic macular degeneration (MMD) is now recognized as a leading cause of irreversible vision impairment worldwide, particularly in East Asian populations.

## Management

### Optical Correction

The primary management of myopia is optical correction to restore clear distance vision:

- **[[Spectacles]]**: Single-vision concave (minus) lenses remain the most common and accessible form of correction. Standard single-vision lenses correct central refractive error but do not address peripheral hyperopic defocus, which may contribute to myopia progression.
- **[[Contact lenses]]**: Soft, rigid gas-permeable ([[RGP]]), and hybrid contact lenses provide excellent optical correction. Standard soft contact lenses, like standard spectacles, do not inherently slow myopia progression.

### Myopia Control (Progression Management)

A rapidly evolving field focused on slowing the rate of myopia progression in children and adolescents. The goal is to reduce the ultimate degree of myopia and thereby lower the lifetime risk of pathological complications.

#### Pharmacological Interventions

- **Low-dose [[atropine]]**: The most extensively studied pharmacological intervention for myopia control.
  - **Mechanism**: Believed to act primarily via non-accommodative muscarinic receptor pathways in the retina and/or sclera, inhibiting scleral remodeling and axial elongation. The exact receptor subtype(s) and downstream signaling remain under active investigation.
  - **Dosing**: The ATOM (Atropine for the Treatment of Myopia) studies from Singapore demonstrated a dose-dependent effect, with higher concentrations (1%, 0.5%, 0.1%) providing greater initial slowing but also greater side effects (mydriasis, loss of accommodation, photophobia) and a significant rebound effect upon cessation. Low-dose atropine (0.01%–0.05%) has emerged as the preferred concentration, offering a favorable balance of efficacy (~30–50% reduction in progression), minimal side effects, and reduced rebound.
  - **LAMP Study**: The Low-Concentration Atropine for Myopia Progression study confirmed that 0.05% atropine was more effective than 0.025% and 0.01% over 2 years, with acceptable side effects.
  - **Clinical considerations**: Typically prescribed as a once-nightly instillation. Monitoring includes periodic refraction, axial length measurement, and assessment for side effects. Treatment duration is generally recommended for at least 2 years, with gradual tapering to minimize rebound.

| **Atropine Concentration** | **Approximate Efficacy (% Reduction in Progression)** | **Side Effects** | **Rebound Risk** |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1.0% | 70–80% | Significant (mydriasis, cycloplegia, photophobia, near blur) | High |
| 0.1% | 50–60% | Moderate | Moderate |
| 0.05% | 40–50% | Mild | Low–Moderate |
| 0.01% | 20–30% | Minimal | Low |

#### Optical Interventions for Myopia Control

- **[[Orthokeratology]] (Ortho-K)**: Specially designed reverse-geometry [[RGP]] lenses worn overnight to temporarily reshape the [[corneal epithelium]], flattening the central cornea and steepening the mid-periphery. This creates relative peripheral myopic defocus, which is hypothesized to provide a signal to slow axial elongation.
  - **Efficacy**: Multiple studies and meta-analyses demonstrate approximately 40–60% reduction in axial elongation compared to single-vision spectacles or contact lenses.
  - **Risks**: [[Microbial keratitis]] (rare but serious), corneal staining, lens binding. Strict hygiene protocols and regular follow-up are essential.

- **[[Multifocal soft contact lenses]] (center-distance design)**: Dual-focus or extended depth-of-focus soft contact lenses that simultaneously correct central refractive error and impose peripheral myopic defocus.
  - **MiSight® 1 day** (CooperVision): The first FDA-approved contact lens specifically indicated for myopia control in children aged 8–12 at initiation. The 3-year BLINK and subsequent studies demonstrated approximately 59% reduction in myopia progression and 52% reduction in axial elongation.
  - Other multifocal designs (e.g., NaturalVue, extended depth-of-focus lenses) have also shown efficacy in clinical trials.

- **[[Defocus Incorporated Multiple Segments (DIMS) spectacle lenses]]** (e.g., MiYOSMART by Hoya): Spectacle lenses incorporating a central optical zone for distance correction surrounded by multiple segments of +3.50 D relative positive power, creating simultaneous myopic defocus across the retina.
  - **Efficacy**: The initial 2-year randomized controlled trial demonstrated approximately 52% reduction in myopia progression and 62% reduction in axial elongation.

- **[[Highly Aspherical Lenslet (HAL) spectacle lenses]]** (e.g., Stellest by Essilor): Incorporate a constellation of aspherical lenslets creating a volume of myopic defocus anterior to the retina.
  - **Efficacy**: Approximately 55–67% reduction in myopia progression reported in clinical trials.

- **[[Peripheral defocus spectacle lenses]]**: Various other designs incorporating peripheral plus power or controlled peripheral defocus are under development and clinical evaluation.

#### Behavioral and Environmental Interventions

- **Increased outdoor time**: Robust evidence from randomized controlled trials (e.g., the Guangzhou Outdoor Activity Longitudinal Study, the Sydney Myopia Study) supports a protective effect of outdoor exposure. A minimum of **80–120 minutes per day** of outdoor activity is recommended. The protective effect is believed to be mediated primarily by high-intensity ambient light stimulating retinal [[dopamine]] release, though other factors (UV exposure, spectral composition, greater viewing distances, increased depth of field) may contribute.
- **Reduced sustained near work**: While the evidence is less robust than for outdoor time, limiting prolonged continuous near work and encouraging regular breaks (e.g., the "20-20-20 rule": every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds) is widely recommended.

#### Combination Therapy

Emerging evidence supports the use of combination strategies (e.g., low-dose atropine combined with orthokeratology, or atropine combined with myopia-control spectacle lenses) for children with rapidly progressing myopia or those who show insufficient response to monotherapy. Several clinical trials are currently investigating optimal combination protocols.

### Refractive Surgery

Refractive surgery aims to permanently reduce or eliminate the need for optical correction in stable adult myopia. It does **not** address the underlying axial elongation or reduce the risk of pathological complications associated with high myopia.

- **[[LASIK]] (Laser-Assisted In Situ Keratomileusis)**: The most commonly performed refractive procedure worldwide. An [[excimer laser]] ablates corneal stromal tissue beneath a hinged flap created by a [[microkeratome]] or [[femtosecond laser]], flattening the central cornea to reduce its refractive power. Approved for myopia up to approximately −12.00 D (varies by regulatory body and corneal thickness).
- **[[PRK]] (Photorefractive Keratectomy)**: Surface ablation technique in which the [[corneal epithelium]] is removed and the excimer laser is applied directly to [[Bowman's layer]] and the anterior stroma. Preferred in patients with thinner corneas or those at higher risk of flap complications.
- **[[SMILE]] (Small Incision Lenticule Extraction)**: A flapless, minimally invasive procedure using a femtosecond laser to create and extract a stromal lenticule through a small peripheral incision. Approved for myopia up to −10.00 D. Advantages include preservation of corneal biomechanical integrity and reduced dry eye symptoms compared to LASIK.
- **[[Phakic intraocular lenses]] (pIOLs)**: Implantable lenses placed in the [[anterior chamber]] or [[posterior chamber]] (e.g., [[Visian ICL]], [[EVO ICL]]) without removing the natural crystalline lens. Indicated for high myopia (typically > −8.00 D) or when corneal thickness is insufficient for laser refractive surgery. Provides excellent optical quality. Risks include [[cataract]] formation, [[endothelial cell loss]], [[pigment dispersion]], and [[pupillary block]].
- **[[Refractive lens exchange]] (RLE)**: Removal of the clear crystalline lens and replacement with an [[intraocular lens (IOL)]], analogous to [[cataract surgery]]. Considered in very high myopia, particularly in presbyopic patients. Carries a higher risk of [[retinal detachment]] in highly myopic eyes.

### Management of Pathological Myopia Complications

- **[[Myopic choroidal neovascularization (mCNV)]]**: First-line treatment is intravitreal injection of [[anti-VEGF]] agents (e.g., [[ranibizumab]], [[aflibercept]], [[bevacizumab]]). Anti-VEGF therapy has revolutionized outcomes for mCNV, with the majority of patients achieving stabilization or improvement in visual acuity.
- **[[Myopic tractional maculopathy]]**: [[Pars plana vitrectomy]] with or without [[internal limiting membrane (ILM)]] peeling and/or gas tamponade may be indicated for progressive foveoschisis, macular hole, or macular detachment.
- **[[Retinal detachment]]**: Surgical repair via [[scleral buckle]], [[pars plana vitrectomy]], or combined procedures. Myopic eyes present unique surgical challenges due to thin sclera, posterior staphyloma, and large retinal breaks.
- **[[Posterior staphyloma]]**: [[Macular buckling]] surgery has been described for selected cases with progressive staphyloma-related maculopathy, though this remains a specialized and relatively uncommon procedure.
- **[[Glaucoma]]**: Standard IOP-lowering therapy ([[topical hypotensives]], [[selective laser trabeculoplasty]], [[filtration surgery]]). Diagnosis and monitoring of glaucoma in highly myopic eyes is challenging due to structural disc anomalies and visual field artifacts.

## Prognosis

The prognosis for simple myopia with appropriate optical correction is excellent, with most patients achieving normal best-corrected visual acuity throughout life. However, the prognosis for pathological myopia is guarded, as progressive degenerative changes may lead to irreversible central or peripheral vision loss despite optimal management. Early identification of at-risk children, aggressive myopia control during the critical growth years, and lifelong surveillance for complications in high myopes are essential to minimizing the burden of this condition.

Reducing final myopia by even 1 diopter has been estimated to reduce the lifetime risk of myopic macular degeneration by approximately 40%, underscoring the profound public health impact of effective myopia control strategies.

---

## Emerging Research and Future Directions

- **Genetic risk scores**: Development of polygenic risk scores to identify children at highest risk for myopia development and rapid progression, enabling targeted early intervention.
- **Novel pharmacological agents**: Investigation of agents targeting specific signaling pathways (e.g., selective muscarinic antagonists, dopamine agonists, nitric oxide donors, scleral cross-linking agents) for myopia control.
- **Scleral cross-linking**: Experimental approaches to strengthen the sclera and resist elongation, analogous to [[corneal cross-linking]] for keratoconus.
- **Light therapy**: Repeated low-level red-light (RLRL) therapy (e.g., 650 nm wavelength, delivered via a desktop device for 3 minutes twice daily) has shown promising results in Chinese clinical trials, with reported efficacy of 60–80% reduction in axial elongation. However, concerns regarding retinal safety and long-term outcomes require further investigation before widespread adoption.
- **Wearable monitoring devices**: Smart glasses and wearable light sensors to objectively quantify outdoor light exposure and near work behavior, enabling personalized behavioral interventions.
- **Artificial intelligence**: AI-driven algorithms for predicting myopia onset and progression based on biometric, genetic, and environmental data.

---

## Summary

Myopia is a complex, multifactorial refractive disorder with a rapidly increasing global prevalence that poses a major threat to public eye health. Its pathophysiology involves a sophisticated interplay of genetic susceptibility, environmental triggers, retinal signaling cascades, choroidal modulation, and scleral remodeling leading to excessive axial elongation. Comprehensive management requires accurate diagnosis (including cycloplegic refraction and axial length measurement), appropriate optical correction, evidence-based myopia control strategies in children (pharmacological, optical, and behavioral), refractive surgical options for stable adult myopia, and vigilant long-term surveillance for the sight-threatening complications of pathological myopia. The field of myopia research is advancing rapidly, with novel therapeutic approaches and precision medicine strategies on the horizon.
