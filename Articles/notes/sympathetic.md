# Sympathetic Ophthalmia

---

**Sympathetic Ophthalmia** (SO), also referred to as **sympathetic uveitis**, is a rare, bilateral, diffuse, [[granulomatous uveitis|granulomatous]] [[panuveitis]] that occurs following penetrating trauma or surgical intervention to one eye (the **exciting eye**), subsequently provoking an autoimmune inflammatory response in the contralateral, previously uninjured eye (the **sympathizing eye**). It represents one of the most feared complications in [[ophthalmology]] and [[optometry]] due to its potential to cause irreversible bilateral blindness if not recognized and treated promptly.

First described comprehensively by William Mackenzie in 1840, sympathetic ophthalmia remains a condition of paramount clinical importance. Despite its rarity—estimated at approximately 0.2–0.5% following penetrating ocular trauma and 0.01% following intraocular surgery—its devastating bilateral visual consequences demand that every eye care professional maintain a high index of suspicion in the appropriate clinical context.

---

## Epidemiology

Sympathetic ophthalmia is exceedingly rare but carries disproportionately severe consequences. Understanding its epidemiological profile is essential for risk stratification and clinical vigilance.

| **Epidemiological Parameter** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| **Overall Incidence** | 0.2–0.5% after penetrating ocular trauma; 0.01–0.03% after intraocular surgery |
| **Post-Vitreoretinal Surgery Incidence** | Approximately 1 in 800 to 1 in 1,500 cases |
| **Age Distribution** | Any age; bimodal peaks in children (under 10) and older adults (60–70 years) |
| **Sex Predilection** | Slight male predominance (likely reflecting higher rates of ocular trauma in males) |
| **Laterality** | Always bilateral by definition (exciting eye + sympathizing eye) |
| **Onset Latency** | 65% within 2 weeks to 3 months; can occur from 5 days to over 60 years post-injury |
| **HLA Associations** | [[HLA-A11]], [[HLA-DR4]], [[HLA-DRB1*04]] |

The incidence of sympathetic ophthalmia has evolved over time. With the advent of modern microsurgical techniques, improved wound closure, and the increasing frequency of [[vitreoretinal surgery]], the etiology profile has shifted. While penetrating trauma historically accounted for the vast majority of cases, surgical causes—including [[pars plana vitrectomy]], [[enucleation]] (paradoxically), [[cyclodestructive procedures]], and even repeated intraocular injections—now constitute a growing proportion of inciting events.

### Risk Factors

- **Penetrating ocular trauma**, especially involving the [[ciliary body]] or [[uveal tissue]]
- **Multiple intraocular surgeries** or surgical revisions
- **Vitreoretinal surgery** ([[pars plana vitrectomy]], [[scleral buckle]])
- **Wound dehiscence** or incarceration of [[uveal tissue]] in the wound
- **Cyclodestructive procedures** (e.g., [[transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]])
- **Retained intraocular foreign bodies**
- **Delayed primary wound repair** (greater than 24 hours post-injury)
- Genetic susceptibility: [[HLA-DR4]], [[HLA-DRB1*04]], [[HLA-A11]]

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of sympathetic ophthalmia is a complex interplay of autoimmune mechanisms, molecular mimicry, and loss of [[immune privilege]]. Understanding these mechanisms is critical for both diagnosis and rational therapeutic intervention.

### Immune Privilege and Its Breakdown

The eye is an [[immune-privileged]] organ, maintained by several mechanisms collectively termed **anterior chamber-associated immune deviation** ([[ACAID]]). These include:

- The [[blood-ocular barrier]] (comprising the [[blood-aqueous barrier]] and [[blood-retinal barrier]])
- Absence of lymphatic drainage from the intraocular compartment
- Expression of [[Fas ligand]] (FasL/CD95L) on ocular surface tissues, inducing apoptosis of infiltrating inflammatory cells
- Secretion of immunosuppressive cytokines such as [[TGF-β]], [[alpha-melanocyte-stimulating hormone]] (α-MSH), and [[vasoactive intestinal peptide]] (VIP) in the [[aqueous humor]]
- Low expression of [[major histocompatibility complex]] ([[MHC]]) class I and II molecules on intraocular cells

Penetrating trauma or surgery disrupts the blood-ocular barrier, exposing previously sequestered intraocular antigens—particularly those derived from [[uveal melanocytes]], [[retinal photoreceptors]], and the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] ([[RPE]])—to the systemic immune system. These antigens, having never been presented to the immune system during thymic education, are recognized as "foreign," triggering a robust adaptive immune response.

### Autoimmune Mechanisms

The prevailing theory of sympathetic ophthalmia pathogenesis centers on a **T-cell-mediated (Type IV) hypersensitivity reaction** directed against uveoretinal antigens. The key steps include:

1. **Antigen Release**: Trauma to the exciting eye releases sequestered ocular antigens, including [[S-antigen]] (retinal soluble antigen/arrestin), [[interphotoreceptor retinoid-binding protein]] ([[IRBP]]), [[melanin]]-associated antigens, [[tyrosinase]]-related proteins, and [[RPE65]].

2. **Antigen Presentation**: Released antigens are captured by [[antigen-presenting cells]] ([[APCs]]), including [[dendritic cells]] and [[macrophages]], which migrate to regional [[lymph nodes]] via disrupted tissue planes.

3. **T-Cell Activation**: APCs present processed uveoretinal antigens via [[MHC class II]] molecules to naïve [[CD4+ T helper cells]], leading to clonal expansion of antigen-specific [[Th1]] and [[Th17]] lymphocytes. [[Th1 cells]] produce [[interferon-gamma]] (IFN-γ) and [[interleukin-2]] (IL-2), while [[Th17 cells]] produce [[interleukin-17]] (IL-17), both of which are potent pro-inflammatory mediators.

4. **Systemic Dissemination and Bilateral Attack**: Activated T lymphocytes enter the systemic circulation and home to the contralateral (sympathizing) eye, where they recognize the same uveoretinal antigens present in normal ocular tissue. This initiates a bilateral granulomatous inflammatory cascade.

5. **Granuloma Formation**: The hallmark histopathological finding is the formation of non-caseating [[granulomas]] composed of [[epithelioid cells]], [[multinucleated giant cells]], and [[lymphocytes]], predominantly involving the [[choroid]] with relative sparing of the [[choriocapillaris]]. [[Dalen-Fuchs nodules]]—collections of epithelioid cells between the RPE and [[Bruch's membrane]]—are a characteristic but not pathognomonic finding.

### Molecular Targets

| **Autoantigen** | **Location** | **Role in Pathogenesis** |
|---|---|---|
| [[S-antigen]] (Arrestin) | [[Photoreceptor]] outer segments | Major target; induces experimental autoimmune uveitis (EAU) in animal models |
| [[IRBP]] (Interphotoreceptor retinoid-binding protein) | [[Interphotoreceptor matrix]] | Potent uveitogenic antigen |
| [[Tyrosinase]]-related proteins (TRP-1, TRP-2) | [[Melanocytes]], [[RPE]] | Melanocyte-specific antigens; link to [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]] |
| [[RPE65]] | [[Retinal pigment epithelium]] | RPE-specific antigen |
| [[Melanin]]-associated antigens | [[Uveal melanocytes]] | Shared antigens with cutaneous melanocytes |

### Histopathology

The classic histopathological picture of sympathetic ophthalmia includes:

- **Diffuse granulomatous inflammation** of the entire [[uveal tract]] ([[iris]], [[ciliary body]], [[choroid]])
- **Thickening of the choroid** with infiltration by [[lymphocytes]], [[epithelioid cells]], and [[multinucleated giant cells]]
- **Dalen-Fuchs nodules**: Focal collections of epithelioid histiocytes and depigmented RPE cells situated between the RPE and Bruch's membrane
- **Relative sparing of the choriocapillaris**: A distinguishing feature from other granulomatous uveitides
- **Absence of necrosis** (non-caseating granulomas)
- **Phagocytosed melanin granules** within epithelioid cells and giant cells
- Possible involvement of the [[optic nerve]], [[sclera]], and [[extraocular tissues]] in severe cases

## Clinical Presentation

### Temporal Profile

Sympathetic ophthalmia can develop at any time following the inciting event, though the majority of cases present within a characteristic window:

| **Time After Inciting Event** | **Percentage of Cases** |
|---|---|
| < 2 weeks | Rare (minimum reported: 5 days) |
| 2 weeks – 3 months | ~65% |
| 3 months – 1 year | ~80–90% (cumulative) |
| > 1 year | 10–20% |
| > 10 years | Rare but well-documented |
| > 50 years | Extremely rare case reports exist |

### Symptoms

Patients with sympathetic ophthalmia typically present with symptoms in the **sympathizing eye** (the previously uninjured eye), though the exciting eye may also demonstrate worsening inflammation. Symptoms include:

- **Blurred vision** or progressive visual loss in the sympathizing eye
- **Photophobia** (often severe)
- **Pain** (dull, aching periorbital or ocular pain)
- **Floaters** (due to [[vitritis]] or inflammatory debris)
- **Redness** ([[conjunctival injection]], [[ciliary flush]])
- **Tearing** ([[epiphora]])
- **Accommodation difficulty** (due to ciliary body inflammation)
- **Metamorphopsia** (if [[serous retinal detachment]] or choroidal thickening is present)

### Signs

#### Anterior Segment Findings

- **[[Granulomatous keratic precipitates]]** (KPs): Large, greasy, "mutton-fat" KPs on the [[corneal endothelium]], often arranged in a Arlt's triangle configuration inferiorly
- **[[Anterior chamber]] cells and flare**: Moderate to severe, reflecting breakdown of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]]
- **[[Iris nodules]]**: [[Koeppe nodules]] (at the pupillary margin) and [[Busacca nodules]] (on the iris stroma)
- **[[Posterior synechiae]]**: Adhesions between the [[iris]] and [[anterior lens capsule]]
- **[[Iris bombé]]** and secondary [[angle-closure glaucoma]] (in severe or chronic cases)
- **Heterochromia** or iris depigmentation (in chronic cases)

#### Posterior Segment Findings

- **[[Vitritis]]**: Mild to moderate vitreous cells and haze
- **[[Choroiditis]]**: Multifocal, creamy-yellow choroidal infiltrates, often in the mid-periphery and posterior pole
- **[[Dalen-Fuchs nodules]]**: Small, yellowish-white lesions at the level of the RPE, visible on [[fundoscopy]] and [[optical coherence tomography]] ([[OCT]])
- **[[Papillitis]]** or [[optic disc edema]]: Swelling and hyperemia of the [[optic nerve head]]
- **[[Exudative retinal detachment]]** (serous): Due to choroidal inflammation and fluid transudation beneath the [[neurosensory retina]]
- **[[Choroidal thickening]]**: Diffuse or focal, best appreciated on [[enhanced depth imaging OCT]] ([[EDI-OCT]])
- **[[Sunset glow fundus]]**: Depigmented, orange-red fundus appearance in chronic or recurrent cases (similar to [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]])
- **[[Peripapillary atrophy]]** and [[subretinal fibrosis]] in chronic stages

#### Extraocular Findings (Rare)

While sympathetic ophthalmia is primarily an ocular disease, rare extraocular manifestations have been reported, particularly in cases overlapping with [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]]:

- **[[Poliosis]]**: Whitening of eyelashes or eyebrows
- **[[Vitiligo]]**: Depigmented skin patches
- **[[Alopecia]]**: Hair loss
- **Meningeal signs**: Headache, neck stiffness (extremely rare)
- **Hearing disturbances**: [[Dysacusis]], [[tinnitus]] (extremely rare)

## Diagnosis

The diagnosis of sympathetic ophthalmia is primarily **clinical**, based on a characteristic history of penetrating ocular trauma or surgery followed by bilateral granulomatous panuveitis. However, ancillary testing plays a crucial role in confirming the diagnosis, assessing disease severity, monitoring treatment response, and excluding mimicking conditions.

### Diagnostic Criteria

There are no universally accepted formal diagnostic criteria, but the following clinical triad is highly suggestive:

1. **History of penetrating ocular trauma or intraocular surgery** in one eye (exciting eye)
2. **Bilateral granulomatous panuveitis** developing after the inciting event
3. **Exclusion of other causes** of bilateral granulomatous uveitis (particularly [[sarcoidosis]], [[tuberculosis]], [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]], and [[syphilis]])

### Ancillary Diagnostic Modalities

#### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

[[Optical coherence tomography]] ([[OCT]]), particularly [[spectral-domain OCT]] (SD-OCT) and [[enhanced depth imaging OCT]] (EDI-OCT), is indispensable in the evaluation and monitoring of sympathetic ophthalmia:

| **OCT Finding** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|
| Choroidal thickening (diffuse) | Active choroidal inflammation; correlates with disease activity |
| Subretinal fluid (SRF) | Exudative retinal detachment; indicates active inflammation |
| RPE undulations/folds | Choroidal infiltration causing mechanical distortion |
| Dalen-Fuchs nodules | Hyperreflective deposits between RPE and Bruch's membrane |
| Intraretinal fluid/cystoid macular edema | [[Cystoid macular edema]] ([[CME]]); secondary to chronic inflammation |
| Ellipsoid zone disruption | Photoreceptor damage; prognostic indicator for visual recovery |
| Subretinal fibrosis | Chronic disease; poor visual prognosis |

#### Fundus Fluorescein Angiography (FFA)

[[Fundus fluorescein angiography]] ([[FFA]]) reveals characteristic findings:

- **Early phase**: Multiple pinpoint areas of [[hyperfluorescence]] at the level of the RPE (corresponding to choroidal inflammation and RPE leakage)
- **Late phase**: Pooling of dye in areas of [[serous retinal detachment]]; diffuse [[optic disc leakage]]
- **Dalen-Fuchs nodules**: Early hypofluorescence (blockage) with late staining
- **Disc hyperfluorescence**: Indicating [[papillitis]]

#### Indocyanine Green Angiography (ICGA)

[[Indocyanine green angiography]] ([[ICGA]]) is particularly valuable for assessing choroidal involvement:

- **Early phase**: Multiple hypofluorescent dark dots (HDDs) representing choroidal granulomas
- **Intermediate phase**: Fuzzy, indistinct choroidal vessels due to stromal infiltration
- **Late phase**: Persistent hypofluorescent spots; diffuse late choroidal hyperfluorescence in severe cases
- ICGA is considered more sensitive than FFA for detecting subclinical choroidal inflammation and monitoring treatment response

#### Ultrasonography (B-Scan)

[[B-scan ultrasonography]] is useful when media opacities (e.g., dense vitritis, cataract) preclude fundoscopic examination:

- **Diffuse choroidal thickening** (>2 mm in severe cases)
- **Serous retinal detachment**
- **Vitreous opacities** (inflammatory debris)
- **Optic disc elevation**

#### Ultrasound Biomicroscopy (UBM)

[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] ([[UBM]]) can demonstrate:

- **Ciliary body thickening** and edema
- **Anterior rotation of the ciliary body** (contributing to angle closure)
- **Ciliochoroidal effusion**

#### Fundus Autofluorescence (FAF)

[[Fundus autofluorescence]] ([[FAF]]) may show:

- **Hyperautofluorescent spots** corresponding to areas of active RPE disturbance
- **Hypoautofluorescent areas** indicating RPE atrophy in chronic disease
- Useful for monitoring disease progression and treatment response

#### Laboratory Investigations

While no laboratory test is diagnostic for sympathetic ophthalmia, investigations are essential to **exclude mimicking conditions**:

| **Investigation** | **Purpose** |
|---|---|
| [[Complete blood count]] (CBC) | General screening; exclude infectious etiologies |
| [[Erythrocyte sedimentation rate]] (ESR) / [[C-reactive protein]] (CRP) | Non-specific inflammatory markers |
| [[Angiotensin-converting enzyme]] (ACE) / [[Lysozyme]] | Exclude [[sarcoidosis]] |
| Chest X-ray / CT chest | Exclude sarcoidosis, [[tuberculosis]] |
| [[Tuberculin skin test]] (TST) / [[QuantiFERON-TB Gold]] | Exclude [[tuberculosis]] |
| [[Rapid plasma reagin]] (RPR) / [[FTA-ABS]] | Exclude [[syphilis]] |
| [[HLA typing]] (HLA-A11, HLA-DR4) | Supportive but not diagnostic |
| [[Lumbar puncture]] | Pleocytosis in CSF if meningeal involvement suspected (overlap with VKH) |

### Differential Diagnosis

The differential diagnosis of sympathetic ophthalmia includes other causes of bilateral granulomatous or diffuse uveitis:

| **Condition** | **Key Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]] (VKH) | No history of ocular trauma/surgery; prominent extraocular findings (meningismus, hearing loss, vitiligo, poliosis); bilateral from onset |
| [[Sarcoidosis]] | Systemic involvement (lungs, skin, joints); elevated ACE/lysozyme; bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy on chest imaging |
| [[Tuberculosis]] (ocular) | Positive TST/QuantiFERON; pulmonary findings; may be unilateral |
| [[Syphilitic uveitis]] | Positive serological testing (RPR, FTA-ABS); protean ocular manifestations |
| [[Lens-induced uveitis]] ([[phacoantigenic uveitis]]) | Related to lens protein exposure; typically unilateral; non-granulomatous or granulomatous |
| [[Intraocular lymphoma]] ([[primary vitreoretinal lymphoma]]) | Older patients; vitreous cells with characteristic "aurora borealis" pattern; subretinal/sub-RPE infiltrates; elevated IL-10:IL-6 ratio in vitreous |
| [[Endophthalmitis]] (chronic/fungal) | Typically unilateral; culture-positive; more acute presentation |

## Management

The management of sympathetic ophthalmia requires a multifaceted approach encompassing **prevention**, **acute immunosuppressive therapy**, **long-term steroid-sparing immunomodulation**, and **surgical intervention** when indicated. The overarching goals are to suppress the autoimmune inflammatory response, preserve visual function in the sympathizing eye, and prevent sight-threatening complications.

### Prevention

#### Prophylactic Enucleation

The role of **prophylactic [[enucleation]]** of a severely traumatized, blind exciting eye has been debated for over a century. Current consensus includes:

- **Enucleation within 10–14 days** of the inciting trauma may reduce (but not eliminate) the risk of sympathetic ophthalmia
- Enucleation is generally recommended if the exciting eye has **no light perception (NLP)** vision and is severely disorganized
- **Evisceration** (removal of intraocular contents while preserving the scleral shell) was historically considered contraindicated due to theoretical risk of leaving uveal tissue behind; however, modern evidence suggests the risk of SO after evisceration is comparable to or lower than after enucleation
- Once sympathetic ophthalmia has developed, enucleation of the exciting eye is **controversial**—it does not reliably halt the disease in the sympathizing eye and may deprive the patient of their better-seeing eye if the exciting eye retains useful vision
- **Primary wound repair** with meticulous closure and removal of incarcerated uveal tissue is the most important preventive measure

#### Surgical Best Practices

- **Prompt, meticulous primary repair** of penetrating ocular injuries (ideally within 24 hours)
- **Removal of prolapsed or incarcerated uveal tissue** from the wound
- **Watertight wound closure** to restore the blood-ocular barrier
- **Minimization of surgical trauma** during intraocular procedures
- **Careful handling of uveal tissue** during vitreoretinal and cataract surgery

### Pharmacological Treatment

#### Acute Phase: Corticosteroids

**[[Corticosteroids]]** remain the first-line treatment for acute sympathetic ophthalmia:

| **Route** | **Agent** | **Dosage** | **Notes** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Systemic (oral)** | [[Prednisone]] / [[Prednisolone]] | 1–1.5 mg/kg/day (typically 60–100 mg/day) | Taper slowly over 3–6 months minimum; rapid taper risks recurrence |
| **Intravenous (pulse)** | [[Methylprednisolone]] | 1 g IV daily × 3 days | For severe, sight-threatening disease; followed by oral taper |
| **Topical** | [[Prednisolone acetate]] 1% | Every 1–2 hours initially, then taper | For anterior segment inflammation |
| **Periocular** | [[Triamcinolone acetonide]] | 40 mg sub-Tenon's injection | Adjunctive for posterior segment inflammation |
| **Intravitreal** | [[Dexamethasone implant]] (Ozurdex®) | 0.7 mg | For refractory [[cystoid macular edema]] or posterior uveitis |
| **Intravitreal** | [[Fluocinolone acetonide implant]] (Retisert®) | 0.59 mg | Long-acting; for chronic, recurrent disease |

**Key principles of corticosteroid therapy:**
- Initiate high-dose systemic corticosteroids immediately upon diagnosis
- Taper **very gradually** (over months, not weeks) to prevent rebound inflammation
- Most patients require a minimum of **3–6 months** of systemic corticosteroid therapy
- Monitor for corticosteroid side effects: [[posterior subcapsular cataract]], [[steroid-induced glaucoma]], [[osteoporosis]], [[hyperglycemia]], [[adrenal suppression]], [[Cushing syndrome]], [[peptic ulcer disease]]

#### Adjunctive Topical Therapy

- **[[Cycloplegic agents]]** ([[Atropine]] 1%, [[cyclopentolate]] 1%): To relieve [[ciliary spasm]], reduce pain, and prevent/break [[posterior synechiae]]
- **Topical [[corticosteroids]]**: As above
- **Topical [[IOP-lowering agents]]**: If secondary [[glaucoma]] develops (avoid [[prostaglandin analogs]] due to potential pro-inflammatory effects)

#### Steroid-Sparing Immunomodulatory Therapy (IMT)

Given the chronic, relapsing nature of sympathetic ophthalmia and the significant morbidity of long-term corticosteroid use, **steroid-sparing [[immunomodulatory therapy]]** is indicated in the majority of patients. IMT should be initiated early—ideally within the first 2–4 weeks of diagnosis—in conjunction with corticosteroids.

| **Drug Class** | **Agent** | **Dosage** | **Onset** | **Key Monitoring** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Antimetabolites** | [[Mycophenolate mofetil]] (CellCept®) | 1–1.5 g BID | 4–8 weeks | CBC, LFTs, GI symptoms |
| | [[Azathioprine]] (Imuran®) | 1–3 mg/kg/day | 6–12 weeks | CBC (myelosuppression), LFTs, [[TPMT]] genotype |
| | [[Methotrexate]] | 7.5–25 mg/week (oral or SC) | 4–8 weeks | CBC, LFTs, renal function; supplement with [[folic acid]] |
| **Calcineurin inhibitors** | [[Cyclosporine A]] (Neoral®) | 2.5–5 mg/kg/day | 2–4 weeks | Blood pressure, renal function (creatinine), drug levels |
| | [[Tacrolimus]] (FK506) | 0.05–0.15 mg/kg/day | 2–4 weeks | Renal function, blood glucose, drug levels |
| **Alkylating agents** | [[Cyclophosphamide]] | 1–2 mg/kg/day (oral) or IV pulse | 2–4 weeks | CBC (leukopenia), urinalysis (hemorrhagic cystitis), fertility counseling |
| | [[Chlorambucil]] | 0.1 mg/kg/day | 4–8 weeks | CBC; reserved for refractory cases |

#### Biologic Agents

For patients refractory to conventional IMT or intolerant of its side effects, **biologic agents** have shown increasing promise:

| **Agent** | **Mechanism** | **Dosage** | **Evidence Level** |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Adalimumab]] (Humira®) | Anti-[[TNF-α]] monoclonal antibody | 40 mg SC every 2 weeks | FDA-approved for non-infectious intermediate, posterior, and panuveitis; strong evidence |
| [[Infliximab]] (Remicade®) | Anti-TNF-α chimeric monoclonal antibody | 5–10 mg/kg IV at 0, 2, 6 weeks, then every 4–8 weeks | Case series support efficacy in refractory SO |
| [[Rituximab]] (Rituxan®) | Anti-[[CD20]] (B-cell depletion) | 1000 mg IV × 2 doses (2 weeks apart) | Case reports in refractory SO |
| [[Tocilizumab]] (Actemra®) | Anti-[[IL-6 receptor]] | 162 mg SC every 1–2 weeks or 8 mg/kg IV monthly | Emerging evidence in refractory uveitis |
| [[Interferon-alpha 2a]] | Immunomodulatory | 3–6 million IU SC daily, then taper | Limited case reports |

### Surgical Management

Surgical intervention in sympathetic ophthalmia is generally reserved for managing **complications** rather than treating the primary disease:

| **Complication** | **Surgical Intervention** |
|---|---|
| [[Cataract]] (secondary or steroid-induced) | [[Phacoemulsification]] with [[IOL]] implantation (during quiescent phase; minimum 3 months inflammation-free) |
| [[Secondary glaucoma]] (refractory) | [[Glaucoma drainage device]] (e.g., [[Ahmed valve]], [[Baerveldt implant]]); [[trabeculectomy]] with [[mitomycin C]]; [[cyclodestructive procedures]] (with caution) |
| [[Epiretinal membrane]] | [[Pars plana vitrectomy]] with membrane peeling |
| [[Vitreous opacities]] (non-clearing) | [[Pars plana vitrectomy]] |
| [[Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment]] | [[Pars plana vitrectomy]] with [[endolaser]] and tamponade |
| [[Choroidal neovascularization]] ([[CNV]]) | [[Anti-VEGF]] intravitreal injections ([[bevacizumab]], [[ranibizumab]], [[aflibercept]]); [[photodynamic therapy]] |
| [[Subretinal fibrosis]] | Generally not amenable to surgical intervention |
| **Enucleation of exciting eye** | Considered if exciting eye is blind and painful; does NOT halt disease in sympathizing eye once established |

**Critical surgical principles:**
- All intraocular surgery should be performed during a **quiescent phase** (minimum 3 months of controlled inflammation)
- **Perioperative immunosuppression** should be intensified (increased corticosteroid dosing before, during, and after surgery)
- Meticulous surgical technique to minimize additional uveal trauma

## Prognosis

The prognosis of sympathetic ophthalmia has improved dramatically with modern immunosuppressive therapy, though it remains a serious, potentially blinding condition:

| **Prognostic Factor** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| **Visual Outcome (with treatment)** | 60–75% of patients achieve ≥20/40 in the sympathizing eye with aggressive, early treatment |
| **Visual Outcome (without treatment)** | Progressive bilateral visual loss leading to blindness |
| **Recurrence Rate** | High (>50%) if immunosuppression is tapered too rapidly |
| **Duration of Treatment** | Most patients require ≥2 years of immunomodulatory therapy; many require lifelong treatment |
| **Poor Prognostic Indicators** | Delayed diagnosis, poor initial visual acuity, presence of exudative retinal detachment, subretinal fibrosis, CNV, young age at onset |
| **Good Prognostic Indicators** | Early diagnosis and treatment, good initial visual acuity, prompt response to corticosteroids |

### Complications

- **[[Cataract]]** (secondary to chronic inflammation and/or corticosteroid use): Most common complication (~50% of patients)
- **[[Secondary glaucoma]]** (from posterior synechiae, pupillary block, steroid response, or trabeculitis): 15–25%
- **[[Cystoid macular edema]]** ([[CME]]): Significant cause of visual morbidity
- **[[Choroidal neovascularization]]** ([[CNV]]): Particularly at sites of Dalen-Fuchs nodules or subretinal fibrosis
- **[[Exudative retinal detachment]]**: In acute, severe disease
- **[[Optic atrophy]]**: From chronic papillitis or secondary glaucoma
- **[[Subretinal fibrosis]]**: In chronic or recurrent disease
- **[[Phthisis bulbi]]**: End-stage, particularly of the exciting eye
- **[[Band keratopathy]]**: From chronic anterior segment inflammation and hypotony

## Special Considerations

### Sympathetic Ophthalmia in Children

Pediatric sympathetic ophthalmia presents unique challenges:

- Children are at higher risk due to greater susceptibility to penetrating ocular trauma (play-related injuries, projectile objects)
- Diagnosis may be delayed due to difficulty in eliciting symptoms and performing comprehensive examinations
- Amblyopia may compound visual loss in younger children
- Long-term immunosuppression carries additional concerns regarding growth, development, and infection risk
- Steroid-sparing agents should be initiated early to minimize corticosteroid-related growth suppression

### Sympathetic Ophthalmia After Vitreoretinal Surgery

The increasing incidence of SO following vitreoretinal surgery warrants heightened awareness:

- Risk is highest after multiple vitreoretinal procedures, particularly those involving extensive manipulation of the [[choroid]] and [[retina]]
- [[Pars plana vitrectomy]], [[scleral buckle]] surgery, and [[endolaser photocoagulation]] have all been implicated
- Patients undergoing repeated vitreoretinal surgeries should be counseled about the (small) risk of SO
- Any new-onset bilateral uveitis following vitreoretinal surgery should prompt consideration of SO

### Relationship to Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada Disease

Sympathetic ophthalmia and [[Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada disease]] ([[VKH]]) share remarkable clinical, histopathological, and immunological similarities:

- Both are bilateral granulomatous panuveitis targeting melanocyte-bearing tissues
- Both demonstrate Dalen-Fuchs nodules, exudative retinal detachments, and choroidal thickening
- Both involve T-cell-mediated autoimmunity against melanocyte-associated antigens
- The key distinguishing feature is the **history of ocular trauma or surgery** in SO (absent in VKH)
- VKH more commonly demonstrates prominent extraocular manifestations (meningismus, hearing loss, vitiligo, poliosis)
- Some authorities consider SO and VKH to represent different triggers for the same underlying autoimmune process

## Role of the Optometrist

Optometrists play a critical role in the detection, co-management, and long-term monitoring of sympathetic ophthalmia:

1. **Early Detection**: Maintaining a high index of suspicion in any patient with a history of penetrating ocular trauma or intraocular surgery who presents with new-onset bilateral uveitis
2. **Urgent Referral**: Immediate referral to a [[uveitis specialist]] or [[ophthalmologist]] upon clinical suspicion
3. **Co-management**: Monitoring anterior segment inflammation, [[intraocular pressure]], and posterior segment findings during treatment
4. **Imaging**: Performing and interpreting [[OCT]], [[fundus photography]], and [[visual field testing]] for disease monitoring
5. **Medication Monitoring**: Screening for corticosteroid-related complications (cataract, glaucoma, central serous chorioretinopathy)
6. **Patient Education**: Counseling patients with a history of ocular trauma about warning symptoms (photophobia, blurred vision, redness in the fellow eye) that should prompt immediate evaluation
7. **Low Vision Rehabilitation**: Providing [[low vision]] services for patients with permanent visual impairment

---

## Summary

Sympathetic ophthalmia remains one of the most clinically significant and feared complications of penetrating ocular trauma and intraocular surgery. Despite its rarity, the potential for bilateral blindness mandates that all eye care professionals—optometrists and ophthalmologists alike—maintain a high index of suspicion in the appropriate clinical context. Early diagnosis, aggressive immunosuppressive therapy with a combination of corticosteroids and steroid-sparing immunomodulatory agents, and meticulous long-term follow-up are the cornerstones of successful management. Advances in biologic therapies and multimodal imaging continue to improve outcomes for patients affected by this devastating autoimmune condition.
