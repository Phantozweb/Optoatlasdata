

# Trigeminal Nerve (Cranial Nerve V)

---

The **trigeminal nerve** ([[Cranial Nerve V]]) is the largest and most complex of the twelve [[cranial nerves]], serving as the principal sensory nerve of the head and face and the motor nerve to the [[muscles of mastication]]. For the optometrist and ophthalmologist, the trigeminal nerve is of paramount clinical importance because it provides sensory innervation to the [[eye]], [[orbit]], [[cornea]], [[conjunctiva]], [[eyelids]], [[lacrimal apparatus]], and surrounding periorbital structures. Dysfunction of the trigeminal nerve—whether from trauma, infection, neoplasm, demyelination, or vascular compression—can produce devastating ocular consequences, including [[neurotrophic keratitis]], [[corneal ulceration]], chronic [[dry eye]], and vision-threatening surface disease. A thorough understanding of the anatomy, physiology, pathophysiology, clinical presentation, diagnostic evaluation, and management of trigeminal nerve disorders is therefore essential for every eye care practitioner.

---

## Anatomy

### Overview and Nuclear Complex

The trigeminal nerve emerges from the lateral aspect of the [[pons]] as a large sensory root and a smaller motor root. Its central nuclear complex is the most extensive of any cranial nerve, spanning from the [[midbrain]] to the upper [[cervical spinal cord]], and comprises four distinct nuclei:

| Nucleus | Location | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Mesencephalic nucleus** | [[Midbrain]] | [[Proprioception]] from muscles of mastication, periodontal ligament, and extraocular muscles (debated) |
| **Principal (chief) sensory nucleus** | Lateral [[pons]] | Discriminative (fine) touch from the face |
| **Spinal trigeminal nucleus** | Medulla to upper cervical cord (C2–C3) | Pain, temperature, and crude touch from the face |
| **Motor nucleus** | Mid-pons, medial to principal sensory nucleus | Motor innervation to muscles of mastication, [[mylohyoid]], anterior belly of [[digastric]], [[tensor tympani]], [[tensor veli palatini]] |

The spinal trigeminal nucleus is further subdivided into three sub-nuclei: **pars oralis** (oral cavity sensation), **pars interpolaris** (dental pain), and **pars caudalis** (pain and temperature from the face, functionally analogous to the dorsal horn of the spinal cord). The pars caudalis is the primary relay for nociceptive input from the [[cornea]] and is a critical target in understanding [[corneal pain]] and [[referred pain]] patterns.

### Trigeminal (Gasserian) Ganglion

The sensory cell bodies of the trigeminal nerve reside in the **[[trigeminal ganglion]]** (also known as the **Gasserian ganglion** or **semilunar ganglion**), located in **[[Meckel's cave]]** (the trigeminal impression on the floor of the [[middle cranial fossa]]). This ganglion is analogous to a [[dorsal root ganglion]] of a spinal nerve. From the ganglion, three major divisions arise:

### Three Divisions

#### 1. Ophthalmic Division (V1)

The **[[ophthalmic nerve]]** (V1) is the smallest division and is purely sensory. It passes through the lateral wall of the [[cavernous sinus]] (inferior to [[Cranial Nerve IV]] and lateral to [[Cranial Nerve VI]]) and enters the [[orbit]] via the [[superior orbital fissure]]. Before entering the orbit, V1 gives off a recurrent meningeal (tentorial) branch. It then divides into three major branches:

| Branch | Course | Sensory Territory |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Frontal nerve]]** | Enters orbit above the [[levator palpebrae superioris]]; divides into [[supratrochlear nerve]] and [[supraorbital nerve]] | Upper [[eyelid]], [[forehead]], [[scalp]] (to vertex), frontal [[sinus]] |
| **[[Lacrimal nerve]]** | Runs along the superior border of the [[lateral rectus muscle]] | [[Lacrimal gland]] (sensory; also carries parasympathetic secretomotor fibers from V2 via the [[zygomaticotemporal nerve]]), lateral upper eyelid, lateral [[conjunctiva]] |
| **[[Nasociliary nerve]]** | Passes through the [[common tendinous ring]] (annulus of Zinn); crosses above [[Cranial Nerve II]] | Most critical branch for the eye care professional |

The **[[nasociliary nerve]]** is the most clinically significant branch of V1 for optometry. Its sub-branches include:

- **[[Long ciliary nerves]]** (2–3): Carry sensory fibers from the [[cornea]], [[iris]], and [[ciliary body]], as well as sympathetic fibers to the [[dilator pupillae]] muscle. These nerves penetrate the [[sclera]] near the [[optic nerve]] and travel in the [[suprachoroidal space]].
- **[[Short ciliary nerves]]**: Arise from the [[ciliary ganglion]] (which receives a sensory root from the nasociliary nerve); carry sensory, parasympathetic, and sympathetic fibers to the [[globe]].
- **[[Posterior ethmoidal nerve]]**: Innervates the [[sphenoid sinus]] and posterior [[ethmoidal air cells]].
- **[[Anterior ethmoidal nerve]]**: Passes through the anterior ethmoidal foramen, enters the [[anterior cranial fossa]], then descends through the [[cribriform plate]] to innervate the anterior nasal cavity and the skin of the nasal tip (as the **[[external nasal nerve]]**). This branch is clinically relevant in **[[Hutchinson's sign]]**—vesicular lesions on the tip of the nose in [[herpes zoster ophthalmicus]], indicating nasociliary nerve involvement and a high risk of ocular complications.
- **[[Infratrochlear nerve]]**: Innervates the medial eyelids, medial [[conjunctiva]], [[lacrimal sac]], and skin of the lateral nose.

#### 2. Maxillary Division (V2)

The **[[maxillary nerve]]** (V2) is purely sensory. It exits the skull through the [[foramen rotundum]], traverses the [[pterygopalatine fossa]], and enters the orbit through the [[inferior orbital fissure]] as the **[[infraorbital nerve]]**, which exits via the [[infraorbital foramen]]. V2 innervates the lower eyelid, cheek, upper lip, upper teeth, nasal cavity, palate, and nasopharynx. The **[[zygomaticotemporal nerve]]** (a branch of V2) carries postganglionic parasympathetic fibers from the [[pterygopalatine ganglion]] to the [[lacrimal nerve]] (V1), facilitating reflex [[lacrimation]].

Key branches relevant to eye care:

- **[[Infraorbital nerve]]**: Sensory to the lower eyelid, lateral nose, and upper lip.
- **[[Zygomatic nerve]]** (divides into [[zygomaticofacial nerve|zygomaticofacial]] and [[zygomaticotemporal nerve|zygomaticotemporal]]): Sensory to the cheek and temple; the zygomaticotemporal branch carries parasympathetic secretomotor fibers to the lacrimal gland.

#### 3. Mandibular Division (V3)

The **[[mandibular nerve]]** (V3) is the largest division and the only one containing both sensory and motor fibers. It exits the skull through the [[foramen ovale]]. V3 provides sensory innervation to the lower face, lower teeth, tongue (anterior two-thirds, general sensation), and temporal region, and motor innervation to the [[muscles of mastication]] ([[masseter]], [[temporalis]], [[medial pterygoid]], [[lateral pterygoid]]), as well as the [[mylohyoid]], anterior belly of the [[digastric]], [[tensor tympani]], and [[tensor veli palatini]]. V3 has limited direct ocular relevance but is important in the differential diagnosis of facial pain and in understanding the complete trigeminal nerve examination.

### Autonomic Connections

The trigeminal nerve serves as a conduit for [[autonomic nervous system|autonomic]] fibers, though it does not originate them. Four parasympathetic ganglia are associated with the trigeminal divisions:

| Ganglion | Associated Division | Parasympathetic Origin | Target |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Ciliary ganglion]]** | V1 (nasociliary nerve) | [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]] via [[Cranial Nerve III]] | [[Sphincter pupillae]], [[ciliary muscle]] |
| **[[Pterygopalatine ganglion]]** | V2 | [[Superior salivatory nucleus]] via [[Cranial Nerve VII]] | [[Lacrimal gland]], nasal and palatal glands |
| **[[Submandibular ganglion]]** | V3 (lingual nerve) | [[Superior salivatory nucleus]] via CN VII | [[Submandibular gland|Submandibular]] and [[sublingual gland]]s |
| **[[Otic ganglion]]** | V3 (auriculotemporal nerve) | [[Inferior salivatory nucleus]] via [[Cranial Nerve IX]] | [[Parotid gland]] |

The **[[ciliary ganglion]]** is of particular importance in optometry. It is located within the orbit, approximately 1 cm anterior to the [[annulus of Zinn]], lateral to the [[optic nerve]]. It receives three roots: a parasympathetic root from CN III (for pupillary constriction and accommodation), a sympathetic root from the [[internal carotid plexus]] (for vasomotor control), and a sensory root from the nasociliary nerve (V1). The [[short ciliary nerves]] (6–10) emerge from the ganglion and penetrate the sclera to innervate intraocular structures.

## Physiology

### Corneal Innervation and the Trophic Function

The [[cornea]] is the most densely innervated tissue in the human body, with an estimated nerve density 300–600 times that of skin. Corneal innervation is derived almost exclusively from the **[[nasociliary nerve]]** (V1) via the [[long ciliary nerves]] and [[short ciliary nerves]]. These nerves enter the cornea at the peripheral [[corneal stroma]] in a radial pattern, lose their [[myelin sheath]] within approximately 1 mm of the [[limbus]], and form the **sub-basal nerve plexus** beneath [[Bowman's layer]]. Terminal nerve endings penetrate the [[corneal epithelium]] as free nerve endings.

Corneal nerves serve three critical functions:

1. **Sensory/Protective**: Detection of mechanical, thermal, and chemical stimuli triggers the [[corneal blink reflex]] (afferent limb: V1; efferent limb: [[Cranial Nerve VII]]) and reflex [[lacrimation]] (afferent: V1; efferent: CN VII via the [[pterygopalatine ganglion]] to the [[lacrimal gland]]).
2. **Trophic**: Corneal nerves release neuropeptides—including **[[Substance P]]**, **[[calcitonin gene-related peptide]]** (CGRP), and **[[nerve growth factor]]** (NGF)—that are essential for corneal epithelial cell proliferation, migration, adhesion, and wound healing. Loss of this trophic support leads to [[neurotrophic keratitis]].
3. **Regulation of Tear Film**: Sensory input from the cornea drives basal and reflex tear secretion, maintaining the [[tear film]] and [[ocular surface]] homeostasis.

### Trigeminal Reflexes Relevant to Eye Care

| Reflex | Afferent | Efferent | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Corneal reflex]] (blink reflex)** | V1 (nasociliary → long ciliary) | [[Cranial Nerve VII]] (orbicularis oculi) | Absent in V1 lesions, CN VII palsy, deep anesthesia; tested in coma assessment |
| **[[Lacrimation reflex]]** | V1 | CN VII → pterygopalatine ganglion → lacrimal gland | Reduced tearing in V1 lesions contributes to [[dry eye]] |
| **[[Jaw jerk reflex]]** | V3 (muscle spindles in masseter) | V3 motor (masseter) | Hyperactive in upper motor neuron lesions (bilateral corticobulbar disease) |
| **[[Oculocardiac reflex]]** | V1 (short/long ciliary nerves) | [[Vagus nerve]] (CN X) | Bradycardia during ocular surgery (especially strabismus surgery with traction on extraocular muscles) |

## Pathophysiology

Trigeminal nerve dysfunction relevant to eye care can arise from lesions at any point along the nerve's course—from the brainstem nuclei to the peripheral terminal branches in the cornea. The pathophysiological mechanisms include:

### 1. Viral Reactivation: Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)

**[[Herpes zoster ophthalmicus]]** results from reactivation of latent **[[varicella-zoster virus]]** (VZV) within the [[trigeminal ganglion]], specifically affecting the V1 division. The virus travels anterogradely along sensory axons, causing inflammation, demyelination, and neuronal destruction. This produces:

- Acute dermatomal vesicular rash in the V1 distribution
- Direct viral invasion of ocular tissues ([[keratitis]], [[uveitis]], [[retinitis]])
- Post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN) from persistent neuronal damage and central sensitization in the [[spinal trigeminal nucleus]]
- [[Neurotrophic keratitis]] from destruction of corneal sensory nerves

### 2. Neurotrophic Keratitis

**[[Neurotrophic keratitis]]** (NK) is a degenerative corneal disease caused by impairment of trigeminal (V1) sensory innervation. Loss of corneal nerve function leads to:

- Decreased corneal sensation ([[corneal hypoesthesia]] or [[corneal anesthesia]])
- Reduced blink rate and reflex tearing
- Loss of trophic neuropeptide release (Substance P, CGRP, NGF)
- Impaired epithelial cell metabolism, proliferation, and wound healing
- Progressive epithelial breakdown → persistent [[epithelial defect]] → [[corneal ulceration]] → [[corneal perforation]]

NK is classified by the **Mackie classification**:

| Stage | Clinical Findings |
|---|---|
| **Stage 1** | Corneal epithelial irregularity, superficial [[punctate keratopathy]], [[rose bengal]] staining, reduced [[tear break-up time]] |
| **Stage 2** | Persistent [[epithelial defect]] (PED), typically oval, in the superior or central cornea, with smooth rolled edges; surrounding epithelial edema |
| **Stage 3** | [[Corneal ulceration]], stromal melting, possible [[descemetocele]] or [[corneal perforation]] |

### 3. Trigeminal Neuralgia

**[[Trigeminal neuralgia]]** (TN, *tic douloureux*) is characterized by sudden, severe, unilateral, shock-like paroxysmal pain in one or more divisions of the trigeminal nerve. The most common cause is **neurovascular compression** of the trigeminal nerve root entry zone by the [[superior cerebellar artery]] or other vessels, leading to focal demyelination and ephaptic transmission (cross-talk between nerve fibers). TN most commonly affects V2 and V3 but can involve V1, producing periorbital and ocular pain that may mimic primary eye disease.

### 4. Demyelinating Disease

**[[Multiple sclerosis]]** (MS) can produce trigeminal nerve dysfunction through demyelinating plaques in the brainstem (particularly the pons), affecting the trigeminal nuclei or root entry zone. This may manifest as facial numbness, trigeminal neuralgia (often bilateral, which is atypical and should raise suspicion for MS in younger patients), or reduced corneal sensation.

### 5. Neoplastic and Compressive Lesions

Tumors of the [[cerebellopontine angle]] (e.g., [[vestibular schwannoma]], [[meningioma]], [[epidermoid cyst]]), [[trigeminal schwannoma]], [[nasopharyngeal carcinoma]], and metastatic disease can compress or infiltrate the trigeminal nerve or ganglion, producing progressive sensory loss, pain, or motor dysfunction.

### 6. Iatrogenic Causes

Surgical procedures—including [[neurosurgery]], [[sinus surgery]], [[dental procedures]], and ophthalmic surgeries such as [[LASIK]], [[PRK]], [[penetrating keratoplasty]], and [[cataract surgery]]—can damage trigeminal nerve branches. [[LASIK]] severs the sub-basal nerve plexus during flap creation, producing transient corneal hypoesthesia and contributing to post-LASIK [[dry eye]], which may persist for 6–12 months or longer.

### 7. Systemic and Metabolic Causes

**[[Diabetes mellitus]]** causes a [[peripheral neuropathy]] that can affect the trigeminal nerve, leading to reduced corneal sensation and predisposition to neurotrophic keratitis. Other systemic causes include [[leprosy]], [[sarcoidosis]], [[amyloidosis]], and [[connective tissue diseases]].

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Sensory Symptoms

- **Pain**: Sharp, lancinating (trigeminal neuralgia), burning, aching, or throbbing pain in the distribution of one or more trigeminal divisions. V1 pain may be periorbital, frontal, or retro-orbital.
- **Numbness/Hypoesthesia**: Reduced sensation in the face, forehead, eyelids, cornea, or nasal mucosa.
- **Paresthesia/Dysesthesia**: Tingling, crawling, or abnormal sensations.
- **Allodynia**: Pain from normally non-painful stimuli (e.g., light touch to the face triggering severe pain in post-herpetic neuralgia).

### Ocular Symptoms and Signs

| Finding | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Reduced or absent [[corneal sensation]] | Damage to V1/nasociliary nerve/long ciliary nerves |
| Absent or reduced [[corneal blink reflex]] | Loss of afferent limb (V1) |
| [[Dry eye]] / reduced [[tear production]] | Loss of reflex lacrimation arc |
| [[Punctate epithelial erosions]] | Loss of trophic support to corneal epithelium |
| [[Persistent epithelial defect]] | Impaired epithelial healing |
| [[Corneal ulceration]] / [[stromal melting]] | Advanced neurotrophic keratitis |
| [[Corneal perforation]] | End-stage neurotrophic keratitis |
| [[Exposure keratopathy]] | May coexist if CN VII is also affected (e.g., in cerebellopontine angle lesions) |
| [[Neurotrophic keratitis]] | Hallmark of V1 sensory denervation |
| Vesicular rash (V1 dermatome) | [[Herpes zoster ophthalmicus]] |
| [[Hutchinson's sign]] | Vesicles on nasal tip (external nasal nerve involvement) → high risk of ocular HZO |
| [[Keratitis]], [[uveitis]], [[scleritis]] | Direct viral or inflammatory involvement in HZO |
| Reduced [[intraocular pressure]] sensation | Patients may not feel elevated IOP-related discomfort |

### Motor Symptoms (V3)

- Weakness of muscles of mastication (jaw deviation toward the affected side upon opening)
- Atrophy of [[masseter]] and [[temporalis]] muscles
- Reduced [[jaw jerk reflex]] (ipsilateral)

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

#### 1. Corneal Sensation Testing

Corneal sensation testing is the single most important clinical assessment of trigeminal (V1) function in eye care.

- **Cotton wisp method**: A fine wisp of cotton is drawn to a thin strand and gently applied to the central cornea (avoiding the lashes and eyelids). The patient's blink response and subjective sensation are compared between eyes. This is a qualitative, screening-level test.
- **[[Cochet-Bonnet esthesiometer]]**: The gold standard quantitative instrument. A retractable nylon monofilament of variable length (0–60 mm) is applied perpendicularly to the cornea. Shorter filament lengths exert greater pressure. Normal central corneal sensation threshold is typically ≥50 mm filament length. Values <40 mm indicate hypoesthesia.
- **[[Belmonte non-contact gas esthesiometer]]**: A research-grade instrument that delivers controlled air jets (mechanical, thermal, or chemical stimuli) to the cornea, allowing selective testing of different nerve fiber types (Aδ and C fibers).

#### 2. Facial Sensation Testing

Light touch, pinprick, and temperature sensation are tested in all three trigeminal divisions (V1: forehead; V2: cheek; V3: chin) and compared bilaterally.

#### 3. Corneal Blink Reflex

The examiner touches the cornea and observes for a bilateral blink (direct and consensual). Absence of the direct reflex with intact consensual reflex on stimulation of the contralateral eye suggests an ipsilateral V1 (afferent) lesion. Absence of the efferent (blink) response with intact sensation suggests a [[Cranial Nerve VII]] (efferent) lesion.

#### 4. Motor Examination (V3)

- Palpation of masseter and temporalis during clenching
- Observation for jaw deviation on mouth opening
- Jaw jerk reflex (afferent and efferent both V3)

#### 5. Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

Essential for evaluating:

- [[Corneal epithelial integrity]] (fluorescein, rose bengal, or [[lissamine green]] staining)
- Presence of [[punctate epithelial erosions]], [[epithelial defects]], or [[ulceration]]
- Corneal clarity, [[neovascularization]], or [[scarring]]
- Signs of [[herpes zoster ophthalmicus]] (e.g., [[pseudodendrites]], [[stromal keratitis]], [[keratic precipitates]])
- [[Anterior chamber]] inflammation

#### 6. In Vivo Confocal Microscopy (IVCM)

**[[In vivo confocal microscopy]]** (e.g., Heidelberg Retina Tomograph with Rostock Corneal Module) allows direct, non-invasive visualization of the corneal sub-basal nerve plexus. Quantitative parameters include:

- **Corneal nerve fiber density** (CNFD)
- **Corneal nerve branch density** (CNBD)
- **Corneal nerve fiber length** (CNFL)

Reduced values indicate corneal nerve damage and correlate with neurotrophic keratitis severity, diabetic neuropathy, and post-surgical nerve recovery.

### Imaging

| Modality | Indication |
|---|---|
| **[[MRI]] of the brain and trigeminal nerve** | Suspected intracranial pathology (tumor, MS plaques, vascular compression); high-resolution MRI with CISS/FIESTA sequences for trigeminal neuralgia evaluation |
| **[[MRA]] (Magnetic Resonance Angiography)** | Neurovascular compression in trigeminal neuralgia |
| **[[CT scan]]** | Bony pathology (fractures, skull base lesions, sinus disease) |
| **[[Anterior segment OCT]]** | Corneal epithelial thickness mapping in neurotrophic keratitis |

### Electrophysiological Testing

- **[[Blink reflex electrophysiology]]** (trigeminal-facial reflex): Electrical stimulation of the supraorbital nerve (V1) with EMG recording from the [[orbicularis oculi]] (CN VII). Measures R1 (oligosynaptic, ipsilateral pontine pathway) and R2 (polysynaptic, bilateral medullary pathway) latencies. Prolonged or absent responses localize lesions to the trigeminal afferent, facial efferent, or central pathways.

### Laboratory Testing

- **VZV serology / PCR**: In suspected herpes zoster ophthalmicus
- **HbA1c / fasting glucose**: To evaluate for [[diabetes mellitus]] as a cause of trigeminal neuropathy
- **ACE level, chest X-ray**: If [[sarcoidosis]] is suspected
- **Autoimmune panel**: In connective tissue disease-related neuropathy

## Treatment and Management

### Management of Neurotrophic Keratitis

Treatment is guided by the Mackie staging system:

#### Stage 1 (Epithelial Irregularity)

- Discontinuation of all [[topical medications]] with preservatives (especially [[benzalkonium chloride]], which is epitheliotoxic)
- Preservative-free [[artificial tears]] (frequent dosing, e.g., every 1–2 hours)
- [[Lubricating ointment]] at bedtime
- Treatment of concurrent [[meibomian gland dysfunction]] and [[blepharitis]]
- Avoidance of [[topical anesthetics]] (which further impair epithelial healing)

#### Stage 2 (Persistent Epithelial Defect)

- All Stage 1 measures
- **[[Autologous serum tears]]** (20% concentration): Contain epitheliotrophic factors (NGF, EGF, fibronectin, Substance P) that promote epithelial healing
- **[[Therapeutic bandage contact lens]]**: Protects the epithelial defect; requires close monitoring for infectious keratitis risk
- **[[Punctal occlusion]]**: Preserves the tear film
- **[[Amniotic membrane transplantation]]** (e.g., [[Prokera]], [[AmbioDisk]]): Provides a biological scaffold with anti-inflammatory and pro-healing properties
- **[[Cenegermin]]** (**Oxervate**): Recombinant human [[nerve growth factor]] (rhNGF), 0.002% (20 μg/mL) ophthalmic solution. FDA-approved specifically for neurotrophic keratitis. Applied 6 times daily for 8 weeks. Clinical trials (REPARO, NGF0212, NGF0214) demonstrated complete corneal healing in approximately 70% of patients with Stage 2–3 NK.
- **[[Tarsorrhaphy]]** (temporary or permanent): Reduces corneal exposure

#### Stage 3 (Corneal Ulceration / Perforation)

- All Stage 1 and 2 measures
- **[[Tissue adhesive]]** (e.g., [[cyanoacrylate glue]]): For small perforations or impending perforations
- **[[Conjunctival flap]]**: Provides vascular supply to promote healing
- **[[Amniotic membrane transplantation]]** (multilayer)
- **[[Corneal transplantation]]** ([[penetrating keratoplasty]] or [[deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]]): Reserved for severe cases; prognosis is guarded due to ongoing neurotrophic state
- **[[Corneal neurotization]]**: A newer surgical technique in which a donor sensory nerve (e.g., [[supraorbital nerve]], [[supratrochlear nerve]], or contralateral [[sural nerve]] graft) is surgically transferred to the corneal limbus to restore corneal innervation. Early results are promising, with restoration of corneal sensation and resolution of neurotrophic keratitis in selected patients.

### Management of Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus

- **Systemic [[antiviral therapy]]**: [[Valacyclovir]] 1000 mg TID, [[acyclovir]] 800 mg 5×/day, or [[famciclovir]] 500 mg TID for 7–10 days (ideally initiated within 72 hours of rash onset)
- **Topical [[corticosteroids]]**: For [[stromal keratitis]], [[anterior uveitis]], or [[scleritis]] (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate]] 1%)
- **[[Cycloplegic agents]]**: For uveitis-associated ciliary spasm (e.g., [[cyclopentolate]] 1%, [[atropine]] 1%)
- **[[Intraocular pressure]] management**: If secondary [[glaucoma]] develops (avoid [[prostaglandin analogs]] which may exacerbate inflammation)
- **Post-herpetic neuralgia management**: [[Gabapentin]], [[pregabalin]], [[amitriptyline]], [[nortriptyline]], topical [[capsaicin]], or [[lidocaine]] patches
- **[[Zoster vaccine]]** (**[[Shingrix]]**, recombinant adjuvanted): Recommended for adults ≥50 years for prevention; >90% efficacy in reducing HZO incidence

### Management of Trigeminal Neuralgia

#### Pharmacological

| Medication | Mechanism | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Carbamazepine]]** | Sodium channel blocker | First-line; 200–1200 mg/day; monitor CBC and LFTs |
| **[[Oxcarbazepine]]** | Sodium channel blocker | Alternative to carbamazepine; better side-effect profile |
| **[[Baclofen]]** | GABA-B agonist | Adjunctive therapy |
| **[[Lamotrigine]]** | Sodium channel blocker | Second-line |
| **[[Gabapentin]] / [[Pregabalin]]** | Calcium channel α2δ ligand | Used in refractory cases or atypical TN |

#### Surgical / Interventional

- **[[Microvascular decompression]]** (MVD, Jannetta procedure): Gold standard surgical treatment; involves posterior fossa craniotomy and placement of a Teflon pad between the offending vessel and the trigeminal nerve root. Highest long-term cure rate (~80% pain-free at 5 years).
- **[[Percutaneous procedures]]**: [[Radiofrequency thermocoagulation]], [[glycerol rhizolysis]], or [[balloon compression]] of the trigeminal ganglion. Less invasive but higher recurrence rates and risk of corneal anesthesia (particularly with V1 involvement).
- **[[Gamma Knife radiosurgery]]**: Focused radiation to the trigeminal root entry zone. Non-invasive; slower onset of pain relief (weeks to months); lower risk of facial numbness.

### Management of Post-Refractive Surgery Dry Eye / Neuropathy

- Preservative-free artificial tears
- [[Cyclosporine A]] 0.05% ([[Restasis]]) or [[lifitegrast]] 5% ([[Xiidra]]) for inflammatory dry eye
- Autologous serum tears
- Punctal occlusion
- Omega-3 fatty acid supplementation
- Corneal nerve regeneration typically occurs over 6–12 months post-LASIK; longer with PRK flap-free techniques (paradoxically, PRK may cause less long-term nerve damage than LASIK in some studies)

## Trigeminal Nerve and the Ocular Surface: Integrative Concepts

The trigeminal nerve is the cornerstone of the **[[lacrimal functional unit]]** (LFU), a concept describing the integrated system comprising the corneal and conjunctival sensory nerves (V1), the [[lacrimal gland]], the [[meibomian glands]], the [[goblet cells]], the [[tear film]], and the central neural connections that regulate ocular surface homeostasis. Disruption of the trigeminal afferent limb—whether from HZO, diabetes, surgery, or other causes—destabilizes the entire LFU, leading to a vicious cycle of reduced sensation → reduced blink rate → reduced tear secretion → epithelial damage → inflammation → further nerve damage.

This concept is critical for understanding why patients with trigeminal nerve dysfunction often present with a clinical picture that overlaps significantly with [[dry eye disease]] (DED) but may not respond to conventional DED therapies. Identification of the underlying neurotrophic component is essential for appropriate management.

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis of trigeminal nerve disorders depends on the etiology, severity, and timeliness of intervention:

- **Herpes zoster ophthalmicus**: With early antiviral therapy, most patients recover well, but 10–20% develop chronic post-herpetic neuralgia, and a subset develop neurotrophic keratitis requiring long-term management.
- **Neurotrophic keratitis**: Stage 1 disease generally responds well to conservative measures. Stage 2–3 disease carries significant risk of vision loss without aggressive intervention. Cenegermin (rhNGF) has improved outcomes substantially. Corneal neurotization offers hope for refractory cases.
- **Trigeminal neuralgia**: Carbamazepine provides initial relief in >70% of patients. Microvascular decompression offers the best long-term outcomes. Recurrence is common with percutaneous procedures.
- **Post-surgical neuropathy**: Corneal nerve regeneration after LASIK is generally favorable but may take 12–24 months for full recovery. Some patients develop chronic neuropathic corneal pain.
