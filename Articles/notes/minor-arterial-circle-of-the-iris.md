

# Minor Arterial Circle of the Iris

---

## Overview

The **Minor Arterial Circle of the Iris** (also known as the **lesser arterial circle of the iris**, **circulus arteriosus iridis minor**, or **minor vascular circle of the iris**) is a critical anastomotic vascular ring located within the [[iris]] stroma at the level of the [[collarette]] — the boundary between the [[pupillary zone]] and the [[ciliary zone]] of the iris. This vascular arcade represents one of the two principal arterial circles that supply the iris, the other being the [[Major Arterial Circle of the Iris]] (circulus arteriosus iridis major), which is situated at the iris root near the [[ciliary body]].

The minor arterial circle is formed by the convergence and anastomosis of radial arteriolar branches that originate from the [[major arterial circle of the iris]] and course centripetally through the iris stroma toward the [[pupillary margin]]. At approximately the junction of the pupillary and ciliary zones — a region anatomically demarcated by the [[collarette]] (the remnant of the embryonic [[pupillary membrane]]) — these radial vessels interconnect to form an incomplete or variably complete vascular ring. From this ring, terminal arteriolar branches continue toward the [[pupil]] margin, supplying the pupillary zone of the iris and contributing to the vascular supply of the [[sphincter pupillae]] muscle.

Understanding the anatomy, embryology, hemodynamics, and clinical significance of the minor arterial circle of the iris is essential for optometrists, ophthalmologists, and ophthalmic surgeons, as this structure plays a pivotal role in iris perfusion, pupillary dynamics, and the pathophysiology of numerous anterior segment conditions including [[anterior uveitis]], [[rubeosis iridis]], [[iris atrophy]], [[hyphema]], and complications arising from intraocular surgical procedures.

---

## Embryology and Development

The vascular architecture of the iris, including the minor arterial circle, develops during the later stages of [[embryogenesis]] and continues to mature postnatally. The iris vasculature is derived primarily from the [[mesoderm]] and [[neural crest]] cells that contribute to the anterior segment of the eye.

### Developmental Timeline

| Gestational Period | Developmental Event |
|---|---|
| ~5th–6th week | [[Optic cup]] formation; primitive hyaloid vascular system develops |
| ~6th–7th week | [[Long posterior ciliary arteries]] begin to extend anteriorly |
| ~8th–10th week | [[Anterior ciliary arteries]] begin contributing to anterior segment vasculature |
| ~3rd month | Primitive iris bud begins to form from the anterior rim of the [[optic cup]] |
| ~4th–5th month | [[Major arterial circle of the iris]] begins to form from anastomosis of long posterior ciliary arteries and anterior ciliary arteries |
| ~5th–6th month | Radial iris vessels extend centripetally from the major circle; [[pupillary membrane]] is fully vascularized |
| ~7th–8th month | Pupillary membrane begins to atrophy; minor arterial circle begins to form at the collarette region |
| ~9th month to postnatal | Pupillary membrane fully resorbs (in most cases); minor arterial circle matures and stabilizes |

The formation of the minor arterial circle is intimately linked to the regression of the [[tunica vasculosa lentis]] and the [[pupillary membrane]]. As the pupillary membrane undergoes programmed [[apoptosis]] and resorption during the third trimester, the residual vascular connections at the collarette region reorganize into the anastomotic arcade that constitutes the minor arterial circle. Failure of complete pupillary membrane regression may result in [[persistent pupillary membrane]] strands, which may be associated with anomalous vascular patterns at the minor circle.

Key molecular signals involved in iris vascular development include [[vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF), [[angiopoietins]], and [[platelet-derived growth factor]] (PDGF), which regulate [[angiogenesis]], vessel stabilization, and [[pericyte]] recruitment within the iris stroma.

## Anatomy

### Gross Anatomy

The minor arterial circle of the iris is situated within the [[iris stroma]], at the junction of the **pupillary zone** (the inner, narrower zone of the iris surrounding the pupil) and the **ciliary zone** (the outer, broader zone extending from the collarette to the iris root). This junction corresponds to the [[collarette]], a scalloped ridge visible on the anterior iris surface that represents the thickest portion of the iris and the site of the former attachment of the pupillary membrane.

Unlike the [[major arterial circle of the iris]], which is a relatively robust and consistently complete vascular ring, the minor arterial circle is often **incomplete** and may be represented by a series of discontinuous anastomotic arcades rather than a single continuous vessel. The degree of completeness varies significantly among individuals and even between the two eyes of the same individual.

### Vascular Supply and Drainage

The blood supply to the minor arterial circle is derived entirely from the **major arterial circle of the iris**, which itself receives contributions from:

1. **[[Long posterior ciliary arteries]]** (two in number, one nasal and one temporal) — branches of the [[ophthalmic artery]]
2. **[[Anterior ciliary arteries]]** (seven in number, derived from the muscular branches supplying the [[rectus muscles]]) — also branches of the ophthalmic artery

From the major arterial circle, **radial arterioles** course centripetally through the ciliary zone of the iris stroma, running in a slightly tortuous fashion to accommodate changes in iris diameter during [[mydriasis]] and [[miosis]]. Upon reaching the collarette region, these radial arterioles anastomose with one another to form the minor arterial circle.

From the minor arterial circle, **terminal arterioles** continue centripetally into the pupillary zone, supplying the [[sphincter pupillae]] muscle and the pupillary margin. These terminal vessels eventually drain into a network of **iris venules** that course centrifugally back through the stroma to drain into the [[vortex veins]] via the ciliary body venous plexus.

### Microanatomy and Histology

The vessels of the minor arterial circle are **arterioles** with the following histological characteristics:

| Layer | Description |
|---|---|
| **[[Tunica intima]]** | Single layer of [[endothelial cells]] resting on a thin [[basement membrane]]; endothelial cells are non-fenestrated, forming part of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]] |
| **[[Tunica media]]** | Thin layer of [[smooth muscle cells]] (typically 1–3 layers); responsible for vasomotor tone and regulation of blood flow |
| **[[Tunica adventitia]]** | Thin connective tissue layer that blends with the surrounding iris stroma; contains [[collagen fibers]], [[fibroblasts]], and occasional [[melanocytes]] |

A critical feature of the iris vasculature, including the minor arterial circle, is the presence of a **non-fenestrated endothelium** with **tight junctions** ([[zonulae occludentes]]), which constitutes a key component of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]] (BAB). This barrier restricts the passage of plasma proteins and inflammatory cells from the iris vasculature into the [[aqueous humor]] of the [[anterior chamber]]. Disruption of this barrier is a hallmark of [[anterior uveitis]] and other inflammatory conditions affecting the anterior segment.

The iris arterioles are also notable for their **thick basement membrane** and the presence of **pericytes** that ensheath the vessel walls, contributing to vascular stability and regulation of capillary blood flow.

### Relationship to Other Iris Structures

The minor arterial circle is closely related to several important iris structures:

- **[[Collarette]]**: The minor circle lies at or just posterior to the collarette, which serves as a surface landmark for its location.
- **[[Sphincter pupillae]]**: The terminal branches of the minor circle supply this [[parasympathetic]]ally innervated smooth muscle, which is responsible for [[miosis]].
- **[[Dilator pupillae]]**: The radial arterioles coursing between the major and minor circles supply the [[sympathetic]]ally innervated dilator muscle in the ciliary zone.
- **[[Iris crypts]]**: These openings in the anterior iris stroma, located both at the collarette and peripherally, allow aqueous humor to percolate through the stroma and may be related to the vascular architecture of the minor circle.
- **[[Fuchs' crypts]]**: Located at the collarette, these are closely associated with the minor arterial circle.

## Physiology and Hemodynamics

### Blood Flow Regulation

The minor arterial circle of the iris plays a central role in regulating blood flow to the pupillary zone of the iris. Blood flow through the iris vasculature is influenced by several factors:

1. **Autonomic nervous system regulation**: [[Sympathetic]] innervation (via the [[superior cervical ganglion]] and [[long ciliary nerves]]) causes vasoconstriction of iris arterioles, while [[parasympathetic]] innervation (via the [[ciliary ganglion]] and [[short ciliary nerves]]) may contribute to vasodilation.

2. **Myogenic autoregulation**: The smooth muscle cells in the tunica media of the iris arterioles exhibit intrinsic myogenic responses to changes in transmural pressure, helping to maintain relatively constant blood flow despite fluctuations in systemic [[blood pressure]].

3. **Metabolic regulation**: Local metabolic factors, including [[nitric oxide]] (NO), [[prostaglandins]], and [[endothelin-1]], modulate arteriolar tone and blood flow in response to tissue metabolic demands.

4. **Pupillary dynamics**: During [[mydriasis]], the iris stroma is compressed and the radial arterioles become more tortuous, potentially increasing vascular resistance. During [[miosis]], the iris is stretched and the vessels straighten, potentially decreasing resistance. The minor arterial circle, being located at the collarette, serves as a critical hemodynamic junction that buffers these changes.

### Role in the Blood-Aqueous Barrier

The non-fenestrated endothelium of the minor arterial circle vessels is a critical component of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]]. Under normal physiological conditions, this barrier prevents the leakage of plasma proteins (such as [[albumin]], [[immunoglobulins]], and [[fibrinogen]]) and cellular elements into the aqueous humor. Maintenance of this barrier is essential for:

- Preserving the optical clarity of the aqueous humor
- Maintaining the low protein concentration of aqueous humor (normally ~0.02 g/dL, compared to ~7 g/dL in plasma)
- Regulating [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) by controlling the composition of aqueous humor
- Providing relative immune privilege to the anterior chamber (part of [[anterior chamber-associated immune deviation]], or [[ACAID]])

## Pathophysiology

Disruption or alteration of the minor arterial circle of the iris is implicated in a wide range of ocular pathologies. The pathophysiological mechanisms can be broadly categorized as **inflammatory**, **ischemic**, **neovascular**, **traumatic**, and **degenerative**.

### Inflammatory Disruption

In [[anterior uveitis]] (iritis/iridocyclitis), inflammatory mediators such as [[prostaglandins]], [[histamine]], [[interleukins]] (particularly [[IL-1]], [[IL-6]], and [[TNF-α]]), and [[leukotrienes]] cause breakdown of the tight junctions in the endothelium of the minor arterial circle and other iris vessels. This results in:

- **Increased vascular permeability** → leakage of proteins into the aqueous humor ([[aqueous flare]])
- **Cellular exudation** → migration of [[leukocytes]] (predominantly [[lymphocytes]] and [[macrophages]]) into the anterior chamber ([[aqueous cells]])
- **Fibrin deposition** → formation of [[fibrin]] clots in the anterior chamber or on the iris surface
- **[[Posterior synechiae]]** → adhesion of the inflamed iris to the [[anterior lens capsule]]
- **[[Keratic precipitates]]** → deposition of inflammatory cells on the [[corneal endothelium]]

The minor arterial circle, due to its location at the collarette and its role as a major anastomotic junction, is particularly vulnerable to inflammatory disruption. Chronic or recurrent inflammation can lead to permanent vascular damage, [[iris atrophy]], and impaired pupillary function.

### Ischemic Pathology

Ischemia affecting the iris vasculature, including the minor arterial circle, can result from:

- **[[Ocular ischemic syndrome]]** (OIS) secondary to [[carotid artery stenosis]] or [[carotid artery occlusion]]
- **[[Central retinal artery occlusion]]** (CRAO) or [[branch retinal artery occlusion]] (BRAO) with associated anterior segment ischemia
- **[[Giant cell arteritis]]** (GCA) affecting the ophthalmic artery or its branches
- **[[Sickle cell disease]]** causing microvascular occlusion
- **Post-surgical ischemia** following procedures that compromise the anterior ciliary arteries (e.g., extensive [[strabismus surgery]] involving multiple rectus muscles)

Ischemia of the minor arterial circle leads to:

- **Iris stromal atrophy** — thinning and loss of iris tissue, particularly in the pupillary zone
- **Pupillary abnormalities** — irregular pupil shape, poor pupillary reactivity, or [[corectopia]]
- **Sector iris atrophy** — segmental loss of iris pigment and stroma
- **[[Neovascularization of the iris]]** ([[rubeosis iridis]]) — a compensatory but pathological response to ischemia-driven VEGF release

### Neovascularization (Rubeosis Iridis)

[[Rubeosis iridis]] is the growth of new, abnormal blood vessels on the surface of the iris, typically originating from the [[pupillary margin]] and the minor arterial circle region, and extending toward the [[angle]] of the [[anterior chamber]]. This process is driven by the release of [[VEGF]] and other pro-angiogenic factors from ischemic retinal tissue.

Common causes include:

| Condition | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| [[Proliferative diabetic retinopathy]] | Retinal ischemia → VEGF release → iris neovascularization |
| [[Central retinal vein occlusion]] (CRVO) | Retinal venous stasis and ischemia → VEGF release |
| [[Ocular ischemic syndrome]] | Global ocular hypoperfusion → VEGF release |
| [[Chronic retinal detachment]] | Retinal ischemia → VEGF release |
| [[Intraocular tumors]] (e.g., [[retinoblastoma]], [[uveal melanoma]]) | Tumor-derived angiogenic factors |

The neovascular vessels that arise in rubeosis iridis are structurally abnormal — they lack the tight junctions characteristic of normal iris vessels and are prone to leakage and hemorrhage. These vessels initially appear at the pupillary margin (near the minor arterial circle) and progressively extend across the iris surface toward the angle, where they can form a fibrovascular membrane that obstructs the [[trabecular meshwork]], leading to [[neovascular glaucoma]] (NVG).

### Traumatic Disruption

Blunt or penetrating [[ocular trauma]] can directly damage the minor arterial circle, leading to:

- **[[Hyphema]]** — hemorrhage into the anterior chamber from torn iris vessels, including branches of the minor arterial circle
- **[[Iridodialysis]]** — disinsertion of the iris root from the ciliary body, disrupting the connection between the major and minor arterial circles
- **[[Traumatic mydriasis]]** — damage to the sphincter pupillae and its vascular supply from the minor circle
- **[[Iris sphincter tears]]** — visible as notches at the pupillary margin, often associated with disruption of terminal branches of the minor circle

### Iatrogenic Disruption

Surgical procedures involving the iris can disrupt the minor arterial circle:

- **[[Iridectomy]]** or **[[iridotomy]]** — surgical or laser creation of an opening in the iris may transect branches of the minor circle
- **[[Pupilloplasty]]** — suture manipulation of the pupil can compromise minor circle blood flow
- **[[Cataract surgery]]** — mechanical iris manipulation, particularly with iris hooks or pupil expansion devices, can damage the minor circle
- **[[Iris-fixated intraocular lenses]]** — chronic mechanical contact with the iris may cause vascular attrition

### Degenerative and Age-Related Changes

With aging, the iris vasculature, including the minor arterial circle, undergoes progressive changes:

- **Arteriolar sclerosis** — thickening of the vessel walls with hyaline deposition
- **Reduced vascular density** — loss of small arteriolar branches
- **Increased vascular tortuosity** — elongation and coiling of radial arterioles
- **Basement membrane thickening** — accumulation of [[type IV collagen]] and [[laminin]]
- **Decreased blood flow** — reduced perfusion of the pupillary zone

These changes contribute to the age-related decrease in pupil size ([[senile miosis]]), reduced pupillary reactivity, and increased susceptibility to iris atrophy.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

The minor arterial circle of the iris is not directly visible on routine clinical examination without specialized imaging. However, pathology affecting this structure manifests through a variety of clinical signs and symptoms:

### Signs

| Clinical Sign | Associated Pathology |
|---|---|
| [[Aqueous flare]] and [[aqueous cells]] | Blood-aqueous barrier breakdown (uveitis, trauma) |
| [[Hyphema]] | Traumatic or spontaneous rupture of iris vessels |
| [[Rubeosis iridis]] | Neovascularization secondary to ischemia |
| [[Iris atrophy]] (sectoral or diffuse) | Ischemia, inflammation, herpes simplex/zoster |
| [[Posterior synechiae]] | Chronic inflammation with fibrin deposition |
| [[Corectopia]] | Ischemic or inflammatory damage to sphincter and its blood supply |
| Irregular pupil | Sphincter tears, ischemia, or synechiae |
| [[Ectropion uveae]] | Neovascular membrane contraction pulling pigment epithelium anteriorly |
| [[Heterochromia iridis]] | Chronic ischemia or inflammation (e.g., [[Fuchs heterochromic iridocyclitis]]) |
| Decreased pupillary light reflex | Sphincter ischemia or denervation |

### Symptoms

Patients with pathology affecting the minor arterial circle may report:

- **Blurred vision** — due to aqueous flare, hyphema, or secondary complications
- **Photophobia** — due to inflammation or traumatic mydriasis
- **Pain** — particularly in acute uveitis or acute angle-closure from neovascular glaucoma
- **Red eye** — from associated conjunctival or ciliary injection
- **Floaters** — from inflammatory debris in the aqueous or vitreous
- **Visual field loss** — if secondary [[glaucoma]] develops
- **Decreased visual acuity** — from corneal edema, cataract, or macular pathology

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] is the primary clinical tool for evaluating the iris and detecting signs of minor arterial circle pathology. Key findings include:

- **Iris transillumination defects** — areas of iris atrophy where the minor circle has been compromised, allowing retroillumination of light through the thinned iris
- **Pupillary margin irregularities** — sphincter tears or atrophy suggesting vascular compromise
- **Neovascularization** — fine, irregular vessels on the iris surface, particularly at the pupillary margin and collarette
- **Anterior chamber reaction** — cells and flare indicating blood-aqueous barrier breakdown
- **Hyphema** — layered blood in the anterior chamber
- **Synechiae** — posterior (iris-to-lens) or anterior (iris-to-cornea) adhesions

### Iris Fluorescein Angiography (IFA)

[[Iris fluorescein angiography]] is the gold standard for evaluating the iris vasculature, including the minor arterial circle. This technique involves intravenous injection of [[sodium fluorescein]] dye followed by sequential photography of the iris using a specialized camera with excitation and barrier filters.

**Normal IFA findings of the minor arterial circle:**
- The radial arterioles fill rapidly from the major circle toward the collarette
- The minor arterial circle appears as an anastomotic arcade at the collarette, with variable completeness
- Terminal arterioles extend from the minor circle toward the pupillary margin
- Venous drainage occurs centrifugally through iris venules
- No leakage of dye is seen in normal eyes (intact blood-aqueous barrier)

**Abnormal IFA findings:**
- **Dye leakage** — indicates blood-aqueous barrier breakdown (uveitis, trauma, surgery)
- **Vascular filling defects** — indicate ischemia or vascular occlusion
- **Neovascularization** — irregular, leaky new vessels, particularly at the pupillary margin
- **Vascular tortuosity** — increased coiling of radial arterioles
- **Microaneurysms** — focal dilations of iris arterioles
- **Non-perfusion areas** — sectors of iris with absent vascular filling

### Indocyanine Green Angiography (ICGA)

[[Indocyanine green angiography]] can provide complementary information to IFA, particularly for evaluating deeper stromal vessels and choroidal circulation. ICG has greater penetration through pigmented tissues and may reveal vascular details not visible on fluorescein angiography.

### Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography (AS-OCT)

[[Anterior segment OCT]] provides high-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the iris and can demonstrate:

- Iris thickness at the collarette (site of the minor arterial circle)
- Iris stromal atrophy
- Iris cysts or masses that may compress or displace the minor circle
- Pupillary membrane remnants

### Anterior Segment OCT Angiography (AS-OCTA)

[[OCT angiography]] of the anterior segment is an emerging, non-invasive technique that can visualize iris blood flow without the need for dye injection. AS-OCTA can demonstrate:

- The radial arteriolar pattern of the iris
- The anastomotic arcade at the collarette (minor arterial circle)
- Areas of vascular dropout or non-perfusion
- Neovascularization
- Changes in vascular density with disease

This modality is particularly promising for longitudinal monitoring of iris vascular changes in conditions such as [[diabetes mellitus]], [[uveitis]], and post-surgical states.

### Ultrasound Biomicroscopy (UBM)

[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (typically 35–50 MHz) provides high-resolution imaging of the anterior segment structures, including the iris, ciliary body, and angle. While UBM does not directly visualize blood flow, it can demonstrate:

- Iris thickness and structural integrity
- Ciliary body anatomy and its relationship to the major arterial circle
- Angle structures relevant to neovascular glaucoma assessment
- Iris and ciliary body tumors that may affect the minor circle

### Gonioscopy

[[Gonioscopy]] is essential for evaluating the [[iridocorneal angle]] in cases where neovascularization from the minor arterial circle region extends toward the angle, potentially causing [[neovascular glaucoma]]. Fine neovascular vessels may be seen crossing the [[trabecular meshwork]] and forming [[peripheral anterior synechiae]] (PAS).

## Management and Treatment

Management of conditions affecting the minor arterial circle of the iris is directed at the underlying pathology. There is no direct treatment for the minor arterial circle itself; rather, therapeutic interventions aim to address the causative disease process and mitigate its consequences.

### Pharmacological Interventions

| Drug Class | Examples | Mechanism / Indication |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Corticosteroids]]** (topical) | [[Prednisolone acetate]] 1%, [[Dexamethasone]] 0.1%, [[Difluprednate]] 0.05% | Suppress inflammation, restore blood-aqueous barrier integrity in uveitis |
| **[[NSAIDs]]** (topical) | [[Ketorolac]] 0.5%, [[Nepafenac]] 0.1%, [[Bromfenac]] 0.07% | Inhibit [[cyclooxygenase]], reduce prostaglandin-mediated vascular permeability |
| **[[Cycloplegics]] / [[Mydriatics]]** | [[Atropine]] 1%, [[Cyclopentolate]] 1%, [[Homatropine]] 2% | Reduce ciliary spasm, prevent/break posterior synechiae, stabilize blood-aqueous barrier |
| **[[Anti-VEGF agents]]** (intravitreal or intracameral) | [[Bevacizumab]], [[Ranibizumab]], [[Aflibercept]] | Inhibit VEGF-driven neovascularization in rubeosis iridis |
| **[[Immunosuppressants]]** (systemic) | [[Methotrexate]], [[Mycophenolate mofetil]], [[Azathioprine]], [[Cyclosporine]] | Control chronic or recurrent uveitis refractory to corticosteroids |
| **[[Biologic agents]]** | [[Adalimumab]], [[Infliximab]] | TNF-α inhibitors for refractory non-infectious uveitis |
| **[[Antiglaucoma medications]]** | [[Timolol]], [[Brimonidine]], [[Dorzolamide]], [[Latanoprost]] | Reduce IOP in secondary glaucoma from neovascularization or inflammation |
| **[[Antifibrinolytics]]** | [[Aminocaproic acid]], [[Tranexamic acid]] | Stabilize clot and reduce rebleeding risk in traumatic hyphema |

### Laser Interventions

- **[[Panretinal photocoagulation]]** (PRP): The definitive treatment for retinal ischemia-driven rubeosis iridis. PRP ablates ischemic retinal tissue, reducing VEGF production and promoting regression of iris neovascularization originating from the minor arterial circle region.

- **[[Nd:YAG laser iridotomy]]**: May be performed to relieve pupillary block in cases where posterior synechiae (secondary to inflammation at the minor circle) cause iris bombé and secondary angle closure. Care must be taken to avoid major iris vessels.

- **[[Argon laser photocoagulation]]**: Can be used to directly treat iris neovascularization in selected cases, though this is generally less effective than addressing the underlying retinal ischemia.

### Surgical Interventions

- **[[Trabeculectomy]]** or **[[glaucoma drainage device]]** implantation: For neovascular glaucoma refractory to medical therapy and PRP.

- **[[Transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]]**: For refractory neovascular glaucoma, particularly in eyes with poor visual potential.

- **[[Anterior chamber washout]]**: For non-resolving hyphema from traumatic disruption of iris vessels including the minor circle.

- **[[Iris repair / pupilloplasty]]**: For traumatic iris damage with disruption of the minor arterial circle, to restore pupillary function and cosmesis.

- **[[Vitrectomy]]** with endolaser: In cases of combined posterior and anterior segment ischemic disease driving rubeosis.

### Monitoring and Follow-Up

Patients with conditions affecting the minor arterial circle require regular monitoring including:

- Serial slit-lamp examination for iris changes, anterior chamber reaction, and neovascularization
- [[Intraocular pressure]] measurement at each visit
- [[Gonioscopy]] to assess for angle neovascularization and synechiae
- [[Fundus examination]] to evaluate for retinal ischemia
- [[Iris fluorescein angiography]] or [[AS-OCTA]] for objective assessment of iris vascular status
- [[Visual field testing]] if glaucoma is suspected

## Comparative Anatomy: Major vs. Minor Arterial Circle

| Feature | [[Major Arterial Circle of the Iris]] | **Minor Arterial Circle of the Iris** |
|---|---|---|
| **Location** | Iris root / ciliary body junction | Collarette (pupillary-ciliary zone junction) |
| **Completeness** | Usually complete | Often incomplete / variable |
| **Vessel caliber** | Larger arterioles | Smaller arterioles |
| **Blood supply source** | Long posterior ciliary arteries + anterior ciliary arteries | Radial branches from the major circle |
| **Territory supplied** | Entire iris, ciliary body, conjunctiva (partially) | Pupillary zone of the iris, sphincter pupillae |
| **Clinical visibility** | Not directly visible (deep location) | Not directly visible (stromal location) |
| **Imaging** | IFA, ICGA, AS-OCTA, UBM | IFA, ICGA, AS-OCTA |
| **Surgical relevance** | At risk during iridectomy, cyclodialysis, glaucoma surgery | At risk during pupilloplasty, sphincterotomy |

## Clinical Significance in Specific Conditions

### Diabetes Mellitus

In [[diabetic eye disease]], chronic hyperglycemia leads to [[pericyte]] loss, basement membrane thickening, and endothelial dysfunction in the iris microvasculature, including the minor arterial circle. These microvascular changes contribute to:

- Impaired pupillary dilation (diabetic autonomic neuropathy compounded by vascular insufficiency)
- Increased susceptibility to blood-aqueous barrier breakdown during surgery
- Progression to rubeosis iridis in advanced proliferative disease

### Herpes Simplex and Herpes Zoster

[[Herpes simplex virus]] (HSV) and [[herpes zoster virus]] (HZV) can cause a necrotizing vasculitis of the iris vessels, including the minor arterial circle, leading to characteristic **sectoral iris atrophy** and **iris transillumination defects**. The ischemic damage to the sphincter pupillae from minor circle compromise results in a **fixed, dilated pupil** or **irregular pupil** that is a hallmark of herpetic anterior uveitis.

### Fuchs Heterochromic Iridocyclitis

In [[Fuchs heterochromic iridocyclitis]] (FHI), chronic low-grade inflammation leads to progressive iris stromal atrophy and depigmentation. The minor arterial circle vessels may show characteristic changes on IFA, including diffuse leakage and vascular remodeling. The fragile iris vessels in FHI are prone to hemorrhage during intraocular surgery (known as **Amsler sign** — hemorrhage from the angle upon paracentesis).

### Pseudoexfoliation Syndrome

[[Pseudoexfoliation syndrome]] (PXF) is associated with deposition of abnormal fibrillar material on iris vessels, including those of the minor arterial circle. This deposition can compromise vascular integrity, contributing to:

- Poor pupillary dilation
- Iris transillumination defects at the pupillary margin
- Increased risk of hyphema during cataract surgery
- Blood-aqueous barrier instability

## Summary

The minor arterial circle of the iris is a variably complete anastomotic vascular arcade located at the collarette of the iris, formed by the convergence of radial arteriolar branches from the [[major arterial circle of the iris]]. It serves as the principal blood supply to the pupillary zone of the iris and the [[sphincter pupillae]] muscle, and its non-fenestrated endothelium is a critical component of the [[blood-aqueous barrier]].

Pathology affecting the minor arterial circle — whether inflammatory, ischemic, neovascular, traumatic, or degenerative — can result in significant clinical consequences including iris atrophy, pupillary dysfunction, hyphema, rubeosis iridis, and secondary glaucoma. Diagnosis relies on slit-lamp biomicroscopy, iris fluorescein angiography, anterior segment OCT angiography, and gonioscopy. Management is directed at the underlying cause and may include topical and systemic anti-inflammatory agents, anti-VEGF therapy, laser photocoagulation, and surgical intervention.

A thorough understanding of the anatomy, physiology, and clinical significance of the minor arterial circle of the iris is essential for all eye care professionals involved in the diagnosis and management of anterior segment disease.
