

# Sheard's Criterion

---

**Sheard's Criterion** is a foundational clinical rule in [[binocular vision]] assessment used by [[optometrists]] and [[orthoptists]] to predict whether a patient's [[heterophoria]] (latent deviation) is likely to become symptomatic or decompensate into a manifest deviation ([[heterotropia]]). First described by **Dr. Charles Sheard** in the 1930s, the criterion provides a quantitative, evidence-based guideline for determining the adequacy of a patient's [[fusional vergence]] reserves relative to their phoria magnitude. It remains one of the most widely taught and clinically applied normative criteria in [[binocular vision]] and [[vergence]] analysis, particularly in the management of [[exophoria]], [[convergence insufficiency]], and other non-strabismic binocular vision disorders.

Sheard's Criterion states:

> **For comfortable, sustained binocular vision, the compensating fusional vergence reserve must be at least twice the magnitude of the heterophoria.**

If this criterion is not met, the patient is considered at risk for asthenopic symptoms, binocular instability, and potential decompensation of the phoria.

---

## Historical Background

Dr. **Charles Sheard** (1883–1962) was an American physicist and physiological optician who made significant contributions to the understanding of [[binocular vision]], [[heterophoria]], and [[fusional vergence]]. Working at the Mayo Clinic in Rochester, Minnesota, Sheard published extensively on the relationship between phorias and vergence reserves during the 1920s and 1930s. His seminal work, published in the *American Journal of Ophthalmology* and other leading journals, sought to establish objective, quantifiable criteria that clinicians could use to determine when a phoria was clinically significant—that is, when it was likely to produce symptoms or require intervention.

Prior to Sheard's work, the clinical significance of a measured phoria was largely a matter of subjective clinical judgment. Sheard's contribution was to formalize the relationship between the magnitude of the phoria and the opposing (compensating) fusional vergence reserve, providing a simple mathematical rule that could be applied at the chairside. His criterion was developed through extensive clinical observation and normative data collection, and it has since been validated and refined by subsequent researchers including **Morgan**, **Percival**, and **Saladin**.

Sheard's Criterion is most commonly applied to [[horizontal phorias]]—particularly [[exophoria]]—and is considered especially reliable for predicting symptoms at [[near fixation]], where vergence demands are greatest. It is frequently contrasted with [[Percival's Criterion]], another normative guideline that uses a different approach to evaluate binocular comfort.

## Pathophysiology

### The Binocular Vision System and Vergence Demand

To understand Sheard's Criterion, one must first appreciate the physiology of [[binocular single vision]] (BSV). The human visual system relies on the coordinated alignment of both eyes so that corresponding retinal images fall on [[corresponding retinal points]], enabling [[sensory fusion]] and [[stereopsis]]. This alignment is maintained by the [[vergence system]], which includes:

- **[[Tonic vergence]]**: The baseline resting position of the eyes, determined by the tonic innervation to the [[extraocular muscles]].
- **[[Accommodative vergence]]**: Vergence driven by the [[accommodation]]-vergence synkinesis (the [[AC/A ratio]]).
- **[[Fusional vergence]]** (also called disparity vergence): Reflexive vergence driven by [[retinal disparity]], which acts to correct small misalignments and maintain fusion.
- **[[Proximal vergence]]**: Vergence driven by the awareness of nearness of a target.

In an ideal system, the eyes are perfectly aligned for the fixation distance, and no additional fusional vergence effort is required. In reality, most individuals have a small [[heterophoria]]—a latent tendency for the eyes to deviate from perfect alignment when fusion is disrupted (e.g., during a [[cover test]] or with a [[Maddox rod]]). This phoria represents the underlying misalignment that the fusional vergence system must continuously compensate for during binocular viewing.

### Compensating Fusional Vergence

The concept of **compensating fusional vergence** is central to Sheard's Criterion. For any given phoria, the compensating vergence is the fusional reserve that acts in the direction opposite to the phoria:

| Phoria Type | Direction of Deviation | Compensating Fusional Vergence |
|---|---|---|
| [[Exophoria]] | Eyes tend outward (diverge) | [[Positive fusional vergence]] (PFV) / [[Base-out]] (BO) reserves |
| [[Esophoria]] | Eyes tend inward (converge) | [[Negative fusional vergence]] (NFV) / [[Base-in]] (BI) reserves |
| [[Hyperphoria]] | One eye tends upward | Opposing [[vertical fusional vergence]] |

For example, a patient with 6 prism diopters (Δ) of [[exophoria]] at near must exert at least 6Δ of [[positive fusional vergence]] (convergence) to maintain alignment. Sheard's Criterion stipulates that the blur point or break point of the compensating vergence reserve must be at least **twice** the phoria value for the patient to maintain comfortable binocular vision.

### Mechanism of Decompensation

When the fusional vergence reserves are insufficient relative to the phoria (i.e., Sheard's Criterion is not met), the vergence system operates near its maximum capacity. This leads to several pathophysiological consequences:

1. **Vergence fatigue**: The [[extraocular muscles]] and the neural vergence control centers (including the [[midbrain]] vergence nuclei and [[cerebellum]]) are subjected to sustained, high-level demand. Over time, this produces fatigue, analogous to muscular fatigue in skeletal muscles.

2. **Increased retinal disparity**: As the vergence system fatigues, small lapses in alignment occur, producing transient [[retinal disparity]] that exceeds [[Panum's fusional area]]. This may result in intermittent [[diplopia]] or suppression.

3. **Accommodative-vergence conflict**: Because [[accommodation]] and [[vergence]] are neurally linked via the [[AC/A ratio]], excessive vergence effort can drive inappropriate accommodative changes, leading to [[blur]], [[asthenopia]], and difficulty sustaining focus—particularly during prolonged near tasks such as reading or screen use.

4. **Sympathetic autonomic activation**: Sustained effort to maintain fusion may trigger sympathetic responses including [[headache]], periorbital discomfort, and general fatigue.

5. **Suppression or avoidance**: In some patients, particularly children, the visual system may adopt [[suppression]] of one eye's image as an adaptive mechanism to avoid diplopia, potentially leading to reduced [[stereoacuity]] or, in younger patients, [[amblyopia]].

The pathophysiology is thus one of **demand exceeding capacity**: the phoria represents the demand on the fusional vergence system, and the compensating vergence reserve represents the capacity. Sheard's Criterion quantifies the minimum capacity-to-demand ratio required for sustained binocular comfort.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Patients who fail Sheard's Criterion—that is, whose compensating fusional vergence reserves are less than twice their phoria—may present with a wide range of symptoms collectively termed **[[asthenopia]]** or binocular vision-related symptoms. The severity and nature of symptoms depend on the magnitude of the phoria-reserve imbalance, the patient's visual demands, and individual factors such as age, general health, and cognitive load.

### Common Symptoms

| Symptom | Description |
|---|---|
| [[Asthenopia]] (eye strain) | A sensation of tiredness, aching, or discomfort in or around the eyes, typically worsening with prolonged visual tasks |
| [[Headache]] | Frontal or temporal headaches, often occurring after sustained near work; may mimic [[tension-type headache]] |
| [[Diplopia]] (double vision) | Intermittent or constant awareness of two images, especially during fatigue or prolonged near tasks |
| Blurred vision | Transient blur at near or distance, often fluctuating, due to accommodative-vergence interaction |
| Difficulty reading | Loss of place, re-reading lines, reduced reading speed, and poor comprehension |
| Print movement | Perception that words or letters are moving, swimming, or overlapping on the page |
| Difficulty with sustained near work | Inability to maintain focus on near tasks (reading, computer use, mobile devices) for extended periods |
| Closing or covering one eye | A compensatory behavior to eliminate diplopia or reduce vergence demand |
| Photophobia | Increased sensitivity to light, possibly related to autonomic co-activation |
| Nausea or motion sickness | In severe cases, particularly with large phorias or significant vergence dysfunction |
| Reduced [[stereopsis]] | Decreased depth perception, which may affect activities such as driving, sports, or fine motor tasks |

### Symptom Patterns by Phoria Type

- **[[Exophoria]]** (the most common scenario where Sheard's Criterion is applied): Symptoms are typically worst at near, where convergence demand is highest. Patients may report difficulty reading, eye strain with computer use, and intermittent diplopia during prolonged near tasks. Symptoms often worsen toward the end of the day or during periods of illness or fatigue.

- **[[Esophoria]]**: Symptoms may be present at distance or near, depending on the fixation distance at which the esophoria is greatest. Patients may report blur (due to accommodative relaxation attempting to reduce convergence), headache, and difficulty shifting focus between distances.

- **[[Hyperphoria]]**: Vertical phorias are generally less well tolerated than horizontal phorias, and even small vertical deviations may fail Sheard's Criterion due to the limited range of [[vertical fusional vergence]] (typically only 2–4Δ). Symptoms may include head tilt, vertical diplopia, and significant asthenopia.

### Populations at Increased Risk

Certain populations are more likely to fail Sheard's Criterion and present with symptoms:

- **Students and office workers** with high near-work demands
- **Patients with [[convergence insufficiency]]** (CI), the most common binocular vision disorder
- **Post-[[concussion]]** or [[traumatic brain injury]] (TBI) patients, who frequently develop vergence dysfunction
- **Presbyopic patients**, in whom the loss of [[accommodative amplitude]] alters the accommodative-vergence balance
- **Patients on medications** that affect the central nervous system (e.g., [[benzodiazepines]], [[anticonvulsants]]), which may reduce vergence tone

## Diagnosis

### Measurement of the Phoria

The first step in applying Sheard's Criterion is accurate measurement of the patient's [[heterophoria]] at the relevant fixation distance (typically both distance [6 meters or optical infinity] and near [40 cm]). Several clinical methods are available:

| Method | Description | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| [[Cover-uncover test]] / [[Alternating cover test]] with [[prism bar]] | Gold standard for measuring the direction and magnitude of the phoria | Requires patient cooperation; measures total phoria |
| [[Von Graefe technique]] | Uses a [[phoropter]] with vertical and horizontal prism dissociation to measure the phoria | Commonly used in clinical optometric practice; may slightly overestimate exophoria |
| [[Maddox rod]] test | A [[Maddox rod]] placed before one eye creates a line image, and the patient reports the position of the line relative to a point light | Quick and easy; useful for screening |
| [[Modified Thorington test]] | A Maddox rod is used with a tangent scale at the fixation distance | Provides a direct reading in prism diopters; good repeatability |
| [[Howell-Dwyer card]] | A near card with a central fixation target and calibrated scale, used with a septum or Maddox rod | Commonly used in the UK and Australia |

The phoria is recorded in **prism diopters (Δ)**, with the direction specified (e.g., 8Δ exophoria at near).

### Measurement of Fusional Vergence Reserves

The second essential measurement is the **compensating fusional vergence reserve**. This is measured using prisms (either a [[prism bar]] or [[Risley prisms]] in a [[phoropter]]) to determine the range of vergence the patient can exert while maintaining single binocular vision.

For each vergence direction, three values are typically recorded:

1. **Blur point**: The prism power at which the patient first reports sustained blur (indicates the limit of fusional vergence before accommodative compensation begins).
2. **Break point**: The prism power at which the patient reports diplopia (fusion is lost).
3. **Recovery point**: The prism power at which single vision is regained as prism is reduced from the break point.

**Sheard's Criterion uses the blur point of the compensating fusional vergence reserve.** If no blur point is reported (as is common with base-out testing), the break point may be used instead, though this is a less conservative measure.

#### Example Measurements

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Near phoria | 8Δ exophoria |
| Positive fusional vergence (BO) at near | Blur: 12Δ / Break: 20Δ / Recovery: 14Δ |
| Compensating vergence (blur point) | 12Δ |
| Sheard's Criterion requirement | ≥ 2 × 8Δ = 16Δ |
| **Result** | **12Δ < 16Δ → Criterion NOT met** |

In this example, the patient's compensating fusional vergence (12Δ) is less than twice the phoria (16Δ), so Sheard's Criterion is **not satisfied**, and the patient is predicted to be symptomatic.

### The Sheard's Criterion Formula

The criterion can be expressed mathematically:

> **Compensating Fusional Vergence (blur point) ≥ 2 × Phoria**

Or equivalently, the criterion is **failed** when:

> **Phoria > ½ × Compensating Fusional Vergence (blur point)**

#### Calculating the Prism Needed to Satisfy Sheard's Criterion

When Sheard's Criterion is not met, the minimum amount of [[prism correction]] (or equivalent vergence change through lenses or therapy) needed to restore the criterion can be calculated:

> **Prism needed = (2/3 × Phoria) − (1/3 × Compensating Fusional Vergence)**

Using the example above:

> Prism needed = (2/3 × 8) − (1/3 × 12) = 5.33 − 4.00 = **1.33Δ base-in**

This formula is derived algebraically from the criterion and assumes that the prescribed prism will both reduce the effective phoria and increase the remaining fusional vergence reserve proportionally.

### Comparison with Other Normative Criteria

Sheard's Criterion is one of several normative guidelines used in binocular vision assessment. Understanding its relationship to other criteria is important for comprehensive clinical decision-making:

| Criterion | Rule | Best Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Sheard's Criterion** | Compensating vergence ≥ 2 × phoria | [[Exophoria]]; near vision analysis |
| **[[Percival's Criterion]]** | The lesser vergence range must be ≥ ½ the greater vergence range | [[Esophoria]]; general binocular balance |
| **1:1 Rule (Saladin)** | Compensating vergence ≥ phoria (less conservative) | Screening; low-demand patients |
| **[[Morgan's Norms]]** | Expected normative values for phorias and vergences by age | Baseline comparison; identifying deviations from population norms |
| **[[Fixation Disparity]] analysis (Ogle, Sheedy & Saladin)** | Uses [[associated phoria]] and forced vergence fixation disparity curves | More sensitive; identifies subclinical decompensation |

Research has consistently shown that **Sheard's Criterion is most predictive for exophoric patients**, while **Percival's Criterion may be more applicable for esophoric patients**. In clinical practice, many practitioners evaluate both criteria and use clinical judgment to integrate the findings.

### Additional Diagnostic Considerations

When Sheard's Criterion is failed, the clinician should perform a comprehensive binocular vision evaluation to characterize the underlying disorder and guide management. This may include:

- **[[Accommodative facility]]** testing (±2.00 D flippers) to assess the speed and flexibility of the accommodative system
- **[[Vergence facility]]** testing (e.g., 12Δ BO / 3Δ BI flippers) to assess the speed of vergence responses
- **[[Near point of convergence]]** (NPC) measurement to evaluate convergence amplitude
- **[[AC/A ratio]]** determination (calculated or gradient method) to understand the accommodative-vergence relationship
- **[[Stereoacuity]]** testing (e.g., [[Randot stereotest]], [[Titmus fly test]], [[TNO test]]) to assess the quality of binocular vision
- **[[Fixation disparity]]** assessment (e.g., [[Wesson card]], [[Sheedy Disparometer]], [[Saladin card]]) to detect subclinical misalignment under binocular conditions
- **Symptom questionnaires** (e.g., [[Convergence Insufficiency Symptom Survey]] [CISS]) to quantify symptom severity and monitor treatment outcomes

## Management and Treatment

When Sheard's Criterion is not met and the patient is symptomatic, several management strategies are available. The choice of intervention depends on the magnitude of the phoria-reserve imbalance, the patient's age, visual demands, motivation, and the underlying etiology of the binocular vision dysfunction.

### 1. Optical Correction (Lenses)

#### a. Refractive Correction

Ensuring an accurate and up-to-date [[refraction]] is the first step. Uncorrected or undercorrected [[refractive error]]—particularly [[hyperopia]]—can exacerbate esophoria through the accommodative-vergence link. Conversely, overcorrection of myopia (excessive minus lenses) can worsen exophoria by stimulating unnecessary accommodation and then relaxation of convergence.

- In **exophoric** patients: Avoid over-minusing. Consider slightly less minus (or more plus) correction if tolerated, to stimulate accommodative convergence and reduce the exophoria.
- In **esophoric** patients: Full correction of hyperopia (or addition of plus lenses at near) can reduce accommodative demand and, via the [[AC/A ratio]], reduce the esophoria.

#### b. Near Addition (Plus Lenses at Near)

For patients with a **high [[AC/A ratio]]** and near exophoria, a **plus add** (e.g., +0.50 to +1.50 D) at near can reduce accommodative demand, which in turn reduces the associated convergence demand, effectively reducing the near exophoria. This is particularly useful in:

- Young patients with [[convergence excess]] esophoria (where plus lenses reduce the eso deviation)
- Presbyopic patients whose loss of accommodation has disrupted the vergence balance

#### c. Prism Correction

[[Prism]] lenses can be prescribed to directly reduce the effective phoria, thereby restoring Sheard's Criterion. The minimum prism required can be calculated using the formula described above:

> **Prism = (2/3 × Phoria) − (1/3 × Compensating Fusional Vergence)**

| Phoria Type | Prism Base Direction |
|---|---|
| [[Exophoria]] | [[Base-in]] (BI) prism |
| [[Esophoria]] | [[Base-out]] (BO) prism |
| [[Hyperphoria]] | Base-down before the hyperphoric eye (or base-up before the other eye) |

Prism may be incorporated into spectacle lenses ([[ground-in prism]]) or applied as temporary [[Fresnel prism]] lenses for diagnostic or trial purposes. The goal is to prescribe the **minimum prism** necessary to satisfy Sheard's Criterion, not to fully neutralize the phoria, as some phoria is normal and complete neutralization may lead to prism adaptation and a worsening cycle.

**Prism adaptation** is an important consideration: the vergence system may adapt to the prescribed prism over time, causing the phoria to shift and potentially requiring increased prism. For this reason, prism is often considered a temporizing measure rather than a definitive treatment, particularly in younger patients who are candidates for [[vision therapy]].

### 2. Vision Therapy (Vergence Training)

**[[Vision therapy]]** (also known as [[orthoptic exercises]] or [[vergence training]]) is considered the gold standard treatment for non-strabismic binocular vision disorders, particularly [[convergence insufficiency]]. The goal of vision therapy in the context of Sheard's Criterion is to **increase the compensating fusional vergence reserves** so that the criterion is met, rather than to change the phoria itself (though phoria reduction may also occur).

#### Evidence Base

The **Convergence Insufficiency Treatment Trial (CITT)**, a landmark multi-center randomized clinical trial funded by the National Eye Institute (NEI), demonstrated that **office-based vergence/accommodative therapy** was significantly more effective than home-based pencil push-ups, home-based computer vergence therapy, or placebo therapy for the treatment of symptomatic [[convergence insufficiency]] in both children and adults. Patients who underwent office-based therapy showed significant improvements in:

- [[Near point of convergence]]
- [[Positive fusional vergence]] at near
- Symptom scores (CISS)
- Satisfaction of Sheard's Criterion

#### Common Vision Therapy Techniques

| Technique | Description | Target |
|---|---|---|
| [[Brock string]] | A string with colored beads at varying distances; the patient practices converging and diverging to each bead while maintaining single vision | [[Convergence]], [[divergence]], anti-suppression |
| [[Vectograms]] (e.g., Quoits, Clown) | Polarized targets viewed through polarized glasses; demand is varied by changing target separation | [[Fusional vergence]] (BO and BI) |
| [[Aperture rule]] | A device with apertures and targets that require vergence changes to achieve fusion | [[Fusional vergence]], [[stereopsis]] |
| [[Prism flippers]] (e.g., 12Δ BO / 3Δ BI) | Alternating prism lenses that require rapid vergence changes | [[Vergence facility]] |
| [[Computer-based vergence therapy]] (e.g., HTS, VividVision, Vivid Vision) | Software programs that present dichoptic stimuli requiring vergence responses | [[Fusional vergence]], [[vergence facility]], [[stereopsis]] |
| [[Pencil push-ups]] (HBPP) | The patient slowly brings a pencil toward the nose while maintaining single vision | [[Convergence amplitude]] (limited efficacy as standalone treatment per CITT) |
| [[Loose lens accommodative facility]] | Alternating plus and minus lenses to train accommodative flexibility | [[Accommodative facility]], indirect vergence benefit via AC/A link |
| [[Eccentric circles]] / [[Free-space fusion cards]] | Cards with slightly different images for each eye; the patient practices convergence and divergence to fuse the images | [[Fusional vergence]], anti-suppression |

A typical vision therapy program consists of **12–24 weekly in-office sessions** (each approximately 45–60 minutes) supplemented by daily home reinforcement exercises (15–20 minutes). Progress is monitored by serial measurement of vergence reserves, NPC, vergence facility, and symptom questionnaires.

### 3. Workplace and Ergonomic Modifications

For patients whose symptoms are primarily related to sustained near work (e.g., computer use), ergonomic modifications can reduce vergence demand and alleviate symptoms:

- **Increase working distance**: Moving the screen or reading material farther away reduces convergence demand.
- **The 20-20-20 rule**: Every 20 minutes, look at a target 20 feet away for 20 seconds to relax convergence and accommodation.
- **Proper lighting**: Reduce glare and ensure adequate illumination to minimize accommodative effort.
- **Screen positioning**: Position the computer screen slightly below eye level and at an appropriate distance (typically 50–70 cm).
- **Reduce multitasking**: Minimize the need for frequent shifts between near and distance fixation.

### 4. Pharmacological Considerations

There are no pharmacological agents that directly treat the vergence imbalance identified by Sheard's Criterion. However, certain medications may be relevant in specific clinical contexts:

- **[[Pilocarpine]]** (low-dose, e.g., 0.5%): Rarely, low-dose miotics have been used to stimulate accommodation and, via the AC/A ratio, increase convergence in selected cases of convergence insufficiency. This is not standard practice and carries side effects including [[miosis]], [[brow ache]], and [[myopic shift]].
- **Discontinuation of offending medications**: Drugs that cause vergence dysfunction (e.g., [[benzodiazepines]], [[opioids]], [[anticonvulsants]], [[anticholinergics]]) should be reviewed, and alternatives considered if clinically appropriate.

### 5. Surgical Intervention

Surgery is rarely indicated for non-strabismic binocular vision disorders identified by Sheard's Criterion. However, in cases of **large, decompensated phorias** that have progressed to intermittent or constant [[heterotropia]] (e.g., [[intermittent exotropia]]) and have failed conservative management, [[strabismus surgery]] (e.g., [[lateral rectus recession]], [[medial rectus resection]]) may be considered. Surgical planning in such cases involves a comprehensive assessment of the deviation at distance and near, the AC/A ratio, and the patient's fusional potential.

### 6. Monitoring and Follow-Up

Patients managed for failure of Sheard's Criterion should be monitored at regular intervals to assess:

- Symptom resolution (using standardized questionnaires such as the [[CISS]])
- Changes in phoria magnitude
- Changes in fusional vergence reserves
- Restoration of Sheard's Criterion
- [[Stereoacuity]]
- Reading performance and visual comfort

Follow-up intervals depend on the management strategy: patients undergoing vision therapy are typically seen weekly during active treatment, while those managed with lenses or prism may be reviewed at 4–6 week intervals initially, then at 3–6 month intervals once stable.

## Clinical Significance and Limitations

### Strengths of Sheard's Criterion

- **Simplicity**: The criterion is easy to calculate and apply at the chairside.
- **Quantitative**: It provides an objective, numerical threshold for clinical decision-making.
- **Predictive validity**: Multiple studies have confirmed its predictive value for symptoms in exophoric patients.
- **Treatment planning**: The prism formula derived from the criterion provides a rational basis for prescribing prism.
- **Widely taught and accepted**: It is a standard component of optometric education worldwide.

### Limitations

- **Best for exophoria**: Sheard's Criterion is most reliable for predicting symptoms in exophoric patients. Its predictive value for esophoria is less well established; [[Percival's Criterion]] may be more appropriate in these cases.
- **Uses blur point**: The criterion relies on the blur point of the compensating vergence, which is not always reported by patients (particularly during base-out testing). When no blur is reported, the break point is used, but this changes the sensitivity of the criterion.
- **Does not account for vergence facility**: A patient may have adequate static vergence reserves but poor vergence dynamics (slow or inaccurate vergence responses), leading to symptoms despite meeting Sheard's Criterion.
- **Does not account for fixation disparity**: Subclinical misalignment under binocular conditions ([[fixation disparity]]) may cause symptoms even when Sheard's Criterion is met. [[Fixation disparity analysis]] provides a more sensitive assessment in such cases.
- **Individual variability**: Some patients are symptomatic despite meeting the criterion, while others are asymptomatic despite failing it. Clinical judgment must always supplement normative criteria.
- **Does not address vertical phorias comprehensively**: While the criterion can theoretically be applied to vertical phorias, the very limited range of vertical fusional vergence makes the 2:1 ratio less clinically meaningful in this context.
