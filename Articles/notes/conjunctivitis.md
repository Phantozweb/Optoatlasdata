

# Conjunctivitis

**Conjunctivitis** is an inflammatory condition of the [[conjunctiva]]—the thin, translucent mucous membrane that lines the inner surface of the [[eyelid]] (palpebral conjunctiva) and covers the anterior [[sclera]] (bulbar conjunctiva). It is one of the most common ocular conditions encountered in primary eye care and general medical practice worldwide. Conjunctivitis may be infectious (viral, bacterial, chlamydial, fungal, or parasitic) or non-infectious (allergic, toxic, mechanical, or autoimmune) in etiology. While typically self-limiting, certain forms of conjunctivitis can lead to significant ocular morbidity, including [[corneal]] scarring, [[symblepharon]] formation, and permanent vision loss if inadequately managed. The condition affects all age groups, from neonates ([[ophthalmia neonatorum]]) to the elderly, and represents a significant burden on healthcare systems globally.

---

## Epidemiology

Conjunctivitis accounts for approximately 1–2% of all primary care consultations and is the most frequent cause of a "red eye" presentation in clinical practice. In the United States alone, an estimated 6 million cases of acute conjunctivitis occur annually. The economic impact is substantial, with direct and indirect costs exceeding $800 million per year in the U.S., largely attributable to lost productivity and inappropriate antibiotic prescribing.

| **Etiological Category** | **Estimated Proportion of Cases** | **Peak Incidence** |
|---|---|---|
| Viral conjunctivitis | 65–90% of infectious cases | Late fall through early spring |
| Bacterial conjunctivitis | 5–25% of infectious cases (higher in children) | Winter and early spring |
| Allergic conjunctivitis | 15–40% of the general population affected | Spring and fall (seasonal); year-round (perennial) |
| Chlamydial conjunctivitis | Variable; endemic in developing nations | No clear seasonal pattern |
| Other (toxic, mechanical, autoimmune) | <5% | Variable |

Viral conjunctivitis is the most common infectious form in adults, whereas bacterial conjunctivitis predominates in the pediatric population. [[Allergic conjunctivitis]], including [[seasonal allergic conjunctivitis]] (SAC) and [[perennial allergic conjunctivitis]] (PAC), affects a substantial proportion of the global population, with prevalence rates increasing in parallel with other atopic diseases.

## Anatomy of the Conjunctiva

A thorough understanding of conjunctival anatomy is essential for comprehending the pathophysiology of conjunctivitis.

The [[conjunctiva]] is a thin, vascularized mucous membrane divided into three anatomical zones:

1. **Palpebral (tarsal) conjunctiva**: Lines the inner surface of the [[eyelids]], firmly adherent to the underlying [[tarsus]]. It is subdivided into marginal, tarsal, and orbital zones.
2. **Forniceal conjunctiva**: The loose, redundant tissue in the superior and inferior [[fornix|fornices]], which allows free movement of the globe and lids.
3. **Bulbar conjunctiva**: Covers the anterior sclera, loosely attached to [[Tenon's capsule]] and the underlying [[episclera]], becoming continuous with the [[corneal epithelium]] at the [[limbus]].

The conjunctival [[epithelium]] is non-keratinized stratified squamous to columnar, containing interspersed [[goblet cells]] that secrete [[mucin]] (primarily MUC5AC), a critical component of the [[tear film]]. The [[substantia propria]] (stroma) is a loose connective tissue layer rich in immune cells, including [[lymphocytes]], [[mast cells]], [[plasma cells]], and [[macrophages]], forming part of the eye's [[conjunctiva-associated lymphoid tissue]] (CALT), a component of the broader [[mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue]] (MALT) system.

The conjunctiva receives its blood supply from the [[anterior ciliary arteries]] and the [[palpebral arterial arcades]], with venous drainage into the [[ophthalmic veins]]. Lymphatic drainage is to the [[preauricular lymph nodes]] (lateral conjunctiva) and [[submandibular lymph nodes]] (medial conjunctiva)—a clinically relevant finding, as preauricular [[lymphadenopathy]] is a hallmark of viral and chlamydial conjunctivitis.

Sensory innervation is provided by branches of the [[ophthalmic nerve]] (V1) and [[maxillary nerve]] (V2) of the [[trigeminal nerve]] (CN V).

## Classification

Conjunctivitis is broadly classified based on etiology, chronicity, and morphological response pattern.

### By Etiology

| **Category** | **Subtypes** |
|---|---|
| **Infectious** | Viral, Bacterial (including hyperacute), Chlamydial, Fungal, Parasitic |
| **Allergic/Immune-mediated** | [[Seasonal allergic conjunctivitis]] (SAC), [[Perennial allergic conjunctivitis]] (PAC), [[Vernal keratoconjunctivitis]] (VKC), [[Atopic keratoconjunctivitis]] (AKC), [[Giant papillary conjunctivitis]] (GPC), [[Contact dermatoconjunctivitis]] |
| **Toxic/Chemical** | Medicamentosa, Chemical burns (acid/alkali), Preservative toxicity |
| **Mechanical/Irritative** | [[Foreign body]], [[Trichiasis]], [[Entropion]], [[Ectropion]], Dry eye-related |
| **Autoimmune/Cicatrizing** | [[Ocular cicatricial pemphigoid]] (OCP), [[Stevens-Johnson syndrome]] (SJS)/[[Toxic epidermal necrolysis]] (TEN), [[Graft-versus-host disease]] (GVHD), [[Ligneous conjunctivitis]] |

### By Duration

- **Hyperacute**: Onset within 12–24 hours, rapidly progressive (e.g., [[Neisseria gonorrhoeae]])
- **Acute**: Duration less than 4 weeks
- **Chronic**: Duration greater than 4 weeks

### By Morphological Response

- **Papillary**: Characterized by papillae—small, polygonal elevations with a central vascular core on the tarsal conjunctiva. Seen in bacterial conjunctivitis, allergic conjunctivitis, and GPC.
- **Follicular**: Characterized by follicles—small, round, avascular lymphoid aggregates, typically in the inferior fornix and lower tarsal conjunctiva. Seen in viral and chlamydial conjunctivitis.
- **Membranous/Pseudomembranous**: Formation of a fibrinous membrane on the conjunctival surface. A true membrane involves necrosis of the epithelium and bleeds upon removal; a pseudomembrane can be peeled without bleeding. Seen in severe adenoviral infection, [[Streptococcus pyogenes]], [[Corynebacterium diphtheriae]], [[Stevens-Johnson syndrome]], and [[ligneous conjunctivitis]].
- **Cicatrizing (Scarring)**: Progressive subconjunctival fibrosis leading to [[symblepharon]], [[ankyloblepharon]], [[fornix foreshortening]], and [[entropion]]/[[trichiasis]]. Seen in [[trachoma]], OCP, SJS/TEN, and chemical burns.
- **Granulomatous**: Nodular conjunctival inflammation with granuloma formation. Seen in [[sarcoidosis]], [[tuberculosis]], [[cat-scratch disease]], and foreign body reactions.

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of conjunctivitis varies significantly depending on the underlying etiology, but all forms share a common final pathway of conjunctival inflammation characterized by vascular dilation, increased vascular permeability, and cellular infiltration.

### Viral Conjunctivitis

[[Adenovirus]] is responsible for the vast majority (65–90%) of viral conjunctivitis cases. Serotypes 8, 19, and 37 are classically associated with [[epidemic keratoconjunctivitis]] (EKC), while serotypes 3, 4, and 7 are associated with [[pharyngoconjunctival fever]] (PCF). The virus binds to host cell receptors (including [[CD46]], [[desmoglein-2]], and sialic acid residues) on the conjunctival epithelium, enters via [[clathrin]]-mediated [[endocytosis]], and replicates within epithelial cells, causing direct cytopathic damage. The host immune response involves activation of [[innate immunity]] (including [[toll-like receptors]], [[natural killer cells]], and [[interferon]] production) followed by a robust [[adaptive immune response]] mediated by [[CD4+ T helper cells]] and [[CD8+ cytotoxic T lymphocytes]]. This immune response is responsible for much of the clinical inflammation, including the formation of subepithelial infiltrates (SEIs) on the [[cornea]]—immune complexes composed of [[antigen-antibody]] aggregates and inflammatory cells that can persist for months to years and cause visual disturbance.

Other viral etiologies include [[herpes simplex virus]] (HSV), [[varicella-zoster virus]] (VZV), [[enterovirus 70]] (causing [[acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis]]), [[coxsackievirus A24]], [[molluscum contagiosum virus]], and, rarely, [[SARS-CoV-2]].

### Bacterial Conjunctivitis

Bacterial conjunctivitis results from direct inoculation of pathogenic organisms onto the conjunctival surface, overcoming the eye's natural defense mechanisms (including the [[tear film]], [[lysozyme]], [[lactoferrin]], [[secretory IgA]], and the mechanical action of [[blinking]]). Common pathogens include:

| **Patient Population** | **Common Organisms** |
|---|---|
| Adults | [[Staphylococcus aureus]], [[Staphylococcus epidermidis]], [[Streptococcus pneumoniae]], [[Haemophilus influenzae]], [[Moraxella catarrhalis]] |
| Children | [[Haemophilus influenzae]], [[Streptococcus pneumoniae]], [[Moraxella catarrhalis]] |
| Hyperacute | [[Neisseria gonorrhoeae]], [[Neisseria meningitidis]] |
| Neonates (Ophthalmia neonatorum) | [[Neisseria gonorrhoeae]], [[Chlamydia trachomatis]], [[Staphylococcus aureus]], [[Streptococcus]] spp., [[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]], [[Herpes simplex virus]] |

Bacterial adhesion to conjunctival epithelial cells is mediated by [[pili]], [[fimbriae]], and surface adhesins. Pathogenic bacteria produce [[exotoxins]], [[endotoxins]], [[proteases]], and [[lipases]] that cause direct tissue damage. The host response involves recruitment of [[polymorphonuclear leukocytes]] (PMNs/neutrophils), which are the predominant cells in the purulent discharge characteristic of bacterial conjunctivitis. [[Complement activation]], [[cytokine]] release (including [[IL-1]], [[IL-6]], [[IL-8]], and [[TNF-α]]), and [[immunoglobulin]] production contribute to the inflammatory cascade.

Hyperacute bacterial conjunctivitis caused by [[Neisseria gonorrhoeae]] is a true ophthalmic emergency due to the organism's ability to penetrate intact [[corneal epithelium]], potentially leading to rapid [[corneal ulceration]], [[perforation]], and [[endophthalmitis]].

### Allergic Conjunctivitis

Allergic conjunctivitis is a [[Type I hypersensitivity]] (IgE-mediated) reaction. Upon initial exposure to an [[allergen]] (e.g., pollen, dust mites, animal dander, mold spores), [[antigen-presenting cells]] process the allergen and stimulate [[B lymphocytes]] to produce allergen-specific [[IgE]] antibodies. These IgE molecules bind to high-affinity [[FcεRI receptors]] on conjunctival [[mast cells]], sensitizing them.

Upon re-exposure, the allergen cross-links surface-bound IgE on mast cells, triggering [[degranulation]] and the release of preformed mediators (including [[histamine]], [[tryptase]], and [[heparin]]) and newly synthesized mediators (including [[prostaglandins]], [[leukotrienes]] such as [[LTC4]], [[LTD4]], and [[LTE4]], and [[platelet-activating factor]]). This constitutes the **early-phase response** (occurring within minutes), characterized by itching, vasodilation, edema, and increased mucus production.

The **late-phase response** (occurring 4–24 hours later) involves recruitment of [[eosinophils]], [[basophils]], [[neutrophils]], and [[T lymphocytes]] to the conjunctiva, mediated by [[chemokines]] and [[cytokines]] (including [[IL-4]], [[IL-5]], [[IL-13]], and [[eotaxin]]). Eosinophils release [[major basic protein]] (MBP) and [[eosinophil cationic protein]] (ECP), which are directly toxic to the [[corneal epithelium]] and contribute to the [[shield ulcers]] and [[corneal plaques]] seen in severe [[vernal keratoconjunctivitis]] and [[atopic keratoconjunctivitis]].

In [[vernal keratoconjunctivitis]] (VKC) and [[atopic keratoconjunctivitis]] (AKC), a [[Type IV hypersensitivity]] (T cell-mediated) component also plays a significant role, with [[Th2 lymphocytes]] driving chronic inflammation and tissue remodeling, including conjunctival fibrosis and giant papilla formation.

### Chlamydial Conjunctivitis

[[Chlamydia trachomatis]] is an obligate intracellular gram-negative bacterium. Serotypes A–C cause [[trachoma]] (the leading infectious cause of blindness worldwide), while serotypes D–K cause [[inclusion conjunctivitis]] (a sexually transmitted infection). The organism infects conjunctival epithelial cells, forming characteristic intracytoplasmic [[inclusion bodies]] (Halberstaedter-Prowazek bodies). The host immune response, particularly repeated cycles of infection and [[cell-mediated immunity]], drives progressive conjunctival scarring in trachoma through [[fibroblast]] activation and [[collagen]] deposition, ultimately leading to [[entropion]], [[trichiasis]], and [[corneal opacification]].

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### General Symptoms Common to Most Forms

- **Conjunctival hyperemia** (injection): Dilation of conjunctival blood vessels, most prominent in the [[fornix|fornices]] and diminishing toward the [[limbus]] (distinguishing it from [[ciliary flush]] seen in [[anterior uveitis]] and [[acute angle-closure glaucoma]])
- **Discharge**: Character varies by etiology (watery, mucoid, mucopurulent, or purulent)
- **Foreign body sensation** or grittiness
- **Tearing** ([[epiphora]])
- **Eyelid edema** and erythema
- **Morning crusting** of the eyelids

### Differentiating Features by Etiology

| **Feature** | **Viral** | **Bacterial** | **Allergic** | **Chlamydial** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Onset** | Acute, often unilateral initially then bilateral | Acute, often unilateral or bilateral | Bilateral, often seasonal | Subacute to chronic, often unilateral initially |
| **Discharge** | Watery/serous | Mucopurulent to purulent | Watery, stringy/ropy mucus | Mucopurulent |
| **Itching** | Minimal | Minimal | **Prominent** (hallmark symptom) | Minimal |
| **Pain** | Mild discomfort, foreign body sensation | Mild to moderate | Minimal (burning/stinging) | Mild |
| **Conjunctival reaction** | Follicular (inferior fornix) | Papillary (tarsal conjunctiva) | Papillary (can be giant papillae in VKC) | Follicular (especially inferior fornix) with superior tarsal pannus in trachoma |
| **Preauricular lymphadenopathy** | **Present** (hallmark) | Usually absent (except hyperacute) | Absent | **Present** |
| **Laterality** | Starts unilateral, becomes bilateral in 1–3 days | Unilateral or bilateral | Bilateral | Unilateral or bilateral |
| **Associated findings** | URI symptoms, pharyngitis, fever (PCF); corneal SEIs (EKC) | Lid matting, collarettes on lashes | [[Chemosis]], lid edema, [[Dennie-Morgan folds]], concurrent [[allergic rhinitis]], [[atopic dermatitis]] | Superior tarsal pannus, [[Herbert's pits]] (trachoma), concurrent urethritis/cervicitis (inclusion conjunctivitis) |
| **Duration** | 1–3 weeks (may be longer with EKC) | 7–10 days (self-limiting); hyperacute is rapidly progressive | Chronic/recurrent | Weeks to months if untreated |

### Specific Clinical Entities

#### Epidemic Keratoconjunctivitis (EKC)
Caused by adenovirus serotypes 8, 19, 37, 53, 54, and 56. Presents with severe watery discharge, marked conjunctival injection, [[chemosis]], lid edema, petechial subconjunctival hemorrhages, pseudomembranes/membranes, and preauricular lymphadenopathy. Corneal involvement includes diffuse punctate epithelial keratitis (days 7–10), focal epithelial keratitis, and subepithelial infiltrates (SEIs) appearing 2–3 weeks after onset. SEIs may persist for months to years and cause glare, photophobia, and reduced [[visual acuity]].

#### Hyperacute Bacterial Conjunctivitis
Caused primarily by [[Neisseria gonorrhoeae]]. Characterized by explosive onset (within 12–24 hours), copious purulent discharge that reaccumulates rapidly after wiping, severe lid edema, marked conjunctival injection and chemosis, and preauricular lymphadenopathy. This is an **ophthalmic emergency** due to the risk of rapid [[corneal perforation]].

#### Ophthalmia Neonatorum
Conjunctivitis occurring within the first 28 days of life. Etiologies include chemical (from prophylactic agents, onset day 1), gonococcal (onset days 2–5), chlamydial (onset days 5–14), and other bacterial or viral causes. Gonococcal ophthalmia neonatorum can cause rapid corneal destruction and requires emergent systemic treatment.

#### Trachoma
Caused by [[Chlamydia trachomatis]] serotypes A–C. The WHO uses the simplified grading system:
- **TF (Trachomatous inflammation—Follicular)**: ≥5 follicles (≥0.5 mm) on the upper tarsal conjunctiva
- **TI (Trachomatous inflammation—Intense)**: Pronounced inflammatory thickening obscuring >50% of deep tarsal vessels
- **TS (Trachomatous scarring)**: Scarring of the tarsal conjunctiva (Arlt's lines)
- **TT (Trachomatous trichiasis)**: ≥1 eyelash touching the globe
- **CO (Corneal opacity)**: Corneal opacity involving the pupillary margin

The WHO SAFE strategy (Surgery, Antibiotics, Facial cleanliness, Environmental improvement) is the cornerstone of trachoma elimination programs.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical History

A thorough history is paramount and should include:
- Onset, duration, and progression of symptoms
- Laterality (unilateral vs. bilateral)
- Character and quantity of discharge
- Presence and severity of itching (strongly suggests allergic etiology)
- Associated symptoms (URI, sore throat, fever, skin rash, genital discharge)
- Contact with infected individuals
- Contact lens wear history
- Medication use (topical and systemic)
- Allergy history and atopic status
- Sexual history (when chlamydial or gonococcal infection is suspected)
- Travel history (trachoma-endemic regions)
- Occupational and environmental exposures

### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

[[Slit-lamp examination]] is the cornerstone of clinical diagnosis and should include:

- **Eyelids and lashes**: Edema, erythema, crusting, collarettes, [[molluscum contagiosum]] lesions, [[blepharitis]]
- **Conjunctiva**: Pattern and degree of injection, follicles vs. papillae, membranes/pseudomembranes, chemosis, subconjunctival hemorrhage, scarring, symblepharon, concretions
- **Cornea**: [[Punctate epithelial erosions]] (PEE), [[punctate epithelial keratitis]] (PEK), subepithelial infiltrates, [[pannus]], [[marginal infiltrates]], [[shield ulcer]], [[corneal ulcer]]
- **Anterior chamber**: Assessed to rule out [[anterior uveitis]] (cells and flare should be absent in uncomplicated conjunctivitis)
- **[[Intraocular pressure]]**: Should be measured to exclude [[acute angle-closure glaucoma]] in the differential diagnosis of a red eye

### Eversion of the Upper Eyelid

Essential for evaluating the superior tarsal conjunctiva for papillae (including giant papillae in VKC and GPC), follicles, scarring, membranes, and foreign bodies.

### Laboratory Investigations

Laboratory testing is not routinely required for typical acute conjunctivitis but is indicated in the following scenarios:

| **Indication** | **Recommended Tests** |
|---|---|
| Hyperacute conjunctivitis (suspected gonococcal) | Gram stain (gram-negative intracellular diplococci), conjunctival culture on [[Thayer-Martin medium]] and [[chocolate agar]], [[nucleic acid amplification test]] (NAAT) |
| Suspected chlamydial conjunctivitis | NAAT (most sensitive), [[direct fluorescent antibody]] (DFA) test, [[Giemsa stain]] (intracytoplasmic inclusion bodies) |
| Ophthalmia neonatorum | Gram stain, culture, NAAT for *N. gonorrhoeae* and *C. trachomatis* |
| Chronic or recurrent conjunctivitis unresponsive to treatment | Conjunctival culture and sensitivity, conjunctival biopsy |
| Suspected membranous conjunctivitis | Culture, biopsy if indicated |
| Suspected autoimmune/cicatrizing conjunctivitis | Conjunctival biopsy with [[direct immunofluorescence]] (linear IgG, IgA, or C3 deposition at the [[basement membrane zone]] in OCP) |

### Point-of-Care Testing

The **AdenoPlus** (now RPS Adeno Detector Plus) is an in-office immunoassay that detects adenoviral hexon protein in tear samples with a sensitivity of approximately 85–90% and specificity of approximately 96%. A positive result confirms adenoviral etiology and can reduce unnecessary antibiotic prescribing.

### Differential Diagnosis

It is critical to differentiate conjunctivitis from other causes of a red eye that may be sight-threatening:

| **Condition** | **Key Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| [[Anterior uveitis]] (iritis) | Ciliary flush, photophobia, pain, miosis, anterior chamber cells and flare, reduced vision |
| [[Acute angle-closure glaucoma]] | Severe pain, nausea/vomiting, halos around lights, corneal edema, mid-dilated fixed pupil, elevated [[IOP]] |
| [[Keratitis]] (infectious) | Pain, photophobia, corneal infiltrate/ulcer, reduced vision, ciliary flush |
| [[Episcleritis]]/[[Scleritis]] | Sectoral or diffuse redness, tenderness (scleritis), deep boring pain (scleritis), non-blanching vessels (scleritis) |
| [[Subconjunctival hemorrhage]] | Bright red, well-demarcated, painless, no discharge |
| [[Dry eye disease]] | Chronic, bilateral, burning, fluctuating vision, reduced [[tear break-up time]], corneal/conjunctival staining |
| [[Blepharitis]] | Lid margin inflammation, collarettes, meibomian gland dysfunction, may coexist with conjunctivitis |
| [[Nasolacrimal duct obstruction]] | Unilateral tearing, discharge expressible from [[punctum]] with pressure over [[lacrimal sac]] |

## Management and Treatment

### General Principles

- **Hygiene measures**: Frequent handwashing, avoidance of touching the eyes, separate towels and pillowcases, disinfection of surfaces. Critical for preventing transmission of infectious conjunctivitis, particularly adenoviral.
- **Contact lens discontinuation**: All contact lens wear should be ceased during active conjunctivitis. Lenses and cases should be discarded and replaced after resolution.
- **Cool compresses**: Provide symptomatic relief, particularly in allergic and viral conjunctivitis.
- **Artificial tears/lubricants**: [[Preservative-free artificial tears]] help dilute allergens and infectious agents, soothe the ocular surface, and supplement the [[tear film]].

### Viral Conjunctivitis

Treatment is primarily **supportive**, as the condition is self-limiting (typically resolving within 2–3 weeks):

- **Cool compresses** applied several times daily
- **Preservative-free artificial tears** (e.g., [[carboxymethylcellulose]], [[hyaluronic acid]]-based drops) for comfort
- **Topical vasoconstrictors/antihistamines** (e.g., [[naphazoline]]/[[pheniramine]]) for symptomatic relief (short-term use only)
- **Topical [[povidone-iodine]]** (0.4–1.0%): Emerging evidence suggests efficacy in reducing viral load and duration of symptoms; currently under investigation in clinical trials
- **Topical corticosteroids** (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate]] 0.5–1%, [[loteprednol etabonate]] 0.5%, [[fluorometholone]] 0.1%): Reserved for severe cases with pseudomembranes, significant SEIs affecting vision, or marked chemosis. Must be used judiciously due to risks of elevated [[IOP]], [[posterior subcapsular cataract]], and secondary infection. Should be tapered slowly to prevent rebound inflammation.
- **Membrane/pseudomembrane removal**: Performed at the slit lamp using forceps or a cotton-tipped applicator to prevent [[symblepharon]] formation in severe membranous conjunctivitis. May need to be repeated.

**For HSV conjunctivitis**: Topical [[ganciclovir]] 0.15% gel or oral [[acyclovir]]/[[valacyclovir]] is indicated. Topical corticosteroids are **contraindicated** without concurrent antiviral coverage due to the risk of exacerbating [[herpes simplex keratitis]].

### Bacterial Conjunctivitis

Most cases of acute bacterial conjunctivitis are self-limiting (resolving within 7–10 days without treatment), but topical antibiotics hasten resolution, reduce transmission, and decrease the risk of complications:

| **Severity** | **Recommended Treatment** | **Duration** |
|---|---|---|
| Mild to moderate | [[Erythromycin]] 0.5% ointment QID, or [[polymyxin B]]/[[trimethoprim]] drops QID, or [[azithromycin]] 1% drops BID × 2 days then QD × 5 days | 5–7 days |
| Moderate (alternative) | [[Fluoroquinolone]] drops (e.g., [[moxifloxacin]] 0.5%, [[besifloxacin]] 0.6%, [[levofloxacin]] 0.5%) QID | 5–7 days |
| Severe/purulent | Fluoroquinolone drops q2h while awake initially, then taper | 7–10 days |
| **Hyperacute (gonococcal)** | **Systemic**: [[Ceftriaxone]] 1g IM single dose + [[azithromycin]] 1g PO single dose (to cover concurrent chlamydial co-infection). **Topical**: Saline irrigation to remove discharge; topical fluoroquinolone as adjunct. **Urgent ophthalmology referral.** | As directed |
| **Gonococcal ophthalmia neonatorum** | [[Ceftriaxone]] 25–50 mg/kg IV/IM (max 125 mg) single dose + saline irrigation. Hospitalization required. | As directed |

**Prophylaxis for ophthalmia neonatorum**: [[Erythromycin]] 0.5% ophthalmic ointment applied to both eyes within 1 hour of birth (Credé prophylaxis historically used silver nitrate 1%, now largely replaced).

### Chlamydial Conjunctivitis

Systemic treatment is mandatory due to the concurrent genital infection:

- **Adults (inclusion conjunctivitis)**: [[Azithromycin]] 1g PO single dose (preferred) or [[doxycycline]] 100 mg PO BID × 7 days. Sexual partners must be treated concurrently.
- **Neonates (chlamydial ophthalmia neonatorum)**: [[Erythromycin]] 12.5 mg/kg PO QID × 14 days (topical treatment alone is insufficient and does not eradicate nasopharyngeal carriage, risking [[chlamydial pneumonia]]).
- **Trachoma**: Azithromycin 20 mg/kg PO single dose (community-based mass drug administration as part of the WHO SAFE strategy); tetracycline 1% eye ointment BID × 6 weeks as an alternative.

### Allergic Conjunctivitis

Management follows a stepwise approach:

**Step 1: Allergen Avoidance and Non-Pharmacological Measures**
- Identification and avoidance of triggers
- Cool compresses
- Preservative-free artificial tears (dilute and wash out allergens)

**Step 2: Topical Antihistamines and Mast Cell Stabilizers**

| **Drug Class** | **Examples** | **Mechanism** | **Dosing** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dual-action antihistamine/mast cell stabilizer (first-line) | [[Olopatadine]] 0.1%/0.2%/0.7%, [[ketotifen]] 0.025%, [[alcaftadine]] 0.25%, [[bepotastine]] 1.5%, [[epinastine]] 0.05% | H1-receptor antagonism + mast cell stabilization | QD to BID |
| Pure mast cell stabilizer | [[Cromolyn sodium]] 4%, [[lodoxamide]] 0.1%, [[nedocromil]] 2% | Prevent mast cell degranulation | QID (require loading period of 1–2 weeks) |
| Pure antihistamine | [[Emedastine]] 0.05%, [[levocabastine]] 0.05% | H1-receptor antagonism | BID to QID |

**Step 3: Topical NSAIDs**
- [[Ketorolac]] 0.5% QID: Reduces itching and conjunctival hyperemia. Use with caution due to potential for corneal melting in predisposed patients.

**Step 4: Topical Corticosteroids**
- Reserved for moderate to severe flares unresponsive to first-line therapy
- [[Loteprednol etabonate]] 0.2% or 0.5% (preferred due to lower risk of IOP elevation—"soft steroid")
- [[Prednisolone acetate]] 1%, [[fluorometholone]] 0.1% for more severe cases
- Short-term "pulse" therapy with close IOP monitoring

**Step 5: Systemic and Advanced Therapies (for VKC and AKC)**
- Oral [[antihistamines]] (e.g., [[cetirizine]], [[loratadine]], [[fexofenadine]])
- Topical [[cyclosporine A]] 0.05–2% (off-label or compounded): Inhibits T-cell activation; effective for steroid-sparing management of VKC and AKC
- Topical [[tacrolimus]] 0.03–0.1% ointment (off-label): Calcineurin inhibitor; highly effective for severe VKC and AKC
- [[Allergen immunotherapy]] (subcutaneous or sublingual): For patients with confirmed IgE-mediated disease refractory to pharmacotherapy
- Supratarsal injection of [[corticosteroids]] (e.g., [[triamcinolone acetonide]]): For severe VKC with giant papillae unresponsive to topical therapy

### Giant Papillary Conjunctivitis (GPC)

- Discontinuation or modification of the offending stimulus (contact lens, ocular prosthesis, exposed suture)
- Switch to daily disposable contact lenses or reduce wearing time
- Topical mast cell stabilizer/antihistamine combination drops
- Topical corticosteroids for acute flares
- Lens material and care solution modification

### Toxic/Medicamentosa Conjunctivitis

- Identification and discontinuation of the offending agent (common culprits include [[benzalkonium chloride]] (BAK)-preserved drops, [[brimonidine]], [[aminoglycosides]], antivirals)
- Switch to preservative-free formulations
- Preservative-free artificial tears
- Short course of mild topical corticosteroid if significant inflammation

## Prognosis

The prognosis for most forms of acute conjunctivitis is excellent, with complete resolution expected within 1–3 weeks. Viral conjunctivitis caused by adenovirus may have a prolonged course (2–4 weeks), and SEIs may persist for months to years, occasionally requiring topical corticosteroid therapy. Bacterial conjunctivitis typically resolves within 5–7 days with appropriate antibiotic therapy. Allergic conjunctivitis tends to be chronic and recurrent but is generally well-controlled with appropriate pharmacotherapy and allergen avoidance.

Sight-threatening complications are uncommon but can occur with hyperacute gonococcal conjunctivitis (corneal perforation), severe trachoma (corneal opacification from trichiasis), and severe VKC/AKC (shield ulcers, corneal scarring). Cicatrizing forms of conjunctivitis (OCP, SJS/TEN) carry a guarded prognosis and require long-term multidisciplinary management.

## Prevention

- **Hand hygiene**: The single most effective measure for preventing transmission of infectious conjunctivitis
- **Infection control in healthcare settings**: Disinfection of ophthalmic instruments ([[tonometer]] tips, trial lenses, slit-lamp chin rests) with appropriate agents (70% isopropyl alcohol, 1:10 sodium hypochlorite solution, or hydrogen peroxide-based disinfectants for adenovirus)
- **Neonatal prophylaxis**: Erythromycin 0.5% ointment within 1 hour of birth
- **Contact lens hygiene**: Proper lens care, hand hygiene before lens handling, avoidance of water exposure with lenses, adherence to replacement schedules
- **Trachoma elimination**: WHO SAFE strategy (Surgery for trichiasis, Antibiotics, Facial cleanliness, Environmental improvement)
- **Allergen avoidance**: Environmental controls for dust mites, pollen, animal dander; use of air purifiers and allergen-proof bedding covers

## Special Considerations

### Conjunctivitis and COVID-19

[[SARS-CoV-2]] can cause a follicular conjunctivitis, reported in approximately 1–3% of confirmed COVID-19 cases. The virus has been detected in tear fluid and conjunctival secretions via RT-PCR, raising concerns about ocular transmission. Conjunctivitis may rarely be the presenting or sole manifestation of COVID-19. Eye care practitioners should maintain appropriate personal protective equipment (PPE) and infection control measures.

### Conjunctivitis in Contact Lens Wearers

Contact lens wearers presenting with conjunctivitis require special attention due to the increased risk of [[microbial keratitis]]. All contact lens wear should be discontinued immediately. The clinician must carefully examine the [[cornea]] for infiltrates or ulceration. [[Giant papillary conjunctivitis]] is a common contact lens-related condition caused by mechanical irritation and immunological response to lens deposits. Management includes lens discontinuation, switching to daily disposable lenses, and topical mast cell stabilizer/antihistamine therapy.

### Conjunctivitis in Immunocompromised Patients

Immunocompromised patients (e.g., those with [[HIV/AIDS]], organ transplant recipients, patients on [[immunosuppressive therapy]]) may present with atypical or severe forms of conjunctivitis, including chronic or recurrent HSV/VZV conjunctivitis, fungal conjunctivitis, and [[microsporidial keratoconjunctivitis]]. A lower threshold for laboratory investigation and specialist referral is warranted.
