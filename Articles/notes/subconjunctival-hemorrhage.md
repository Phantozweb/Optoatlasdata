

# Subconjunctival Hemorrhage

---

**Subconjunctival hemorrhage** (SCH), also referred to as **hyposphagma**, is a common, typically benign ocular condition characterized by the extravasation of blood from ruptured small [[blood vessels]] — specifically [[conjunctival]] or [[episcleral]] vessels — into the potential space between the [[bulbar conjunctiva]] and the underlying [[Tenon's capsule]] (episclera). Clinically, it presents as a sharply demarcated, flat, bright-red or dark-red patch on the [[sclera]] that is visible through the transparent conjunctival tissue. Although the dramatic appearance of a subconjunctival hemorrhage frequently causes significant patient alarm, the condition is overwhelmingly self-limiting and resolves without intervention in the vast majority of cases. Nonetheless, recurrent or spontaneous episodes warrant a thorough systemic and ocular workup to exclude underlying coagulopathies, [[hypertension]], or other serious etiologies.

---

## Epidemiology

Subconjunctival hemorrhage is one of the most frequently encountered ocular conditions in both primary eye care and emergency department settings. Key epidemiological features include:

- **Prevalence:** SCH has been reported in approximately 2.9% of all ophthalmic emergency visits. Population-based studies, including the Beijing Eye Study, have reported a prevalence of approximately 2.9% in adults over 40 years of age.
- **Age distribution:** While SCH can occur at any age — including neonates following vaginal delivery — the incidence increases significantly with advancing age, particularly in individuals over 50 years. This is attributed to age-related vascular fragility, increased prevalence of [[systemic hypertension]], and greater use of [[anticoagulant]] and [[antiplatelet]] medications.
- **Sex predilection:** Most large-scale studies report no significant sex predilection, though some data suggest a slight male predominance, potentially related to higher rates of trauma and [[Valsalva maneuver]]-related activities.
- **Laterality:** SCH is typically unilateral, though bilateral presentations can occur, particularly in the setting of coagulopathy, severe [[conjunctivitis]], or traumatic injury.
- **Seasonal variation:** Some studies have noted a slight increase in SCH during colder months, potentially related to increased blood pressure variability and upper respiratory infections causing coughing and sneezing.

## Anatomy and Pathophysiology

### Relevant Anatomy

A thorough understanding of the anatomy of the [[conjunctiva]] and its vascular supply is essential for comprehending the pathophysiology of subconjunctival hemorrhage.

The **[[bulbar conjunctiva]]** is a thin, transparent mucous membrane that covers the anterior surface of the [[sclera]] from the [[limbus]] to the [[fornix]]. It is composed of a non-keratinized stratified columnar [[epithelium]] with interspersed [[goblet cells]], overlying a loose, highly vascularized connective tissue stroma known as the **substantia propria**. Beneath the conjunctiva lies **[[Tenon's capsule]]** (the episcleral fascia), a thin fibrous layer that separates the conjunctiva from the sclera.

The vascular supply to the bulbar conjunctiva is derived from two primary arterial systems:

| **Arterial System** | **Origin** | **Region Supplied** |
|---|---|---|
| **Posterior conjunctival arteries** | Branches of the [[peripheral arterial arcade]] from the [[ophthalmic artery]] via the palpebral arteries | Fornix and posterior bulbar conjunctiva |
| **Anterior conjunctival arteries** | Branches of the [[anterior ciliary arteries]] | Limbal and perilimbal conjunctiva |

These arteries form an extensive anastomotic network within the conjunctival stroma. The corresponding venous drainage follows a parallel course, with posterior conjunctival veins draining into the palpebral and orbital veins, and anterior conjunctival veins draining into the [[episcleral veins]] and ultimately the [[vortex veins]] or [[anterior ciliary veins]].

The conjunctival vessels are thin-walled, lack significant muscular support, and are relatively superficial. These anatomical characteristics render them particularly susceptible to rupture from even minor mechanical or hemodynamic stress.

### Pathophysiology

The fundamental pathophysiological mechanism of subconjunctival hemorrhage involves the **rupture of small conjunctival or episcleral blood vessels** with subsequent hemorrhage into the subconjunctival space — the loose areolar tissue plane between the conjunctival epithelium and Tenon's capsule.

Several pathophysiological mechanisms can lead to vessel rupture:

1. **Mechanical disruption:** Direct trauma, surgical manipulation, or vigorous eye rubbing can physically disrupt vessel walls.
2. **Increased intravascular pressure:** Sudden elevations in venous pressure — as occurs during the [[Valsalva maneuver]] (coughing, sneezing, straining, vomiting, heavy lifting) — can cause transmural pressure to exceed the tensile strength of the vessel wall, leading to rupture.
3. **Vascular wall fragility:** Age-related degeneration, [[arteriosclerosis]], [[diabetes mellitus]], and [[hypertensive vasculopathy]] weaken vessel walls, predisposing them to spontaneous rupture.
4. **Coagulation defects:** Disorders of [[primary hemostasis]] (platelet dysfunction, [[thrombocytopenia]]) or [[secondary hemostasis]] (coagulation factor deficiencies), as well as pharmacological anticoagulation (e.g., [[warfarin]], [[heparin]], [[direct oral anticoagulants]]) and antiplatelet therapy (e.g., [[aspirin]], [[clopidogrel]]), impair the normal hemostatic response, allowing even minor vessel disruptions to produce clinically significant hemorrhage.
5. **Inflammatory vasculitis:** Infectious or non-infectious [[conjunctivitis]], [[episcleritis]], or systemic vasculitides can compromise vascular integrity.

Once blood extravasates into the subconjunctival space, it spreads freely within the loose connective tissue, limited only by the firm attachments of the conjunctiva at the [[limbus]] and the [[fornices]]. The blood does not cross the limbus because of the tight adherence of the conjunctiva to the underlying [[corneal limbus]]. Over time, the extravasated blood undergoes a characteristic color evolution — from bright red (oxyhemoglobin) to dark red/purple, then brown/yellow (biliverdin, bilirubin) — as hemoglobin is progressively degraded by tissue macrophages. Complete resorption typically occurs within **1 to 3 weeks**, depending on the volume of hemorrhage.

## Etiology and Risk Factors

The etiology of subconjunctival hemorrhage is diverse and can be broadly categorized as follows:

### Idiopathic / Spontaneous

In the majority of cases (up to **50–70%**), no identifiable cause is found, and the hemorrhage is classified as idiopathic. Many of these cases are presumed to result from minor, unrecognized trauma or transient increases in venous pressure.

### Traumatic Causes

| **Type of Trauma** | **Examples** |
|---|---|
| **Blunt ocular trauma** | Sports injuries, falls, assault, airbag deployment |
| **Penetrating/perforating injury** | Must be excluded in all traumatic SCH presentations |
| **Iatrogenic/surgical** | Post-[[cataract surgery]], [[strabismus surgery]], [[refractive surgery]] (e.g., [[LASIK]]), [[intravitreal injection]], [[subconjunctival injection]], [[trabeculectomy]] |
| **Minor mechanical** | Vigorous eye rubbing, contact lens insertion/removal |
| **Birth trauma** | Vaginal delivery, forceps-assisted delivery |

### Valsalva-Related Causes

Any activity that produces a sudden increase in intrathoracic or intra-abdominal pressure can precipitate SCH:

- Severe coughing (e.g., [[pertussis]], [[COVID-19]])
- Forceful sneezing
- Straining during defecation ([[constipation]])
- Heavy weightlifting or strenuous exercise
- Vomiting or retching
- Labor and delivery (maternal)

### Systemic and Vascular Causes

- **[[Systemic hypertension]]** — The most commonly identified systemic association. Chronic hypertension causes arteriolar wall damage and increased vascular fragility. Acute hypertensive crises can precipitate SCH.
- **[[Diabetes mellitus]]** — Diabetic microangiopathy affects conjunctival vessels, predisposing to hemorrhage.
- **[[Arteriosclerosis]]** — Age-related vascular stiffening and fragility.
- **Bleeding disorders** — [[Hemophilia]], [[von Willebrand disease]], [[thrombocytopenia]], [[disseminated intravascular coagulation]] (DIC), [[liver disease]] with coagulopathy.
- **Pharmacological anticoagulation** — [[Warfarin]], [[enoxaparin]], [[rivaroxaban]], [[apixaban]], [[dabigatran]], [[aspirin]], [[clopidogrel]], [[NSAIDs]], and herbal supplements (e.g., [[ginkgo biloba]], [[garlic]], [[ginseng]]).

### Infectious and Inflammatory Causes

- **Acute hemorrhagic [[conjunctivitis]]** — Caused by [[Enterovirus 70]] or [[Coxsackievirus A24]], characterized by sudden onset of bilateral SCH with conjunctival injection and discharge.
- **Severe bacterial or viral conjunctivitis**
- **[[Leptospirosis]]** — Systemic infection with ocular manifestations including SCH.
- **[[Episcleritis]]** or [[scleritis]] — Inflammatory conditions that may be associated with localized hemorrhage.

### Other Associations

- **[[Amyloidosis]]** — Conjunctival amyloid deposits can cause recurrent SCH due to vascular fragility.
- **[[Sickle cell disease]]** — Vaso-occlusive events can affect conjunctival vasculature.
- **Acute febrile illnesses** — Including [[malaria]], [[dengue fever]], and [[rickettsial infections]].
- **Post-procedural** — Following [[colonoscopy]], [[endoscopy]], or any procedure requiring the Valsalva maneuver.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

Subconjunctival hemorrhage is most commonly **asymptomatic** or associated with only minimal symptoms. Patients typically present with:

- **Cosmetic concern:** The most common reason for presentation. Patients or their family members notice a bright red area on the white of the eye, often causing significant anxiety.
- **Mild foreign body sensation:** A slight gritty or scratchy feeling may be reported, particularly with larger hemorrhages that cause mild elevation of the conjunctival surface.
- **Mild tearing:** Reflex [[epiphora]] may occur secondary to surface irritation.
- **Absence of pain:** True pain is not a feature of uncomplicated SCH. The presence of significant pain should prompt investigation for alternative or concurrent diagnoses such as [[scleritis]], [[acute angle-closure glaucoma]], [[uveitis]], or occult globe rupture.
- **Absence of visual disturbance:** [[Visual acuity]] is unaffected in uncomplicated SCH. Any reduction in vision mandates further investigation.

### Signs

On [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] and external examination, the following signs are characteristic:

| **Clinical Feature** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Appearance** | Flat, sharply demarcated, homogeneous bright-red to dark-red patch on the bulbar conjunctiva |
| **Location** | Can occur in any quadrant; temporal and inferior locations are most common |
| **Extent** | Ranges from a small, focal petechial hemorrhage to a diffuse, 360-degree hemorrhage covering the entire visible sclera |
| **Borders** | Well-defined margins; does not cross the limbus onto the [[cornea]] |
| **Conjunctival vessels** | Obscured by the hemorrhage; underlying scleral and episcleral vessels are not visible through the blood |
| **Mobility** | The hemorrhage moves with the conjunctiva during eye movement |
| **Pupil** | Normal, round, and reactive |
| **Anterior chamber** | Clear and deep (no [[hyphema]]) |
| **Intraocular pressure** | Normal |
| **Fundus** | Normal (unless concurrent posterior segment pathology exists) |

### Natural History and Color Evolution

The hemorrhage undergoes a predictable color change as hemoglobin is metabolized:

1. **Day 0–2:** Bright red (oxyhemoglobin)
2. **Day 3–7:** Dark red to purple (deoxyhemoglobin)
3. **Day 7–14:** Brown to greenish-yellow (biliverdin, bilirubin)
4. **Day 14–21:** Yellow, then complete resolution

Larger hemorrhages may take up to **3–4 weeks** for full resorption. The hemorrhage may appear to spread or enlarge slightly in the first 24–48 hours due to continued redistribution of blood within the subconjunctival space before hemostasis is fully achieved.

## Differential Diagnosis

While the diagnosis of subconjunctival hemorrhage is usually straightforward, several conditions must be considered in the differential diagnosis:

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| **[[Conjunctival injection]] (hyperemia)** | Dilated, visible conjunctival vessels; blanches with topical [[phenylephrine]] 2.5%; vessels are individually discernible |
| **[[Episcleritis]]** | Sectoral or diffuse redness with visible episcleral vessels; tender to palpation; blanches with phenylephrine |
| **[[Scleritis]]** | Deep, violaceous hue; severe boring pain; scleral edema; does not blanch with phenylephrine |
| **[[Kaposi sarcoma]]** | Violaceous, raised subconjunctival lesion; associated with [[HIV/AIDS]] |
| **[[Conjunctival lymphoma]]** | "Salmon-patch" lesion; smooth, mobile, fleshy mass |
| **[[Conjunctival amyloidosis]]** | Waxy, yellowish subconjunctival deposits; may cause recurrent SCH |
| **[[Hyphema]]** | Blood in the [[anterior chamber]], not subconjunctival; layered blood visible on slit-lamp examination |
| **[[Pterygium]] or [[pinguecula]]** | Fibrovascular or degenerative conjunctival lesion; may have associated hyperemia but not hemorrhage |
| **Occult [[globe rupture]]** | Must be excluded in all traumatic SCH; 360-degree or bullous hemorrhage, reduced visual acuity, low IOP, shallow anterior chamber, restricted motility |

### Critical Red Flags Requiring Urgent Evaluation

The following features in the setting of SCH should raise immediate concern and prompt urgent referral or further investigation:

- **History of significant trauma** — Exclude open globe injury, orbital fracture
- **360-degree (circumferential) hemorrhage** — Suggestive of occult globe rupture or orbital/basilar skull fracture
- **Bullous, elevated hemorrhage** — May indicate significant underlying bleeding or globe perforation
- **Reduced visual acuity** — Suggests concurrent intraocular pathology
- **Proptosis or restricted extraocular motility** — Suggests retrobulbar hemorrhage or orbital pathology
- **Hypotony (low [[intraocular pressure]])** — Suggestive of globe perforation
- **Recurrent episodes** — Warrants systemic workup for coagulopathy, hypertension, or vascular disease
- **Bilateral presentation in a child** — Consider [[non-accidental injury]] (child abuse)

## Diagnostic Evaluation

### Clinical Diagnosis

The diagnosis of subconjunctival hemorrhage is primarily **clinical**, based on the characteristic appearance on external examination and [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]. No specific ophthalmic imaging or laboratory testing is required for an isolated, first-episode, uncomplicated SCH in an otherwise healthy patient.

### Ophthalmic Examination

A comprehensive ophthalmic examination should be performed to exclude concurrent ocular pathology, particularly in traumatic cases:

1. **[[Visual acuity]]** — Should be normal; any decrease warrants further investigation.
2. **External examination** — Assess for periorbital ecchymosis, lid lacerations, proptosis, or signs of orbital fracture.
3. **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]** — Evaluate the conjunctiva, cornea (for abrasions, lacerations, or foreign bodies), anterior chamber (for [[hyphema]], [[cell and flare]]), [[iris]] (for sphincter tears, iridodialysis), and [[crystalline lens]] (for subluxation or cataract).
4. **[[Intraocular pressure]] (IOP)** — Measure with [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]] or non-contact tonometry. Hypotony suggests globe rupture; elevated IOP may indicate [[traumatic glaucoma]] or [[hyphema]].
5. **[[Gonioscopy]]** — If trauma is suspected, to evaluate for [[angle recession]].
6. **[[Dilated fundus examination]]** — To exclude [[vitreous hemorrhage]], [[retinal detachment]], [[commotio retinae]], or [[choroidal rupture]] in traumatic cases.
7. **[[Seidel test]]** — If penetrating injury is suspected, apply [[fluorescein]] dye and observe for streaming of aqueous humor through a wound.
8. **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** — If fundus view is obscured or if posterior segment pathology is suspected.

### Systemic Workup

A systemic workup is indicated in the following scenarios:

- **Recurrent SCH** (≥2 episodes within 6–12 months)
- **Spontaneous SCH without identifiable precipitant**
- **Known or suspected bleeding disorder**
- **Patient on anticoagulant or antiplatelet therapy**
- **Bilateral SCH without trauma**
- **Associated systemic symptoms** (e.g., easy bruising, epistaxis, gingival bleeding, menorrhagia)

Recommended investigations include:

| **Investigation** | **Purpose** |
|---|---|
| **Blood pressure measurement** | Screen for [[systemic hypertension]] |
| **Complete blood count (CBC)** | Evaluate for [[thrombocytopenia]], [[leukemia]], [[polycythemia]] |
| **Prothrombin time (PT) / INR** | Assess extrinsic coagulation pathway; monitor [[warfarin]] therapy |
| **Activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT)** | Assess intrinsic coagulation pathway |
| **Bleeding time / platelet function assay** | Evaluate [[primary hemostasis]] |
| **Fasting blood glucose / HbA1c** | Screen for [[diabetes mellitus]] |
| **Liver function tests** | Hepatic synthetic dysfunction causing coagulopathy |
| **Renal function tests** | [[Uremia]]-associated platelet dysfunction |
| **Lipid profile** | Assess cardiovascular risk factors |

## Management and Treatment

### General Principles

The management of uncomplicated subconjunctival hemorrhage is primarily **conservative and supportive**, as the condition is self-limiting. The cornerstone of management is **patient education and reassurance**.

### Patient Education and Reassurance

- **Explain the benign nature** of the condition and its expected natural history.
- **Reassure** the patient that the hemorrhage will resolve completely without permanent sequelae.
- **Advise** that the hemorrhage may appear to worsen (spread or darken) over the first 24–48 hours before gradually improving.
- **Provide a timeline** for resolution: typically 1–3 weeks, depending on the size of the hemorrhage.
- **Counsel** regarding the color changes that will occur during resolution (red → purple → brown → yellow → clear).

### Symptomatic Treatment

| **Symptom** | **Management** |
|---|---|
| **Foreign body sensation / mild irritation** | Preservative-free [[artificial tears]] (e.g., [[carboxymethylcellulose]] 0.5%, [[hyaluronic acid]] 0.1–0.2%) applied 4–6 times daily |
| **Cosmetic concern** | Reassurance; tinted spectacles if desired for social comfort |
| **Mild discomfort** | Cool compresses in the first 24–48 hours to promote vasoconstriction and limit hemorrhage expansion; warm compresses after 48 hours to promote resorption |

### Pharmacological Considerations

- **Avoid topical vasoconstrictors** (e.g., [[naphazoline]], [[tetrahydrozoline]]) — These are ineffective for subconjunctival hemorrhage and may cause rebound hyperemia.
- **Avoid aspirin and NSAIDs** — If not medically necessary, advise temporary avoidance of medications that impair platelet function, as these may prolong hemorrhage duration or increase the risk of recurrence.
- **Do NOT discontinue prescribed anticoagulants** without consultation with the prescribing physician. The risk of thromboembolic events from discontinuation typically far outweighs the benign nature of SCH.
- **Topical antibiotics** — Not indicated for uncomplicated SCH. May be prescribed if concurrent [[corneal abrasion]] or [[conjunctivitis]] is present.
- **Topical corticosteroids** — Not indicated and should be avoided.

### Management of Underlying Causes

Addressing the underlying etiology is critical for preventing recurrence:

- **Hypertension:** Refer to primary care or cardiology for blood pressure optimization. Target blood pressure per current guidelines (e.g., <130/80 mmHg per ACC/AHA 2017 guidelines).
- **Coagulopathy:** Refer to hematology for evaluation and management of bleeding disorders. Adjust anticoagulant dosing if INR is supratherapeutic.
- **Diabetes mellitus:** Optimize glycemic control in coordination with the patient's endocrinologist or primary care provider.
- **Valsalva-related causes:** Treat underlying conditions (e.g., antitussives for chronic cough, stool softeners for constipation, lifestyle modification for heavy lifting).
- **Contact lens-related:** Review lens fit, insertion/removal technique, and wearing schedule.

### Surgical Intervention

Surgical intervention is **exceedingly rare** and is not indicated for uncomplicated subconjunctival hemorrhage. Surgical considerations arise only in the following exceptional circumstances:

- **Massive, non-resolving hemorrhage** with significant conjunctival chemosis causing [[exposure keratopathy]] — Conjunctival incision and drainage may be considered.
- **Underlying conjunctival or subconjunctival mass** (e.g., [[lymphoma]], [[amyloidosis]], [[pyogenic granuloma]]) — Excisional or incisional biopsy with histopathological examination.
- **Traumatic globe rupture** — Emergent surgical repair of the globe, with the SCH being a secondary finding.

## Special Populations

### Neonates and Infants

Subconjunctival hemorrhage is relatively common in neonates, occurring in approximately **2–5%** of vaginal deliveries and at higher rates with instrumental (forceps or vacuum-assisted) deliveries. Neonatal SCH is typically benign and resolves spontaneously within 1–2 weeks. However, bilateral or extensive SCH in a neonate should prompt consideration of:

- Birth trauma
- [[Neonatal coagulopathy]] (e.g., [[vitamin K deficiency bleeding]])
- [[Non-accidental injury]]

### Pediatric Patients

In children, SCH is most commonly traumatic. However, unexplained or recurrent SCH — particularly if bilateral — should raise suspicion for:

- **[[Non-accidental injury]] (child abuse)** — A critical consideration; appropriate safeguarding referrals must be made.
- **Bleeding disorders** (e.g., [[hemophilia]], [[idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura]])
- **[[Leukemia]]** or other hematologic malignancies

### Patients on Anticoagulant Therapy

SCH is more frequent and may be more extensive in patients on anticoagulant or antiplatelet therapy. Management considerations include:

- **Do not discontinue anticoagulation** for SCH alone.
- **Check INR** if on warfarin — supratherapeutic INR should be communicated to the prescribing physician.
- **Document** the hemorrhage and communicate with the patient's primary care provider or anticoagulation clinic.
- **Reassure** the patient that SCH does not indicate a dangerous level of anticoagulation in isolation.

### Post-Surgical Patients

SCH is an expected and common finding following many ocular surgeries, including:

- **[[Cataract surgery]]** — Particularly at the site of conjunctival peritomy or clear corneal incision
- **[[Strabismus surgery]]** — At the site of conjunctival incision and muscle manipulation
- **[[Glaucoma surgery]]** (e.g., [[trabeculectomy]], [[tube shunt]] implantation)
- **[[Intravitreal injections]]** — At the injection site
- **[[Pterygium excision]]**

Post-surgical SCH is managed expectantly and typically resolves within 2–3 weeks.

## Prognosis

The prognosis for subconjunctival hemorrhage is **excellent**. Key prognostic points include:

- **Complete resolution** occurs in virtually all cases without any residual scarring, pigmentation, or visual impairment.
- **Typical resolution time:** 7–21 days for most hemorrhages; up to 4 weeks for very large hemorrhages.
- **Recurrence:** Isolated episodes rarely recur. Recurrent SCH should prompt systemic investigation as outlined above.
- **Complications:** Exceedingly rare in uncomplicated cases. Potential complications of very large hemorrhages include transient [[dellen]] formation (localized corneal thinning due to adjacent conjunctival elevation) and, very rarely, subconjunctival fibrosis.

---

## Prevention

While many episodes of SCH are idiopathic and not preventable, the following measures may reduce the risk of occurrence or recurrence:

- **Blood pressure control** — Regular monitoring and pharmacological management of [[systemic hypertension]]
- **Anticoagulation monitoring** — Regular INR checks for patients on [[warfarin]]; adherence to prescribed dosing of [[DOACs]]
- **Protective eyewear** — Use of [[polycarbonate]] safety glasses or sports goggles during high-risk activities
- **Avoidance of eye rubbing** — Particularly in patients with [[allergic conjunctivitis]] or [[dry eye disease]]
- **Management of Valsalva triggers** — Treatment of chronic cough, constipation, and counseling regarding proper breathing techniques during exercise
- **Gentle contact lens handling** — Proper insertion and removal techniques for [[contact lens]] wearers
