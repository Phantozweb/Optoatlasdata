

# Near Point of Convergence

---

**Near Point of Convergence (NPC)** is a fundamental clinical measurement in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] that quantifies the closest point in space at which the two eyes can maintain [[binocular single vision]] through maximal convergence effort. It represents the nearest point at which the [[visual axes]] of both eyes intersect while sustaining [[fusion]] of a target into a single percept. NPC is one of the most critical assessments of the [[vergence system]], specifically the [[convergence]] mechanism, and serves as a primary diagnostic indicator for [[convergence insufficiency]] (CI) and other [[binocular vision]] disorders. The measurement is routinely performed during comprehensive eye examinations, pediatric vision screenings, and specialized [[binocular vision]] assessments.

A **normal NPC** is generally accepted as a break point of **≤5–6 cm** from the bridge of the nose (or the plane of the spectacles) with a recovery point of **≤7–8 cm**, although normative values vary slightly across published literature and patient populations. A **receded NPC**—typically defined as a break point **>6 cm** and often clinically significant when **>10 cm**—is a hallmark finding in [[convergence insufficiency]] and may also be associated with other neurological, systemic, or oculomotor conditions.

---

## History and Background

The clinical assessment of NPC has been a cornerstone of optometric examination since the early development of [[orthoptics]] and [[binocular vision]] science in the 19th and early 20th centuries. Pioneers such as **Ernest Edmund Maddox** and **Charles Sheard** contributed significantly to the understanding of [[vergence]] physiology and the standardization of clinical tests for convergence function. Maddox's classification of vergence components—[[tonic vergence]], [[accommodative vergence]], [[fusional vergence]], and [[proximal vergence]]—provided the theoretical framework within which NPC testing is understood.

The **Convergence Insufficiency Treatment Trial (CITT)**, a landmark series of multicenter, randomized clinical trials funded by the [[National Eye Institute]] (NEI), beginning in the early 2000s, significantly advanced the evidence base for NPC measurement, its clinical significance, and the efficacy of treatments for [[convergence insufficiency]]. The CITT studies established standardized protocols for NPC measurement and validated office-based [[vergence therapy]] as the gold-standard treatment for CI in both children and adults.

## Anatomy and Physiology of Convergence

### Relevant Ocular Anatomy

NPC measurement fundamentally assesses the function of the [[medial rectus muscles]] of both eyes, which are the primary effectors of [[convergence]]. The [[medial rectus]] is one of six [[extraocular muscles]] (EOMs) in each orbit, innervated by the inferior division of the [[oculomotor nerve]] (cranial nerve III, [[CN III]]). During convergence, both medial recti contract simultaneously in a coordinated, symmetrical fashion—a movement classified as a [[vergence]] eye movement, distinct from [[version]] (conjugate) movements.

Key anatomical structures involved in the convergence pathway include:

| Structure | Role in Convergence |
|---|---|
| [[Medial rectus muscle]] | Primary effector of adduction during convergence |
| [[Oculomotor nerve]] (CN III) | Motor innervation to the medial rectus |
| [[Oculomotor nucleus]] | Located in the [[midbrain]], provides lower motor neuron output |
| [[Vergence center]] (midbrain) | Supranuclear control center for vergence commands |
| [[Frontal eye fields]] (FEF) | Cortical input for voluntary convergence |
| [[Occipital cortex]] (V1/V2) | Processes [[retinal disparity]] cues driving fusional convergence |
| [[Cerebellum]] | Calibration and adaptive control of vergence gain |
| [[Ciliary muscle]] | Mediates [[accommodation]], which is neurologically linked to convergence via the [[near triad]] |
| [[Pupillary sphincter]] | Mediates [[miosis]], the third component of the [[near triad]] |

### The Near Triad

Convergence does not occur in isolation. It is one component of the **[[near triad]]** (also called the **near response** or **accommodation-convergence synkinesis**), which consists of:

1. **[[Convergence]]** – Simultaneous adduction of both eyes to direct the visual axes toward a near target.
2. **[[Accommodation]]** – Increase in [[crystalline lens]] curvature via [[ciliary muscle]] contraction to focus on the near target.
3. **[[Miosis]]** – Pupillary constriction to increase [[depth of focus]].

These three responses are neurologically linked through interconnections between the [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]], the [[oculomotor nucleus]], and the [[vergence center]] in the [[mesencephalic reticular formation]]. The relationship between accommodation and convergence is quantified by the **[[AC/A ratio]]** (accommodative convergence to accommodation ratio), which is a critical parameter in understanding NPC findings and managing binocular vision disorders.

### Components of Convergence

The total convergence demand at any given near fixation distance is met by the summation of four components, as originally described by Maddox:

| Component | Description |
|---|---|
| **[[Tonic vergence]]** | Baseline vergence tone present even in the absence of visual stimuli; determined by resting position of the eyes |
| **[[Accommodative vergence]]** | Convergence driven reflexively by the act of [[accommodation]]; quantified by the [[AC/A ratio]] |
| **[[Fusional (disparity) vergence]]** | Convergence driven by [[retinal disparity]] to achieve and maintain [[bifoveal fixation]] and [[sensory fusion]] |
| **[[Proximal (psychic) vergence]]** | Convergence driven by the awareness or perception of nearness of a target |

During NPC testing, as the target approaches the patient, all four components are maximally recruited. The **break point** occurs when the total convergence demand exceeds the sum of all available convergence components, and one eye deviates outward ([[exodeviation]]), resulting in [[diplopia]] or suppression.

## Pathophysiology

### Mechanism of a Receded NPC

A receded NPC indicates an inability to sustain adequate convergence at near distances. The pathophysiology can be understood through the following mechanisms:

1. **Reduced Fusional Convergence Amplitude**: The most common cause of a receded NPC is insufficient [[positive fusional vergence]] (PFV) reserves. In [[convergence insufficiency]], the patient's fusional convergence system cannot generate sufficient motor output to meet the convergence demand at very near distances. This is often an idiopathic neuromuscular inefficiency of the vergence system.

2. **Decompensation of an Exophoria**: Many patients have a baseline [[exophoria]] (latent outward deviation) at near. When fusional vergence reserves are inadequate to compensate for this phoria, the NPC recedes. The relationship between the magnitude of [[near exophoria]] and the NPC is clinically significant—patients with a large near exophoria and reduced PFV are more likely to be symptomatic.

3. **Accommodative-Vergence Decoupling**: Since [[accommodative vergence]] contributes to total convergence, conditions that reduce accommodative effort (e.g., [[presbyopia]], [[accommodative insufficiency]], or overcorrected [[myopia]]) can indirectly reduce convergence and cause a receded NPC.

4. **Neurological Disruption**: Lesions affecting the [[vergence center]] in the dorsal [[midbrain]] (rostral to the [[oculomotor nucleus]]), the [[oculomotor nerve]], or higher cortical areas (e.g., [[traumatic brain injury]], [[stroke]], [[multiple sclerosis]], [[Parkinson's disease]]) can impair convergence and result in a receded NPC. Post-concussive convergence insufficiency is increasingly recognized in the literature.

5. **Mechanical Restriction or Paresis**: Rarely, a receded NPC may result from [[medial rectus]] weakness (e.g., partial [[CN III palsy]]) or mechanical restriction of adduction (e.g., [[orbital fracture]], [[thyroid eye disease]] affecting the [[lateral rectus]] with restrictive myopathy).

6. **Pharmacological Effects**: Certain medications, including [[anticholinergics]], [[antihistamines]], [[benzodiazepines]], and other CNS depressants, can impair convergence by affecting the neural substrate of the vergence system or by reducing accommodative drive.

### Convergence Insufficiency as the Primary Associated Condition

[[Convergence insufficiency]] is the most common binocular vision disorder associated with a receded NPC. The diagnostic criteria for CI, as established by the CITT, include:

- **Receded NPC** (break ≥6 cm, often >10 cm)
- **Reduced [[positive fusional vergence]]** at near (failing Sheard's criterion or below expected norms)
- **[[Exophoria]]** at near greater than at distance
- **Symptomatic complaints** consistent with near-vision stress

CI has a reported prevalence of approximately **2–13%** in school-age children and young adults, depending on the diagnostic criteria used and the population studied. It is a significant cause of reading difficulties, reduced academic performance, and asthenopic symptoms in children and adults.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Patients with a receded NPC—particularly those with [[convergence insufficiency]]—typically present with a constellation of symptoms related to prolonged near-vision tasks. These symptoms are often collectively referred to as **[[asthenopia]]** or near-vision stress.

### Common Symptoms

| Symptom | Description |
|---|---|
| **[[Diplopia]]** (intermittent) | Seeing double, especially during reading or near work; may be transient |
| **[[Asthenopia]]** | Eye strain, fatigue, or discomfort during near tasks |
| **Headaches** | Frontal or periorbital headaches, often worsening with sustained near work |
| **Blurred vision at near** | Intermittent blur during reading, often due to associated [[accommodative insufficiency]] |
| **Difficulty concentrating** | Reduced attention span during reading or desk work |
| **Loss of place while reading** | Words appear to move or jump; frequent re-reading of lines |
| **Slow reading speed** | Reduced reading fluency and comprehension |
| **Closing or covering one eye** | Compensatory behavior to eliminate diplopia |
| **Pulling sensation in the eyes** | Subjective sensation of effort or strain during convergence |
| **Avoidance of near work** | Behavioral avoidance of reading, homework, or screen use |

### Symptom Quantification

The **[[Convergence Insufficiency Symptom Survey]]** (CISS), also known as the **CI Symptom Survey**, is a validated 15-item questionnaire used to quantify the frequency and severity of symptoms associated with convergence insufficiency. Each item is scored on a 5-point Likert scale (0 = never, 4 = always), yielding a total score of 0–60. A score of **≥16 in children (9–18 years)** or **≥21 in adults (≥19 years)** is considered clinically significant and suggestive of symptomatic CI.

### Differential Symptom Considerations

It is important to differentiate symptoms of a receded NPC/CI from those of other conditions that may present similarly:

- **[[Accommodative insufficiency]]**: May coexist with CI; blurred near vision is more prominent.
- **[[Accommodative infacility]]**: Difficulty with rapid focus changes between distance and near.
- **[[Intermittent exotropia]]**: Larger magnitude deviation; may manifest at distance as well.
- **[[Vertical heterophoria]]**: May cause similar asthenopic symptoms but with a vertical component.
- **[[Dry eye disease]]**: Can cause reading-related discomfort but without convergence deficits.

## Diagnosis

### NPC Measurement Technique

NPC is measured using a simple, non-invasive clinical procedure. The standard technique involves the following steps:

1. **Patient Positioning**: The patient is seated comfortably with appropriate [[refractive correction]] in place (spectacles or [[contact lenses]]).

2. **Target Selection**: A small, detailed accommodative target is used. Common targets include:
   - A single column of **20/30 reduced Snellen letters** on a near-point card
   - A small letter or symbol on a tongue depressor or pen tip
   - The tip of a **[[Wolff wand]]** or similar instrument
   - A **penlight** (used to assess NPC without accommodative demand; less preferred for routine testing as it may underestimate convergence ability)
   - A **penlight with red-green (anaglyphic) glasses** (red lens over one eye) to detect suppression

3. **Procedure**:
   - The target is held at approximately **40 cm** (or arm's length) directly in front of the patient at eye level, in the [[midline]].
   - The patient is instructed to fixate on the target and report when it becomes double ([[diplopia]]).
   - The examiner slowly moves the target toward the patient's nose at a steady rate (approximately **1–2 cm/second**).
   - The examiner simultaneously observes the patient's eyes for loss of [[binocular alignment]]—specifically, an outward deviation ([[exodeviation]]) of one eye.
   - The **break point** is recorded as the distance (in centimeters) from the bridge of the nose (or spectacle plane) at which:
     - The patient reports diplopia, **OR**
     - The examiner observes a loss of fusion (one eye deviates outward)
   - The target is then slowly moved **away** from the patient until single vision is restored or the eyes realign.
   - The **recovery point** is recorded as the distance at which fusion is regained.
   - The test is typically repeated **three times**, and the average or the most representative value is recorded. Some clinicians note whether the NPC fatigues (recedes) with repeated testing, which is a characteristic finding in CI.

4. **Recording**: NPC is recorded as **break/recovery** in centimeters. For example: **NPC: 8/12 cm** indicates a break at 8 cm and recovery at 12 cm.

### Normative Values

| Population | Expected NPC Break | Expected NPC Recovery |
|---|---|---|
| Children (6–12 years) | ≤5 cm | ≤7 cm |
| Young adults (13–30 years) | ≤5–6 cm | ≤7–8 cm |
| Adults (30–50 years) | ≤6–8 cm | ≤8–10 cm |
| Older adults / [[Presbyopia]] | May be slightly receded due to reduced accommodative convergence | Variable |

**Note**: Normative values can vary based on the target type used. An accommodative target (letters) typically yields a closer NPC than a non-accommodative target (penlight), because the accommodative target recruits [[accommodative vergence]] in addition to [[fusional vergence]].

### Factors Affecting NPC Measurement

| Factor | Effect on NPC |
|---|---|
| **Target type** | Accommodative targets yield closer NPC than penlight |
| **Speed of target approach** | Too-rapid approach may yield artificially close NPC |
| **Patient attention/effort** | Poor cooperation or inattention may yield receded NPC |
| **Fatigue** | NPC may recede with repeated measurements (fatigue effect characteristic of CI) |
| **Refractive error** | Uncorrected [[hyperopia]] may improve NPC (increased accommodative convergence); uncorrected [[myopia]] may worsen it |
| **Age** | NPC may recede slightly with age, particularly after onset of [[presbyopia]] |
| **Medications** | CNS depressants, anticholinergics may recede NPC |
| **Neurological status** | [[Traumatic brain injury]], concussion, or neurological disease may recede NPC |

### Additional Diagnostic Tests for Convergence Function

NPC should not be interpreted in isolation. A comprehensive binocular vision assessment includes:

| Test | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **[[Cover test]]** (distance and near) | Quantify [[heterophoria]] or [[heterotropia]]; identify near exophoria |
| **[[Positive fusional vergence]]** (PFV) at near | Measured with [[prism bar]] or [[phoropter]]; assesses convergence reserves |
| **[[Negative fusional vergence]]** (NFV) at near | Assesses divergence reserves |
| **[[AC/A ratio]]** | Quantifies the relationship between accommodation and convergence |
| **[[Accommodative amplitude]]** | Rule out [[accommodative insufficiency]] |
| **[[Accommodative facility]]** | Rule out [[accommodative infacility]] |
| **[[Vergence facility]]** | Assessed with 12 BO / 3 BI flippers; evaluates speed and sustainability of vergence changes |
| **[[Stereoacuity]]** (e.g., [[Randot stereotest]], [[Titmus test]]) | Assess quality of [[binocular vision]] and [[stereopsis]] |
| **[[CISS questionnaire]]** | Quantify symptom severity |
| **[[Developmental Eye Movement test]] (DEM)** | Assess oculomotor function and saccadic efficiency |
| **[[Fixation disparity]]** assessment | Evaluate fine binocular alignment under binocular conditions |

### Diagnostic Criteria for Convergence Insufficiency (CITT Criteria)

For a definitive diagnosis of [[convergence insufficiency]], the following criteria (based on the CITT) should be met:

1. **Receded NPC** ≥6 cm (break), with many clinicians using ≥10 cm as a more definitive threshold
2. **Exophoria at near** ≥4 prism diopters greater than at distance
3. **Reduced PFV at near** failing [[Sheard's criterion]] (i.e., fusional vergence reserves less than twice the phoria)
4. **CISS score** ≥16 (children) or ≥21 (adults)

## Management and Treatment

### Overview of Treatment Approaches

The management of a receded NPC depends on the underlying etiology. For the most common cause—[[convergence insufficiency]]—the evidence base strongly supports **office-based [[vergence]]/[[accommodative therapy]]** (OBVAT) as the most effective treatment. The CITT studies provided Level I evidence for this recommendation.

### 1. Office-Based Vergence/Accommodative Therapy (OBVAT)

**OBVAT** is the gold-standard treatment for convergence insufficiency and receded NPC. It involves structured, supervised therapy sessions conducted in a clinical setting by a trained therapist (optometrist, orthoptist, or vision therapist), supplemented by home reinforcement activities.

**Typical Protocol (based on CITT)**:
- **Frequency**: 1 session per week (60 minutes) for **12–16 weeks**
- **Home reinforcement**: 15 minutes/day, 5 days/week
- **Techniques employed**:

| Technique | Description |
|---|---|
| **[[Brock string]]** | A string with colored beads used to train convergence awareness, [[physiological diplopia]], and voluntary convergence at varying distances |
| **[[Vectograms]]** (e.g., Quoits, Clown, Spirangle) | Polarized targets viewed through [[polarized glasses]] to train fusional vergence in free space |
| **[[Tranaglyphs]]** | Red-green anaglyphic targets for vergence training |
| **[[Aperture rule]]** | A device that trains flat and stereoscopic fusion at various vergence demands |
| **[[Eccentric circles]] / [[Free-space fusion cards]]** | Cards used for convergence and divergence training without optical aids |
| **[[Prism bar]] vergence training** | Step or smooth vergence training using [[prism bars]] |
| **[[Computer-based vergence therapy]]** (e.g., [[HTS]], [[VividVision]], [[Vivid Vision Home]]) | Software programs that provide interactive vergence and accommodative exercises |
| **[[Loose lens accommodative facility]]** | Flipper lenses (e.g., ±2.00 D) to train accommodative flexibility, which supports convergence |
| **Push-up convergence training** | Voluntary convergence exercises using a near target (used as a component of OBVAT, not as sole treatment) |

**CITT Outcomes**: The CITT demonstrated that OBVAT produced:
- **Normalization of NPC** (break ≤6 cm) in approximately **73%** of children
- **Clinically significant improvement in CISS scores** in the majority of patients
- **Improvement in PFV** at near
- Superiority over home-based pencil push-ups (HBPP), home-based computer vergence therapy, and placebo therapy

### 2. Home-Based Pencil Push-Ups (HBPP)

**Pencil push-ups** (also called **convergence push-ups** or **pen-to-nose exercises**) involve the patient fixating on a small target on a pencil or pen and slowly bringing it toward the nose, attempting to maintain single vision as long as possible.

- **Protocol**: Typically 15 minutes/day, 5 days/week
- **Efficacy**: The CITT found that HBPP alone was **not significantly more effective than placebo** in improving NPC, PFV, or symptoms in children with CI. Despite this, HBPP remains widely prescribed due to its simplicity and zero cost.
- **Limitations**: HBPP does not adequately train fusional vergence in a controlled manner, may promote [[suppression]] rather than true fusion, and lacks the progressive demand structure of OBVAT.

### 3. Home-Based Computer Vergence/Accommodative Therapy

Computer-based programs (e.g., **[[HTS]]** – Home Therapy System, **[[CVAT]]** – Computer-based Vergence/Accommodative Therapy) provide interactive vergence exercises that can be performed at home.

- **Efficacy**: The CITT found that home-based computer therapy combined with pencil push-ups was **less effective than OBVAT** but showed some improvement over placebo in certain outcome measures.
- **Role**: May serve as a useful adjunct to OBVAT or as an alternative when access to office-based therapy is limited.

### 4. Prism Therapy

**[[Base-in prisms]]** can be prescribed to reduce the convergence demand at near, providing symptomatic relief.

- **Indications**: Patients who are unable or unwilling to undergo vision therapy; elderly patients with CI; patients with neurological CI where recovery is limited.
- **Prescription**: The prism amount is typically determined by the [[fixation disparity]] curve or by prescribing the minimum prism that alleviates symptoms (often guided by [[Sheard's criterion]] or [[Percival's criterion]]).
- **Limitations**: Prisms treat the symptom (excessive convergence demand) rather than the underlying vergence insufficiency. They do not improve NPC or fusional vergence reserves.

### 5. Plus Lens Addition at Near

In patients where [[accommodative insufficiency]] coexists with CI, or where the [[AC/A ratio]] is high, a **plus lens addition** (e.g., +0.50 to +1.50 D) for near work can reduce accommodative demand and, through the [[AC/A ratio]], reduce convergence demand.

- **Indications**: CI with coexisting accommodative dysfunction; high AC/A ratio CI.
- **Limitations**: Does not address the underlying vergence deficit; may be used as a temporary measure or adjunct.

### 6. Surgical Intervention

Surgery is **rarely indicated** for convergence insufficiency. However, in refractory cases—particularly those with a large, constant [[exotropia]] at near that does not respond to prolonged therapy—surgical options may be considered.

- **Procedure**: Bilateral [[medial rectus resection]] or unilateral medial rectus resection with ipsilateral [[lateral rectus recession]] may be performed to reduce the near exodeviation.
- **Indications**: Severe, symptomatic CI unresponsive to maximal non-surgical management; CI secondary to neurological disease with poor prognosis for recovery.
- **Outcomes**: Surgical outcomes for CI are less predictable than for other forms of [[strabismus]], and there is a risk of consecutive [[esotropia]] at distance.

### 7. Management of Neurological or Secondary Causes

When a receded NPC is secondary to a neurological condition (e.g., [[traumatic brain injury]], [[concussion]], [[Parkinson's disease]], [[progressive supranuclear palsy]], [[multiple sclerosis]]), management should address the underlying condition in conjunction with vergence rehabilitation.

- **Post-concussive CI**: NPC testing is now a standard component of concussion assessment protocols (e.g., **[[VOMS]]** – Vestibular/Ocular Motor Screening). Receded NPC post-concussion often improves with time and targeted vergence therapy.
- **Neurodegenerative disease**: Prism therapy may be more appropriate than active vergence therapy in progressive conditions.

## Pharmacological Considerations

There are currently **no FDA-approved pharmacological agents** specifically indicated for the treatment of convergence insufficiency or receded NPC. However, pharmacological considerations include:

| Agent | Relevance |
|---|---|
| **[[Pilocarpine]]** (low-dose) | Miotic agent; theoretically could enhance the near triad, but not used clinically for CI |
| **Anticholinergic medications** | May worsen NPC by reducing accommodative drive and, consequently, accommodative convergence |
| **CNS stimulants** (e.g., methylphenidate) | Some studies have explored the overlap between CI and [[ADHD]]; stimulants do not treat CI but may improve attention during therapy |
| **CNS depressants** (benzodiazepines, anticonvulsants) | May worsen convergence function |

Clinicians should review the patient's medication list when evaluating a receded NPC, as pharmacological side effects may be contributory.

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Patients

- CI is one of the most common binocular vision disorders in school-age children.
- A receded NPC in children may be misdiagnosed as [[ADHD]], [[dyslexia]], or a learning disability due to overlapping symptoms (difficulty concentrating, avoidance of reading, poor academic performance).
- The CITT demonstrated that OBVAT is highly effective in children aged 9–17 years.
- Younger children (under 9) may require modified therapy approaches with age-appropriate activities.

### Traumatic Brain Injury and Concussion

- Receded NPC is one of the most common oculomotor findings following [[mild traumatic brain injury]] (mTBI) / [[concussion]], with reported prevalence rates of **30–50%** in concussed patients.
- NPC testing is incorporated into the **[[VOMS]]** (Vestibular/Ocular Motor Screening) tool used in sports medicine and emergency settings.
- A receded NPC post-concussion is a predictor of prolonged recovery and may warrant referral for specialized neuro-optometric rehabilitation.

### Presbyopic Patients

- The onset of [[presbyopia]] reduces [[accommodative amplitude]], which in turn reduces [[accommodative vergence]]. This can unmask a latent convergence insufficiency or cause a previously compensated NPC to recede.
- Management may include near plus addition optimization and base-in prism if symptomatic.

## Prognosis

The prognosis for a receded NPC depends on the underlying etiology:

| Etiology | Prognosis |
|---|---|
| **Idiopathic CI** | Excellent with OBVAT; ~73% achieve normal NPC; symptoms significantly improve in majority |
| **Post-concussive CI** | Good; many patients improve spontaneously or with therapy within 3–6 months |
| **CI secondary to neurodegenerative disease** | Guarded; may require long-term prism management |
| **CI secondary to CN III palsy** | Variable; depends on degree of nerve recovery |
| **Decompensated exophoria** | Good with therapy and/or prism correction |

Long-term maintenance of gains achieved through OBVAT is generally good, though some patients may require periodic "booster" therapy sessions, particularly during periods of increased near-vision demand (e.g., academic examinations, increased screen time).

---

## Summary

Near Point of Convergence is a simple yet profoundly informative clinical measurement that provides direct insight into the integrity of the [[vergence system]]. A receded NPC is the hallmark finding of [[convergence insufficiency]], one of the most prevalent and treatable binocular vision disorders. Accurate measurement, appropriate differential diagnosis, and evidence-based management—particularly office-based vergence/accommodative therapy—can dramatically improve patient symptoms, visual function, and quality of life. NPC testing should be a routine component of every comprehensive eye examination, with particular attention in pediatric populations, patients with reading or learning difficulties, and individuals with a history of [[traumatic brain injury]].
