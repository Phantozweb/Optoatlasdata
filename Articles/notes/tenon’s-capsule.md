

# Tenon's Capsule

**Tenon's capsule** (also known as the **fascia bulbi**, **vagina bulbi**, or **bulbar fascia**) is a thin, dense, fibrous connective tissue membrane that envelops the [[globe]] from the [[limbus]] anteriorly to the [[optic nerve]] posteriorly. Named after the French surgeon and anatomist **Jacques-René Tenon** (1724–1816), who first described it in detail in 1806 in his work *Mémoires et observations sur l'anatomie, la pathologie et la chirurgie*, this structure serves as the socket lining within which the eyeball rotates and plays a critical role in [[orbital anatomy]], [[strabismus surgery]], [[glaucoma filtration surgery]], and the pathophysiology of various orbital and ocular surface disorders.

Tenon's capsule is of paramount clinical importance to both [[optometrists]] and [[ophthalmologists]] because of its involvement in surgical wound healing, its role as a barrier to infection and tumor spread, and its contribution to the biomechanics of [[extraocular muscle]] function. A thorough understanding of this structure is essential for any eye care professional engaged in the diagnosis and management of orbital disease, post-surgical complications, and ocular motility disorders.

---

## Embryology and Development

Tenon's capsule is derived from [[mesenchyme]] of [[neural crest]] origin. During embryonic development, the mesenchymal tissue surrounding the developing [[optic cup]] differentiates into several distinct layers. By approximately the **fifth month of gestation**, the fascia bulbi begins to differentiate as a recognizable connective tissue layer separate from the [[periorbita]] (orbital periosteum) and the [[sclera]].

The development of Tenon's capsule is closely linked to the maturation of the [[extraocular muscles]]. As the muscles develop and elongate, they penetrate through the capsule, and specialized connective tissue sleeves (muscle sheaths) form at the points of penetration. These sleeves are continuous with the intermuscular septum and the broader fascial system of the [[orbit]]. The capsule is fully formed at birth, though it continues to undergo minor structural remodeling throughout childhood and into early adulthood.

Understanding the embryological origin of Tenon's capsule is clinically relevant because developmental anomalies in neural crest–derived tissues can result in structural abnormalities of the capsule, potentially contributing to congenital motility disorders or predisposing to complications during surgical intervention.

## Gross Anatomy

### General Structure

Tenon's capsule is a smooth, glistening, avascular membrane that forms a true fascial envelope around the [[globe]]. It is composed primarily of **Type I and Type III [[collagen]]** fibers arranged in a lamellar pattern, interspersed with [[elastic fibers]] and [[fibroblasts]]. The capsule varies in thickness from approximately **0.1 mm to 0.5 mm**, being thinnest anteriorly near the limbus and thickest posteriorly near the optic nerve.

### Anterior Attachment

Anteriorly, Tenon's capsule fuses with the [[conjunctiva]] and the underlying [[episclera]] approximately **1.5 to 2.0 mm posterior to the [[corneoscleral limbus]]**. At this junction, the capsule blends with the subconjunctival connective tissue, making surgical dissection in this region particularly important during procedures such as [[trabeculectomy]], [[pterygium]] excision, and [[strabismus surgery]]. The anterior fusion creates a closed potential space—the **sub-Tenon's space** (also called the **episcleral space** or **Tenon's space**)—between the capsule and the sclera.

### Posterior Attachment

Posteriorly, Tenon's capsule fuses with the [[dural sheath]] of the [[optic nerve]] and the surrounding [[meninges]] at the point where the optic nerve exits the globe. This posterior attachment is reinforced by connective tissue septa that blend with the [[optic nerve sheath]] and the orbital fat. The posterior fusion effectively seals the sub-Tenon's space, creating a closed compartment that has significant implications for regional [[anesthesia]] techniques (e.g., [[sub-Tenon's block]]).

### Relationship to Extraocular Muscles

The six [[extraocular muscles]] (four [[rectus muscles]]—[[medial rectus]], [[lateral rectus]], [[superior rectus]], and [[inferior rectus]]—and two [[oblique muscles]]—[[superior oblique]] and [[inferior oblique]]) pierce Tenon's capsule as they course from their origins in the orbital apex to their insertions on the sclera. At each point of penetration, the capsule reflects back along the muscle belly to form a **tubular fascial sheath** (muscle sleeve). These sheaths are interconnected by the **[[intermuscular septum]]**, a thin fascial membrane that extends between adjacent rectus muscles and divides the orbit into intraconal and extraconal compartments.

The fascial sheaths of the rectus muscles give rise to important **check ligaments** (also called **expansions** or **retinacula**), which attach to the orbital walls and limit excessive rotation of the globe. The most clinically significant of these are:

| Check Ligament | Origin | Insertion |
|---|---|---|
| Lateral check ligament | Sheath of [[lateral rectus muscle]] | [[Whitnall's tubercle]] on the [[zygomatic bone]] |
| Medial check ligament | Sheath of [[medial rectus muscle]] | [[Lacrimal bone]] / posterior lacrimal crest |
| Inferior suspensory ligament ([[Lockwood's ligament]]) | Sheaths of [[inferior rectus]] and [[inferior oblique]] | Medial and lateral orbital walls |
| Superior suspensory complex | Sheath of [[superior rectus]] blending with [[levator palpebrae superioris]] | Superior orbital rim |

**[[Lockwood's ligament]]** is of particular clinical importance as it forms a hammock-like sling beneath the globe, providing critical support. Disruption of this ligament—whether through trauma, surgery, or degenerative processes—can result in [[enophthalmos]] or inferior displacement of the globe.

### Sub-Tenon's Space (Episcleral Space)

The **sub-Tenon's space** is the potential space between the inner surface of Tenon's capsule and the outer surface of the [[sclera]]. This space contains loose areolar tissue, the [[episcleral vasculature]], and a small amount of serous fluid that facilitates smooth rotation of the globe within the capsule. The space is traversed by delicate connective tissue strands and by the [[vortex veins]], [[posterior ciliary arteries]], and [[ciliary nerves]] as they pass through the sclera.

The sub-Tenon's space is clinically exploited during:

- **[[Sub-Tenon's anesthesia]]** (also called **episcleral anesthesia** or **parabulbar block**): Local anesthetic is injected into this space to achieve regional anesthesia for intraocular surgery.
- **Sub-Tenon's drug delivery**: Corticosteroids (e.g., [[triamcinolone acetonide]]) or anti-VEGF agents may be delivered via this route for posterior segment diseases.
- **Surgical dissection**: During strabismus surgery, trabeculectomy, or scleral buckling procedures.

## Histology and Ultrastructure

Histologically, Tenon's capsule is composed of **dense irregular connective tissue** with the following key components:

| Component | Description |
|---|---|
| **Collagen fibers** | Predominantly [[Type I collagen]] (providing tensile strength) and [[Type III collagen]] (providing structural flexibility); arranged in interwoven lamellae |
| **Elastic fibers** | Scattered throughout the matrix; provide elasticity and recoil during globe rotation |
| **Fibroblasts** | The predominant cell type; responsible for synthesis and maintenance of the extracellular matrix |
| **Ground substance** | Rich in [[glycosaminoglycans]] (GAGs), particularly [[hyaluronic acid]] and [[chondroitin sulfate]], which provide hydration and facilitate gliding |
| **Myofibroblasts** | Present in small numbers; may increase significantly during wound healing and fibrosis |
| **Mast cells and macrophages** | Present in low density; involved in immune surveillance and inflammatory responses |

The inner (scleral) surface of Tenon's capsule is smooth and lined by a thin layer of **mesothelial-like cells** that secrete a lubricating fluid, facilitating frictionless movement of the globe. The outer (orbital) surface is rougher and blends with the orbital fat and connective tissue septa.

Electron microscopy reveals that the collagen fibrils within Tenon's capsule have a **diameter of approximately 50–80 nm** and are arranged in a characteristic **basket-weave pattern** that confers multidirectional tensile strength. This arrangement is distinct from the more parallel collagen organization seen in [[tendons]] and is optimized for the multiaxial mechanical stresses experienced during eye movement.

## Physiology and Biomechanical Function

Tenon's capsule serves several critical physiological and biomechanical functions:

### 1. Frictionless Globe Rotation
The smooth inner surface of the capsule, combined with the lubricating fluid in the sub-Tenon's space, creates a **low-friction bearing surface** that allows the globe to rotate freely in all directions of gaze. This function is analogous to a [[synovial membrane]] in a diarthrodial joint, though Tenon's capsule is not a true synovial structure.

### 2. Structural Support and Suspension
Through its check ligaments and suspensory apparatus (particularly [[Lockwood's ligament]]), Tenon's capsule contributes to maintaining the globe in its proper anatomical position within the orbit. The capsule distributes the forces generated by the extraocular muscles and prevents excessive displacement of the globe during eye movements.

### 3. Barrier Function
Tenon's capsule acts as a **physical barrier** that compartmentalizes the orbital contents. It separates the sub-Tenon's (episcleral) space from the retrobulbar (intraconal) space and limits the spread of infection, hemorrhage, and neoplastic processes between compartments. This barrier function is clinically significant in the staging and management of [[ocular surface squamous neoplasia]] (OSSN), [[conjunctival melanoma]], and other periocular malignancies.

### 4. Wound Healing Modulation
Tenon's capsule fibroblasts play a central role in the wound healing response following ocular surgery. The proliferative and fibrotic capacity of these fibroblasts is a major determinant of surgical outcomes, particularly in [[glaucoma filtration surgery]] where excessive scarring of the filtering bleb by Tenon's fibroblasts is the leading cause of surgical failure.

## Pathophysiology

### Fibrosis and Scarring

The most clinically significant pathophysiological process involving Tenon's capsule is **fibrosis**. Tenon's capsule fibroblasts are highly responsive to inflammatory mediators and growth factors, particularly:

- **[[Transforming growth factor-beta]] (TGF-β)**: The most potent profibrotic cytokine; stimulates fibroblast proliferation, myofibroblast differentiation, and collagen deposition.
- **[[Vascular endothelial growth factor]] (VEGF)**: Promotes angiogenesis and vascular permeability in the wound healing response.
- **[[Platelet-derived growth factor]] (PDGF)**: Stimulates fibroblast migration and proliferation.
- **[[Connective tissue growth factor]] (CTGF)**: Acts downstream of TGF-β to amplify the fibrotic response.

Following surgical disruption (e.g., during [[trabeculectomy]] or [[strabismus surgery]]), Tenon's fibroblasts undergo activation, proliferation, and differentiation into **[[myofibroblasts]]**—contractile cells that produce excessive [[extracellular matrix]] (ECM) and contribute to scar formation. This fibrotic response is the primary mechanism of **bleb failure** after glaucoma filtration surgery and of **adhesion formation** after strabismus surgery.

Risk factors for excessive Tenon's capsule fibrosis include:

| Risk Factor | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Young age | Higher fibroblast density and proliferative capacity |
| African descent | Genetically determined increased fibroblast activity |
| Previous ocular surgery | Pre-existing inflammation and fibroblast priming |
| Chronic topical medication use (e.g., [[prostaglandin analogs]], preserved drops) | Subclinical conjunctival and sub-Tenon's inflammation |
| [[Uveitis]] | Chronic intraocular inflammation with cytokine spillover |
| [[Neovascular glaucoma]] | High levels of VEGF and inflammatory mediators |

### Tenonitis (Idiopathic Orbital Inflammation Involving Tenon's Capsule)

**Tenonitis** (also called **tendonitis of the globe** or **idiopathic inflammation of Tenon's capsule**) is a form of [[idiopathic orbital inflammation]] (previously termed "orbital pseudotumor") that specifically involves Tenon's capsule. It may occur in isolation or as part of a more diffuse orbital inflammatory process.

The pathophysiology of tenonitis involves a **non-granulomatous or granulomatous inflammatory infiltrate** within Tenon's capsule, characterized by:

- Lymphocytic and plasmacytic infiltration
- Edema and thickening of the capsule
- Possible extension to involve the episcleral tissue, extraocular muscle sheaths, and orbital fat
- In chronic cases, fibrosis and cicatrization

The etiology is typically idiopathic, but secondary causes must be excluded, including [[sarcoidosis]], [[granulomatosis with polyangiitis]] (formerly Wegener's granulomatosis), [[IgG4-related disease]], [[lymphoma]], and infectious etiologies.

### Tenon's Capsule Cyst (Tenon's Cyst)

A **Tenon's capsule cyst** is an encapsulated fluid collection within or adjacent to Tenon's capsule, most commonly occurring as a complication of [[glaucoma filtration surgery]]. These cysts represent a form of **encapsulated bleb** (also called a **Tenon's cyst bleb** or **ring of steel**) in which the filtering bleb becomes surrounded by a thick, fibrotic wall of Tenon's capsule tissue, resulting in elevated [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) due to impaired aqueous humor drainage.

Tenon's cysts typically develop **2 to 8 weeks** after trabeculectomy and are characterized by:

- A dome-shaped, tense, non-fluctuant elevation at the surgical site
- Prominent surface vascularity
- Elevated IOP despite a patent sclerostomy
- Histologically: dense collagenous wall with minimal or absent microcystic spaces

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Tenonitis

Patients with tenonitis may present with the following symptoms and signs:

**Symptoms:**
- Acute or subacute onset of **periorbital pain**, often described as deep, aching, and exacerbated by eye movement
- **Diplopia** (particularly if extraocular muscle sheaths are involved)
- **Reduced visual acuity** (if posterior segment involvement or optic nerve compression occurs)
- **Eyelid swelling** and erythema

**Signs:**
- [[Proptosis]] (usually mild to moderate, axial)
- [[Chemosis]] (conjunctival edema)
- Injection of the [[episcleral vessels]] and conjunctival hyperemia
- Restricted ocular motility with pain on attempted movement
- Tenderness to palpation over the globe
- In severe cases, [[papilledema]] or choroidal folds on [[fundoscopy]]

### Tenon's Capsule Cyst (Post-Trabeculectomy)

- Elevated IOP (often returning to pre-surgical levels)
- Dome-shaped, tense bleb with prominent vascularity
- Discomfort or foreign body sensation at the bleb site
- Possible blurred vision related to IOP elevation

### Post-Surgical Adhesions

Following strabismus surgery or other procedures involving Tenon's capsule:
- Restricted ocular motility
- Diplopia
- Globe retraction or displacement
- Pain with eye movement

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

Diagnosis of Tenon's capsule pathology begins with a thorough **clinical history** and **ophthalmic examination**, including:

- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: Assessment of conjunctival and episcleral injection, chemosis, bleb morphology (in post-trabeculectomy patients), and anterior segment inflammation.
- **[[Tonometry]]**: Measurement of IOP, particularly relevant in Tenon's cyst evaluation.
- **Ocular motility assessment**: Evaluation of ductions and versions; identification of restriction patterns.
- **[[Exophthalmometry]]** (e.g., [[Hertel exophthalmometer]]): Quantification of proptosis.
- **[[Fundoscopy]]**: Assessment for choroidal folds, disc edema, or posterior segment involvement.

### Imaging

| Modality | Findings in Tenon's Capsule Pathology |
|---|---|
| **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** | Thickening of Tenon's capsule ("T-sign" in posterior tenonitis—fluid in sub-Tenon's space surrounding the optic nerve); cystic lesions; bleb morphology |
| **[[Computed tomography]] (CT)** | Diffuse or focal thickening of the scleral-Tenon's complex; enhancement with contrast in inflammatory conditions; proptosis quantification |
| **[[Magnetic resonance imaging]] (MRI)** | Superior soft tissue contrast; T2-weighted hyperintensity in edematous/inflamed Tenon's capsule; gadolinium enhancement in active inflammation; differentiation from lymphoma or other neoplasms |
| **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | High-resolution imaging of anterior Tenon's capsule, bleb architecture, and sub-Tenon's space; particularly useful in evaluating encapsulated blebs |
| **[[Anterior segment optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT)** | Non-invasive assessment of bleb wall thickness, internal reflectivity, and microcystic spaces; useful for monitoring Tenon's cyst blebs |

### The "T-Sign" in Posterior Tenonitis

The **T-sign** (or **crescent sign**) on [[B-scan ultrasonography]] is a pathognomonic finding in posterior tenonitis. It is produced by the accumulation of fluid in the sub-Tenon's space surrounding the optic nerve, creating a characteristic T-shaped or crescent-shaped echolucent area posterior to the globe. This finding helps distinguish tenonitis from other causes of posterior scleritis or orbital inflammation.

### Laboratory Investigations

When tenonitis is suspected, laboratory workup should be directed at excluding secondary causes:

- **Complete blood count (CBC)** with differential
- **Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)** and **C-reactive protein (CRP)**
- **Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE)** level (to exclude [[sarcoidosis]])
- **Antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies (ANCA)** (to exclude [[granulomatosis with polyangiitis]])
- **IgG4 subclass levels** (to exclude [[IgG4-related disease]])
- **Chest X-ray** or **CT chest** (to exclude sarcoidosis or lymphoma)
- **Serum protein electrophoresis** and **lactate dehydrogenase (LDH)** (if lymphoma is suspected)

### Biopsy

In cases refractory to treatment or where malignancy is suspected, **incisional or excisional biopsy** of Tenon's capsule may be necessary. Histopathological examination can differentiate between idiopathic inflammation, granulomatous disease, IgG4-related disease, and lymphoproliferative disorders.

## Treatment and Management

### Pharmacological Interventions

#### Treatment of Tenonitis

| Agent | Route | Indication | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Corticosteroids]]** (e.g., [[prednisone]] 1 mg/kg/day) | Oral | First-line for moderate-to-severe tenonitis | Taper over 4–6 weeks; monitor for systemic side effects |
| **[[Prednisolone acetate]] 1%** | Topical | Mild anterior inflammation | Adjunctive to systemic therapy |
| **[[Triamcinolone acetonide]]** (40 mg) | Sub-Tenon's injection | Localized inflammation; steroid-sparing | Risk of IOP elevation, particularly in [[steroid responders]] |
| **[[NSAIDs]]** (e.g., [[indomethacin]], [[ibuprofen]]) | Oral | Mild cases or adjunctive therapy | Less effective than corticosteroids for moderate-to-severe disease |
| **[[Methotrexate]]** | Oral | Steroid-sparing agent for chronic/recurrent tenonitis | Requires monitoring of hepatic and hematologic parameters |
| **[[Mycophenolate mofetil]]** | Oral | Second-line steroid-sparing agent | Used in refractory cases |
| **[[Rituximab]]** | Intravenous | Refractory IgG4-related disease or severe idiopathic orbital inflammation | Biologic anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody |
| **[[Radiation therapy]]** (low-dose) | External beam | Refractory idiopathic orbital inflammation | Reserved for cases unresponsive to immunosuppression |

#### Antifibrotic Agents in Glaucoma Surgery

The management of Tenon's capsule fibrosis in the context of [[glaucoma filtration surgery]] is one of the most important clinical applications of Tenon's capsule pharmacology. The following antifibrotic agents are used to modulate the wound healing response:

| Agent | Mechanism | Application | Key Considerations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Mitomycin C]] (MMC)** | Alkylating agent; inhibits fibroblast proliferation and collagen synthesis | Intraoperative application (0.2–0.4 mg/mL for 1–5 minutes) on sponge placed over Tenon's capsule/scleral flap | Risk of thin, avascular blebs; bleb leaks; [[hypotony maculopathy]]; [[endophthalmitis]] |
| **[[5-Fluorouracil]] (5-FU)** | Antimetabolite; inhibits thymidylate synthase and DNA synthesis in fibroblasts | Intraoperative or postoperative subconjunctival injection (5 mg in 0.1 mL) | Less potent than MMC; may require multiple injections; risk of corneal epithelial toxicity |
| **Anti-VEGF agents** (e.g., [[bevacizumab]]) | Inhibits VEGF-mediated angiogenesis and vascular permeability | Subconjunctival injection; adjunctive to MMC or 5-FU | Emerging evidence; may reduce bleb vascularity and fibrosis |
| **[[Beta-radiation]]** | Inhibits fibroblast proliferation | Intraoperative application using strontium-90 applicator | Historical use; largely replaced by MMC |

#### Management of Tenon's Capsule Cyst (Encapsulated Bleb)

- **Observation**: Some encapsulated blebs resolve spontaneously over weeks to months.
- **Aqueous suppressants**: [[Topical beta-blockers]] (e.g., [[timolol]]), [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]] (e.g., [[dorzolamide]]), or [[alpha-agonists]] (e.g., [[brimonidine]]) to control IOP while awaiting resolution.
- **Digital massage / bleb massage**: Gentle compression of the bleb to promote aqueous flow through the sclerostomy.
- **Subconjunctival 5-FU injection**: To reduce fibroblast activity in the cyst wall; typically 5 mg in 0.1 mL, administered adjacent to the bleb; may require multiple injections.
- **Needling revision**: A minimally invasive procedure in which a needle (typically 27- or 30-gauge) is used to puncture and disrupt the fibrotic cyst wall, often combined with subconjunctival MMC or 5-FU injection.
- **Surgical revision**: In refractory cases, formal bleb revision with excision of the fibrotic Tenon's capsule tissue and reapplication of antifibrotic agents may be necessary.

### Surgical Considerations

#### Tenon's Capsule in Strabismus Surgery

During [[strabismus surgery]], the surgeon must carefully dissect through Tenon's capsule to access the [[extraocular muscles]]. Key surgical principles include:

- **Meticulous dissection**: Minimizing trauma to Tenon's capsule reduces postoperative adhesion formation and scarring.
- **Preservation of the capsule**: Whenever possible, Tenon's capsule should be preserved and closed as a separate layer to reduce the risk of fat adherence syndrome and restrictive strabismus.
- **Use of adjustable sutures**: Allows postoperative adjustment of muscle position, mitigating the effects of unpredictable wound healing in Tenon's capsule.
- **Antifibrotic agents**: In re-operations or high-risk cases, intraoperative application of MMC may be considered to reduce adhesion formation.

#### Sub-Tenon's Anesthesia

**[[Sub-Tenon's block]]** (also called **episcleral block** or **parabulbar anesthesia**) is a widely used regional anesthesia technique for intraocular surgery. The procedure involves:

1. Topical anesthesia with [[proparacaine]] or [[tetracaine]]
2. A small conjunctival and Tenon's capsule incision (typically in the inferonasal quadrant, 5–7 mm from the limbus)
3. Blunt dissection into the sub-Tenon's space using curved, blunt-tipped cannula (e.g., **Stevens cannula** or **19-gauge blunt cannula**)
4. Injection of local anesthetic (e.g., 3–5 mL of [[lidocaine]] 2% with or without [[bupivacaine]] 0.5% and [[hyaluronidase]])

The anesthetic spreads within the sub-Tenon's space, bathing the sclera, ciliary nerves, and posterior ciliary vessels, providing effective [[akinesia]] and [[analgesia]] for procedures such as [[cataract surgery]], [[vitreoretinal surgery]], and [[trabeculectomy]].

**Complications** of sub-Tenon's anesthesia include:
- Subconjunctival hemorrhage (most common; usually self-limiting)
- Chemosis
- Orbital hemorrhage (rare)
- Globe perforation (extremely rare)
- Damage to extraocular muscles (rare)
- Post-operative ptosis (usually transient)

#### Tenon's Capsule in Orbital Implant Surgery

Following [[enucleation]] or [[evisceration]], an **orbital implant** (e.g., porous polyethylene [[Medpor]], hydroxyapatite, or silicone) is placed within the orbit. Tenon's capsule is closed as a separate layer over the implant to:

- Provide a biological barrier between the implant and the conjunctiva
- Reduce the risk of implant exposure and extrusion
- Facilitate vascularization of porous implants
- Improve prosthetic motility by transmitting extraocular muscle forces through the capsule to the implant

Adequate closure of Tenon's capsule is one of the most critical steps in preventing **implant exposure**, which is the most common complication of orbital implant surgery.

#### Tenon's Capsule Grafting

In cases of conjunctival or Tenon's capsule deficiency (e.g., after multiple surgeries, chemical burns, or extensive tumor excision), **Tenon's capsule autograft** or **allograft** tissue may be used to reconstruct the ocular surface and provide structural support. Additionally, Tenon's capsule flaps (advancement or rotational) are used in:

- Repair of scleral thinning or [[staphyloma]]
- Coverage of exposed [[scleral buckle]] elements
- Reinforcement of trabeculectomy blebs
- Closure of conjunctival defects

## Tenon's Capsule in Specific Clinical Contexts

### Relationship to Posterior Scleritis

**[[Posterior scleritis]]** frequently involves inflammation of Tenon's capsule, and the two conditions may coexist or be difficult to distinguish clinically. The sub-Tenon's space fluid accumulation seen in posterior scleritis produces the characteristic **T-sign** on B-scan ultrasonography, identical to that seen in isolated posterior tenonitis. Differentiation relies on the presence of scleral thickening and edema in posterior scleritis versus isolated capsular inflammation in tenonitis.

### Relationship to Thyroid Eye Disease

In **[[thyroid eye disease]]** (Graves' orbitopathy), the extraocular muscles undergo inflammatory enlargement. Because the muscle sheaths are continuous with Tenon's capsule, the inflammatory process may extend to involve the capsule itself, contributing to periorbital edema, chemosis, and restriction of globe motility. Fibrosis of Tenon's capsule and its check ligaments in chronic thyroid eye disease may contribute to the restrictive strabismus pattern characteristic of this condition.

### Tenon's Capsule and Ocular Surface Neoplasia

Tenon's capsule serves as an important **anatomical barrier** to the deep invasion of [[ocular surface squamous neoplasia]] (OSSN) and [[conjunctival melanoma]]. Tumors that breach Tenon's capsule have a significantly worse prognosis due to the potential for orbital invasion. Staging systems for conjunctival malignancies (e.g., the **AJCC TNM classification**) specifically reference Tenon's capsule penetration as a criterion for advanced disease (T3 or T4 staging).

## Age-Related Changes

With aging, Tenon's capsule undergoes several degenerative changes:

- **Thinning and attenuation** of the collagen matrix
- **Loss of elastic fibers**, reducing capsular elasticity
- **Decreased fibroblast density** and reduced capacity for tissue repair
- **Weakening of check ligaments and Lockwood's ligament**, potentially contributing to age-related [[enophthalmos]] and inferior scleral show
- **Increased laxity of the sub-Tenon's space**, which may facilitate the spread of sub-Tenon's anesthetic but also predispose to subconjunctival hemorrhage

These age-related changes have implications for surgical planning, as elderly patients may have thinner, more friable Tenon's capsule tissue that is more susceptible to tearing during dissection and less reliable for wound closure.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of Tenon's capsule pathology depends on the specific condition:

- **Tenonitis**: Generally responds well to corticosteroid therapy, with most patients achieving complete resolution. However, recurrence rates of 30–50% have been reported, and chronic cases may require long-term immunosuppression.
- **Tenon's capsule cyst**: Many resolve with conservative management (massage, aqueous suppressants, 5-FU injections). Needling revision is successful in approximately 70–80% of cases. Surgical revision may be required in refractory cases.
- **Post-surgical adhesions**: Outcomes depend on the severity of fibrosis and the success of re-operation. Use of antifibrotic agents and meticulous surgical technique improve outcomes.
