

# Anisometropia

**Anisometropia** (from Greek: *anisos* "unequal," *metron* "measure," *ops* "eye") is a clinically significant binocular refractive condition in which there is a meaningful difference in the [[refractive error]] between the two eyes. While minor interocular refractive differences are nearly universal in the general population, anisometropia is conventionally defined as a difference of **≥1.00 diopter (D)** in [[spherical equivalent]] between the right and left eyes, though many authorities reserve clinical significance for differences of **≥2.00 D** or greater, particularly when evaluating risk for [[amblyopia]] in pediatric patients. Anisometropia represents one of the most common causes of [[refractive amblyopia]] in children and is a frequent source of [[asthenopia]], impaired [[binocular vision]], and reduced [[stereopsis]] in patients of all ages. Its management is a cornerstone of clinical [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]], requiring careful consideration of optical, pharmacological, and surgical interventions.

---

## Epidemiology

Anisometropia is remarkably prevalent across all age groups. Population-based studies have reported the following approximate prevalence rates:

| **Population** | **Prevalence (≥1.00 D)** | **Prevalence (≥2.00 D)** |
|---|---|---|
| Neonates / Infants | 25–50% | 5–10% |
| Children (ages 6–12) | 3–6% | 1–2% |
| Adults (ages 20–40) | 5–10% | 2–4% |
| Older Adults (>60 years) | 10–20% | 5–10% |

Notably, a high degree of anisometropia is common in neonates due to the immature state of [[emmetropization]], but this typically resolves during the first years of life through active refractive development. Persistent or increasing anisometropia in childhood is of particular clinical concern due to its strong association with [[amblyopia]]. In older adults, anisometropia may increase due to asymmetric progression of [[nuclear sclerotic cataract]], [[lenticular]] changes, or following unilateral [[cataract surgery]].

The Multi-Ethnic Pediatric Eye Disease Study (MEPEDS) and the Baltimore Pediatric Eye Disease Study (BPEDS) have both confirmed that anisometropia is a leading risk factor for amblyopia, second only to [[strabismus]] in many populations.

---

## Classification

Anisometropia is classified according to the type and magnitude of the interocular refractive difference:

### By Type of Refractive Error

| **Subtype** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Simple anisometropia** | One eye is [[emmetropic]] and the other eye is either [[myopic]] or [[hyperopic]] |
| **Compound anisometropia** | Both eyes have the same type of refractive error (both myopic or both hyperopic), but to different degrees |
| **Mixed anisometropia** (Antimetropia) | One eye is myopic and the other is hyperopic |
| **Simple anisometropic astigmatism** | One eye has no [[astigmatism]] while the other has a clinically significant cylindrical error |
| **Compound anisometropic astigmatism** | Both eyes have astigmatism but of different magnitudes or axes |

### By Magnitude

| **Degree** | **Spherical Equivalent Difference** |
|---|---|
| Low / Mild | 1.00–2.00 D |
| Moderate | 2.00–3.00 D |
| High / Significant | 3.00–6.00 D |
| Very High / Extreme | >6.00 D |

### Special Subtypes

- **Anisomyopia**: Anisometropia in which both eyes are myopic but with a significant interocular difference.
- **Anisohyperopia (Anisohypermetropia)**: Anisometropia in which both eyes are hyperopic but with a significant interocular difference. This subtype carries the highest risk for [[refractive amblyopia]] in children because the more hyperopic eye may never receive a clear retinal image even with [[accommodation]].
- **Antimetropia**: One eye is myopic and the other is hyperopic. This is the rarest subtype but can be particularly challenging to manage optically.

## Pathophysiology

### Optical Basis

The refractive state of the eye is determined by the combined optical power of the [[cornea]], the [[crystalline lens]], the depth of the [[anterior chamber]], and the [[axial length]] of the globe. Anisometropia arises when one or more of these parameters differ significantly between the two eyes. The two principal mechanisms are:

1. **Axial anisometropia**: The most common form, in which the interocular refractive difference is primarily attributable to a difference in [[axial length]]. Approximately 1 mm of axial length difference corresponds to roughly 2.50–3.00 D of refractive difference. Axial anisometropia is the predominant mechanism in developmental and myopic anisometropia.

2. **Refractive (index/curvature) anisometropia**: The interocular difference is due to asymmetry in the curvature of the cornea or lens, or in the refractive index of the crystalline lens. This is more commonly seen in conditions such as [[keratoconus]], unilateral [[lenticonus]], or asymmetric [[nuclear sclerosis]].

In clinical practice, most cases of anisometropia involve a combination of both axial and refractive components, though one mechanism typically predominates.

### Emmetropization and Development

The process of [[emmetropization]]—the coordinated growth of the eye toward a refractive state near emmetropia—is driven by visual feedback mechanisms. In normal development, both eyes undergo emmetropization in a largely symmetric fashion. Anisometropia may result from:

- **Failure of emmetropization** in one eye (e.g., due to unilateral form deprivation from [[ptosis]], [[congenital cataract]], or [[corneal opacity]])
- **Asymmetric emmetropization**, where one eye achieves emmetropia while the other stabilizes at a different refractive endpoint
- **Genetic predisposition**, as twin studies and family-based studies have demonstrated a heritable component to anisometropia
- **Acquired factors**, including unilateral pathology (e.g., [[posterior staphyloma]], [[retinal detachment]] surgery, unilateral [[scleral buckle]])

Animal models (particularly chick and primate studies) have demonstrated that the emmetropization process is locally regulated within each eye, which explains why the two eyes can develop different refractive errors despite sharing the same visual environment.

### Neurophysiological Consequences

The most critical pathophysiological consequence of anisometropia is its impact on [[binocular vision]] and cortical visual development:

- **Aniseikonia**: Because the two eyes have different refractive errors, the retinal image sizes differ (particularly in axial anisometropia corrected with [[spectacle lenses]]). A difference in retinal image size of more than approximately **3–5%** can impair [[fusion]] and [[stereopsis]]. The relationship between anisometropia and [[aniseikonia]] depends on whether the anisometropia is axial or refractive in origin (see Knapp's Rule below).

- **Differential defocus**: In uncorrected anisometropia, one eye consistently receives a more defocused retinal image than the other. The visual cortex may suppress the input from the more defocused eye, leading to [[suppression]] and, in children during the [[critical period]] of visual development (typically birth to approximately 7–8 years), [[amblyopia]].

- **Amblyopia**: Anisometropic amblyopia is a form of [[refractive amblyopia]] that develops because the brain preferentially processes the clearer image from the less ametropic eye and suppresses the blurred image from the more ametropic eye. This is particularly insidious because, unlike [[strabismic amblyopia]], there is often no visible misalignment of the eyes, and the condition may go undetected without formal vision screening. Anisohyperopia carries a higher amblyogenic risk than anisomyopia because the hyperopic eye cannot achieve clear focus at any distance without accommodative effort, whereas the myopic eye may still have clear near vision.

- **Reduced stereopsis**: Even in the absence of frank amblyopia, anisometropia can degrade [[stereoacuity]] due to interocular differences in image clarity, size, and contrast.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

The clinical presentation of anisometropia varies considerably depending on the patient's age, the magnitude and type of the refractive difference, and whether the condition is corrected.

### Pediatric Patients

- Often **asymptomatic** because the child may not recognize that vision is abnormal in one eye
- Detected on routine [[vision screening]] or comprehensive [[pediatric eye examination]]
- May present with **reduced visual acuity** in one eye (the more ametropic eye)
- [[Amblyopia]] may be the presenting finding
- Rarely, may present with [[strabismus]] secondary to disrupted binocular vision

### Adult Patients

Symptoms in adults are more varied and may include:

| **Symptom** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|
| [[Asthenopia]] (eye strain) | Unequal accommodative demand; difficulty maintaining fusion |
| [[Diplopia]] (double vision) | Failure of fusion due to aniseikonia or differential prismatic effects |
| Headaches | Sustained effort to maintain binocular single vision |
| Difficulty with depth perception | Reduced [[stereopsis]] from image size/clarity disparity |
| Blurred vision (one or both eyes) | Uncorrected or undercorrected refractive error |
| Dizziness or spatial disorientation | Differential magnification and prismatic effects, especially with new spectacle correction |
| Intolerance to spectacle correction | Aniseikonia and differential prismatic effects through spectacle lenses |

### Signs on Examination

- **Unequal [[visual acuity]]** between the two eyes (with or without correction)
- **Reduced [[stereoacuity]]** on testing (e.g., [[Titmus stereo test]], [[Randot stereo test]], [[TNO stereo test]])
- **Suppression** of the more ametropic eye on [[Worth 4-dot test]] or [[Bagolini striated lenses]]
- **Amblyopia** (best-corrected visual acuity reduced in the more ametropic eye, in the absence of structural pathology)
- **Aniseikonia** demonstrable on specialized testing (e.g., [[New Aniseikonia Test]], [[Space Eikonometer]])

## Diagnosis

### Comprehensive Refraction

The cornerstone of diagnosing anisometropia is an accurate, thorough [[refraction]] of both eyes:

- **[[Cycloplegic refraction]]**: Essential in pediatric patients and strongly recommended in young adults to eliminate the confounding effect of [[accommodation]]. Standard cycloplegic agents include [[cyclopentolate]] 1% and [[atropine]] 1% (the latter particularly in young children with dark irides or suspected high hyperopia).
- **[[Manifest refraction]]**: Performed in cooperative adults; should be confirmed with [[retinoscopy]] or [[autorefraction]] for accuracy.
- **[[Retinoscopy]]**: Provides an objective measure of refractive error and is particularly valuable in preverbal children, patients with intellectual disabilities, or those unable to provide reliable subjective responses.
- **[[Autorefraction]]**: Useful as a screening tool and for confirming subjective findings, though cycloplegia is still required in children.

### Binocular Vision Assessment

A comprehensive binocular vision evaluation is critical in all patients with anisometropia:

- **[[Cover test]]** (unilateral and alternating): To detect any associated [[strabismus]] or [[phoria]]
- **[[Stereoacuity]] testing**: Quantitative assessment of depth perception (e.g., [[Titmus]], [[Randot]], [[Frisby]])
- **[[Sensory fusion]] testing**: [[Worth 4-dot test]], [[Bagolini striated lenses]]
- **[[Accommodative]] function**: [[Accommodative amplitude]], [[accommodative facility]], [[accommodative response]] (e.g., [[MEM retinoscopy]], [[Nott retinoscopy]])
- **[[Vergence]] testing**: [[Near point of convergence]], [[fusional vergence ranges]]

### Aniseikonia Assessment

When anisometropia is significant (particularly >2.00 D), formal aniseikonia testing should be considered:

- **[[New Aniseikonia Test (NAT)]]**: A clinical test using red-green dissociation to quantify perceived image size differences
- **[[Space Eikonometer]]**: A more precise but less commonly available instrument
- **Subjective comparison methods**: Direct comparison of perceived image sizes using monocular alternation

### Biometry and Imaging

In cases where the etiology of anisometropia is unclear, or when surgical correction is being considered:

- **[[Optical biometry]]** (e.g., [[IOLMaster]], [[Lenstar]]): Measures [[axial length]], [[anterior chamber depth]], [[keratometry]], and [[lens thickness]] to determine whether the anisometropia is axial or refractive in origin
- **[[A-scan ultrasonography]]**: Alternative method for axial length measurement
- **[[Corneal topography]]** / **[[Tomography]]** (e.g., [[Pentacam]], [[Orbscan]]): To evaluate corneal curvature asymmetry, rule out [[keratoconus]] or other [[ectatic disorders]]
- **[[Optical coherence tomography (OCT)]]**: To rule out structural retinal or optic nerve pathology as a cause of reduced vision

### Differential Diagnosis

When evaluating a patient with apparent anisometropia, the clinician must consider and exclude other causes of interocular visual acuity difference:

- [[Amblyopia]] (which may coexist with or result from anisometropia)
- [[Strabismus]]
- Unilateral [[media opacity]] (e.g., [[cataract]], [[corneal scar]])
- [[Keratoconus]] or other [[corneal ectasia]]
- Unilateral [[macular pathology]] (e.g., [[epiretinal membrane]], [[macular hole]])
- [[Optic neuropathy]]
- [[Retinal detachment]] or history of retinal surgery

## Knapp's Rule and Optical Considerations

### Knapp's Rule

**[[Knapp's Rule]]** is a fundamental optical principle in the management of anisometropia. It states that:

> *In purely axial anisometropia, a spectacle lens placed at the anterior focal point of the eye (approximately 15–17 mm from the cornea) will produce a retinal image of the same size in both eyes, thereby minimizing aniseikonia.*

Conversely, in purely **refractive anisometropia** (where the interocular difference is due to corneal or lenticular power rather than axial length), a [[contact lens]] placed on the cornea will minimize aniseikonia more effectively than a spectacle lens.

In clinical practice, most anisometropia is predominantly axial, which means that spectacle correction theoretically produces less aniseikonia than contact lens correction. However, the practical application of Knapp's Rule is complicated by several factors:

- Most anisometropia has both axial and refractive components
- Spectacle lenses introduce differential [[prismatic effects]] (especially in peripheral gaze), which can be more disruptive than the aniseikonia they minimize
- Patient comfort and compliance often favor contact lenses despite theoretical optical disadvantages

### Spectacle-Induced Aniseikonia and Prismatic Effects

The magnification produced by a spectacle lens is approximately **1.5% per diopter** for a standard spectacle lens at a 12–15 mm [[vertex distance]]. Therefore:

| **Anisometropia (D)** | **Approximate Aniseikonia (%)** |
|---|---|
| 1.00 D | ~1.5% |
| 2.00 D | ~3.0% |
| 3.00 D | ~4.5% |
| 4.00 D | ~6.0% |

Aniseikonia exceeding **3–5%** is generally considered the threshold at which binocular fusion becomes difficult, and symptoms such as asthenopia, headache, and spatial distortion become prominent. Additionally, by [[Prentice's Rule]], differential prismatic effects increase with gaze eccentricity, causing further disruption of binocular alignment in peripheral gaze.

## Management and Treatment

The management of anisometropia is guided by the patient's age, the magnitude and type of the refractive difference, the presence or absence of amblyopia, and the patient's visual demands and tolerance.

### 1. Spectacle Correction

Spectacles remain the first-line correction for many patients with anisometropia, particularly children:

**Advantages:**
- Non-invasive, readily available, and easily modified
- Appropriate for young children who cannot manage contact lenses
- In axial anisometropia, spectacle correction may minimize aniseikonia per Knapp's Rule

**Limitations:**
- Differential magnification (aniseikonia) becomes problematic above ~3.00 D difference
- Differential prismatic effects in peripheral gaze
- Cosmetically unacceptable with large interocular power differences (lens thickness asymmetry)
- May be poorly tolerated in adults with newly diagnosed or newly corrected high anisometropia

**Optical Strategies to Reduce Aniseikonia in Spectacles:**
- Use of **high-index lens materials** to reduce thickness and weight asymmetry
- **Iseikonic lens design**: Manipulation of lens parameters (base curve, center thickness, vertex distance) to equalize magnification between the two lenses. This is rarely done in modern practice due to cost and complexity but remains a valid optical solution.
- **Slab-off prism** (bicentric grinding): Used in multifocal lenses to compensate for differential prismatic effects in the reading position

### 2. Contact Lens Correction

[[Contact lenses]] are frequently the preferred optical correction for moderate to high anisometropia:

**Advantages:**
- Dramatically reduce aniseikonia compared to spectacles (because the lens moves with the eye and is closer to the nodal point)
- Eliminate differential prismatic effects in peripheral gaze
- Provide a wider, more natural field of view
- Cosmetically superior

**Limitations:**
- Require adequate manual dexterity and compliance with care regimens
- May not be suitable for very young children (though [[rigid gas permeable (RGP)]] lenses and [[soft contact lenses]] can be fitted in infants and toddlers by experienced practitioners)
- Risk of [[contact lens-related complications]] (e.g., [[microbial keratitis]], [[corneal neovascularization]])
- In purely axial anisometropia, contact lenses may theoretically increase aniseikonia (per Knapp's Rule), though in practice this is rarely clinically significant

**Contact Lens Options:**
- **[[Soft contact lenses]]**: Most commonly prescribed; available in a wide range of powers including [[toric]] designs for astigmatic anisometropia
- **[[Rigid gas permeable (RGP) lenses]]**: Provide superior optical quality, particularly in cases with irregular astigmatism or corneal asymmetry
- **[[Orthokeratology]]**: Overnight wear of specially designed RGP lenses to temporarily reshape the cornea; may be considered in selected cases of anisomyopia
- **[[Scleral lenses]]**: For complex cases with corneal irregularity

### 3. Amblyopia Treatment (Pediatric Patients)

In children with anisometropic amblyopia, correction of the refractive error alone is the essential first step, but additional amblyopia therapy is usually required:

- **Full refractive correction**: Prescribe the full cycloplegic refraction (or near-full correction) in both eyes. Studies such as the Pediatric Eye Disease Investigator Group (PEDIG) trials have demonstrated that refractive correction alone can improve visual acuity in the amblyopic eye in many children with anisometropic amblyopia, a phenomenon termed **"refractive adaptation"** or **"optical treatment."**

- **[[Occlusion therapy]] (patching)**: The gold standard for amblyopia treatment. The sound eye is patched for a prescribed number of hours per day to force use of the amblyopic eye. PEDIG studies have shown that:
  - For moderate amblyopia (20/40 to 20/80): 2 hours/day of patching is as effective as 6 hours/day
  - For severe amblyopia (20/100 to 20/400): 6 hours/day of patching is as effective as full-time patching

- **[[Atropine penalization]]**: Instillation of [[atropine]] 1% in the sound eye to blur near vision and encourage use of the amblyopic eye. PEDIG studies have demonstrated that weekend atropine penalization is effective for moderate amblyopia and may improve compliance compared to patching.

- **[[Bangerter filters]]**: Graded translucent filters applied to the spectacle lens of the sound eye to degrade its image and promote use of the amblyopic eye.

- **Binocular treatment approaches**: Emerging evidence supports the use of dichoptic training (e.g., binocular iPad games, [[virtual reality]]-based therapies) that present different contrast levels to each eye to promote binocular integration. While promising, these approaches are not yet standard of care.

### 4. Pharmacological Interventions

Pharmacological agents play a supportive role in the management of anisometropia:

| **Agent** | **Role** |
|---|---|
| [[Atropine]] 1% | Penalization of the sound eye in amblyopia treatment |
| [[Cyclopentolate]] 1% | Cycloplegic refraction for accurate diagnosis |
| [[Tropicamide]] 1% | Mydriasis for fundus examination; limited cycloplegic effect |
| Low-dose [[atropine]] (0.01–0.05%) | Under investigation for [[myopia control]]; may have a role in slowing asymmetric myopia progression |

### 5. Surgical Approaches

Surgical intervention may be considered in selected cases of anisometropia, particularly when optical correction is inadequate or poorly tolerated:

#### a. Refractive Surgery

- **[[LASIK]] (Laser-Assisted In Situ Keratomileusis)**: Can correct myopic, hyperopic, and astigmatic anisometropia in adults with stable refraction. Particularly effective for reducing spectacle dependence and eliminating aniseikonia. Contraindicated in patients with [[keratoconus]], thin corneas, or unstable refraction.

- **[[PRK]] (Photorefractive Keratectomy)**: Surface ablation alternative to LASIK; preferred in patients with thinner corneas or those at higher risk for flap complications.

- **[[SMILE]] (Small Incision Lenticule Extraction)**: A flapless refractive procedure for myopic anisometropia; offers potential advantages in corneal biomechanical stability.

- **[[Phakic intraocular lens (IOL)]] implantation** (e.g., [[Visian ICL]], [[Artisan/Artiflex]]): For high anisometropia beyond the range of corneal refractive surgery. Particularly useful in young patients with very high myopic anisometropia.

#### b. Clear Lens Extraction / Refractive Lens Exchange

- **[[Refractive lens exchange (RLE)]]**: Removal of the clear crystalline lens and implantation of an [[intraocular lens (IOL)]] calculated to achieve the desired refractive outcome. Considered in older patients (typically >45 years) with high anisometropia, particularly when [[presbyopia]] is also a factor. Carries risks of [[retinal detachment]] (especially in highly myopic eyes), [[posterior capsule opacification]], and [[endophthalmitis]].

#### c. Cataract Surgery

- Unilateral or bilateral [[cataract surgery]] with appropriate [[IOL]] power calculation is a common cause of iatrogenic anisometropia if biometry or IOL selection is inaccurate. Conversely, cataract surgery provides an opportunity to correct pre-existing anisometropia by selecting IOL powers that minimize the interocular refractive difference.

- **Monovision IOL strategy**: In some patients, intentional mild anisometropia (typically ~1.00–1.50 D) is induced at the time of cataract surgery to provide one eye focused for distance and the other for near ([[monovision]]). This must be carefully discussed with the patient and ideally trialed with contact lenses preoperatively.

### 6. Myopia Control Considerations

In children with progressive anisomyopia, asymmetric myopia progression may worsen anisometropia over time. [[Myopia control]] strategies may be considered to slow progression in the more myopic eye:

- **Low-dose [[atropine]]** (0.01–0.05%)
- **[[Orthokeratology]]**
- **[[Multifocal soft contact lenses]]** (e.g., center-distance designs with peripheral plus)
- **Increased outdoor time**
- **[[Specially designed spectacle lenses]]** (e.g., [[DIMS lenses]], [[HAL lenses]])

The application of myopia control specifically to reduce anisometropia progression is an area of active research.

## Prognosis

The prognosis for anisometropia depends on several factors:

- **Age at detection and treatment**: Early detection and correction in childhood (ideally before age 3–4 years) offers the best prognosis for preventing or reversing amblyopia. Treatment initiated after the critical period (approximately 7–8 years) is less effective but may still yield improvement, particularly in older children and even some adults.

- **Magnitude of anisometropia**: Higher degrees of anisometropia are associated with deeper amblyopia and greater difficulty achieving binocular fusion.

- **Compliance with treatment**: Adherence to spectacle or contact lens wear, patching, or atropine penalization is the single most important modifiable factor in outcomes.

- **Type of anisometropia**: Anisohyperopia carries a worse prognosis for amblyopia than anisomyopia, as the hyperopic eye is defocused at all distances.

With appropriate and timely management, many patients with anisometropia achieve excellent visual outcomes, including restoration of binocular vision and stereopsis.

## Complications and Sequelae

| **Complication** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| [[Amblyopia]] | Most significant complication in children; may be irreversible if untreated |
| Reduced [[stereopsis]] | Impaired depth perception even with optical correction |
| [[Strabismus]] | May develop secondary to disrupted binocular vision |
| [[Aniseikonia]] | Persistent image size difference despite correction; may limit tolerance to optical correction |
| Psychosocial impact | Reduced self-esteem, difficulty with sports and activities requiring depth perception |
| Spectacle intolerance | Inability to tolerate full correction in spectacles due to aniseikonia and prismatic effects |

## Special Considerations

### Post-Surgical Anisometropia

Anisometropia may be iatrogenically induced following:
- Unilateral [[cataract surgery]] (especially if the fellow eye is not operated or if there is a refractive surprise)
- [[Refractive surgery]] with asymmetric outcomes
- Unilateral [[scleral buckle]] or [[vitrectomy]] (which may alter axial length or lens position)
- [[Corneal transplantation]] ([[penetrating keratoplasty]], [[DALK]])

In these cases, management follows the same principles outlined above, with contact lenses or secondary surgical intervention often required.

### Anisometropia in Presbyopia

The onset of [[presbyopia]] adds complexity to the management of anisometropia, as the patient loses the ability to accommodate differentially. Conversely, some patients with mild anisometropia (particularly those with one eye slightly myopic) may find that they have a natural "monovision" arrangement that partially compensates for presbyopia.

### Screening Recommendations

Given the insidious nature of anisometropic amblyopia, professional organizations including the [[American Academy of Ophthalmology (AAO)]], the [[American Optometric Association (AOA)]], and the [[American Association for Pediatric Ophthalmology and Strabismus (AAPOS)]] recommend:

- Vision screening in all children by age 3–5 years
- Comprehensive eye examination in children with risk factors (family history of amblyopia, strabismus, or high refractive error)
- Use of instrument-based screening (e.g., [[photoscreening]], [[autorefraction]]) in preverbal children to detect significant anisometropia

---

## Summary

Anisometropia is a common and clinically important binocular refractive condition with significant implications for visual development, binocular function, and quality of life. Its management requires a thorough understanding of optics, visual development, and the full spectrum of optical, pharmacological, and surgical interventions. Early detection through comprehensive vision screening and timely, appropriate treatment are essential to prevent amblyopia and optimize visual outcomes. The practicing [[optometrist]] and [[ophthalmologist]] must be adept at diagnosing anisometropia, assessing its impact on binocular vision, and tailoring management to the individual patient's needs.
