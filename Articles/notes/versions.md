

# Versions

**Versions** are conjugate (yoked) binocular eye movements in which both eyes move simultaneously in the same direction by equal amounts. They represent one of the fundamental categories of [[ocular motility]] and are essential for maintaining [[binocular vision]], spatial orientation, and efficient visual scanning of the environment. Clinically, the assessment of versions is a cornerstone of the [[extraocular muscle]] evaluation, the diagnosis of [[strabismus]], [[cranial nerve]] palsies, and a wide spectrum of neuro-ophthalmic and orbital disorders.

Versions are governed by precise neurological control mechanisms, including [[Hering's law of equal innervation]], which states that equal and simultaneous innervation flows to [[yoked muscles]] (contralateral synergists) during any conjugate eye movement. Disruption of versional movements—whether from supranuclear, nuclear, infranuclear, neuromuscular, or mechanical causes—produces characteristic patterns of motility deficit that are of paramount diagnostic importance in clinical optometry and ophthalmology.

---

## Terminology and Classification

### Definition and Distinction from Other Eye Movements

It is critical to distinguish **versions** from **[[vergences]]**. Versions are *conjugate* movements (both eyes move in the same direction), whereas vergences are *disconjugate* movements (the eyes move in opposite directions, as in [[convergence]] and [[divergence]]). Additionally, versions differ from [[ductions]], which refer to monocular rotations of a single eye.

### Named Versional Movements

Each direction of conjugate gaze is assigned a specific clinical term:

| **Term** | **Direction of Gaze** | **Primary Yoked Muscle Pair** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Dextroversion]]** | Both eyes move to the right | Right [[lateral rectus]] (CNVI) & Left [[medial rectus]] (CNIII) |
| **[[Levoversion]]** | Both eyes move to the left | Left [[lateral rectus]] (CNVI) & Right [[medial rectus]] (CNIII) |
| **[[Supraversion]]** (Sursumversion) | Both eyes move upward | Bilateral [[superior rectus]] (CNIII) & [[inferior oblique]] (CNIII) |
| **[[Infraversion]]** (Deorsumversion) | Both eyes move downward | Bilateral [[inferior rectus]] (CNIII) & [[superior oblique]] (CNIV) |
| **Dextrocycloversion** | Both eyes intort (right) / extort (left) | Right [[superior oblique]] & Left [[inferior oblique]] |
| **Levocycloversion** | Both eyes intort (left) / extort (right) | Left [[superior oblique]] & Right [[inferior oblique]] |

In clinical practice, the six cardinal positions of gaze—right, left, up-right, up-left, down-right, and down-left—are the standard positions tested during version assessment, as each position preferentially isolates the action of a specific [[extraocular muscle]].

### The Nine Diagnostic Positions of Gaze

The full versional assessment typically employs the **nine diagnostic positions of gaze**: primary position (straight ahead), the six cardinal positions, and direct upgaze and downgaze. These positions allow the clinician to systematically evaluate the function of all twelve extraocular muscles (six per eye) and the integrity of [[cranial nerve III (oculomotor)]], [[cranial nerve IV (trochlear)]], and [[cranial nerve VI (abducens)]].

## Anatomy and Neuroanatomy

### Extraocular Muscles

Each eye is controlled by six [[extraocular muscles]] (EOMs):

| **Muscle** | **Primary Action** | **Innervation** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Medial rectus]] | [[Adduction]] | [[Oculomotor nerve]] (CN III) |
| [[Lateral rectus]] | [[Abduction]] | [[Abducens nerve]] (CN VI) |
| [[Superior rectus]] | [[Elevation]] (in abduction) | [[Oculomotor nerve]] (CN III) |
| [[Inferior rectus]] | [[Depression]] (in abduction) | [[Oculomotor nerve]] (CN III) |
| [[Superior oblique]] | [[Intorsion]], depression (in adduction) | [[Trochlear nerve]] (CN IV) |
| [[Inferior oblique]] | [[Extorsion]], elevation (in adduction) | [[Oculomotor nerve]] (CN III) |

The coordinated contraction and relaxation of these muscles in both eyes simultaneously produces smooth, accurate versional movements.

### Yoked Muscles and Hering's Law

**[[Hering's law of equal innervation]]** is the foundational neurological principle governing versions. It states that during any conjugate eye movement, equal innervation is sent to the yoked muscles—the agonist muscle in one eye and its contralateral synergist in the other eye. For example, during dextroversion, the right lateral rectus and the left medial rectus receive equal and simultaneous innervation.

The corollary, **[[Sherrington's law of reciprocal innervation]]**, states that increased innervation to an agonist muscle is accompanied by a corresponding decrease in innervation (inhibition) to its ipsilateral antagonist. Together, these two laws explain the precise coordination of all twelve extraocular muscles during versional movements.

### Supranuclear Control of Versions

Versional eye movements are generated and modulated by a complex hierarchy of supranuclear structures:

- **[[Frontal eye fields]] (FEF):** Located in the [[frontal lobe]] (Brodmann area 8), the FEF generate contralateral voluntary [[saccades]]—rapid versional movements used to redirect gaze.
- **[[Parietal-occipital-temporal junction]]:** Involved in the generation of ipsilateral [[smooth pursuit]] movements—slow versional movements used to track a moving target.
- **[[Superior colliculus]]:** A midbrain structure that integrates visual and other sensory inputs to coordinate reflexive saccadic versions.
- **[[Paramedian pontine reticular formation]] (PPRF):** The immediate premotor center (horizontal gaze center) for horizontal saccadic versions. Neurons in the PPRF project to the ipsilateral [[abducens nucleus]].
- **[[Rostral interstitial nucleus of the medial longitudinal fasciculus]] (riMLF):** The premotor center for vertical saccadic versions, located in the midbrain.
- **[[Medial longitudinal fasciculus]] (MLF):** A critical white matter tract connecting the [[abducens nucleus]] to the contralateral [[oculomotor nucleus]], enabling coordinated horizontal versions. Lesions of the MLF produce [[internuclear ophthalmoplegia]] (INO).
- **[[Vestibular nuclei]]:** Generate compensatory versional eye movements (the [[vestibulo-ocular reflex]], VOR) in response to head movement.
- **[[Cerebellum]]:** Modulates the accuracy, velocity, and smoothness of all types of versional movements. Cerebellar lesions produce [[dysmetric saccades]], [[gaze-evoked nystagmus]], and impaired smooth pursuit.

### Types of Versional Eye Movements

Versions encompass several physiologically distinct subtypes:

1. **[[Saccades]]:** Rapid, ballistic conjugate eye movements that redirect the [[fovea]] to a new target. Velocities can reach 500–700°/sec.
2. **[[Smooth pursuit]]:** Slow, tracking conjugate movements that maintain the image of a moving target on the fovea. Maximum velocity is approximately 30–100°/sec.
3. **[[Vestibulo-ocular reflex]] (VOR):** Compensatory conjugate eye movements driven by the [[vestibular system]] to stabilize retinal images during head rotation.
4. **[[Optokinetic nystagmus]] (OKN):** A combination of smooth pursuit and saccadic resetting movements elicited by a large moving visual field.

## Pathophysiology

Abnormalities of versions arise from disruption at any level of the neural pathway—from the cerebral cortex to the extraocular muscles themselves. The pathophysiology can be broadly categorized as follows:

### Supranuclear Lesions

Lesions above the level of the ocular motor nuclei produce characteristic version abnormalities:

- **Frontal eye field lesions** (e.g., stroke): Acute lesions cause a transient conjugate gaze deviation *toward* the side of the lesion (the eyes "look at the lesion") due to loss of contralateral saccade generation. Irritative lesions (e.g., seizures) drive the eyes *away* from the lesion.
- **PPRF lesions:** Produce an ipsilateral horizontal [[gaze palsy]]—inability to generate saccades toward the side of the lesion.
- **riMLF lesions:** Produce vertical gaze palsies, particularly affecting upgaze or downgaze saccades.
- **[[Dorsal midbrain syndrome]] (Parinaud syndrome):** Compression or damage to the [[posterior commissure]] and dorsal midbrain (e.g., by [[pinealoma]]) causes upgaze palsy, convergence-retraction nystagmus, light-near dissociation of the pupils, and lid retraction ([[Collier sign]]).
- **[[Progressive supranuclear palsy]] (PSP):** A neurodegenerative condition causing progressive vertical gaze palsy (initially downgaze), followed by horizontal gaze impairment.

### Nuclear and Fascicular Lesions

- **[[Abducens nucleus]] lesion:** Produces an ipsilateral conjugate horizontal gaze palsy (not merely a lateral rectus palsy), because the abducens nucleus contains both motor neurons to the ipsilateral lateral rectus and interneurons projecting via the MLF to the contralateral medial rectus subnucleus.
- **[[Oculomotor nucleus]] lesion:** Produces complex patterns of motility deficit, often bilateral, due to the unique subnuclear organization (e.g., the superior rectus subnucleus innervates the contralateral superior rectus; the [[levator palpebrae superioris]] subnucleus is a single midline structure).

### Internuclear Lesions

- **[[Internuclear ophthalmoplegia]] (INO):** A lesion of the [[MLF]] between the abducens nucleus and the contralateral oculomotor nucleus produces impaired adduction of the ipsilateral eye during contralateral gaze, with [[abducting nystagmus]] of the contralateral eye. Bilateral INO is highly suggestive of [[multiple sclerosis]] in younger patients and ischemia in older patients.
- **[[One-and-a-half syndrome]]:** A combined lesion of the ipsilateral PPRF (or abducens nucleus) and the ipsilateral MLF produces a complete ipsilateral horizontal gaze palsy plus an INO on contralateral gaze. The only remaining horizontal movement is abduction of the contralateral eye.

### Infranuclear (Peripheral) Lesions

- **[[Cranial nerve III palsy]]:** Produces deficits in adduction, elevation, depression (partial), and intorsion of the affected eye, along with [[ptosis]] and potentially a fixed, dilated [[pupil]]. Versions are asymmetric in all fields except abduction.
- **[[Cranial nerve IV palsy]]:** Produces [[hypertropia]] of the affected eye, worse in contralateral gaze and ipsilateral head tilt ([[Parks-Bielschowsky three-step test]]). Versions reveal underaction of the [[superior oblique]] and often overaction of the ipsilateral [[inferior oblique]].
- **[[Cranial nerve VI palsy]]:** Produces an [[esotropia]] that increases on ipsilateral gaze. Versions reveal limited abduction of the affected eye.

### Neuromuscular Junction Disorders

- **[[Myasthenia gravis]]:** An autoimmune disorder affecting [[acetylcholine receptors]] at the [[neuromuscular junction]]. Ocular myasthenia can mimic virtually any pattern of version abnormality, including gaze palsies and individual muscle palsies. Fatigability is the hallmark—version deficits worsen with sustained gaze or repeated testing.

### Myopathic and Mechanical Causes

- **[[Thyroid eye disease]] (Graves ophthalmopathy):** Inflammatory infiltration and fibrosis of the extraocular muscles (most commonly the [[inferior rectus]] and [[medial rectus]]) produce restrictive version deficits, particularly limitation of upgaze and abduction.
- **[[Orbital fracture]]:** Entrapment of extraocular muscles (classically the inferior rectus in a [[blowout fracture]] of the orbital floor) mechanically restricts versions.
- **[[Chronic progressive external ophthalmoplegia]] (CPEO):** A [[mitochondrial myopathy]] causing slowly progressive, symmetric limitation of versions in all directions, often with bilateral ptosis.
- **[[Brown syndrome]]:** Restriction of the [[superior oblique]] tendon-trochlea complex limits elevation in adduction.
- **[[Duane retraction syndrome]]:** A congenital cranial dysinnervation disorder with co-contraction of the lateral and medial rectus muscles, producing characteristic version abnormalities including limited abduction (Type I), limited adduction (Type II), or both (Type III), with globe retraction and palpebral fissure narrowing on attempted adduction.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Patients with version abnormalities may present with a wide range of symptoms, depending on the etiology, acuity of onset, and degree of deficit:

### Primary Symptoms

- **[[Diplopia]] (double vision):** The most common presenting symptom of acquired version abnormalities. Binocular diplopia resolves with occlusion of either eye and results from misalignment of the visual axes. The diplopia is typically worse in the field of action of the affected muscle(s).
- **[[Asthenopia]]:** Eye strain, headache, and fatigue, particularly with sustained gaze or reading.
- **Blurred vision:** May occur if the version deficit causes the image to fall off the [[fovea]].

### Compensatory Signs

- **[[Abnormal head posture]] (AHP):** Patients frequently adopt a compensatory head turn, tilt, or chin elevation/depression to place the eyes in a gaze position where the version deficit is minimized and binocular single vision is maintained. For example, a patient with a right lateral rectus palsy may adopt a right face turn to avoid right gaze.
- **Squinting or closing one eye:** To eliminate diplopia.

### Associated Symptoms (Depending on Etiology)

- **[[Oscillopsia]]:** A sensation that the visual world is moving, often associated with [[nystagmus]] or vestibular dysfunction affecting the VOR.
- **Vertigo, nausea:** If the version abnormality is associated with vestibular or cerebellar pathology.
- **Ptosis, pupil abnormalities:** In CN III palsy.
- **Pain:** Orbital pain may accompany inflammatory, ischemic, or compressive etiologies.

### Congenital vs. Acquired Presentation

Congenital version abnormalities (e.g., [[Duane retraction syndrome]], [[congenital fibrosis of the extraocular muscles]]) are typically non-progressive, may be associated with amblyopia rather than diplopia, and often present with a longstanding abnormal head posture. Acquired version deficits are more likely to produce acute diplopia and require urgent investigation.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical History

A thorough history is essential and should include:

- Onset (acute vs. gradual vs. congenital)
- Monocular vs. binocular diplopia
- Direction of diplopia (horizontal, vertical, torsional)
- Variability (worse at end of day → [[myasthenia gravis]]; worse in morning → suggests elevated [[intracranial pressure]])
- Associated neurological symptoms (weakness, numbness, dysarthria, ataxia)
- History of trauma, thyroid disease, diabetes, hypertension, malignancy
- Medications

### Version Testing Technique

The clinical assessment of versions is performed as follows:

1. **Patient positioning:** The patient is seated with the head stabilized (the examiner may gently hold the patient's chin to prevent compensatory head movements).
2. **Fixation target:** A penlight, accommodative target, or small toy (in pediatric patients) is held at approximately 33 cm (near) or at distance (1 meter or more).
3. **Monocular fixation:** Both eyes are open during version testing (unlike duction testing, which is monocular).
4. **Movement through diagnostic positions:** The examiner moves the target smoothly into the six cardinal positions of gaze (and often the nine diagnostic positions), observing the range, speed, smoothness, and symmetry of movement of both eyes simultaneously.
5. **Grading:** Version abnormalities are typically graded on a clinical scale:

| **Grade** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| 0 | Full movement (normal) |
| −1 | Slight underaction (approximately 75% of normal range) |
| −2 | Moderate underaction (approximately 50% of normal range) |
| −3 | Marked underaction (approximately 25% of normal range) |
| −4 | No movement in that direction |
| +1 to +4 | Overaction (graded similarly, with +1 being slight and +4 being maximal) |

Overactions are commonly seen in the antagonist of a paretic muscle (e.g., [[inferior oblique overaction]] in CN IV palsy) or in the contralateral yoked muscle (secondary deviation per Hering's law).

### Specific Diagnostic Tests Related to Versions

- **[[Cover test]] and [[alternate cover test]]:** Performed in multiple gaze positions to quantify the deviation (using [[prism bars]] or loose prisms) and determine comitancy. An **incomitant** deviation (one that varies with gaze direction) strongly suggests a version abnormality due to a specific muscle or nerve palsy.
- **[[Parks-Bielschowsky three-step test]]:** A systematic method for identifying the paretic muscle in cases of vertical diplopia, using three steps: (1) which eye is hypertropic, (2) is the hypertropia worse in right or left gaze, and (3) is the hypertropia worse with right or left head tilt.
- **[[Maddox rod]] testing:** Used to detect and measure small deviations in various gaze positions.
- **[[Lancaster red-green test]] / [[Hess screen test]]:** Quantitative charting methods that map the field of action of each extraocular muscle, graphically demonstrating underactions and overactions. These are invaluable for documenting version deficits, monitoring progression, and planning surgical intervention.
- **[[Forced duction test]] (passive forced duction):** Performed under topical anesthesia by grasping the conjunctiva/tendon with forceps and attempting to passively rotate the globe. Restriction indicates a mechanical cause (e.g., fibrosis, entrapment), whereas free passive movement suggests a paretic or supranuclear cause.
- **[[Force generation test]] (active force generation):** The patient attempts to look in the direction of the deficit while the examiner assesses the force of contraction, helping differentiate paresis from restriction.
- **[[Saccade testing]]:** Observation of saccadic velocity, accuracy, and latency. Slow saccades suggest a nuclear or infranuclear lesion, INO, or neurodegenerative disease. Hypometric or hypermetric saccades suggest cerebellar dysfunction.
- **[[Smooth pursuit testing]]:** Assessed by having the patient track a slowly moving target. Impaired pursuit (saccadic or "cogwheel" pursuit) may indicate parietal lobe, cerebellar, or brainstem pathology.
- **[[Optokinetic nystagmus]] (OKN) testing:** Using an OKN drum or tape to assess the integrity of the pursuit and saccadic systems.

### Imaging and Laboratory Studies

When a version abnormality is identified, further investigation may be warranted:

- **[[Magnetic resonance imaging]] (MRI) of the brain and orbits:** Essential for suspected supranuclear, nuclear, or infranuclear lesions; orbital masses; demyelinating disease; or cranial nerve compression.
- **[[Computed tomography]] (CT) of the orbits:** Preferred for suspected orbital fractures or bony pathology.
- **[[MR angiography]] or [[CT angiography]]:** If a compressive aneurysm is suspected (e.g., CN III palsy with pupil involvement → posterior communicating artery aneurysm).
- **Blood work:** [[Erythrocyte sedimentation rate]] (ESR) and [[C-reactive protein]] (CRP) in patients over 50 to rule out [[giant cell arteritis]]; [[thyroid function tests]]; [[hemoglobin A1c]] for diabetes; [[acetylcholine receptor antibodies]] for myasthenia gravis.
- **[[Tensilon test]] (edrophonium test) or [[ice pack test]]:** For suspected ocular myasthenia gravis.
- **[[Electromyography]] (EMG) and [[single-fiber EMG]]:** For neuromuscular junction disorders.
- **[[Lumbar puncture]]:** If elevated intracranial pressure or infectious/inflammatory CNS disease is suspected.

## Management and Treatment

The management of version abnormalities is directed at the underlying etiology and the alleviation of symptoms (particularly diplopia).

### Observation and Monitoring

- Many acquired cranial nerve palsies (especially isolated CN IV or CN VI palsies in patients with vascular risk factors such as [[diabetes mellitus]] and [[hypertension]]) are presumed microvascular in origin and are expected to resolve spontaneously within 3–6 months. Serial version assessments and deviation measurements are performed to document recovery.
- If no improvement occurs within 3–6 months, or if atypical features are present (e.g., aberrant regeneration, multiple cranial nerve involvement, progressive deficit), neuroimaging and further workup are mandatory.

### Optical and Prismatic Management

- **[[Fresnel prisms]]:** Temporary press-on prisms applied to spectacle lenses to compensate for the deviation and restore binocular single vision. They are particularly useful during the observation period for acute palsies.
- **[[Ground-in prisms]]:** Permanent prisms incorporated into spectacle lenses for stable, small-to-moderate deviations.
- **[[Occlusion]]:** Patching or fogging one eye may be necessary to eliminate intractable diplopia, especially in the acute phase.

### Pharmacological Interventions

- **[[Botulinum toxin]] (Botox) injection:** Chemodenervation of the antagonist muscle can be used to temporarily reduce the deviation in acute cranial nerve palsies, prevent contracture of the antagonist, and facilitate recovery. It is also used diagnostically and therapeutically in certain forms of strabismus.
- **Treatment of underlying systemic disease:**
  - **[[Corticosteroids]]:** For [[giant cell arteritis]], inflammatory orbital disease, or neurosarcoidosis.
  - **[[Pyridostigmine]]:** An [[acetylcholinesterase inhibitor]] used as first-line treatment for [[myasthenia gravis]].
  - **[[Immunosuppressive agents]]:** For refractory myasthenia gravis or inflammatory conditions.
  - **Glycemic and blood pressure control:** For microvascular cranial nerve palsies.
  - **Thyroid management:** For [[thyroid eye disease]].

### Orthoptic Exercises

- In select cases, particularly those involving mild decompensation of a pre-existing phoria or mild convergence insufficiency contributing to version-related symptoms, orthoptic exercises (e.g., [[pencil push-ups]], [[vergence therapy]]) may be beneficial. However, orthoptic exercises are generally not effective for true paralytic or restrictive version deficits.

### Surgical Management

Surgical intervention is considered when:

1. The version deficit and associated strabismus are stable (typically after at least 6 months of observation for acquired palsies).
2. Spontaneous recovery has not occurred or is incomplete.
3. Prismatic correction is insufficient or cosmetically unacceptable.
4. The deviation significantly impairs quality of life.

#### Surgical Principles

- **Recession:** Weakening a muscle by moving its insertion posteriorly on the globe. Used for overacting muscles or contractured antagonists.
- **Resection:** Strengthening a muscle by removing a portion and reattaching it at the original insertion. Used for underacting muscles.
- **Transposition procedures:** Shifting the insertion of adjacent rectus muscles toward the field of action of the paretic muscle. Examples include the **[[Knapp procedure]]** (vertical rectus transposition for CN VI palsy) and the **[[Hummelsheim procedure]]**.
- **Adjustable sutures:** Allow postoperative fine-tuning of muscle position under topical anesthesia, improving surgical outcomes.
- **Inferior oblique weakening procedures:** Including myectomy, recession, or anteriorization, commonly performed for inferior oblique overaction associated with CN IV palsy or [[dissociated vertical deviation]].
- **Superior oblique tuck:** Performed for superior oblique underaction in CN IV palsy.

#### Surgical Considerations by Etiology

| **Condition** | **Typical Surgical Approach** |
|---|---|
| CN VI palsy (complete, stable) | Medial rectus recession + lateral rectus resection; or vertical rectus transposition (Knapp/Hummelsheim) with or without medial rectus recession |
| CN IV palsy (unilateral) | Ipsilateral inferior oblique weakening ± ipsilateral superior rectus recession or contralateral inferior rectus recession |
| CN III palsy (partial) | Targeted recession/resection based on residual muscle function |
| Thyroid eye disease (restrictive) | Recession of fibrotic muscles (typically inferior rectus for upgaze restriction, medial rectus for abduction restriction) |
| Duane retraction syndrome | Recession of the medial rectus (Type I) or lateral rectus (Type II/III); transposition procedures in select cases |
| Orbital fracture with entrapment | Surgical repair of the fracture with release of entrapped tissue |

### Neuro-Rehabilitation

For supranuclear version abnormalities (e.g., post-stroke gaze palsies), neuro-rehabilitation strategies including visual scanning training, saccadic exercises, and compensatory head movement strategies may improve functional outcomes.

## Prognosis

The prognosis for version abnormalities depends heavily on the underlying etiology:

- **Microvascular cranial nerve palsies:** Excellent prognosis; approximately 85–90% resolve spontaneously within 3–6 months.
- **Traumatic cranial nerve palsies:** Variable; partial palsies have a better prognosis than complete palsies.
- **Compressive lesions:** Prognosis depends on the nature of the lesion and the success of treatment (e.g., surgical decompression, radiation).
- **Myasthenia gravis:** Chronic but manageable with appropriate pharmacotherapy; ocular myasthenia may generalize in approximately 50% of patients within 2 years.
- **Thyroid eye disease:** Often stabilizes after the active inflammatory phase; surgical correction of residual restrictive strabismus is generally successful.
- **Congenital conditions (Duane syndrome, Brown syndrome):** Typically stable; surgery is performed for cosmesis or abnormal head posture rather than for cure.
- **Neurodegenerative diseases (PSP, CPEO):** Progressive; management is supportive.

---

## Summary

Versions are fundamental conjugate binocular eye movements essential for normal visual function. Their clinical assessment is a critical component of the optometric and ophthalmologic examination, providing invaluable diagnostic information about the integrity of the [[extraocular muscles]], [[cranial nerves]], [[brainstem]], and [[cerebral cortex]]. A systematic approach to version testing—combined with knowledge of the underlying neuroanatomy, Hering's and Sherrington's laws, and the differential diagnosis of version abnormalities—enables the clinician to accurately localize lesions, guide appropriate investigations, and implement effective management strategies ranging from observation and prisms to pharmacotherapy and surgery.
