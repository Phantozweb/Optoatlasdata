

# Ciliary Body

The **ciliary body** is a critical ring-shaped structure of the [[Uveal Tract]] (middle vascular coat of the eye), situated between the [[Iris]] anteriorly and the [[Choroid]] posteriorly. It is one of the most functionally significant tissues in the eye, responsible for the production of [[Aqueous Humor]], accommodation of the [[Crystalline Lens]], and maintenance of [[Intraocular Pressure]] (IOP). Pathology of the ciliary body can lead to devastating visual consequences, including [[Glaucoma]], [[Hypotony]], [[Uveitis]], and intraocular neoplasia. A thorough understanding of its anatomy, physiology, pathophysiology, and clinical relevance is indispensable for every optometrist and ophthalmologist.

---

## Gross and Microscopic Anatomy

### Topographic Anatomy

The ciliary body extends approximately **6 mm** in the sagittal plane, from the [[Scleral Spur]] anteriorly to the [[Ora Serrata]] posteriorly. It is roughly triangular in cross-section, with its base facing the [[Anterior Chamber]] and its apex tapering toward the choroid. The ciliary body is divided into two distinct zones:

| Region | Description | Width |
|---|---|---|
| **Pars Plicata** (Corona Ciliaris) | The anterior, ridged portion containing approximately 70–80 [[Ciliary Processes]]; the primary site of aqueous humor production | ~2 mm |
| **Pars Plana** (Orbiculus Ciliaris) | The posterior, flat portion; relatively avascular; a common surgical entry point for [[Pars Plana Vitrectomy]] | ~4 mm |

The ciliary body is anchored externally to the [[Sclera]] via the [[Supraciliary Space]] and internally gives rise to the [[Zonular Fibers]] (zonules of Zinn), which suspend the crystalline lens.

### Histological Layers

From external to internal, the ciliary body consists of the following layers:

1. **Supraciliary lamina** – loose connective tissue containing melanocytes, bridging the ciliary body to the sclera.
2. **Ciliary muscle** – the smooth muscle responsible for [[Accommodation]].
3. **Vascular layer (stroma)** – rich in blood vessels derived from the [[Long Posterior Ciliary Arteries]] and the [[Anterior Ciliary Arteries]].
4. **Bilayered ciliary epithelium**:
   - **Outer pigmented epithelium (PE)** – continuous with the [[Retinal Pigment Epithelium]] (RPE).
   - **Inner non-pigmented epithelium (NPE)** – continuous with the [[Neurosensory Retina]]; the actual secretory layer responsible for aqueous humor production.
5. **Internal limiting membrane (ILM)** – a thin basement membrane facing the [[Posterior Chamber]].

The apices of the pigmented and non-pigmented epithelial cells are apposed to each other, connected by [[Gap Junctions]] and [[Tight Junctions]], forming the **blood-aqueous barrier (BAB)**.

### Ciliary Muscle

The [[Ciliary Muscle]] is a smooth muscle composed of three fiber orientations:

| Fiber Orientation | Alternative Name | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Longitudinal (meridional)** | Brücke's muscle | Opens [[Trabecular Meshwork]] via scleral spur traction; facilitates aqueous outflow |
| **Radial (oblique)** | — | Transitional fibers assisting both accommodation and outflow |
| **Circular (sphincteric)** | Müller's muscle | Primary role in [[Accommodation]]; contraction relaxes zonular tension on the lens |

The ciliary muscle is innervated primarily by [[Parasympathetic]] fibers from the [[Edinger-Westphal Nucleus]], traveling via the [[Oculomotor Nerve]] (CN III) and synapsing in the [[Ciliary Ganglion]]. Postganglionic fibers reach the muscle via the [[Short Posterior Ciliary Nerves]]. Sympathetic innervation (from the [[Superior Cervical Ganglion]]) plays a minor inhibitory role.

### Vascular Supply

The ciliary body receives a dual arterial supply:

- **[[Long Posterior Ciliary Arteries]]** (two branches from the [[Ophthalmic Artery]]) – enter the eye through the sclera near the optic nerve and course anteriorly within the suprachoroidal space to supply the ciliary body and iris.
- **[[Anterior Ciliary Arteries]]** (branches from the muscular arteries supplying the [[Extraocular Muscles]]) – penetrate the sclera near the limbus and anastomose with the long posterior ciliary arteries to form the **[[Major Arterial Circle of the Iris]]** (circle of Zinn-Haller at the ciliary body level).

Venous drainage occurs via the [[Vortex Veins]].

## Physiology

### Aqueous Humor Production

The ciliary body, specifically the [[Ciliary Processes]] of the pars plicata, is the sole site of [[Aqueous Humor]] production. Aqueous humor is produced at a rate of approximately **2.0–2.5 µL/min** (total volume ~250 µL in the anterior and posterior chambers combined, with a turnover rate of approximately 1% per minute).

Three mechanisms contribute to aqueous humor formation:

| Mechanism | Contribution | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Active secretion** | ~80–90% | Energy-dependent transport by the non-pigmented ciliary epithelium (NPE); involves [[Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase]], [[Carbonic Anhydrase]], and [[Cl⁻ channels]]; the primary target of pharmacological IOP reduction |
| **Ultrafiltration** | ~10–15% | Pressure-dependent movement of plasma across the fenestrated capillaries of the ciliary processes into the stroma |
| **Diffusion** | Minor | Passive movement of lipid-soluble substances along concentration gradients |

The enzyme **[[Carbonic Anhydrase]]** (isoenzyme II) in the NPE catalyzes the formation of bicarbonate ions (HCO₃⁻), which drives sodium (Na⁺) and water transport into the posterior chamber. This is the pharmacological basis for [[Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors]] (e.g., [[Dorzolamide]], [[Brinzolamide]], [[Acetazolamide]]).

### Accommodation

The ciliary body is the effector organ of the [[Accommodation]] reflex. When the [[Ciliary Muscle]] contracts (primarily the circular Müller's fibers), the diameter of the ciliary ring decreases, releasing tension on the [[Zonular Fibers]]. This allows the elastic [[Crystalline Lens]] to assume a more spherical shape, increasing its refractive power for near vision. This mechanism is described by the **[[Helmholtz Theory of Accommodation]]**.

With aging, the lens becomes progressively sclerotic and loses elasticity, leading to [[Presbyopia]]—the age-related loss of accommodative amplitude—despite continued ciliary muscle function.

### Blood-Aqueous Barrier

The tight junctions between the non-pigmented ciliary epithelial cells, along with the non-fenestrated endothelium of the [[Iris]] vasculature, constitute the **[[Blood-Aqueous Barrier]] (BAB)**. This barrier selectively restricts the passage of large molecules (proteins, immune cells) from the blood into the aqueous humor, maintaining the immune-privileged status of the anterior segment. Disruption of the BAB is a hallmark of [[Anterior Uveitis]] and results in the clinical finding of **[[Flare]]** (protein) and **[[Cells]]** in the anterior chamber on [[Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy]].

## Pathophysiology

Pathological processes affecting the ciliary body can be broadly categorized into inflammatory, neoplastic, traumatic, degenerative, and developmental conditions.

### Inflammatory Conditions

#### Cyclitis and Anterior Uveitis

Inflammation of the ciliary body is termed **[[Cyclitis]]**. When it occurs in conjunction with [[Iritis]] (inflammation of the iris), the combined condition is called **[[Iridocyclitis]]** or **[[Anterior Uveitis]]**. Intermediate uveitis ([[Pars Planitis]]) specifically involves inflammation of the pars plana and the peripheral retina/vitreous base.

**Pathophysiological cascade:**

1. An inciting stimulus (infectious, autoimmune, traumatic, or idiopathic) triggers an immune response.
2. Inflammatory mediators (prostaglandins, cytokines, chemokines) cause vasodilation and increased vascular permeability in the ciliary body vasculature.
3. The [[Blood-Aqueous Barrier]] breaks down, allowing proteins (flare) and inflammatory cells to enter the aqueous humor.
4. Inflammatory debris may deposit on the [[Corneal Endothelium]] as [[Keratic Precipitates]] (KPs).
5. Fibrin exudation may cause [[Posterior Synechiae]] (adhesion of the iris to the anterior lens capsule) or, in severe cases, [[Seclusio Pupillae]] and [[Iris Bombé]].
6. Chronic inflammation can damage the ciliary epithelium, reducing aqueous production and leading to **[[Hypotony]]** and potentially **[[Phthisis Bulbi]]**.

Conversely, inflammation can obstruct aqueous outflow via the [[Trabecular Meshwork]] (trabeculitis) or cause peripheral anterior synechiae, leading to **secondary [[Glaucoma]]**.

#### Pars Planitis

**[[Pars Planitis]]** is a subset of [[Intermediate Uveitis]] characterized by the presence of **snowbanking** (white exudative material over the pars plana, especially inferiorly) and **snowball opacities** (vitreous inflammatory aggregates). It is often bilateral, affects young adults, and is frequently idiopathic, though associations with [[Multiple Sclerosis]] and [[Sarcoidosis]] have been reported.

### Neoplastic Conditions

#### Ciliary Body Melanoma

**[[Ciliary Body Melanoma]]** is the most clinically significant primary neoplasm of the ciliary body. It arises from the [[Uveal Melanocytes]] within the ciliary body stroma.

- **Epidemiology:** Ciliary body melanomas account for approximately **5–10%** of all [[Uveal Melanoma]] cases. They tend to present later than iris melanomas due to their concealed location.
- **Clinical features:** Sentinel vessels (dilated episcleral vessels overlying the tumor), lens subluxation, sector [[Cataract]], secondary glaucoma, and visual field defects. The tumor may be visible on [[Gonioscopy]] if it extends into the [[Anterior Chamber Angle]].
- **Diagnosis:** [[Ultrasound Biomicroscopy]] (UBM), [[B-Scan Ultrasonography]], [[Transillumination]], [[Fluorescein Angiography]], and [[MRI]] of the orbits.
- **Prognosis:** Ciliary body melanomas carry a **worse prognosis** than iris melanomas due to their larger size at diagnosis, higher mitotic rate, and greater propensity for extraocular extension and hepatic metastasis.
- **Management:** Options include [[Plaque Brachytherapy]] (e.g., iodine-125 or ruthenium-106), [[Proton Beam Radiotherapy]], local resection ([[Iridocyclectomy]]), or [[Enucleation]] for large tumors.

#### Medulloepithelioma (Diktyoma)

**[[Medulloepithelioma]]** is a rare congenital tumor arising from the non-pigmented ciliary epithelium. It is the most common primary intraocular tumor of the ciliary body in **children**. It may be benign (non-teratoid or teratoid) or malignant. Clinical features include a white or pink retrolental mass, [[Neovascular Glaucoma]], cataract, and lens subluxation. Treatment is typically [[Enucleation]] due to the risk of malignant transformation.

#### Ciliary Body Cysts

**[[Ciliary Body Cysts]]** (also called ciliary body epithelial cysts) are common, usually benign, and often incidental findings. They arise from the pigmented or non-pigmented epithelium of the pars plicata or pars plana. They may be:

- **Primary (developmental):** Usually asymptomatic; found in up to 30% of autopsy eyes.
- **Secondary:** Associated with chronic inflammation, trauma, or medications (e.g., topical [[Miotics]] such as [[Pilocarpine]], or systemic [[Sulfonamides]]).

Large or multiple cysts can cause angle narrowing and secondary [[Angle-Closure Glaucoma]]. Diagnosis is confirmed with [[Ultrasound Biomicroscopy]] (UBM), which demonstrates a thin-walled, echo-lucent structure.

### Traumatic Conditions

#### Cyclodialysis Cleft

A **[[Cyclodialysis Cleft]]** is a separation of the ciliary body from the [[Scleral Spur]], creating an abnormal communication between the [[Anterior Chamber]] and the [[Suprachoroidal Space]]. This results in markedly increased uveoscleral outflow and profound **[[Hypotony]]** (IOP often < 5 mmHg).

- **Etiology:** Blunt ocular trauma, surgical complication.
- **Consequences:** Hypotony maculopathy (chorioretinal folds, disc edema, reduced vision), choroidal effusion, shallow anterior chamber.
- **Diagnosis:** [[Gonioscopy]] (visible cleft at the angle), [[Ultrasound Biomicroscopy]], [[Anterior Segment OCT]].
- **Management:** [[Atropine]] (cycloplegia to promote ciliary body apposition to the sclera), [[Laser Photocoagulation]] (argon or diode laser to the cleft), [[Cryotherapy]], or surgical repair (direct cyclopexy).

#### Ciliary Body Detachment and Contusion

Blunt trauma can cause ciliary body contusion, edema, or hemorrhage, leading to transient or permanent reduction in aqueous humor production. Angle recession ([[Angle Recession Glaucoma]]) involves a tear between the longitudinal and circular fibers of the ciliary muscle, visible on gonioscopy as an abnormally wide [[Ciliary Body Band]].

### Degenerative and Age-Related Changes

With aging, the ciliary body undergoes:

- **Hyalinization** and thickening of the basement membranes.
- **Reduction in ciliary muscle mass** and contractility.
- **Decreased aqueous humor production** (approximately 2–3% reduction per decade after age 40).
- **Accumulation of lipofuscin** in the ciliary epithelium.

These changes contribute to [[Presbyopia]] and the age-related decline in IOP observed in some populations.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Symptoms attributable to ciliary body pathology vary depending on the underlying condition:

| Condition | Symptoms | Signs |
|---|---|---|
| **Anterior Uveitis / Cyclitis** | Pain, photophobia, redness, blurred vision, tearing | Ciliary flush, cells and flare in AC, KPs, posterior synechiae, miosis |
| **Pars Planitis** | Floaters, blurred vision (often painless) | Snowbanking, snowball opacities, vitritis, CME |
| **Ciliary Body Melanoma** | Often asymptomatic early; blurred vision, visual field defect | Sentinel vessels, lens displacement, sector cataract, elevated IOP |
| **Medulloepithelioma** | Leukocoria, decreased vision (in children) | Retrolental mass, neovascular glaucoma, cataract |
| **Cyclodialysis Cleft** | Blurred vision, history of trauma | Profound hypotony, shallow AC, choroidal folds, disc edema |
| **Ciliary Body Cysts** | Usually asymptomatic; may cause blurred vision if large | Angle narrowing, plateau iris configuration, visible cyst on gonioscopy/UBM |
| **Ciliary Spasm (Accommodative Spasm)** | Brow ache, eye strain, pseudomyopia, blurred distance vision | Miosis, fluctuating refraction, induced myopia |

**Ciliary body tenderness** can be elicited by gentle palpation over the sclera overlying the ciliary body (approximately 3–4 mm posterior to the limbus), a clinical sign suggestive of cyclitis.

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

[[Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy]] is the primary tool for evaluating the anterior segment. While the ciliary body itself is not directly visible, secondary signs of ciliary body pathology—such as anterior chamber cells and flare, keratic precipitates, posterior synechiae, and iris abnormalities—are readily assessed.

### Gonioscopy

[[Gonioscopy]] allows visualization of the [[Anterior Chamber Angle]], including the **ciliary body band** (the most posterior visible structure in an open angle). Abnormalities such as angle recession (widened ciliary body band), cyclodialysis clefts, tumors extending into the angle, and peripheral anterior synechiae can be identified.

### Ultrasound Biomicroscopy (UBM)

**[[Ultrasound Biomicroscopy]]** (typically 35–50 MHz) is the gold standard for imaging the ciliary body in vivo. It provides high-resolution cross-sectional images of the ciliary body, ciliary processes, pars plana, zonules, and posterior chamber. UBM is indispensable for:

- Evaluating ciliary body tumors (size, extent, internal reflectivity)
- Detecting ciliary body cysts
- Identifying cyclodialysis clefts
- Assessing plateau iris syndrome (anteriorly rotated ciliary processes)
- Evaluating ciliary body detachment or effusion

### Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography (AS-OCT)

**[[Anterior Segment OCT]]** provides non-contact, high-resolution imaging of the anterior segment. While it has limited penetration through the iris pigment epithelium, newer swept-source OCT devices can partially visualize the ciliary body and are useful for assessing angle anatomy and ciliary body cysts.

### B-Scan Ultrasonography

**[[B-Scan Ultrasonography]]** (10 MHz) is used for evaluating larger ciliary body masses, particularly melanomas, and for assessing associated features such as retinal detachment, choroidal effusion, and extraocular extension.

### Transillumination

**[[Transillumination]]** involves placing a bright light source on the sclera and observing the pupil. A ciliary body mass will block light transmission, creating a shadow (transillumination defect). This is a simple, rapid screening tool for ciliary body tumors.

### Fluorescein and Indocyanine Green Angiography

**[[Fluorescein Angiography]]** (FA) and **[[Indocyanine Green Angiography]]** (ICGA) can demonstrate abnormal vascularity associated with ciliary body tumors and assess the integrity of the blood-aqueous barrier.

### Advanced Imaging

- **[[MRI]]** (with gadolinium enhancement): Useful for characterizing ciliary body melanomas (typically hyperintense on T1, hypointense on T2 due to melanin content) and assessing extraocular extension.
- **[[Fine-Needle Aspiration Biopsy]] (FNAB):** Reserved for diagnostically uncertain intraocular masses; provides cytological confirmation.

## Management and Treatment

### Pharmacological Interventions Targeting the Ciliary Body

The ciliary body is the primary pharmacological target for [[Intraocular Pressure]] reduction in [[Glaucoma]] management. Several drug classes act directly on the ciliary body:

| Drug Class | Mechanism of Action | Examples | Route |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Beta-Adrenergic Antagonists]]** | Reduce aqueous humor production by blocking β₂-receptors on the ciliary epithelium | [[Timolol]], [[Betaxolol]], [[Levobunolol]] | Topical |
| **[[Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors]]** | Inhibit carbonic anhydrase II in the NPE, reducing bicarbonate and sodium transport, thereby decreasing aqueous secretion | [[Dorzolamide]], [[Brinzolamide]] (topical); [[Acetazolamide]], [[Methazolamide]] (systemic) | Topical / Oral / IV |
| **[[Alpha-2 Adrenergic Agonists]]** | Reduce aqueous production (and increase uveoscleral outflow) | [[Brimonidine]], [[Apraclonidine]] | Topical |
| **[[Parasympathomimetics]] (Miotics)** | Contract the ciliary muscle (longitudinal fibers), pulling on the scleral spur to open the trabecular meshwork; increase conventional outflow | [[Pilocarpine]], [[Carbachol]] | Topical |
| **[[Cycloplegics / Mydriatics]]** | Paralyze the ciliary muscle (relax accommodation); used therapeutically in uveitis to prevent posterior synechiae and relieve ciliary spasm pain | [[Atropine]], [[Cyclopentolate]], [[Homatropine]] | Topical |

### Management of Inflammatory Conditions

#### Anterior Uveitis / Cyclitis

- **[[Topical Corticosteroids]]** (e.g., [[Prednisolone Acetate]] 1%, [[Difluprednate]] 0.05%): Mainstay of treatment; suppress inflammation and restore the blood-aqueous barrier.
- **[[Cycloplegic Agents]]** (e.g., [[Atropine]] 1%, [[Cyclopentolate]] 1%): Relieve ciliary spasm pain, prevent and break posterior synechiae, stabilize the blood-aqueous barrier.
- **[[Systemic Immunosuppression]]**: For recurrent, bilateral, or sight-threatening uveitis; agents include [[Methotrexate]], [[Mycophenolate Mofetil]], [[Azathioprine]], and biologic agents such as [[Adalimumab]].
- **[[Periocular or Intravitreal Corticosteroid Injections]]**: For refractory cases (e.g., [[Triamcinolone Acetonide]], [[Dexamethasone Intravitreal Implant]]).

#### Pars Planitis

- Observation if mild and vision is unaffected.
- **Periocular corticosteroids** (sub-Tenon's [[Triamcinolone]]) for unilateral disease.
- **Systemic corticosteroids** or **steroid-sparing immunosuppressive agents** for bilateral or chronic disease.
- **[[Pars Plana Vitrectomy]]** for non-clearing vitreous hemorrhage, epiretinal membrane, or tractional retinal detachment.
- **[[Peripheral Retinal Cryotherapy]] or [[Laser Photocoagulation]]** for peripheral neovascularization.

### Management of Neoplastic Conditions

#### Ciliary Body Melanoma

Management depends on tumor size, extent, and patient factors:

| Tumor Size | Preferred Treatment |
|---|---|
| Small (< 10 mm base, < 3 mm height) | Observation with serial imaging; [[Plaque Brachytherapy]] if growth documented |
| Medium (10–16 mm base, 3–8 mm height) | [[Plaque Brachytherapy]] (iodine-125); [[Proton Beam Radiotherapy]] |
| Large (> 16 mm base or > 8 mm height) | [[Enucleation]]; consider [[Proton Beam Radiotherapy]] in select cases |
| With extraocular extension | [[Enucleation]] with or without adjuvant [[External Beam Radiotherapy]] |

- **[[Iridocyclectomy]]** (partial lamellar sclerouvectomy): Surgical excision of localized ciliary body tumors; requires careful patient selection.
- **Systemic surveillance:** Liver function tests, hepatic imaging (ultrasound or MRI) every 6–12 months due to the propensity for hepatic metastasis.
- **Genetic prognostication:** [[Gene Expression Profiling]] (e.g., DecisionDx-UM) and [[Chromosome 3 Monosomy]] testing provide prognostic information regarding metastatic risk.

#### Medulloepithelioma

- **[[Enucleation]]** remains the standard treatment for most cases, particularly malignant variants.
- **Local resection** may be considered for small, well-circumscribed benign tumors.
- **[[Plaque Brachytherapy]]** has been used in select cases.

### Surgical Procedures Involving the Ciliary Body

#### Cyclodestructive Procedures

Cyclodestructive procedures aim to reduce aqueous humor production by destroying the ciliary epithelium. They are typically reserved for **refractory glaucoma** that has failed medical and conventional surgical therapy.

| Procedure | Mechanism | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Transscleral Cyclophotocoagulation]] (TSCPC)** | Diode laser (810 nm) applied transclerally to the ciliary body; thermal destruction of ciliary epithelium | Most commonly used cyclodestructive procedure; can be performed with continuous-wave or micropulse delivery |
| **[[Micropulse Transscleral Cyclophotocoagulation]] (MP-TSCPC)** | Subthreshold, repetitive short laser pulses; selectively targets pigmented ciliary epithelium while sparing adjacent tissue | Lower risk of hypotony and phthisis compared to continuous-wave TSCPC; increasingly favored |
| **[[Endoscopic Cyclophotocoagulation]] (ECP)** | Diode laser delivered via an intraocular endoscope directly visualizing the ciliary processes | More precise; often combined with [[Cataract Surgery]]; lower risk of over-treatment |
| **[[Cyclocryotherapy]]** | Cryoprobe applied to the sclera overlying the ciliary body; freezing destroys ciliary epithelium | Largely replaced by laser-based methods due to higher complication rates (pain, inflammation, hypotony) |

#### Pars Plana Vitrectomy (PPV)

**[[Pars Plana Vitrectomy]]** is a posterior segment surgical procedure in which instruments are inserted through the pars plana (typically 3.5–4.0 mm posterior to the limbus) to access the vitreous cavity. The pars plana is chosen because it is the most avascular and structurally simple region of the ciliary body, minimizing the risk of hemorrhage and retinal damage.

#### Cyclodialysis Cleft Repair

As described above, management ranges from conservative (atropine, laser) to surgical (direct cyclopexy, vitrectomy with gas tamponade and internal tamponade of the cleft).

## Pharmacology: Drugs Acting on the Ciliary Body

### Aqueous Humor Suppressants

- **[[Beta-Blockers]]:** Timolol (non-selective), betaxolol (β₁-selective). Reduce aqueous production by 20–30%. Contraindicated in asthma, COPD, bradycardia.
- **[[Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors]]:** Dorzolamide, brinzolamide (topical); acetazolamide, methazolamide (oral). Reduce aqueous production by 15–20% (topical) or 40–60% (systemic). Systemic side effects include paresthesias, metallic taste, metabolic acidosis, and aplastic anemia (rare).
- **[[Alpha-2 Agonists]]:** Brimonidine. Dual mechanism: reduces aqueous production and enhances uveoscleral outflow. Risk of allergic conjunctivitis with chronic use.

### Cycloplegics and Mydriatics

These agents paralyze the ciliary muscle by blocking [[Muscarinic Receptors]] (M₃ subtype) on the ciliary muscle:

| Agent | Onset | Duration of Cycloplegia | Clinical Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Atropine]] 1% | 30–40 min | 7–14 days | Severe uveitis, penalization therapy, cycloplegic refraction in children |
| [[Homatropine]] 2–5% | 30–60 min | 1–3 days | Moderate uveitis |
| [[Cyclopentolate]] 0.5–2% | 25–75 min | 6–24 hours | Cycloplegic refraction, mild-moderate uveitis |
| [[Tropicamide]] 0.5–1% | 20–40 min | 4–6 hours | Primarily mydriatic; weak cycloplegic; routine dilation |

### Prostaglandin Analogues

While [[Prostaglandin Analogues]] (e.g., [[Latanoprost]], [[Travoprost]], [[Bimatoprost]], [[Tafluprost]]) primarily reduce IOP by increasing [[Uveoscleral Outflow]] through the ciliary muscle interstitial spaces (by remodeling the extracellular matrix of the ciliary muscle), they do not significantly suppress aqueous production. Their mechanism is intimately related to ciliary body physiology, as the uveoscleral pathway traverses the ciliary muscle.

## Developmental Anomalies

### Aniridia and Ciliary Body Hypoplasia

In [[Aniridia]] (caused by mutations in the [[PAX6]] gene), the ciliary body may be hypoplastic, contributing to lens subluxation, glaucoma, and reduced accommodative function.

### Persistent Hyperplastic Primary Vitreous (PHPV) / Persistent Fetal Vasculature (PFV)

**[[Persistent Fetal Vasculature]]** may involve elongated ciliary processes that are drawn centrally and visible behind a clear or cataractous lens. This is a developmental anomaly resulting from failure of regression of the [[Hyaloid Vascular System]].

---

## Summary of Key Clinical Pearls

- The ciliary body is the **sole source of aqueous humor** and the **effector organ of accommodation**.
- The **non-pigmented ciliary epithelium** is the secretory layer; the **blood-aqueous barrier** is formed by its tight junctions.
- **UBM** is the gold standard for in vivo ciliary body imaging.
- **Ciliary body melanoma** has a worse prognosis than iris melanoma; sentinel vessels and sector cataract are key clinical clues.
- **Cyclodestructive procedures** (TSCPC, MP-TSCPC, ECP) target the ciliary body to reduce aqueous production in refractory glaucoma.
- **Cyclodialysis clefts** cause hypotony via abnormal uveoscleral outflow through the suprachoroidal space.
- **Pars planitis** is the prototypical intermediate uveitis involving the pars plana.
- The **pars plana** is the preferred surgical entry site for vitreoretinal surgery due to its relative avascularity.
