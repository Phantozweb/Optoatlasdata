# Ciliary Body

The **ciliary body** is a critical ring-shaped structure of the [[uveal tract]] (middle vascular coat of the eye) that extends from the root of the [[iris]] anteriorly to the [[ora serrata]] posteriorly. It is one of the most functionally significant tissues in the eye, responsible for the production of [[aqueous humor]], the maintenance of [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP), and the mechanism of [[accommodation]]. Diseases and dysfunction of the ciliary body have profound implications for vision and ocular health, contributing to conditions ranging from [[glaucoma]] to [[uveitis]] to [[presbyopia]]. A thorough understanding of ciliary body anatomy, physiology, pathophysiology, and clinical management is essential for every optometrist and ophthalmologist.

---

## Gross and Microscopic Anatomy

### Topographic Anatomy

The ciliary body is a triangular structure in cross-section, with its base facing the anterior chamber and its apex tapering posteriorly toward the [[ora serrata]]. It measures approximately **6 mm in width** (anteroposterior dimension), though this varies slightly between individuals and with age. The ciliary body is divided into two principal zones:

| Region | Description | Width |
|---|---|---|
| **Pars plicata** (corona ciliaris) | The anterior, ridged portion bearing approximately 70–80 ciliary processes | ~2 mm |
| **Pars plana** (orbiculus ciliaris) | The posterior, flat portion extending to the ora serrata | ~4 mm |

The **pars plicata** is the metabolically active region, housing the [[ciliary processes]]—finger-like projections that are the primary site of [[aqueous humor]] secretion. Each ciliary process is approximately 0.5–1.0 mm in height and 2 mm in length. The **pars plana** is relatively avascular and is the preferred surgical entry site for [[pars plana vitrectomy]] and intravitreal injections due to its relative safety from damage to the [[retina]] and [[lens]].

### Layers of the Ciliary Body

From external to internal, the ciliary body consists of the following layers:

1. **Supraciliary lamina (supraciliaris):** A thin layer of connective tissue and melanocytes continuous with the [[suprachoroidal space]]. It serves as a potential space for fluid accumulation (e.g., in [[ciliary body detachment]] or [[suprachoroidal hemorrhage]]).

2. **Ciliary muscle:** The smooth muscle responsible for [[accommodation]]. It is composed of three fiber orientations:
   - **Longitudinal (meridional) fibers (Brücke's muscle):** The outermost fibers, inserting into the [[scleral spur]] anteriorly and the [[choroid]] posteriorly. Contraction pulls the choroid forward.
   - **Radial (oblique) fibers:** Intermediate fibers connecting the longitudinal and circular layers.
   - **Circular (sphincter-like) fibers (Müller's muscle):** The innermost fibers, forming a ring near the base of the ciliary processes. Contraction releases tension on the [[zonular fibers]], allowing the [[crystalline lens]] to become more convex for near vision.

3. **Stroma:** A loose connective tissue layer rich in blood vessels, nerves, and melanocytes. The major arterial circle of the iris ([[circulus arteriosus iridis major]]) is located within the ciliary body stroma.

4. **Ciliary epithelium:** A bilayer of epithelial cells covering the inner surface of the ciliary body and its processes:
   - **Outer pigmented epithelium (PE):** Continuous with the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE). Cells contain melanin granules.
   - **Inner non-pigmented epithelium (NPE):** Continuous with the [[neurosensory retina]]. This layer is the primary site of aqueous humor secretion and is joined to the PE at their apical surfaces.

5. **Internal limiting membrane (ILM):** A basement membrane of the NPE, continuous with the [[internal limiting membrane]] of the retina.

### Vascular Supply

The ciliary body receives its blood supply from two sources:

- **[[Long posterior ciliary arteries]]** (two branches from the [[ophthalmic artery]]) — enter the eye through the sclera near the optic nerve and travel anteriorly within the suprachoroidal space to reach the ciliary body.
- **[[Anterior ciliary arteries]]** (branches of the muscular arteries supplying the [[rectus muscles]]) — penetrate the sclera near the limbus and contribute to the **major arterial circle of the iris**, which lies within the ciliary body stroma.

Venous drainage occurs via the [[vortex veins]] posteriorly.

### Innervation

The ciliary body is innervated by:

- **Parasympathetic fibers** from the [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]] via the [[oculomotor nerve]] (CN III) and the [[ciliary ganglion]]. These fibers stimulate contraction of the ciliary muscle (accommodation) and are the target of [[miotic]] agents such as [[pilocarpine]].
- **Sympathetic fibers** from the [[superior cervical ganglion]] via the [[long ciliary nerves]]. These fibers cause relaxation of the ciliary muscle and may reduce aqueous humor production.
- **Sensory fibers** from the [[nasociliary nerve]] (branch of [[CN V1]]), conveying pain and other sensory information.

## Physiology

### Aqueous Humor Production

The ciliary body is the sole source of [[aqueous humor]], a clear fluid that fills the [[anterior chamber]] and [[posterior chamber]] of the eye. Aqueous humor is produced at a rate of approximately **2.0–2.5 µL/min** (total volume ~250 µL, with a turnover rate of approximately 1% per minute). Production occurs through three mechanisms:

| Mechanism | Contribution | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Active secretion** | ~80–90% | Energy-dependent transport of ions (Na⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻) by the non-pigmented ciliary epithelium, primarily via [[Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase]] and [[carbonic anhydrase]]. Water follows osmotically. |
| **Ultrafiltration** | ~10–20% | Pressure-dependent movement of plasma across the ciliary body capillary fenestrations into the stroma, driven by the hydrostatic pressure gradient. |
| **Diffusion** | Minor | Passive movement of lipid-soluble substances along concentration gradients. |

The [[blood-aqueous barrier]] (BAB) is maintained by tight junctions between the non-pigmented ciliary epithelial cells and the non-fenestrated endothelium of the iridial vasculature. Disruption of this barrier (e.g., in [[anterior uveitis]]) leads to increased protein and cellular content in the aqueous humor, clinically observed as [[flare]] and [[cells]] on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]].

### Accommodation

The ciliary body is the effector organ of the [[accommodation reflex]]. When the [[ciliary muscle]] contracts (primarily the circular fibers), the diameter of the ciliary ring decreases, releasing tension on the [[zonular fibers]] (zonules of Zinn). This allows the elastic [[crystalline lens]] to assume a more spherical shape, increasing its refractive power for near vision. This mechanism is described by the **[[Helmholtz theory of accommodation]]**.

- **Near vision:** Ciliary muscle contracts → zonules relax → lens thickens → increased refractive power.
- **Distance vision:** Ciliary muscle relaxes → zonules taut → lens flattens → decreased refractive power.

With aging, the lens loses elasticity and the ciliary muscle undergoes structural changes, leading to [[presbyopia]]—the progressive loss of accommodative amplitude, typically becoming clinically significant around age 40.

### Maintenance of Intraocular Pressure

The balance between aqueous humor production by the ciliary body and its outflow through the [[trabecular meshwork]]/[[Schlemm's canal]] (conventional pathway, ~80–90%) and the [[uveoscleral pathway]] (~10–20%) determines [[intraocular pressure]]. Normal IOP ranges from **10–21 mmHg**. Pharmacological manipulation of ciliary body function (e.g., with [[beta-blockers]], [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]], or [[alpha-agonists]]) is a cornerstone of [[glaucoma]] management.

## Pathophysiology

### Inflammation: Ciliary Body and Anterior Uveitis

Inflammation of the ciliary body is termed **[[cyclitis]]**. When it occurs in conjunction with [[iritis]] (inflammation of the iris), the condition is called **[[iridocyclitis]]** or **[[anterior uveitis]]**. Cyclitis may be:

- **Acute or chronic**
- **Granulomatous or non-granulomatous**
- **Infectious** (e.g., [[herpes simplex virus]], [[toxoplasmosis]], [[syphilis]], [[tuberculosis]]) or **non-infectious/autoimmune** (e.g., [[HLA-B27]]-associated, [[sarcoidosis]], [[Behçet disease]], [[juvenile idiopathic arthritis]])

Inflammation disrupts the [[blood-aqueous barrier]], leading to:
- Protein leakage ([[flare]])
- Inflammatory cell infiltration ([[cells]])
- [[Keratic precipitates]] (KPs) on the [[corneal endothelium]]
- [[Posterior synechiae]] (adhesions between the iris and lens)
- Secondary [[glaucoma]] (from trabecular meshwork obstruction or pupillary block)
- [[Hypotony]] (from reduced aqueous production by inflamed ciliary epithelium)
- [[Cystoid macular edema]] (CME)

Chronic or recurrent cyclitis can lead to [[phthisis bulbi]] (end-stage shrunken, non-functional eye) due to persistent hypotony and ciliary body atrophy.

### Ciliary Body Tumors

The ciliary body is a recognized site for both benign and malignant neoplasms:

| Tumor Type | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **[[Ciliary body melanoma]]** | Most common primary malignant intraocular tumor in adults involving the ciliary body. Often presents late due to its hidden location behind the iris. May cause lens subluxation, sector cataract, sentinel episcleral vessels, secondary glaucoma, or visual field defects. |
| **[[Medulloepithelioma]] (diktyoma)** | Rare embryonal tumor of the non-pigmented ciliary epithelium, typically presenting in childhood. May be benign or malignant. Can produce [[cyclitic membrane]], [[neovascular glaucoma]], or [[lens subluxation]]. |
| **[[Ciliary body adenoma / adenocarcinoma]]** | Rare tumors arising from the pigmented or non-pigmented ciliary epithelium. |
| **[[Leiomyoma]]** | Rare benign smooth muscle tumor of the ciliary body. |
| **[[Metastatic tumors]]** | Secondary deposits from breast, lung, or other primary cancers. |

### Ciliary Body Detachment and Effusion

Separation of the ciliary body from the [[scleral spur]] is termed **[[cyclodialysis]]**. This may occur traumatically or iatrogenically and creates a direct communication between the [[anterior chamber]] and the [[suprachoroidal space]], resulting in profound [[hypotony]] and potential [[maculopathy]]. **Ciliary body effusion** or **ciliochoroidal effusion** may occur in the setting of inflammation, post-surgical hypotony, or nanophthalmos.

### Ciliary Spasm and Ciliary Muscle Dysfunction

- **[[Ciliary spasm]] (accommodative spasm):** Sustained involuntary contraction of the ciliary muscle, causing pseudomyopia, brow ache, and fluctuating vision. May be associated with excessive near work, anxiety, or pharmacological agents (e.g., [[miotics]]).
- **[[Ciliary muscle paralysis]] (cycloplegia):** Loss of accommodative function, which may be pharmacologically induced (e.g., with [[atropine]], [[cyclopentolate]]) or result from trauma, inflammation, or neurological disease affecting the [[parasympathetic pathway]].

### Role in Glaucoma Pathophysiology

The ciliary body is central to the pathophysiology of several forms of [[glaucoma]]:

- **[[Primary open-angle glaucoma]] (POAG):** Overproduction of aqueous humor by the ciliary body (though outflow resistance at the [[trabecular meshwork]] is the predominant mechanism).
- **[[Angle-closure glaucoma]]:** Anterior rotation or swelling of the ciliary body can contribute to appositional or synechial angle closure ([[plateau iris configuration]]).
- **[[Malignant glaucoma]] (aqueous misdirection):** Posterior diversion of aqueous humor behind the lens or into the [[vitreous cavity]], often involving ciliary body processes.
- **[[Neovascular glaucoma]]:** Neovascularization of the ciliary body and iris in the setting of retinal ischemia.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Symptoms attributable to ciliary body pathology vary depending on the underlying condition:

| Condition | Key Symptoms |
|---|---|
| **Cyclitis / Iridocyclitis** | Pain (deep, aching), photophobia, tearing, blurred vision, redness (ciliary flush) |
| **Ciliary body tumor** | Often asymptomatic early; later: blurred vision, visual field defect, visible mass, lens changes |
| **Ciliary spasm** | Brow ache, headache, pseudomyopia, fluctuating near/distance vision |
| **Cyclodialysis** | Blurred vision, hypotony, shallow anterior chamber |
| **Presbyopia** | Progressive difficulty with near tasks, typically after age 40 |

**Ciliary tenderness** (pain on palpation of the globe over the ciliary body region) is a classic clinical sign of cyclitis and may be elicited during examination.

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

[[Slit-lamp examination]] is the primary tool for evaluating the anterior segment and detecting signs of ciliary body inflammation, including:
- [[Cells and flare]] in the anterior chamber
- [[Keratic precipitates]]
- [[Posterior synechiae]]
- [[Iris bombé]]
- Lens changes (e.g., sector [[cataract]])

### Gonioscopy

[[Gonioscopy]] allows visualization of the [[anterior chamber angle]], including the [[ciliary body band]]—the most posterior visible structure in an open angle. Widening of the ciliary body band may indicate [[cyclodialysis cleft]]. Gonioscopy is also essential for detecting angle closure, plateau iris, and tumors extending into the angle.

### Ultrasound Biomicroscopy (UBM)

[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM) uses high-frequency ultrasound (35–100 MHz) to provide high-resolution cross-sectional images of the ciliary body and surrounding structures. UBM is invaluable for:

- Measuring ciliary body thickness and position
- Detecting [[ciliary body cysts]], tumors, and effusions
- Identifying [[cyclodialysis clefts]]
- Evaluating [[plateau iris syndrome]]
- Assessing the relationship between the ciliary body, zonules, and lens in angle-closure mechanisms

### Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography (AS-OCT)

[[Anterior segment OCT]] provides non-contact, high-resolution imaging of the anterior segment. While it has limited penetration through the iris pigment epithelium, newer swept-source OCT devices can partially visualize the ciliary body and are useful for evaluating angle anatomy and ciliary body position.

### B-Scan Ultrasonography

Standard [[B-scan ultrasonography]] (10 MHz) is used to detect larger ciliary body masses, ciliochoroidal effusions, and suprachoroidal hemorrhages, particularly when direct visualization is obscured by media opacities.

### Fluorescein and Indocyanine Green Angiography

[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA) and [[indocyanine green angiography]] (ICGA) may demonstrate abnormal vascularity associated with ciliary body tumors or inflammation, though their utility for direct ciliary body imaging is limited.

### Transillumination

[[Transillumination]] of the globe can reveal ciliary body masses as shadow defects, particularly useful in differentiating solid tumors from cysts.

### Histopathology and Fine-Needle Aspiration Biopsy (FNAB)

In cases of suspected ciliary body neoplasm, [[fine-needle aspiration biopsy]] (FNAB) may be performed under ultrasound guidance to obtain cytological specimens for diagnosis. Histopathological examination remains the gold standard for definitive tumor classification.

## Management and Treatment

### Pharmacological Interventions Targeting the Ciliary Body

The ciliary body is a major pharmacological target in the management of [[glaucoma]] and [[ocular hypertension]]. Drugs that reduce aqueous humor production by the ciliary body include:

| Drug Class | Mechanism | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Beta-adrenergic antagonists]] (beta-blockers)** | Block β₂-receptors on ciliary epithelium, reducing aqueous production | [[Timolol]], [[betaxolol]], [[levobunolol]] |
| **[[Alpha-adrenergic agonists]]** | Stimulate α₂-receptors, reducing aqueous production and increasing uveoscleral outflow | [[Brimonidine]], [[apraclonidine]] |
| **[[Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]] (CAIs)** | Inhibit carbonic anhydrase in ciliary epithelium, reducing bicarbonate and aqueous secretion | [[Dorzolamide]], [[brinzolamide]], [[acetazolamide]] (systemic) |

Drugs that act on the ciliary muscle to enhance uveoscleral outflow:

| Drug Class | Mechanism | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Prostaglandin analogs]]** | Remodel extracellular matrix of ciliary muscle, increasing uveoscleral outflow | [[Latanoprost]], [[travoprost]], [[bimatoprost]], [[tafluprost]] |
| **[[Parasympathomimetics]] (miotics)** | Contract ciliary muscle (longitudinal fibers), opening trabecular meshwork; also contract sphincter pupillae | [[Pilocarpine]], [[echothiophate]] |

**Cycloplegic agents** (e.g., [[atropine]], [[cyclopentolate]], [[tropicamide]], [[homatropine]]) paralyze the ciliary muscle and are used therapeutically in:
- [[Anterior uveitis]] (to relieve ciliary spasm and prevent posterior synechiae)
- [[Cycloplegic refraction]] (to eliminate accommodative tone for accurate refraction, especially in children)
- Post-operative management

### Management of Cyclitis / Anterior Uveitis

Treatment of ciliary body inflammation follows established protocols for [[anterior uveitis]]:

1. **Topical [[corticosteroids]]** (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate]] 1%, [[difluprednate]] 0.05%) — mainstay of anti-inflammatory therapy.
2. **Cycloplegic/mydriatic agents** (e.g., [[cyclopentolate]] 1%, [[atropine]] 1%) — relieve pain from ciliary spasm and prevent/break posterior synechiae.
3. **Systemic immunosuppression** — for severe, recurrent, or bilateral disease: [[methotrexate]], [[mycophenolate mofetil]], [[azathioprine]], [[adalimumab]], [[infliximab]].
4. **Antimicrobial therapy** — when an infectious etiology is identified (e.g., [[valacyclovir]] for herpetic uveitis).
5. **Periocular or intravitreal corticosteroid injections** — for refractory cases (e.g., [[triamcinolone acetonide]]).

### Surgical and Procedural Interventions Involving the Ciliary Body

#### Cyclodestructive Procedures

Cyclodestructive procedures aim to reduce aqueous humor production by destroying a portion of the ciliary body epithelium. These are typically reserved for refractory [[glaucoma]] that has failed medical and conventional surgical therapy:

| Procedure | Description |
|---|---|
| **[[Transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]] (TSCPC)** | Diode laser (810 nm) energy is applied through the sclera to ablate ciliary processes. Can be performed in continuous-wave or micropulse mode. |
| **[[Micropulse transscleral cyclophotocoagulation]] (MP-TSCPC)** | Delivers laser energy in short, repetitive pulses ("on" and "off" cycles), reducing collateral tissue damage and the risk of hypotony compared to continuous-wave TSCPC. |
| **[[Endoscopic cyclophotocoagulation]] (ECP)** | Diode laser is delivered directly to the ciliary processes under endoscopic visualization, allowing precise treatment. Often combined with [[cataract surgery]]. |
| **[[Cyclocryotherapy]]** | Application of a cryoprobe to the sclera overlying the ciliary body to freeze and destroy ciliary processes. Largely supplanted by laser techniques due to higher complication rates. |

Complications of cyclodestructive procedures include [[hypotony]], [[phthisis bulbi]], chronic inflammation, [[sympathetic ophthalmia]] (rare), and loss of vision.

#### Cyclodialysis Cleft Repair

[[Cyclodialysis cleft]] repair may be necessary following trauma or surgery. Techniques include:
- **Argon laser photocoagulation** to the cleft
- **Direct cyclopexy** (surgical reattachment of the ciliary body to the scleral spur)
- **Cryotherapy** to the cleft region
- **Intracameral injection of viscoelastic** to tamponade the cleft

#### Ciliary Body Tumor Management

Management of ciliary body tumors depends on tumor type, size, and extent:

- **Observation:** Small, stable lesions may be monitored with serial UBM and photography.
- **[[Plaque brachytherapy]]:** Radioactive plaque (e.g., iodine-125, ruthenium-106) applied to the sclera overlying the tumor for localized radiation.
- **[[Proton beam radiotherapy]]:** External beam radiation for larger or posteriorly extending tumors.
- **[[Local resection]] (iridocyclectomy / partial lamellar sclerouvectomy):** Surgical excision of the tumor with surrounding tissue. Reserved for localized tumors.
- **[[Enucleation]]:** Removal of the eye, indicated for large tumors, those with extraocular extension, or when vision is unsalvageable.

#### Pars Plana as a Surgical Landmark

The **[[pars plana]]** region of the ciliary body is the standard entry site for:
- **[[Pars plana vitrectomy]]** (PPV)
- **[[Intravitreal injections]]** (e.g., [[anti-VEGF]] agents, corticosteroids)
- **[[Intravitreal implants]]** (e.g., [[fluocinolone acetonide]] implant)

Entry is typically made 3.0–4.0 mm posterior to the [[limbus]] (3.0 mm in pseudophakic/aphakic eyes, 3.5–4.0 mm in phakic eyes) to avoid damage to the [[crystalline lens]] and [[retina]].

## Age-Related Changes

The ciliary body undergoes significant age-related changes:

- **Ciliary muscle:** Progressive fibrosis and loss of contractile efficiency, contributing to [[presbyopia]]. The longitudinal fibers become more prominent relative to the circular fibers.
- **Ciliary epithelium:** Thickening of the basement membrane, accumulation of lipofuscin, and decreased secretory capacity, leading to a gradual decline in aqueous humor production (approximately 2–3% per decade after age 40).
- **Stroma:** Increased collagen deposition and hyalinization.
- **Zonular fibers:** Progressive loss of elasticity and structural integrity, predisposing to [[zonular dehiscence]] and [[lens subluxation]] in older patients.

These changes collectively contribute to the age-related decline in accommodative amplitude and the gradual reduction in IOP observed in elderly populations.

## Ciliary Body Cysts

[[Ciliary body cysts]] are relatively common and may arise from either the pigmented or non-pigmented epithelium. They are classified as:

- **Primary cysts:** Developmental or degenerative; usually asymptomatic and discovered incidentally on UBM or gonioscopy. They may be located at the pars plicata or pars plana.
- **Secondary cysts:** Associated with trauma, inflammation, [[miotics]] (especially long-term [[pilocarpine]] or [[echothiophate]] use), or tumors.

Most ciliary body cysts are benign and require no treatment. However, large or multiple cysts may cause:
- Angle narrowing and secondary [[angle-closure glaucoma]]
- Lens displacement
- Visual disturbance

Differentiation from solid tumors is critical and is best accomplished with [[UBM]] or [[anterior segment OCT]], which demonstrate the characteristic thin-walled, anechoic (fluid-filled) nature of cysts. Argon laser cystotomy or Nd:YAG laser cystotomy may be performed for symptomatic cysts.

---

## Congenital Anomalies

Congenital anomalies of the ciliary body are rare but clinically significant:

- **[[Aniridia]]:** Often associated with hypoplasia of the ciliary body and zonules, contributing to lens subluxation and glaucoma.
- **[[Coloboma]] of the ciliary body:** A developmental defect resulting from incomplete closure of the [[embryonic fissure]], often associated with colobomas of the iris, choroid, and optic nerve.
- **[[Persistent hyperplastic primary vitreous]] (PHPV) / [[Persistent fetal vasculature]] (PFV):** May involve abnormal ciliary body processes and fibrovascular tissue.
