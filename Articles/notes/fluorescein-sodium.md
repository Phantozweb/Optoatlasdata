

# Fluorescein Sodium

**Fluorescein Sodium** (C₂₀H₁₀Na₂O₅) is a synthetic, water-soluble [[fluorescent dye]] of the xanthene family that serves as one of the most indispensable diagnostic agents in [[Optometry]] and [[Ophthalmology]]. Since its first ophthalmic application by Pfluger in 1882, fluorescein sodium has become a cornerstone of clinical eye examination, enabling the visualization of [[corneal epithelial]] defects, assessment of [[tear film]] dynamics, measurement of [[intraocular pressure]] via [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]], evaluation of [[contact lens]] fitting, and angiographic imaging of the [[retinal vasculature]] and [[choroidal vasculature]]. Its unique photophysical properties—absorbing blue light (peak excitation ~490 nm) and emitting yellow-green fluorescence (peak emission ~520 nm)—allow clinicians to detect pathology that would otherwise remain invisible under standard white-light [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]].

Fluorescein sodium is available in multiple pharmaceutical formulations including topical ophthalmic strips, topical ophthalmic solutions, and intravenous injectable solutions. It is classified by the U.S. [[Food and Drug Administration]] (FDA) as a diagnostic agent and carries a generally favorable safety profile, although rare but serious adverse reactions—particularly with intravenous administration—mandate careful patient selection and preparedness for emergency management.

## Chemical Properties and Pharmacology

### Chemical Structure

Fluorescein sodium is the disodium salt of fluorescein, a member of the xanthene dye family. Its molecular formula is **C₂₀H₁₀Na₂O₅**, with a molecular weight of **376.27 g/mol**. The compound exists as an orange-red, hygroscopic powder that is freely soluble in water, producing an intensely fluorescent yellow-green solution. The fluorescence of fluorescein sodium is pH-dependent; it fluoresces most intensely in alkaline environments (pH 7.4–8.0), which is fortuitous given that the normal pH of the [[tear film]] is approximately 7.4. In acidic environments (pH < 5), fluorescence is markedly diminished because the molecule shifts toward its non-fluorescent lactone form.

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| **IUPAC Name** | Disodium 2-(6-hydroxy-3-oxo-3H-xanthen-9-yl)benzoate |
| **Molecular Formula** | C₂₀H₁₀Na₂O₅ |
| **Molecular Weight** | 376.27 g/mol |
| **Excitation Peak** | ~490 nm (blue light) |
| **Emission Peak** | ~520 nm (yellow-green light) |
| **Solubility** | Freely soluble in water |
| **pKa** | ~6.4 (phenolic hydroxyl) |
| **Color of Powder** | Orange-red |
| **Color of Solution** | Intense yellow-green fluorescent |
| **Log P (Partition Coefficient)** | ~0.35 (hydrophilic) |

### Mechanism of Fluorescence

Fluorescein sodium exhibits fluorescence through a process in which the molecule absorbs photons of a specific wavelength (excitation) and subsequently emits photons at a longer wavelength (emission). When illuminated with a [[cobalt blue filter]] (wavelength approximately 465–490 nm) on a slit-lamp biomicroscope, fluorescein molecules in the tear film or within tissue defects absorb the blue light and undergo electronic excitation from the ground state (S₀) to a higher singlet excited state (S₁). The molecule then rapidly returns to the ground state via radiative decay, emitting a photon at approximately 520 nm (yellow-green). This Stokes shift of approximately 25–30 nm allows the emitted fluorescence to be distinguished from the excitation light, particularly when a yellow barrier filter (Wratten #12 or equivalent) is placed in the observation pathway to block reflected blue light and enhance contrast.

The quantum yield of fluorescein sodium in aqueous solution at physiological pH is approximately **0.92**, making it one of the most efficient fluorescent dyes known. This exceptionally high quantum yield is a primary reason for its clinical utility—even trace amounts of the dye produce readily visible fluorescence.

### Pharmacokinetics

#### Topical Administration

When applied topically to the [[ocular surface]], fluorescein sodium remains primarily within the [[precorneal tear film]] and does not significantly penetrate intact [[corneal epithelium]] due to the epithelium's tight junctions ([[zonulae occludentes]]). However, where epithelial integrity is compromised—as in [[corneal abrasion]], [[corneal ulcer]], [[punctate epithelial erosions]], or [[recurrent corneal erosion]]—the dye penetrates into the [[corneal stroma]], which is hydrophilic, producing bright green fluorescence that delineates the area of damage. The dye is progressively diluted and washed away by the natural [[tear turnover]] rate (approximately 16% per minute in normal eyes) and by [[blinking]].

Fluorescein sodium does not bind significantly to intact epithelial cells; rather, it pools in areas of epithelial disruption and intercellular spaces. This property is the fundamental basis for its use in detecting [[corneal staining]] patterns.

#### Intravenous Administration

When administered intravenously for [[fluorescein angiography]] (FA), fluorescein sodium rapidly distributes throughout the vascular compartment. Approximately **70–85%** of the dye is bound to [[plasma proteins]], primarily [[albumin]], while the remaining 15–30% circulates as free (unbound) fluorescein. Only the unbound fraction fluoresces significantly, as protein binding quenches fluorescence. The dye reaches the [[retinal circulation]] within approximately **10–15 seconds** after antecubital injection (the arm-to-retina circulation time), and the [[choroidal circulation]] slightly earlier due to the higher flow rate of the [[ophthalmic artery]] branches supplying the [[choroid]].

Fluorescein sodium is metabolized primarily in the [[liver]] by glucuronidation to **fluorescein monoglucuronide**, which retains approximately 50% of the fluorescence intensity of the parent compound. Both the parent compound and its metabolite are excreted by the [[kidneys]], with the majority eliminated within **24–36 hours**. Patients should be counseled that their urine will appear bright yellow-orange for 24–36 hours following intravenous injection, and their skin may take on a yellowish discoloration for several hours.

| Pharmacokinetic Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| **Protein Binding** | 70–85% (primarily albumin) |
| **Arm-to-Retina Time** | 10–15 seconds |
| **Primary Metabolite** | Fluorescein monoglucuronide |
| **Route of Elimination** | Renal |
| **Elimination Half-Life** | ~23.5 minutes (distribution); ~264 minutes (elimination) |
| **Time to Complete Urinary Excretion** | 24–36 hours |
| **Skin Discoloration Duration** | 6–12 hours |

## Pharmaceutical Formulations

Fluorescein sodium is commercially available in several formulations, each designed for specific clinical applications:

### Topical Ophthalmic Strips

Fluorescein-impregnated paper strips (e.g., **Flu-Glo®**, **BioGlo®**, **Ful-Glo®**) are the most commonly used formulation in routine optometric practice. Each strip contains approximately **0.6 mg or 1.0 mg** of fluorescein sodium dried onto a sterile paper tip. The strip is moistened with a drop of sterile, non-preserved [[saline solution]] or the patient's own tears and then gently touched to the inferior [[bulbar conjunctiva]] or the inner aspect of the lower [[eyelid]]. Strips are preferred over solution formulations in many clinical settings because they minimize the risk of microbial contamination—fluorescein solutions are notoriously susceptible to contamination by [[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]], which can thrive in fluorescein solutions and cause devastating [[Pseudomonas keratitis]].

### Topical Ophthalmic Solutions

Fluorescein sodium is available as a **0.25%** or **2%** topical ophthalmic solution. Due to the aforementioned contamination risk, single-use unit-dose vials are strongly recommended. Multi-dose bottles, if used, must contain an effective preservative and be handled with strict aseptic technique. The **0.25%** concentration is often combined with [[benoxinate hydrochloride]] **0.4%** (e.g., **Fluress®**) to provide simultaneous corneal [[anesthesia]] and fluorescein staining, which is particularly useful for [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]].

### Combination Products

- **Fluorescein sodium 0.25% / Benoxinate hydrochloride 0.4%** (Fluress®): Used for applanation tonometry and procedures requiring both staining and anesthesia.
- **Fluorescein sodium 0.25% / Proparacaine hydrochloride 0.5%** (Flurox®): An alternative combination with a different topical anesthetic.

### Intravenous Injectable Solution

For [[fluorescein angiography]], fluorescein sodium is available as a **10%** or **25%** sterile aqueous solution for intravenous injection. The standard adult dose is **500 mg** (5 mL of 10% solution or 2 mL of 25% solution), administered as a rapid bolus injection into an antecubital vein. The 25% concentration is sometimes preferred to reduce injection volume. Intravenous formulations include **AK-Fluor®** and **Fluorescite®**.

## Clinical Applications in Optometry and Ophthalmology

### Assessment of Corneal Epithelial Integrity

The most fundamental and frequent application of fluorescein sodium in clinical practice is the evaluation of [[corneal epithelium]] integrity. Because fluorescein sodium does not penetrate intact corneal epithelium but readily pools in areas of epithelial disruption, it serves as an exquisitely sensitive indicator of surface damage. When viewed under [[cobalt blue light]] with a [[slit-lamp biomicroscope]] (ideally with a yellow barrier filter), areas of epithelial loss appear as bright green fluorescent zones against the darker background of intact epithelium.

Clinical conditions detected by fluorescein staining include:

- **[[Corneal abrasion]]**: Appears as a well-demarcated area of bright fluorescence corresponding to the area of epithelial loss.
- **[[Punctate epithelial erosions]] (PEE)**: Appear as scattered, fine punctate areas of fluorescence. The distribution pattern provides diagnostic clues (e.g., inferior punctate staining in [[lagophthalmos]] or [[exposure keratopathy]]; interpalpebral staining in [[dry eye disease]]; superior staining in [[superior limbic keratoconjunctivitis]]).
- **[[Corneal ulcer]]**: Fluorescein pools in the ulcer crater, delineating its size and depth.
- **[[Dendritic ulcer]]**: The classic branching, terminal-bulb pattern of [[herpes simplex keratitis]] is dramatically highlighted by fluorescein staining.
- **[[Recurrent corneal erosion]]**: Areas of loose or absent epithelium are revealed.
- **[[Foreign body]] tracks**: Linear staining patterns indicate foreign body movement across the corneal surface, as seen with a subtarsal foreign body.
- **[[Corneal perforation]]**: The [[Seidel test]] uses concentrated fluorescein to detect aqueous humor leakage (see below).

### Seidel Test

The **[[Seidel test]]** is a critical application of fluorescein sodium for detecting [[corneal perforation]] or wound leak. A moistened fluorescein strip is applied directly over the suspected site of perforation. If aqueous humor is leaking, it dilutes the concentrated (dark orange) fluorescein, producing a bright green rivulet streaming from the wound site—a positive Seidel test. This test is essential in the evaluation of penetrating [[ocular trauma]], post-surgical wound integrity (e.g., after [[cataract surgery]] or [[corneal transplantation]]), and suspected [[filtering bleb]] leaks following [[trabeculectomy]].

### Tear Film Assessment

#### Tear Break-Up Time (TBUT)

**[[Tear break-up time]]** (TBUT) is a standard clinical test for evaluating [[tear film stability]], a key parameter in the diagnosis of [[dry eye disease]] (DED) and [[meibomian gland dysfunction]] (MGD). After instilling fluorescein into the tear film, the patient is asked to blink several times to distribute the dye evenly, and then to refrain from blinking. The examiner observes the tear film under cobalt blue illumination and records the time (in seconds) from the last complete blink to the appearance of the first dry spot (dark area) in the fluorescein-stained tear film.

| TBUT Value | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| **> 10 seconds** | Normal tear film stability |
| **5–10 seconds** | Borderline / marginal tear film stability |
| **< 5 seconds** | Unstable tear film; suggestive of [[dry eye disease]] |

It is important to note that the volume of fluorescein instilled can affect TBUT measurements. Excessive fluorescein may artificially prolong TBUT, while insufficient fluorescein may make observation difficult. Standardized micropipette instillation of 1–2 µL of 2% fluorescein has been recommended for research purposes, though in clinical practice, a lightly moistened strip is typically sufficient.

#### Tear Meniscus Height

Fluorescein sodium can enhance visualization of the [[tear meniscus]] along the lower eyelid margin. A reduced tear meniscus height (< 0.2 mm) is suggestive of [[aqueous-deficient dry eye]].

### Goldmann Applanation Tonometry

**[[Goldmann applanation tonometry]]** (GAT) remains the gold standard for measuring [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) and is fundamentally dependent on fluorescein sodium. The technique requires fluorescein in the tear film to visualize the semicircular mires (fluorescent rings) produced when the tonometer prism applanates the [[cornea]]. The clinician adjusts the tonometer dial until the inner edges of the two semicircular mires just overlap, at which point the area of corneal applanation equals 3.06 mm in diameter, and the IOP reading is taken.

Optimal fluorescein concentration is critical for accurate GAT readings. Insufficient fluorescein produces thin, difficult-to-visualize mires, leading to underestimation of IOP. Excessive fluorescein produces thick mires that may cause overestimation. Studies have demonstrated that IOP measurement errors of **1–4 mmHg** can result from improper fluorescein concentration, which has significant clinical implications in the management of [[glaucoma]] and [[ocular hypertension]].

### Contact Lens Fitting Assessment

Fluorescein sodium is extensively used in the evaluation of [[rigid gas permeable]] (RGP) contact lens fitting. When fluorescein is instilled into the tear film with an RGP lens in situ, the fluorescent tear layer between the lens and the cornea reveals the fitting relationship:

| Fluorescein Pattern | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| **Bright green (thick fluorescein layer)** | Clearance / tear pooling (lens vaults over cornea) |
| **Dark / black (thin or absent fluorescein)** | Touch / bearing (lens contacts cornea) |
| **Even, moderate green glow** | Alignment (ideal fit) |
| **Central dark zone with peripheral green** | Flat (steep cornea relative to lens base curve) |
| **Central green pool with peripheral dark** | Steep (lens base curve steeper than cornea) |

Fluorescein assessment is essential for evaluating the central optic zone fit, peripheral curve design, edge lift, and overall centration of RGP lenses. It is also used in [[orthokeratology]] lens fitting to verify the reverse geometry design and ensure appropriate central touch with mid-peripheral clearance.

**Important note**: Fluorescein sodium should **not** be used with [[soft contact lenses]] (hydrogel or [[silicone hydrogel]]) because the dye is absorbed into the lens matrix, causing discoloration. For soft lens assessment, **[[fluorexon]]** (high-molecular-weight fluorescein) may be used, as its larger molecular size prevents absorption into the lens material.

### Fluorescein Angiography (FA)

**[[Fluorescein angiography]]** is a vital diagnostic imaging technique in which intravenous fluorescein sodium is used to evaluate the [[retinal vasculature]], [[choroidal vasculature]], and [[optic disc]] circulation. Following rapid intravenous injection of 500 mg of fluorescein sodium, sequential photographs of the [[fundus]] are captured using a fundus camera equipped with excitation (blue, 465–490 nm) and barrier (yellow-green, 520–530 nm) filters.

#### Phases of Fluorescein Angiography

| Phase | Timing Post-Injection | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Choroidal (Pre-arterial)** | ~8–12 seconds | Patchy choroidal filling visible as background fluorescence |
| **Arterial** | ~10–12 seconds | Filling of [[retinal arterioles]] |
| **Arteriovenous (Capillary)** | ~13–15 seconds | Complete arteriolar filling; early venous laminar flow |
| **Venous** | ~15–20 seconds | Progressive venous filling (laminar → complete) |
| **Late (Recirculation)** | ~3–5 minutes | Gradual fading; staining of [[optic disc]] and vessel walls |
| **Very Late** | ~5–10+ minutes | Near-complete dye elimination from retinal vasculature |

#### Hyperfluorescence Patterns

- **Leakage**: Progressive increase in fluorescence intensity and area over time, indicating breakdown of the [[blood-retinal barrier]] (e.g., [[diabetic macular edema]], [[choroidal neovascularization]])
- **Pooling**: Accumulation of dye in an anatomic space (e.g., [[serous retinal detachment]], [[pigment epithelial detachment]])
- **Staining**: Late-phase fluorescence of tissue (e.g., [[drusen]], scar tissue, [[optic disc]] staining)
- **Window defect (Transmission hyperfluorescence)**: Increased visibility of choroidal fluorescence due to [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE) atrophy (e.g., [[geographic atrophy]])

#### Hypofluorescence Patterns

- **Blocked fluorescence**: Masking of normal fluorescence by overlying material (e.g., [[retinal hemorrhage]], [[pigment]])
- **Vascular filling defect**: Absence of fluorescence due to vascular non-perfusion (e.g., [[retinal artery occlusion]], [[capillary non-perfusion]] in [[diabetic retinopathy]])

Fluorescein angiography is indispensable in the diagnosis and management of:

- [[Diabetic retinopathy]] and [[diabetic macular edema]]
- [[Age-related macular degeneration]] (wet and dry forms)
- [[Retinal vein occlusion]]
- [[Retinal artery occlusion]]
- [[Central serous chorioretinopathy]]
- [[Ocular inflammatory disease]] ([[uveitis]], [[vasculitis]])
- [[Choroidal neovascularization]] from any cause
- [[Retinal macroaneurysm]]
- [[Ocular ischemic syndrome]]
- [[Papilledema]] and other [[optic disc]] pathology

### Jones Dye Test

The **[[Jones dye test]]** (fluorescein dye disappearance test) evaluates the patency of the [[nasolacrimal drainage system]]. Fluorescein is instilled into the [[conjunctival sac]], and after 5 minutes, the presence of fluorescein in the nasal cavity is assessed (using a cotton-tipped applicator or by having the patient blow their nose onto a white tissue). Absence of fluorescein in the nose suggests [[nasolacrimal duct obstruction]]. This test is particularly useful in the evaluation of [[epiphora]].

## Adverse Effects and Safety Profile

### Topical Administration

Topical fluorescein sodium is remarkably safe. Adverse effects are rare and typically mild:

- Transient stinging or burning upon instillation
- Temporary yellow-orange discoloration of the [[tear film]] and periocular skin
- Allergic reaction (exceedingly rare)
- Risk of [[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]] contamination in multi-dose solution bottles (mitigated by using single-use strips or unit-dose vials)

### Intravenous Administration

Intravenous fluorescein sodium carries a more significant adverse effect profile, ranging from common mild reactions to rare life-threatening events:

| Severity | Reaction | Approximate Incidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | Nausea, vomiting | 3–15% |
| **Mild** | Yellow skin discoloration | Nearly universal (transient) |
| **Mild** | Yellow-orange urine | Nearly universal (24–36 hours) |
| **Mild** | Transient pruritus, urticaria | 1–2% |
| **Moderate** | Vasovagal syncope | < 1% |
| **Moderate** | Thrombophlebitis at injection site | < 1% |
| **Moderate** | Skin necrosis (extravasation) | Rare |
| **Severe** | [[Anaphylaxis]] | 1 in 1,900 to 1 in 222,000 |
| **Severe** | Bronchospasm | Rare |
| **Severe** | Laryngeal edema | Very rare |
| **Severe** | Cardiac arrest | Extremely rare |
| **Severe** | Death | ~1 in 222,000 (estimated) |

#### Risk Factors for Adverse Reactions

- History of prior adverse reaction to fluorescein sodium
- History of [[atopy]], [[asthma]], or multiple drug allergies
- Cardiac disease
- Renal insufficiency (impaired dye clearance)

#### Contraindications

- Known hypersensitivity to fluorescein sodium
- Relative contraindication in pregnancy (FDA Pregnancy Category C; fluorescein crosses the [[placenta]])
- Caution in patients with significant renal or hepatic impairment

#### Emergency Preparedness

Any facility performing intravenous fluorescein angiography must have emergency resuscitation equipment and medications readily available, including:

- [[Epinephrine]] (1:1,000 for intramuscular injection)
- [[Diphenhydramine]] (IV/IM)
- [[Corticosteroids]] (IV)
- Oxygen and airway management equipment
- IV access supplies
- [[Cardiopulmonary resuscitation]] (CPR) capability

A thorough patient history regarding allergies, prior fluorescein reactions, asthma, and cardiac history should be obtained before every angiographic procedure. Some practitioners administer prophylactic antihistamines or corticosteroids to patients with a history of mild prior reactions, though this practice varies by institution.

## Drug Interactions and Considerations

Fluorescein sodium has relatively few clinically significant drug interactions. However, the following considerations are relevant:

- **[[Topical anesthetics]]**: Proparacaine and benoxinate are commonly co-administered with fluorescein and do not interfere with its diagnostic properties.
- **[[Timolol]]** and other topical [[beta-blockers]]: No significant interaction, though the preservative [[benzalkonium chloride]] (BAK) in some glaucoma medications may cause punctate staining that could be confused with disease-related staining.
- **[[Tetracycline]] antibiotics**: Theoretical interaction due to chelation, but not clinically significant at ophthalmic doses.
- **Soft contact lenses**: Fluorescein sodium is absorbed by hydrogel and silicone hydrogel lens materials, causing discoloration. Lenses should be removed before fluorescein instillation and not reinserted for at least **15–30 minutes** after the dye has been flushed from the tear film.

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Patients

Fluorescein sodium strips and solutions are safe for use in pediatric patients. In neonates and infants, fluorescein is used to assess for [[congenital nasolacrimal duct obstruction]] (using the fluorescein dye disappearance test) and to evaluate corneal integrity in cases of suspected [[congenital glaucoma]], [[birth trauma]], or [[ophthalmia neonatorum]]. For intravenous fluorescein angiography in children, the dose is weight-based (typically **7.5 mg/kg**), and sedation or general anesthesia may be required for adequate cooperation.

### Geriatric Patients

No specific dose adjustments are required for topical use in elderly patients. For intravenous angiography, renal function should be assessed, as age-related decline in [[glomerular filtration rate]] may prolong dye clearance and increase the risk of adverse effects.

### Pregnant and Lactating Women

Fluorescein sodium crosses the placenta and is excreted in breast milk. While topical ophthalmic use is generally considered low-risk, intravenous fluorescein angiography is typically deferred during pregnancy unless the diagnostic benefit clearly outweighs potential fetal risk. Lactating women are often advised to pump and discard breast milk for **24–48 hours** following intravenous fluorescein administration.

## Storage and Handling

- Fluorescein strips should be stored in a cool, dry environment and protected from moisture.
- Topical solutions should be stored at controlled room temperature (15–30°C) and protected from light.
- Intravenous solutions should be inspected for particulate matter and discoloration before use.
- Multi-dose fluorescein solutions must be discarded if contamination is suspected; single-use formulations are strongly preferred.
- Expired fluorescein products should not be used, as degradation may reduce fluorescence intensity and increase the risk of adverse reactions.

## Emerging Applications and Research

### Oral Fluorescein Angiography

Oral administration of fluorescein sodium (as a solution or capsule) has been investigated as a less invasive alternative to intravenous fluorescein angiography. While oral FA produces lower-intensity fluorescence and delayed transit times, it may be useful for screening purposes or in patients with poor venous access. However, it has not gained widespread clinical adoption due to inferior image quality compared to intravenous FA.

### Anterior Segment Fluorescein Angiography

Fluorescein angiography of the [[anterior segment]] (iris and conjunctival vasculature) is used in research and select clinical settings to evaluate [[iris neovascularization]] ([[rubeosis iridis]]), [[anterior segment ischemia]], and conjunctival/[[limbal]] vascular abnormalities.

### Intraoperative Fluorescein

Intravenous or intravitreal fluorescein sodium is increasingly used as an intraoperative adjunct during [[vitreoretinal surgery]], particularly for [[internal limiting membrane]] (ILM) peeling in [[macular hole]] repair and [[epiretinal membrane]] surgery. Fluorescein stains the ILM, improving visualization and facilitating complete membrane removal.

### Fluorescein-Guided Tumor Surgery

Emerging research has explored the use of fluorescein sodium in the identification and delineation of [[ocular surface neoplasia]] and intraocular tumors, leveraging the dye's preferential accumulation in tissues with disrupted vascular barriers.

---

## Summary

Fluorescein sodium remains an irreplaceable diagnostic tool in optometric and ophthalmic practice. Its exceptional fluorescence properties, favorable safety profile (particularly with topical use), and versatility across a wide range of clinical applications—from the detection of a simple [[corneal abrasion]] to the complex angiographic evaluation of [[diabetic retinopathy]]—ensure its continued central role in eye care. Clinicians must be familiar with proper instillation techniques, interpretation of fluorescein patterns, potential adverse effects (especially with intravenous use), and the critical importance of aseptic handling to prevent contamination.
