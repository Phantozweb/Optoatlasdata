

# Type VIII Collagen

---

**Type VIII collagen** is a short-chain, non-fibrillar [[collagen]] that plays a critical and specialized role in the structural biology of the [[eye]], particularly within the [[cornea]]. Classified as a network-forming collagen, Type VIII collagen is a major macromolecular component of [[Descemet's membrane]], the specialized [[basement membrane]] that underlies the [[corneal endothelium]]. Its significance in optometric and ophthalmologic practice extends from fundamental corneal physiology to the pathogenesis of several visually significant [[corneal dystrophies]], most notably [[Fuchs endothelial corneal dystrophy]] (FECD). Understanding the molecular biology, genetic underpinnings, and clinical implications of Type VIII collagen is essential for eye care professionals involved in the diagnosis and management of corneal disease.

---

## Molecular Biology and Biochemistry

### Gene Structure

Type VIII collagen is encoded by two distinct genes:

| Gene | Chromosomal Locus | Protein Product | Size (Approximate) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[COL8A1]]** | 3q12.1 | α1(VIII) chain | ~744 amino acids |
| **[[COL8A2]]** | 1p34.3-p32.3 | α2(VIII) chain | ~703 amino acids |

Both genes encode alpha chains that assemble into a heterotrimeric or homotrimeric molecule. The predominant stoichiometry in vivo is debated, but evidence supports the existence of both **α1(VIII)₂α2(VIII)₁ heterotrimers** and **α1(VIII)₃ or α2(VIII)₃ homotrimers** depending on the tissue context. Each alpha chain contains a characteristic collagenous domain flanked by non-collagenous (NC) domains at both the amino-terminal (NC2) and carboxy-terminal (NC1) ends. The NC1 domain is particularly significant, as it shares structural homology with the C1q complement family and is responsible for chain recognition, trimerization, and supramolecular assembly.

### Protein Structure

Type VIII collagen belongs to the **short-chain collagen** superfamily, which also includes [[Type X collagen]]. The molecule is approximately 130 nm in length, considerably shorter than the classical fibrillar collagens such as [[Type I collagen]] or [[Type V collagen]]. The triple-helical collagenous domain is interrupted by several imperfections in the Gly-X-Y repeat sequence, which confer flexibility to the molecule and are critical for its ability to form the characteristic hexagonal lattice networks observed in [[Descemet's membrane]].

The supramolecular assembly of Type VIII collagen results in a **hexagonal lattice** ultrastructure. This lattice is visible by [[transmission electron microscopy]] (TEM) and is one of the defining morphological features of the posterior non-banded zone (PNBZ) of Descemet's membrane. The lattice spacing is approximately 160 nm, and the network provides both structural support and a filtration scaffold for the [[corneal endothelium]].

### Post-Translational Modifications

Like other collagens, Type VIII collagen undergoes extensive [[post-translational modification]], including:

- **Hydroxylation** of [[proline]] and [[lysine]] residues by [[prolyl hydroxylase]] and [[lysyl hydroxylase]]
- **Glycosylation** of hydroxylysine residues
- **Disulfide bond formation** within the NC1 domain, critical for trimer stabilization
- **Proteolytic processing** of propeptide domains during secretion and extracellular assembly

These modifications are essential for proper folding, secretion, and incorporation into the [[extracellular matrix]] (ECM).

## Tissue Distribution in the Eye

### Descemet's Membrane

The primary ocular location of Type VIII collagen is **[[Descemet's membrane]]**, the thick (10–15 μm in adults) basement membrane situated between the [[corneal stroma]] and the [[corneal endothelium]]. Descemet's membrane is composed of two ultrastructurally distinct zones:

| Zone | Description | Type VIII Collagen Content | Developmental Timing |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Anterior Banded Layer (ABL)** | Exhibits a characteristic 110 nm banding pattern | Present, but in lower concentration | Produced prenatally (in utero) |
| **Posterior Non-Banded Layer (PNBL)** | Amorphous, homogeneous appearance | Major structural component; hexagonal lattice | Produced postnatally, accumulates throughout life |

The PNBL thickens progressively with age, and its hexagonal lattice architecture is directly attributable to the supramolecular assembly of Type VIII collagen. This lattice is believed to serve as a structural scaffold that supports [[corneal endothelial cell]] adhesion, migration, and function. In addition to Type VIII collagen, Descemet's membrane contains [[Type IV collagen]], [[laminin]], [[fibronectin]], [[nidogen]], and [[perlecan]], but Type VIII collagen is the dominant and defining structural macromolecule of the posterior layer.

### Other Ocular Tissues

While Descemet's membrane is the principal site, Type VIII collagen has also been detected in:

- **[[Bruch's membrane]]** of the [[retina]]/[[choroid]] complex, where it may contribute to the structural integrity of the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE) basement membrane
- **[[Trabecular meshwork]]**, where it may influence [[aqueous humor]] outflow dynamics and has potential implications for [[glaucoma]] pathophysiology
- **Vascular endothelium** of ocular blood vessels, consistent with its known expression in systemic vascular endothelia

### Extraocular Distribution

Outside the eye, Type VIII collagen is expressed in vascular [[endothelial cells]], [[smooth muscle cells]], and certain epithelial tissues. It has been implicated in [[atherosclerosis]], vascular remodeling, and wound healing. However, its most clinically significant role—and the focus of this article—remains its function within the cornea.

## Physiological Functions

Type VIII collagen serves multiple critical functions in corneal biology:

### 1. Structural Scaffold of Descemet's Membrane

The hexagonal lattice formed by Type VIII collagen provides the mechanical framework of Descemet's membrane. This scaffold must withstand [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) and maintain corneal integrity. The lattice also serves as a molecular sieve, regulating the passage of nutrients and metabolites between the [[aqueous humor]] and the [[corneal stroma]].

### 2. Corneal Endothelial Cell Support

[[Corneal endothelial cells]] are terminally differentiated, non-replicative cells in humans. Their attachment to Descemet's membrane is mediated in part by [[integrin]] receptors that bind to Type VIII collagen and associated ECM molecules. Disruption of this interaction—whether through genetic mutation, aging, or disease—can lead to endothelial cell dysfunction, detachment, and ultimately [[corneal edema]].

### 3. Regulation of Cell Behavior

In vitro studies have demonstrated that Type VIII collagen influences [[cell proliferation]], [[cell migration]], and [[matrix metalloproteinase]] (MMP) expression. The NC1 domain of the α1(VIII) chain, in particular, has been shown to promote endothelial cell spreading and to modulate signaling pathways involved in tissue remodeling. These properties suggest that Type VIII collagen is not merely a passive structural element but an active participant in corneal homeostasis and wound healing.

### 4. Biomechanical Properties

The flexibility imparted by interruptions in the triple-helical domain allows Type VIII collagen networks to accommodate mechanical stress without catastrophic failure. This is particularly important in the cornea, which is subject to continuous biomechanical forces from IOP, blinking, and eye rubbing.

## Pathophysiology

### Genetic Mutations in COL8A2 and Corneal Dystrophies

Mutations in the **[[COL8A2]]** gene are the most clinically significant genetic alterations involving Type VIII collagen in ophthalmology. These mutations have been directly linked to two corneal dystrophies:

#### Fuchs Endothelial Corneal Dystrophy (FECD)

**[[Fuchs endothelial corneal dystrophy]]** is the most common corneal dystrophy requiring [[corneal transplantation]] in developed nations. It is characterized by progressive loss of [[corneal endothelial cells]], formation of excrescences on Descemet's membrane called **[[guttae]]** (singular: gutta), thickening of Descemet's membrane, and eventual [[corneal edema]] with loss of visual acuity.

The **early-onset form** of FECD (presenting in the third to fourth decade of life) has been definitively linked to missense mutations in COL8A2. The most well-characterized mutation is:

- **p.Gln455Lys (Q455K)**: A glutamine-to-lysine substitution at position 455 of the α2(VIII) chain. This mutation was identified in a large, multi-generational family with autosomal dominant early-onset FECD. Functional studies have demonstrated that the Q455K mutation disrupts the normal folding and assembly of the Type VIII collagen trimer, leading to intracellular accumulation of misfolded protein, [[endoplasmic reticulum]] (ER) stress, activation of the [[unfolded protein response]] (UPR), and ultimately [[apoptosis]] of corneal endothelial cells.

- **p.Leu450Trp (L450W)**: Another missense mutation in COL8A2 associated with early-onset FECD and posterior polymorphous corneal dystrophy (PPCD). This mutation similarly affects the collagenous domain and disrupts triple-helix stability.

The pathophysiological cascade in COL8A2-related FECD can be summarized as follows:

1. **Missense mutation** in COL8A2 → abnormal α2(VIII) chain
2. **Misfolding** of the Type VIII collagen trimer in the [[endoplasmic reticulum]]
3. **ER stress** and activation of the [[unfolded protein response]] (UPR)
4. **Apoptosis** of [[corneal endothelial cells]]
5. **Abnormal Descemet's membrane** deposition → formation of [[guttae]]
6. **Progressive endothelial cell loss** → failure of the endothelial pump mechanism
7. **[[Corneal edema]]** → [[corneal decompensation]] → vision loss

| Mutation | Amino Acid Change | Associated Condition | Inheritance | Onset |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Q455K | Gln → Lys at 455 | Early-onset FECD | [[Autosomal dominant]] | 3rd–4th decade |
| L450W | Leu → Trp at 450 | Early-onset FECD / PPCD | Autosomal dominant | 3rd–4th decade |
| R155Q | Arg → Gln at 155 | FECD (reported in some cohorts) | Autosomal dominant | Variable |
| R304Q | Arg → Gln at 304 | FECD (reported in some cohorts) | Autosomal dominant | Variable |

It is important to note that the **late-onset form** of FECD (presenting in the fifth to seventh decade), which is far more common, is typically associated with mutations in other genes, most notably the **[[TCF4]]** trinucleotide repeat expansion (CTG18.1) on chromosome 18. However, the downstream pathology—guttae formation, Descemet's membrane thickening, and endothelial cell loss—shares common features with COL8A2-related disease, suggesting convergent pathophysiological mechanisms involving Descemet's membrane and its Type VIII collagen scaffold.

#### Posterior Polymorphous Corneal Dystrophy (PPCD)

**[[Posterior polymorphous corneal dystrophy]]** is a rare, autosomal dominant [[corneal dystrophy]] characterized by abnormal morphology of the corneal endothelium, which may exhibit epithelial-like characteristics including [[microvilli]], [[desmosomes]], and [[cytokeratin]] expression. PPCD has been linked to mutations in several genes, including **COL8A2** (specifically the L450W mutation), **[[ZEB1]]** (also known as TCF8), and **[[OVOL2]]**.

In COL8A2-related PPCD, the disruption of Type VIII collagen assembly is thought to alter the Descemet's membrane microenvironment, leading to aberrant differentiation signals for the overlying endothelial cells. This results in the characteristic metaplastic transformation of endothelial cells toward an epithelial phenotype.

### Age-Related Changes

Even in the absence of genetic mutations, Type VIII collagen and Descemet's membrane undergo significant age-related changes:

- **Progressive thickening** of the PNBL (from ~3 μm at birth to 10–15 μm in elderly individuals)
- **Accumulation of non-collagenous material** within the membrane
- **Alterations in hexagonal lattice regularity**
- **Formation of peripheral Hassall-Henle bodies** (peripheral guttae), which are considered a normal aging phenomenon but share ultrastructural similarities with the central guttae of FECD

These age-related changes may predispose to endothelial dysfunction and lower the threshold for clinically significant disease in individuals with subclinical genetic variants in COL8A1 or COL8A2.

### Role in Corneal Wound Healing

Following corneal injury or surgery (e.g., [[cataract surgery]], [[Descemet's stripping automated endothelial keratoplasty]] [DSAEK]), the corneal endothelium must regenerate Descemet's membrane. Type VIII collagen is actively synthesized and deposited during this process. Abnormalities in Type VIII collagen production or assembly can impair wound healing and contribute to graft failure in [[corneal transplantation]].

## Clinical Presentation (Symptoms)

The clinical manifestations of Type VIII collagen-related pathology are primarily those of the associated corneal dystrophies, particularly FECD and PPCD.

### Early Symptoms

- **Morning [[visual blur]]**: Due to overnight corneal edema (reduced evaporation during sleep exacerbates endothelial pump failure). Vision typically improves as the day progresses and evaporation assists in corneal dehydration.
- **[[Glare]]** and **[[halos]]** around lights: Caused by light scattering from corneal edema and guttae
- **Reduced [[contrast sensitivity]]**: Even before measurable loss of [[visual acuity]]
- **[[Photophobia]]**: Mild to moderate, related to stromal haze

### Progressive Symptoms

- **Persistent [[corneal edema]]**: As endothelial cell density falls below the critical threshold (~500–800 cells/mm²), the cornea can no longer maintain deturgescence
- **[[Epithelial bullae]]** ([[bullous keratopathy]]): Fluid accumulates beneath and within the [[corneal epithelium]], forming painful blisters that may rupture, causing acute pain and increasing the risk of [[microbial keratitis]]
- **Progressive loss of [[visual acuity]]**: From stromal and epithelial edema, subepithelial fibrosis, and eventual [[corneal scarring]]
- **[[Ocular pain]]**: From ruptured epithelial bullae and secondary inflammation

### PPCD-Specific Findings

- Often **asymptomatic** in mild cases
- **Vesicular, band-like, or geographic lesions** on the posterior corneal surface visible on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]
- Potential for **[[corectopia]]**, **[[peripheral anterior synechiae]]**, and secondary [[glaucoma]] in severe cases
- Rarely, **corneal edema** if endothelial function is significantly compromised

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

| Diagnostic Modality | Findings in Type VIII Collagen-Related Disease | Clinical Utility |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]** | Central corneal guttae (beaten-metal appearance), Descemet's membrane thickening, corneal edema, epithelial bullae | Primary screening tool; essential for grading severity |
| **[[Specular microscopy]]** | Reduced [[endothelial cell density]] (ECD), [[polymegathism]] (variation in cell size), [[pleomorphism]] (variation in cell shape), dark areas corresponding to guttae | Quantitative assessment of endothelial health |
| **[[Confocal microscopy]]** (e.g., Heidelberg HRT-III with Rostock Cornea Module) | Hyperreflective guttae, endothelial cell morphology, Descemet's membrane abnormalities | High-resolution in vivo imaging of corneal layers |
| **[[Anterior segment optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT)** | Descemet's membrane thickening, corneal edema (increased [[central corneal thickness]]), Descemet's membrane detachment | Non-contact, cross-sectional imaging |
| **[[Pachymetry]]** (ultrasonic or optical) | Increased [[central corneal thickness]] (CCT) >600 μm suggests edema | Monitoring disease progression |
| **[[Corneal topography]]/[[Tomography]]** | Irregular astigmatism in advanced disease; posterior elevation changes | Assessment of corneal shape and refractive impact |

### Genetic Testing

**[[Genetic testing]]** for COL8A2 mutations is available and is particularly indicated in:

- Patients with **early-onset FECD** (before age 50)
- Patients with a **strong family history** of corneal dystrophy
- Patients with **atypical presentations** of posterior corneal dystrophy
- **Genetic counseling** for affected families

Testing typically involves **[[Sanger sequencing]]** or **[[next-generation sequencing]] (NGS)** panels targeting corneal dystrophy-associated genes, including COL8A1, COL8A2, [[TCF4]], [[ZEB1]], [[SLC4A11]], and [[LOXHD1]].

### Histopathology and Ultrastructural Analysis

In cases where corneal tissue is available (e.g., from [[penetrating keratoplasty]] or [[Descemet's membrane endothelial keratoplasty]] [DMEK] specimens):

- **[[Light microscopy]]**: Thickened Descemet's membrane with posterior excrescences (guttae); attenuated endothelial cell layer
- **[[Periodic acid-Schiff]] (PAS) staining**: Highlights the thickened, PAS-positive Descemet's membrane
- **[[Transmission electron microscopy]] (TEM)**: Disruption of the normal hexagonal lattice of Type VIII collagen in the PNBL; abnormal collagen fibril deposition; posterior collagenous layer (PCL) formation in advanced disease
- **[[Immunohistochemistry]]**: Antibodies against α1(VIII) and α2(VIII) chains can demonstrate altered expression patterns and distribution within Descemet's membrane

### Grading Systems

The **Krachmer grading scale** is widely used to classify the severity of FECD based on guttae distribution:

| Grade | Description |
|---|---|
| 0 | No guttae |
| 1 | 1–12 scattered central guttae |
| 2 | >12 central non-confluent guttae |
| 3 | 1–2 mm zone of confluent central guttae |
| 4 | 2–5 mm zone of confluent central guttae |
| 5 | >5 mm zone of confluent central guttae with or without edema |

## Management and Treatment

### Conservative (Non-Surgical) Management

In early-stage disease, conservative measures aim to manage symptoms and delay surgical intervention:

- **Hypertonic saline solutions and ointments** (e.g., [[sodium chloride]] 5% drops and ointment): These hyperosmotic agents draw fluid from the edematous cornea, temporarily improving vision and comfort. Drops are used during the day; ointment is applied at bedtime to counteract overnight edema accumulation.
- **Hair dryer technique**: Directing warm air (at arm's length) toward the open eyes upon waking can accelerate corneal dehydration and improve morning vision.
- **[[Bandage contact lens]]**: In cases with painful epithelial bullae, a therapeutic soft contact lens can protect exposed nerve endings and promote epithelial healing. However, this carries a risk of [[infectious keratitis]] and requires close monitoring.
- **[[Intraocular pressure]] management**: Elevated IOP exacerbates corneal edema by increasing the hydrostatic pressure gradient across the endothelium. [[Topical anti-glaucoma medications]] may be beneficial, though [[prostaglandin analogs]] should be used with caution due to potential pro-inflammatory effects.
- **Anti-inflammatory therapy**: Topical [[corticosteroids]] or [[non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs]] (NSAIDs) may be used to manage associated inflammation.

### Pharmacological Interventions and Emerging Therapies

#### Rho-Kinase (ROCK) Inhibitors

**[[Rho-kinase inhibitors]]** (e.g., [[ripasudil]], [[netarsudil]]) represent a promising pharmacological approach to Type VIII collagen-related endothelial disease. ROCK inhibitors promote:

- [[Corneal endothelial cell]] proliferation
- Cell migration and wound healing
- Inhibition of apoptosis

Clinical trials, particularly those led by Kinoshita and colleagues in Japan, have demonstrated that topical ROCK inhibitor application can promote corneal clearing in early FECD and after [[Descemet's stripping only]] (DSO) procedures. While not yet standard of care globally, ROCK inhibitors represent a paradigm shift toward pharmacological management of endothelial dysfunction.

#### Gene Therapy

Preclinical research is exploring **[[gene therapy]]** approaches to correct COL8A2 mutations. Strategies include:

- **[[CRISPR-Cas9]]** gene editing to correct specific missense mutations
- **[[Antisense oligonucleotides]]** to modulate aberrant mRNA expression
- **Viral vector-mediated gene delivery** to restore normal α2(VIII) chain production

These approaches remain investigational but hold significant promise for early-onset, monogenic forms of FECD caused by COL8A2 mutations.

#### Cell-Based Therapies

**Cultured [[corneal endothelial cell]] injection** is an emerging therapy in which laboratory-expanded corneal endothelial cells are injected into the anterior chamber after Descemet's stripping. Combined with ROCK inhibitor supplementation, this approach has shown remarkable success in Phase I/II clinical trials in Japan, with significant improvements in corneal clarity and endothelial cell density. The success of this therapy depends critically on the ability of transplanted cells to adhere to and interact with the residual Descemet's membrane or bare stroma—a process mediated in part by Type VIII collagen and its integrin-binding properties.

### Surgical Management

When conservative measures fail and vision is significantly impaired, surgical intervention is indicated:

#### Endothelial Keratoplasty (EK)

Endothelial keratoplasty has largely replaced [[penetrating keratoplasty]] (PK) as the surgical treatment of choice for endothelial dysfunction:

| Procedure | Description | Advantages | Relevance to Type VIII Collagen |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[DSAEK]]** (Descemet's Stripping Automated Endothelial Keratoplasty) | Transplantation of donor posterior stroma, Descemet's membrane, and endothelium | Faster recovery than PK; maintains globe integrity | Replaces diseased Descemet's membrane containing mutant Type VIII collagen with healthy donor tissue |
| **[[DMEK]]** (Descemet's Membrane Endothelial Keratoplasty) | Transplantation of donor Descemet's membrane and endothelium only | Superior visual outcomes; lower rejection rates | Directly replaces the Type VIII collagen-containing Descemet's membrane |
| **[[Ultra-thin DSAEK]]** | DSAEK with graft thickness <100 μm | Intermediate between DSAEK and DMEK in outcomes | Similar to DSAEK |
| **[[DSO]]** (Descemet's Stripping Only) | Removal of central Descemet's membrane without transplantation; relies on endothelial cell migration from periphery | Avoids donor tissue; no rejection risk | Removes mutant Type VIII collagen scaffold; relies on regeneration of new Descemet's membrane with potentially normal Type VIII collagen from peripheral endothelial cells |

#### Penetrating Keratoplasty (PK)

**[[Penetrating keratoplasty]]** involves full-thickness corneal replacement and is reserved for cases with significant stromal scarring or when EK is not feasible. While effective, PK carries higher risks of [[graft rejection]], [[astigmatism]], and wound-related complications compared to EK.

#### Descemet's Stripping Only (DSO) with Adjunctive ROCK Inhibitor

This innovative approach, sometimes termed **"Descemetorhexis without endothelial keratoplasty" (DWEK)**, involves:

1. Central descemetorhexis (removal of 4–5 mm of central Descemet's membrane)
2. Topical ROCK inhibitor application postoperatively
3. Reliance on peripheral endothelial cell migration to repopulate the denuded area

This technique is particularly appealing for COL8A2-related disease because it removes the structurally abnormal Descemet's membrane (containing mutant Type VIII collagen) and allows the peripheral endothelial cells—which may produce more normal Type VIII collagen—to regenerate the membrane. Early results are promising, particularly in patients with good peripheral endothelial cell reserves.

## Animal Models

Several animal models have been developed to study Type VIII collagen function and COL8A2-related disease:

- **Col8a2 Q455K knock-in mouse**: Recapitulates features of early-onset FECD, including endothelial cell loss, Descemet's membrane thickening, and guttae-like excrescences. This model has been instrumental in elucidating the role of ER stress and UPR activation in disease pathogenesis.
- **Col8a1/Col8a2 double knockout mouse**: Demonstrates that complete absence of Type VIII collagen leads to a thinner Descemet's membrane with altered biomechanical properties, though the corneal phenotype is surprisingly mild, suggesting compensatory mechanisms involving other ECM components.
- **Col8a2 L450W knock-in mouse**: Models the PPCD-associated mutation and exhibits posterior corneal abnormalities.

These models have been invaluable for testing potential therapeutic interventions, including ROCK inhibitors and gene therapy approaches.

## Biomarkers and Future Directions

### Aqueous Humor Biomarkers

Research has identified fragments of Type VIII collagen (particularly NC1 domain fragments) in the [[aqueous humor]] of patients with FECD. These fragments may serve as **biomarkers** for disease activity and progression, potentially enabling:

- Early detection of subclinical disease
- Monitoring of treatment response
- Prognostication for surgical outcomes

### Tissue Engineering

Type VIII collagen is being investigated as a component of **bioengineered corneal endothelial scaffolds**. Recombinant Type VIII collagen, either alone or in combination with other ECM proteins, can be used to create artificial Descemet's membrane substitutes that support endothelial cell attachment and function. This approach could eventually reduce dependence on donor corneal tissue.

### Proteomics and Systems Biology

Advanced **[[proteomic]]** analyses of Descemet's membrane from FECD patients versus age-matched controls have revealed not only alterations in Type VIII collagen abundance and processing but also changes in associated ECM proteins, providing a systems-level understanding of disease pathogenesis.

---

## Summary

Type VIII collagen is a cornerstone molecule of corneal biology, serving as the primary structural component of the posterior non-banded layer of [[Descemet's membrane]]. Mutations in the [[COL8A2]] gene cause early-onset [[Fuchs endothelial corneal dystrophy]] and contribute to [[posterior polymorphous corneal dystrophy]], both of which can lead to significant visual morbidity. Advances in [[specular microscopy]], [[confocal microscopy]], [[anterior segment OCT]], and [[genetic testing]] have improved diagnostic precision, while innovations in [[endothelial keratoplasty]], [[ROCK inhibitors]], [[cell-based therapies]], and [[gene therapy]] are transforming the therapeutic landscape. A thorough understanding of Type VIII collagen—from its molecular assembly to its clinical implications—is essential for the modern eye care professional.
