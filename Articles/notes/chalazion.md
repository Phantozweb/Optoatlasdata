

# Chalazion

---

**Chalazion** (plural: **chalazia**; from the Greek *khalazion*, meaning "small hailstone") is a chronic, sterile, lipogranulomatous inflammatory lesion of the [[eyelid]] that arises from obstruction and subsequent granulomatous inflammation of a [[Meibomian gland]] (or, less commonly, a [[gland of Zeis]]). It is one of the most frequently encountered eyelid pathologies in clinical [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] practice. Although often confused with a [[hordeolum]] (stye), a chalazion is distinguished by its chronic, non-infectious granulomatous nature, its typical lack of acute pain, and its tendency to persist for weeks to months without spontaneous resolution. Chalazia can occur at any age but are most prevalent in adults between 30 and 50 years of age. Understanding the pathophysiology, clinical presentation, differential diagnosis, and stepwise management of chalazion is essential for every eye care practitioner.

---

## Epidemiology

Chalazia are among the most common eyelid lesions presenting to eye care professionals. Key epidemiological features include:

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Peak age of onset** | 30–50 years |
| **Sex predilection** | Slight male predominance in some studies; others report equal distribution |
| **Recurrence rate** | Approximately 10–25% of patients experience recurrence |
| **Bilateral involvement** | Common, particularly in patients with underlying [[blepharitis]] or [[meibomian gland dysfunction]] (MGD) |
| **Pediatric prevalence** | Less common than in adults; when present, may be associated with [[refractive error]] (particularly [[hyperopia]]) or [[astigmatism]] |
| **Associated systemic conditions** | [[Rosacea]], [[seborrheic dermatitis]], [[acne vulgaris]], [[hyperlipidemia]], viral conjunctivitis (preceding), [[diabetes mellitus]] |

Population-based studies suggest that chalazia account for a significant proportion of eyelid referrals to both optometric and ophthalmic clinics. Seasonal variation has been reported, with some studies noting increased incidence during periods of high environmental allergen load or dry atmospheric conditions, both of which may exacerbate [[meibomian gland dysfunction]].

---

## Anatomy and Relevant Structures

A thorough understanding of eyelid anatomy is essential for comprehending chalazion pathogenesis.

### Meibomian Glands

The [[Meibomian glands]] (tarsal glands) are large, modified [[sebaceous glands]] embedded within the [[tarsal plate]] of both the upper and lower eyelids. There are approximately **25–40 glands** in the upper lid and **20–30 glands** in the lower lid. Each gland consists of multiple acini (secretory units) that drain into a central duct, which opens at the [[lid margin]] posterior to the [[gray line]] (the mucocutaneous junction). The glands produce **meibum**, a complex lipid secretion that forms the outermost [[lipid layer]] of the [[tear film]]. This lipid layer is critical for:

- Preventing evaporation of the underlying [[aqueous layer]]
- Stabilizing the [[tear film]]
- Providing a smooth optical surface over the [[cornea]]
- Preventing overflow of tears ([[epiphora]])

### Glands of Zeis

The [[glands of Zeis]] are smaller, modified sebaceous glands associated with the [[eyelash]] follicles. Obstruction of a gland of Zeis can produce a superficial chalazion (or external chalazion), though this is far less common than the deep (internal) chalazion arising from a Meibomian gland.

### Tarsal Plate

The [[tarsal plate]] is a dense connective tissue structure that provides structural rigidity to the eyelid. The Meibomian glands are entirely embedded within this plate. The upper tarsal plate measures approximately 10–12 mm in vertical height, while the lower tarsal plate measures approximately 4–5 mm. The anatomical relationship between the glands and the tarsal plate is clinically significant because chalazia arising within the tarsus may present as firm, well-defined nodules that are palpable through the eyelid skin or visible upon [[eversion of the eyelid]].

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of chalazion involves a well-characterized sequence of events:

### 1. Meibomian Gland Obstruction

The initiating event is obstruction of the orifice or central duct of a Meibomian gland. This obstruction may result from:

- **Inspissation (thickening) of meibum**: Changes in the composition of meibomian lipids—particularly an increase in saturated fatty acids and a decrease in unsaturated fatty acids—lead to increased melting point and viscosity of the secretion. This altered meibum solidifies within the duct, creating a physical blockage.
- **Ductal epithelial hyperkeratinization**: Keratinization of the ductal epithelium narrows or occludes the gland orifice.
- **Chronic [[blepharitis]]**: Both anterior blepharitis (often staphylococcal) and posterior blepharitis ([[meibomian gland dysfunction]]) predispose to gland obstruction through inflammatory mediators, bacterial lipases that alter meibum composition, and biofilm formation along the lid margin.
- **External factors**: Use of [[contact lenses]], heavy eye makeup, poor [[lid hygiene]], and environmental irritants may contribute to obstruction.

### 2. Lipid Retention and Glandular Rupture

Once the duct is obstructed, meibum continues to be produced by the acinar cells but cannot be expressed onto the lid margin. This leads to progressive distension of the gland and eventual rupture of the acinar walls or ductal epithelium. The retained lipid material is released into the surrounding tarsal connective tissue.

### 3. Granulomatous Inflammatory Response

The extravasated lipid material acts as a foreign body within the tissue, provoking a **chronic granulomatous inflammatory reaction**. Histopathologically, this is characterized by:

- **Lipogranuloma formation**: Central lipid vacuoles surrounded by epithelioid histiocytes, multinucleated giant cells (including [[Langhans giant cells]] and foreign-body giant cells), lymphocytes, plasma cells, and polymorphonuclear leukocytes.
- **Fibrous capsule**: Over time, a fibrous capsule may form around the granuloma, contributing to the firm, well-circumscribed nature of the lesion.
- **Absence of infectious organisms**: Unlike a hordeolum, a chalazion is sterile. Bacterial cultures of chalazion contents are typically negative, although some studies have identified commensal organisms such as *[[Staphylococcus epidermidis]]* or *[[Propionibacterium acnes]]* within the lesion, suggesting a possible contributory role in some cases.

### 4. Chronic Lesion Formation

The granulomatous nodule may remain stable, slowly enlarge, or occasionally undergo secondary infection (converting to an acute hordeolum-like presentation). Without intervention, chalazia may persist for months or even years. Spontaneous resolution occurs in a subset of cases, typically through gradual resorption of the lipid material and resolution of the inflammatory infiltrate.

### Role of Meibomian Gland Dysfunction (MGD)

[[Meibomian gland dysfunction]] is the single most important predisposing factor for chalazion formation. MGD is characterized by chronic abnormalities of the Meibomian glands, commonly involving terminal duct obstruction and/or qualitative/quantitative changes in glandular secretion. Patients with MGD have altered meibum that is more viscous, opaque, and granular, predisposing to recurrent duct obstruction and chalazion formation. The relationship between MGD and chalazion is bidirectional: MGD predisposes to chalazia, and recurrent chalazia may further damage gland architecture, worsening MGD over time.

### Role of Rosacea

[[Ocular rosacea]] is a significant systemic risk factor. Patients with rosacea demonstrate increased levels of inflammatory cytokines, altered meibomian gland function, and increased colonization of the lid margin by *[[Demodex]]* mites, all of which contribute to recurrent chalazion formation.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

Patients with chalazion typically present with the following complaints:

- **Painless or mildly tender eyelid lump**: The hallmark symptom. The nodule develops gradually over days to weeks and is usually non-tender or only mildly tender to palpation, distinguishing it from the acutely painful [[hordeolum]].
- **Eyelid swelling**: Localized swelling of the upper or lower eyelid, which may be cosmetically bothersome.
- **Eyelid heaviness or fullness**: Particularly with larger lesions of the upper lid.
- **Blurred vision**: Large chalazia may induce [[astigmatism]] (typically against-the-rule astigmatism) by exerting mechanical pressure on the [[cornea]], causing corneal flattening in the meridian of the lesion. This is more common with upper lid chalazia.
- **Foreign body sensation**: Especially when the lesion is large enough to contact the [[bulbar conjunctiva]] or corneal surface.
- **Tearing ([[epiphora]])**: Due to mechanical disruption of the tear film or irritation of the ocular surface.
- **Redness**: Mild erythema of the overlying skin or [[palpebral conjunctiva]] may be present, particularly in early or inflamed lesions.

### Signs

| Sign | Description |
|---|---|
| **Firm, round, non-tender nodule** | Palpable within the tarsal plate; typically 2–8 mm in diameter but may be larger |
| **Conjunctival granuloma** | Eversion of the eyelid reveals a localized area of elevated, hyperemic, or grayish-yellow tissue on the [[palpebral conjunctiva]] overlying the lesion |
| **Pointing** | Some chalazia may "point" toward the skin surface (external chalazion) or toward the conjunctival surface (internal chalazion) |
| **Lid margin changes** | Inspissated meibomian gland orifices, lid margin telangiectasia, collarettes, or other signs of [[blepharitis]]/[[MGD]] |
| **Corneal changes** | [[Corneal dellen]], [[superficial punctate keratitis]] (SPK), or induced [[astigmatism]] in cases of large or long-standing chalazia |
| **Mechanical [[ptosis]]** | Large upper lid chalazia may cause drooping of the eyelid due to the weight of the lesion |
| **Preseptal cellulitis** | Rarely, secondary infection of a chalazion may produce [[preseptal cellulitis]] with diffuse lid erythema, warmth, and tenderness |

### Location

Chalazia occur more frequently in the **upper eyelid** than the lower eyelid, reflecting the greater number and size of Meibomian glands in the upper tarsus. They may be solitary or multiple, unilateral or bilateral.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis

The diagnosis of chalazion is primarily **clinical**, based on history and physical examination. Key diagnostic steps include:

1. **History**: Duration of the lesion, presence or absence of pain, history of recurrence, associated conditions (rosacea, blepharitis, seborrheic dermatitis), contact lens use, and cosmetic product use.
2. **External examination**: Inspection of the eyelids for a visible or palpable nodule, assessment of lid margin health, and evaluation for signs of MGD or blepharitis.
3. **Eyelid eversion**: Essential for visualizing the conjunctival surface overlying the lesion. A chalazion typically appears as a localized, elevated, grayish or yellowish area surrounded by hyperemic conjunctiva. A lipogranuloma or polypoid granulation tissue may be visible.
4. **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: Allows detailed examination of the lid margin, Meibomian gland orifices, tear film quality, and corneal surface. Meibomian gland expression can be performed to assess the quality and expressibility of meibum.
5. **Assessment of visual acuity and refraction**: To detect any induced [[astigmatism]] or visual disturbance caused by the lesion.

### Differential Diagnosis

It is critical to differentiate chalazion from other eyelid lesions, some of which may be malignant. The differential diagnosis includes:

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| **[[Hordeolum]] (stye)** | Acute, painful, erythematous, often with a pointing abscess; may be external (gland of Zeis/Moll) or internal (Meibomian gland) |
| **[[Sebaceous gland carcinoma]]** | Rare but aggressive malignancy; may masquerade as a recurrent or atypical chalazion; suspect in elderly patients with recurrent chalazia in the same location, loss of lashes ([[madarosis]]), or pagetoid spread to the conjunctiva |
| **[[Basal cell carcinoma]]** | Pearly, nodular lesion with telangiectasia; may ulcerate; most common eyelid malignancy |
| **[[Squamous cell carcinoma]]** | Firm, irregular, ulcerated lesion; less common than BCC on the eyelid |
| **[[Pyogenic granuloma]]** | Rapidly growing, pedunculated, vascular lesion; often follows trauma or surgery |
| **[[Dermoid cyst]]** | Congenital, firm, subcutaneous nodule; typically at the lateral brow |
| **[[Epidermoid cyst]] (inclusion cyst)** | Smooth, round, subcutaneous cyst; freely mobile |
| **[[Molluscum contagiosum]]** | Umbilicated papule(s); may cause chronic follicular [[conjunctivitis]] |

> **⚠ Clinical Pearl:** Any chalazion that recurs in the same location, fails to respond to standard treatment, or presents with atypical features (e.g., loss of eyelashes, ulceration, destruction of lid architecture, unilateral chronic blepharoconjunctivitis) should raise suspicion for [[sebaceous gland carcinoma]] and warrants **histopathological examination** of excised tissue. This is particularly important in patients over 60 years of age.

### Histopathology

When tissue is obtained (via incision and curettage or excisional biopsy), the characteristic histopathological findings of a chalazion include:

- **Lipogranulomatous inflammation**: Central lipid vacuoles surrounded by epithelioid histiocytes and multinucleated giant cells
- **Chronic inflammatory infiltrate**: Lymphocytes, plasma cells, and occasional neutrophils
- **Fibrous capsule**: Variable degree of fibrosis surrounding the granuloma
- **Absence of caseation necrosis**: Distinguishes chalazion from [[tuberculosis]]-related granulomas

### Imaging

Imaging is rarely required for the diagnosis of chalazion. However, in atypical or recurrent cases, the following may be considered:

- **[[Meibography]]**: Infrared imaging of the Meibomian glands to assess gland architecture, dropout, and obstruction. Useful for evaluating underlying MGD.
- **Anterior segment [[optical coherence tomography]] (AS-OCT)**: Can delineate the size, depth, and internal structure of the lesion.
- **Ultrasound biomicroscopy (UBM)**: Rarely used; may help differentiate cystic from solid lesions.
- **MRI/CT**: Reserved for cases where deep orbital extension or malignancy is suspected.

## Management

The management of chalazion follows a stepwise approach, beginning with conservative measures and escalating to procedural or surgical intervention when necessary.

### Conservative Management (First-Line)

Conservative therapy is the initial approach for most chalazia, particularly those of recent onset (< 4–6 weeks) and small size. Spontaneous resolution rates with conservative management range from **25% to 50%** within 6 months.

#### Warm Compresses

- **Mechanism**: Application of heat to the eyelid softens the inspissated meibum, promotes gland expression, and enhances blood flow to the area, facilitating resolution of the granulomatous inflammation.
- **Technique**: A clean, warm compress (heated to approximately 40–45°C / 104–113°F) is applied to the closed eyelid for **10–15 minutes**, **2–4 times daily**. Commercially available heated eye masks (e.g., microwaveable bead masks, electric heated masks) provide more consistent and sustained heat delivery than warm washcloths.
- **Duration**: Should be continued for a minimum of **2–4 weeks** before considering the treatment a failure.

#### Lid Hygiene

- **Lid scrubs**: Gentle cleansing of the lid margin with diluted baby shampoo, commercially available lid scrub pads (e.g., containing [[tea tree oil]] or [[hypochlorous acid]]), or prescribed lid hygiene solutions helps remove debris, biofilm, and inspissated material from the gland orifices.
- **Meibomian gland expression**: Following warm compresses, gentle digital massage of the eyelid in a direction toward the lid margin can help express retained meibum from obstructed glands.

#### Lid Massage

- Performed immediately after warm compresses, using a clean fingertip or cotton-tipped applicator.
- For upper lid chalazia: massage downward toward the lid margin.
- For lower lid chalazia: massage upward toward the lid margin.
- Firm but gentle pressure is applied to promote expression of gland contents.

### Pharmacological Interventions

#### Topical Antibiotics

- **Indication**: Not routinely required for uncomplicated chalazion (which is sterile). However, topical antibiotics may be prescribed when there is concurrent [[blepharitis]], secondary infection, or as prophylaxis following procedural intervention.
- **Common agents**: [[Erythromycin]] ointment, [[bacitracin]] ointment, [[azithromycin]] ophthalmic solution (1%), or [[tobramycin]] drops/ointment.
- **Azithromycin ophthalmic solution**: Has anti-inflammatory properties in addition to antibacterial activity and has been shown to improve meibomian gland function.

#### Topical Corticosteroids

- **Indication**: May be used for inflamed chalazia to reduce surrounding inflammation.
- **Common agents**: [[Loteprednol etabonate]] 0.5%, [[fluorometholone]] 0.1%, or [[prednisolone acetate]] 1%.
- **Caution**: Prolonged use carries risks of elevated [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP), [[posterior subcapsular cataract]], and secondary infection. Use should be time-limited and monitored.

#### Oral Antibiotics (Anti-inflammatory Doses)

- **Indication**: Recurrent chalazia, multiple chalazia, or chalazia associated with [[ocular rosacea]] or chronic MGD.
- **Common agents**:
  - **[[Doxycycline]]**: 50–100 mg once or twice daily for 6–12 weeks. Doxycycline has anti-inflammatory, anti-angiogenic, and lipase-inhibiting properties that improve meibomian gland function.
  - **[[Minocycline]]**: 50–100 mg daily; similar mechanism to doxycycline.
  - **[[Azithromycin]]** (oral): Pulsed dosing regimens (e.g., 500 mg daily for 3 days, repeated monthly for 3 months) have shown efficacy in reducing recurrence.
- **Contraindications**: Tetracyclines are contraindicated in pregnant women, nursing mothers, and children under 8 years of age due to risk of dental staining and bone growth inhibition. In these populations, [[erythromycin]] or azithromycin may be substituted.

#### Omega-3 Fatty Acid Supplementation

- **Rationale**: [[Omega-3 fatty acids]] (EPA and DHA) have anti-inflammatory properties and may improve meibum quality by altering the fatty acid composition of meibomian gland secretions.
- **Dosage**: Typically 1,000–2,000 mg of combined EPA/DHA daily.
- **Evidence**: Some clinical studies support a role in reducing MGD severity and chalazion recurrence, though evidence remains mixed.

### Intralesional Corticosteroid Injection

Intralesional injection of corticosteroid is a well-established, minimally invasive treatment for chalazion that is refractory to conservative management.

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Agent** | [[Triamcinolone acetonide]] (Kenalog) |
| **Concentration** | 5–40 mg/mL (most commonly 10 mg/mL or 40 mg/mL) |
| **Volume** | 0.05–0.3 mL per injection |
| **Route** | Transconjunctival (preferred) or transcutaneous |
| **Anesthesia** | Topical anesthetic drops ± subcutaneous/subconjunctival lidocaine |
| **Success rate** | 60–90% resolution after one or two injections |
| **Repeat injection** | May be performed after 2–4 weeks if the first injection is partially effective |

#### Technique

1. Topical anesthetic (e.g., [[proparacaine]] 0.5%) is instilled.
2. The eyelid is everted using a chalazion clamp or manually.
3. The needle (typically 27–30 gauge) is inserted through the conjunctival surface directly into the center of the lesion.
4. The corticosteroid suspension is injected slowly.
5. The clamp is released, and gentle pressure is applied.

#### Complications

- **Skin depigmentation** (hypopigmentation): More common with transcutaneous injection and in patients with darker skin pigmentation. Transconjunctival injection minimizes this risk.
- **Dermal/subcutaneous fat atrophy**: Can cause a visible depression in the eyelid skin.
- **Elevated [[intraocular pressure]]**: Transient IOP elevation may occur; patients should be monitored.
- **Inadvertent globe penetration**: Rare but serious; risk is minimized by directing the needle away from the globe and using a chalazion clamp.
- **Retinal or choroidal vascular occlusion**: Extremely rare; reported in isolated case reports following periocular corticosteroid injection.

### Surgical Management: Incision and Curettage (I&C)

Incision and curettage is the definitive surgical treatment for chalazion and is indicated when conservative and pharmacological measures have failed, or when the lesion is large, long-standing, or causing significant visual or cosmetic disturbance.

#### Indications

- Chalazion persisting > 4–6 weeks despite conservative management
- Large chalazion causing induced astigmatism or visual disturbance
- Cosmetically unacceptable lesion
- Suspicion of malignancy (tissue should be sent for [[histopathology]])
- Patient preference for rapid resolution

#### Technique

1. **Anesthesia**: Subcutaneous injection of [[lidocaine]] (1–2%) with or without [[epinephrine]] around the lesion. Topical anesthetic drops are also instilled.
2. **Chalazion clamp application**: A chalazion clamp (Desmarres clamp) is applied to the eyelid, centered over the lesion. The clamp provides hemostasis and stabilizes the tissue.
3. **Eyelid eversion**: The eyelid is everted to expose the conjunctival surface.
4. **Incision**: A vertical incision (perpendicular to the lid margin) is made through the [[palpebral conjunctiva]] and into the chalazion cavity using a #11 or #15 blade. The incision is made on the conjunctival side to avoid a visible skin scar. (Note: For chalazia pointing through the skin, a horizontal incision through the skin surface, parallel to the lid crease, may be preferred for cosmetic reasons.)
5. **Curettage**: The contents of the chalazion (granulomatous tissue, lipid material, and fibrous capsule) are thoroughly curetted using a small chalazion curette.
6. **Hemostasis**: The clamp is released, and gentle pressure is applied. Cautery is rarely needed.
7. **Postoperative care**: Topical antibiotic ointment (e.g., erythromycin) is applied. A pressure patch may be placed for several hours. Warm compresses are resumed after 24–48 hours.

#### Success Rate

Incision and curettage has a success rate of approximately **90–95%** for complete resolution.

#### Complications

- Hemorrhage (usually self-limited)
- Recurrence (5–10%)
- Eyelid notching or scarring (rare with proper technique)
- Damage to the [[lacrimal punctum]] or [[canaliculus]] (if the lesion is near the medial canthus)
- Secondary infection (rare)

### Comparison of Treatment Modalities

| Treatment | Success Rate | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Warm compresses + lid hygiene** | 25–50% | Non-invasive, no risk | Slow, requires compliance, low success rate for established lesions |
| **Intralesional corticosteroid injection** | 60–90% | Minimally invasive, quick, office-based | Risk of depigmentation, fat atrophy, IOP elevation |
| **Incision and curettage** | 90–95% | Definitive, rapid resolution, tissue for histopathology | Invasive, requires anesthesia, minor surgical risks |
| **Combined injection + I&C** | >95% | Highest success rate | Combined risks of both procedures |

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Chalazion

Chalazia in children present unique management challenges:

- Children are less tolerant of warm compresses and lid hygiene regimens.
- Incision and curettage typically requires **general anesthesia** in young children, increasing procedural risk and cost.
- Intralesional corticosteroid injection may be performed under brief sedation or general anesthesia.
- Conservative management is generally attempted for a longer period before surgical intervention.
- Persistent large upper lid chalazia in children may cause [[amblyopia]] due to induced astigmatism or mechanical [[ptosis]] obstructing the [[visual axis]]. Prompt treatment is essential in these cases to prevent permanent visual impairment.
- Recurrent chalazia in children should prompt evaluation for uncorrected [[refractive error]], [[Demodex]] infestation, and underlying systemic conditions.

### Chalazion in Immunocompromised Patients

Patients with [[HIV/AIDS]], those on immunosuppressive therapy, or those with other immunocompromising conditions may experience:

- Increased frequency and multiplicity of chalazia
- Atypical presentations
- Higher risk of secondary infection
- Slower response to conservative management

### Chalazion and Contact Lens Wear

[[Contact lens]] wear, particularly with poor hygiene practices, is a recognized risk factor for chalazion development. Mechanisms include:

- Mechanical irritation of the lid margin and Meibomian gland orifices
- Alteration of the lid margin microbiome
- Disruption of the tear film lipid layer
- Increased exposure to bacterial biofilm

Patients should be counseled on proper contact lens hygiene and may benefit from temporary discontinuation of lens wear during active chalazion treatment.

## Recurrent Chalazia

Recurrence of chalazia is a common clinical problem. Management of recurrent chalazia should include:

1. **Comprehensive evaluation for underlying MGD**: [[Meibography]], gland expression assessment, and tear film analysis.
2. **Treatment of concurrent [[blepharitis]]**: Aggressive lid hygiene, topical azithromycin, and/or oral tetracyclines.
3. **Evaluation for [[ocular rosacea]]**: Facial examination for telangiectasia, papules, pustules, and rhinophyma. Referral to dermatology may be appropriate.
4. **[[Demodex]] assessment**: Examination of epilated lashes for *[[Demodex folliculorum]]* and *[[Demodex brevis]]*. Treatment with [[tea tree oil]]-based lid scrubs or topical [[ivermectin]] if infestation is confirmed.
5. **Biopsy of recurrent lesions**: Any chalazion recurring in the same location should be biopsied to exclude [[sebaceous gland carcinoma]] or other malignancy.
6. **Systemic workup**: In patients with multiple recurrent chalazia, consider evaluation for [[hyperlipidemia]], [[diabetes mellitus]], and other systemic inflammatory conditions.
7. **In-office thermal pulsation therapy**: Devices such as [[LipiFlow]] provide controlled heat and pulsatile pressure to the Meibomian glands, improving gland function and potentially reducing chalazion recurrence in patients with MGD.
8. **Intense pulsed light (IPL) therapy**: [[IPL]] has demonstrated efficacy in treating MGD and ocular rosacea, and may reduce the frequency of chalazion recurrence by improving meibomian gland function, reducing periocular inflammation, and decreasing *Demodex* populations.

## Complications of Chalazion

While chalazia are generally benign, complications can occur:

| Complication | Description |
|---|---|
| **Induced [[astigmatism]]** | Mechanical pressure on the cornea, particularly from upper lid lesions; typically resolves after chalazion treatment |
| **[[Preseptal cellulitis]]** | Secondary bacterial infection of the surrounding eyelid tissue; requires systemic antibiotics |
| **Conjunctival granuloma** | Polypoid granulation tissue protruding through the conjunctival surface; may require excision |
| **Cosmetic deformity** | Chronic chalazia may cause lid distortion, skin discoloration, or scarring |
| **[[Amblyopia]]** | In children, large chalazia obstructing the visual axis or inducing significant astigmatism may lead to deprivation or refractive amblyopia |
| **Mechanical [[ptosis]]** | Weight of the lesion causes drooping of the upper eyelid |
| **Corneal complications** | [[Corneal dellen]], [[superficial punctate keratitis]], or [[corneal erosion]] from mechanical contact |

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis for chalazion is excellent. The majority of chalazia resolve with conservative management or minor procedural intervention. Key prognostic factors include:

- **Size**: Smaller chalazia are more likely to resolve spontaneously or with conservative measures.
- **Duration**: Lesions present for less than 4–6 weeks are more responsive to conservative therapy.
- **Underlying MGD/blepharitis**: Patients with untreated MGD are more likely to experience recurrence.
- **Patient compliance**: Adherence to warm compress and lid hygiene regimens significantly impacts outcomes.

| Key Point | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Definition** | Chronic, sterile, lipogranulomatous inflammation of a Meibomian gland |
| **Most common cause** | Meibomian gland duct obstruction |
| **Primary risk factor** | [[Meibomian gland dysfunction]] (MGD) |
| **Hallmark symptom** | Painless eyelid nodule |
