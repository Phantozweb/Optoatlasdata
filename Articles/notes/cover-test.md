

# Cover Test

The **Cover Test** (also referred to as the **cover–uncover test**) is one of the most fundamental, indispensable, and widely utilized clinical examination techniques in [[Optometry]] and [[Ophthalmology]]. It serves as the gold-standard objective method for detecting, differentiating, and quantifying ocular misalignments, encompassing both manifest deviations ([[strabismus]] or [[tropia]]) and latent deviations ([[heterophoria]] or [[phoria]]). The test exploits the principle of dissociation of [[binocular vision]] by selectively interrupting [[fusion]] to reveal the underlying alignment status of the [[visual axes]]. Because of its simplicity, reliability, and non-invasive nature, the cover test is performed routinely in virtually every comprehensive eye examination, pediatric vision screening, [[orthoptic]] assessment, and neuro-ophthalmic evaluation.

The cover test requires no specialized instrumentation beyond an [[occluder]] (or the examiner's hand/thumb) and a fixation target, yet it yields profoundly important clinical information about the sensorimotor status of the [[binocular vision]] system. Mastery of the cover test is considered an essential competency for all eye care practitioners.

---

## Historical Background

The origins of the cover test trace back to the 19th century, when pioneers in the study of [[strabismus]] and [[binocular vision]] sought objective methods to evaluate ocular alignment. **Louis Émile Javal** and **Alfred Graefe** were among the early clinicians who recognized that occluding one eye could reveal latent deviations. The technique was subsequently refined and standardized throughout the 20th century, becoming a cornerstone of the orthoptic and optometric examination. The work of **Ernest Edmund Maddox**, who developed the [[Maddox rod]] and contributed extensively to the understanding of [[heterophoria]], further cemented the importance of dissociation techniques in clinical practice. Today, the cover test remains essentially unchanged in its fundamental methodology, a testament to its enduring clinical validity.

## Pathophysiology

### Binocular Vision and Fusional Mechanisms

To understand the cover test, one must first appreciate the neurophysiology of [[binocular single vision]] (BSV). Under normal circumstances, the [[extraocular muscles]] of both eyes are coordinated by the supranuclear gaze centers in the [[brainstem]] and [[cerebral cortex]] so that the [[fovea]] of each eye is directed at the same object of regard simultaneously. This precise alignment is maintained by the [[fusional vergence]] system, which continuously makes fine motor adjustments to keep the images on corresponding [[retinal]] points, thereby enabling [[stereopsis]] and single binocular perception.

The fusional vergence system comprises:

| Component | Function |
|---|---|
| **[[Fusional convergence]]** (positive fusional vergence) | Corrects outward (exo) deviations by converging the eyes |
| **[[Fusional divergence]]** (negative fusional vergence) | Corrects inward (eso) deviations by diverging the eyes |
| **Vertical fusional vergence** | Corrects vertical misalignments (typically limited amplitude) |
| **[[Cyclovergence]]** | Corrects torsional misalignments |

When the fusional vergence system is intact and the deviation is within its compensatory range, the eyes appear aligned despite an underlying tendency to deviate. This underlying tendency is termed a **[[phoria]]** (latent deviation). The cover test unmasks this latent deviation by removing the stimulus to fusion (i.e., by occluding one eye).

### Manifest vs. Latent Deviations

A **[[tropia]]** (manifest deviation or [[strabismus]]) exists when the fusional vergence system is unable to maintain alignment, resulting in a visible misalignment of the visual axes even under binocular viewing conditions. In contrast, a **[[phoria]]** is controlled by fusion and only becomes apparent when binocular vision is disrupted—precisely the condition created by the cover test.

The pathophysiology underlying deviations detected by the cover test may involve:

- **Innervational abnormalities**: Dysfunction of the [[cranial nerves]] (III — [[oculomotor nerve]], IV — [[trochlear nerve]], VI — [[abducens nerve]]) or their supranuclear pathways.
- **Mechanical/restrictive factors**: Orbital pathology such as [[thyroid eye disease]], [[orbital fracture]], or fibrosis of [[extraocular muscles]].
- **Anatomical variations**: Anomalous insertion or structure of extraocular muscles.
- **Refractive factors**: Uncorrected [[hyperopia]] driving excessive [[accommodative convergence]], leading to [[accommodative esotropia]].
- **Sensory deprivation**: Reduced visual acuity in one eye (e.g., from [[amblyopia]], [[cataract]], or [[macular]] pathology) disrupting the stimulus to fusion, resulting in [[sensory strabismus]].
- **Decompensation of phorias**: A previously compensated phoria may decompensate due to fatigue, illness, medication, aging, or changes in refractive error, converting to an intermittent or constant tropia.

### Neural Basis of the Cover Test Response

When an occluder is placed before one eye, the visual input from that eye is eliminated, removing the sensory drive for [[fusional vergence]]. In a patient with a phoria, the occluded eye drifts to its "resting" or "tonic" vergence position. Upon removal of the occluder, the fusional vergence system is re-engaged, and a corrective movement (recovery movement) is observed as the eye returns to alignment. The speed and completeness of this recovery movement provide additional clinical information about the robustness of the patient's fusional reserves.

In a patient with a tropia, the non-fixating (deviating) eye is already misaligned under binocular conditions. When the fixating eye is covered, the deviating eye must take up fixation (assuming it has adequate visual acuity), producing a visible refixation movement. The direction of this movement reveals the type of tropia.

## Clinical Indications

The cover test is indicated in a broad range of clinical scenarios:

- **Routine comprehensive eye examinations** (all ages)
- **Pediatric vision screening** and developmental assessments
- **Evaluation of [[diplopia]]** (double vision)
- **Assessment of suspected [[strabismus]]**
- **Monitoring of known phorias or tropias**
- **Pre- and post-operative evaluation** for [[strabismus surgery]]
- **Neuro-ophthalmic assessment** (e.g., suspected [[cranial nerve palsy]])
- **Assessment of [[binocular vision]] dysfunction** in patients with asthenopia, reading difficulties, or convergence insufficiency
- **Evaluation of decompensating phorias**
- **Occupational vision assessments** (e.g., pilots, drivers)

## Equipment and Setup

### Required Equipment

| Item | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Occluder** (opaque paddle or card) | To fully block vision in one eye, dissociating binocular fusion |
| **Distance fixation target** | Typically a single letter or symbol at 6 meters (20 feet), one line above the patient's [[visual acuity]] threshold |
| **Near fixation target** | A detailed accommodative target (e.g., small letter or picture) held at 40 cm (16 inches) |
| **[[Prism bar]]** or loose [[prisms]]** (for quantification) | Used during the **[[prism cover test]]** (alternate cover test with prisms) to measure the magnitude of deviation |

### Patient Positioning

The patient should be seated comfortably with the head in primary position, facing the fixation target. The examiner should ensure:

- Appropriate [[refractive correction]] is worn (spectacles or [[contact lenses]]) unless the test is specifically being performed without correction.
- The fixation target is appropriate for the patient's age and cognitive ability (e.g., pictures for young children, letters for adults).
- Room illumination is adequate.
- The patient is instructed to fixate steadily on the target and to keep looking at it throughout the test.

## Technique and Methodology

The cover test is performed in a systematic sequence comprising three distinct components:

### 1. Unilateral Cover Test (Cover–Uncover Test)

**Purpose:** To detect and differentiate a **[[tropia]]** (manifest deviation) from a **[[phoria]]** (latent deviation).

**Procedure:**

1. The patient fixates on the distance target with both eyes open.
2. The examiner observes both eyes for any obvious misalignment.
3. The occluder is placed before the **right eye** while the examiner carefully observes the **left eye** (the uncovered eye).
   - If the left eye makes a **refixation movement** to take up fixation, a **tropia** is present (the left eye was the deviating eye).
   - The direction of the refixation movement indicates the type of tropia:
     - Movement **inward → [[exotropia]]** (the eye was deviated outward)
     - Movement **outward → [[esotropia]]** (the eye was deviated inward)
     - Movement **downward → [[hypertropia]]** (the eye was deviated upward)
     - Movement **upward → [[hypotropia]]** (the eye was deviated downward)
   - If the left eye does **not** move, either the left eye is the fixating eye (no tropia of the left eye) or the eyes are aligned.
4. The occluder is then **removed** from the right eye, and the examiner observes the **right eye** as it is uncovered.
   - If the right eye makes a **recovery movement** to regain alignment, a **phoria** is present.
   - If no movement is observed, the eyes are orthophoric (no phoria or tropia).
5. The procedure is **repeated** by covering the **left eye** and observing the **right eye** for a refixation movement (to detect a right tropia).
6. The entire sequence is then repeated at **near** (40 cm) with an accommodative target.

**Key Observation Points:**

| Observation | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Movement of the **uncovered** eye when the other eye is covered | **Tropia** (manifest deviation) |
| Movement of the **covered** eye upon removal of the occluder | **Phoria** (latent deviation) |
| No movement of either eye | **[[Orthophoria]]** (no deviation) |

### 2. Alternating Cover Test

**Purpose:** To reveal the **total deviation** (the sum of the tropia and any superimposed phoria) by fully dissociating binocular fusion.

**Procedure:**

1. The patient fixates on the target.
2. The occluder is placed before one eye for approximately 2–3 seconds, then **rapidly transferred** to the other eye without allowing any period of binocular viewing.
3. The examiner observes the **eye that is being uncovered** each time the occluder is moved.
4. The direction and magnitude of the refixation movement of the uncovered eye are noted.
5. The occluder is alternated back and forth several times until the deviation stabilizes (i.e., no further increase in the observed movement).
6. Performed at both **distance** and **near**.

**Interpretation:**

- The alternating cover test **does not differentiate** between a tropia and a phoria; it measures the **total deviation**.
- It is therefore essential to perform the **unilateral cover test first** to establish whether a tropia is present before proceeding to the alternating cover test.

### 3. Prism Alternating Cover Test (PACT)

**Purpose:** To **quantify** the magnitude of the total deviation in [[prism dioptres]] (Δ).

**Procedure:**

1. The alternating cover test is performed as described above.
2. A [[prism bar]] (or loose prisms) is held before one eye with the base oriented to **neutralize** the observed deviation:
   - **Base-out** prism for [[esodeviations]]
   - **Base-in** prism for [[exodeviations]]
   - **Base-down** prism for [[hyperdeviations]]
   - **Base-up** prism for [[hypodeviations]]
3. The prism power is incrementally increased while alternating the cover until **no refixation movement** is observed in either eye upon transfer of the occluder.
4. The prism power at which neutralization occurs represents the **magnitude of the total deviation**.
5. Measurements are taken at both **distance** and **near**, and in multiple positions of gaze if a [[noncomitant]] (incomitant) deviation is suspected.

## Interpretation and Clinical Significance

### Types of Deviations Detected

| Deviation Type | Cover Test Finding | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Orthophoria]]** | No movement on cover or uncover | Normal alignment; no deviation |
| **[[Esophoria]]** | Outward recovery movement on uncover | Latent inward deviation; may cause [[asthenopia]] |
| **[[Exophoria]]** | Inward recovery movement on uncover | Latent outward deviation; common, often asymptomatic |
| **[[Hyperphoria]]** | Downward recovery movement on uncover | Latent vertical deviation |
| **[[Esotropia]]** | Outward refixation movement of uncovered eye on cover test | Manifest inward deviation |
| **[[Exotropia]]** | Inward refixation movement of uncovered eye on cover test | Manifest outward deviation |
| **[[Hypertropia]]** | Downward refixation movement of uncovered eye | Manifest upward deviation |
| **Intermittent tropia** | Tropia present inconsistently; may vary with attention, fatigue, or fixation distance | Deviation that breaks down intermittently |

### Comitant vs. Noncomitant Deviations

The cover test can help distinguish between **[[comitant]]** and **[[noncomitant]]** (incomitant) deviations:

- **Comitant deviation**: The magnitude of the deviation is approximately equal in all positions of gaze. This is typical of childhood-onset [[strabismus]] (e.g., [[infantile esotropia]], [[intermittent exotropia]]).
- **Noncomitant deviation**: The magnitude varies with gaze direction. This pattern is characteristic of [[paralytic strabismus]] (e.g., [[sixth nerve palsy]], [[fourth nerve palsy]], [[third nerve palsy]]) or [[restrictive strabismus]] (e.g., [[thyroid eye disease]], [[orbital floor fracture]]).

To evaluate comitance, the cover test (with prism measurement) is performed in the **nine diagnostic positions of gaze**.

### Unilateral vs. Alternating Tropias

The unilateral cover test also reveals **fixation preference**:

- If covering the right eye causes no movement of the left eye, but covering the left eye causes a refixation movement of the right eye, the patient has a **left tropia** with **left eye fixation preference** (or equivalently, the right eye is the habitually deviating eye).
- If the patient freely alternates fixation (either eye can take up fixation when the other is covered), the deviation is said to be **alternating**, which generally suggests relatively equal visual acuity in both eyes and a lower risk of [[amblyopia]].
- A strong, consistent fixation preference for one eye raises concern for [[amblyopia]] in the non-preferred eye, particularly in children.

## Special Considerations

### Cover Test in Children

Performing the cover test in young or uncooperative children requires particular skill:

- **Fixation targets** must be age-appropriate (e.g., small toys, animated videos, stickers).
- The examiner must be adept at observing subtle eye movements quickly, as the child may not maintain fixation for long.
- The **objection to occlusion** can itself be a diagnostic clue: a child who strongly objects to having one eye covered but tolerates occlusion of the other may have reduced vision ([[amblyopia]]) in the non-objecting eye.
- The **Hirschberg test** ([[corneal light reflex]]) and **[[Brückner test]]** may be used as adjuncts or alternatives when the cover test cannot be reliably performed.

### Cover Test in Patients with Reduced Vision

If one eye has significantly reduced [[visual acuity]] (e.g., from [[macular degeneration]], dense [[cataract]], or [[optic neuropathy]]), the cover test may be difficult to interpret because the eye with poor vision may not take up fixation reliably. In such cases:

- The examiner should note whether the eye with reduced vision can take up and **maintain** fixation when the better eye is occluded.
- [[Eccentric fixation]] may be present, complicating interpretation.
- The presence of a [[sensory strabismus]] secondary to the visual loss should be considered.

### Cover Test with Refractive Correction

The cover test should ideally be performed both **with** and **without** the patient's habitual refractive correction, and sometimes with the full cycloplegic refraction, particularly in cases of suspected [[accommodative esotropia]]. The difference in deviation with and without correction provides critical information:

- A deviation that is significantly reduced or eliminated with full hyperopic correction suggests an **[[accommodative component]]**.
- The **AC/A ratio** ([[accommodative convergence to accommodation ratio]]) can be estimated by comparing the deviation at distance and near with correction in place.

### Near vs. Distance Measurements

Comparing the deviation at distance (6 m) and near (40 cm) is essential:

| Pattern | Distance Deviation | Near Deviation | Possible Diagnosis |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Convergence insufficiency** | Small exophoria or orthophoria | Large exophoria or intermittent exotropia | [[Convergence insufficiency]] |
| **Divergence insufficiency** | Esophoria/esotropia | Orthophoria or small esophoria | [[Divergence insufficiency]]; rule out [[sixth nerve palsy]] |
| **Basic esotropia** | Moderate esotropia | Similar esotropia | Basic type [[esotropia]] |
| **High AC/A ratio esotropia** | Small esotropia | Large esotropia | High [[AC/A ratio]] esotropia |

## Symptoms Associated with Deviations Detected by Cover Test

Patients with deviations identified on cover testing may present with a variety of symptoms, depending on whether the deviation is manifest or latent, its magnitude, and the patient's fusional vergence reserves:

- **[[Diplopia]]** (double vision) — particularly in acquired tropias in adults
- **[[Asthenopia]]** (eye strain) — common with decompensating phorias
- **Headaches** — often frontal or periorbital, associated with sustained fusional effort
- **Blurred vision** — especially at near, due to interaction between accommodation and vergence
- **Difficulty reading** or loss of place — associated with [[convergence insufficiency]] or vertical phorias
- **Closing or covering one eye** — a compensatory behavior in intermittent exotropia
- **Abnormal head posture** ([[torticollis]]) — to maintain binocular fusion in noncomitant deviations (e.g., head turn in [[sixth nerve palsy]], head tilt in [[fourth nerve palsy]])
- **Reduced [[stereopsis]]** or depth perception
- **Cosmetic concern** — in large-angle manifest strabismus

In children, symptoms may be absent due to sensory adaptations such as [[suppression]] or [[anomalous retinal correspondence]] (ARC), which eliminate diplopia but may lead to [[amblyopia]].

## Diagnostic Role and Relationship to Other Tests

The cover test is the primary objective test for ocular alignment but is often used in conjunction with other diagnostic modalities:

| Test | Relationship to Cover Test |
|---|---|
| **[[Hirschberg test]]** (corneal light reflex) | Provides a gross estimate of alignment; useful when cover test cannot be performed (e.g., in infants) |
| **[[Krimsky test]]** | Prism-modified Hirschberg test for quantification when cover test is not feasible |
| **[[Maddox rod test]]** | Dissociates binocular vision using a different method; useful for measuring small vertical phorias |
| **[[Modified Thorington test]]** | Dissociation technique for phoria measurement; provides quantitative data |
| **[[Von Graefe technique]]** | Phoropter-based phoria measurement using prism dissociation |
| **[[Synoptophore]] (major amblyoscope)** | Measures subjective and objective angles of deviation; assesses sensory fusion and stereopsis |
| **[[Worth four-dot test]]** | Assesses presence of fusion or suppression at distance and near |
| **[[Bagolini striated lenses]]** | Evaluates binocular status under near-natural viewing conditions |
| **[[Stereoacuity tests]]** (e.g., [[Titmus test]], [[Randot test]], [[TNO test]]) | Quantifies stereopsis; reduced stereoacuity may correlate with deviations found on cover test |

## Management of Deviations Detected by Cover Test

The management of ocular deviations identified through cover testing depends on the type, magnitude, frequency, and symptomatic impact of the deviation, as well as the patient's age and visual needs.

### Optical Correction

- **Full refractive correction**: Essential in all cases; correcting [[hyperopia]] may reduce or eliminate [[accommodative esotropia]].
- **[[Bifocal]] lenses or progressive addition lenses**: Indicated in high AC/A ratio esotropia to reduce accommodative demand at near.
- **[[Prism]] correction**: Incorporating [[Fresnel prisms]] or ground-in prisms into spectacles can compensate for small-to-moderate deviations, alleviating diplopia and asthenopia.

### Orthoptic / Vision Therapy

- **[[Vergence therapy]]**: Exercises to improve fusional vergence amplitudes, particularly effective for [[convergence insufficiency]] and decompensating exophoria.
- **Anti-suppression therapy**: Techniques to eliminate [[suppression]] and restore bifoveal fusion.
- **Accommodative therapy**: Improving accommodative facility and accuracy when accommodative dysfunction coexists.
- The **Convergence Insufficiency Treatment Trial (CITT)** demonstrated that office-based vergence/accommodative therapy is the most effective treatment for symptomatic [[convergence insufficiency]] in children and adults.

### Pharmacological Interventions

- **[[Atropine]] penalization**: Used in some cases of [[amblyopia]] associated with strabismus to encourage use of the amblyopic eye.
- **[[Phospholine iodide]] (echothiophate iodide)**: A long-acting [[miotic]] and [[anticholinesterase]] agent historically used to reduce accommodative esotropia by augmenting accommodation at near, thereby reducing the accommodative convergence demand. Its use has declined due to side effects.
- **[[Botulinum toxin]] (Botox) injection**: Injected into an [[extraocular muscle]] to produce temporary paralysis, used as a diagnostic or therapeutic tool in certain types of strabismus (e.g., acute [[sixth nerve palsy]], small-angle esotropia post-surgery).

### Surgical Approaches

- **[[Strabismus surgery]]** (extraocular muscle surgery): Indicated for large-angle tropias, cosmetically significant deviations, deviations not responsive to optical or orthoptic treatment, and to restore or improve binocular function.
  - **[[Recession]]**: Weakening procedure; the muscle insertion is moved posteriorly on the globe.
  - **[[Resection]]**: Strengthening procedure; a portion of the muscle is removed and the shortened muscle is reattached.
  - **Adjustable suture technique**: Allows postoperative fine-tuning of alignment.
  - **[[Inferior oblique]] weakening procedures**: For [[inferior oblique overaction]] associated with [[fourth nerve palsy]] or [[V-pattern]] strabismus.
- Surgical planning relies heavily on accurate cover test measurements (prism alternating cover test) in multiple gaze positions and at distance and near.

### Monitoring and Follow-Up

- Patients with phorias within normal limits and no symptoms require routine monitoring only.
- Patients with decompensating phorias, intermittent tropias, or post-surgical patients require regular follow-up with serial cover test measurements to track stability or progression.
- In children with strabismus, ongoing monitoring for [[amblyopia]] development is critical.

## Limitations and Sources of Error

Despite its clinical utility, the cover test has several potential limitations:

- **Patient cooperation**: Requires the patient to maintain steady fixation; difficult in very young children, cognitively impaired patients, or those with very poor vision.
- **Examiner skill**: Subtle movements may be missed by inexperienced examiners; inter-examiner variability exists.
- **Latent nystagmus**: In patients with [[latent nystagmus]], occluding one eye induces [[nystagmus]], which may confound interpretation.
- **Eccentric fixation**: Patients with [[eccentric fixation]] may not demonstrate expected refixation movements.
- **Dissociation effect**: The alternating cover test maximally dissociates fusion and may overestimate the clinically relevant deviation (i.e., the deviation the patient actually experiences under binocular conditions).
- **Accommodative control**: If the patient does not accurately accommodate on the near target, the measured deviation may not reflect the true habitual deviation.

## Recording and Documentation

Cover test findings should be documented systematically. A common notation system includes:

- **Type of deviation**: Tropia (T) or phoria (Φ or φ)
- **Direction**: Eso (E), Exo (X), Hyper (H), Hypo
- **Magnitude**: In prism dioptres (Δ)
- **Distance and near**: Specified separately
- **With or without correction**: Noted
- **Frequency** (for tropias): Constant or intermittent
- **Laterality**: Unilateral (specifying which eye) or alternating

**Example documentation:**

> **Cover Test (with Rx):**
> - Distance: 4Δ exophoria
> - Near: 8Δ exophoria
> - No tropia detected at distance or near
>
> **Cover Test (without Rx):**
> - Distance: Orthophoria
> - Near: 6Δ exophoria

## Summary

The **Cover Test** remains the single most important objective clinical test for the assessment of ocular alignment. Its three components—the **unilateral cover test**, the **alternating cover test**, and the **prism alternating cover test**—provide a comprehensive evaluation that enables the clinician to detect the presence of a deviation, differentiate between manifest and latent deviations, determine the direction and laterality of the deviation, and quantify its magnitude. The information obtained from the cover test is foundational to the diagnosis and management of [[strabismus]], [[heterophoria]], [[amblyopia]], [[binocular vision]] disorders, and neuro-ophthalmic conditions. Proficiency in performing and interpreting the cover test is an essential clinical skill for every [[optometrist]] and [[ophthalmologist]].
