# Cataract

**Cataract** is a progressive opacification of the [[crystalline lens]] of the eye that results in gradual degradation of visual acuity and optical quality. It is the leading cause of reversible blindness worldwide and one of the most common conditions encountered in both [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] clinical practice. The World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that cataracts are responsible for approximately 33% of visual impairment and 51% of global blindness, affecting tens of millions of individuals. Surgical extraction of the opacified lens with implantation of an [[intraocular lens]] (IOL) remains the definitive treatment and is the most frequently performed elective surgical procedure in the world.

---

## Etymology and Historical Background

The term "cataract" derives from the Latin *cataracta* and the Greek *katarrhaktēs* (καταρράκτης), meaning "waterfall" or "portcullis." Ancient physicians believed that an opaque fluid flowed down from the brain and solidified in front of the lens, obstructing vision—hence the metaphor of a waterfall. The earliest documented surgical treatment of cataract is **couching** (reclination), described in ancient Indian texts attributed to [[Sushruta]] around 600 BCE, in which a sharp instrument was used to displace the opacified lens inferiorly into the [[vitreous cavity]]. Modern extracapsular and intracapsular techniques evolved over centuries, culminating in Sir Harold Ridley's implantation of the first [[intraocular lens]] in 1949 at St Thomas' Hospital, London, after observing that fragments of [[polymethyl methacrylate]] (PMMA) from shattered aircraft canopies remained inert within the eyes of World War II pilots.

## Epidemiology

Cataract prevalence increases dramatically with age. Population-based studies, including the Beaver Dam Eye Study, the Blue Mountains Eye Study, and the Salisbury Eye Evaluation, consistently demonstrate that some degree of lens opacity is present in over 50% of individuals aged 65–74 years and in more than 70% of those older than 75 years.

| **Age Group** | **Approximate Prevalence of Any Lens Opacity** |
|---|---|
| 40–54 years | 5–10% |
| 55–64 years | 15–25% |
| 65–74 years | 40–60% |
| 75–84 years | 60–80% |
| ≥85 years | >80% |

Key epidemiological risk factors include:

- **Age** – the single strongest risk factor
- **Sex** – females have a slightly higher prevalence, potentially related to hormonal factors and longer life expectancy
- **Ultraviolet (UV) radiation** – chronic [[UV-B]] exposure accelerates cortical and posterior subcapsular cataract formation
- **Diabetes mellitus** – [[diabetes]] is strongly associated with earlier onset and more rapid progression of cataracts, particularly posterior subcapsular and cortical types
- **Smoking** – dose-dependent increase in nuclear sclerotic and posterior subcapsular cataracts
- **Corticosteroid use** – systemic and topical [[corticosteroids]] are well-established risk factors for [[posterior subcapsular cataract]]
- **Ocular trauma** – blunt or penetrating injury may cause [[traumatic cataract]]
- **Previous intraocular surgery** – especially [[vitrectomy]]
- **High myopia** – associated with earlier nuclear sclerosis
- **Alcohol consumption**
- **Nutritional deficiencies** – low antioxidant intake (vitamins C, E, carotenoids)
- **Socioeconomic factors** – limited access to healthcare and nutrition in developing nations

## Anatomy of the Crystalline Lens

Understanding cataract pathophysiology requires detailed knowledge of [[crystalline lens]] anatomy.

The lens is a transparent, biconvex, avascular structure located posterior to the [[iris]] and anterior to the [[vitreous humor]], suspended by [[zonular fibers]] (zonules of Zinn) that attach it to the [[ciliary body]]. It contributes approximately +18 to +20 [[diopters]] of the eye's total refractive power (~+60 D).

### Structural Components

| **Structure** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **[[Lens capsule]]** | Elastic, transparent basement membrane composed primarily of type IV collagen; thickest basement membrane in the body; encloses the entire lens |
| **[[Lens epithelium]]** | Single layer of cuboidal cells beneath the anterior capsule; responsible for metabolic homeostasis, ion transport, and generation of new lens fibers |
| **[[Lens cortex]]** | Younger, more superficial lens fibers with relatively lower protein density |
| **[[Lens nucleus]]** | Central, oldest, most compact region of the lens; subdivided into embryonic, fetal, infantile (juvenile), and adult nuclear zones |

The lens is composed of approximately 65% water and 35% protein (the highest protein concentration of any tissue in the body). The major structural proteins are the **[[crystallins]]**—α-crystallin, β-crystallin, and γ-crystallin—which are arranged in a highly ordered, short-range spatial configuration that minimizes light scattering and maintains transparency.

## Pathophysiology

Cataractogenesis is a multifactorial process involving biochemical, structural, and environmental insults to the lens. The fundamental mechanism underlying all cataracts is the disruption of the highly ordered arrangement of [[crystallin]] proteins and/or lens fiber cells, leading to increased light scattering and decreased transparency.

### Key Pathophysiological Mechanisms

#### 1. Oxidative Stress
The lens possesses a robust antioxidant defense system, including [[glutathione]] (GSH), [[superoxide dismutase]], [[catalase]], and ascorbic acid. With aging, the concentration of reduced glutathione in the nucleus declines significantly, while oxidized glutathione (GSSG) accumulates. This shift permits oxidative damage to lens proteins, lipids, and DNA. Reactive oxygen species (ROS) cause:

- **Protein aggregation** – oxidation of sulfhydryl groups on crystallins leads to disulfide cross-linking and formation of high-molecular-weight (HMW) aggregates that scatter light
- **Lipid peroxidation** – damage to lens fiber cell membranes, altering permeability
- **DNA damage** – in the lens epithelium, impairing cellular function

#### 2. Post-Translational Modification of Crystallins
Over decades, crystallin proteins undergo cumulative post-translational modifications including:

- Deamidation
- Glycation (non-enzymatic glycosylation, accelerated in [[diabetes mellitus]])
- Truncation
- Racemization
- Phosphorylation
- Carbamylation

These modifications alter protein conformation, reduce solubility, and promote aggregation. α-Crystallin, which normally functions as a molecular [[chaperone]] preventing aggregation of other proteins, loses its chaperone capacity with age and modification.

#### 3. Osmotic Stress (Diabetic/Sugar Cataracts)
In [[diabetes mellitus]], elevated glucose levels in the [[aqueous humor]] lead to increased glucose uptake by the lens. The enzyme [[aldose reductase]] converts excess glucose to [[sorbitol]] via the polyol pathway. Sorbitol, being osmotically active and poorly permeable across cell membranes, accumulates intracellularly, drawing water into lens fibers and causing swelling, disruption of fiber architecture, and opacification. A similar mechanism occurs with [[galactosemia]], where galactitol accumulates.

#### 4. Disruption of Ion and Water Homeostasis
The lens maintains transparency partly through precise regulation of intracellular ion concentrations via the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump and Ca²⁺-ATPase. Aging and pathological insults impair these pumps, leading to increased intracellular sodium and calcium, water influx, and cellular swelling. Elevated intracellular calcium activates [[calpains]] (calcium-dependent proteases), which degrade cytoskeletal proteins and crystallins.

#### 5. UV Radiation-Induced Damage
[[UV-B radiation]] (280–315 nm) is absorbed by the lens and generates free radicals, photo-oxidizes tryptophan residues in crystallins (producing fluorescent chromophores such as [[kynurenine]] derivatives), and cross-links proteins. Chronic UV-B exposure is particularly associated with [[cortical cataract]].

#### 6. Genetic Factors
Congenital and hereditary cataracts may result from mutations in genes encoding crystallins (e.g., *CRYAA*, *CRYBB2*, *CRYGD*), connexins (e.g., *GJA3*, *GJA8*), membrane proteins (e.g., *MIP/AQP0*), and transcription factors (e.g., *PAX6*, *PITX3*, *MAF*). Twin studies suggest that genetic factors account for approximately 48–59% of the variance in nuclear cataract and 58–75% in cortical cataract.

## Classification

Cataracts are classified by **etiology**, **morphology**, and **maturity**.

### Classification by Etiology

| **Type** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Age-related (senile)** | Most common; multifactorial degeneration associated with aging |
| **Congenital** | Present at birth; may be hereditary (autosomal dominant most common) or secondary to intrauterine infection ([[TORCH infections]]: [[toxoplasmosis]], [[rubella]], [[cytomegalovirus]], [[herpes simplex]]) |
| **Traumatic** | Following blunt or penetrating [[ocular trauma]]; may present as rosette-shaped opacity, [[Vossius ring]], or total opacification |
| **Metabolic** | Associated with [[diabetes mellitus]], [[galactosemia]], [[hypocalcemia]], [[Wilson disease]], [[Fabry disease]] |
| **Drug-induced (toxic)** | [[Corticosteroids]] (posterior subcapsular), [[chlorpromazine]], [[amiodarone]], [[miotics]] (e.g., [[pilocarpine]]), [[busulfan]] |
| **Secondary (complicated)** | Complicating chronic [[uveitis]], [[retinitis pigmentosa]], [[retinal detachment]], [[glaucoma]] |
| **Radiation-induced** | Following exposure to [[ionizing radiation]] or [[infrared radiation]] ("glassblower's cataract") |

### Classification by Morphology (Age-Related)

#### Nuclear Cataract (Nuclear Sclerosis)
- Most common subtype of age-related cataract
- Progressive hardening (sclerosis) and yellowing/browning (brunescence) of the [[lens nucleus]]
- Caused by compaction of older lens fibers and accumulation of chromophores (urochrome pigment)
- Increases the refractive index of the nucleus, producing a **myopic shift** ("second sight" phenomenon—temporary improvement in near vision in previously [[presbyopic]] patients)
- Graded clinically using the **Lens Opacities Classification System III (LOCS III)** on a scale of nuclear opalescence (NO1–NO6) and nuclear color (NC1–NC6)
- Advanced nuclear cataracts may appear deep brown (**brunescent cataract**) or black (**cataracta nigra**)

#### Cortical Cataract
- Opacification of the [[lens cortex]]
- Begins as vacuoles and water clefts in the peripheral cortex, progressing to spoke-like (cuneiform) or wedge-shaped opacities radiating toward the center
- Best visualized with [[retroillumination]] on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]
- Strongly associated with [[UV-B]] exposure and [[diabetes mellitus]]
- May cause significant [[glare]] and difficulty with driving, especially at night

#### Posterior Subcapsular Cataract (PSC)
- Opacification located just anterior to the [[posterior lens capsule]], in the axial (central) region
- Appears as a granular, plaque-like opacity on retroillumination
- Disproportionately affects near vision and causes significant glare and haloes, even when relatively small
- Strongly associated with [[corticosteroid]] use (systemic, topical ophthalmic, inhaled), [[diabetes mellitus]], [[uveitis]], [[retinitis pigmentosa]], and [[radiation]] exposure
- Tends to occur in younger patients compared to nuclear and cortical cataracts
- Progresses more rapidly than other subtypes

#### Anterior Subcapsular Cataract
- Opacity located just beneath the [[anterior lens capsule]]
- May be associated with [[atopic dermatitis]], trauma, or chronic [[anterior uveitis]]
- Fibrous metaplasia of the lens epithelium may produce a dense, white plaque

### Classification by Maturity

| **Stage** | **Clinical Features** |
|---|---|
| **Immature** | Partially opaque lens; some cortex remains clear; [[red reflex]] partially visible; iris shadow present on oblique illumination |
| **Mature** | Completely opaque lens; no red reflex; no iris shadow; cortex entirely opacified |
| **Hypermature (Morgagnian)** | Liquefied cortex; dense, shrunken nucleus sinks inferiorly within the capsular bag; lens capsule may appear wrinkled; risk of [[phacolytic glaucoma]] (leakage of HMW proteins through intact capsule) and [[phacoantigenic uveitis]] |
| **Intumescent** | Swollen lens due to water absorption; may cause [[pupillary block]] and [[phacomorphic glaucoma]] (secondary angle-closure); shallow [[anterior chamber]] |

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

Cataract symptoms are typically insidious in onset and progressive. The specific symptom profile depends on the type, location, and density of the opacity.

### Common Symptoms

- **Gradual, painless decrease in [[visual acuity]]** – the hallmark symptom
- **[[Glare]]** – increased sensitivity to bright lights, oncoming headlights; particularly prominent with PSC and cortical cataracts
- **Haloes around lights** – due to diffraction of light by the opacity
- **Reduced [[contrast sensitivity]]** – difficulty distinguishing objects against similar backgrounds; may be impaired even when Snellen acuity is relatively preserved
- **Myopic shift** – nuclear sclerosis increases the refractive index of the lens, causing a shift toward [[myopia]]; patients may report temporary improvement in reading vision ("second sight")
- **Monocular [[diplopia]] or [[polyopia]]** – irregular refraction through different zones of the lens
- **Color vision changes** – nuclear brunescence acts as a yellow-brown filter, reducing perception of blue and violet wavelengths; patients may not notice until after surgery when colors appear brighter and "cooler"
- **Difficulty with night driving** – due to glare and reduced contrast
- **Frequent changes in spectacle prescription** – progressive refractive instability
- **Faded or washed-out appearance of colors**

### Symptoms by Cataract Type

| **Cataract Type** | **Characteristic Symptoms** |
|---|---|
| Nuclear sclerotic | Myopic shift, "second sight," gradual distance blur, yellowed color perception |
| Cortical | Glare (especially with oncoming headlights), monocular diplopia |
| Posterior subcapsular | Disproportionate near vision difficulty, significant glare in bright light, rapid progression |

It is critical to note that cataracts are **painless**. The presence of pain, redness, or acute visual loss should prompt investigation for alternative or concurrent diagnoses such as [[acute angle-closure glaucoma]], [[uveitis]], or [[endophthalmitis]].

## Diagnosis and Clinical Evaluation

### History
A thorough history should include:

- Duration and progression of visual symptoms
- Impact on activities of daily living (ADLs), driving, reading, occupational tasks
- Medication history (especially [[corticosteroids]], [[tamsulosin]] for [[intraoperative floppy iris syndrome]] risk)
- Ocular history (trauma, surgery, [[uveitis]], [[glaucoma]])
- Systemic history ([[diabetes mellitus]], [[atopic dermatitis]], [[connective tissue disorders]])
- Family history of cataracts or hereditary eye disease

### Visual Acuity Testing
- **Best-corrected [[visual acuity]] (BCVA)** using a [[Snellen chart]] or [[LogMAR chart]] (ETDRS)
- **[[Pinhole acuity]]** – improvement with pinhole suggests refractive error; limited improvement may indicate media opacity or retinal pathology
- **[[Contrast sensitivity]] testing** – more sensitive than Snellen acuity for detecting functional impairment from early cataracts (e.g., [[Pelli-Robson chart]], [[CSV-1000]])
- **[[Glare testing]]** – [[brightness acuity tester]] (BAT) or similar devices assess visual function under glare conditions, which may reveal significant disability even with good Snellen acuity

### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy
[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] is the gold standard for cataract evaluation.

- **Direct focal illumination** – assesses nuclear color and opalescence
- **Retroillumination** – essential for detecting cortical spokes, posterior subcapsular plaques, and vacuoles; performed by reflecting light off the [[fundus]] through a dilated [[pupil]]
- **Oblique illumination** – evaluates iris shadow sign (present in immature cataracts)
- **Slit-beam cross-section** – determines the depth and location of opacities within the lens

### Grading Systems

| **Grading System** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **LOCS III (Lens Opacities Classification System III)** | Standard photographic grading system; grades nuclear opalescence (NO), nuclear color (NC), cortical opacity (C), and posterior subcapsular opacity (P) on decimal scales |
| **Wisconsin Grading System** | Used in epidemiological studies |
| **Oxford Clinical Cataract Classification and Grading System** | Alternative clinical grading |
| **WHO Simplified Cataract Grading System** | Designed for field surveys in developing countries |

### Dilated Fundus Examination
A thorough [[dilated fundus examination]] is essential to:

- Rule out concurrent posterior segment pathology (e.g., [[age-related macular degeneration]], [[diabetic retinopathy]], [[epiretinal membrane]], [[macular hole]]) that may limit visual recovery after cataract surgery
- Assess the [[red reflex]] – diminished or absent red reflex indicates significant lens opacity

### Additional Diagnostic and Preoperative Investigations

| **Investigation** | **Purpose** |
|---|---|
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)** | Evaluate macular anatomy (rule out [[macular edema]], [[epiretinal membrane]], [[macular hole]]); essential preoperative assessment |
| **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** | Assess posterior segment when fundus view is obscured by dense cataract |
| **[[Optical biometry]] (e.g., IOLMaster, Lenstar)** | Measure [[axial length]], [[keratometry]], and [[anterior chamber depth]] for [[IOL power calculation]] |
| **[[A-scan ultrasonography]] (immersion or contact)** | Alternative biometry when optical methods fail (e.g., dense cataract) |
| **[[Corneal topography]]/[[tomography]]** | Detect irregular [[astigmatism]], [[keratoconus]], or previous [[refractive surgery]]; guide [[toric IOL]] selection |
| **[[Specular microscopy]]** | Assess [[corneal endothelial cell]] density, especially in patients with [[Fuchs endothelial dystrophy]] or prior surgery |
| **[[Potential acuity meter]] (PAM) / [[laser interferometry]]** | Estimate potential postoperative visual acuity by projecting an image directly onto the retina, bypassing the cataract |
| **[[IOL power calculation]] formulas** | SRK/T, Holladay 1 & 2, Haigis, Barrett Universal II, Hill-RBF, Kane formula |

## Management

### Non-Surgical Management (Conservative)

In early cataracts where visual impairment is mild and does not significantly affect daily activities, conservative management is appropriate:

- **Updated refraction and spectacle correction** – optimizing BCVA with new glasses
- **Increased illumination** for reading tasks
- **Anti-glare coatings** and **polarized sunglasses**
- **Magnification aids**
- **UV-protective eyewear** – may slow progression
- **Patient education** regarding the progressive nature of cataracts and indications for surgery
- **Pupillary dilation** – in select cases, dilating drops (e.g., low-dose [[phenylephrine]]) may temporarily improve vision by allowing light to bypass peripheral cortical opacities (rarely used in practice)

### Pharmacological Interventions (Investigational)

Currently, **no pharmacological agent has been approved by the FDA or EMA for the prevention or reversal of cataracts**. However, several compounds are under active investigation:

| **Agent** | **Mechanism / Status** |
|---|---|
| **[[Lanosterol]]** | Endogenous sterol; shown in animal models to partially dissolve crystallin aggregates; human clinical trials pending |
| **[[25-Hydroxycholesterol]]** | Oxysterol with similar proposed mechanism to lanosterol |
| **[[N-acetylcarnosine]] (NAC) eye drops** | Proposed antioxidant; marketed commercially but **lacks robust evidence** from large randomized controlled trials; not endorsed by major ophthalmological societies |
| **[[Pirenoxine]] (Catalin)** | Competitive inhibitor of quinoid substances; used in some Asian countries; limited evidence of efficacy |
| **Antioxidant supplementation** (Vitamins C, E, lutein, zeaxanthin) | Epidemiological associations suggest protective effect; AREDS/AREDS2 did not demonstrate significant cataract prevention benefit |
| **[[Aldose reductase inhibitors]]** | Target the polyol pathway in diabetic cataracts; limited clinical success to date |

### Surgical Management

Surgery is the definitive treatment for visually significant cataracts. The decision to operate is based on:

1. **Patient symptoms and functional impairment** – difficulty with ADLs, driving, reading, occupational tasks
2. **Best-corrected visual acuity** – while no absolute acuity threshold mandates surgery, many guidelines consider surgery when BCVA is 20/40 (6/12) or worse, or when functional impairment exists regardless of acuity
3. **Glare disability** – significant glare may warrant surgery even with relatively good Snellen acuity
4. **Medical indications** – cataract preventing adequate fundus examination or treatment of posterior segment disease (e.g., [[diabetic retinopathy]] requiring [[laser photocoagulation]]); lens-induced pathology ([[phacolytic glaucoma]], [[phacomorphic glaucoma]], [[phacoantigenic uveitis]])

#### Phacoemulsification

**[[Phacoemulsification]]** is the current gold standard surgical technique worldwide.

**Procedure:**
1. **Anesthesia** – [[topical anesthesia]] (e.g., [[proparacaine]], [[tetracaine]]) ± intracameral [[lidocaine]]; [[peribulbar block]] or [[retrobulbar block]] for complex cases
2. **Incision** – small (2.2–2.8 mm) clear [[corneal]] or scleral tunnel incision
3. **[[Viscoelastic]]** (OVD – ophthalmic viscosurgical device) injection – to maintain the [[anterior chamber]] and protect the [[corneal endothelium]] (e.g., [[sodium hyaluronate]], [[hydroxypropyl methylcellulose]])
4. **[[Capsulorhexis]]** (continuous curvilinear capsulorhexis, CCC) – circular opening in the [[anterior lens capsule]] using a cystotome or forceps
5. **[[Hydrodissection]] and [[hydrodelineation]]** – injection of balanced salt solution (BSS) to separate the cortex from the capsule and the nucleus from the epinucleus
6. **Phacoemulsification** – ultrasonic probe emulsifies and aspirates the lens nucleus using various techniques (divide-and-conquer, phaco chop, stop-and-chop)
7. **Irrigation/aspiration (I/A)** – removal of residual cortical material
8. **IOL implantation** – foldable [[intraocular lens]] inserted through the small incision into the [[capsular bag]]
9. **Wound hydration** – stromal hydration of the incision to achieve a self-sealing wound; sutures rarely required

**Advantages:**
- Small, self-sealing incision (sutureless)
- Rapid visual rehabilitation
- Low complication rate
- Outpatient procedure (typically 15–30 minutes)

#### Femtosecond Laser-Assisted Cataract Surgery (FLACS)

**[[Femtosecond laser-assisted cataract surgery]]** uses a femtosecond laser to perform:
- Corneal incisions
- Capsulotomy (more precise and reproducible than manual capsulorhexis)
- Lens fragmentation (pre-chopping the nucleus, reducing phacoemulsification energy)
- Arcuate keratotomy for [[astigmatism]] correction

While FLACS offers increased precision, current evidence (including the FEMCAT trial and Cochrane reviews) has not demonstrated clinically significant superiority over conventional phacoemulsification in terms of final visual outcomes for routine cases. It remains more costly and may be beneficial in complex cases (e.g., [[zonular weakness]], dense cataracts, pediatric cataracts).

#### Extracapsular Cataract Extraction (ECCE)

**[[Extracapsular cataract extraction]]** involves a larger incision (10–12 mm) through which the intact nucleus is expressed manually. It is still performed in settings where phacoemulsification is unavailable or for extremely dense (brunescent/white mature) cataracts. Requires suturing and has a longer visual rehabilitation period.

#### Intracapsular Cataract Extraction (ICCE)

**[[Intracapsular cataract extraction]]** involves removal of the entire lens including the capsule, typically using a [[cryoprobe]]. Largely obsolete in modern practice due to high complication rates (vitreous loss, [[retinal detachment]], [[cystoid macular edema]]). May still be performed in rare circumstances (e.g., subluxated lens with extensive [[zonular dialysis]]).

#### Manual Small-Incision Cataract Surgery (MSICS)

**[[Manual small-incision cataract surgery]]** is a modified ECCE technique using a self-sealing scleral tunnel incision (6–7 mm). It is widely used in developing countries due to lower cost, no requirement for phacoemulsification equipment, and excellent outcomes. Studies (e.g., the LAHAN trial) have shown comparable visual outcomes to phacoemulsification for dense cataracts.

### Intraocular Lens (IOL) Options

| **IOL Type** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Monofocal** | Single focal point; most commonly set for distance; patient requires reading glasses for near tasks |
| **[[Toric IOL]]** | Corrects pre-existing corneal [[astigmatism]]; requires precise axis alignment |
| **[[Multifocal IOL]]** | Multiple focal zones (diffractive or refractive) for distance, intermediate, and near vision; may cause [[dysphotopsia]] (haloes, glare) |
| **[[Extended depth of focus]] (EDOF) IOL** | Elongated focal range; less dysphotopsia than multifocal; good intermediate and distance vision |
| **[[Accommodating IOL]]** | Designed to shift position with ciliary muscle contraction; limited real-world accommodative range |
| **[[Light-adjustable lens]] (LAL)** | Allows postoperative refractive adjustment using UV light; enables customized correction |

### Postoperative Care

Standard postoperative regimen typically includes:

- **Topical [[corticosteroid]]** (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate]] 1%, [[dexamethasone]] 0.1%) – tapered over 4–6 weeks to control inflammation
- **Topical [[NSAID]]** (e.g., [[ketorolac]] 0.5%, [[nepafenac]] 0.1%, [[bromfenac]] 0.07%) – reduces inflammation and helps prevent [[cystoid macular edema]] (CME/Irvine-Gass syndrome)
- **Topical [[antibiotic]]** (e.g., [[moxifloxacin]] 0.5%, [[gatifloxacin]] 0.3%) – prophylaxis against [[endophthalmitis]]; typically 1–2 weeks
- **Protective eye shield** – especially at night for the first week
- **Activity restrictions** – avoid heavy lifting, bending, eye rubbing, swimming for 2–4 weeks
- **Follow-up schedule** – typically day 1, week 1, month 1, and as needed

## Complications of Cataract Surgery

While modern cataract surgery is remarkably safe, complications can occur:

### Intraoperative Complications

| **Complication** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **[[Posterior capsule rupture]]** | Most common serious intraoperative complication (~1–3%); may lead to vitreous loss, dropped nucleus, and need for anterior [[vitrectomy]] |
| **[[Zonular dialysis]]** | Weakness or dehiscence of [[zonular fibers]]; risk factors include [[pseudoexfoliation syndrome]], trauma, high myopia |
| **Dropped nucleus** | Nuclear fragments fall into the [[vitreous cavity]]; requires [[pars plana vitrectomy]] for retrieval |
| **[[Descemet membrane]] detachment** | Separation of Descemet membrane from corneal stroma during incision or instrumentation |
| **Suprachoroidal hemorrhage** | Rare but devastating; hemorrhage in the [[suprachoroidal space]] |
| **[[Intraoperative floppy iris syndrome]] (IFIS)** | Associated with [[tamsulosin]] (alpha-1 blocker) use; iris billows, prolapses, and constricts intraoperatively |

### Postoperative Complications

| **Complication** | **Incidence** | **Description** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Posterior capsule opacification]] (PCO)** | 20–50% within 2–5 years | Most common long-term complication; proliferation and migration of residual [[lens epithelial cells]] on the posterior capsule; treated with [[Nd:YAG laser capsulotomy]] |
| **[[Cystoid macular edema]] (CME)** | 1–2% (clinical) | Irvine-Gass syndrome; fluid accumulation in the [[macula]]; treated with topical NSAIDs and corticosteroids |
| **[[Endophthalmitis]]** | 0.03–0.1% | Devastating intraocular infection; most commonly caused by [[Staphylococcus epidermidis]]; requires urgent intravitreal antibiotics ([[vancomycin]] + [[ceftazidime]]) ± vitrectomy (per EVS guidelines) |
| **[[Retinal detachment]]** | 0.5–1% | Higher risk with high myopia, posterior capsule rupture, vitreous loss |
| **Persistent corneal edema** | Variable | Due to endothelial cell loss; risk increased with pre-existing [[Fuchs dystrophy]] or prolonged phacoemulsification |
| **[[Dysphotopsia]]** | 10–15% (positive); 2–4% (negative) | Positive: glare, haloes, streaks; Negative: temporal dark crescent/shadow; related to IOL edge design |
| **IOL dislocation/decentration** | <1% | May require surgical repositioning, exchange, or scleral fixation |
| **Elevated [[intraocular pressure]]** | Variable | Transient IOP spike common; may be due to retained viscoelastic, inflammation, or steroid response |
| **Refractive surprise** | Variable | Unexpected postoperative refractive error; may require spectacle correction, IOL exchange, or piggyback IOL |

## Posterior Capsule Opacification (PCO) – "Secondary Cataract"

[[Posterior capsule opacification]] deserves special mention as the most common long-term complication of cataract surgery. It is sometimes colloquially (and inaccurately) termed "secondary cataract" or "after-cataract."

**Pathophysiology:** Residual lens epithelial cells (LECs) on the anterior capsule and equatorial lens bow proliferate, migrate onto the posterior capsule, and undergo:
- **Fibrous metaplasia** – anterior LECs form a fibrotic membrane (fibrous PCO)
- **Regenerative/pearl formation** – equatorial LECs form Elschnig pearls (regenerative PCO/Soemmering ring)

**Treatment:** [[Nd:YAG laser capsulotomy]] – a quick, painless outpatient procedure that creates an opening in the opacified posterior capsule, restoring the visual axis. Complications of YAG capsulotomy include transient IOP elevation, IOL pitting, vitreous floaters, and rarely [[retinal detachment]] or CME.

**Prevention strategies:** Square-edge IOL optic design (creates a barrier effect against LEC migration), hydrophobic acrylic IOL material, and thorough cortical cleanup during surgery.

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Cataract
- [[Congenital cataract]] and developmental cataracts require urgent evaluation and management to prevent [[amblyopia]]
- Surgery is typically performed within the first few weeks of life for dense unilateral cataracts and within the first 6–10 weeks for bilateral cataracts
- Primary posterior capsulotomy with anterior vitrectomy is often performed in young children to prevent PCO
- IOL implantation in infants remains controversial (Infant Aphakia Treatment Study – IATS); contact lens correction of [[aphakia]] may be preferred in very young infants
- Aggressive [[amblyopia]] treatment (patching, atropine penalization) is essential postoperatively

### Cataract in Uveitis
- [[Uveitis]]-associated cataracts (particularly PSC) require meticulous preoperative control of inflammation (minimum 3 months of quiescence recommended)
- Higher risk of postoperative complications (CME, posterior synechiae, hypotony, glaucoma)
- Aggressive perioperative anti-inflammatory therapy is essential

### Cataract with Concurrent Glaucoma
- Combined [[phacoemulsification]] with [[glaucoma]] surgery (e.g., phaco-[[trabeculectomy]], phaco-[[MIGS]]) may be considered
- Phacoemulsification alone can reduce [[intraocular pressure]] by 1.5–4 mmHg, particularly in angle-closure eyes

## Prevention

While age-related cataract cannot be entirely prevented, evidence supports the following risk-reduction strategies:

- **UV protection** – wearing UV-blocking sunglasses and wide-brimmed hats
- **Smoking cessation** – one of the most modifiable risk factors
- **Glycemic control** in [[diabetes mellitus]]
- **Judicious use of [[corticosteroids]]** – using the lowest effective dose for the shortest duration
- **Antioxidant-rich diet** – fruits, vegetables, and foods high in vitamins C, E, lutein, and zeaxanthin (though supplementation trials have not shown definitive benefit)
- **Avoidance of ocular trauma** – protective eyewear in high-risk activities
- **Limiting alcohol consumption**

## Prognosis

The prognosis following modern cataract surgery is excellent. Large-scale studies consistently demonstrate:

- **>95% of patients achieve BCVA of 20/40 (6/12) or better** postoperatively in the absence of concurrent ocular pathology
- **>85% achieve 20/20 (6/6) or better** with appropriate IOL selection and absence of comorbidities
- Patient satisfaction rates exceed 95% in most series
- Cataract surgery has been shown to improve quality of life, reduce fall risk in the elderly, decrease motor vehicle accidents, and lower all-cause mortality in observational studies

Visual outcomes may be limited by pre-existing conditions such as [[age-related macular degeneration]], [[diabetic macular edema]], [[glaucoma]], [[amblyopia]], or [[corneal disease]].

## Summary

Cataract remains the most prevalent cause of treatable blindness globally. Its pathophysiology involves a complex interplay of oxidative stress, protein aggregation, osmotic imbalance, UV damage, and genetic predisposition. Diagnosis relies on comprehensive clinical evaluation including slit-lamp biomicroscopy, visual function testing, and ancillary imaging. While no proven pharmacological therapy exists, surgical intervention via phacoemulsification with IOL implantation offers outstanding visual rehabilitation with a high safety profile. Ongoing research into pharmacological prevention, advanced IOL technology, and femtosecond laser applications continues to refine the management of this ubiquitous condition.
