

# Nystagmus

---

**Nystagmus** is a complex oculomotor disorder characterized by involuntary, rhythmic oscillations of the eyes that may be congenital or acquired, physiological or pathological. The term derives from the Greek word *nystagmos* (νυσταγμός), meaning "drowsiness" or "nodding," reflecting the repetitive nature of the eye movements. Nystagmus represents a failure of the mechanisms responsible for holding [[gaze]] steady, and its clinical significance spans the full breadth of [[optometry]], [[ophthalmology]], [[neurology]], and [[neuro-ophthalmology]]. It can serve as a critical localizing sign for lesions within the [[central nervous system]] (CNS), or it may be the hallmark of early-onset visual deprivation. Understanding the classification, pathophysiology, clinical presentation, diagnostic evaluation, and management of nystagmus is essential for every eye care practitioner.

---

## Definition and Classification

Nystagmus is broadly defined as an involuntary, repetitive, to-and-fro movement of the eyes. The oscillations may be **jerk** (with a slow drift phase and a fast corrective [[saccade]]) or **pendular** (with roughly equal velocity in both directions). Clinically, nystagmus is classified along several axes:

### By Age of Onset

| Category | Onset | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Infantile (Congenital) Nystagmus** | Birth to 6 months | Horizontal, conjugate, often with a null zone; associated with [[infantile nystagmus syndrome]] (INS) |
| **Acquired Nystagmus** | After 6 months of age | May be in any direction; often associated with neurological disease; frequently symptomatic (oscillopsia) |

### By Waveform

| Waveform | Description | Typical Association |
|---|---|---|
| **Jerk Nystagmus** | Slow phase drift followed by fast corrective saccade; named by direction of fast phase | Vestibular, gaze-evoked, downbeat, upbeat nystagmus |
| **Pendular Nystagmus** | Sinusoidal oscillation with equal velocity in both directions | [[Multiple sclerosis]], oculopalatal tremor, infantile nystagmus |
| **Mixed** | Pendular in primary gaze, jerk in lateral gaze | Infantile nystagmus syndrome |

### By Etiology

- **Physiological Nystagmus**: [[Optokinetic nystagmus]] (OKN), end-point nystagmus, caloric-induced nystagmus — these are normal responses.
- **Pathological Nystagmus**: Indicates dysfunction of the visual, vestibular, or cerebellar gaze-holding systems.

### By Plane of Oscillation

Nystagmus may be **horizontal**, **vertical** (upbeat or downbeat), **torsional** (rotary), or a combination thereof. The plane of oscillation provides critical localizing information:

| Direction | Likely Localization |
|---|---|
| Horizontal | [[Vestibular nuclei]], [[cerebellum]], peripheral [[vestibular system]], or infantile nystagmus |
| Downbeat | [[Craniocervical junction]] (e.g., [[Arnold-Chiari malformation]]), [[cervicomedullary junction]], [[flocculus]] of cerebellum |
| Upbeat | [[Medulla oblongata]], ventral [[tegmentum]], anterior [[vermis]] |
| Torsional | Central or peripheral vestibular pathology, [[brainstem]] lesions |
| See-saw | Parasellar lesions, [[chiasmal]] pathology |

## Epidemiology

Nystagmus affects approximately **24 per 10,000 individuals** in the general population, though prevalence varies by type and ethnicity. Infantile nystagmus syndrome (INS) is the most common form, with an estimated prevalence of 14 per 10,000. Males are more frequently affected than females in X-linked forms such as those associated with [[ocular albinism]] and some forms of [[congenital stationary night blindness]]. Acquired nystagmus prevalence is more difficult to estimate, as it is secondary to a vast array of neurological and systemic conditions.

---

## Pathophysiology

The stability of ocular fixation depends on three principal neural integrator systems:

1. **The [[vestibulo-ocular reflex]] (VOR)**: Stabilizes images on the [[retina]] during head movements via input from the [[semicircular canals]] and [[otolith organs]], processed through the [[vestibular nuclei]] and relayed to the [[extraocular muscles]] via cranial nerves [[CN III]], [[CN IV]], and [[CN VI]].

2. **The optokinetic system**: Uses visual motion information to supplement the VOR during sustained head rotation.

3. **The neural integrator (gaze-holding network)**: Converts velocity-coded saccadic commands into position-coded signals to maintain eccentric gaze. Key structures include the [[nucleus prepositus hypoglossi]] (for horizontal gaze), the [[interstitial nucleus of Cajal]] (for vertical and torsional gaze), and the [[flocculus]] and [[paraflocculus]] of the [[cerebellum]].

Nystagmus arises when any of these systems is disrupted:

### Infantile Nystagmus Syndrome (INS)

The pathophysiology of INS remains incompletely understood but is believed to involve instability in the smooth pursuit and optokinetic subsystems during a critical period of visual development. The characteristic waveform of INS is the **accelerating slow phase** (increasing velocity exponential waveform), which distinguishes it from vestibular nystagmus (linear slow phase) and gaze-evoked nystagmus (decelerating slow phase). Current models suggest that INS results from a miscalibration of the ocular motor system, possibly due to abnormal visual input during infancy (as in [[foveal hypoplasia]], [[aniridia]], [[achromatopsia]], [[optic nerve hypoplasia]], or [[albinism]]) or from a primary motor defect in the absence of sensory abnormality (termed "idiopathic INS").

The **null zone** — a gaze angle at which nystagmus intensity is minimal and visual acuity is best — is a hallmark of INS. Patients often adopt an **anomalous head posture** (AHP) to place the eyes in the null zone. The phenomenon of **foveation periods** — brief intervals during each nystagmus cycle when the eye velocity is low and the image is on the [[fovea]] — is the primary determinant of visual acuity in INS.

### Acquired Nystagmus

#### Vestibular Nystagmus

Peripheral vestibular nystagmus results from asymmetric input from the two [[labyrinths]]. According to **Ewald's laws** and the **tonic imbalance theory**, loss of function in one labyrinth creates a velocity signal imbalance, producing a slow drift toward the lesioned side and a corrective fast phase away from it. Peripheral vestibular nystagmus is typically **unidirectional**, **mixed horizontal-torsional**, suppressed by [[visual fixation]], and accompanied by [[vertigo]], [[nausea]], and [[hearing loss]].

Central vestibular nystagmus arises from lesions in the [[vestibular nuclei]], [[cerebellum]], or brainstem pathways. It is often **purely vertical or torsional**, **not suppressed by fixation**, may change direction with gaze, and may lack prominent vertigo.

#### Gaze-Evoked Nystagmus (GEN)

GEN is the most common form of acquired nystagmus and results from failure of the neural integrator to maintain eccentric gaze. The eyes drift back toward primary position (decelerating slow phase) with corrective saccades in the direction of gaze. Causes include medications (e.g., [[anticonvulsants]], [[benzodiazepines]], [[alcohol]]), [[cerebellar]] disease, and brainstem lesions.

#### Downbeat Nystagmus (DBN)

DBN is characterized by a slow upward drift with a fast downward corrective phase, most prominent in downgaze and lateral gaze. The most common structural cause is a [[Chiari malformation type I]], but it may also result from [[cerebellar degeneration]], [[multiple sclerosis]], [[lithium]] toxicity, [[magnesium]] deficiency, or [[Wernicke encephalopathy]]. The pathophysiology involves disruption of the [[flocculus]], which normally inhibits the anterior [[semicircular canal]] pathways; loss of this inhibition produces an upward slow phase.

#### Upbeat Nystagmus (UBN)

UBN features a slow downward drift with a fast upward corrective phase. It localizes to the medulla or pontomesencephalic junction and may be caused by demyelination, infarction, or [[Wernicke encephalopathy]]. The mechanism involves disruption of the upward VOR pathways, specifically the ventral tegmental tract.

#### Periodic Alternating Nystagmus (PAN)

PAN is a horizontal jerk nystagmus that periodically reverses direction, typically cycling every 90–120 seconds. It may be congenital or acquired (associated with [[Chiari malformation]], [[cerebellar degeneration]], or [[multiple sclerosis]]). The pathophysiology involves disruption of the velocity storage mechanism in the vestibular nuclei, with loss of cerebellar (nodular) inhibition leading to an unstable oscillation.

#### See-Saw Nystagmus

In see-saw nystagmus, one eye elevates and intorts while the other depresses and extorts, then the pattern reverses. It is associated with parasellar masses (e.g., [[craniopharyngioma]], [[pituitary adenoma]]) compressing the [[optic chiasm]] and with large [[bitemporal hemianopia]]. The mechanism may involve disruption of the [[interstitial nucleus of Cajal]] or loss of visual input guiding torsional eye position.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Infantile Nystagmus

- **Onset**: Within the first 6 months of life.
- **Direction**: Predominantly horizontal, even in upgaze (a distinguishing feature from acquired vertical nystagmus).
- **Oscillopsia**: Typically **absent** — the brain adapts to the retinal image motion during development.
- **Visual acuity**: Reduced but variable; depends on foveation time and underlying sensory condition.
- **Null zone and anomalous head posture**: Present in the majority of patients.
- **Convergence dampening**: Nystagmus often decreases with [[convergence]] (the "convergence null").
- **Inversion of the optokinetic response**: OKN drum rotation may produce nystagmus in the opposite direction to normal.
- **Head nodding or oscillation**: May be present as a compensatory mechanism.
- **Associated conditions**: [[Albinism]] (oculocutaneous or ocular), [[aniridia]], [[achromatopsia]], [[congenital cataracts]], [[optic nerve hypoplasia]], [[Leber congenital amaurosis]], [[foveal hypoplasia]], [[congenital stationary night blindness]], and [[retinal dystrophies]].

### Acquired Nystagmus

- **Oscillopsia**: The hallmark symptom — the subjective sensation that the visual world is oscillating or bouncing. This is often profoundly disabling.
- **Reduced visual acuity**: Due to retinal image instability.
- **Vertigo and disequilibrium**: Especially with vestibular nystagmus.
- **Nausea and vomiting**: Accompanying acute vestibular nystagmus.
- **Diplopia**: May occur if nystagmus is asymmetric or disconjugate.
- **Head tilt or turn**: May be adopted to minimize nystagmus or to compensate for a torsional component.

### Impact on Quality of Life

Nystagmus significantly impacts daily functioning. Patients with infantile nystagmus may experience difficulty with tasks requiring fine visual acuity (reading, facial recognition), while those with acquired nystagmus may be unable to drive, read, or ambulate safely due to oscillopsia. Psychosocial effects — including self-consciousness about visible eye movements, difficulty with eye contact, and social stigma — are well-documented and should not be underestimated.

## Diagnosis and Diagnostic Modalities

### Clinical History

A thorough history is the cornerstone of nystagmus evaluation:

- **Age of onset**: Infantile vs. acquired — this single factor dramatically narrows the differential.
- **Symptom profile**: Presence or absence of oscillopsia, vertigo, hearing changes, headache, or neurological symptoms.
- **Medication and substance use**: [[Anticonvulsants]] (phenytoin, carbamazepine), [[sedatives]], [[alcohol]], [[lithium]], [[aminoglycosides]].
- **Family history**: X-linked or autosomal inheritance patterns (e.g., [[albinism]], [[idiopathic infantile nystagmus]]).
- **Associated systemic conditions**: [[Multiple sclerosis]], [[stroke]], [[Arnold-Chiari malformation]], [[cerebellar ataxia]].

### Clinical Examination

| Examination Component | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Observation of nystagmus waveform** | Determine jerk vs. pendular, direction, amplitude, frequency |
| **Gaze testing (9 cardinal positions)** | Identify null zone, gaze-evoked nystagmus, direction-changing nystagmus |
| **Cover test** | Detect [[latent nystagmus]] (manifest latent nystagmus), [[strabismus]] |
| **Fixation testing (with and without fixation)** | Peripheral vestibular nystagmus suppresses with fixation; central does not. Use Frenzel goggles or infrared video goggles to remove fixation |
| **Convergence testing** | Assess convergence dampening (characteristic of INS) |
| **Head posture assessment** | Document anomalous head posture and its relationship to null zone |
| **Pupil examination** | Detect [[afferent pupillary defect]], [[transillumination defects]] (albinism) |
| **Anterior segment examination** | [[Iris transillumination]] (albinism), [[aniridia]], [[cataract]] |
| **Fundoscopy** | [[Foveal hypoplasia]], [[optic nerve hypoplasia]], [[optic atrophy]], [[retinal dystrophy]], [[macular pathology]] |
| **Visual acuity** | Best corrected, with and without head posture; use of [[NOTT card]] or electronic [[visual acuity]] charts |
| **Optokinetic nystagmus testing** | Assess OKN symmetry; reversed OKN suggests INS |
| **Head impulse test (HIT)** | Assess VOR gain; abnormal in peripheral vestibular lesions |
| **Dix-Hallpike maneuver** | If positional nystagmus is suspected ([[benign paroxysmal positional vertigo]]) |

### Instrumentation and Ancillary Testing

| Test | Clinical Utility |
|---|---|
| **[[Eye movement recording]] (infrared oculography / video-oculography)** | Quantitative analysis of waveform, frequency, amplitude, foveation time; gold standard for nystagmus characterization |
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)** | Detect [[foveal hypoplasia]] (grading per Thomas et al. classification), measure retinal nerve fiber layer thickness |
| **[[Electroretinography]] (ERG)** | Diagnose [[achromatopsia]], [[congenital stationary night blindness]], [[Leber congenital amaurosis]], and other [[retinal dystrophies]] |
| **[[Visual evoked potential]] (VEP)** | Detect chiasmal misrouting (pathognomonic for [[albinism]]); assess visual pathway integrity |
| **[[Magnetic resonance imaging]] (MRI) of brain and orbits** | Essential for acquired nystagmus — evaluate for [[demyelination]], [[Chiari malformation]], [[posterior fossa]] tumors, [[brainstem]] lesions, [[cerebellar]] pathology |
| **[[Genetic testing]]** | Identify mutations in genes such as *FRMD7* (X-linked idiopathic INS), *GPR143* (ocular albinism), *TYR*, *OCA2* (oculocutaneous albinism), *CNGA3*/*CNGB3* (achromatopsia) |
| **[[Caloric testing]] and [[electronystagmography]] (ENG)** | Assess peripheral vestibular function in suspected vestibular nystagmus |
| **Blood work** | Magnesium, thiamine (B1), B12 levels; drug levels (phenytoin, carbamazepine, lithium); anti-GAD antibodies (in autoimmune cerebellar ataxia) |

### Foveal Hypoplasia Grading (Thomas et al., 2011)

Foveal hypoplasia, commonly associated with [[albinism]] and [[aniridia]], is graded on [[OCT]]:

| Grade | Features |
|---|---|
| **Grade 1** | Shallow foveal pit; all retinal layers present |
| **Grade 2** | Absent foveal pit; outer nuclear layer (ONL) widening present |
| **Grade 3** | Absent foveal pit; inner retinal layers cross the foveal center |
| **Grade 4** | Absent foveal pit; inner retinal layers cross; absent outer segment lengthening |

## Differential Diagnosis

It is critical to distinguish true nystagmus from other ocular oscillations:

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| **[[Saccadic intrusions]] (square wave jerks)** | Brief, involuntary saccades away from fixation followed by a return saccade; no slow phase |
| **[[Opsoclonus]]** | Chaotic, multidirectional saccadic oscillations without intersaccadic intervals; associated with [[neuroblastoma]] in children, paraneoplastic syndromes in adults |
| **[[Ocular flutter]]** | Horizontal back-to-back saccades without intersaccadic interval; similar etiology to opsoclonus |
| **[[Superior oblique myokymia]]** | Monocular, high-frequency, low-amplitude torsional oscillation; episodic |
| **[[Voluntary nystagmus]]** | Rapid, low-amplitude, horizontal pendular oscillation; cannot be sustained >30 seconds; requires convergence effort |
| **[[Spasmus nutans]]** | Triad of nystagmus (often asymmetric/monocular, high-frequency, low-amplitude), head nodding, and torticollis; onset 4–12 months; benign and self-limiting, but must exclude [[optic pathway glioma]] with MRI |

## Management and Treatment

Management of nystagmus is multifaceted and depends on the type, etiology, severity, and impact on the patient's visual function and quality of life.

### Optical Correction

- **Full cycloplegic [[refraction]]**: Accurate correction of [[refractive error]] is the single most important first step. Patients with nystagmus have a high prevalence of significant [[astigmatism]], [[myopia]], and [[hyperopia]].
- **[[Contact lenses]]**: May be superior to spectacles in some patients with INS. The proprioceptive feedback from the lens on the [[cornea]] may dampen nystagmus (the "contact lens effect"), and the optical correction moves with the eye, reducing retinal image slip during oscillation.
- **[[Prisms]]**: Base-out prisms can be used to stimulate convergence (exploiting convergence dampening in INS). Yoked prisms can shift the visual field to allow the patient to use the null zone without an anomalous head posture.

### Low Vision Aids

- **Magnification devices**: Handheld magnifiers, stand magnifiers, [[electronic magnification]] systems (CCTV/video magnifiers).
- **Large print materials** and increased contrast.
- **Bioptic telescopes**: For distance tasks, including driving in jurisdictions that permit bioptic driving.

### Pharmacological Interventions

Pharmacotherapy is primarily directed at acquired nystagmus, particularly when oscillopsia is disabling:

| Drug | Nystagmus Type | Mechanism | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Gabapentin]]** (up to 2400 mg/day) | Acquired pendular nystagmus (e.g., in [[multiple sclerosis]]) | GABAergic modulation; stabilizes neural integrator | Level I evidence (Averbuch-Heller et al., 1997) |
| **[[Memantine]]** (up to 40 mg/day) | Acquired pendular nystagmus | NMDA receptor antagonist | Level I evidence (Starck et al., 1997) |
| **[[Baclofen]]** (15–30 mg/day) | [[Periodic alternating nystagmus]] (PAN) | GABA-B agonist; restores velocity storage inhibition | Drug of choice for acquired PAN (Halmagyi et al., 1980) |
| **[[4-Aminopyridine]]** (5–10 mg BID) | [[Downbeat nystagmus]], [[upbeat nystagmus]], [[episodic ataxia type 2]] | Potassium channel blocker; enhances Purkinje cell excitability in [[flocculus]] | Level I evidence (Strupp et al., 2011); considered first-line for DBN |
| **[[3,4-Diaminopyridine]]** | Downbeat nystagmus | Similar to 4-AP | Supportive evidence |
| **[[Clonazepam]]** | Various acquired forms | GABAergic; general CNS depressant | Limited evidence; sedation limits use |
| **[[Carbamazepine]] / [[Oxcarbazepine]]** | Acquired pendular nystagmus (oculopalatal tremor) | Sodium channel blockade | Case reports |

> **Note**: No pharmacological agent has been proven consistently effective for infantile nystagmus syndrome. Anecdotal reports exist for memantine and gabapentin in INS, but robust evidence is lacking.

### Botulinum Toxin Injection

**[[Botulinum toxin]]** (Botox) injection into the [[retrobulbar space]] or individual [[extraocular muscles]] can reduce nystagmus amplitude and improve visual acuity temporarily. However, the effect is transient (typically 3–6 months), and complications include [[ptosis]], [[diplopia]], and [[strabismus]]. It is generally reserved for patients with severe acquired nystagmus refractory to pharmacotherapy.

### Surgical Approaches

Surgery for nystagmus is primarily indicated for infantile nystagmus syndrome with a significant anomalous head posture or for improving foveation time.

#### Anderson-Kestenbaum Procedure

The classic surgical approach for INS with an eccentric null zone and AHP. The procedure involves **recession and resection of the horizontal [[rectus muscles]]** to shift the null zone toward primary position, thereby eliminating or reducing the head turn. For example, a patient with a left head turn (null zone in right gaze) would undergo recession of the right [[lateral rectus]] and left [[medial rectus]], with resection of the right [[medial rectus]] and left [[lateral rectus]]. Augmented dosing (larger amounts of surgery than standard strabismus surgery) is typically employed.

#### Artificial Divergence Surgery (Dell'Osso Procedure)

Bilateral medial rectus recession combined with bilateral lateral rectus resection creates a mild divergence, stimulating convergence effort to maintain [[binocular vision]]. This exploits the convergence dampening effect seen in INS. Studies have demonstrated improvements in foveation time, visual acuity, and nystagmus intensity.

#### Four-Muscle Tenotomy and Reattachment (Dell'Osso Tenotomy)

All four horizontal rectus muscles are detached and reattached at their original insertions. The proposed mechanism involves disruption of proprioceptive feedback from the extraocular muscles, which may alter the nystagmus waveform and improve foveation. Evidence is mixed, with some studies showing modest improvements in visual acuity and expanded null zone.

#### Combined Procedures

In patients with both strabismus and nystagmus, surgical planning must address both conditions. Augmented recession-resection procedures can simultaneously correct the AHP and the [[strabismus]].

### Emerging and Investigational Therapies

- **Gene therapy**: For nystagmus secondary to specific genetic conditions (e.g., [[RPE65]]-associated [[Leber congenital amaurosis]] treated with [[voretigene neparvovec]] (Luxturna)), correction of the underlying retinal dystrophy may secondarily improve nystagmus.
- **Non-invasive brain stimulation**: Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) and transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) targeting the [[cerebellum]] or [[visual cortex]] are under investigation for modulating nystagmus.
- **Afferent visual training and biofeedback**: Experimental approaches using auditory or visual biofeedback to help patients learn to suppress nystagmus.
- **Optogenetics and retinal prostheses**: In the distant future, restoration of retinal function in conditions causing sensory deprivation nystagmus may reduce nystagmus.

## Special Subtypes and Clinical Considerations

### Latent Nystagmus (Fusion Maldevelopment Nystagmus Syndrome)

**[[Latent nystagmus]]** (LN), more accurately termed **fusion maldevelopment nystagmus syndrome (FMNS)**, is a conjugate jerk nystagmus that appears or intensifies when one eye is occluded. The fast phase beats toward the viewing (non-occluded) eye. It is almost invariably associated with [[infantile esotropia]] and [[dissociated vertical deviation]] (DVD). When present with both eyes open (manifest latent nystagmus), it indicates poor binocular fusion. Clinical significance: during monocular visual acuity testing, the examiner should use a high plus lens (+10 D) or translucent occluder over the non-tested eye rather than full occlusion, to minimize induction of latent nystagmus and obtain a more accurate acuity measurement.

### Spasmus Nutans

A benign, self-limiting condition of infancy (onset 4–12 months, resolution by 3–5 years) characterized by the triad of **asymmetric or monocular, high-frequency, low-amplitude nystagmus**, **head nodding**, and **torticollis**. However, an identical clinical picture can be produced by [[optic pathway glioma]] (particularly [[chiasmal glioma]]), making **MRI of the brain and orbits mandatory** in all cases of suspected spasmus nutans.

### Nystagmus in Albinism

[[Albinism]] is the most common identifiable cause of infantile nystagmus. Key diagnostic features include:
- [[Iris transillumination defects]]
- [[Foveal hypoplasia]] on [[OCT]]
- **Chiasmal misrouting** on [[VEP]] (excessive decussation of temporal retinal fibers at the [[optic chiasm]])
- Hypopigmentation of the [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE)
- Reduced visual acuity

### Drug-Induced Nystagmus

Numerous medications can induce nystagmus, most commonly **gaze-evoked nystagmus**:
- [[Anticonvulsants]]: [[Phenytoin]], [[carbamazepine]], [[lamotrigine]], [[topiramate]]
- [[Sedatives]]/[[hypnotics]]: [[Benzodiazepines]], [[barbiturates]]
- [[Lithium]]
- [[Alcohol]]
- [[Aminoglycosides]] (vestibulotoxicity)
- [[Ketamine]] and other dissociative agents

Drug-induced nystagmus is typically dose-dependent and reversible upon discontinuation or dose reduction.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of nystagmus depends heavily on its etiology:

- **Infantile nystagmus syndrome**: Generally stable or may improve slightly with age. Visual acuity typically ranges from 20/30 to 20/200 depending on the underlying condition and foveation characteristics. Patients develop robust adaptation and rarely experience oscillopsia.
- **Acquired nystagmus**: Prognosis depends on the underlying cause. Drug-induced nystagmus resolves with medication adjustment. Nystagmus from [[multiple sclerosis]] may fluctuate with disease activity. Nystagmus from structural lesions (e.g., [[Chiari malformation]]) may improve with surgical decompression. Nystagmus from irreversible neurodegeneration carries a poor prognosis.
- **Spasmus nutans**: Excellent prognosis; spontaneous resolution by age 3–5 years (after exclusion of optic pathway glioma).

## Summary of Key Clinical Pearls

| Clinical Pearl | Detail |
|---|---|
| Horizontal nystagmus in all gaze positions (including upgaze) | Strongly suggests infantile nystagmus syndrome |
| Oscillopsia absent | Suggests congenital/infantile onset |
| Oscillopsia present | Suggests acquired nystagmus — warrants neuroimaging |
| Nystagmus suppressed by fixation | Suggests peripheral vestibular etiology |
| Nystagmus NOT suppressed by fixation | Suggests central etiology |
| Downbeat nystagmus | MRI craniocervical junction — rule out [[Chiari malformation]] |
| Periodic alternating nystagmus | Trial of [[baclofen]] |
| Acquired pendular nystagmus | Trial of [[gabapentin]] or [[memantine]] |
| Asymmetric/monocular nystagmus in infant | MRI brain — rule out [[optic pathway glioma]] |
| Iris transillumination + foveal hypoplasia + nystagmus | [[Albinism]] — confirm with [[VEP]] for chiasmal misrouting |
