

# Keratoconus

**Keratoconus** (from the Greek *kerato-* meaning "horn" or "cornea" and *konos* meaning "cone") is a progressive, bilateral, asymmetric, non-inflammatory [[ectatic corneal disorder]] characterized by progressive thinning and steepening of the [[cornea]], resulting in an irregular, cone-shaped protrusion that induces significant [[irregular astigmatism]], [[myopia]], and visual impairment. It is one of the most common primary [[corneal ectasia|corneal ectasias]] encountered in clinical optometric and ophthalmologic practice and represents a leading indication for [[corneal transplantation]] in the developed world.

Keratoconus typically manifests during puberty or the second decade of life, progresses variably through the third and fourth decades, and often stabilizes by the fifth decade, although exceptions to this natural history are well documented. The condition affects all ethnicities and both sexes, though recent epidemiological data suggest a higher prevalence in individuals of South Asian, Middle Eastern, and Polynesian descent. Reported prevalence varies widely—from approximately 1 in 2,000 in older population-based studies to as high as 1 in 375 in more recent investigations employing modern [[corneal topography]]—reflecting improved diagnostic sensitivity and evolving diagnostic criteria.

## Epidemiology

The epidemiology of keratoconus has been substantially revised over the past two decades owing to advances in [[corneal imaging]] technology. Classical estimates placed the prevalence at approximately 54.5 per 100,000 (roughly 1:2,000) based on the landmark Nottingham study by Kennedy, Bourne, and Dyer (1986). However, contemporary studies utilizing [[Scheimpflug imaging]], [[optical coherence tomography]] (OCT), and automated topographic screening have revealed significantly higher prevalence rates.

| **Study / Region** | **Year** | **Prevalence (per 100,000)** | **Notes** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Kennedy et al. (Nottingham, USA) | 1986 | 54.5 | Retrospective, clinical diagnosis |
| Georgiou et al. (UK) | 2004 | 229 | Asian population in UK |
| Torres Netto et al. (Saudi Arabia) | 2015 | ~3,300 | Pediatric screening cohort |
| Godefrooij et al. (Netherlands) | 2017 | 265 | National registry data |
| Chan et al. (Australia) | 2021 | ~150 | Population-based |
| Hashemi et al. (Iran) | 2020 | ~2,500 | Topographic screening |

The incidence is estimated at approximately 1.5–25 per 100,000 per year depending on the population studied. Males have historically been reported to be affected more frequently than females (approximately 60:40), though some recent studies suggest a more equal distribution. Onset typically occurs between ages 10 and 25, with earlier onset generally portending a more aggressive clinical course.

### Risk Factors

| **Risk Factor** | **Mechanism / Association** |
|---|---|
| [[Eye rubbing]] | Mechanical trauma; strongest modifiable risk factor; associated with epithelial microtrauma and release of inflammatory mediators |
| [[Atopy]] / [[Allergic conjunctivitis]] | Chronic ocular surface inflammation; promotes eye rubbing |
| Family history | Genetic predisposition; first-degree relatives have 15–67× increased risk |
| [[Down syndrome]] | Prevalence of keratoconus 5–15%; multifactorial including eye rubbing and connective tissue abnormality |
| [[Connective tissue disorders]] ([[Ehlers-Danlos syndrome]], [[Marfan syndrome]], [[Osteogenesis imperfecta]]) | Abnormal collagen structure and cross-linking |
| [[Obstructive sleep apnea]] / Floppy eyelid syndrome | Nocturnal eye rubbing, mechanical compression |
| Contact lens wear (rigid) | Controversial; may be detection bias |
| Ultraviolet light exposure | Oxidative stress contribution |
| Hormonal factors | Progression during [[pregnancy]], puberty; estrogen receptor expression in corneal stroma |

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of keratoconus is multifactorial and incompletely understood, involving a complex interplay of genetic susceptibility, biomechanical failure, biochemical alterations, and environmental triggers. Despite its traditional classification as a "non-inflammatory" disorder, accumulating evidence supports a role for subclinical, localized inflammatory and oxidative stress processes.

### Genetic Basis

Keratoconus demonstrates a complex, non-Mendelian inheritance pattern with variable penetrance. Approximately 6–23.5% of patients report a positive family history. Twin studies demonstrate high concordance in monozygotic twins. Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) and linkage analyses have identified numerous candidate loci and genes, including:

- **[[VSX1]]** (Visual System Homeobox 1) – 20p11.21
- **[[SOD1]]** (Superoxide Dismutase 1) – 21q22.11; involved in oxidative stress defense
- **[[LOX]]** (Lysyl Oxidase) – 5q23.1; critical for [[collagen]] cross-linking in vivo
- **[[COL5A1]]** – 9q34.3; encodes type V collagen
- **[[ZNF469]]** – 16q24; associated with central corneal thickness
- **[[FOXO1]]** – 13q14.11
- **[[HGF]]** (Hepatocyte Growth Factor) – 7q21.11
- **[[MPDZ]]** / **[[NFIB]]** – identified in large GWAS meta-analyses
- **[[MIR184]]** – microRNA implicated in familial keratoconus with [[anterior polar cataract]]

No single gene accounts for the majority of cases, supporting a polygenic model with environmental modulation (the "two-hit" hypothesis).

### Biochemical and Structural Alterations

The hallmark of keratoconus is progressive thinning and weakening of the [[corneal stroma]], which constitutes approximately 90% of corneal thickness and is composed primarily of [[type I collagen]] fibrils arranged in highly organized [[lamellae]].

Key biochemical findings include:

1. **Reduced collagen cross-linking**: Decreased activity of [[lysyl oxidase]] (LOX) results in fewer inter- and intra-molecular covalent cross-links between collagen fibrils, reducing biomechanical strength.

2. **Altered collagen fibril organization**: Normal corneal stroma exhibits orthogonal lamellar arrangement. In keratoconus, lamellae demonstrate slippage, folding, and loss of the normal interwoven architecture, particularly in the anterior stroma. Lamellar interweaving is reduced, and collagen fibril diameter distribution is altered.

3. **Decreased total collagen content**: The keratoconic cornea contains fewer collagen lamellae in the region of the cone compared to normal corneas.

4. **Proteolytic enzyme imbalance**: Elevated levels of [[matrix metalloproteinases]] (MMPs), particularly [[MMP-2]], [[MMP-9]], and [[MMP-13]], and decreased levels of tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases ([[TIMPs]]) have been documented in keratoconic corneas and tear fluid.

5. **Oxidative stress**: Keratoconic corneas demonstrate reduced levels of [[aldehyde dehydrogenase]] (ALDH3A1), [[superoxide dismutase]] (SOD), and other antioxidant enzymes, with increased levels of reactive oxygen species (ROS), [[malondialdehyde]], and other oxidative damage markers. This oxidative imbalance leads to [[keratocyte]] [[apoptosis]].

6. **Keratocyte apoptosis**: Increased apoptosis of [[corneal keratocytes]] (stromal fibroblasts) is a consistent finding, contributing to stromal thinning. This is mediated in part by oxidative stress, mechanical trauma (eye rubbing), and inflammatory cytokines such as [[interleukin-6]] (IL-6) and [[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] (TNF-α).

7. **Inflammatory mediators**: Despite the classical "non-inflammatory" designation, tear fluid analysis in keratoconus patients reveals elevated levels of IL-6, TNF-α, [[MMP-9]], and [[cathepsin B]], suggesting a localized, subclinical inflammatory process. The term "chronic subclinical inflammation" has been proposed to describe this state.

### Biomechanical Failure

The cumulative effect of these biochemical alterations is a progressive reduction in corneal [[biomechanical]] rigidity. The [[corneal hysteresis]] (CH) and [[corneal resistance factor]] (CRF), as measured by the [[Ocular Response Analyzer]] (ORA), are significantly reduced in keratoconic eyes. Similarly, the [[Corvis ST]] tonometer demonstrates reduced stiffness parameter at first applanation (SP-A1) and altered deformation amplitude. This biomechanical weakening allows the normal [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) to cause progressive ectasia.

### Bowman Layer Disruption

[[Bowman's layer]] (the anterior limiting lamina), an acellular condensation of the anterior stroma approximately 8–12 μm thick, demonstrates characteristic breaks and fragmentation in keratoconus. These breaks are filled with stromal scarring tissue and are visible histopathologically and on [[anterior segment OCT]]. Bowman layer disruption is considered an early structural change and may serve as a nidus for progressive stromal thinning.

### Epithelial Changes

The [[corneal epithelium]] in keratoconus demonstrates irregular thickness distribution, with thinning over the apex of the cone and compensatory thickening in surrounding areas. This "epithelial doughnut pattern" is detectable on [[epithelial thickness mapping]] using [[spectral-domain OCT]] and represents an important early diagnostic sign, as the epithelium partially masks underlying stromal thinning on conventional pachymetry.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Symptoms

Patients with keratoconus typically present with:

- **Progressive blurring of vision** – often initially correctable with [[spectacles]] but becoming increasingly resistant to spectacle correction
- **Frequent changes in spectacle prescription** – particularly increasing [[myopia]] and [[astigmatism]], often with an oblique or irregular axis
- **[[Monocular diplopia]]** or **ghost images** – due to irregular astigmatism and higher-order [[optical aberrations]], particularly [[coma]]
- **[[Photophobia]]** and **glare sensitivity** – especially at night; halos around lights
- **Asthenopia** (eye strain)
- **Difficulty with night driving**
- **Reduced contrast sensitivity**

Symptoms are typically bilateral but asymmetric, with one eye often significantly more affected than the other. In early stages, patients may be asymptomatic or have only mild refractive symptoms.

### Clinical Signs

| **Sign** | **Description** | **Stage** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Scissoring reflex]] on [[retinoscopy]] | Irregular, "scissors-like" movement of the retinoscopic reflex | Early |
| [[Irregular astigmatism]] on [[keratometry]] / topography | Steep, asymmetric corneal curvature | Early–Moderate |
| [[Rizzuti's sign]] | Conical reflection of light on the nasal cornea when light is shone from the temporal side | Moderate–Advanced |
| [[Munson's sign]] | V-shaped indentation of the lower eyelid on downgaze caused by the corneal cone | Advanced |
| [[Vogt's striae]] | Fine, vertical or oblique stress lines in the deep stroma and [[Descemet's membrane]], which disappear with gentle digital pressure | Moderate |
| [[Fleischer ring]] | Partial or complete ring of [[hemosiderin]] (iron) deposition in the [[corneal epithelium]] at the base of the cone, best visualized with [[cobalt blue filter]] | Moderate |
| Corneal thinning | Focal thinning at the apex of the cone, typically inferotemporal | All stages |
| [[Corneal scarring]] | Superficial or deep stromal scarring at the cone apex | Moderate–Advanced |
| Prominent [[corneal nerves]] | Increased visibility of corneal nerve fibers | Early–Moderate |
| [[Acute corneal hydrops]] | Sudden rupture of [[Descemet's membrane]] causing aqueous humor influx into the stroma, resulting in acute corneal edema, pain, and severe vision loss | Advanced (acute event) |

## Classification and Staging

Several classification systems have been proposed for keratoconus staging. The most widely used include:

### Amsler-Krumeich Classification

| **Stage** | **Findings** |
|---|---|
| **Stage I** | Eccentric corneal steepening; induced myopia and/or astigmatism ≤5.00 D; mean central K readings ≤48.00 D; Vogt's striae, no scarring |
| **Stage II** | Induced myopia and/or astigmatism >5.00 D and ≤8.00 D; mean central K readings ≤53.00 D; minimum corneal thickness ≥400 μm; no central scarring |
| **Stage III** | Induced myopia and/or astigmatism >8.00 D and ≤10.00 D; mean central K readings >53.00 D; minimum corneal thickness 200–400 μm; no central scarring |
| **Stage IV** | Refraction not measurable; mean central K readings >55.00 D; central scarring; minimum corneal thickness ≤200 μm |

### ABCD Grading System (Belin ABCD)

The Belin ABCD system, integrated into the [[Pentacam]] (Oculus), provides a more comprehensive staging based on:

- **A** – Anterior radius of curvature (3 mm zone, best-fit toric ellipsoid)
- **B** – Back (posterior) radius of curvature (3 mm zone, best-fit toric ellipsoid)
- **C** – Thinnest corneal pachymetry
- **D** – Best-corrected distance visual acuity (CDVA)

Each parameter is graded from 0 (normal) to 4 (severe), with a "–" modifier for the presence of scarring. This system is considered superior to the Amsler-Krumeich classification as it incorporates posterior corneal surface data and is more sensitive to early disease.

## Diagnosis

Early and accurate diagnosis of keratoconus is critical, particularly in the era of [[corneal collagen cross-linking]] (CXL), which can halt progression if performed early. Diagnosis relies on a combination of clinical examination, corneal topography, tomography, biomechanical assessment, and increasingly, artificial intelligence (AI)-assisted analysis.

### Corneal Topography

[[Placido disc]]-based [[corneal topography]] remains a fundamental screening tool. Characteristic topographic patterns include:

- **Inferior steepening** (asymmetric bow-tie pattern with skewed radial axes, or "IS" pattern)
- **Elevated [[KISA%]] index** (a composite index incorporating central K, I-S value, skewed radial axis, and regular/irregular astigmatism)
- **Abnormal [[Klyce/Maeda]] keratoconus indices** (KCI, KPI)
- **Inferior-Superior (I-S) asymmetry value >1.4 D** (Rabinowitz criteria)
- **Central K >47.2 D** (Rabinowitz criteria)

### Corneal Tomography

[[Scheimpflug imaging]] (e.g., [[Pentacam]], [[Galilei]]) and [[slit-scanning topography]] (e.g., [[Orbscan]]) provide critical additional data:

- **Anterior and posterior elevation maps**: Posterior corneal elevation is often the earliest detectable change in keratoconus, preceding anterior surface changes. Elevation is measured relative to a best-fit sphere (BFS) or best-fit toric ellipsoid (BFTE).
- **Pachymetric maps**: Demonstrate focal thinning and abnormal thickness distribution. The **thinnest corneal point** is displaced inferiorly and temporally.
- **Pachymetric progression indices**: The rate of change of corneal thickness from the thinnest point to the periphery is abnormally rapid in keratoconus (Ambrósio Relational Thickness indices).
- **Belin/Ambrósio Enhanced Ectasia Display (BAD-D)**: A composite index incorporating anterior elevation, posterior elevation, pachymetric, and thickness progression data. A **BAD-D value >1.6** is considered suspicious; **>2.6** is highly suggestive of keratoconus.

### Epithelial Thickness Mapping

[[Anterior segment OCT]] (AS-OCT) devices (e.g., [[RTVue]], [[MS-39]], [[CASIA2]]) can generate high-resolution [[epithelial thickness maps]]. The characteristic "doughnut pattern"—epithelial thinning over the cone apex surrounded by a ring of compensatory thickening—is a highly sensitive early marker. A minimum epithelial thickness <45 μm or a maximum-minimum difference >12 μm is suggestive.

### Corneal Biomechanical Assessment

- **[[Ocular Response Analyzer]] (ORA)**: Measures [[corneal hysteresis]] (CH) and [[corneal resistance factor]] (CRF). Both are reduced in keratoconus.
- **[[Corvis ST]]**: A non-contact tonometer with ultra-high-speed Scheimpflug camera that captures corneal deformation in response to an air puff. Parameters such as deformation amplitude (DA), stiffness parameter at first applanation (SP-A1), and the **Tomographic Biomechanical Index (TBI)** (when combined with Pentacam data) provide excellent diagnostic accuracy. **TBI >0.29** is considered abnormal.

### Wavefront Aberrometry

[[Wavefront analysis]] demonstrates elevated [[higher-order aberrations]] (HOAs), particularly:
- **Vertical [[coma]]** (Zernike coefficient Z3^-1) – the dominant aberration in keratoconus
- **[[Trefoil]]**
- **[[Spherical aberration]]**

Total HOA root mean square (RMS) is significantly elevated compared to normal eyes.

### Subclinical / Forme Fruste Keratoconus

**[[Forme fruste keratoconus]]** (FFKC) refers to the earliest, subclinical stage of the disease, where topographic and tomographic abnormalities are present but clinical signs and symptoms are absent or minimal. Detection of FFKC is critically important in the context of [[refractive surgery]] screening, as performing [[LASIK]] or [[PRK]] on an eye with undetected FFKC can precipitate iatrogenic [[post-LASIK ectasia]]. AI-based screening algorithms (e.g., Pentacam Random Forest Index, GALILEI Ectasia Risk Score) are increasingly employed for this purpose.

## Differential Diagnosis

Keratoconus must be differentiated from other causes of corneal ectasia and irregular astigmatism:

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| [[Pellucid marginal degeneration]] | Inferior band of thinning (4–8 mm from center), "crab-claw" or "butterfly" topographic pattern, ectasia above the zone of maximal thinning |
| [[Keratoglobus]] | Globular corneal protrusion with diffuse limbus-to-limbus thinning |
| [[Post-LASIK ectasia]] | History of refractive surgery; central or paracentral ectasia |
| [[Terrien marginal degeneration]] | Peripheral, superior corneal thinning with lipid deposition; typically non-progressive |
| [[Contact lens-induced warpage]] | Reversible topographic irregularity; resolves after lens discontinuation |

## Management and Treatment

Management of keratoconus is stage-dependent and aims to: (1) optimize visual acuity, (2) halt disease progression, and (3) rehabilitate the corneal surface when necessary.

### Spectacle Correction

In early keratoconus (Stage I), [[spectacles]] may provide adequate visual correction. However, as the disease progresses and irregular astigmatism increases, spectacle correction becomes insufficient due to the inability of spherocylindrical lenses to correct higher-order aberrations.

### Contact Lens Fitting

[[Contact lenses]] are the mainstay of visual rehabilitation in moderate keratoconus. The rigid lens surface creates a new, regular refracting surface, with the tear film filling the gap between the irregular cornea and the lens (the "tear lens" effect).

| **Lens Type** | **Indications / Features** |
|---|---|
| **[[Rigid gas permeable]] (RGP) lenses** | First-line for moderate keratoconus; excellent optics; various designs (multicurve, aspheric, keratoconus-specific geometries such as [[Rose K]], [[Rose K2]], [[Soper]], [[McGuire]]) |
| **[[Scleral lenses]]** | Large-diameter RGP lenses (≥15.0 mm) that vault the entire cornea and rest on the [[sclera]]; excellent for advanced keratoconus, irregular corneas, and patients intolerant of corneal RGPs; provide superior comfort and centration |
| **[[Mini-scleral lenses]]** | Diameter 15.0–18.0 mm; bridge between corneal RGPs and full scleral lenses |
| **[[Hybrid lenses]]** (e.g., [[SynergEyes]], [[UltraHealth]]) | RGP center with soft skirt; combine optics of RGP with comfort of soft lens |
| **[[Piggyback system]]** | Soft lens worn beneath an RGP lens to improve comfort and centration |
| **Custom [[soft toric lenses]]** | For early keratoconus with mild irregular astigmatism; limited by inability to correct HOAs |
| **[[Kerasoft IC]]** | Custom soft lens designed specifically for irregular corneas |

### Corneal Collagen Cross-Linking (CXL)

**[[Corneal collagen cross-linking]]** (CXL) is the only treatment proven to halt the progression of keratoconus and is now considered the standard of care for progressive disease. The procedure strengthens the corneal stroma by creating additional covalent bonds between collagen fibrils through a photochemical reaction involving [[riboflavin]] (vitamin B2) and [[ultraviolet-A]] (UVA) light (370 nm).

#### Standard (Dresden) Protocol
1. [[Epithelial debridement]] (central 8–9 mm)
2. Riboflavin 0.1% in 20% [[dextran]] solution applied every 2 minutes for 30 minutes
3. UVA irradiation at 3 mW/cm² for 30 minutes (total energy dose: 5.4 J/cm²)
4. Bandage contact lens placement

#### Accelerated CXL Protocols
Higher irradiance (9, 18, or 30 mW/cm²) for shorter durations (10, 5, or 3 minutes, respectively) to maintain the same total energy dose (5.4 J/cm²), based on the [[Bunsen-Roscoe law]] of reciprocity. Clinical evidence suggests that moderate acceleration (9 mW/cm² × 10 min) achieves comparable outcomes to the standard protocol, while ultra-accelerated protocols (30 mW/cm² × 3 min) may be less effective due to oxygen depletion.

#### Transepithelial (Epi-On) CXL
The epithelium is left intact, and riboflavin penetration is enhanced using chemical enhancers (e.g., [[EDTA]], [[benzalkonium chloride]], [[NanoVitamin]] formulations) or iontophoresis. Advantages include reduced pain, faster recovery, and lower infection risk. However, traditional epi-on protocols have demonstrated inferior biomechanical strengthening compared to epi-off CXL. Newer protocols (e.g., supplemental oxygen delivery, pulsed light protocols) aim to improve epi-on efficacy.

#### Indications for CXL
- Documented progression (increase in Kmax ≥1.0 D over 12 months, or increase in manifest cylinder, or decrease in thinnest pachymetry)
- Age <35 years (higher risk of progression)
- Minimum corneal thickness ≥400 μm at the thinnest point (for epi-off; lower thresholds with customized protocols)

#### CXL Outcomes
- Stabilization of Kmax in >90% of treated eyes
- Mean flattening of Kmax by 1.0–2.0 D at 1 year
- Improvement in CDVA by 1–2 Snellen lines in many patients
- Demarcation line visible at approximately 250–350 μm depth on AS-OCT, indicating treatment depth

### Intracorneal Ring Segments (ICRS)

**[[Intracorneal ring segments]]** (e.g., [[Intacs]], [[Ferrara rings]], [[Kerarings]]) are small, arc-shaped [[PMMA]] implants inserted into the corneal stroma (typically at 70–80% depth) to flatten the central cornea, reduce irregular astigmatism, and improve contact lens tolerance. They do not halt progression and are often combined with CXL ("CXL-Plus" protocols).

| **Parameter** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| Mechanism | Arc-shortening effect; redistributes corneal tissue away from the center |
| Insertion technique | Mechanical dissection or [[femtosecond laser]]-assisted channel creation |
| Typical outcomes | Reduction in Kmax by 2–5 D; improvement in UDVA and CDVA |
| Complications | Segment migration, extrusion, corneal melting, infectious keratitis, halos/glare |

### Topography-Guided Photorefractive Keratectomy (TG-PRK)

**[[Topography-guided PRK]]** combined with CXL (the "Athens Protocol" or "Cretan Protocol") involves limited surface ablation to regularize the corneal surface followed by CXL. This approach can improve both uncorrected and corrected visual acuity while simultaneously stabilizing the cornea. It is generally reserved for patients with adequate corneal thickness and mild-to-moderate keratoconus.

### Corneal Transplantation

Approximately 10–20% of keratoconus patients eventually require [[corneal transplantation]] due to contact lens intolerance, severe corneal scarring, or extreme thinning.

#### Penetrating Keratoplasty (PK)
**[[Penetrating keratoplasty]]** involves full-thickness replacement of the central cornea with donor tissue. It has been the traditional surgical approach for advanced keratoconus, with excellent graft survival rates (>90% at 10 years). However, it carries risks of [[graft rejection]], high postoperative astigmatism, suture-related complications, and prolonged visual rehabilitation.

#### Deep Anterior Lamellar Keratoplasty (DALK)
**[[Deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]]** (DALK) replaces the anterior corneal stroma while preserving the host's [[Descemet's membrane]] and [[endothelium]]. Techniques include the **big-bubble technique** (Anwar) and manual dissection. Advantages over PK include:
- Elimination of endothelial rejection
- Reduced long-term graft failure risk
- Greater structural integrity (closed-globe procedure)
- Comparable visual outcomes in experienced hands

DALK is now considered the preferred surgical technique for keratoconus when the endothelium is healthy and Descemet's membrane is intact (i.e., no prior hydrops with Descemet's scarring).

#### Bowman Layer Transplantation
**[[Bowman layer transplantation]]** is a newer, minimally invasive technique in which an isolated donor [[Bowman's layer]] graft is inserted into a mid-stromal pocket. It has been shown to flatten the cornea by 5–7 D and improve contact lens tolerance in advanced keratoconus, potentially delaying or avoiding the need for PK/DALK.

### Acute Corneal Hydrops Management

**[[Acute corneal hydrops]]** results from a spontaneous rupture of [[Descemet's membrane]], allowing [[aqueous humor]] to enter the stroma, causing acute edema, pain, and severe vision loss. Management includes:

- **Conservative**: Hypertonic saline drops (e.g., [[sodium chloride 5%]]), [[cycloplegics]], and observation. Resolution typically occurs over 6–12 weeks as Descemet's membrane heals.
- **Intracameral air or [[SF6 gas]] injection**: Accelerates resolution of hydrops by tamponading the Descemet's tear. Increasingly used as first-line intervention.
- **Compressive sutures**: Rarely used.

Paradoxically, the scarring that results from hydrops may flatten the cone and occasionally improve contact lens fitting, though visual acuity is often compromised.

## Pharmacological Considerations

While no pharmacological agent currently reverses keratoconus, several areas of active research and adjunctive therapy exist:

- **[[Cyclosporine A]] ophthalmic emulsion**: Used to manage concurrent [[allergic eye disease]] and reduce ocular surface inflammation that may contribute to progression.
- **[[Antihistamines]] and [[mast cell stabilizers]]**: For management of [[allergic conjunctivitis]] and reduction of eye rubbing.
- **Topical [[NSAIDs]]**: Occasionally used post-CXL for pain management.
- **Riboflavin eye drops**: Essential component of CXL; various formulations (iso-osmolar, hypo-osmolar, with/without dextran, with HPMC) are used depending on the protocol.
- **Investigational agents**: Topical [[losartan]] (TGF-β pathway modulation), [[decorin]], and other anti-fibrotic agents are under investigation for their potential to modulate corneal wound healing and biomechanics.

## Monitoring and Follow-Up

Patients with keratoconus require regular monitoring to detect progression and guide treatment decisions:

| **Parameter** | **Modality** | **Frequency** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Visual acuity]] (UDVA, CDVA) | Snellen / logMAR chart | Every 6–12 months |
| [[Refraction]] (manifest and cycloplegic) | [[Phoropter]] / trial frame | Every 6–12 months |
| Corneal topography / tomography | [[Pentacam]], [[Orbscan]], [[Galilei]], [[MS-39]] | Every 6–12 months (more frequently if progression suspected) |
| Pachymetry (thinnest point) | Scheimpflug, AS-OCT, [[ultrasound pachymetry]] | Every 6–12 months |
| Epithelial thickness mapping | AS-OCT | As indicated |
| Biomechanical assessment | [[Corvis ST]], [[ORA]] | As indicated |
| Contact lens fit assessment | [[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]], [[fluorescein pattern]] evaluation | Every 6–12 months |

**Progression criteria** (any one of the following over 12 months):
- Increase in maximum keratometry (Kmax) ≥1.0 D
- Increase in manifest refractive cylinder ≥1.0 D
- Increase in manifest spherical equivalent ≥0.50 D
- Decrease in thinnest pachymetry ≥10 μm

## Prognosis

The prognosis for keratoconus has improved dramatically with the advent of CXL and modern contact lens technology. Key prognostic considerations include:

- **Age of onset**: Earlier onset (childhood/early adolescence) is associated with more aggressive progression and worse visual outcomes.
- **Ethnicity**: South Asian and Middle Eastern patients may experience more rapid progression.
- **Eye rubbing**: Cessation of eye rubbing is associated with stabilization in some cases even without CXL.
- **CXL timing**: Early CXL in progressive disease significantly improves long-term visual and structural outcomes.
- **Contact lens rehabilitation**: The vast majority of patients (>80%) can achieve functional vision with appropriate contact lens fitting.
- **Corneal transplantation outcomes**: Keratoconus has among the best graft survival rates of any indication for keratoplasty, with >90% clear graft survival at 10 years for both PK and DALK.

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Keratoconus
Keratoconus in children and adolescents tends to be more aggressive, with faster progression rates. CXL is increasingly performed in pediatric populations (as young as age 9–10 in some centers), with evidence supporting its safety and efficacy. Scleral lenses are well-tolerated in this age group. Screening of siblings is recommended.

### Keratoconus and Pregnancy
Hormonal changes during [[pregnancy]] may accelerate keratoconus progression. Estrogen and progesterone receptors are expressed in the corneal stroma, and pregnancy-associated changes in corneal biomechanics have been documented. CXL is contraindicated during pregnancy. Close monitoring is advised during and after pregnancy.

### Keratoconus and Down Syndrome
Patients with [[Down syndrome]] have a significantly elevated prevalence of keratoconus (5–15%), often with aggressive disease. Eye rubbing is particularly common in this population. Diagnosis may be delayed due to communication challenges. Scleral lenses and CXL are effective but may require modified approaches.

---

## Summary

Keratoconus is a common, progressive corneal ectasia with a complex, multifactorial etiology involving genetic predisposition, biomechanical failure, oxidative stress, and environmental triggers—most notably eye rubbing. Advances in [[corneal imaging]], including Scheimpflug tomography, epithelial thickness mapping, and biomechanical assessment, have revolutionized early detection. [[Corneal collagen cross-linking]] has transformed the management paradigm by providing the first treatment capable of halting progression. Visual rehabilitation through modern [[contact lens]] designs—particularly [[scleral lenses]]—enables the vast majority of patients to maintain functional vision. For advanced disease, [[deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty]] has emerged as the preferred surgical approach, offering excellent outcomes with reduced rejection risk. Ongoing research into the genetic, molecular, and biomechanical underpinnings of keratoconus continues to refine diagnostic and therapeutic strategies.
