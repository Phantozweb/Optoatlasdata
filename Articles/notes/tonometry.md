

# Tonometry

---

**Tonometry** is the clinical measurement of [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP), a fundamental diagnostic procedure in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]]. It is universally regarded as one of the most critical assessments in comprehensive eye examinations, serving as a primary screening and monitoring tool for [[glaucoma]] and other conditions associated with abnormal IOP. The technique encompasses a broad family of instruments and methodologies—ranging from contact applanation devices to non-contact air-puff systems—each with distinct physical principles, clinical indications, advantages, and limitations. Accurate tonometry is essential for the detection, staging, and longitudinal management of [[glaucomatous optic neuropathy]], the leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide.

---

## Historical Background

The concept of measuring the pressure within the eye dates to the mid-19th century. [[Albrecht von Graefe]], a pioneer of modern ophthalmology, was among the first to recognize the clinical significance of elevated IOP. In 1905, [[Hjalmar Schiøtz]] introduced the first practical indentation tonometer, which remained the clinical standard for over half a century. The landmark development of the [[Goldmann applanation tonometer]] (GAT) by [[Hans Goldmann]] and T. Schmidt in 1957 revolutionized the field by applying the [[Imbert-Fick principle]] to a small, defined area of the [[cornea]]. The GAT rapidly became—and remains—the international gold standard for IOP measurement. Subsequent decades saw the introduction of non-contact tonometry (NCT), dynamic contour tonometry (DCT), rebound tonometry, and ocular response analyzers (ORA), each designed to address specific limitations of earlier devices or to expand the clinical settings in which IOP can be reliably measured.

## Pathophysiology of Intraocular Pressure

### Aqueous Humor Dynamics

[[Intraocular pressure]] is determined by the dynamic equilibrium between the production and drainage of [[aqueous humor]]. Aqueous humor is actively secreted by the [[ciliary body]] epithelium (specifically the non-pigmented [[ciliary epithelium]]) at a rate of approximately 2.0–2.5 µL/min in the healthy adult eye. It flows from the [[posterior chamber]], through the [[pupil]], into the [[anterior chamber]], and exits primarily via two outflow pathways:

| Outflow Pathway | Approximate Contribution | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Trabecular meshwork]] (conventional)** | ~80–90% | Pressure-dependent flow through the [[trabecular meshwork]], [[Schlemm's canal]], collector channels, and [[episcleral veins]] |
| **[[Uveoscleral outflow]] (unconventional)** | ~10–20% | Pressure-independent flow through the [[ciliary muscle]] interstitial spaces, [[suprachoroidal space]], and [[sclera]] |

The steady-state IOP is governed by the **modified Goldmann equation**:

> **IOP = (F / C) + P_v**

Where:
- **F** = rate of aqueous humor formation (µL/min)
- **C** = facility of outflow (µL/min/mmHg)
- **P_v** = [[episcleral venous pressure]] (typically 8–10 mmHg)

Any disruption in this equilibrium—whether from increased aqueous production, decreased outflow facility, or elevated episcleral venous pressure—results in altered IOP. Tonometry quantifies this resultant pressure.

### Normal IOP Range and Diurnal Variation

The statistically normal IOP in the adult population is approximately **10–21 mmHg**, with a mean of approximately 15.5 mmHg and a standard deviation of about 2.6 mmHg. However, it is critical to recognize that IOP follows a non-Gaussian distribution, skewed slightly toward higher values. IOP exhibits significant [[diurnal variation]], typically peaking in the early morning hours (often between 6:00 AM and 8:00 AM) and reaching its nadir in the afternoon or evening. The magnitude of diurnal fluctuation in healthy individuals is generally 2–6 mmHg, but may exceed 10 mmHg in patients with [[glaucoma]]. This physiological variation has profound implications for tonometric assessment, as a single office-based measurement may not capture the true peak IOP.

### Factors Influencing IOP

Numerous physiological and pathological factors influence IOP and, consequently, tonometric readings:

- **[[Central corneal thickness]] (CCT):** Thicker corneas tend to overestimate IOP, while thinner corneas tend to underestimate IOP, particularly with applanation techniques.
- **[[Corneal biomechanics]]:** Corneal hysteresis (CH) and corneal resistance factor (CRF) independently affect the measurement.
- **[[Corneal curvature]]:** Steep or flat corneas may introduce measurement error in applanation tonometry.
- **[[Corneal edema]]:** Edematous corneas are more compliant, potentially leading to falsely low readings.
- **Post-refractive surgery status:** Procedures such as [[LASIK]], [[PRK]], and [[SMILE]] alter corneal thickness and biomechanics, often resulting in artifactually low IOP readings.
- **[[Scleral rigidity]]:** Particularly relevant in indentation tonometry.
- **Heart rate and [[Valsalva maneuver]]:** Transient IOP elevations occur with straining.
- **Body position:** IOP is higher in the supine position compared to sitting.
- **Medications:** Topical and systemic [[corticosteroids]] can elevate IOP ([[steroid-induced glaucoma]]).

## Clinical Significance and Indications

Tonometry is indicated in virtually every comprehensive eye examination. Its primary clinical applications include:

1. **Screening for [[ocular hypertension]] and [[glaucoma]]**
2. **Monitoring IOP in established glaucoma patients** to assess treatment efficacy
3. **Pre- and post-operative assessment** in [[cataract surgery]], [[refractive surgery]], and [[glaucoma surgery]]
4. **Evaluation of [[uveitis]]** (which may cause either elevated or reduced IOP)
5. **Assessment following [[ocular trauma]]** (with appropriate technique selection)
6. **Monitoring patients on chronic [[corticosteroid]] therapy** (topical, periocular, intravitreal, or systemic)
7. **Detection of [[angle-closure glaucoma]]** during acute episodes
8. **Evaluation of [[hypotony]]** (abnormally low IOP, typically <6 mmHg)

## Diagnostic Modalities: Types of Tonometry

### 1. Goldmann Applanation Tonometry (GAT)

#### Principle

The [[Goldmann applanation tonometer]] operates on the **[[Imbert-Fick principle]]**, which states that the pressure inside a sphere is equal to the force required to flatten (applanate) its surface divided by the area of flattening:

> **P = F / A**

Goldmann and Schmidt refined this principle for the eye by selecting an applanation diameter of **3.06 mm**, at which the opposing forces of [[corneal rigidity]] (which would overestimate pressure) and the [[surface tension]] of the [[tear film]] (which would underestimate pressure) effectively cancel each other out in a cornea of average thickness (~520 µm) and curvature.

#### Technique

1. The patient is seated at the [[slit lamp biomicroscope]].
2. A topical [[anesthetic]] (e.g., [[proparacaine]] 0.5% or [[oxybuprocaine]]) is instilled.
3. [[Fluorescein sodium]] dye is applied to the [[tear film]] (often via a fluorescein-impregnated strip).
4. The cobalt blue filter of the slit lamp is engaged.
5. The tonometer prism (biprism) is gently apposed to the central [[cornea]].
6. The examiner observes two semicircular [[fluorescein]] mires through the prism.
7. The calibrated dial is adjusted until the inner edges of the two mires just overlap (the correct endpoint).
8. The dial reading (multiplied by 10) gives the IOP in mmHg.

#### Advantages

- Gold standard; most extensively validated
- High accuracy and reproducibility
- Minimal effect of [[scleral rigidity]]

#### Limitations

- Requires a [[slit lamp]], topical anesthesia, and fluorescein
- Affected by [[central corneal thickness]] (CCT)
- Affected by corneal surface irregularities (e.g., [[corneal scarring]], [[corneal dystrophy]])
- Risk of [[corneal abrasion]] or [[infection]] transmission (e.g., [[adenoviral keratoconjunctivitis]], [[prion disease]]) if prism is not properly disinfected
- Not suitable for uncooperative patients or young children
- Operator-dependent

#### CCT Correction

Because GAT was calibrated for an average CCT of approximately 520 µm, deviations from this value introduce systematic error. Various correction algorithms have been proposed, though none is universally accepted. A commonly cited approximation is:

| CCT (µm) | Approximate IOP Correction |
|---|---|
| 445 | Subtract ~4–7 mmHg from measured IOP |
| 500 | Subtract ~1–2 mmHg |
| 520 | No correction (reference) |
| 550 | Add ~1–2 mmHg |
| 600 | Add ~4–7 mmHg |

> **Note:** These corrections are approximations. The relationship between CCT and true IOP is complex and nonlinear, and correction nomograms remain controversial. The [[Ocular Hypertension Treatment Study]] (OHTS) demonstrated that thinner CCT is an independent risk factor for the development of [[primary open-angle glaucoma]] (POAG), beyond its effect on IOP measurement artifact.

### 2. Non-Contact Tonometry (NCT) / Air-Puff Tonometry

#### Principle

[[Non-contact tonometry]] uses a precisely metered puff of air directed at the [[cornea]]. The instrument measures the time required for the air puff to flatten a defined area of the corneal surface. Since the force of the air puff increases linearly with time, the time to applanation is directly proportional to IOP.

#### Technique

- No topical anesthesia or fluorescein is required.
- The patient fixates on an internal target.
- The instrument automatically aligns and delivers the air puff.
- Multiple readings are typically averaged.

#### Advantages

- Non-contact; no risk of corneal abrasion or cross-infection
- No anesthesia required
- Rapid; suitable for screening
- Can be performed by trained technicians

#### Limitations

- Less accurate and reproducible than GAT, especially at higher IOPs (>20 mmHg)
- Affected by CCT and corneal biomechanics
- Patient discomfort/startle response may affect readings
- Not suitable for irregular corneas

---

### 3. Dynamic Contour Tonometry (DCT) — Pascal Tonometer

#### Principle

The [[Pascal tonometer]] (DCT) uses a concave-tipped sensor that matches the corneal curvature. Rather than applanating the cornea, it conforms to its natural contour, allowing a piezoresistive pressure sensor embedded in the tip to directly measure transcorneal pressure. This approach is theoretically independent of corneal thickness and biomechanical properties.

#### Advantages

- Largely independent of CCT and corneal biomechanics
- Provides a continuous IOP waveform, from which the **[[ocular pulse amplitude]]** (OPA) can be derived—a measure of pulsatile [[choroidal]] blood flow
- High reproducibility

#### Limitations

- Requires a slit lamp and topical anesthesia
- More expensive than GAT
- Slightly longer measurement time
- Readings tend to be 1–2 mmHg higher than GAT on average

---

### 4. Rebound Tonometry (iCare)

#### Principle

[[Rebound tonometry]] (e.g., [[iCare tonometer]]) uses a small, lightweight magnetized probe that is propelled toward the cornea. The instrument measures the deceleration and rebound characteristics of the probe upon contact. Higher IOP results in faster deceleration and rebound.

#### Technique

- No topical anesthesia required (the probe contact is so brief and light that it is generally imperceptible).
- Handheld; does not require a slit lamp.
- Six measurements are taken automatically, and the instrument provides a median value.

#### Advantages

- No anesthesia needed
- Portable and handheld
- Excellent for pediatric patients, bedridden patients, and screening in community settings
- Easy to use; minimal training required
- Available in home-monitoring versions (e.g., [[iCare HOME]])

#### Limitations

- Affected by CCT (though to a lesser degree than GAT in some studies)
- Accuracy decreases at higher IOP ranges
- Requires disposable probes (cost consideration)
- Measurement position (upright vs. supine) may affect readings
- Less extensively validated than GAT

---

### 5. Ocular Response Analyzer (ORA)

#### Principle

The [[Ocular Response Analyzer]] (Reichert) is a modified non-contact tonometer that delivers an air puff and measures two applanation events—one during corneal inward deformation and one during recovery. The difference between these two pressure values defines **[[corneal hysteresis]]** (CH), a measure of the cornea's viscoelastic damping capacity. The ORA provides:

- **Goldmann-correlated IOP (IOPg)**
- **Corneal-compensated IOP (IOPcc):** An IOP estimate adjusted for corneal biomechanical properties, theoretically less affected by CCT and corneal biomechanics
- **Corneal hysteresis (CH)**
- **Corneal resistance factor (CRF)**

#### Clinical Significance

Low corneal hysteresis has been identified as an independent risk factor for [[glaucoma]] progression, beyond its effect on IOP measurement. The IOPcc value may provide a more accurate estimate of true IOP in patients with atypical corneal properties (e.g., post-[[LASIK]], [[keratoconus]]).

---

### 6. Schiøtz (Indentation) Tonometry

#### Principle

The [[Schiøtz tonometer]] measures IOP by quantifying the degree to which a weighted plunger indents the [[cornea]] when placed on the supine patient's eye. Greater indentation corresponds to lower IOP. A conversion table is used to translate the scale reading and plunger weight into mmHg.

#### Advantages

- Inexpensive and portable
- Does not require a slit lamp

#### Limitations

- Highly dependent on [[scleral rigidity]] (overestimates IOP in rigid eyes, underestimates in myopic/elongated eyes)
- Requires supine positioning
- Less accurate than applanation methods
- Largely obsolete in modern clinical practice in developed countries

---

### 7. Pneumatonometry (Mackay-Marg Principle)

#### Principle

The [[pneumatonometer]] uses a small, air-filled plunger covered by a silicone membrane. When applied to the cornea, the membrane conforms to the corneal surface, and the air pressure required to maintain applanation is measured. It operates on a principle similar to the [[Mackay-Marg tonometer]], which is relatively independent of corneal rigidity.

#### Advantages

- Can be used on irregular corneas (e.g., post-[[penetrating keratoplasty]], [[corneal scarring]])
- Can be used over soft [[contact lenses]]
- Provides a continuous pressure tracing

#### Limitations

- Requires topical anesthesia
- Less widely available
- Slightly less precise than GAT on normal corneas

---

### 8. Transpalpebral Tonometry

#### Principle

[[Transpalpebral tonometry]] (e.g., Diaton tonometer) measures IOP through the [[eyelid]], targeting the [[sclera]] rather than the [[cornea]]. This avoids corneal contact entirely.

#### Advantages

- No corneal contact; useful in patients with corneal pathology
- No anesthesia or fluorescein required

#### Limitations

- Lower accuracy and reproducibility compared to GAT
- Affected by eyelid thickness and tissue properties
- Not widely adopted in clinical practice

---

### Comparison of Tonometric Methods

| Method | Contact | Anesthesia | Slit Lamp | Gold Standard Agreement | CCT Dependence | Special Utility |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **GAT** | Yes | Yes | Yes | Reference standard | High | Standard clinical care |
| **NCT** | No | No | No | Moderate | High | Screening, technician-operated |
| **DCT (Pascal)** | Yes | Yes | Yes | Good (slightly higher) | Low | Post-refractive surgery, research |
| **Rebound (iCare)** | Yes (minimal) | No | No | Good | Moderate | Pediatrics, home monitoring, screening |
| **ORA** | No | No | No | Good | Low (IOPcc) | Corneal biomechanics assessment |
| **Schiøtz** | Yes | Yes | No | Poor–Moderate | Low (but scleral rigidity dependent) | Resource-limited settings |
| **Pneumatonometer** | Yes | Yes | No | Good | Moderate | Irregular corneas, continuous monitoring |
| **Transpalpebral** | No (cornea) | No | No | Poor–Moderate | Low | Corneal pathology |

## Sources of Error in Tonometry

Accurate IOP measurement requires awareness of numerous potential sources of error:

### Operator-Related Errors (GAT)
- Incorrect calibration of the tonometer
- Excessive or insufficient [[fluorescein]]
- Off-center applanation (measuring peripheral cornea)
- Excessive pressure on the globe through the lids
- Allowing the patient to hold their breath or perform a [[Valsalva maneuver]]
- Reading the dial at the wrong endpoint (mires too wide or too narrow)

### Patient-Related Factors
- Tight collar or necktie (elevates [[episcleral venous pressure]])
- Breath-holding or straining
- Squeezing of the [[orbicularis oculi]] muscle
- Recent consumption of large volumes of fluid
- Corneal pathology (edema, scarring, dystrophy, ectasia)
- Post-surgical corneal changes

### Instrument-Related Factors
- Calibration drift (GAT should be calibrated regularly per manufacturer guidelines)
- Probe contamination or damage
- Incorrect prism orientation (GAT: the red mark should be aligned at 180° for standard measurement, or at 43° for correction of [[corneal astigmatism]] >3 diopters)

## Role of Tonometry in Glaucoma Management

### Screening and Diagnosis

While elevated IOP is the most significant modifiable risk factor for [[glaucoma]], it is neither necessary nor sufficient for diagnosis. [[Normal-tension glaucoma]] (NTG) occurs with statistically normal IOP, and many individuals with [[ocular hypertension]] (OHT) never develop glaucomatous damage. Therefore, tonometry must always be interpreted in conjunction with:

- [[Optic nerve head]] assessment (e.g., [[cup-to-disc ratio]], [[neuroretinal rim]] evaluation)
- [[Retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL) analysis (e.g., [[optical coherence tomography]])
- [[Visual field]] testing (e.g., [[Humphrey visual field]], [[standard automated perimetry]])
- [[Gonioscopy]] (assessment of the [[anterior chamber angle]])
- [[Pachymetry]] (measurement of [[central corneal thickness]])
- Assessment of risk factors (age, race, family history, CCT, corneal hysteresis)

### Target IOP

The concept of **[[target IOP]]** is central to glaucoma management. Target IOP is the IOP level at which further glaucomatous damage is expected to be halted or slowed. It is individualized for each patient based on:

- Baseline (pre-treatment) IOP
- Severity of existing damage
- Rate of progression
- Life expectancy
- Risk factor profile

A commonly used guideline is a **20–30% reduction from baseline IOP** for early glaucoma, with more aggressive targets for advanced disease or rapidly progressing cases. Serial tonometric measurements are essential for assessing whether the target IOP is being achieved and maintained.

### IOP Fluctuation

Emerging evidence suggests that **IOP fluctuation**—both short-term (diurnal) and long-term (visit-to-visit)—may be an independent risk factor for glaucoma progression. This has led to increased interest in:

- **Diurnal IOP curves:** Multiple IOP measurements throughout the day (e.g., phasing)
- **Home tonometry:** Devices such as the [[iCare HOME]] allow patients to measure their own IOP at various times, capturing peaks that may be missed during office visits
- **24-hour IOP monitoring:** Experimental devices (e.g., [[Sensimed Triggerfish]] contact lens sensor) that provide continuous IOP-related data

## Pharmacological Considerations Related to Tonometry

While tonometry itself is a diagnostic procedure, its results directly guide pharmacological management of IOP. The major classes of IOP-lowering medications include:

| Drug Class | Mechanism | Examples | Approximate IOP Reduction |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Prostaglandin analogs]]** | Increase [[uveoscleral outflow]] | [[Latanoprost]], [[Travoprost]], [[Bimatoprost]], [[Tafluprost]] | 25–33% |
| **[[Beta-adrenergic antagonists]]** | Decrease aqueous production | [[Timolol]], [[Betaxolol]], [[Levobunolol]] | 20–25% |
| **[[Alpha-adrenergic agonists]]** | Decrease aqueous production, increase uveoscleral outflow | [[Brimonidine]], [[Apraclonidine]] | 20–25% |
| **[[Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]]** (topical) | Decrease aqueous production | [[Dorzolamide]], [[Brinzolamide]] | 15–20% |
| **[[Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]]** (oral) | Decrease aqueous production | [[Acetazolamide]], [[Methazolamide]] | 25–40% |
| **[[Rho kinase inhibitors]]** | Increase [[trabecular meshwork]] outflow | [[Netarsudil]] | 15–20% |
| **[[Nitric oxide donors]]** | Increase trabecular outflow | [[Latanoprostene bunod]] | 25–33% |
| **[[Cholinergic agonists]]** | Increase trabecular outflow via [[ciliary muscle]] contraction | [[Pilocarpine]] | 20–25% |
| **[[Osmotic agents]]** | Decrease [[vitreous]] volume | [[Mannitol]] (IV), [[Glycerol]] (oral) | Acute, significant |

Tonometry is performed at every follow-up visit to assess the efficacy of these agents and to guide therapeutic adjustments, including escalation, switching, or addition of agents.

## Surgical and Procedural Relevance

Tonometry plays a critical role in the pre-operative, intra-operative, and post-operative assessment of numerous ophthalmic procedures:

- **[[Trabeculectomy]]:** Post-operative IOP monitoring is essential to detect hypotony or failure.
- **[[Glaucoma drainage devices]]** (e.g., [[Ahmed valve]], [[Baerveldt implant]]): IOP monitoring guides assessment of device function.
- **[[Minimally invasive glaucoma surgery]] (MIGS):** Procedures such as [[iStent]], [[Hydrus Microstent]], [[Kahook Dual Blade goniotomy]], [[XEN Gel Stent]], and [[canaloplasty]] require serial tonometric follow-up.
- **[[Selective laser trabeculoplasty]] (SLT):** IOP is measured before and after the procedure to assess efficacy.
- **[[Laser peripheral iridotomy]] (LPI):** IOP is checked post-procedure to detect pressure spikes.
- **[[Cataract surgery]]:** IOP is monitored post-operatively, particularly in patients with pre-existing glaucoma or those receiving intracameral agents.
- **[[Intravitreal injections]]:** Transient IOP spikes are common; tonometry may be performed immediately post-injection and at follow-up.

## Special Populations and Considerations

### Pediatric Tonometry

IOP measurement in children presents unique challenges due to limited cooperation. Preferred methods include:

- **Rebound tonometry (iCare):** Often the first-line choice due to its speed, portability, and lack of anesthesia requirement.
- **Handheld applanation tonometry (e.g., [[Perkins tonometer]]):** A portable version of GAT; requires topical anesthesia.
- **Tonometry under anesthesia (TUA):** Performed in the operating room for infants and young children (e.g., suspected [[congenital glaucoma]]). It is critical to note that general anesthetic agents (especially volatile agents such as [[sevoflurane]]) and [[ketamine]] can significantly affect IOP readings. [[Ketamine]] tends to raise IOP, while most other agents lower it.

### Post-Refractive Surgery Patients

Patients who have undergone [[LASIK]], [[PRK]], [[SMILE]], or other corneal refractive procedures present a significant challenge for tonometry because:

- Corneal thickness is reduced
- Corneal biomechanics are altered
- Standard GAT readings are artifactually low, potentially masking elevated IOP

In these patients, **DCT (Pascal)** and **ORA (IOPcc)** are considered more reliable, as they are less dependent on corneal properties. Clinicians must document the patient's refractive surgical history and interpret IOP readings with appropriate caution.

### Corneal Pathology

In eyes with significant corneal irregularity (e.g., [[keratoconus]], post-[[penetrating keratoplasty]], [[corneal scarring]]), standard applanation tonometry may be unreliable. The **pneumatonometer** and **rebound tonometer** may provide more consistent readings in these cases.

## Infection Control and Tonometer Disinfection

Contact tonometry carries a theoretical risk of transmitting infectious agents, including:

- [[Adenovirus]] ([[epidemic keratoconjunctivitis]])
- [[Herpes simplex virus]] (HSV)
- [[Hepatitis B virus]]
- [[Prion diseases]] (e.g., [[Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease]])

Standard disinfection protocols for GAT prisms include:

- Wiping with 70% [[isopropyl alcohol]] swab (effective against most bacteria and viruses; **not effective against prions**)
- Soaking in 1:10 dilute [[sodium hypochlorite]] (household bleach) for 5–10 minutes (effective against prions)
- Soaking in 3% [[hydrogen peroxide]] for 10 minutes
- Use of disposable tonometer prism covers (e.g., Tonosafe) to eliminate cross-contamination risk entirely

The use of **single-use disposable prism covers** or **disposable prisms** is increasingly recommended, particularly in the context of heightened infection control awareness.

---

## Calibration and Quality Assurance

The accuracy of tonometric instruments depends on regular calibration and maintenance:

- **GAT:** Should be checked at each session or at minimum weekly. The Goldmann tonometer has a built-in calibration check: at the zero setting, the reading should be 0 ± 0.5 mmHg; at 2 (20 mmHg) and 6 (60 mmHg), the readings should be within ± 0.5 mmHg.
- **NCT and ORA:** Calibration per manufacturer's schedule.
- **Rebound tonometers:** Probe replacement per manufacturer guidelines; instrument calibration as recommended.
- **Schiøtz tonometer:** Plunger should move freely; calibration checked with the test block provided.

---

## Emerging Technologies

Several novel approaches to IOP measurement are under active investigation:

- **Continuous IOP monitoring:** Implantable sensors (e.g., [[Eyemate]] system, an intraocular pressure sensor implanted during cataract surgery) and contact lens–based sensors (e.g., [[Sensimed Triggerfish]]) aim to provide 24-hour IOP profiles.
- **Smartphone-based tonometry:** Experimental devices leveraging smartphone technology for portable IOP assessment.
- **Optical coherence tomography (OCT)-based IOP estimation:** Research into correlating corneal and anterior segment deformation patterns with IOP.
- **Implantable micro-electromechanical systems (MEMS):** Miniaturized pressure sensors designed for long-term intraocular implantation.

These technologies hold promise for capturing the full spectrum of IOP fluctuation, which may significantly improve glaucoma management in the future.

---

## Summary

Tonometry is an indispensable component of ophthalmic examination, providing the only direct clinical measurement of [[intraocular pressure]]. While [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]] remains the gold standard, a diverse array of instruments is available, each suited to specific clinical scenarios. Accurate interpretation of tonometric data requires an understanding of [[aqueous humor dynamics]], [[corneal biomechanics]], and the inherent limitations of each measurement technique. Tonometry is central to the detection, monitoring, and management of [[glaucoma]] and must be integrated with structural and functional assessments of the [[optic nerve]] and [[visual field]] for optimal patient care.
