

# Optic Neuritis

---

**Optic Neuritis** is an acute inflammatory, demyelinating condition of the [[optic nerve]] (cranial nerve II) that results in sudden, typically unilateral, loss of vision accompanied by pain on eye movement. It is one of the most common optic neuropathies encountered in clinical practice and represents a critical diagnostic entity for both optometrists and ophthalmologists due to its strong association with [[multiple sclerosis]] (MS) and other central nervous system (CNS) demyelinating diseases. The condition predominantly affects young adults, with a peak incidence between 20 and 45 years of age, and demonstrates a notable female predilection. The landmark [[Optic Neuritis Treatment Trial]] (ONTT), initiated in 1988, remains the foundational clinical study that has shaped the modern understanding, treatment, and prognostic counseling of this condition.

---

## Epidemiology

Optic neuritis has an estimated annual incidence of approximately 1–5 per 100,000 population in Western countries, with higher rates observed in northern latitudes, mirroring the geographic distribution of [[multiple sclerosis]]. The condition is more prevalent among Caucasian populations compared to African, Asian, and Hispanic populations, although the clinical phenotype and prognosis may differ significantly across ethnic groups.

| **Epidemiological Parameter** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| Annual incidence | 1–5 per 100,000 |
| Peak age of onset | 20–45 years |
| Female-to-male ratio | Approximately 3:1 |
| Bilateral involvement (adults) | ~10% of typical cases; more common in children |
| Bilateral involvement (children) | Up to 60% of pediatric cases |
| Lifetime risk of MS after optic neuritis | 50% at 15 years (ONTT data) |
| Geographic predilection | Higher incidence at northern latitudes |
| Racial predilection | More common in Caucasians; more severe in African descent and Asian populations |

In pediatric populations, optic neuritis tends to present bilaterally more frequently, is more commonly associated with a preceding viral illness or vaccination, and generally carries a more favorable visual prognosis compared to adult-onset disease. However, pediatric optic neuritis associated with [[myelin oligodendrocyte glycoprotein]] (MOG) antibodies or [[aquaporin-4]] (AQP4) antibodies may follow a relapsing course and requires distinct management considerations.

## Etiology and Classification

Optic neuritis can be classified based on its underlying etiology, anatomical location, and associated systemic conditions. The most clinically relevant classification distinguishes between **typical** (demyelinating) and **atypical** optic neuritis, as this distinction carries profound implications for prognosis, workup, and management.

### Etiological Classification

| **Category** | **Examples / Associations** |
|---|---|
| **Demyelinating (Typical)** | [[Multiple sclerosis]]-associated; [[Clinically Isolated Syndrome]] (CIS) |
| **Autoimmune / Antibody-Mediated** | [[Neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder]] (NMOSD/AQP4-IgG); [[MOG antibody-associated disease]] (MOGAD) |
| **Infectious** | [[Syphilis]], [[Lyme disease]], [[tuberculosis]], [[herpes zoster]], [[HIV]], [[COVID-19]], [[bartonellosis]] (cat-scratch disease) |
| **Post-infectious / Para-infectious** | Post-viral (measles, mumps, varicella), post-vaccination |
| **Systemic Autoimmune** | [[Systemic lupus erythematosus]] (SLE), [[sarcoidosis]], [[granulomatosis with polyangiitis]] |
| **Drug/Toxin-Induced** | [[Ethambutol]], [[isoniazid]], [[linezolid]], [[tumor necrosis factor inhibitors]], [[checkpoint inhibitors]] |
| **Idiopathic** | No identifiable cause after thorough investigation |

### Anatomical Classification

- **Retrobulbar optic neuritis**: Inflammation occurs posterior to the [[lamina cribrosa]], within the orbital, canalicular, or intracranial segments of the optic nerve. The [[optic disc]] appears normal on [[fundoscopy]] at presentation. This is the most common form in adults and is classically associated with MS. The clinical aphorism "the patient sees nothing and the doctor sees nothing" aptly describes this presentation.
- **Papillitis (anterior optic neuritis)**: Inflammation involves the optic nerve head, resulting in visible [[optic disc edema]] on fundoscopic examination. This form is more common in children and in non-MS-associated etiologies.
- **Neuroretinitis**: Characterized by optic disc edema accompanied by a [[macular star]] pattern of hard exudates. This form is generally considered **atypical** for MS-associated demyelination and should prompt investigation for infectious etiologies, particularly [[bartonella henselae]] (cat-scratch disease), [[syphilis]], and [[Lyme disease]].
- **Perineuritis**: Inflammation primarily involves the optic nerve sheath rather than the nerve parenchyma itself. This is associated with [[sarcoidosis]], [[IgG4-related disease]], and other granulomatous conditions.

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of optic neuritis varies according to its underlying etiology, but the most extensively studied mechanism pertains to the **demyelinating (MS-associated)** form.

### Demyelinating Optic Neuritis

In typical demyelinating optic neuritis, the fundamental pathological process involves **autoimmune-mediated inflammatory demyelination** of the optic nerve. The cascade begins with the activation of autoreactive [[T-lymphocytes]] (primarily CD4+ T-helper cells) in the peripheral immune system, which recognize [[myelin]] antigens—including [[myelin basic protein]] (MBP), [[proteolipid protein]] (PLP), and [[myelin oligodendrocyte glycoprotein]] (MOG)—presented by [[antigen-presenting cells]].

These activated T-cells cross the [[blood-brain barrier]] (BBB) and the [[blood-nerve barrier]] through interactions between [[integrins]] (such as [[VLA-4]]/α4β1 integrin) on the T-cell surface and [[vascular cell adhesion molecule-1]] (VCAM-1) on endothelial cells. Once within the optic nerve parenchyma, the T-cells are reactivated upon encountering their target myelin antigens presented by local [[microglia]] and [[macrophages]].

This reactivation triggers a robust inflammatory cascade involving:

1. **Pro-inflammatory cytokine release**: [[Interferon-gamma]] (IFN-γ), [[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] (TNF-α), [[interleukin-17]] (IL-17), and [[interleukin-6]] (IL-6) amplify the local inflammatory response.
2. **Recruitment of additional immune cells**: [[Macrophages]], [[B-lymphocytes]], and additional T-cells are recruited to the site of inflammation, perpetuating the immune attack.
3. **Complement activation**: The [[complement cascade]] contributes to myelin destruction and [[oligodendrocyte]] injury.
4. **Antibody-mediated damage**: [[B-cells]] and [[plasma cells]] produce antibodies directed against myelin components, further contributing to demyelination.

The resulting **demyelination** disrupts saltatory conduction along the optic nerve [[axons]]. In myelinated nerve fibers, [[action potentials]] propagate rapidly by jumping between [[nodes of Ranvier]]. Loss of the [[myelin sheath]] forces conduction to become continuous rather than saltatory, dramatically slowing nerve impulse transmission and, in severe cases, causing complete [[conduction block]]. This conduction failure is the primary mechanism underlying the acute visual loss experienced by patients.

### Conduction Block and Recovery

During the acute phase, both demyelination and inflammatory edema contribute to conduction failure. The edema compresses axons and disrupts the ionic microenvironment necessary for normal signal propagation. As inflammation subsides—either spontaneously or with treatment—edema resolves, and partial remyelination occurs through the action of [[oligodendrocyte precursor cells]] (OPCs). Additionally, redistribution of [[sodium channels]] along demyelinated axonal segments can partially restore conduction even in the absence of complete remyelination.

This explains the characteristic pattern of visual recovery observed in most patients: initial severe visual loss followed by gradual improvement over weeks to months. However, the recovery is often incomplete because:

- **Axonal loss**: In addition to demyelination, there is variable degrees of primary and secondary axonal degeneration. Axonal transection occurs due to direct inflammatory injury, energy failure from mitochondrial dysfunction in demyelinated segments, and [[Wallerian degeneration]]. This axonal loss is **irreversible** and is the primary substrate for permanent visual deficit.
- **Incomplete remyelination**: Remyelinated segments have thinner myelin sheaths and shorter internodal distances compared to normally myelinated fibers, resulting in persistently slowed conduction.
- **Retrograde and anterograde degeneration**: Axonal injury at the site of inflammation leads to both retrograde degeneration (toward the [[retinal ganglion cells]]) and anterograde degeneration (toward the [[lateral geniculate nucleus]]).

### Pathophysiology of AQP4-IgG (NMOSD) Optic Neuritis

In [[neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder]], the pathophysiology is fundamentally different. [[Aquaporin-4]] (AQP4) antibodies target the AQP4 water channel, which is highly expressed on [[astrocyte]] foot processes at the [[blood-brain barrier]]. Binding of AQP4-IgG to astrocytes triggers complement-dependent cytotoxicity (CDC) and antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC), leading to primary **astrocytopathy** with secondary demyelination and axonal loss. This mechanism tends to produce more severe, destructive lesions with greater axonal loss and poorer visual recovery compared to MS-associated optic neuritis.

### Pathophysiology of MOG-IgG Optic Neuritis

[[MOG antibody-associated disease]] involves antibodies targeting [[myelin oligodendrocyte glycoprotein]], which is expressed on the outermost surface of the myelin sheath and on oligodendrocyte cell bodies. The pathology is characterized by perivenous demyelination with relative axonal preservation, which may explain the generally better visual recovery observed in MOGAD compared to NMOSD.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

The hallmark presentation of typical optic neuritis includes the following cardinal features:

#### Visual Loss
- **Onset**: Subacute, developing over hours to days (typically 1–14 days), with nadir usually reached within 1–2 weeks.
- **Severity**: Ranges from mild blurring to complete loss of light perception. In the ONTT, approximately 10% of patients had visual acuity of 20/20 or better at presentation, while approximately 36% had visual acuity of 20/200 or worse.
- **Pattern**: Typically unilateral in adults. The visual field loss is most commonly a central or cecocentral [[scotoma]], but virtually any pattern of field loss can occur, including altitudinal, arcuate, and diffuse depression.

#### Pain
- **Periocular or retrobulbar pain** is present in approximately **92%** of patients (ONTT data).
- Pain is characteristically **exacerbated by eye movement** due to traction on the inflamed optic nerve by the [[rectus muscles]] at their insertion near the [[annulus of Zinn]].
- Pain typically precedes or accompanies the onset of visual loss and usually resolves within days, often before visual recovery begins.

#### Dyschromatopsia
- **Color vision impairment** is a hallmark feature and is often disproportionate to the degree of visual acuity loss ("color desaturation out of proportion to acuity loss").
- Red desaturation is the most commonly reported subjective complaint, with colors appearing "washed out" or "faded" in the affected eye.
- Quantitative assessment with [[Ishihara color plates]], [[Hardy-Rand-Rittler plates]], or the [[Farnsworth-Munsell 100-hue test]] typically reveals significant deficits.

#### Other Visual Symptoms
- **[[Uhthoff phenomenon]]**: Transient worsening of vision with increased body temperature (e.g., exercise, hot bath, fever). This occurs in approximately 50% of patients and is due to temperature-dependent conduction block in demyelinated fibers. It is highly suggestive of a demyelinating etiology.
- **[[Pulfrich phenomenon]]**: An illusory perception of altered depth or trajectory of moving objects, caused by asymmetric conduction velocity between the two optic nerves.
- **Phosphenes (photopsias)**: Brief flashes of light, often provoked by eye movement, reported by approximately 30% of patients. These are thought to result from mechanical stimulation of demyelinated axons.
- **Reduced contrast sensitivity**: Often persists even after visual acuity returns to normal and may be the most sensitive measure of residual visual dysfunction.

### Signs

#### Afferent Pupillary Defect
A **[[relative afferent pupillary defect]]** (RAPD), also known as a **[[Marcus Gunn pupil]]**, is present in virtually all cases of unilateral optic neuritis and is detected using the **[[swinging flashlight test]]**. In the affected eye, both the direct and consensual pupillary responses are diminished relative to the unaffected eye. The RAPD is one of the most important clinical signs, as its absence in a patient with suspected unilateral optic neuritis should prompt reconsideration of the diagnosis. In bilateral cases, the RAPD may be absent or present on the more severely affected side.

#### Fundoscopic Findings

| **Finding** | **Frequency** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|---|
| Normal optic disc (retrobulbar neuritis) | ~65% of adult cases | Most common presentation in MS-associated ON |
| [[Optic disc edema]] (papillitis) | ~35% of adult cases | More common in children, MOGAD, and infectious etiologies |
| [[Optic disc pallor]] | Develops weeks to months after acute episode | Indicates axonal loss; temporal pallor is characteristic |
| Peripapillary hemorrhages | Rare in typical ON | If prominent, consider atypical etiologies |
| [[Macular star]] exudates | Atypical for MS-associated ON | Suggests neuroretinitis; investigate for infectious causes |
| Retinal [[nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL) thickening | Acute phase | Reflects edema; measured by [[optical coherence tomography]] |
| RNFL thinning | Chronic phase (3–6 months post-onset) | Reflects axonal loss; correlates with visual outcome |

#### Visual Field Defects
While a central or cecocentral scotoma is the most classic pattern, the ONTT demonstrated that virtually any pattern of visual field loss can occur in optic neuritis:

| **Visual Field Pattern** | **Approximate Frequency** |
|---|---|
| Diffuse depression | 48% |
| Central/cecocentral scotoma | 8% |
| Altitudinal defect | 15% |
| Arcuate defect | 10% |
| Other focal defects | 19% |

## Diagnosis

The diagnosis of optic neuritis is primarily **clinical**, based on the characteristic triad of subacute unilateral visual loss, pain with eye movement, and a relative afferent pupillary defect. However, ancillary testing is essential to confirm the diagnosis, exclude mimicking conditions, and assess the risk of associated systemic disease.

### Clinical Diagnostic Criteria

The diagnosis of **typical** optic neuritis requires the following features:
1. Subacute unilateral visual loss (developing over hours to days)
2. Periocular pain, especially with eye movement
3. Relative afferent pupillary defect (in unilateral cases)
4. Dyschromatopsia
5. Age 20–45 years
6. Spontaneous visual recovery beginning within 2–4 weeks

**Atypical features** that should prompt expanded workup include:
- Age <12 or >50 years
- Bilateral simultaneous involvement
- Absence of pain
- No light perception vision
- Severe optic disc edema with hemorrhages
- Macular star exudates
- Progressive worsening beyond 2 weeks
- No recovery after 4–6 weeks
- Recurrence in the same eye (especially if steroid-dependent)

### Diagnostic Modalities

#### Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

[[Magnetic resonance imaging]] is the single most important ancillary test in the evaluation of optic neuritis. It serves two critical purposes:

1. **Confirmation of optic nerve inflammation**: Dedicated orbital MRI with fat-suppressed, gadolinium-enhanced T1-weighted sequences demonstrates enhancement of the affected optic nerve segment in approximately 95% of acute cases. [[Short tau inversion recovery]] (STIR) and T2-weighted fat-suppressed sequences show hyperintensity of the affected nerve.

2. **Assessment of MS risk**: Brain MRI with and without gadolinium is essential for evaluating the presence of white matter lesions suggestive of [[multiple sclerosis]]. The ONTT and subsequent studies established that the presence and number of T2-hyperintense white matter lesions on brain MRI at the time of optic neuritis is the **strongest predictor** of future MS development.

| **Brain MRI Findings at Presentation** | **15-Year Risk of MS (ONTT)** |
|---|---|
| No T2 lesions (normal brain MRI) | 25% |
| ≥1 T2 lesion | 72% |
| ≥3 T2 lesions | Higher risk (specific data varies) |

Spinal cord MRI should be considered when there is clinical suspicion for [[neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder]] or when the presentation includes features suggestive of [[transverse myelitis]].

#### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

[[Optical coherence tomography]] has become an indispensable tool in the evaluation and monitoring of optic neuritis. Both **peripapillary retinal nerve fiber layer** (pRNFL) and **ganglion cell-inner plexiform layer** (GCIPL) thickness measurements provide objective, quantitative assessment of structural damage.

- **Acute phase**: pRNFL may be normal or thickened (due to edema) in the affected eye.
- **Chronic phase (3–6 months)**: pRNFL thinning develops, reflecting retrograde axonal degeneration from the site of optic nerve injury to the [[retinal ganglion cell]] layer. Average pRNFL loss of approximately 10–20 μm is typical after a single episode.
- **GCIPL thinning**: May be detectable earlier than pRNFL thinning (as early as 2–3 months) and is less confounded by acute disc edema.
- **Prognostic value**: Greater RNFL and GCIPL thinning correlates with worse visual outcomes, including reduced visual acuity, contrast sensitivity, and visual field sensitivity.

#### Visual Evoked Potentials (VEP)

[[Visual evoked potentials]] measure the electrical response of the [[visual cortex]] to visual stimulation (typically pattern-reversal checkerboard stimuli). In optic neuritis:

- **Acute phase**: Reduced amplitude and prolonged [[P100 latency]] (normally ~100 ms). The P100 latency prolongation reflects slowed conduction through the demyelinated optic nerve.
- **Recovery phase**: Amplitude typically recovers, but P100 latency prolongation often **persists indefinitely**, even after full visual recovery. This persistent latency delay serves as an electrophysiological "fingerprint" of prior demyelination.
- **Clinical utility**: VEP is particularly useful for confirming subclinical optic nerve involvement in the fellow eye, supporting a diagnosis of MS by demonstrating dissemination in space.

#### Laboratory Investigations

Laboratory testing is guided by the clinical presentation and is particularly important when **atypical features** are present:

| **Test** | **Indication** |
|---|---|
| [[AQP4-IgG]] (aquaporin-4 antibodies) | Suspected NMOSD; severe visual loss, bilateral involvement, poor recovery |
| [[MOG-IgG]] (MOG antibodies) | Recurrent ON, bilateral ON, prominent disc edema, pediatric cases |
| [[Erythrocyte sedimentation rate]] (ESR) / [[C-reactive protein]] (CRP) | Suspected inflammatory or infectious etiology |
| [[Angiotensin-converting enzyme]] (ACE) level | Suspected [[sarcoidosis]] |
| [[Antinuclear antibody]] (ANA), [[anti-dsDNA]] | Suspected [[systemic lupus erythematosus]] |
| [[RPR]]/[[VDRL]], [[FTA-ABS]] | Suspected [[syphilis]] |
| [[Lyme serology]] (ELISA, Western blot) | Endemic areas; suspected [[Lyme disease]] |
| [[Bartonella serology]] | Neuroretinitis presentation; cat exposure |
| [[Chest X-ray]] or [[CT chest]] | Suspected sarcoidosis or tuberculosis |
| [[Lumbar puncture]] with CSF analysis | Atypical presentations; oligoclonal bands for MS risk stratification |

#### Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) Analysis

[[Lumbar puncture]] is not routinely required for typical optic neuritis but may be valuable in atypical cases or for MS risk stratification. Relevant CSF findings include:

- **[[Oligoclonal bands]]** (OCBs): Present in approximately 60–70% of patients with a first episode of optic neuritis and in >95% of patients with established MS. Their presence increases the risk of MS conversion.
- **Elevated [[IgG index]]**: Indicates intrathecal immunoglobulin synthesis.
- **CSF pleocytosis**: Mild lymphocytic pleocytosis may be present; marked pleocytosis (>50 cells/μL) is atypical for MS-associated ON and should raise concern for infectious or NMOSD-related etiologies.

## Differential Diagnosis

It is essential to distinguish optic neuritis from other causes of acute optic neuropathy, as the management and prognosis differ significantly.

| **Condition** | **Key Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| [[Anterior ischemic optic neuropathy]] (AION) | Older age (>50), painless, altitudinal field defect, disc edema with hemorrhages, associated with vascular risk factors |
| [[Compressive optic neuropathy]] | Progressive (not subacute), proptosis, motility restriction, neuroimaging reveals mass lesion |
| [[Leber hereditary optic neuropathy]] (LHON) | Young males, painless, bilateral sequential, peripapillary telangiectasia, mitochondrial DNA mutations |
| [[Papilledema]] | Bilateral disc edema, elevated intracranial pressure, preserved visual acuity initially, enlarged blind spots |
| [[Toxic/nutritional optic neuropathy]] | Bilateral, symmetric, painless, cecocentral scotomas, history of toxin exposure or nutritional deficiency |
| [[Sarcoid optic neuropathy]] | May mimic ON; granulomatous inflammation, elevated ACE, chest imaging abnormalities |
| [[Optic nerve glioma]] or [[meningioma]] | Progressive visual loss, proptosis, characteristic neuroimaging findings |

## Treatment and Management

### Acute Treatment

The management of acute optic neuritis was definitively shaped by the **[[Optic Neuritis Treatment Trial]]** (ONTT), a landmark multicenter randomized controlled trial that enrolled 457 patients between 1988 and 1991.

#### ONTT Treatment Arms and Key Findings

| **Treatment Arm** | **Regimen** | **Key Findings** |
|---|---|---|
| **IV methylprednisolone → oral prednisone** | IV [[methylprednisolone]] 250 mg q6h × 3 days, then oral [[prednisone]] 1 mg/kg/day × 11 days | Accelerated visual recovery but no long-term visual benefit at 6 months or beyond; reduced rate of MS development at 2 years (but not at 3+ years) |
| **Oral prednisone alone** | Oral prednisone 1 mg/kg/day × 14 days | **Increased** recurrence rate of optic neuritis compared to placebo; this regimen is **contraindicated** |
| **Oral placebo** | Placebo × 14 days | Spontaneous recovery occurred in most patients |

#### Critical Clinical Implications from the ONTT

1. **Intravenous corticosteroids accelerate recovery** but do not improve final visual outcome at 6 months or beyond.
2. **Oral prednisone alone at standard doses (1 mg/kg/day) is contraindicated** as initial treatment due to increased recurrence risk.
3. **Spontaneous recovery** occurs in the vast majority of patients regardless of treatment.
4. Treatment decisions should be individualized based on severity of visual loss, patient occupation, need for rapid recovery, and fellow eye status.

#### Current Standard of Care for Acute Typical Optic Neuritis

- **Observation**: Appropriate for patients with mild visual loss (e.g., 20/40 or better) who do not require rapid visual recovery.
- **Intravenous methylprednisolone**: 1 gram IV daily for 3–5 days, with or without an oral prednisone taper. Indicated when rapid recovery is desired (e.g., monocular patient, bilateral involvement, occupational necessity, severe visual loss).
- **High-dose oral corticosteroids**: Recent studies have investigated high-dose oral bioequivalent regimens (e.g., oral [[methylprednisolone]] 1250 mg/day or oral [[prednisone]] 1250 mg/day for 3 days) as alternatives to IV administration, with some evidence suggesting comparable efficacy. However, this remains an area of ongoing investigation.

#### Treatment of Atypical and Antibody-Mediated Optic Neuritis

The management of **NMOSD-associated** and **MOGAD-associated** optic neuritis differs significantly from typical MS-associated optic neuritis:

| **Condition** | **Acute Treatment** | **Maintenance/Prevention** |
|---|---|---|
| **NMOSD (AQP4-IgG+)** | High-dose IV methylprednisolone (1g/day × 5 days); [[plasma exchange]] (PLEX) if poor response | [[Rituximab]], [[eculizumab]], [[inebilizumab]], [[satralizumab]]; long-term immunosuppression is essential |
| **MOGAD** | High-dose IV methylprednisolone with prolonged oral taper (often ≥6 months); PLEX for refractory cases | Controversial; [[azathioprine]], [[mycophenolate mofetil]], [[rituximab]], or [[IVIG]] for relapsing disease |
| **Infectious ON** | Treat underlying infection (e.g., antibiotics for syphilis, Lyme, bartonella); corticosteroids may be adjunctive | Pathogen-specific antimicrobial therapy |
| **Sarcoid ON** | High-dose IV methylprednisolone; prolonged oral corticosteroid taper | Steroid-sparing agents: [[methotrexate]], [[azathioprine]], [[mycophenolate mofetil]], [[infliximab]] |

### Disease-Modifying Therapy for MS Prevention

A critical aspect of managing optic neuritis is the assessment and counseling regarding the risk of [[multiple sclerosis]]. The **Controlled High-Risk Subjects Avonax Multiple Sclerosis Prevention Study** ([[CHAMPS]]) and subsequent trials (ETOMS, BENEFIT, REFLEX, TOPIC) demonstrated that early initiation of [[disease-modifying therapy]] (DMT) in patients with a first demyelinating event (clinically isolated syndrome) and MRI evidence of demyelination significantly delays or reduces the risk of conversion to clinically definite MS.

Current recommendations include:
- **Referral to [[neurology]]** for all patients with a first episode of optic neuritis, particularly those with abnormal brain MRI.
- **Initiation of DMT** should be strongly considered in patients meeting criteria for [[clinically isolated syndrome]] (CIS) with high-risk MRI features (≥2 T2 lesions).
- Available first-line DMTs include [[interferon beta-1a]], [[interferon beta-1b]], [[glatiramer acetate]], [[teriflunomide]], [[dimethyl fumarate]], and others, with selection guided by neurological consultation.

### Long-Term Visual Prognosis

The visual prognosis for typical optic neuritis is generally favorable:

| **Outcome Measure** | **ONTT Data (15-Year Follow-Up)** |
|---|---|
| Visual acuity ≥20/20 | 72% of affected eyes |
| Visual acuity ≥20/40 | 92% of affected eyes |
| Visual acuity ≤20/200 | 3% of affected eyes |
| Recurrence of ON (same or fellow eye) | 35% over 15 years |
| Persistent contrast sensitivity deficit | Common even with 20/20 acuity |
| Persistent color vision deficit | Common even with 20/20 acuity |
| Persistent visual field deficit | Common even with 20/20 acuity |

It is important to counsel patients that even with excellent Snellen acuity recovery, subtle deficits in [[contrast sensitivity]], [[color vision]], [[stereopsis]], and visual field sensitivity frequently persist and may affect quality of life and functional vision.

Prognostic factors associated with **worse visual outcomes** include:
- Low presenting visual acuity (especially no light perception)
- Absence of pain (atypical feature)
- AQP4-IgG seropositivity (NMOSD)
- Greater RNFL/GCIPL thinning on OCT
- African American or Afro-Caribbean descent
- Recurrent episodes in the same eye

## Follow-Up and Monitoring

Appropriate follow-up for patients with optic neuritis includes:

1. **Acute follow-up (1–2 weeks)**: Reassess visual acuity, color vision, pupillary function, and visual fields. Ensure no progressive worsening (which would suggest atypical etiology).
2. **Subacute follow-up (4–6 weeks)**: Confirm onset of visual recovery. If no recovery is evident, reconsider diagnosis and expand workup.
3. **Chronic follow-up (3–6 months)**: Assess final visual outcome. Obtain OCT to document RNFL/GCIPL thinning as a baseline for future comparison.
4. **Long-term monitoring**: Annual comprehensive eye examinations with attention to signs of recurrence, fellow eye involvement, and development of MS-related findings (e.g., [[internuclear ophthalmoplegia]], [[nystagmus]], [[intermediate uveitis]]/[[pars planitis]]).

## Special Considerations

### Optic Neuritis in Children

Pediatric optic neuritis differs from adult-onset disease in several important respects:
- Higher rate of bilateral involvement (up to 60%)
- More commonly preceded by viral illness or vaccination
- More frequently associated with disc edema (papillitis)
- Higher association with MOGAD than with MS
- Generally better visual prognosis than adult-onset disease
- Lower risk of MS conversion compared to adults (though still significant)

### Optic Neuritis in Pregnancy

Optic neuritis may occur during pregnancy or the postpartum period. Management considerations include:
- MRI without gadolinium can be performed if clinically necessary
- IV methylprednisolone is generally considered safe in pregnancy when indicated
- DMT decisions require careful risk-benefit analysis in consultation with neurology and obstetrics

### Chronic Relapsing Inflammatory Optic Neuropathy (CRION)

[[Chronic relapsing inflammatory optic neuropathy]] is a distinct entity characterized by recurrent optic neuritis that is steroid-responsive but relapses upon steroid withdrawal. It is seronegative for both AQP4-IgG and MOG-IgG and requires long-term immunosuppressive therapy to prevent recurrence and cumulative visual loss.

---

## Summary

Optic neuritis remains one of the most clinically significant conditions encountered in eye care practice. Its diagnosis requires careful clinical assessment, appropriate ancillary testing, and a thorough understanding of the spectrum of etiologies that can produce optic nerve inflammation. The distinction between typical MS-associated optic neuritis and atypical/antibody-mediated forms is critical, as it fundamentally alters the treatment approach and prognosis. Optometrists play a vital role in the initial recognition, diagnosis, and co-management of optic neuritis, and must maintain a high index of suspicion for atypical features that warrant expanded investigation and urgent referral.
