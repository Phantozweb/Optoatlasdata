

# Perimetry

**Perimetry** is the systematic measurement and quantification of the [[visual field]], defined as the entire area in which objects can be perceived when the eye is fixated on a central point. As a cornerstone of ophthalmic and optometric diagnostic testing, perimetry enables clinicians to map the topography of visual sensitivity across the [[retina]], detect and monitor [[scotoma]]ta (areas of reduced or absent vision), and track the progression of numerous ocular and neurological diseases. The term derives from the Greek *peri* (around) and *metron* (measure). Perimetry is indispensable in the diagnosis and management of [[glaucoma]], [[optic neuropathy]], [[retinal disease]], and lesions along the entire [[visual pathway]] from the [[retina]] to the [[visual cortex]].

---

## Historical Background

The concept of visual field assessment dates to antiquity. [[Hippocrates]] is credited with early descriptions of [[hemianopia]]. In the 19th century, [[Albrecht von Graefe]] formalized the clinical evaluation of the visual field, and in 1856, he introduced the use of a simple arc perimeter. The development of the [[Goldmann perimeter]] in the mid-20th century by Hans Goldmann represented a major advance, providing a standardized kinetic perimetry instrument with controlled stimulus parameters. The advent of computerized [[static automated perimetry]] (SAP) in the 1970s and 1980s—most notably the [[Humphrey Field Analyzer]] (HFA) developed by the Allergan Humphrey company—revolutionized the field by enabling reproducible, quantitative, and statistically analyzed visual field testing. Since then, numerous additional technologies have emerged, including [[short-wavelength automated perimetry]] (SWAP), [[frequency doubling technology]] (FDT) perimetry, [[microperimetry]], and [[multifocal visual evoked potential]] (mfVEP)-based perimetry.

## Anatomy and Pathophysiology of the Visual Field

### Neuroanatomical Basis

The visual field is a functional representation of the [[retina]]'s sensitivity to light stimuli. Each point in the visual field corresponds to a specific retinal locus, and the neural signal generated at that locus traverses a well-defined anatomical pathway:

1. **[[Photoreceptors]]** ([[rods]] and [[cones]]) in the outer retina transduce light into electrochemical signals.
2. Signals pass through [[bipolar cells]] and are modulated by [[horizontal cells]] and [[amacrine cells]] in the inner retina.
3. **[[Retinal ganglion cells]]** (RGCs) integrate these signals and transmit them via their axons, which form the [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL).
4. RGC axons converge at the [[optic disc]] to form the [[optic nerve]] (cranial nerve II).
5. The optic nerves from each eye partially decussate at the [[optic chiasm]], where nasal fibers cross to the contralateral side.
6. Post-chiasmal fibers continue as the [[optic tract]] to the [[lateral geniculate nucleus]] (LGN) of the [[thalamus]].
7. From the LGN, fibers project via the [[optic radiations]] (including [[Meyer's loop]] in the [[temporal lobe]] and the parietal radiations) to the [[primary visual cortex]] (V1, [[Brodmann area 17]]) in the [[occipital lobe]].

Damage at any point along this pathway produces characteristic and often localizable visual field defects.

### Pathophysiology of Visual Field Loss

Visual field defects detected by perimetry arise from diverse pathological mechanisms:

| **Mechanism** | **Examples** | **Typical Visual Field Defect** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Retinal ganglion cell]] loss | [[Primary open-angle glaucoma]] (POAG), [[normal-tension glaucoma]] | [[Arcuate scotoma]], [[nasal step]], [[paracentral scotoma]] |
| [[Optic nerve]] compression or inflammation | [[Optic neuritis]], [[papilledema]], [[optic nerve glioma]] | Central or cecocentral scotoma, enlarged [[blind spot]] |
| Chiasmal lesion | [[Pituitary adenoma]], [[craniopharyngioma]] | [[Bitemporal hemianopia]] |
| Post-chiasmal lesion | [[Cerebrovascular accident]] (stroke), [[brain tumor]] | [[Homonymous hemianopia]], [[quadrantanopia]] |
| Retinal vascular occlusion | [[Branch retinal artery occlusion]] (BRAO), [[central retinal vein occlusion]] (CRVO) | Altitudinal defect, sectoral loss |
| Photoreceptor degeneration | [[Retinitis pigmentosa]], [[cone-rod dystrophy]] | Peripheral constriction, ring scotoma |
| Macular disease | [[Age-related macular degeneration]] (AMD) | Central scotoma |
| Medication toxicity | [[Hydroxychloroquine]] toxicity, [[ethambutol]] toxicity | Paracentral or central scotoma, bitemporal defect |

The hallmark of [[glaucoma]]tous visual field loss is the preferential damage to specific populations of RGCs—particularly the larger, magnocellular-pathway RGCs—resulting in characteristic patterns such as the [[nasal step of Rønne]], [[arcuate (Bjerrum) scotoma]], [[paracentral scotoma]], and progressive generalized depression. The structure-function relationship between [[RNFL]] thickness (measured by [[optical coherence tomography]]) and perimetric sensitivity is a critical concept: significant structural damage (estimated at 25–40% RGC loss) may precede detectable perimetric loss, a phenomenon termed **pre-perimetric glaucoma**.

## Types of Perimetry

### Kinetic Perimetry

In **kinetic perimetry**, a stimulus of defined size and luminance is moved from a non-seeing area toward a seeing area (or vice versa) along predetermined meridians. The point at which the patient first perceives the stimulus defines an **isopter**—a contour line connecting points of equal visual sensitivity. Multiple isopters are plotted using stimuli of varying size and intensity.

- **[[Goldmann perimeter]]**: The gold standard for kinetic perimetry. Uses a hemispheric bowl with standardized background illumination (31.5 apostilbs). Stimulus size is designated by Roman numerals (I–V) and intensity by Arabic numerals and letters (e.g., V4e, I2e). Requires a skilled perimetrist to manually present stimuli and record responses.
- **Advantages**: Excellent for mapping the peripheral visual field, detecting neurological defects (e.g., hemianopias), and testing patients who cannot perform automated perimetry (e.g., children, cognitively impaired individuals).
- **Limitations**: Operator-dependent, less reproducible, and less amenable to statistical analysis compared to automated methods.

### Static Automated Perimetry (SAP)

In **[[static automated perimetry]]**, stimuli of fixed location but variable intensity are presented at predetermined points across the visual field. The patient indicates perception by pressing a response button. The instrument determines the **threshold** (minimum stimulus intensity perceived) at each test location using staircase or adaptive algorithms.

- **[[Humphrey Field Analyzer]] (HFA)**: The most widely used SAP instrument globally. Employs a Goldmann size III (0.43° diameter) white stimulus on a white background (31.5 apostilbs). Key test protocols include:
  - **24-2**: Tests 54 points within the central 24° (with two nasal points extending to 30°). Standard for glaucoma monitoring.
  - **30-2**: Tests 76 points within the central 30°. Broader coverage.
  - **10-2**: Tests 68 points within the central 10° with 2° spacing. Essential for advanced glaucoma, macular disease, and [[hydroxychloroquine]] toxicity screening.
  - **24-2C**: A newer protocol adding 10 additional points in the macula to the standard 24-2 grid, improving detection of early macular damage in glaucoma.

- **Thresholding Strategies**:
  - **Full Threshold**: Original bracketing strategy; accurate but time-consuming (12–18 minutes per eye).
  - **FASTPAC**: Faster but less precise.
  - **[[SITA Standard]]** (Swedish Interactive Thresholding Algorithm): Uses Bayesian statistical modeling and maximum likelihood estimation to reduce test time (~50% faster than Full Threshold) while maintaining accuracy.
  - **[[SITA Fast]]**: Further reduces test time with slightly reduced precision.
  - **[[SITA Faster]]**: The newest iteration; completes testing in approximately 2 minutes with comparable sensitivity to SITA Standard for detecting moderate-to-advanced defects.
  - **ZEST** (Zippy Estimation by Sequential Testing): An alternative Bayesian strategy used in some instruments.

- **[[Octopus perimeter]]** (Haag-Streit): Another widely used SAP instrument, particularly in Europe. Offers similar test strategies (Dynamic, Normal, TOP [Tendency Oriented Perimetry]) and unique analytical features such as the **Octopus Cluster Analysis** and **Eyesuite** progression software.

### Suprathreshold Perimetry (Screening Perimetry)

Suprathreshold testing presents stimuli at intensities above the expected threshold for each location. The patient either sees or does not see the stimulus. This approach is faster but provides less detailed information. It is used primarily for screening purposes (e.g., detecting gross visual field defects in driver's license evaluations or initial screening for neurological disease).

### Specialized Perimetric Techniques

| **Technique** | **Principle** | **Primary Clinical Application** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Short-wavelength automated perimetry]] (SWAP)** | Uses a blue (440 nm) stimulus on a bright yellow background to isolate the [[short-wavelength-sensitive (S-cone)]] pathway | Early glaucoma detection (targets the small bistratified RGCs, which may be damaged early) |
| **[[Frequency doubling technology]] (FDT) perimetry** | Presents a low spatial frequency sinusoidal grating undergoing high temporal frequency counterphase flicker, exploiting the frequency doubling illusion mediated by magnocellular RGCs | Glaucoma screening and detection; rapid testing |
| **[[Matrix perimetry]] (Humphrey Matrix / FDT2)** | Second-generation FDT with a denser test grid (24-2 pattern) and improved spatial resolution | More detailed assessment than original FDT |
| **[[Microperimetry]] (fundus perimetry)** | Combines perimetry with simultaneous [[fundus imaging]] and real-time eye tracking, allowing precise correlation of sensitivity with retinal anatomy | [[Macular disease]] (AMD, [[macular hole]], [[epiretinal membrane]]), [[central serous chorioretinopathy]], low vision rehabilitation |
| **[[Multifocal visual evoked potential]] (mfVEP)** | Objectively measures visual cortex responses to multiple simultaneous stimuli; does not require subjective patient responses | Objective visual field assessment in unreliable patients, [[optic neuritis]], medicolegal cases |
| **[[Flicker-defined form]] (FDF) perimetry** | Uses flickering random-dot stimuli to define a shape, targeting magnocellular pathway | Early glaucoma detection |
| **[[Pupil perimetry]] (chromatic pupillometry)** | Measures pupillary responses to localized stimuli | Objective assessment; research applications |

## Clinical Indications for Perimetry

Perimetry is indicated in a wide range of clinical scenarios:

- **[[Glaucoma]]**: Diagnosis, staging, and monitoring of progression in all forms of glaucoma (POAG, [[angle-closure glaucoma]], [[normal-tension glaucoma]], [[secondary glaucoma]])
- **[[Optic nerve]] disorders**: [[Optic neuritis]], [[ischemic optic neuropathy]] (arteritic and non-arteritic), [[papilledema]], [[optic atrophy]], compressive optic neuropathies
- **Neurological visual pathway lesions**: [[Pituitary tumors]], [[craniopharyngioma]], [[meningioma]], [[cerebrovascular accident]], [[multiple sclerosis]], [[traumatic brain injury]]
- **Retinal diseases**: [[Retinitis pigmentosa]], [[retinal detachment]], [[retinal vascular occlusions]], [[macular degeneration]]
- **Drug toxicity monitoring**: [[Hydroxychloroquine]]/[[chloroquine]] retinal toxicity screening (per [[American Academy of Ophthalmology]] guidelines, 10-2 visual field testing is recommended), [[ethambutol]] toxicity, [[vigabatrin]] toxicity
- **Functional (non-organic) visual loss**: Differentiation from organic disease
- **Legal and occupational requirements**: Driver's license visual field standards, disability assessment, aviation medical certification

## Performing Perimetry: Technique and Patient Preparation

### Pre-Test Considerations

1. **Refractive correction**: Appropriate near correction must be placed in the trial lens holder for the test distance (typically 33 cm for the HFA). Incorrect correction causes artifactual generalized depression and may simulate or mask scotomata. The instrument typically calculates the required add based on the patient's age and spherical equivalent.
2. **Pupil size**: A [[pupil]] diameter of ≥3 mm is generally recommended. [[Miosis]] (e.g., from [[pilocarpine]]) can reduce retinal illumination and artifactually depress sensitivity. Pupil size should be documented.
3. **Lens rim artifact**: The trial lens rim can produce an artifactual arcuate scotoma, particularly with high-powered lenses. Proper lens positioning and use of appropriate lens sizes are essential.
4. **Ptosis**: Upper [[eyelid]] [[ptosis]] can cause a superior visual field defect. Taping the lid may be necessary.
5. **Patient instruction**: Clear, standardized instructions are critical. The patient should understand to fixate on the central target, press the response button whenever a light is perceived (even if dim or peripheral), and maintain a comfortable but alert posture.
6. **Testing environment**: The room should be dimly lit (not completely dark) to allow adaptation to the bowl luminance.

### Test Execution

- The untested eye is occluded with an opaque patch.
- The patient is positioned comfortably at the instrument with proper chin and forehead rest alignment.
- A fixation target (central or eccentric for patients with central scotomata) is presented.
- The instrument monitors fixation via gaze tracking (infrared camera in modern instruments), [[Heijl-Krakau method]] (periodic presentation of a stimulus in the blind spot), or both.
- Stimuli are presented according to the selected algorithm, and the patient responds by pressing a button.
- Test duration varies: SITA Standard 24-2 typically takes 5–7 minutes per eye; SITA Faster may take 2–3 minutes.

## Interpretation of Perimetric Results

Accurate interpretation of perimetric output is a critical clinical skill. The HFA printout (single field analysis) contains several key components:

### Reliability Indices

| **Index** | **Definition** | **Acceptable Threshold** |
|---|---|---|
| **Fixation losses (FL)** | Proportion of times the patient responds to a stimulus presented in the [[blind spot]] (Heijl-Krakau method) | <20% (or <33% in some guidelines) |
| **False positives (FP)** | Patient presses the button when no stimulus is presented (trigger-happy behavior) | <15% (SITA); high FP causes artifactually elevated sensitivity ("white scotoma" or "clover-leaf" pattern) |
| **False negatives (FN)** | Patient fails to respond to a stimulus brighter than one previously seen at the same location | <33%; elevated FN may indicate inattention but is also associated with true visual field loss |
| **Gaze tracker** | Graphical record of eye position during testing | Minimal upward deflections (indicating fixation losses) |

A test with poor reliability indices should be interpreted with extreme caution and may need to be repeated.

### Numerical and Graphical Displays

1. **Raw Sensitivity (Threshold) Values**: Numerical display of measured threshold (in decibels, dB) at each test point. Normal foveal sensitivity is approximately 34–36 dB in young adults and declines with age. Peripheral sensitivity is normally lower than central sensitivity.

2. **Grayscale Map**: A graphical representation where darker areas indicate lower sensitivity. Useful for pattern recognition but can be misleading; should never be used as the sole basis for interpretation.

3. **Total Deviation (TD) Plot**: Shows the difference (in dB) between the patient's measured sensitivity at each point and the age-matched normative value. Highlights both localized and generalized depression.

4. **Total Deviation Probability Plot**: Statistical significance of each TD value, displayed as symbols:
   - Blank: within normal limits
   - Light gray: p < 5%
   - Dark gray: p < 2%
   - Darker: p < 1%
   - Black: p < 0.5%

5. **Pattern Deviation (PD) Plot**: Adjusts the total deviation values by removing the overall height (generalized depression component), thereby highlighting localized (focal) defects. This is particularly important because generalized depression can be caused by media opacities (e.g., [[cataract]]) or refractive error, which are not indicative of glaucomatous damage.

6. **Pattern Deviation Probability Plot**: Statistical significance of PD values, using the same symbol scheme as the TD probability plot.

### Global Indices

| **Index** | **Description** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Mean Deviation]] (MD)** | Weighted average of all TD values; represents overall field sensitivity relative to age-matched normals | Negative values indicate overall depression. Used for staging and progression analysis. Normal: approximately 0 ± 2 dB |
| **[[Pattern Standard Deviation]] (PSD)** | Measures the irregularity or localized variability of the visual field after correcting for generalized depression | Elevated PSD indicates focal loss (e.g., scotoma). Low PSD with low MD suggests diffuse loss |
| **[[Visual Field Index]] (VFI)** | Expressed as a percentage (0–100%); represents the overall visual field status weighted toward central points | 100% = normal field; 0% = perimetrically blind. Used in VFI trend analysis for progression |
| **[[Glaucoma Hemifield Test]] (GHT)** | Compares corresponding sectors in the superior and inferior hemifields | Results: "Within Normal Limits," "Borderline," "Outside Normal Limits," "General Reduction of Sensitivity," "Abnormally High Sensitivity" |

### Recognizing Common Visual Field Defect Patterns

| **Pattern** | **Description** | **Likely Etiology** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Arcuate scotoma]] (Bjerrum scotoma)** | Arc-shaped defect following the arcuate nerve fiber bundle, typically from the blind spot curving above or below fixation toward the nasal horizontal meridian | [[Glaucoma]], [[ischemic optic neuropathy]] |
| **[[Nasal step]]** | Asymmetric sensitivity across the nasal horizontal meridian | [[Glaucoma]] (due to differential damage to superior vs. inferior arcuate bundles) |
| **[[Paracentral scotoma]]** | Small, isolated area of depressed sensitivity within the central 10° | Early [[glaucoma]], [[optic neuritis]] |
| **Central scotoma** | Loss of sensitivity at or near fixation | [[Optic neuritis]], [[macular disease]], [[toxic optic neuropathy]] |
| **Cecocentral scotoma** | Scotoma involving both the fixation point and the blind spot | [[Toxic/nutritional optic neuropathy]], [[Leber hereditary optic neuropathy]] |
| **Enlarged blind spot** | Expansion of the physiological blind spot | [[Papilledema]], [[optic disc drusen]], [[peripapillary atrophy]] |
| **Altitudinal defect** | Loss of the entire superior or inferior hemifield, respecting the horizontal meridian | [[Anterior ischemic optic neuropathy]] (AION), [[branch retinal artery occlusion]] |
| **[[Bitemporal hemianopia]]** | Loss of both temporal hemifields, respecting the vertical meridian | [[Chiasmal]] lesion (e.g., [[pituitary adenoma]]) |
| **[[Homonymous hemianopia]]** | Loss of the same hemifield in both eyes (e.g., right homonymous hemianopia) | Post-chiasmal lesion (e.g., [[stroke]], [[tumor]]) contralateral to the field loss |
| **[[Quadrantanopia]]** | Loss of one quadrant of the visual field | Lesion in [[optic radiations]] (superior quadrantanopia = temporal lobe/[[Meyer's loop]]; inferior quadrantanopia = parietal lobe) |
| **Generalized depression** | Diffuse reduction in sensitivity across the entire field | [[Cataract]], [[miosis]], uncorrected refractive error, fatigue, or diffuse retinal/optic nerve disease |
| **Ring scotoma** | Annular zone of depressed sensitivity surrounding fixation | [[Retinitis pigmentosa]], advanced [[glaucoma]] |
| **Constricted visual field (tunnel vision)** | Severe peripheral field loss with preserved central island | Advanced [[glaucoma]], advanced [[retinitis pigmentosa]], [[functional visual loss]] (non-organic) |

## Perimetry in Glaucoma Management

### Diagnosis

Perimetry is one of the three pillars of glaucoma diagnosis, alongside [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) measurement and [[optic nerve head]] assessment (including [[optical coherence tomography]] [OCT] of the RNFL and [[ganglion cell complex]]). The diagnosis of glaucomatous visual field loss typically requires:

- A reproducible defect on at least two consecutive reliable visual field tests
- A defect pattern consistent with glaucoma (e.g., arcuate scotoma, nasal step, paracentral scotoma)
- Correlation with structural findings (RNFL thinning, optic disc cupping)

The **Hodapp-Parrish-Anderson (HPA) classification** is widely used to stage glaucomatous visual field loss:

| **Stage** | **Criteria (HFA 24-2 or 30-2)** |
|---|---|
| **Early** | MD better than −6 dB; fewer than 25% of points depressed below the 5% level on PD; fewer than 10 points depressed below the 1% level; no point within 5° of fixation with sensitivity <15 dB |
| **Moderate** | MD between −6 and −12 dB; 25–50% of points depressed below the 5% level on PD; one hemifield with point(s) within 5° of fixation with sensitivity <15 dB but not both hemifields |
| **Severe (Advanced)** | MD worse than −12 dB; more than 50% of points depressed below the 5% level on PD; any point within the central 5° with sensitivity of 0 dB; points within 5° of fixation with sensitivity <15 dB in both hemifields |

### Progression Analysis

Detecting visual field progression is essential for guiding treatment decisions. Two primary approaches are used:

1. **Event-based analysis (e.g., [[Guided Progression Analysis]] [GPA] on HFA)**: Compares each follow-up test to a baseline (average of two reliable baseline fields). Points that show statistically significant deterioration are flagged. If deterioration is confirmed on consecutive tests, progression is declared. GPA classifies results as "No Progression Detected," "Possible Progression," or "Likely Progression."

2. **Trend-based analysis (e.g., VFI regression)**: Plots the VFI (or MD) over time and performs linear regression to estimate the rate of change (e.g., %/year or dB/year). This allows prediction of future visual field status and estimation of time to functional disability, which is invaluable for clinical decision-making.

**Recommended testing frequency**: The [[World Glaucoma Association]] and other expert bodies recommend performing at least **six visual field tests in the first two years** after diagnosis to establish a reliable baseline and detect rapid progressors early. Thereafter, testing frequency is guided by the rate of progression and risk profile (typically 1–3 tests per year).

## Perimetry in Neuro-Ophthalmology

Perimetry is indispensable in the evaluation of neurological visual pathway disorders. Key principles include:

- **Respect for the vertical meridian**: Chiasmal and post-chiasmal lesions produce defects that respect the vertical midline, a hallmark of neurological (as opposed to retinal or optic nerve) disease.
- **Congruity**: Post-chiasmal lesions closer to the [[occipital cortex]] tend to produce more congruent (similar in both eyes) defects, while lesions closer to the [[optic tract]] produce less congruent defects.
- **Goldmann kinetic perimetry** remains valuable in neuro-ophthalmology for mapping the full extent of peripheral defects and for testing patients who cannot perform automated perimetry.
- **Confrontation visual field testing** (e.g., finger counting, red target comparison) serves as a rapid bedside screening tool but has low sensitivity for detecting subtle defects.

## Artifacts and Sources of Error in Perimetry

Clinicians must be vigilant for artifacts that can mimic or obscure true visual field defects:

| **Artifact** | **Cause** | **Appearance** | **Solution** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lens rim artifact** | Trial lens rim blocking peripheral stimuli | Arcuate or ring-shaped peripheral depression | Proper lens positioning; use correct lens size |
| **Ptosis artifact** | Drooping upper eyelid | Superior field depression | Tape the eyelid |
| **Incorrect refractive correction** | Wrong lens power or missing correction | Generalized depression, especially centrally | Verify and correct the lens |
| **Media opacity** | [[Cataract]], [[corneal opacity]] | Generalized depression; reduced sensitivity | Interpret with caution; note on report; consider PD plot |
| **Learning effect** | Patient unfamiliarity with the test (first-time testers) | Artifactually depressed first field, improvement on subsequent tests | Repeat testing; use first test as practice |
| **Fatigue effect** | Declining attention during prolonged testing | Peripheral sensitivity loss, especially in the second half of the test | Use faster algorithms (SITA Fast/Faster); encourage breaks |
| **Trigger-happy patient** | Excessive false-positive responses | Abnormally high sensitivity values; "white scotoma" | Re-instruct patient; repeat test |
| **Inattentive patient** | High false-negative rate | Scattered depressed points; unreliable field | Re-instruct; ensure comfort; repeat test |
| **Miosis** | Small pupil (<2 mm) | Generalized depression | Dilate if appropriate; document pupil size |

## Emerging Technologies and Future Directions

The field of perimetry continues to evolve rapidly:

- **[[Virtual reality (VR) perimetry]]**: Head-mounted VR devices (e.g., **imo®**, **Virtual Field**, **Olleyes VisuALL**) offer portable, self-administered visual field testing. These devices may improve access to perimetry in remote or underserved settings and enable home monitoring.
- **[[Tablet-based perimetry]]**: Applications such as **Melbourne Rapid Fields (MRF)** and **Eyecatcher** use tablet computers for visual field assessment, offering portability and reduced cost.
- **Artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning**: AI algorithms are being developed to improve detection of progression, predict future visual field loss from structural OCT data, and reduce test-retest variability. Deep learning models can estimate visual field maps from OCT scans (structure-function mapping).
- **Structural-functional integration**: Combined analysis of OCT structural data and perimetric functional data is increasingly used to improve diagnostic accuracy and progression detection. The concept of the **structure-function map** links specific RNFL sectors to corresponding visual field regions.
- **Faster and more patient-friendly algorithms**: Continued refinement of thresholding strategies aims to reduce test time while maintaining or improving accuracy, addressing the significant issue of patient fatigue and compliance.
- **Objective perimetry**: Techniques such as **[[multifocal electroretinography]]** (mfERG), **mfVEP**, and **pupil perimetry** provide objective measures of visual field function that do not depend on patient responses, which is particularly valuable in pediatric, cognitively impaired, or malingering populations.

## Role of Perimetry in Treatment Monitoring

While perimetry is primarily a diagnostic tool, its results directly influence treatment decisions:

- **[[Glaucoma]]**: Documented visual field progression may prompt escalation of therapy—including adjustment of [[topical hypotensive agents]], [[selective laser trabeculoplasty]] (SLT), or [[glaucoma surgery]] (e.g., [[trabeculectomy]], [[tube shunt surgery]], [[minimally invasive glaucoma surgery]] [MIGS]). The rate of progression (dB/year or VFI%/year) is a key metric in determining the aggressiveness of treatment.
- **[[Hydroxychloroquine]] toxicity**: Detection of characteristic paracentral scotomata on 10-2 perimetry (often in conjunction with [[OCT]] and [[multifocal ERG]]) may necessitate drug discontinuation.
- **Neurological lesions**: Visual field defects may prompt urgent neuroimaging ([[MRI]] of the brain and orbits with contrast) and neurosurgical consultation.
- **[[Pituitary adenoma]]**: Serial perimetry is used to monitor for progression or improvement of [[bitemporal hemianopia]] before and after surgical or medical treatment.
- **Driving and occupational fitness**: Perimetric results are used to determine whether patients meet legal visual field requirements for driving and certain occupations.

---

## Summary

Perimetry remains an irreplaceable tool in the comprehensive assessment of visual function. Its ability to detect, characterize, and monitor visual field defects makes it essential in the management of [[glaucoma]], neuro-ophthalmic disease, retinal disorders, and drug toxicity. Mastery of perimetric techniques, interpretation of results, recognition of artifacts, and understanding of the underlying neuroanatomy are fundamental competencies for every optometrist and ophthalmologist. As technology advances—with virtual reality platforms, artificial intelligence, and objective testing methods—perimetry will continue to evolve, offering greater accessibility, accuracy, and clinical utility.
