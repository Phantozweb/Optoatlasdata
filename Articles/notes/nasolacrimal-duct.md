

# Nasolacrimal Duct

---

The **nasolacrimal duct** (NLD), also referred to as the **ductus nasolacrimalis**, is a critical anatomical conduit within the [[lacrimal drainage system]] responsible for the transport of [[tears]] from the [[lacrimal sac]] into the [[inferior meatus]] of the [[nasal cavity]]. Functionally, the nasolacrimal duct represents the terminal segment of the lacrimal outflow pathway and plays an indispensable role in maintaining [[ocular surface]] homeostasis by ensuring the continuous drainage of the [[tear film]]. Obstruction, infection, or congenital malformation of the nasolacrimal duct constitutes one of the most common causes of [[epiphora]] (excessive tearing) encountered in both pediatric and adult clinical practice. A thorough understanding of the nasolacrimal duct's embryology, anatomy, physiology, pathophysiology, and the spectrum of associated clinical conditions is essential for every [[optometrist]] and [[ophthalmologist]] engaged in comprehensive eye care.

---

## Embryology and Development

The nasolacrimal duct develops from the [[nasolacrimal groove]] (also known as the naso-optic fissure), a surface ectodermal invagination located between the lateral nasal process and the [[maxillary process]] of the developing embryo. During the **sixth week of gestation**, a solid cord of [[ectodermal]] cells buries itself beneath the surface and begins to canalize in a cranial-to-caudal direction. This process of canalization is typically completed by the **eighth month of gestation**, although the distal (inferior) end of the duct is the last segment to open.

A thin mucosal membrane known as the **[[valve of Hasner]]** (plica lacrimalis) covers the distal orifice of the nasolacrimal duct at its opening into the inferior meatus. In approximately **30–70%** of full-term neonates, this membrane is patent at birth. However, in a significant proportion of infants, the valve of Hasner remains imperforate, resulting in **[[congenital nasolacrimal duct obstruction]]** (CNLDO), the most common congenital anomaly of the lacrimal drainage system. Failure of canalization at other levels, or the development of bony abnormalities within the [[nasolacrimal canal]], can also contribute to congenital obstruction.

### Key Embryological Milestones

| Gestational Period | Developmental Event |
|---|---|
| Week 6 | Solid ectodermal cord forms in the nasolacrimal groove |
| Weeks 7–12 | Progressive canalization begins (cranial to caudal) |
| Month 5–6 | Upper portions of the lacrimal drainage system are patent |
| Month 7–8 | Canalization reaches the distal nasolacrimal duct |
| Birth | Valve of Hasner opens spontaneously in ~50–70% of neonates |

## Anatomy

### Gross Anatomy

The nasolacrimal duct is a membranous canal enclosed within a bony channel called the **[[nasolacrimal canal]]** (osseous nasolacrimal canal). It extends from the inferior aspect of the [[lacrimal sac]] to its opening (ostium) beneath the [[inferior nasal turbinate]] (inferior concha) in the lateral wall of the nasal cavity.

- **Length:** Approximately **12–18 mm** in adults (average ~12 mm for the intraosseous portion; total membranous length including the submucosal segment may reach 18–24 mm).
- **Diameter:** Approximately **3–5 mm** at its widest point.
- **Course:** The duct descends in a slightly posterolateral direction from the lacrimal sac fossa, passing through the nasolacrimal canal formed by the [[maxillary bone]], the [[lacrimal bone]], and the [[inferior nasal concha]].

The nasolacrimal duct terminates at the **[[valve of Hasner]]**, a mucosal fold that acts as a one-way valve preventing retrograde airflow from the nasal cavity into the lacrimal sac. The ostium of the nasolacrimal duct opens into the **anterior third of the inferior meatus**, typically located approximately **30 mm** posterior to the external nares.

### Surrounding Structures

| Structure | Relationship to Nasolacrimal Duct |
|---|---|
| [[Lacrimal sac]] | Directly superior; continuous with the duct |
| [[Maxillary sinus]] | Lateral wall of the nasolacrimal canal borders the medial wall of the maxillary sinus |
| [[Inferior nasal turbinate]] | The duct opens beneath this structure |
| [[Maxillary bone]] | Forms the primary bony wall of the nasolacrimal canal |
| [[Lacrimal bone]] | Contributes to the superior portion of the nasolacrimal canal |
| [[Anterior ethmoidal air cells]] | May be in close proximity superiorly |

### Mucosal Lining and Valves

The nasolacrimal duct is lined by a **bilaminar columnar [[epithelium]]** with interspersed [[goblet cells]] that secrete [[mucin]], facilitating the smooth transit of tears. The subepithelial [[lamina propria]] is richly vascularized with a prominent **cavernous venous plexus** (analogous to erectile tissue), which can engorge in response to inflammation, allergic stimuli, or autonomic changes, thereby narrowing the ductal lumen and contributing to functional or partial obstruction.

Several mucosal folds and valves have been described along the lacrimal drainage pathway, though their functional significance varies:

- **[[Valve of Rosenmüller]]** (plica lacrimalis superior): Located at the junction of the [[common canaliculus]] and the lacrimal sac; prevents reflux from the sac into the canaliculi.
- **[[Valve of Krause]]**: Located at the junction of the lacrimal sac and the nasolacrimal duct.
- **[[Valve of Taillefer]]**: Located in the mid-portion of the nasolacrimal duct.
- **[[Valve of Hasner]]** (plica lacrimalis inferior): Located at the distal opening of the nasolacrimal duct into the inferior meatus; the most clinically significant valve.

### Vascular Supply and Innervation

The nasolacrimal duct receives its arterial blood supply from branches of the **[[infraorbital artery]]** (a branch of the [[maxillary artery]]) and the **[[angular artery]]** (a terminal branch of the [[facial artery]]). Venous drainage follows the corresponding veins into the [[anterior facial vein]] and the [[pterygoid venous plexus]]. Sensory innervation is provided by the **[[infraorbital nerve]]** (a branch of the [[maxillary division of the trigeminal nerve]], V2).

## Physiology of Tear Drainage

The nasolacrimal duct functions as the final conduit in the **[[lacrimal drainage system]]**, which operates through a combination of gravity, capillary action, and an active pumping mechanism known as the **[[lacrimal pump]]** (Rosengren-Doane mechanism, also called the Horner-Doane mechanism).

### The Lacrimal Pump Mechanism

1. **Blinking:** During eyelid closure, the [[orbicularis oculi muscle]] (specifically the [[Horner's muscle]] or pars lacrimalis) contracts, compressing the [[lacrimal sac]] and creating negative pressure within the sac.
2. **Tear aspiration:** Upon eyelid opening, the lacrimal sac expands, generating a suction effect that draws tears from the [[conjunctival sac]] through the [[puncta]] and [[canaliculi]] into the lacrimal sac.
3. **Gravity-assisted drainage:** Tears accumulated in the lacrimal sac are propelled downward through the nasolacrimal duct by gravity, hydrostatic pressure, and mucosal capillary action.
4. **Nasal delivery:** Tears exit through the valve of Hasner into the inferior meatus, where they are absorbed by the nasal mucosa or evaporate.

The **rate of tear drainage** is approximately **0.5–1.0 μL/min** under basal conditions, closely matching the rate of [[tear production]] by the [[lacrimal gland]] and accessory lacrimal glands ([[glands of Krause]] and [[glands of Wolfring]]).

## Pathophysiology

Pathology of the nasolacrimal duct can be broadly categorized into **congenital** and **acquired** conditions. The central pathophysiological mechanism in most nasolacrimal duct disorders is **obstruction**, which impedes normal tear drainage and leads to tear stasis, secondary infection, and chronic inflammation.

### Congenital Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction (CNLDO)

[[Congenital nasolacrimal duct obstruction]] is the most prevalent lacrimal disorder in infants, affecting approximately **5–20%** of all newborns. The obstruction is most commonly caused by a persistent membrane at the **valve of Hasner**. Less common causes include bony obstruction within the nasolacrimal canal, [[dacryocystocele]] (lacrimal sac mucocele), and atresia of the nasolacrimal duct.

**Pathophysiological cascade in CNLDO:**
1. Imperforate valve of Hasner → tear stasis in the lacrimal sac
2. Stagnant tears → bacterial colonization (commonly [[Staphylococcus aureus]], [[Streptococcus pneumoniae]], [[Haemophilus influenzae]])
3. Chronic mucoid or mucopurulent discharge → [[dacryocystitis]] (inflammation/infection of the lacrimal sac)
4. Potential complications: [[preseptal cellulitis]], [[orbital cellulitis]] (rare), [[dacryocystocele]] with secondary infection (dacryocystitis neonatorum)

### Acquired Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction (ANLDO)

Acquired nasolacrimal duct obstruction is more common in **middle-aged and elderly women** (female-to-male ratio approximately 3:1). The pathophysiology involves a progressive inflammatory and fibrotic process:

1. **Primary acquired nasolacrimal duct obstruction (PANDO):** Chronic low-grade inflammation of the nasolacrimal duct mucosa → subepithelial fibrosis → progressive luminal narrowing → complete obstruction. The exact etiology is often idiopathic, but hormonal factors, anatomical predisposition (narrower nasolacrimal canal in women), and chronic subclinical infection are implicated.

2. **Secondary acquired nasolacrimal duct obstruction:** Caused by identifiable factors including:

| Etiology | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Infectious** | Chronic [[dacryocystitis]], [[rhinosinusitis]], [[trachoma]], [[herpes simplex virus]] |
| **Inflammatory** | [[Sarcoidosis]], [[granulomatosis with polyangiitis]] (Wegener's), [[sinus histiocytosis]] |
| **Neoplastic** | [[Lacrimal sac tumors]], [[nasal cavity tumors]], [[maxillary sinus carcinoma]], [[lymphoma]] |
| **Traumatic** | [[Naso-orbital fractures]], [[Le Fort fractures]], iatrogenic (sinus surgery, rhinoplasty) |
| **Iatrogenic** | Topical medications (chronic use of [[timolol]], [[pilocarpine]], [[phospholine iodide]]), systemic chemotherapy ([[docetaxel]], [[5-fluorouracil]]), [[radioactive iodine]] therapy |
| **Mechanical** | [[Dacryoliths]] (lacrimal stones), foreign bodies, [[nasal polyps]] |

### Functional Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction

In some patients, the nasolacrimal duct is anatomically patent but functionally insufficient. This condition, termed **functional nasolacrimal duct obstruction** or **lacrimal pump failure**, may result from:
- [[Orbicularis oculi]] weakness (e.g., [[facial nerve palsy]], [[Bell's palsy]])
- [[Eyelid laxity]] or [[ectropion]] causing punctal malposition
- Partial stenosis of the nasolacrimal duct with engorgement of the submucosal venous plexus
- Mucosal edema secondary to allergic or vasomotor [[rhinitis]]

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Congenital Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction

Symptoms typically manifest within the **first few weeks of life** (often by 2–4 weeks of age, coinciding with the onset of reflex tear production):

- **[[Epiphora]]** (excessive tearing): The hallmark symptom; unilateral in ~65% of cases, bilateral in ~35%
- **Mucopurulent discharge:** Chronic mucoid or yellow-green discharge accumulating on the eyelashes, especially upon waking
- **Crusting of the eyelids:** Matting of lashes with dried secretions
- **Reflux of mucopurulent material** upon digital pressure over the [[lacrimal sac]] (positive **[[regurgitation test]]**)
- **Erythema and swelling** over the medial canthal region (if secondary [[dacryocystitis]] develops)
- **Absence of conjunctival injection** (distinguishing CNLDO from [[neonatal conjunctivitis]]/[[ophthalmia neonatorum]])

### Acquired Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction

- **Chronic epiphora:** Progressive, often unilateral initially; tears may overflow onto the cheek (**lacrimation**)
- **Recurrent [[dacryocystitis]]:** Episodes of painful swelling, erythema, and tenderness over the lacrimal sac region (medial to the [[medial canthus]], below the [[medial canthal tendon]])
- **Mucocele formation:** Painless, non-tender swelling below the medial canthal tendon due to chronic mucus accumulation in the lacrimal sac
- **Chronic [[conjunctivitis]]:** Secondary to tear stasis and bacterial colonization
- **Blurred vision:** Intermittent, due to excessive tear meniscus disrupting the [[optical zone]]
- **Skin maceration:** Chronic wetness of the periocular skin leading to [[dermatitis]]
- **Acute dacryocystitis:** Sudden onset of severe pain, erythema, swelling, and warmth over the lacrimal sac; may progress to [[preseptal cellulitis]], [[lacrimal sac abscess]], or rarely [[orbital cellulitis]] and [[fistula]] formation

### Red Flags Requiring Urgent Evaluation

| Finding | Concern |
|---|---|
| Bloody tears ([[hemolacria]]) | Lacrimal sac or nasal tumor |
| Mass above the medial canthal tendon | Lacrimal sac neoplasm (not a simple mucocele) |
| Unilateral nasal obstruction with epistaxis | Nasal or sinus malignancy |
| Rapid onset in a young patient | Inflammatory or neoplastic etiology |
| Failure to respond to standard treatment | Atypical pathology; biopsy may be indicated |

## Diagnostic Modalities

A systematic approach to the evaluation of nasolacrimal duct pathology includes clinical examination, functional testing, and imaging.

### Clinical Examination

1. **External inspection:** Assess for epiphora, discharge, medial canthal swelling, skin changes, eyelid position, and punctal patency.
2. **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]:** Evaluate the [[tear meniscus height]] (normal: ~0.2–0.3 mm; elevated in NLD obstruction), [[puncta]] (size, position, patency), [[conjunctiva]] (injection, papillae, follicles), and [[cornea]] (for secondary exposure or infectious keratitis).
3. **Digital pressure over the lacrimal sac (Crigler massage/regurgitation test):** Reflux of mucoid or mucopurulent material from the puncta confirms lacrimal sac distension and NLD obstruction.

### Functional Tests

| Test | Procedure | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Fluorescein dye disappearance test]] (FDDT)** | Instill [[fluorescein]] into the conjunctival sac; observe residual dye at 5 minutes using [[cobalt blue filter]] | Persistent dye = impaired drainage; asymmetry between eyes is significant |
| **[[Jones I test]] (Primary dye test)** | Instill fluorescein; check for dye in the ipsilateral nasal cavity (using a cotton-tipped applicator under the inferior turbinate) at 5 minutes | Positive (dye recovered) = patent system; Negative = obstruction or pump failure |
| **[[Jones II test]] (Secondary dye test)** | If Jones I is negative, irrigate the lacrimal system with saline; check for fluorescein-stained fluid in the nose | Positive (dye-stained fluid) = partial obstruction or pump failure; Negative (clear fluid) = complete NLD obstruction; No fluid recovered = canalicular obstruction |
| **[[Lacrimal irrigation]] (syringing)** | Cannulate the punctum and canaliculus with a lacrimal cannula; irrigate with sterile saline | Free flow to nose = patent NLD; Reflux from same punctum = canalicular obstruction; Reflux from opposite punctum = NLD obstruction |
| **[[Probing]]** | Pass a lacrimal probe through the canaliculus into the lacrimal sac | Assess for canalicular stenosis, hard stop (contact with lacrimal bone = patent canaliculus), or soft stop (obstruction before the sac) |

### Imaging Studies

1. **[[Dacryocystography]] (DCG):** Contrast-enhanced radiographic study in which radiopaque dye is injected into the lacrimal system via the canaliculus. Provides detailed anatomical information about the site and nature of obstruction. Can be performed with conventional radiography, [[CT dacryocystography]], or [[MRI dacryocystography]].

2. **[[Dacryoscintigraphy]] (lacrimal scintigraphy):** A physiological (functional) test using a drop of [[technetium-99m]] pertechnetate placed in the conjunctival sac. Serial gamma camera images track the transit of the radiotracer through the lacrimal drainage system. Particularly useful for evaluating **functional obstruction** and **partial obstruction** where anatomical patency is preserved but drainage is delayed.

3. **[[Computed tomography]] (CT) of the orbits and sinuses:** Indicated when neoplastic, traumatic, or complex bony pathology is suspected. Provides excellent bony detail of the nasolacrimal canal and surrounding structures.

4. **[[Magnetic resonance imaging]] (MRI):** Superior soft tissue contrast; useful for evaluating lacrimal sac masses, differentiating mucocele from tumor, and assessing extension of neoplastic processes.

5. **[[Nasal endoscopy]]:** Direct visualization of the inferior meatus and the nasolacrimal duct ostium using a rigid or flexible endoscope. Essential for evaluating intranasal pathology (polyps, turbinate hypertrophy, septal deviation) contributing to NLD obstruction and for planning [[endoscopic dacryocystorhinostomy]].

6. **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM) / High-frequency ultrasound:** Occasionally used to assess lacrimal sac dimensions and contents non-invasively.

## Management

### Conservative Management of Congenital Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction

Given that **approximately 90% of CNLDO cases resolve spontaneously by 12 months of age**, initial management is conservative:

1. **[[Crigler massage]] (lacrimal sac massage / hydrostatic massage):**
   - Technique: The caregiver places a clean fingertip over the lacrimal sac (just medial and inferior to the medial canthus) and applies firm, downward (toward the nose) strokes 10 times, 2–3 times daily.
   - Mechanism: Generates hydrostatic pressure within the lacrimal sac, which may rupture the membranous obstruction at the valve of Hasner.
   - Efficacy: Studies report success rates of **60–95%** when performed correctly and consistently during the first year of life.

2. **Topical antibiotics:**
   - Indicated for episodes of mucopurulent discharge.
   - Common agents: [[Erythromycin]] ophthalmic ointment, [[polymyxin B]]/[[trimethoprim]] drops, [[fluoroquinolone]] drops (e.g., [[moxifloxacin]]).
   - Note: Antibiotics treat secondary infection but do not resolve the underlying obstruction.

3. **Warm compresses and lid hygiene:** To manage crusting and periocular skin irritation.

### Surgical Management of Congenital Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction

If CNLDO persists beyond **12 months of age** (some clinicians wait until 13–15 months), surgical intervention is considered:

1. **[[Nasolacrimal duct probing]]:**
   - **First-line surgical treatment** for CNLDO.
   - Performed under brief general anesthesia or, in select cases, in-office with topical anesthesia in younger infants.
   - A blunt-tipped lacrimal probe (e.g., Bowman probe) is passed through the punctum, canaliculus, and lacrimal sac, then advanced through the nasolacrimal duct to perforate the membranous obstruction at the valve of Hasner.
   - **Success rate:** 80–95% for initial probing when performed between 6 and 18 months of age; success decreases with increasing age.

2. **[[Balloon dacryoplasty]] (balloon catheter dilation):**
   - A balloon-tipped catheter is passed through the nasolacrimal duct and inflated to dilate the stenotic segment.
   - Indicated for failed initial probing or in older children (>24 months).
   - **Success rate:** 75–90%.

3. **[[Nasolacrimal duct intubation]] (silicone stent placement):**
   - Bicanalicular or monocanalicular [[silicone stents]] (e.g., Crawford tubes) are threaded through the lacrimal drainage system and left in place for **3–6 months** to maintain patency during healing.
   - Indicated for recurrent obstruction after probing or complex CNLDO.
   - **Success rate:** 80–95%.

4. **[[Dacryocystorhinostomy]] (DCR):**
   - Reserved for refractory cases of CNLDO that fail multiple less invasive procedures.
   - Creates a direct anastomosis between the lacrimal sac and the nasal cavity, bypassing the nasolacrimal duct entirely.
   - Rarely necessary in children.

### Management of Acquired Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction

#### Medical Management

- **Topical and systemic antibiotics** for acute [[dacryocystitis]]:
  - Empiric oral antibiotics: [[Amoxicillin-clavulanate]], [[cephalexin]], or [[fluoroquinolones]] (adults).
  - Topical antibiotics: [[Moxifloxacin]], [[tobramycin]], or [[polymyxin B/trimethoprim]].
  - Severe cases (preseptal/orbital cellulitis): Intravenous antibiotics (e.g., [[ampicillin-sulbactam]], [[ceftriaxone]]).
- **Warm compresses** and **analgesics** for symptomatic relief.
- **Incision and drainage** of a pointing lacrimal sac abscess.
- **Note:** Medical management alone does not resolve the anatomical obstruction; definitive treatment is surgical.

#### Surgical Management

1. **[[Dacryocystorhinostomy]] (DCR):**
   - **Gold standard** surgical treatment for acquired NLD obstruction.
   - **External DCR:**
     - A skin incision is made along the side of the nose; a bony ostium is created in the lacrimal bone and frontal process of the maxilla to create a direct communication between the lacrimal sac and the nasal cavity.
     - Mucosal flaps from the lacrimal sac and nasal mucosa are sutured together to create an epithelialized fistula.
     - **Success rate:** 90–98%.
   - **Endoscopic (endonasal) DCR:**
     - Performed entirely through the nasal cavity using endoscopic visualization; no external skin incision.
     - Advantages: No cutaneous scar, shorter operative time, preservation of the lacrimal pump mechanism, ability to address concurrent nasal pathology.
     - **Success rate:** 85–95% (approaching external DCR rates with experienced surgeons).
   - **Laser-assisted DCR:** Uses [[diode laser]], [[holmium:YAG laser]], or [[KTP laser]] to create the bony ostium endoscopically. Success rates are generally lower (70–85%) than mechanical endoscopic or external DCR.

2. **[[Conjunctivodacryocystorhinostomy]] (CDCR) with [[Jones tube]] placement:**
   - Indicated when canalicular obstruction accompanies NLD obstruction, rendering standard DCR ineffective.
   - A [[Pyrex glass tube]] (Jones tube) is placed to create a direct conduit from the [[conjunctival sac]] (medial canthus) to the nasal cavity.
   - Requires long-term maintenance and monitoring.

3. **[[Dacryocystectomy]]:**
   - Excision of the lacrimal sac.
   - Indicated in elderly or medically unfit patients with chronic dacryocystitis who are not candidates for DCR, or when lacrimal sac neoplasm is suspected.
   - Does not restore tear drainage; epiphora persists.

4. **Silicone intubation alone:**
   - May be attempted for partial NLD obstruction or functional obstruction.
   - Stents are left in place for 3–6 months.
   - Success rates are lower than DCR for complete obstruction.

### Pharmacological Considerations

| Agent | Indication | Route | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Erythromycin]] ointment | CNLDO with discharge | Topical ophthalmic | First-line in neonates |
| [[Moxifloxacin]] 0.5% | Acute/chronic dacryocystitis | Topical ophthalmic | Broad-spectrum fluoroquinolone |
| [[Amoxicillin-clavulanate]] | Acute dacryocystitis | Oral | Covers common pathogens |
| [[Cephalexin]] | Acute dacryocystitis | Oral | Alternative first-generation cephalosporin |
| [[Ampicillin-sulbactam]] | Severe dacryocystitis/cellulitis | Intravenous | Hospital-based management |
| [[Dexamethasone]] 0.1% | Post-DCR inflammation | Topical ophthalmic | Short-term use to reduce scarring |
| [[Mitomycin C]] | Intraoperative adjunct during DCR | Topical application to ostium | Antifibrotic; reduces ostium closure rates |
| [[Fluorometholone]] | Post-procedural inflammation | Topical ophthalmic | Lower IOP risk than dexamethasone |

## Complications of Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction

Untreated or inadequately managed NLD obstruction can lead to a cascade of complications:

- **Chronic [[dacryocystitis]]:** Persistent infection of the lacrimal sac with mucopurulent discharge, recurrent acute exacerbations, and potential fistula formation.
- **Acute dacryocystitis with abscess formation:** May require incision and drainage; risk of [[preseptal cellulitis]] or, rarely, [[orbital cellulitis]] and [[cavernous sinus thrombosis]].
- **[[Lacrimal sac mucocele]] (dacryocystocele):** Distension of the lacrimal sac with mucus; in neonates, may cause nasal obstruction and respiratory distress.
- **Chronic [[conjunctivitis]] and [[blepharitis]]:** Secondary to persistent tear stasis and bacterial colonization.
- **Corneal complications:** Chronic epiphora may predispose to [[corneal erosion]], [[infectious keratitis]], or compromise the safety of intraocular surgery (e.g., [[cataract surgery]], [[intravitreal injections]]) due to the reservoir of bacteria in the obstructed lacrimal sac.
- **Periocular [[dermatitis]] and skin breakdown:** From chronic moisture exposure.

---

## Special Considerations

### Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction and Intraocular Surgery

The presence of NLD obstruction with active [[dacryocystitis]] is a **relative contraindication** to elective intraocular surgery, particularly [[cataract surgery]] and [[corneal transplantation]]. The chronically infected lacrimal sac serves as a bacterial reservoir that significantly increases the risk of postoperative [[endophthalmitis]]. It is recommended that NLD obstruction be addressed (typically via DCR) **prior to** elective intraocular procedures.

### Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction in the Context of Systemic Disease

Several systemic conditions may predispose to or be associated with NLD obstruction:
- **[[Sarcoidosis]]:** Granulomatous inflammation of the nasolacrimal duct mucosa.
- **[[Granulomatosis with polyangiitis]]:** Necrotizing vasculitis affecting the nasal and lacrimal mucosa.
- **[[Trachoma]]:** Chronic [[Chlamydia trachomatis]] infection leading to cicatricial NLD obstruction; a leading cause of acquired NLD obstruction in endemic regions.
- **Systemic chemotherapy:** Agents such as [[docetaxel]] and [[5-fluorouracil]] cause canalicular and NLD stenosis through direct toxic effects on the lacrimal epithelium.
- **[[Radioactive iodine]] (I-131) therapy:** Used for [[thyroid cancer]]; concentrated in tears, causing radiation-induced NLD stenosis.

### Age-Related Changes

With aging, the nasolacrimal duct undergoes progressive narrowing due to:
- Submucosal fibrosis
- Decreased goblet cell density
- Reduced vascular plexus compliance
- Bony remodeling of the nasolacrimal canal

These changes contribute to the increased prevalence of acquired NLD obstruction in the elderly population.

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis for nasolacrimal duct disorders is generally excellent with appropriate management:

| Condition | Treatment | Success Rate |
|---|---|---|
| CNLDO (conservative) | Crigler massage + observation | ~90% resolve by 12 months |
| CNLDO (probing, age 6–12 months) | Nasolacrimal duct probing | 85–95% |
| CNLDO (probing, age >24 months) | Probing ± intubation/balloon | 60–80% |
| ANLDO | External DCR | 90–98% |
| ANLDO | Endoscopic DCR | 85–95% |
| ANLDO | Laser-assisted DCR | 70–85% |
| Functional obstruction | Silicone intubation | 60–75% |

---

## Summary

The nasolacrimal duct is a vital component of the [[lacrimal drainage system]], and its obstruction—whether congenital or acquired—represents one of the most frequently encountered conditions in ophthalmic and optometric practice. A comprehensive understanding of the anatomy, embryology, physiology, and pathophysiology of the nasolacrimal duct enables clinicians to accurately diagnose the site and nature of obstruction and to implement evidence-based management strategies ranging from conservative measures (Crigler massage, topical antibiotics) to definitive surgical intervention ([[dacryocystorhinostomy]]). Early recognition and appropriate referral are essential to prevent complications such as chronic dacryocystitis, lacrimal sac abscess, and the potential compromise of intraocular surgical outcomes.
