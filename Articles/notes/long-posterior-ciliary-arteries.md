# Long Posterior Ciliary Arteries

---

The **long posterior ciliary arteries** (LPCAs) are two critical branches of the [[ophthalmic artery]] that serve as the principal arterial supply to the [[anterior segment]] of the [[eye]]. These paired vessels—one medial (nasal) and one lateral (temporal)—traverse the [[sclera]] alongside the [[optic nerve]], course anteriorly between the [[sclera]] and [[choroid]], and ultimately anastomose to form the [[major arterial circle of the iris]] (circulus arteriosus iridis major). Their anatomical position, extensive intrascleral and suprachoroidal course, and role in perfusing the [[iris]], [[ciliary body]], and portions of the [[anterior choroid]] make them indispensable structures in ocular vascular physiology. Compromise of the LPCAs is implicated in [[anterior segment ischemia]], certain forms of [[ischemic optic neuropathy]], and complications following [[strabismus surgery]] and other extraocular procedures. A thorough understanding of the anatomy, hemodynamics, pathophysiology, and clinical significance of the long posterior ciliary arteries is essential for optometrists, ophthalmologists, and all eye care professionals involved in the diagnosis and management of ocular vascular disease.

---

## Embryology and Development

The vascular supply of the eye develops from the primitive [[hyaloid artery]] system and the periocular mesenchyme during the first trimester of gestation. The [[ophthalmic artery]], a branch of the [[internal carotid artery]], differentiates early in embryonic life and gives rise to multiple branches, including the [[central retinal artery]], the [[short posterior ciliary arteries]], and the long posterior ciliary arteries.

During the sixth to eighth weeks of embryonic development, the posterior ciliary arteries begin to differentiate from the ophthalmic artery. The LPCAs emerge as two distinct trunks—medial and lateral—that penetrate the developing scleral mesenchyme near the [[optic nerve head]]. As the [[anterior segment]] structures (iris, ciliary body, and [[anterior chamber]]) form during the second and third months, the LPCAs extend anteriorly to establish the [[major arterial circle of the iris]], which becomes the dominant blood supply to the developing iris and ciliary body.

The [[tunica vasculosa lentis]], a transient embryonic vascular network nourishing the developing [[lens]], receives contributions from both the hyaloid system and the ciliary vasculature. As the hyaloid system regresses (typically by the seventh month of gestation), the LPCAs and their terminal anastomotic networks assume full responsibility for anterior segment perfusion. Anomalies in LPCA development can result in persistent fetal vasculature, iris hypoplasia, or congenital anterior segment dysgenesis syndromes.

## Gross Anatomy

### Origin

The long posterior ciliary arteries arise from the [[ophthalmic artery]], typically as two distinct branches, although anatomical variations exist. In most individuals, the ophthalmic artery gives off the posterior ciliary arteries as a common trunk that subsequently divides into:

- **Short posterior ciliary arteries (SPCAs):** Approximately 15–20 small branches that penetrate the sclera in a ring around the optic nerve to supply the [[posterior choroid]] and the [[optic nerve head]].
- **Long posterior ciliary arteries (LPCAs):** Two arteries—one medial (nasal) and one lateral (temporal)—that take a distinctly longer intrascleral and suprachoroidal course to reach the anterior segment.

| Feature | Medial (Nasal) LPCA | Lateral (Temporal) LPCA |
|---|---|---|
| **Origin** | [[Ophthalmic artery]] (or common posterior ciliary trunk) | [[Ophthalmic artery]] (or common posterior ciliary trunk) |
| **Scleral penetration** | Nasal to [[optic nerve]] | Temporal to [[optic nerve]] |
| **Horizontal meridian** | Approximately 3 o'clock (right eye) / 9 o'clock (left eye) | Approximately 9 o'clock (right eye) / 3 o'clock (left eye) |
| **Course** | Suprachoroidal space, nasal side | Suprachoroidal space, temporal side |
| **Terminal anastomosis** | [[Major arterial circle of the iris]] | [[Major arterial circle of the iris]] |

### Scleral Penetration

The LPCAs penetrate the [[sclera]] approximately 3–4 mm from the optic nerve, entering the globe in the horizontal meridian (the 3 o'clock and 9 o'clock positions). The point of scleral penetration is posterior to the [[equator]] of the globe. Each artery passes through a short oblique intrascleral canal before entering the [[suprachoroidal space]].

### Intrascleral and Suprachoroidal Course

After penetrating the sclera, each LPCA courses anteriorly within the [[suprachoroidal space]]—the potential space between the inner surface of the sclera and the outer surface of the [[choroid]]. This course is remarkably long (approximately 20–25 mm in the adult eye), making the LPCAs the longest intraocular arteries. During this transit, the LPCAs are embedded in the loose connective tissue of the [[lamina fusca]] and are accompanied by the [[long posterior ciliary nerves]], which are sensory branches of the [[nasociliary nerve]] (a division of the [[ophthalmic nerve]], V1).

Importantly, during their suprachoroidal course, the LPCAs give off only minimal branches to the surrounding choroid. Their primary function is to deliver blood to the anterior segment, not to supply the posterior choroid (which is the domain of the [[short posterior ciliary arteries]]).

### Anterior Segment Distribution

Upon reaching the [[ciliary body]], each LPCA divides into two branches—one superior and one inferior—that curve circumferentially to anastomose with their counterparts from the opposite LPCA and with the [[anterior ciliary arteries]] (ACAs). This anastomotic ring, located in the [[ciliary body]] near the root of the [[iris]], constitutes the **[[major arterial circle of the iris]]** (circulus arteriosus iridis major).

From the major arterial circle, branches radiate to supply:

- **[[Iris]]:** Radial arterioles course toward the [[pupillary margin]], forming the **[[minor arterial circle of the iris]]** (circulus arteriosus iridis minor) near the [[collarette]].
- **[[Ciliary body]]:** Branches supply the [[ciliary muscle]], [[ciliary processes]], and the [[pars plana]].
- **[[Anterior choroid]]:** Recurrent choroidal branches supply the anterior portion of the [[choriocapillaris]].
- **[[Conjunctiva]] and [[episclera]]:** Via anastomoses with the anterior ciliary arteries.

### Anastomotic Relationships

The LPCAs do not function in isolation. Their terminal branches form critical anastomoses with:

1. **[[Anterior ciliary arteries]] (ACAs):** These are branches of the [[muscular arteries]] supplying the [[extraocular muscles]] (specifically, two ACAs from each [[rectus muscle]], except the [[lateral rectus]], which contributes one). The ACAs penetrate the sclera near the [[limbus]] and join the major arterial circle.
2. **[[Short posterior ciliary arteries]]:** Minor anastomoses exist between the LPCAs and the SPCAs in the choroid, though these are typically insufficient to compensate for LPCA occlusion.
3. **Recurrent branches of the [[central retinal artery]]:** Minimal and clinically insignificant under normal conditions.

| Arterial Source | Contribution to Major Arterial Circle | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Long posterior ciliary arteries (×2)** | Primary supply; major contributors | Occlusion → severe [[anterior segment ischemia]] |
| **Anterior ciliary arteries (×7)** | Secondary supply; reinforce the circle | Disruption during [[strabismus surgery]] → ischemia risk |
| **Short posterior ciliary arteries** | Minimal recurrent branches | Insufficient collateral in LPCA compromise |

## Histology and Microanatomy

The LPCAs are medium-caliber muscular arteries with a typical arterial wall structure:

- **Tunica intima:** A single layer of [[endothelial cells]] resting on a thin [[basement membrane]] and a delicate subendothelial connective tissue layer. The [[internal elastic lamina]] is well-defined.
- **Tunica media:** Composed of several layers of [[smooth muscle cells]] arranged circumferentially, interspersed with elastic fibers. The media is relatively thick compared to the vessel's overall diameter, reflecting the need for autoregulatory tone.
- **Tunica adventitia:** A thin outer layer of collagenous and elastic connective tissue that blends with the surrounding suprachoroidal tissue. [[Vasa vasorum]] are generally absent given the small caliber of the vessel, and nutrition is derived by diffusion from the lumen and surrounding tissues.

The intrascleral segment of the LPCA is notable for its intimate relationship with the dense collagenous scleral stroma, which provides structural support but also renders the vessel vulnerable to compression in conditions of elevated [[intraocular pressure]] or scleral inflammation ([[scleritis]]).

## Physiology and Hemodynamics

### Blood Flow Regulation

The LPCAs are subject to both local (autoregulatory) and systemic influences on blood flow:

- **Myogenic autoregulation:** Smooth muscle in the tunica media responds to changes in transmural pressure, constricting when pressure rises and dilating when it falls, thereby maintaining relatively constant flow over a range of [[perfusion pressures]].
- **Metabolic autoregulation:** Local metabolites (CO₂, lactate, adenosine) and oxygen tension modulate arteriolar tone in the downstream ciliary body and iris vasculature.
- **Neurogenic regulation:** The LPCAs and their branches receive sympathetic innervation (from the [[superior cervical ganglion]] via the [[long ciliary nerves]] and [[short ciliary nerves]]) and parasympathetic innervation (from the [[ciliary ganglion]]). Sympathetic stimulation causes vasoconstriction (via α₁-adrenergic receptors), while parasympathetic and sensory neuropeptide (e.g., [[substance P]], [[calcitonin gene-related peptide]]) influences promote vasodilation.
- **Endothelial factors:** [[Nitric oxide]] (NO), [[endothelin-1]], and [[prostacyclin]] produced by the endothelium modulate vascular tone.

### Perfusion Pressure

Ocular perfusion pressure (OPP) is defined as:

> **OPP = Mean Arterial Pressure (MAP) − Intraocular Pressure (IOP)**

Because the LPCAs traverse the suprachoroidal space, they are directly exposed to [[intraocular pressure]]. Elevated IOP can compress these vessels, reducing flow to the anterior segment. This is clinically relevant in [[acute angle-closure glaucoma]], where markedly elevated IOP can cause iris and ciliary body ischemia, contributing to [[iris atrophy]], [[pupillary sphincter damage]], and [[aqueous humor]] production changes.

### Flow Velocity

[[Color Doppler imaging]] and [[laser Doppler flowmetry]] studies have demonstrated that LPCA blood flow velocity is influenced by age, systemic blood pressure, IOP, and autonomic tone. Reduced LPCA flow has been documented in:

- [[Diabetes mellitus]]
- [[Systemic hypertension]]
- [[Glaucoma]] (particularly [[normal-tension glaucoma]])
- [[Giant cell arteritis]]
- [[Carotid artery stenosis]]

## Pathophysiology

Pathological processes affecting the LPCAs can be broadly categorized into occlusive, inflammatory, compressive, and iatrogenic etiologies.

### 1. Occlusive Disease

**[[Atherosclerosis]]** and **[[thromboembolism]]** can affect the ophthalmic artery or its branches, including the LPCAs. Because the LPCAs are end-arteries with limited collateral supply (the ACAs provide some redundancy), occlusion can result in:

- **[[Anterior segment ischemia]] (ASI):** Characterized by corneal edema, [[aqueous flare]], iris atrophy, cataract formation, and hypotony. Severe cases may progress to [[phthisis bulbi]].
- **Ischemic [[ciliary body]] dysfunction:** Reduced [[aqueous humor]] production leading to [[hypotony]].

**[[Giant cell arteritis]]** (GCA) is a granulomatous vasculitis with a predilection for medium and large arteries, including the ophthalmic artery and its branches. LPCA involvement in GCA can cause anterior segment ischemia in addition to the more commonly recognized [[anterior ischemic optic neuropathy]] (AION) and [[central retinal artery occlusion]] (CRAO).

### 2. Inflammatory Disease

**[[Posterior scleritis]]** and **[[pan-scleritis]]** can directly involve the LPCAs as they course through the sclera and suprachoroidal space. Inflammatory infiltration and edema can compress or occlude the vessels, leading to secondary anterior segment ischemia. Conditions associated with scleritis that may affect the LPCAs include:

- [[Rheumatoid arthritis]]
- [[Granulomatosis with polyangiitis]]
- [[Systemic lupus erythematosus]]
- [[Relapsing polychondritis]]

### 3. Compressive Pathology

- **Elevated [[intraocular pressure]]:** As discussed, acutely elevated IOP (e.g., in [[acute angle-closure glaucoma]]) can compress the LPCAs in the suprachoroidal space.
- **[[Choroidal effusion]] and [[suprachoroidal hemorrhage]]:** Fluid or blood in the suprachoroidal space can compress the LPCAs, compromising anterior segment perfusion.
- **[[Orbital tumors]] and [[orbital inflammation]]:** Retrobulbar masses or inflammatory processes (e.g., [[orbital pseudotumor]]) can compress the LPCAs before they enter the globe.

### 4. Iatrogenic Injury

This is the most clinically relevant category for practicing eye care professionals:

- **[[Strabismus surgery]]:** Disinsertion of [[rectus muscles]] disrupts the [[anterior ciliary arteries]] that travel with those muscles. When multiple rectus muscles are operated on simultaneously (especially three or more), the remaining blood supply to the anterior segment depends heavily on the LPCAs. If the LPCAs are compromised (e.g., by pre-existing vascular disease), clinically significant [[anterior segment ischemia]] can result.
- **[[Scleral buckling]] surgery:** Encircling bands or segmental buckles can compress the LPCAs, particularly if placed in the horizontal meridian near the vessels' suprachoroidal course.
- **[[Retinal detachment]] surgery with [[cryotherapy]]:** Extensive cryotherapy near the equator can damage the LPCAs.
- **[[Periocular injections]]:** Retrobulbar or peribulbar injections can rarely cause direct vascular injury or vasospasm affecting the LPCAs.

## Clinical Presentation (Symptoms and Signs)

Because the LPCAs are deep intraocular structures, their pathology manifests through the consequences of anterior segment ischemia rather than through direct symptoms referable to the arteries themselves.

### Symptoms

Patients with LPCA compromise may report:

- **Blurred vision:** Due to [[corneal edema]], [[aqueous flare]], or [[cataract]] formation.
- **Pain:** Particularly in inflammatory or acute ischemic etiologies; may be severe in [[scleritis]] or [[acute angle-closure glaucoma]].
- **Photophobia:** Secondary to [[anterior uveitis]] (ischemic iritis).
- **Redness:** From [[conjunctival]] and [[episcleral]] vascular congestion.
- **Decreased vision:** Progressive, if ischemia leads to [[cataract]], [[corneal decompensation]], or [[phthisis bulbi]].

### Signs

| Sign | Mechanism | Clinical Context |
|---|---|---|
| [[Corneal edema]] (stromal and epithelial) | Endothelial pump failure from ischemia | ASI post-strabismus surgery, GCA |
| [[Descemet folds]] | Corneal stromal swelling | Early ASI |
| [[Aqueous flare]] and [[anterior chamber cells]] | Blood-aqueous barrier breakdown | Ischemic [[iritis]] |
| [[Iris atrophy]] (sectoral or diffuse) | Ischemic necrosis of iris stroma and sphincter | Chronic ASI, post-acute angle closure |
| [[Pupil]] irregularity / [[corectopia]] | Sphincter muscle ischemia | ASI |
| [[Posterior synechiae]] | Inflammatory adhesions | Ischemic iritis |
| [[Cataract]] (cortical or subcapsular) | Lens epithelial ischemia | Chronic ASI |
| [[Hypotony]] | Ciliary body shutdown (reduced aqueous production) | Severe ASI |
| [[Phthisis bulbi]] | End-stage globe shrinkage | Severe, untreated ASI |
| [[Anterior chamber]] fibrin | Severe blood-aqueous barrier breakdown | Acute ASI |
| [[Rubeosis iridis]] ([[neovascularization of the iris]]) | Ischemia-driven [[VEGF]] release | Chronic ASI, [[ocular ischemic syndrome]] |

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]:** Essential for detecting corneal edema, aqueous flare, iris atrophy, posterior synechiae, and lens changes. Careful examination of the iris vasculature with high magnification can reveal vascular attenuation or neovascularization.
- **[[Intraocular pressure]] measurement ([[tonometry]]):** Hypotony suggests ciliary body ischemia; elevated IOP may be a causative factor in LPCA compression.
- **[[Gonioscopy]]:** To assess the [[anterior chamber angle]] for neovascularization, synechiae, or angle closure.
- **[[Fundoscopy]]:** To evaluate for concurrent posterior segment ischemia (e.g., [[choroidal ischemia]], [[retinal artery occlusion]]).

### Imaging Modalities

| Modality | Application to LPCA Assessment | Key Findings |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA)** | Iris and anterior segment angiography | Delayed iris filling, sector non-perfusion, neovascularization |
| **[[Indocyanine green angiography]] (ICGA)** | Choroidal and LPCA-territory perfusion | Delayed choroidal filling in LPCA watershed zones |
| **[[Color Doppler imaging]] (CDI)** | Direct measurement of LPCA flow velocity | Reduced peak systolic velocity, increased resistive index |
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)** | Anterior segment structural assessment | Iris thinning, ciliary body atrophy |
| **[[Anterior segment OCT]] (AS-OCT)** | High-resolution iris and angle imaging | Iris stromal atrophy, angle abnormalities |
| **[[OCT angiography]] (OCTA)** | Non-invasive vascular imaging | Iris and anterior segment vascular density mapping |
| **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | Ciliary body and suprachoroidal space imaging | Ciliary body atrophy, suprachoroidal fluid, LPCA course visualization |
| **[[Magnetic resonance angiography]] (MRA)** | Orbital and ophthalmic artery assessment | Ophthalmic artery stenosis, orbital mass compression |
| **[[Computed tomography angiography]] (CTA)** | Carotid and ophthalmic artery evaluation | Carotid stenosis, calcific emboli |

### Anterior Segment Fluorescein Angiography

This is the most specific diagnostic tool for evaluating LPCA perfusion. The technique involves:

1. Intravenous injection of [[sodium fluorescein]].
2. Sequential photography of the iris and anterior segment using a slit-lamp camera with appropriate excitation and barrier filters.
3. Analysis of filling patterns:
   - **Normal:** Rapid, symmetric filling of the iris vasculature from the major arterial circle within 10–15 seconds of dye appearance.
   - **LPCA compromise:** Delayed filling, sectoral non-perfusion (corresponding to the territory of the affected LPCA), and late leakage from damaged vessels.

### Laboratory Investigations

When systemic vascular disease is suspected as the cause of LPCA compromise:

- **[[Erythrocyte sedimentation rate]] (ESR) and [[C-reactive protein]] (CRP):** Elevated in [[giant cell arteritis]].
- **[[Complete blood count]] (CBC):** Thrombocytosis may accompany GCA.
- **[[Hemoglobin A1c]]:** To assess diabetic control.
- **[[Lipid panel]]:** Hyperlipidemia as an atherosclerotic risk factor.
- **[[Carotid duplex ultrasonography]]:** To evaluate for [[carotid artery stenosis]] in suspected [[ocular ischemic syndrome]].
- **[[Temporal artery biopsy]]:** Gold standard for confirming [[giant cell arteritis]].
- **Autoimmune panel (ANA, ANCA, RF):** When [[scleritis]]-related LPCA compromise is suspected.

## Management and Treatment

Management of LPCA-related pathology is directed at the underlying etiology and the consequences of anterior segment ischemia.

### Medical (Pharmacological) Management

| Condition | Treatment | Mechanism / Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Anterior segment ischemia]] (mild)** | Topical [[corticosteroids]] (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate]] 1%) | Reduce ischemia-induced inflammation |
| **Ischemic [[iritis]]** | Topical [[cycloplegics]] (e.g., [[atropine]] 1%, [[cyclopentolate]] 1%) | Reduce pain, prevent posterior synechiae, stabilize blood-aqueous barrier |
| **[[Giant cell arteritis]]** | High-dose systemic [[corticosteroids]] (e.g., [[prednisone]] 60–100 mg/day; IV [[methylprednisolone]] 1 g/day ×3 days in severe cases) | Suppress granulomatous vasculitis, prevent fellow eye involvement |
| **[[Ocular ischemic syndrome]]** | Systemic anticoagulation, antiplatelet therapy ([[aspirin]]), management of carotid stenosis | Improve perfusion, prevent thromboembolic events |
| **[[Scleritis]]** | Systemic [[NSAIDs]], systemic corticosteroids, [[immunosuppressive agents]] ([[methotrexate]], [[mycophenolate]], [[cyclophosphamide]]) | Treat underlying autoimmune inflammation |
| **[[Neovascularization]] (rubeosis)** | Intravitreal [[anti-VEGF]] agents ([[bevacizumab]], [[ranibizumab]], [[aflibercept]]) | Inhibit ischemia-driven angiogenesis |
| **Elevated IOP (compressive)** | Topical and systemic [[IOP-lowering agents]] ([[timolol]], [[brimonidine]], [[acetazolamide]], IV [[mannitol]]) | Reduce IOP to restore LPCA perfusion |
| **[[Hypotony]]** | Observation; treat underlying cause; consider [[atropine]] to reduce uveoscleral outflow paradox | Ciliary body recovery may restore aqueous production |

### Surgical Management

- **[[Carotid endarterectomy]] or [[carotid stenting]]:** For hemodynamically significant [[carotid artery stenosis]] causing [[ocular ischemic syndrome]] with LPCA hypoperfusion.
- **[[Panretinal photocoagulation]] (PRP):** For ischemia-driven [[neovascularization]] (rubeosis iridis, [[neovascular glaucoma]]) when anti-VEGF alone is insufficient.
- **[[Glaucoma drainage device]] implantation or [[trabeculectomy]]:** For [[neovascular glaucoma]] refractory to medical therapy.
- **[[Scleral buckle]] revision or removal:** If a buckle is compressing the LPCAs and causing anterior segment ischemia.
- **Staged [[strabismus surgery]]:** To minimize the risk of anterior segment ischemia, surgeons should avoid operating on more than two rectus muscles simultaneously, particularly in patients with risk factors for LPCA insufficiency (diabetes, elderly, prior surgery).

### Prevention of Iatrogenic LPCA Compromise

Preventive strategies are paramount, particularly in the context of strabismus and retinal surgery:

1. **Limit simultaneous rectus muscle surgery:** Avoid operating on more than two rectus muscles at one session. If three or more muscles require surgery, stage the procedures at least 2–3 months apart.
2. **Preserve anterior ciliary arteries:** Techniques such as vessel-sparing strabismus surgery (isolating and preserving the ACAs during muscle disinsertion) can reduce the risk of anterior segment ischemia.
3. **Preoperative vascular assessment:** In high-risk patients (diabetics, elderly, those with prior strabismus surgery), consider preoperative [[anterior segment fluorescein angiography]] or [[color Doppler imaging]] to assess baseline LPCA and ACA perfusion.
4. **Careful scleral buckle placement:** Avoid placing encircling elements directly over the horizontal meridian where the LPCAs course.
5. **Judicious cryotherapy:** Minimize the extent and duration of cryotherapy near the equator.

## Anatomical Variations

Anatomical studies have documented several clinically relevant variations in LPCA anatomy:

- **Accessory LPCAs:** Some individuals possess a third (or rarely, fourth) LPCA, which may provide additional collateral supply to the anterior segment.
- **Asymmetric caliber:** The medial LPCA is often slightly larger than the lateral LPCA, though this varies.
- **Variable origin:** The LPCAs may arise independently from the ophthalmic artery or from a common posterior ciliary trunk shared with the SPCAs.
- **Anomalous course:** Rarely, an LPCA may deviate from the horizontal meridian, coursing obliquely through the suprachoroidal space.
- **Incomplete major arterial circle:** In some individuals, the [[major arterial circle of the iris]] is incomplete, with gaps in the anastomotic ring. This renders the anterior segment more vulnerable to ischemia if one LPCA is compromised.

These variations are important considerations in surgical planning and in understanding individual susceptibility to anterior segment ischemia.

## Relationship to Specific Clinical Conditions

### [[Anterior Segment Ischemia]]

ASI is the most direct clinical consequence of LPCA compromise. The classic presentation includes the triad of corneal edema, anterior uveitis (flare and cells), and iris atrophy. Severe cases progress to cataract, hypotony, and phthisis bulbi. ASI is most commonly iatrogenic (post-strabismus surgery) but can also result from vascular disease (GCA, carotid stenosis, diabetes).

### [[Ocular Ischemic Syndrome]]

[[Ocular ischemic syndrome]] (OIS) results from chronic hypoperfusion of the entire globe, typically due to severe [[carotid artery stenosis]] (>90%). The LPCAs, as branches of the ophthalmic artery, are directly affected. Anterior segment findings in OIS include rubeosis iridis, anterior chamber flare, iris atrophy, and asymmetric cataract—all reflecting chronic LPCA insufficiency.

### [[Giant Cell Arteritis]]

GCA can cause acute LPCA occlusion, leading to sudden anterior segment ischemia. This may accompany or precede the more commonly recognized [[anterior ischemic optic neuropathy]]. The presence of anterior segment ischemia in a patient over 50 years of age should prompt urgent evaluation for GCA, including ESR, CRP, and [[temporal artery biopsy]].

### [[Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma]]

The markedly elevated IOP in acute angle closure (often >40–60 mmHg) can compress the LPCAs in the suprachoroidal space, causing ischemic damage to the iris sphincter (resulting in a fixed, mid-dilated pupil), iris stromal atrophy ([[glaukomflecken]]-associated lens changes), and sectoral iris atrophy. These findings persist after the acute episode resolves and serve as clinical evidence of prior LPCA compromise.

### [[Sickle Cell Disease]]

Patients with [[sickle cell disease]] or [[sickle cell trait]] are at increased risk of vaso-occlusive events affecting the LPCAs, particularly under conditions of hypoxia, dehydration, or elevated IOP. Anterior segment ischemia in sickle cell patients can be precipitated by surgical procedures or acute IOP elevation.

## Comparative Anatomy

The LPCA system is present in most mammalian species but shows notable interspecies variation:

- **Primates:** Two LPCAs (medial and lateral), similar to humans.
- **Rabbits:** A single LPCA is present, making the rabbit a useful but imperfect model for studying anterior segment ischemia.
- **Cats and dogs:** Variable numbers of LPCAs; the major arterial circle is often more robust than in humans, providing greater collateral reserve.

These differences are important when interpreting animal model data on anterior segment perfusion and ischemia.

## Research Directions

Current and emerging areas of research related to the LPCAs include:

- **[[OCT angiography]] of the anterior segment:** Non-invasive, high-resolution mapping of iris and ciliary body vasculature is enabling earlier detection of LPCA compromise.
- **Pharmacological neuroprotection and vasodilation:** Agents such as [[fasudil]] (a Rho-kinase inhibitor) and [[sildenafil]] (a PDE-5 inhibitor) are being investigated for their ability to improve ocular blood flow, including LPCA perfusion.
- **Vessel-sparing surgical techniques:** Ongoing refinement of strabismus surgery techniques to preserve anterior ciliary arteries and reduce dependence on LPCA collateral flow.
- **Biomarkers of anterior segment ischemia:** Aqueous humor proteomics and metabolomics may identify early markers of LPCA insufficiency before clinical signs manifest.
- **3D printing and surgical simulation:** Anatomically accurate models of the LPCA system are being developed for surgical training and preoperative planning.

---

## Summary

The long posterior ciliary arteries are indispensable components of the ocular vascular system, serving as the primary arterial conduit to the [[anterior segment]] of the eye. Their unique anatomical course—originating from the [[ophthalmic artery]], penetrating the [[sclera]] near the [[optic nerve]], traversing the [[suprachoroidal space]] for over 20 mm, and terminating in the [[major arterial circle of the iris]]—renders them both vital and vulnerable. Compromise of the LPCAs, whether from vascular disease, inflammation, elevated intraocular pressure, or iatrogenic injury, can result in devastating [[anterior segment ischemia]]. Eye care professionals must possess a thorough understanding of LPCA anatomy, physiology, and pathophysiology to recognize, prevent, and manage conditions affecting these critical vessels.
