

# Canal of Schlemm

---

The **Canal of Schlemm** (also known as **Schlemm's canal**, **sinus venosus sclerae**, or the **scleral venous sinus**) is a unique, circumferential, endothelium-lined channel located at the [[iridocorneal angle]] (also called the [[anterior chamber angle]]) of the [[eye]]. It serves as the principal conduit for the drainage of [[aqueous humor]] from the [[anterior chamber]] into the [[episcleral venous system]], and is therefore a critical structure in the regulation of [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP). Dysfunction, collapse, or obstruction of the Canal of Schlemm is intimately linked to the pathogenesis of [[primary open-angle glaucoma]] (POAG), the leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide. Named after the German anatomist **Friedrich Schlemm** (1795–1858), who first described the structure in 1830, the canal remains a central focus of modern glaucoma research, diagnostic imaging, and minimally invasive surgical intervention.

---

## Historical Background

Friedrich Schlemm initially identified the canal during cadaveric dissections of the human eye in the early 19th century. He described it as a circular vessel embedded within the [[sclera]], near the junction of the [[cornea]] and the [[iris]]. For over a century, debate persisted regarding whether the canal was a blood vessel, a lymphatic channel, or a unique hybrid structure. Advances in [[electron microscopy]], [[immunohistochemistry]], and [[molecular biology]] in the late 20th and early 21st centuries have clarified that the Canal of Schlemm possesses characteristics of both blood vascular and lymphatic endothelium, expressing markers such as [[PROX1]], [[VEGFR-3]], and [[FOXC2]], while also expressing the blood vascular marker [[CD31]]. This hybrid identity has led to its classification as a specialized vascular structure with lymphatic-like features, uniquely adapted for its role in aqueous humor outflow.

## Anatomy

### Gross Anatomy

The Canal of Schlemm is an annular (ring-shaped) channel that encircles the full 360 degrees of the [[limbus]], situated within the inner [[scleral sulcus]] at the level of the [[trabecular meshwork]] (TM). It lies posterior to the [[Schwalbe's line]] and external to the [[juxtacanalicular tissue]] (JCT), which is the outermost layer of the trabecular meshwork. The canal is located approximately 0.5–0.8 mm from the [[corneal endothelium]] and is embedded within the scleral tissue at the angle recess.

Key anatomical dimensions include:

| Parameter | Typical Value |
|---|---|
| **Circumference** | ~36 mm (full 360°) |
| **Cross-sectional diameter (lumen)** | 190–370 µm (varies with IOP) |
| **Cross-sectional height** | ~25 µm (collapsed) to ~50 µm (distended) |
| **Distance from anterior chamber** | ~0.5–0.8 mm |
| **Number of collector channel ostia** | 25–35 |
| **Wall thickness (inner wall)** | ~1–2 µm (single endothelial cell layer) |

The canal is not a perfectly uniform tube; rather, it demonstrates regional variations in diameter, with areas of focal dilation and narrowing. It may also contain intraluminal septae or partitions that partially divide the lumen, creating a complex, multi-chambered architecture in some regions.

### Microanatomy and Ultrastructure

The Canal of Schlemm is lined by a continuous monolayer of specialized [[endothelial cells]]. The canal wall is divided into two functionally and morphologically distinct regions:

#### Inner Wall

The **inner wall** of the Canal of Schlemm faces the [[anterior chamber]] and is separated from it by the [[juxtacanalicular tissue]] (JCT) and the outermost layers of the [[trabecular meshwork]]. This inner wall is the site of greatest resistance to aqueous humor outflow in the [[conventional outflow pathway]] and is therefore of paramount importance in IOP regulation.

Inner wall endothelial cells are remarkable for several features:

- **Giant vacuoles**: These are large, balloon-like outpouchings of the endothelial cell cytoplasm that bulge into the canal lumen in the direction of aqueous flow. They are not true intracellular vacuoles but rather transcellular channels or paracellular pores that facilitate the bulk flow of aqueous humor across the endothelium. Giant vacuoles are pressure-dependent structures—they increase in number and size with increasing IOP and decrease or disappear when IOP is low or when the eye is hypotonus.
- **Intracellular pores (I-pores)**: These are transcellular openings (typically 0.1–2 µm in diameter) that traverse the full thickness of the inner wall endothelial cell, often associated with giant vacuoles. They provide a direct pathway for aqueous humor to pass from the JCT into the canal lumen.
- **Paracellular pores (B-pores)**: These are intercellular openings located at the borders between adjacent endothelial cells. They are less numerous than I-pores but contribute to aqueous outflow.
- **Basement membrane**: The inner wall endothelium rests on a discontinuous [[basement membrane]], which is notably thinner and more fenestrated than the basement membrane of the outer wall. This discontinuity is thought to facilitate fluid transport.
- **Glycocalyx**: The luminal surface of inner wall cells is coated with a [[glycocalyx]] layer that may modulate flow resistance and cell signaling.

The inner wall endothelium is subjected to significant biomechanical forces, including shear stress from aqueous flow and transendothelial pressure gradients. These forces are critical regulators of endothelial cell behavior, pore formation, and ultimately, outflow facility.

#### Outer Wall

The **outer wall** of the Canal of Schlemm faces the scleral tissue and is the site where [[collector channels]] (also called **collector channel ostia** or **external collector channels**) originate. The outer wall endothelium is morphologically more similar to conventional vascular endothelium, with a more continuous basement membrane and fewer specialized pores. The outer wall serves as the exit point for aqueous humor, channeling it into the collector channels and subsequently into the [[aqueous veins]] (veins of [[Ascher]]), the deep and intrascleral venous plexuses, and ultimately the [[episcleral veins]].

### Collector Channels and Downstream Outflow Pathway

Approximately **25–35 collector channels** arise from the outer wall of the Canal of Schlemm at irregular intervals around its circumference. These channels are not uniformly distributed; they tend to be more concentrated in the nasal and inferior quadrants. Collector channels connect the canal to the:

1. **Deep scleral venous plexus**
2. **Intrascleral venous plexus**
3. **Episcleral veins**
4. **Aqueous veins (of Ascher)** — these are unique veins that carry clear aqueous humor (or a mixture of aqueous and blood) and are visible on the surface of the [[conjunctiva]] and [[episclera]] by [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]].

The collector channel ostia are critical bottlenecks in the outflow pathway. Obstruction or narrowing of these ostia—whether by herniation of the inner wall endothelium, accumulation of extracellular matrix, or other pathological processes—can significantly impair aqueous drainage and elevate IOP.

### Relationship to Adjacent Structures

| Adjacent Structure | Relationship to Canal of Schlemm |
|---|---|
| [[Trabecular meshwork]] (TM) | Lies internal (anterior chamber side) to the canal; aqueous passes through TM before reaching the canal |
| [[Juxtacanalicular tissue]] (JCT) | Outermost layer of TM, directly apposed to the inner wall; site of greatest outflow resistance |
| [[Schwalbe's line]] | Anterior boundary of the TM; marks the transition from corneal to trabecular endothelium |
| [[Scleral spur]] | Posterior boundary of the TM; attachment site for the [[ciliary muscle]] |
| [[Iris root]] | Located posterior and internal to the canal; its position determines the configuration of the [[anterior chamber angle]] |
| [[Ciliary body]] | Posterior to the scleral spur; contraction of the [[ciliary muscle]] (longitudinal fibers) opens the TM and widens the canal |
| [[Collector channels]] | Arise from the outer wall; connect the canal to the episcleral venous system |

## Physiology of Aqueous Humor Outflow

### The Conventional (Trabecular) Outflow Pathway

The Canal of Schlemm is the central structure of the **[[conventional outflow pathway]]** (also called the **trabecular** or **pressure-dependent** outflow pathway), which is responsible for approximately **70–90%** of total aqueous humor drainage in the human eye. The pathway proceeds as follows:

1. **[[Aqueous humor]]** is produced by the [[ciliary epithelium]] of the [[ciliary body]] and secreted into the [[posterior chamber]].
2. Aqueous flows through the [[pupil]] into the [[anterior chamber]].
3. At the [[anterior chamber angle]], aqueous percolates through the layers of the [[trabecular meshwork]]:
   - [[Uveal meshwork]] (innermost, largest pores)
   - [[Corneoscleral meshwork]] (intermediate)
   - [[Juxtacanalicular tissue]] (outermost, smallest pores, greatest resistance)
4. Aqueous crosses the **inner wall endothelium** of the Canal of Schlemm via giant vacuoles, intracellular pores, and paracellular pores.
5. Aqueous enters the **lumen** of the Canal of Schlemm.
6. Aqueous exits via **[[collector channels]]** into the deep and intrascleral venous plexuses.
7. Aqueous drains into the **[[episcleral veins]]** and ultimately into the systemic venous circulation.

### Regulation of Outflow Resistance

The total resistance to aqueous outflow in the conventional pathway is distributed among several sites, but the **inner wall of the Canal of Schlemm and the adjacent JCT** together account for the majority (estimated 50–75%) of total outflow resistance. The remaining resistance is contributed by the trabecular meshwork beams, collector channels, and episcleral veins.

Key factors regulating outflow resistance at the level of the Canal of Schlemm include:

- **Transendothelial pressure gradient**: Higher IOP increases the pressure differential across the inner wall, promoting giant vacuole formation and pore opening, thereby increasing outflow facility.
- **Canal lumen dimensions**: The canal is a compliant structure. At higher IOPs, the canal may partially collapse (especially in regions distant from collector channel ostia), paradoxically increasing resistance. Conversely, at lower IOPs, the canal may be more patent.
- **Endothelial cell stiffness**: Increased stiffness of inner wall endothelial cells (as seen in [[glaucoma]]) reduces their ability to form giant vacuoles and pores, increasing resistance.
- **Extracellular matrix (ECM) composition of the JCT**: Accumulation of ECM material (e.g., [[fibronectin]], [[elastin]], [[collagen]], and [[glycosaminoglycans]]) in the JCT increases resistance.
- **Nitric oxide (NO) signaling**: [[Nitric oxide]] produced by the trabecular meshwork and canal endothelium acts as a local vasodilator and regulator of outflow facility. Reduced NO bioavailability is implicated in glaucomatous outflow dysfunction.
- **Ciliary muscle tone**: Contraction of the longitudinal fibers of the [[ciliary muscle]] (e.g., via [[pilocarpine]]) pulls on the [[scleral spur]], opening the trabecular meshwork and widening the Canal of Schlemm, thereby reducing outflow resistance.
- **Rho kinase (ROCK) pathway**: The [[Rho kinase]] signaling pathway regulates actomyosin contractility in TM and canal endothelial cells. Inhibition of ROCK (e.g., with [[netarsudil]]) relaxes these cells, increases outflow facility, and may dilate the canal.

### The Unconventional (Uveoscleral) Outflow Pathway

For completeness, it should be noted that approximately **10–30%** of aqueous humor exits the eye via the **[[uveoscleral outflow pathway]]**, which bypasses the Canal of Schlemm entirely. In this pathway, aqueous passes through the [[ciliary muscle]] interstitial spaces, into the [[suprachoroidal space]], and is absorbed by the [[choroidal vasculature]] or exits through the sclera. The uveoscleral pathway is largely pressure-independent and is the target of [[prostaglandin analogue]] medications (e.g., [[latanoprost]], [[travoprost]], [[bimatoprost]]).

## Pathophysiology

### Role in Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma (POAG)

The Canal of Schlemm is central to the pathophysiology of [[primary open-angle glaucoma]], the most common form of [[glaucoma]]. In POAG, the [[anterior chamber angle]] appears open on [[gonioscopy]], yet there is increased resistance to aqueous outflow, leading to elevated [[intraocular pressure]] and progressive damage to the [[optic nerve]] and [[retinal ganglion cells]].

Pathological changes at the level of the Canal of Schlemm and its inner wall in POAG include:

1. **Reduction in canal lumen area**: Histological and imaging studies have demonstrated that the Canal of Schlemm is significantly narrower (reduced cross-sectional area) in eyes with POAG compared to age-matched normal eyes. This narrowing may result from increased IOP compressing the canal, loss of structural support from the surrounding tissue, or fibrotic changes in the canal wall.

2. **Loss of giant vacuoles and pores**: The number and size of giant vacuoles and intracellular pores in the inner wall endothelium are reduced in glaucomatous eyes, indicating impaired transcellular transport of aqueous humor.

3. **Increased inner wall endothelial cell stiffness**: Atomic force microscopy studies have shown that inner wall endothelial cells from glaucomatous eyes are significantly stiffer than those from normal eyes. This increased stiffness impairs the cells' ability to deform and form giant vacuoles in response to pressure gradients.

4. **Accumulation of extracellular matrix in the JCT**: There is increased deposition of [[fibronectin]], [[collagen types I, III, IV, and VI]], [[elastin]]-derived material (so-called "sheath-derived plaques" or "plaque material"), and [[glycosaminoglycans]] in the JCT of glaucomatous eyes. This ECM accumulation increases the resistance to aqueous flow reaching the inner wall.

5. **Reduced cellularity of the trabecular meshwork and canal endothelium**: There is an age-related and glaucoma-associated loss of TM cells and canal endothelial cells, reducing the tissue's ability to maintain homeostasis, phagocytose debris, and regulate ECM turnover.

6. **Collector channel obstruction**: Herniation of the inner wall endothelium into collector channel ostia, or accumulation of debris and ECM at these sites, can obstruct the downstream outflow pathway.

7. **Impaired nitric oxide signaling**: Reduced expression of [[endothelial nitric oxide synthase]] (eNOS) and decreased NO bioavailability in the TM and canal endothelium contribute to increased outflow resistance.

8. **Segmental flow patterns**: Aqueous outflow through the Canal of Schlemm is not uniform around its circumference. Studies using tracers and imaging have demonstrated that outflow is highly segmental, with "high-flow" and "low-flow" regions. In glaucoma, the proportion of high-flow regions decreases, and the overall pattern becomes more heterogeneous.

### Role in Other Conditions

| Condition | Relationship to Canal of Schlemm |
|---|---|
| [[Normal-tension glaucoma]] (NTG) | Similar structural changes (canal narrowing, reduced pore density) may be present even at statistically normal IOP levels, suggesting increased susceptibility of the optic nerve or subtle outflow dysfunction |
| [[Steroid-induced glaucoma]] | [[Corticosteroids]] increase ECM deposition (especially [[myocilin]] and [[fibronectin]]) in the TM and JCT, increasing outflow resistance and compressing the canal |
| [[Pigmentary glaucoma]] | [[Pigment granules]] released from the [[iris pigment epithelium]] accumulate in the TM and can obstruct the canal and collector channels |
| [[Pseudoexfoliation glaucoma]] | [[Pseudoexfoliative material]] deposits in the TM and JCT, increasing outflow resistance and potentially obstructing the canal |
| [[Posner-Schlossman syndrome]] | Inflammatory [[trabeculitis]] may cause transient obstruction of the canal |
| [[Blood reflux into Schlemm's canal]] | Seen with elevated [[episcleral venous pressure]] (e.g., [[carotid-cavernous fistula]], [[Sturge-Weber syndrome]], [[thyroid eye disease]]), or during [[gonioscopy]] with excessive pressure on the episcleral veins; blood in the canal is visible as a red band at the angle |
| [[Congenital glaucoma]] / [[Primary congenital glaucoma]] | Developmental anomalies of the canal (absence, hypoplasia, or malformation) may contribute to elevated IOP in infants |
| [[Aging]] | Progressive loss of TM and canal endothelial cells, increased ECM deposition, and reduced canal lumen area occur with normal aging, contributing to the age-related increase in IOP and glaucoma risk |

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

The Canal of Schlemm itself does not produce symptoms in isolation. Rather, dysfunction of the canal manifests clinically through its effect on [[intraocular pressure]] and, consequently, on the [[optic nerve]]. The clinical presentation is therefore that of [[glaucoma]] or [[ocular hypertension]]:

- **Elevated intraocular pressure** (typically >21 mmHg, though damage can occur at lower pressures in susceptible individuals)
- **Progressive [[optic disc]] cupping** (increased [[cup-to-disc ratio]])
- **[[Retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL) thinning**
- **[[Visual field]] loss** (typically arcuate scotomas, nasal steps, or paracentral defects in early-to-moderate disease; advanced disease may result in tunnel vision or complete blindness)
- **Asymptomatic in early stages** — glaucoma is often called "the silent thief of sight" because patients are typically unaware of the disease until significant, irreversible damage has occurred

In cases of **blood reflux into the Canal of Schlemm** (e.g., due to elevated episcleral venous pressure), the canal may be directly visualized as a **red or blood-filled band** at the [[anterior chamber angle]] during [[gonioscopy]].

## Diagnostic Modalities

### Gonioscopy

[[Gonioscopy]] is the gold-standard clinical technique for evaluating the [[anterior chamber angle]] and, indirectly, the region of the Canal of Schlemm. Using a goniolens (e.g., [[Goldmann three-mirror lens]], [[Zeiss four-mirror lens]], [[Posner lens]], or [[Sussman lens]]) placed on the [[cornea]], the clinician can visualize the angle structures, including:

- [[Schwalbe's line]]
- [[Trabecular meshwork]] (non-pigmented and pigmented portions)
- [[Scleral spur]]
- [[Ciliary body band]]
- [[Iris root]]

While the Canal of Schlemm itself is not directly visible on routine gonioscopy (it is embedded within the sclera), **blood reflux into the canal** can be observed as a reddish band at the level of the posterior trabecular meshwork. This finding may be elicited by compressing the episcleral veins (e.g., with a Posner or Sussman lens) or may be seen spontaneously in conditions with elevated episcleral venous pressure.

### Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography (AS-OCT)

[[Anterior segment OCT]] (AS-OCT) is a non-invasive, high-resolution imaging modality that can visualize the Canal of Schlemm in cross-section. Using spectral-domain or swept-source OCT platforms (e.g., [[Heidelberg Spectralis]], [[Zeiss CIRRUS]], [[Tomey CASIA2]]), the canal appears as a hypo-reflective (dark) space within the scleral tissue at the angle. AS-OCT allows measurement of:

- **Canal lumen area** (cross-sectional)
- **Canal height and width**
- **Trabecular meshwork thickness**
- **Relationship of the canal to adjacent structures**

Studies using AS-OCT have demonstrated that canal lumen area is significantly reduced in eyes with POAG compared to normal eyes, and that canal dimensions correlate with outflow facility and IOP.

### Enhanced Depth Imaging OCT (EDI-OCT)

[[Enhanced depth imaging OCT]] improves visualization of deeper structures, including the Canal of Schlemm, by optimizing the focal plane and signal-to-noise ratio for structures located deeper within the tissue. EDI-OCT can provide more detailed images of the canal and its relationship to the TM and scleral spur.

### OCT Angiography (OCTA)

[[OCT angiography]] is a functional extension of OCT that detects blood flow by comparing sequential B-scans. While the Canal of Schlemm does not normally contain blood, OCTA of the anterior segment can visualize the **episcleral and intrascleral venous plexuses** and **aqueous veins** that receive outflow from the canal. Reduced flow in these downstream vessels may indicate canal dysfunction.

### Ultrasound Biomicroscopy (UBM)

[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM) uses high-frequency ultrasound (typically 35–50 MHz) to image the anterior segment, including the angle and the Canal of Schlemm. UBM can visualize the canal as an echolucent (dark) space and is particularly useful in eyes with opaque corneas or other media opacities that preclude OCT imaging.

### Canalography and Aqueous Angiography

**Canalography** involves the injection of a viscoelastic or fluorescent tracer into the Canal of Schlemm (typically during surgery) to assess its patency and the distribution of collector channels. **Aqueous angiography** is a research technique in which a fluorescent tracer (e.g., fluorescein) is perfused into the anterior chamber, and its drainage through the conventional outflow pathway is visualized in real time using a fluorescence-equipped surgical microscope. These techniques have revealed the segmental nature of aqueous outflow and have guided the development of targeted surgical interventions.

### Tonometry and Tonography

While not directly imaging the Canal of Schlemm, [[tonometry]] (measurement of IOP) and [[tonography]] (measurement of outflow facility) provide indirect functional assessments of the conventional outflow pathway, of which the canal is the central component. Reduced outflow facility (C value < 0.18 µL/min/mmHg) suggests increased resistance at the level of the TM, canal, or collector channels.

### Summary of Diagnostic Modalities

| Modality | What It Assesses | Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[Gonioscopy]] | Angle anatomy, blood reflux into canal | Gold standard, widely available, dynamic assessment | Cannot directly visualize canal lumen; subjective |
| [[AS-OCT]] | Canal lumen dimensions, TM thickness | Non-invasive, high resolution, quantitative | Limited penetration in heavily pigmented eyes |
| [[EDI-OCT]] | Deeper canal structures | Better depth imaging | Requires specific protocols |
| [[OCTA]] | Downstream episcleral/intrascleral vasculature | Functional flow information | Does not image canal directly |
| [[UBM]] | Canal and angle in opaque media | Penetrates opaque corneas | Contact technique, lower resolution than OCT |
| Canalography / Aqueous angiography | Canal patency, collector channel distribution | Direct visualization of outflow | Invasive, primarily research/surgical use |
| [[Tonography]] | Outflow facility (indirect) | Functional assessment | Non-specific; does not localize site of resistance |

## Management and Treatment

Management of Canal of Schlemm dysfunction is directed at reducing [[intraocular pressure]] to prevent or slow [[glaucomatous optic neuropathy]]. Therapeutic strategies can be broadly categorized into pharmacological, laser, and surgical approaches, many of which specifically target the canal or its adjacent structures.

### Pharmacological Interventions

Several classes of IOP-lowering medications act, at least in part, by modulating outflow through the Canal of Schlemm and the conventional pathway:

| Drug Class | Examples | Mechanism Related to Canal of Schlemm |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Rho kinase inhibitors]]** | [[Netarsudil]] (Rhopressa) | Inhibit ROCK pathway → relax TM and canal endothelial cells → increase outflow facility, dilate canal, reduce episcleral venous pressure |
| **[[Nitric oxide donors]]** | [[Latanoprostene bunod]] (Vyzulta) — releases NO and latanoprost acid | NO relaxes TM and canal endothelium → increases conventional outflow; latanoprost acid increases uveoscleral outflow |
| **[[Parasympathomimetics]] (miotics)** | [[Pilocarpine]] | Contract longitudinal ciliary muscle → pull on scleral spur → open TM and widen Canal of Schlemm → increase conventional outflow |
| **[[Prostaglandin analogues]]** | [[Latanoprost]], [[Travoprost]], [[Bimatoprost]], [[Tafluprost]] | Primarily increase uveoscleral outflow; some evidence of modest effect on conventional pathway via ECM remodeling in TM |
| **[[Beta-adrenergic antagonists]]** | [[Timolol]], [[Betaxolol]] | Primarily reduce aqueous humor production; minimal direct effect on canal |
| **[[Alpha-adrenergic agonists]]** | [[Brimonidine]], [[Apraclonidine]] | Reduce aqueous production and may increase uveoscleral outflow; minimal direct canal effect |
| **[[Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]]** | [[Dorzolamide]], [[Brinzolamide]], [[Acetazolamide]] (oral) | Reduce aqueous humor production; no direct canal effect |

The **Rho kinase inhibitors** and **nitric oxide donors** represent the most canal-specific pharmacological agents, as they directly target the cellular and molecular mechanisms governing outflow resistance at the inner wall of the Canal of Schlemm and the JCT.

### Laser Procedures

#### Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty (SLT)

[[Selective laser trabeculoplasty]] (SLT) uses a frequency-doubled [[Nd:YAG laser]] (532 nm) to selectively target pigmented cells in the [[trabecular meshwork]] without causing thermal damage to adjacent structures. The mechanism of IOP reduction is thought to involve:

- Stimulation of TM cell division and ECM remodeling
- Recruitment of macrophages to clear debris from the TM
- Release of cytokines that enhance outflow facility
- Possible indirect effects on Canal of Schlemm endothelial function

SLT is effective in reducing IOP by approximately 20–30% and is increasingly used as a first-line treatment for [[open-angle glaucoma]].

#### Argon Laser Trabeculoplasty (ALT)

[[Argon laser trabeculoplasty]] (ALT) applies thermal laser energy to the TM, causing focal coagulative damage and contraction of the trabecular beams. This is thought to mechanically open adjacent intertrabecular spaces and increase outflow into the Canal of Schlemm. ALT has largely been supplanted by SLT due to the latter's repeatability and reduced tissue damage.

### Surgical Approaches: Minimally Invasive Glaucoma Surgery (MIGS)

The Canal of Schlemm is the primary anatomical target of many **[[minimally invasive glaucoma surgery]]** (MIGS) procedures. These procedures aim to bypass or reduce the resistance at the TM and inner wall of the canal, thereby enhancing aqueous outflow through the conventional pathway with minimal tissue disruption.

#### Canal-Based MIGS Procedures

| Procedure | Device/Technique | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Trabectome]]** | Electrosurgical ablation | Ab interno ablation of TM and inner wall of canal, creating a direct communication between the anterior chamber and canal lumen |
| **[[iStent]]** / **[[iStent inject W]]** | Titanium micro-bypass stent(s) | Implanted ab interno through the TM into the canal lumen; bypasses TM resistance and maintains canal patency |
| **[[Hydrus Microstent]]** | Nitinol scaffold (8 mm) | Implanted ab interno into the canal; scaffolds open ~90° of the canal, dilating it and maintaining patency; also bypasses TM at the point of entry |
| **[[Kahook Dual Blade]] (KDB)** | Dual-blade goniotomy knife | Ab interno excision of a strip of TM, creating a direct opening into the canal |
| **[[GATT]] (Gonioscopy-Assisted Transluminal Trabeculotomy)** | Suture or microcatheter (e.g., [[iTrack]]) | Ab interno 360° trabeculotomy; a suture or catheter is threaded through the full circumference of the canal, then pulled to unroof the canal and create a complete cleft into the anterior chamber |
| **[[Ab interno canaloplasty]] (ABiC)** | [[iTrack]] microcatheter | Catheter is threaded 360° through the canal, viscoelastic is injected to dilate the canal and open collector channels; no permanent implant |
| **[[OMNI Surgical System]]** | Combined canaloplasty + trabeculotomy | Allows 360° viscodilation of the canal (canaloplasty) followed by 180° trabeculotomy, addressing both canal and TM resistance |
| **[[Excimer Laser Trabeculostomy]] (ELT)** | Excimer laser | Creates small channels through the TM into the canal using photoablation |

#### Traditional Canaloplasty

**[[Canaloplasty]]** (ab externo) is a non-penetrating surgical procedure in which a microcatheter (e.g., iTrack) is introduced into the Canal of Schlemm via an ab externo approach (through a scleral flap, similar to a [[deep sclerectomy]]). The catheter is advanced 360° around the canal, and viscoelastic ([[sodium hyaluronate]]) is injected to dilate the canal and open collector channel ostia. A tensioning suture ([[prolene]]) is then placed within the canal and tightened to maintain the canal in a distended state, reducing outflow resistance. Canaloplasty avoids the creation of a filtering bleb and has a favorable safety profile.

### Surgical Approaches: Filtering and Drainage Procedures

When canal-based interventions are insufficient, procedures that bypass the conventional outflow pathway entirely may be necessary:

- **[[Trabeculectomy]]**: Creates a fistula from the anterior chamber to the subconjunctival space, bypassing the TM and canal entirely. Aqueous drains into a filtering [[bleb]].
- **[[Glaucoma drainage devices]]** (e.g., [[Ahmed valve]], [[Baerveldt implant]], [[Molteno implant]]): Shunt aqueous from the anterior chamber to an equatorial plate, bypassing the canal.
- **[[XEN Gel Stent]]**: Ab interno subconjunctival microstent that creates a direct pathway from the anterior chamber to the subconjunctival space.

These procedures do not target the Canal of Schlemm directly but are relevant in the context of canal dysfunction that is refractory to canal-based interventions.

## Emerging Research and Future Directions

### Molecular and Cellular Biology

- **Schlemm's canal endothelial cell mechanobiology**: Ongoing research is elucidating how mechanical forces (shear stress, pressure gradients) regulate pore formation, gene expression, and barrier function in inner wall endothelial cells. Key signaling pathways under investigation include [[PIEZO1]] (mechanosensitive ion channel), [[KLF2]]/[[KLF4]] transcription factors, and the [[Wnt]]/[[β-catenin]] pathway.
- **Lymphatic identity and development**: The discovery that Schlemm's canal endothelium expresses lymphatic markers ([[PROX1]], [[VEGFR-3]], [[FOXC2]]) has opened new avenues for understanding canal development, maintenance, and potential regeneration. The canal develops postnatally from a venous vascular plexus that undergoes lymphatic reprogramming, a process dependent on [[VEGF-C]]/[[VEGFR-3]] signaling.
- **Gene therapy**: Preclinical studies are exploring the delivery of genes encoding outflow-enhancing factors (e.g., [[matrix metalloproteinases]], [[VEGF-C]], [[eNOS]]) directly to the TM and canal endothelium to restore outflow facility.

### Advanced Imaging

- **Phase-sensitive OCT** and **adaptive optics OCT** are being developed to provide even higher-resolution, three-dimensional imaging of the Canal of Schlemm and collector channels in vivo.
- **Real-time aqueous angiography** during MIGS procedures is being used to identify high-flow and low-flow regions of the canal, enabling surgeons to target interventions to areas of greatest dysfunction.

### Novel Pharmacological Targets

- **PIEZO1 agonists**: Activation of the mechanosensitive channel PIEZO1 in canal endothelial cells may enhance pore formation and outflow facility.
- **VEGF-C supplementation**: Exogenous VEGF-C may promote canal maintenance and prevent age-related or glaucoma-related canal degeneration.
- **Adenosine receptor agonists**: Targeting [[adenosine receptors]] (e.g., A1, A2A) in the TM and canal may modulate outflow resistance.

## Summary

The Canal of Schlemm is a uniquely specialized, circumferential vascular channel that serves as the critical gateway for aqueous humor drainage from the eye. Its inner wall endothelium, with its giant vacuoles and transcellular pores, represents the principal site of outflow resistance in the conventional pathway. Dysfunction of the canal—through narrowing, endothelial stiffening, loss of pores, ECM accumulation, or collector channel obstruction—is a central mechanism in the pathogenesis of [[primary open-angle glaucoma]]. Modern diagnostic imaging (AS-OCT, UBM, aqueous angiography) allows increasingly detailed assessment of canal structure and function, while a rapidly expanding array of canal-targeted pharmacological agents (Rho kinase inhibitors, NO donors) and surgical procedures (MIGS, canaloplasty) offer the potential for more physiological and effective IOP reduction. Continued advances in molecular biology, imaging technology, and surgical innovation promise to further refine our understanding and management of this critical structure.
