

# Heterophoria

---

**Heterophoria** (from Greek: *heteros* = different, *phoria* = tendency to bear) is a latent deviation of the visual axes that is maintained in alignment by the [[fusional vergence]] mechanism. Unlike [[heterotropia]] (manifest strabismus), in which misalignment is constantly present, heterophoria becomes apparent only when [[binocular fusion]] is disrupted—such as during cover testing or prolonged visual fatigue. Heterophoria is an extremely common finding in the general population; indeed, perfect alignment of the visual axes (orthophoria) is relatively rare. While many individuals with heterophoria remain entirely asymptomatic due to adequate compensatory fusional reserves, a significant subset develops a constellation of asthenopic symptoms that can profoundly affect quality of life, academic performance, and occupational productivity. The clinical evaluation, quantification, and management of heterophoria constitute a cornerstone of optometric practice and [[binocular vision]] assessment.

---

## Epidemiology

Heterophoria is ubiquitous. Population-based studies consistently demonstrate that the vast majority of individuals exhibit some degree of latent deviation. Estimates suggest that **70–80%** of the general population has a measurable phoria, with [[exophoria]] (latent outward deviation) being the most prevalent type at near fixation distances, and small [[esophoria]] or orthophoria being more common at distance.

| **Type** | **Prevalence (approximate)** | **Typical Fixation Distance** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Exophoria]] | 60–70% of population | More common at near |
| [[Esophoria]] | 5–10% of population | Can occur at distance or near |
| [[Hyperphoria]] | 2–5% of population | Distance and near |
| [[Orthophoria]] | 10–20% of population | Both distances |
| [[Cyclophoria]] | Rare in isolation | Both distances |

The prevalence of **symptomatic** heterophoria (also termed **decompensated heterophoria**) is considerably lower, estimated at approximately **10–15%** of the population. Symptomatic heterophoria is more frequently encountered in patients with high visual demands, such as students, computer workers, and individuals engaged in prolonged near-point tasks.

---

## Classification

Heterophoria is classified according to the direction of the latent deviation:

### By Direction of Deviation

| **Classification** | **Direction of Latent Deviation** | **Fusional Demand** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Esophoria]]** | Inward (nasal) deviation | Requires [[fusional divergence]] (negative fusional vergence) to maintain alignment |
| **[[Exophoria]]** | Outward (temporal) deviation | Requires [[fusional convergence]] (positive fusional vergence) to maintain alignment |
| **[[Hyperphoria]]** | Upward deviation of one eye | Requires vertical fusional vergence |
| **[[Hypophoria]]** | Downward deviation of one eye (equivalent to contralateral hyperphoria) | Requires vertical fusional vergence |
| **[[Cyclophoria]]** (Incyclophoria / Excyclophoria) | Torsional deviation around the anteroposterior axis | Requires cyclovergence |

### By Distance of Fixation

- **Distance heterophoria**: Measured at optical infinity (typically 6 meters or 20 feet).
- **Near heterophoria**: Measured at the habitual near working distance (typically 40 cm or 16 inches).

### By Etiology

- **Anatomical (static) heterophoria**: Arising from structural factors such as orbital geometry, [[extraocular muscle]] insertion anomalies, or facial asymmetry.
- **Innervational (dynamic) heterophoria**: Arising from imbalances in the tonic, accommodative, proximal, or fusional vergence innervation.
- **Sensory heterophoria**: Secondary to reduced visual acuity in one eye (e.g., from [[amblyopia]], [[cataract]], or [[macular degeneration]]) that weakens the fusional stimulus.

### Compensated vs. Decompensated Heterophoria

A critical clinical distinction exists between:

- **Compensated heterophoria**: The fusional vergence reserves are sufficient to overcome the phoria without symptoms. Sheard's criterion and Percival's criterion (discussed below) are met.
- **Decompensated heterophoria**: The fusional vergence reserves are inadequate relative to the magnitude of the phoria, resulting in asthenopic symptoms, intermittent diplopia, or breakdown into manifest strabismus ([[heterotropia]]).

## Pathophysiology

### The Vergence System and Phoria

The maintenance of [[binocular single vision]] (BSV) depends on the precise alignment of the two visual axes on the object of regard. This alignment is achieved through the [[vergence]] system, which comprises four principal components as described by Maddox:

1. **Tonic vergence**: The baseline vergence position determined by the resting tone of the [[extraocular muscles]], typically slightly divergent (the anatomical position of rest).
2. **Accommodative vergence**: The vergence response driven by the [[accommodation]]-vergence synkinesis, mediated through the [[AC/A ratio]] (accommodative convergence to accommodation ratio).
3. **Proximal (psychic) vergence**: Vergence driven by the awareness of nearness of an object.
4. **Fusional (disparity) vergence**: The reflexive vergence response driven by [[retinal disparity]], which acts as the final corrective mechanism to achieve and maintain bifoveal fixation.

**Heterophoria represents the vergence posture that exists when fusional vergence is eliminated.** In other words, the phoria is the algebraic sum of tonic, accommodative, and proximal vergence demands minus the actual vergence demand for the given fixation distance. When fusion is intact, fusional vergence compensates for any residual misalignment (the phoria), maintaining bifoveal fixation.

### Maddox's Classification of Vergence Components

Maddox conceptualized the total vergence demand as:

> **Total Vergence = Tonic Vergence + Accommodative Vergence + Proximal Vergence + Fusional Vergence**

When fusional vergence is removed (e.g., by occluding one eye), the eyes assume the phoric position. The magnitude and direction of the phoria therefore reflect the net imbalance among the non-fusional vergence components relative to the vergence demand.

### Factors Contributing to Heterophoria

Multiple anatomical and physiological factors contribute to the development and magnitude of heterophoria:

- **Orbital anatomy**: The [[orbits]] are oriented approximately 23° laterally from the midsagittal plane, creating a natural divergent tendency. Variations in orbital geometry, [[interpupillary distance]], and globe size influence the resting position.
- **Extraocular muscle factors**: Variations in the length, elasticity, insertion point, and innervational tone of the six [[extraocular muscles]] (the four [[rectus muscles]]—[[medial rectus]], [[lateral rectus]], [[superior rectus]], [[inferior rectus]]—and two [[oblique muscles]]—[[superior oblique]], [[inferior oblique]]) contribute to phoria magnitude.
- **Refractive error**: [[Uncorrected refractive error]], particularly [[hyperopia]], influences accommodative vergence through the AC/A ratio. Uncorrected hyperopia increases accommodative demand, which in turn drives accommodative convergence, potentially producing esophoria. Conversely, myopic overcorrection can produce a similar effect. [[Anisometropia]] may contribute to vertical or horizontal phoria through differential prismatic effects.
- **AC/A ratio abnormalities**: A high [[AC/A ratio]] amplifies the vergence response to accommodation, predisposing to esophoria at near. A low AC/A ratio may predispose to exophoria at near (convergence insufficiency pattern).
- **Tonic vergence abnormalities**: Alterations in the baseline tonic innervation to the extraocular muscles shift the phoric posture.
- **Age-related changes**: Presbyopic changes reduce accommodative amplitude, altering the accommodative vergence contribution and potentially unmasking or modifying a pre-existing phoria. Additionally, age-related weakening of fusional vergence reserves may lead to decompensation of a previously compensated phoria.
- **General health factors**: Fatigue, illness, medications (particularly [[sedatives]], [[anticonvulsants]], and [[anticholinergics]]), alcohol consumption, and systemic conditions can reduce fusional vergence amplitude and precipitate decompensation.
- **Neurological factors**: Subtle cranial nerve palsies (particularly of [[cranial nerve III]], [[cranial nerve IV]], or [[cranial nerve VI]]) may present initially as a phoria before progressing to a tropia.

### Decompensation Mechanism

Decompensation of heterophoria occurs when the demand on fusional vergence exceeds the available fusional vergence reserves. This can result from:

1. **Increased phoria magnitude** (e.g., due to new refractive correction, progressive muscle imbalance, or neurological change).
2. **Decreased fusional vergence reserves** (e.g., due to fatigue, illness, aging, or medication effects).
3. **Increased visual demand** (e.g., prolonged near work, computer use, or visually demanding occupational tasks).

The relationship between phoria magnitude and fusional reserves is quantified clinically using **Sheard's criterion** and **Percival's criterion** (see Diagnosis section).

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Asymptomatic Heterophoria

The majority of individuals with heterophoria are entirely asymptomatic. Their fusional vergence reserves comfortably compensate for the latent deviation, and binocular vision is maintained without conscious effort or discomfort.

### Symptomatic (Decompensated) Heterophoria

When fusional reserves are insufficient, patients may present with a wide range of symptoms collectively termed **[[asthenopia]]** (eye strain). Symptoms are characteristically:

- **Worse with prolonged visual tasks** (reading, computer work, driving)
- **Worse toward the end of the day**
- **Relieved by closing or covering one eye**
- **Exacerbated by fatigue, illness, or stress**

#### Common Symptoms

| **Symptom Category** | **Specific Symptoms** |
|---|---|
| **Ocular discomfort** | Eye strain, aching eyes, burning sensation, heaviness of eyelids, periorbital pain |
| **Visual symptoms** | Blurred vision (especially at near), intermittent [[diplopia]], difficulty changing focus between distances, words "running together" during reading |
| **Headache** | Frontal headache, temporal headache, occipital headache (especially with vertical phorias); typically worse after visual tasks |
| **Reading difficulties** | Loss of place, re-reading lines, reduced reading speed, reduced comprehension, avoidance of reading |
| **Systemic symptoms** | Nausea, dizziness, motion sensitivity, general fatigue, difficulty concentrating |
| **Postural adaptations** | Head tilt (especially with vertical or cyclophoria), abnormal head posture to minimize deviation |

#### Symptom Patterns by Phoria Type

- **[[Esophoria]]**: Symptoms typically worse at near; associated with convergence excess patterns; may report blurring at near, difficulty relaxing focus after near work.
- **[[Exophoria]]**: Symptoms may occur at distance (divergence excess) or near ([[convergence insufficiency]]); intermittent diplopia, especially when fatigued; difficulty sustaining near focus.
- **[[Hyperphoria]]**: Often the most symptomatic type even at small magnitudes (1–2 prism diopters); vertical fusional reserves are physiologically limited (typically 2–4 prism diopters); headache, asthenopia, and compensatory head tilt are common.

## Diagnosis

The diagnosis and quantification of heterophoria require systematic clinical evaluation using a combination of subjective and objective techniques. The goal is not merely to identify and measure the phoria but to determine whether it is **compensated or decompensated** and to guide management accordingly.

### History

A thorough case history is essential and should explore:

- Nature, onset, duration, and frequency of symptoms
- Relationship of symptoms to visual tasks, time of day, and fatigue
- Occupational and recreational visual demands
- Previous spectacle or prism prescriptions
- History of [[strabismus]], [[amblyopia]], or [[strabismus surgery]]
- General health, medications, and neurological history

### Cover Test

The **[[cover test]]** is the gold-standard clinical technique for detecting and differentiating heterophoria from heterotropia.

- **[[Unilateral cover test]] (cover-uncover test)**: Detects manifest deviation (tropia). The examiner observes the uncovered eye for a fixation movement when the fellow eye is covered. No movement of the uncovered eye indicates the absence of tropia.
- **[[Alternating cover test]]**: Disrupts fusion completely by alternately occluding each eye. The examiner observes the direction and magnitude of the recovery movement of the eye as it is uncovered. This reveals the **total deviation** (phoria + tropia). In pure heterophoria, the alternating cover test reveals a deviation, but the unilateral cover test shows no movement of the uncovered eye.

The direction of the recovery movement indicates the type of phoria:

| **Recovery Movement** | **Phoria Type** |
|---|---|
| Outward to inward (temporal to nasal) | [[Esophoria]] |
| Inward to outward (nasal to temporal) | [[Exophoria]] |
| Downward to upward | [[Hyperphoria]] (of that eye) |
| Upward to downward | [[Hypophoria]] (of that eye) |

### Quantification of Heterophoria

#### Prism Cover Test

The **[[prism cover test]]** (also called the **prism neutralization test**) combines the alternating cover test with a [[prism bar]] or loose prisms to neutralize the deviation. The prism power that eliminates the recovery movement quantifies the phoria in [[prism diopters]] (Δ).

#### Von Graefe Technique

The **[[von Graefe technique]]** is a subjective method performed using the [[phoropter]]. A dissociating prism (typically 6Δ base-up before one eye) separates the images, and a measuring prism (typically base-in for horizontal measurement) is reduced until the patient reports vertical alignment of the two targets. This technique measures the phoria at both distance and near.

#### Maddox Rod Test

The **[[Maddox rod]]** consists of a series of fused cylindrical lenses (typically red) that convert a point light source into a streak perpendicular to the axis of the cylinders. When placed before one eye, the Maddox rod dissociates the eyes by presenting dissimilar images (a streak to one eye and a point light to the other). The patient reports the position of the streak relative to the light, and prisms are used to achieve superimposition, quantifying the phoria. The Maddox rod is particularly useful for measuring **vertical** and **torsional** phorias.

#### Maddox Wing

The **[[Maddox wing]]** is a handheld instrument that uses septum-based dissociation to measure heterophoria at near (33 cm). One eye sees arrows (horizontal and vertical) while the other sees calibrated scales. The patient reports where the arrows point on the scales, directly indicating the magnitude and direction of the phoria.

#### Modified Thorington Test

The **[[Modified Thorington test]]** uses a Maddox rod before one eye and a tangent scale with a central light source. The patient reports where the streak falls on the numbered scale, providing a rapid and reliable measurement of the phoria. This test has demonstrated excellent **test-retest reliability** and is widely used in clinical practice and research.

#### Howell Phoria Card

The **[[Howell phoria card]]** is a near-point card that uses polaroid dissociation to measure horizontal and vertical phorias at near. It is a convenient screening tool.

### Assessment of Fusional Vergence Reserves

Measuring the phoria alone is insufficient; the clinician must also assess the **fusional vergence reserves** to determine whether the phoria is compensated.

#### Prism Vergence Testing (Step Vergences)

Using a [[prism bar]] or phoropter, increasing prism power is placed before one eye while the patient fixates a target. The endpoints recorded are:

- **Blur point**: The prism power at which the target first blurs (indicates the limit of fusional vergence before accommodative compensation occurs).
- **Break point**: The prism power at which the patient reports diplopia (fusion is lost).
- **Recovery point**: The prism power at which single vision is restored as prism is reduced.

| **Vergence Type** | **Prism Direction** | **Expected Norms (Near)** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Positive fusional vergence]] (convergence) | Base-out | Blur: 17Δ, Break: 21Δ, Recovery: 11Δ (Morgan's norms) |
| [[Negative fusional vergence]] (divergence) | Base-in | Blur: 13Δ, Break: 21Δ, Recovery: 13Δ (Morgan's norms) |
| Vertical fusional vergence | Base-up / Base-down | Break: 2–4Δ |

#### Smooth Vergence Testing

Smooth (ramp) vergences can be measured using a [[rotary prism]] in the phoropter, providing a more physiological assessment of vergence dynamics.

### Sheard's Criterion

**[[Sheard's criterion]]** states that for comfortable binocular vision, the compensating fusional vergence reserve (the reserve that opposes the phoria) should be **at least twice the magnitude of the phoria**.

- For **exophoria**: The positive fusional vergence (base-out to blur) should be ≥ 2× the exophoria.
- For **esophoria**: The negative fusional vergence (base-in to blur) should be ≥ 2× the esophoria.

**Example**: A patient with 6Δ exophoria at near should have a positive fusional vergence blur point of at least 12Δ base-out at near.

Failure to meet Sheard's criterion suggests the phoria is likely **decompensated** and symptomatic.

### Percival's Criterion

**[[Percival's criterion]]** states that for comfortable binocular vision, the smaller fusional vergence reserve should be **at least one-half the magnitude of the larger fusional vergence reserve**. This criterion is independent of the phoria direction and focuses on the balance between positive and negative fusional vergence.

### 1:1 Rule (Saladin's Criterion)

For vertical phorias, the **1:1 rule** is often applied: the vertical fusional vergence reserve should be at least equal to the vertical phoria for comfortable binocular vision. Given the limited vertical fusional reserves (typically 2–4Δ), even small vertical phorias (1–2Δ) can be symptomatic.

### Fixation Disparity Analysis

**[[Fixation disparity]]** is a small residual misalignment of the visual axes (typically < 0.5°) that persists even during bifoveal fusion. It represents the vergence "error signal" that drives fusional vergence. Fixation disparity analysis, using instruments such as the **[[Sheedy disparometer]]**, **[[Wesson fixation disparity card]]**, or **[[Mallet unit]]**, provides a more sensitive assessment of binocular stress than phoria measurement alone.

The **associated phoria** (the prism that reduces fixation disparity to zero) is considered by many clinicians to be the optimal prismatic correction for symptomatic heterophoria, as it represents the minimum prism needed to eliminate binocular stress.

### Additional Diagnostic Considerations

- **[[AC/A ratio]]**: Calculated using the gradient method (change in phoria per diopter of accommodative stimulus change) or the calculated (heterophoria) method. The AC/A ratio helps classify the phoria pattern and guides management (e.g., plus lenses for high AC/A esophoria).
- **[[Near point of convergence]] (NPC)**: Receded NPC (> 10 cm) is a hallmark of [[convergence insufficiency]], a common cause of symptomatic exophoria at near.
- **[[Accommodative function]]**: Assessment of [[accommodative amplitude]], [[accommodative facility]], and [[accommodative response]] (using [[dynamic retinoscopy]] or [[MEM retinoscopy]]) is essential, as accommodative dysfunction frequently coexists with and exacerbates heterophoria.

## Differential Diagnosis

It is critical to differentiate heterophoria from conditions that may present similarly:

| **Condition** | **Key Differentiating Feature** |
|---|---|
| [[Heterotropia]] (manifest strabismus) | Deviation present on unilateral cover test; fusion disrupted |
| [[Intermittent strabismus]] | Deviation intermittently manifest; may alternate between phoria and tropia |
| [[Convergence insufficiency]] | A specific pattern of exophoria at near with receded NPC and reduced PFV; a subset of heterophoria |
| [[Convergence excess]] | A specific pattern of esophoria at near, often with high AC/A ratio |
| [[Cranial nerve palsy]] (III, IV, VI) | Acute onset, may be comitant or incomitant; neuroimaging may be required |
| [[Decompensated vertical phoria]] | May mimic posterior fossa pathology; careful evaluation needed |
| [[Accommodative dysfunction]] | May coexist with phoria; isolated accommodative insufficiency can cause similar symptoms |

## Management and Treatment

The management of heterophoria is guided by the principle that **treatment is indicated only when the phoria is symptomatic (decompensated)** or when there is a significant risk of decompensation. Asymptomatic, compensated phorias generally require monitoring but not active intervention.

### Optical Correction of Refractive Error

The first step in managing any heterophoria is ensuring optimal [[refractive correction]]. Uncorrected or inappropriately corrected refractive error can significantly influence the phoria through the accommodative vergence mechanism.

- **[[Hyperopia]]**: Correction of significant hyperopia reduces accommodative demand, thereby reducing accommodative convergence and alleviating esophoria.
- **[[Myopia]]**: Avoiding overcorrection of myopia prevents excessive accommodative demand at near.
- **[[Anisometropia]]**: Correction may introduce differential prismatic effects (via [[Prentice's rule]]) that must be considered, particularly in vertical phorias.

### Prismatic Correction

**[[Prism]]** correction is a primary optical treatment for decompensated heterophoria, particularly when vision therapy is not feasible or has been unsuccessful.

#### Prescribing Principles

- **Sheard's criterion prism**: The minimum prism that satisfies Sheard's criterion. Calculated as: **Prism = (2/3 × Phoria) − (1/3 × Compensating fusional vergence blur point)**.
- **Associated phoria prism**: The prism that eliminates fixation disparity, measured using the Mallet unit or similar instrument. Many practitioners consider this the most clinically relevant prismatic prescription.
- **Partial prism correction**: It is common practice to prescribe only a portion (typically 1/3 to 1/2) of the total measured phoria to avoid disrupting the patient's adaptive vergence mechanisms and to prevent prism adaptation.
- **Vertical prism**: Vertical phorias are more frequently corrected with prism than horizontal phorias due to the limited vertical fusional reserves. Even 0.5–1.0Δ of vertical prism can provide significant symptomatic relief.

#### Prism Delivery Methods

| **Method** | **Description** | **Indication** |
|---|---|---|
| **Ground-in prism** | Prism incorporated into spectacle lenses during fabrication | Stable, long-term correction |
| **[[Fresnel prism]]** | Thin, press-on plastic prism applied to spectacle lens | Temporary or diagnostic use; higher powers available; reduces visual acuity slightly |
| **Prism in progressive lenses** | Prism can be incorporated into [[progressive addition lenses]] | Combined presbyopic and phoria correction |

### Vision Therapy (Orthoptics)

**[[Vision therapy]]** (also known as **[[orthoptic exercises]]** or **vergence therapy**) is the treatment of choice for many forms of symptomatic heterophoria, particularly exophoria with convergence insufficiency. The goal is to improve fusional vergence amplitude, facility, and stamina.

#### Evidence Base

The **Convergence Insufficiency Treatment Trial (CITT)** provided Level I evidence that office-based vergence/accommodative therapy is superior to home-based pencil push-ups, home-based computer vergence therapy, and placebo therapy for symptomatic convergence insufficiency in children and adults.

#### Common Vision Therapy Techniques

| **Technique** | **Target** | **Description** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Pencil push-ups]]** | Convergence | Patient fixates a near target and slowly brings it closer, maintaining single vision; limited efficacy as sole treatment |
| **[[Brock string]]** | Fusional vergence, convergence | A string with beads at various distances; patient practices converging and diverging to achieve physiological diplopia patterns |
| **[[Vectograms]] / [[Tranaglyphs]]** | Fusional vergence (flat fusion, stereopsis) | Polarized or red-green targets that allow variable vergence demand |
| **[[Aperture rule]]** | Fusional vergence | Instrument with septum and sliding targets for convergence and divergence training |
| **[[Prism flipper exercises]]** | Vergence facility | Alternating base-in and base-out prisms to train rapid vergence changes |
| **[[Computer-based vergence therapy]]** | Vergence amplitude and facility | Software programs (e.g., HTS, VividVision) providing interactive vergence training |
| **[[Loose prism jumps]]** | Step vergence | Rapid introduction and removal of prisms to train vergence step responses |
| **[[Stereoscope-based training]]** | Fusional vergence, stereopsis | Instruments such as the [[Brewster stereoscope]] or [[Bernell-O-Scope]] |

#### Therapy Protocols

- **Office-based therapy**: Typically 12–24 sessions (once or twice weekly, 45–60 minutes each) with concurrent home reinforcement exercises.
- **Home-based therapy**: May be appropriate for motivated patients with mild decompensation; less effective than office-based therapy for convergence insufficiency per CITT data.
- **Maintenance therapy**: Periodic reinforcement exercises may be necessary to prevent regression, particularly in patients with large phorias.

### Lens Modifications

#### Plus Lenses for Near (Esophoria)

In patients with esophoria at near, particularly those with a **high AC/A ratio**, prescribing additional plus power for near tasks (e.g., [[bifocal]] lenses or [[progressive addition lenses]]) reduces accommodative demand, thereby reducing accommodative convergence and alleviating the esophoria.

#### Minus Lenses for Near (Exophoria)

In select cases of exophoria at near with a high AC/A ratio, reducing plus power at near (or adding minus) can stimulate accommodative convergence. However, this approach is less commonly employed and must be used judiciously to avoid accommodative fatigue.

### Pharmacological Interventions

Pharmacological management plays a limited but specific role in heterophoria management:

- **[[Miotics]] (e.g., [[pilocarpine]])**: Rarely used; historically employed to stimulate accommodation and accommodative convergence in divergence excess patterns.
- **[[Atropine]] penalization**: Occasionally used in pediatric esophoria management to reduce accommodative convergence by paralyzing accommodation, though this is more commonly applied in [[accommodative esotropia]] (a tropia, not a phoria).
- **Botulinum toxin ([[Botox]])**: Has been investigated for injection into extraocular muscles to modify phoria magnitude, but this is not standard practice for heterophoria and is reserved for specific strabismus indications.

### Surgical Intervention

**[[Strabismus surgery]]** (extraocular muscle surgery) is rarely indicated for heterophoria alone. Surgical intervention may be considered in the following circumstances:

- **Large-magnitude heterophoria** that is persistently symptomatic despite optimal optical correction, prism therapy, and vision therapy.
- **Heterophoria with frequent decompensation to intermittent tropia** that is socially or functionally debilitating.
- **Vertical heterophoria** of significant magnitude (e.g., > 8–10Δ) that cannot be adequately managed with prism.
- **Heterophoria secondary to identifiable anatomical cause** (e.g., [[superior oblique palsy]] presenting as hyperphoria).

Surgical procedures include:

| **Procedure** | **Mechanism** | **Indication** |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Recession]]** | Weakening procedure; muscle insertion moved posteriorly | Overacting muscle |
| **[[Resection]]** | Strengthening procedure; muscle shortened | Underacting muscle |
| **[[Adjustable suture surgery]]** | Allows postoperative fine-tuning of muscle position | Precise alignment needed |
| **[[Tucking procedure]]** (e.g., superior oblique tuck) | Strengthening of lax muscle | [[Superior oblique palsy]] |

### Environmental and Ergonomic Modifications

Practical modifications can significantly reduce symptoms in patients with decompensated heterophoria:

- **Optimizing working distance**: Ensuring appropriate near working distance (Harmon distance: elbow to knuckle distance).
- **Adequate lighting**: Reducing glare and ensuring appropriate illumination.
- **Regular breaks**: The **20-20-20 rule** (every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds) reduces sustained vergence and accommodative demand.
- **Screen positioning**: Ensuring computer screens are at appropriate height and distance.
- **Reducing visual clutter**: Simplifying the visual environment during symptomatic periods.

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Heterophoria

In children, decompensated heterophoria can significantly impact academic performance and reading development. Children may not articulate visual symptoms clearly, and behavioral signs (avoidance of near tasks, short attention span during reading, covering one eye) should raise clinical suspicion. Early identification and management—particularly through vision therapy—can yield excellent outcomes.

### Presbyopic Patients

The onset of [[presbyopia]] alters the accommodative-vergence relationship. Patients who were previously compensated may decompensate as accommodative amplitude decreases. The introduction of [[reading glasses]] or [[progressive lenses]] changes the accommodative demand and may unmask or modify a pre-existing phoria. Careful binocular vision assessment is essential when prescribing presbyopic corrections.

### Post-Concussion / Traumatic Brain Injury

**[[Traumatic brain injury]]** (TBI), including [[concussion]], frequently results in new-onset or worsened heterophoria, particularly vertical and torsional phorias. The term **[[binocular vision dysfunction]]** post-TBI encompasses a spectrum of findings including decompensated heterophoria, convergence insufficiency, and accommodative dysfunction. These patients often require a combination of prism correction and vision therapy, and management may be prolonged.

## Prognosis

The prognosis for symptomatic heterophoria is generally excellent with appropriate management:

- **Vision therapy**: Success rates of 70–90% have been reported for convergence insufficiency (the most studied form of symptomatic exophoria), with sustained improvement at 1-year follow-up per CITT data.
- **Prismatic correction**: Provides immediate symptomatic relief in the majority of patients; however, prism adaptation may occur over time, necessitating periodic reassessment.
- **Optical correction**: Correction of underlying refractive error alone may resolve symptoms in a significant proportion of patients.
- **Surgical correction**: Reserved for refractory cases; outcomes are generally favorable but carry the risks inherent to any surgical procedure.

Without treatment, decompensated heterophoria may lead to:

- Chronic asthenopia and reduced quality of life
- Avoidance of visual tasks with academic or occupational consequences
- Progressive decompensation to intermittent or constant [[heterotropia]]
- Development of [[suppression]] or [[anomalous retinal correspondence]] (particularly in children)

---

## Summary

Heterophoria is a latent misalignment of the visual axes that is maintained in check by fusional vergence. While the vast majority of individuals with heterophoria are asymptomatic, decompensation produces a significant burden of asthenopic symptoms that can impair daily function. Comprehensive clinical evaluation—including phoria measurement, fusional vergence reserve assessment, and application of diagnostic criteria such as Sheard's and Percival's criteria—is essential for identifying patients who require intervention. Management options span a continuum from refractive correction and ergonomic modifications through prism therapy and vision therapy to, rarely, surgical intervention. With appropriate diagnosis and management, the prognosis for symptomatic heterophoria is excellent.
