

# Mydriasis

**Mydriasis** (from the Greek *mydríasis*, meaning "dilation of the pupil") is the physiological or pathological dilation of the [[pupil]] of the eye, resulting from contraction of the [[dilator pupillae muscle]] (radial muscle of the [[iris]]) and/or relaxation of the [[sphincter pupillae muscle]] (constrictor muscle of the iris). Mydriasis represents one of the two cardinal abnormalities of pupillary size, the other being [[miosis]] (pupillary constriction). In clinical optometric and ophthalmologic practice, mydriasis may be encountered as a pharmacologically induced state for diagnostic or therapeutic purposes, as a physiological response to environmental stimuli, or as a pathological sign indicative of underlying neurological, pharmacological, or ocular disease.

Understanding the mechanisms, clinical significance, differential diagnosis, and management of mydriasis is essential for every eye care practitioner, as the pupillary examination remains one of the most informative and readily accessible components of the neuro-ophthalmic assessment.

---

## Etymology and Historical Context

The term "mydriasis" has been used in medical literature since antiquity. [[Galen]] described pupillary dilation in the context of neurological disease, and the use of [[belladonna]] (from the Italian *bella donna*, meaning "beautiful woman") by Renaissance-era women to dilate their pupils for cosmetic purposes represents one of the earliest documented instances of pharmacological mydriasis. The active compound in belladonna, [[atropine]], remains one of the most potent mydriatic agents in clinical use today.

The formal study of pupillary physiology accelerated in the 19th century with the work of [[Douglas Argyll Robertson]], who described the pupillary abnormality associated with [[neurosyphilis]], and later with the contributions of [[Irene Loewenfeld]], whose exhaustive treatise *The Pupil: Anatomy, Physiology, and Clinical Applications* remains the definitive reference on pupillary science.

## Anatomy and Physiology of the Pupil

### Relevant Iris Anatomy

The [[iris]] is a thin, circular, pigmented diaphragm situated between the [[anterior chamber]] and the [[posterior chamber]] of the eye. It contains two smooth muscles that are antagonistic in function:

| Muscle | Innervation | Action | Fiber Orientation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Sphincter pupillae]]** (pupillary sphincter) | [[Parasympathetic nervous system]] via [[cranial nerve III]] ([[oculomotor nerve]]) | Constriction ([[miosis]]) | Circular (concentric around the pupil margin) |
| **[[Dilator pupillae]]** (pupillary dilator) | [[Sympathetic nervous system]] via the long [[ciliary nerves]] | Dilation (**mydriasis**) | Radial (extending from the pupillary margin to the iris root) |

The resting pupil size at any given moment represents the dynamic equilibrium between the tonic activity of these two muscles, modulated by the [[autonomic nervous system]].

### Parasympathetic Pathway (Pupillary Constriction)

The parasympathetic pathway responsible for pupillary constriction follows a well-defined anatomical course:

1. **Preganglionic neurons** originate in the [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]] (accessory oculomotor nucleus) in the dorsal [[midbrain]].
2. Preganglionic fibers travel within the [[oculomotor nerve]] (CN III), running superficially along the nerve trunk, making them vulnerable to compressive lesions.
3. These fibers synapse in the [[ciliary ganglion]], located in the posterior [[orbit]].
4. **Postganglionic fibers** travel via the [[short ciliary nerves]] to innervate the [[sphincter pupillae]] and the [[ciliary muscle]] (responsible for [[accommodation]]).

Disruption at any point along this pathway results in loss of pupillary constriction and, consequently, unopposed mydriasis.

### Sympathetic Pathway (Pupillary Dilation)

The sympathetic pathway responsible for pupillary dilation is a three-neuron arc:

1. **First-order neurons** (central neurons) originate in the [[hypothalamus]] and descend through the [[brainstem]] and [[spinal cord]] to the [[ciliospinal center of Budge]] (C8–T2).
2. **Second-order neurons** (preganglionic neurons) exit the spinal cord, travel over the [[lung apex]], and ascend along the [[carotid artery]] to synapse in the [[superior cervical ganglion]].
3. **Third-order neurons** (postganglionic neurons) travel along the [[internal carotid artery]], through the [[cavernous sinus]], and enter the orbit via the [[long ciliary nerves]] to innervate the [[dilator pupillae]] and [[Müller's muscle]] of the upper eyelid.

Activation of this pathway produces mydriasis, lid retraction, and mild [[exophthalmos]] (the reverse of [[Horner syndrome]]).

### Pupillary Light Reflex

The [[pupillary light reflex]] is the primary reflex arc governing pupillary constriction in response to light:

1. Light stimulates [[photoreceptors]] ([[rods]] and [[cones]]) and intrinsically photosensitive [[retinal ganglion cells]] (ipRGCs) containing [[melanopsin]].
2. Afferent signals travel via the [[optic nerve]] (CN II), [[optic chiasm]], and [[optic tract]] to the [[pretectal nucleus]] in the [[midbrain]].
3. Bilateral projections from the pretectal nucleus reach both [[Edinger-Westphal nuclei]], producing both a direct and [[consensual pupillary response]].
4. Efferent signals travel via CN III to the ciliary ganglion and then to the sphincter pupillae.

Mydriasis occurs when this reflex arc is interrupted (afferent or efferent defect) or when the sympathetic system is pharmacologically or pathologically activated.

## Pathophysiology

Mydriasis can be classified based on its underlying mechanism into several broad categories:

### 1. Pharmacological Mydriasis

Pharmacological mydriasis is the most common form encountered in clinical practice and is produced by two principal drug classes:

- **Parasympatholytics (anticholinergics/muscarinic antagonists):** These agents block [[muscarinic receptors]] (specifically M3 subtype) on the sphincter pupillae, preventing [[acetylcholine]]-mediated constriction. Examples include [[tropicamide]], [[cyclopentolate]], [[homatropine]], [[scopolamine]], and [[atropine]]. The resulting mydriasis is due to unopposed sympathetic tone on the dilator pupillae.

- **Sympathomimetics (adrenergic agonists):** These agents stimulate [[alpha-1 adrenergic receptors]] on the dilator pupillae, causing active contraction and dilation. Examples include [[phenylephrine]] (a direct alpha-1 agonist) and [[hydroxyamphetamine]] (an indirect sympathomimetic that releases [[norepinephrine]] from nerve terminals).

| Agent | Class | Mechanism | Onset | Duration | Cycloplegia |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| [[Tropicamide]] 0.5%–1% | Anticholinergic | M3 receptor blockade | 15–30 min | 4–6 hours | Minimal |
| [[Phenylephrine]] 2.5%–10% | Sympathomimetic | Alpha-1 agonist | 15–30 min | 3–5 hours | None |
| [[Cyclopentolate]] 0.5%–2% | Anticholinergic | M3 receptor blockade | 25–75 min | 6–24 hours | Significant |
| [[Homatropine]] 2%–5% | Anticholinergic | M3 receptor blockade | 30–60 min | 24–72 hours | Moderate |
| [[Atropine]] 0.5%–1% | Anticholinergic | M3 receptor blockade | 30–40 min | 7–14 days | Profound |
| [[Scopolamine]] 0.25% | Anticholinergic | M3 receptor blockade | 20–30 min | 3–7 days | Significant |

Inadvertent pharmacological mydriasis can occur through accidental exposure to anticholinergic substances, including certain plants ([[Datura stramonium]], [[Atropa belladonna]]), transdermal scopolamine patches (when residue is transferred to the eye by the fingers), nebulized [[ipratropium bromide]], and various over-the-counter medications.

### 2. Physiological Mydriasis

Under normal physiological conditions, the pupil dilates in response to:

- **Decreased ambient illumination** (darkness): Reduced stimulation of the pupillary light reflex leads to decreased parasympathetic tone and relative sympathetic dominance.
- **Sympathetic activation ("fight-or-flight" response):** Emotional arousal, fear, pain, and stress activate the [[sympathetic nervous system]], producing mydriasis along with other systemic effects (tachycardia, diaphoresis).
- **Near-to-far refocusing:** The [[near response]] (accommodation, convergence, miosis) relaxes when gaze shifts to distance, producing relative mydriasis.
- **Cognitive effort and arousal:** Pupillary dilation has been demonstrated to correlate with cognitive load, emotional processing, and arousal states, mediated by the [[locus coeruleus]]–noradrenergic system.

### 3. Pathological Mydriasis

Pathological mydriasis may result from a wide range of neurological, traumatic, and ocular conditions:

#### a. Cranial Nerve III (Oculomotor) Palsy

Compressive lesions of the [[oculomotor nerve]]—most critically a [[posterior communicating artery aneurysm]]—can produce a fixed, dilated pupil (often the earliest sign) due to compression of the superficially located parasympathetic fibers. This constitutes a **neurological emergency**. Other causes include [[uncal herniation]] (transtentorial herniation), [[cavernous sinus]] lesions, and orbital apex pathology.

Key distinguishing features:
- Mydriasis is typically **unilateral**
- Associated with [[ptosis]], [[diplopia]], and limitation of [[extraocular movements]] (the eye is classically "down and out")
- Pupil is poorly reactive or non-reactive to light

#### b. Tonic Pupil ([[Adie's Tonic Pupil]])

[[Adie's tonic pupil]] results from damage to the [[ciliary ganglion]] or [[short ciliary nerves]], producing postganglionic parasympathetic denervation of the sphincter pupillae. Characteristic features include:

- Unilateral mydriasis (bilateral in 4% per year)
- Sluggish or absent light reaction with **light-near dissociation** (better constriction to near than to light)
- **Vermiform (segmental) iris movements** visible on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]
- **Denervation supersensitivity** to dilute [[pilocarpine]] (0.0625%–0.125%), which constricts the tonic pupil but not a normal pupil (the basis of the pharmacological diagnostic test)
- Often associated with diminished [[deep tendon reflexes]] ([[Holmes-Adie syndrome]])

#### c. Traumatic Mydriasis

Direct blunt or penetrating [[ocular trauma]] can damage the [[sphincter pupillae]] muscle or its nerve supply, producing a fixed, dilated pupil. This is termed **traumatic mydriasis** or **traumatic iridoplegia**. Features include:

- History of ocular or periocular trauma
- Irregular pupil shape due to sphincter tears (visible as [[transillumination defects]] or notches at the pupillary margin)
- May be associated with [[iridodialysis]], [[hyphema]], [[angle recession]], or [[lens subluxation]]
- Often permanent if sphincter damage is extensive

#### d. Pharmacological Mydriasis (Inadvertent or Intentional)

As discussed above, exposure to mydriatic agents—whether intentional (clinical dilation) or inadvertent (accidental contact with anticholinergic substances)—is a common cause of unilateral or bilateral mydriasis. This is an important differential diagnosis to exclude before pursuing neuroimaging.

#### e. Acute Angle-Closure Crisis

Mid-dilation of the pupil (semi-mydriasis) is a well-recognized precipitant of [[acute angle-closure glaucoma]] in anatomically predisposed eyes (shallow [[anterior chamber]], thick [[crystalline lens]], short [[axial length]], plateau iris configuration). The mid-dilated pupil maximizes contact between the iris and lens, exacerbating [[pupillary block]] and causing forward bowing of the peripheral iris to occlude the [[trabecular meshwork]]. This is a critical consideration when performing pharmacological mydriasis.

#### f. Other Causes of Pathological Mydriasis

- **[[Seizures]]:** Bilateral mydriasis may occur during or after generalized tonic-clonic seizures.
- **[[Migraine]]:** Ophthalmoplegic migraine (now classified as recurrent painful ophthalmoplegic neuropathy) can produce transient mydriasis.
- **[[Botulism]]:** Bilateral mydriasis with impaired accommodation is an early sign of [[Clostridium botulinum]] toxicity.
- **Brainstem lesions:** Dorsal midbrain syndrome ([[Parinaud syndrome]]) can produce bilateral mid-dilated pupils with light-near dissociation.
- **Iris damage from [[uveitis]] or surgery:** Posterior [[synechiae]], iris atrophy, or surgical trauma (e.g., after [[cataract surgery]]) can produce a fixed or irregular mydriatic pupil.
- **Systemic drug effects:** Systemic anticholinergics, sympathomimetics, [[serotonin syndrome]], and recreational drugs (e.g., [[cocaine]], [[methamphetamine]], [[MDMA]], [[LSD]]) can produce bilateral mydriasis.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Symptoms Reported by Patients

Patients with mydriasis—whether pharmacological, physiological, or pathological—may report the following symptoms:

- **[[Photophobia]] (light sensitivity):** The dilated pupil allows excessive light to enter the eye, causing discomfort, particularly in bright environments. This is the most common symptom.
- **[[Glare]] and [[haloes]]:** Increased light scatter from the dilated pupil produces glare disability and perception of haloes around point light sources, especially at night.
- **Blurred near vision:** When mydriasis is accompanied by [[cycloplegia]] (paralysis of the [[ciliary muscle]]), the patient loses the ability to [[accommodate]], resulting in blurred vision at near. This is particularly prominent with anticholinergic agents such as [[atropine]] and [[cyclopentolate]].
- **Cosmetic concern:** Patients may notice or be told that their pupil appears abnormally large, which can cause anxiety.
- **Headache:** Photophobia-related headache may occur, particularly in sustained mydriasis.

### Signs Observed on Examination

| Sign | Description | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Enlarged pupil diameter | Pupil diameter >5 mm in normal room illumination (or significant asymmetry, [[anisocoria]] >1 mm) | Indicates mydriasis; must determine laterality and reactivity |
| Absent or sluggish direct light reflex | Pupil fails to constrict or constricts poorly when light is directed into the affected eye | Suggests efferent parasympathetic dysfunction (CN III palsy, Adie's, pharmacological blockade) |
| Absent or sluggish consensual reflex | Contralateral pupil fails to constrict when light is directed into the affected eye | Suggests afferent defect if ipsilateral direct reflex is also absent |
| [[Anisocoria]] | Asymmetry of pupil size between the two eyes | Greater in light suggests the larger pupil is pathological (parasympathetic defect or pharmacological mydriasis) |
| Light-near dissociation | Pupil constricts better to a near target than to light | Characteristic of [[Adie's tonic pupil]] and dorsal midbrain lesions |
| Vermiform (segmental) iris movements | Irregular, worm-like contractions of iris sectors visible on slit-lamp examination | Pathognomonic for Adie's tonic pupil (segmental palsy of the sphincter) |
| Sphincter tears/transillumination defects | Visible notches or defects at the pupillary margin | Indicates traumatic sphincter damage |
| Associated [[ptosis]] and [[ophthalmoplegia]] | Drooping eyelid and restricted eye movements | Suggests CN III palsy—requires urgent neuroimaging |

## Diagnosis

### Clinical History

A thorough clinical history is paramount in evaluating mydriasis and should include:

- **Onset and duration:** Acute vs. chronic; episodic vs. persistent
- **Laterality:** Unilateral vs. bilateral
- **Associated symptoms:** Pain, headache, diplopia, ptosis, blurred vision, photophobia
- **Medication history:** Use of topical or systemic anticholinergics, sympathomimetics, or other mydriatic agents; use of transdermal scopolamine patches; recent nebulizer treatments
- **Trauma history:** Recent ocular or head trauma
- **Recreational drug use:** Cocaine, amphetamines, MDMA, LSD, cannabis
- **Occupational/environmental exposure:** Contact with plants (jimsonweed, belladonna), chemicals
- **Neurological symptoms:** Weakness, numbness, speech difficulty, altered consciousness (suggesting intracranial pathology)

### Pupillary Examination

The pupillary examination should be performed systematically:

1. **Inspection in ambient light:** Note pupil size, shape, and symmetry. Measure pupil diameter with a pupil gauge or [[infrared pupillometry]].
2. **Assessment in dim illumination:** Evaluate for anisocoria that increases in darkness (suggesting sympathetic defect, i.e., [[Horner syndrome]] on the smaller side) vs. anisocoria that increases in light (suggesting parasympathetic defect or pharmacological mydriasis on the larger side).
3. **Direct light reflex:** Shine a bright light into each eye and observe the ipsilateral pupillary response.
4. **Consensual light reflex:** Observe the contralateral pupil's response when light is directed into the tested eye.
5. **[[Swinging flashlight test]]:** Alternate the light between the two eyes to detect a [[relative afferent pupillary defect]] (RAPD or [[Marcus Gunn pupil]]), which indicates asymmetric afferent pathway disease (optic nerve or extensive retinal pathology). Note: An RAPD does not cause mydriasis per se but is essential to differentiate afferent from efferent causes.
6. **Near response:** Ask the patient to fixate on a near target and observe for pupillary constriction (miosis component of the [[near triad]]).
7. **Slit-lamp examination:** Examine the iris for structural abnormalities (sphincter tears, posterior synechiae, iris atrophy, vermiform movements).

### Pharmacological Testing

Pharmacological testing is invaluable in differentiating the causes of mydriasis:

| Test | Agent | Expected Result | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pilocarpine 1%** | [[Pilocarpine]] 1% (full-strength cholinergic agonist) | Normal pupil: constricts. Pharmacologically blocked pupil (e.g., atropine): **does not constrict** | Failure to constrict confirms pharmacological blockade (anticholinergic exposure) |
| **Dilute pilocarpine 0.0625%–0.125%** | Dilute [[pilocarpine]] | Normal pupil: no significant constriction. Adie's tonic pupil: **constricts** (denervation supersensitivity) | Constriction to dilute pilocarpine confirms postganglionic parasympathetic denervation (Adie's) |
| **Cocaine 4%–10%** | [[Cocaine]] (blocks norepinephrine reuptake) | Normal pupil: dilates. Horner syndrome: **fails to dilate** | Used to confirm Horner syndrome (the miotic pupil), not mydriasis per se, but important in the differential of anisocoria |
| **Hydroxyamphetamine 1%** | [[Hydroxyamphetamine]] (releases stored norepinephrine) | Normal pupil: dilates. Third-order Horner's: fails to dilate | Localizes Horner syndrome to postganglionic neuron |

The **pilocarpine test** is the single most important pharmacological test in the evaluation of a fixed, dilated pupil. If 1% pilocarpine fails to constrict the pupil, pharmacological blockade is confirmed, and neuroimaging can be avoided. If the pupil constricts to 1% pilocarpine but also constricts to dilute pilocarpine (0.0625%–0.125%), Adie's tonic pupil is confirmed.

### Neuroimaging

When a fixed, dilated pupil is accompanied by signs of [[oculomotor nerve palsy]] (ptosis, ophthalmoplegia) and pharmacological blockade has been excluded, **urgent neuroimaging** is mandatory. The primary concern is a compressive lesion, most critically a [[posterior communicating artery aneurysm]].

- **[[CT angiography]] (CTA)** or **[[MR angiography]] (MRA)** of the head is the initial imaging modality of choice to evaluate for intracranial aneurysm.
- **[[Magnetic resonance imaging]] (MRI)** of the brain and orbits with contrast may be indicated to evaluate for other compressive or infiltrative lesions.
- **Conventional [[cerebral angiography]]** (digital subtraction angiography) remains the gold standard for detecting small aneurysms if non-invasive imaging is inconclusive.

### Additional Diagnostic Considerations

- **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)** of the [[anterior segment]] can provide detailed imaging of iris structure and may reveal sphincter damage.
- **[[Infrared pupillometry]]** provides objective, quantitative measurement of pupil size and dynamics, useful for monitoring and documentation.
- **[[Anterior segment photography]]** documents pupil size, shape, and iris abnormalities for longitudinal comparison.

## Differential Diagnosis of Mydriasis

The differential diagnosis of a dilated pupil can be organized by mechanism:

| Category | Condition | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Pharmacological** | Anticholinergic exposure (topical or systemic) | Fixed, dilated pupil; does not constrict to 1% pilocarpine |
| **Pharmacological** | Sympathomimetic exposure | Dilated pupil; constricts to 1% pilocarpine (sphincter not blocked) |
| **Neurological** | CN III palsy (compressive) | Mydriasis + ptosis + ophthalmoplegia; EMERGENCY |
| **Neurological** | CN III palsy (ischemic/microvascular) | Usually pupil-sparing; mydriasis less common |
| **Neurological** | [[Adie's tonic pupil]] | Sluggish light reaction; light-near dissociation; denervation supersensitivity |
| **Neurological** | Dorsal midbrain syndrome ([[Parinaud syndrome]]) | Bilateral mid-dilated pupils; light-near dissociation; upgaze palsy |
| **Traumatic** | Sphincter rupture/traumatic iridoplegia | History of trauma; irregular pupil; transillumination defects |
| **Inflammatory** | Posterior synechiae from [[anterior uveitis]] | Irregular pupil; may be fixed in dilation if 360° synechiae |
| **Surgical** | Post-surgical iris damage | History of intraocular surgery; irregular pupil |
| **Systemic** | Recreational drug use (cocaine, amphetamines, MDMA) | Bilateral mydriasis; systemic signs of intoxication |
| **Systemic** | [[Serotonin syndrome]] | Bilateral mydriasis; agitation, hyperthermia, clonus |
| **Systemic** | [[Botulism]] | Bilateral mydriasis; descending paralysis; impaired accommodation |
| **Physiological** | Darkness, emotional arousal, cognitive effort | Bilateral; reactive; resolves with stimulus removal |
| **Benign** | [[Benign episodic unilateral mydriasis]] (BEUM) | Episodic; often in young women; associated with migraine; self-resolving |

## Management and Treatment

The management of mydriasis depends entirely on its underlying etiology. The following sections outline treatment strategies organized by cause.

### 1. Pharmacologically Induced Mydriasis (Clinical)

When mydriasis is intentionally induced for diagnostic purposes (e.g., [[dilated fundus examination]], [[cycloplegic refraction]]), management is primarily supportive:

- **Patient education:** Inform patients about expected duration of dilation, photophobia, and near blur.
- **Protective eyewear:** Provide disposable sunglasses or advise patients to wear their own sunglasses.
- **Driving precautions:** Advise patients that driving may be impaired, particularly in bright conditions or if cycloplegia is present.
- **Reversal agents:** [[Dapiprazole]] (Rev-Eyes, 0.5%) was previously available as an alpha-adrenergic antagonist to reverse phenylephrine-induced mydriasis, though it has been discontinued in many markets. Dilute [[pilocarpine]] (0.5%–1%) can be used off-label to hasten pupillary constriction after dilation, though this is not routine practice.
- **Monitoring for angle closure:** In patients with narrow [[anterior chamber angles]] (assessed by [[Van Herick technique]], [[gonioscopy]], or [[anterior segment OCT]]), the risk of [[acute angle-closure glaucoma]] must be assessed before dilation. Prophylactic [[laser peripheral iridotomy]] (LPI) may be considered in high-risk patients.

### 2. Inadvertent Pharmacological Mydriasis

- **Identify and remove the offending agent** (e.g., wash hands after handling scopolamine patches; discontinue nebulized ipratropium if causing unilateral mydriasis via mask leak).
- **Reassurance:** The mydriasis will resolve spontaneously once the agent is cleared.
- **Documentation:** Record the finding and its cause to prevent unnecessary neuroimaging.

### 3. Adie's Tonic Pupil

- **Reassurance:** Adie's tonic pupil is a benign condition in the vast majority of cases.
- **Dilute pilocarpine (0.0625%–0.125%):** May be prescribed for symptomatic relief of photophobia and glare, as the denervated sphincter is supersensitive to cholinergic stimulation. However, long-term use is often impractical.
- **Photochromic or tinted lenses:** Reduce photophobia.
- **Monitoring:** Annual examination to monitor for bilateral involvement and to reassess deep tendon reflexes.
- **Workup for underlying cause:** In atypical cases (bilateral, young patient, associated systemic findings), consider evaluation for [[sarcoidosis]], [[syphilis]], [[herpes zoster]], [[diabetes mellitus]], [[Guillain-Barré syndrome]], or other causes of autonomic neuropathy.

### 4. Compressive CN III Palsy

- **This is a neurological emergency.** Immediate referral for neuroimaging (CTA or MRA) and neurosurgical consultation is mandatory.
- **Treatment is directed at the underlying cause:** Aneurysm clipping or coiling for [[posterior communicating artery aneurysm]]; surgical decompression for mass lesions; medical management for [[uncal herniation]].
- **The mydriasis itself is not treated directly** but is expected to improve if the compressive lesion is addressed promptly.

### 5. Traumatic Mydriasis

- **Acute management:** Treat associated injuries (hyphema, lens subluxation, retinal pathology).
- **Observation:** Mild traumatic mydriasis may resolve spontaneously over weeks to months as the sphincter muscle recovers.
- **Symptomatic treatment:** Tinted lenses or photochromic lenses for photophobia. Prosthetic contact lenses with an artificial iris print can be used for cosmesis and light reduction in cases of permanent, severely dilated pupils.
- **Surgical options:** In severe, symptomatic cases with permanent sphincter damage, [[iris reconstruction surgery]] (iris cerclage suture, artificial iris implantation such as the [[CustomFlex Artificial Iris]] or [[HumanOptics]] device) may be considered. [[Pupilloplasty]] techniques using suture-based methods can mechanically reduce pupil size.

### 6. Mydriasis Secondary to Uveitis (Posterior Synechiae)

- **Aggressive treatment of the underlying [[uveitis]]** with topical [[corticosteroids]] and [[cycloplegic agents]].
- **Synechiolysis:** Intensive mydriatic-cycloplegic therapy (e.g., atropine 1%, phenylephrine 2.5%–10%) to break posterior synechiae. Paradoxically, mydriatics are used here to mobilize the iris and prevent permanent pupillary distortion.
- **[[Nd:YAG laser]] synechiolysis** may be attempted in refractory cases.

### 7. Drug-Induced Bilateral Mydriasis (Systemic)

- **Identify and treat the underlying cause:** Discontinue or adjust the offending systemic medication if possible.
- **Serotonin syndrome, anticholinergic toxicity, and sympathomimetic overdose** require emergency medical management (supportive care, specific antidotes such as [[cyproheptadine]] for serotonin syndrome or [[physostigmine]] for anticholinergic toxicity).
- **Mydriasis resolves** as the systemic condition is treated.

### 8. Benign Episodic Unilateral Mydriasis (BEUM)

- **Reassurance** is the primary management.
- **Migraine prophylaxis** may reduce episode frequency if BEUM is associated with [[migraine]].
- **Documentation** of episodes and associated symptoms helps confirm the diagnosis over time.

## Special Considerations

### Mydriasis in Pediatric Patients

- [[Cyclopentolate]] and [[atropine]] are the most commonly used agents for [[cycloplegic refraction]] in children.
- **Systemic toxicity** is a greater concern in pediatric patients due to lower body weight. Anticholinergic toxicity (flushing, fever, tachycardia, agitation, hallucinations) can occur, particularly with cyclopentolate and atropine. Nasolacrimal occlusion (punctal occlusion) after instillation reduces systemic absorption.
- **Atropine penalization** (low-dose atropine 0.01%–1% in the dominant eye) is used therapeutically to treat [[amblyopia]] by inducing mydriasis and cycloplegia, blurring the better eye to encourage use of the amblyopic eye.

### Mydriasis and Intraocular Pressure

- Pharmacological mydriasis can precipitate [[acute angle-closure glaucoma]] in anatomically predisposed eyes. Risk factors include [[hyperopia]], shallow anterior chamber depth (<2.5 mm), short axial length, thick lens, and plateau iris configuration.
- Conversely, in eyes with open angles, mydriasis may transiently **decrease** [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) by increasing [[uveoscleral outflow]] or may have minimal effect.
- Pre-dilation assessment of anterior chamber depth (Van Herick grading ≥ Grade 2 is generally considered safe) is standard practice.

### Mydriasis in Comatose or Critically Ill Patients

- **Bilateral fixed, dilated pupils** in a comatose patient are an ominous sign, potentially indicating [[brainstem death]], bilateral uncal herniation, or severe global cerebral injury.
- However, pharmacological causes must always be excluded (e.g., atropine administered during resuscitation, topical mydriatics applied for fundoscopy in the ICU).
- The **pilocarpine test** can be invaluable in the ICU setting to differentiate pharmacological from neurological mydriasis.

### Mydriasis and Refractive Considerations

- Mydriasis increases the effective pupil size, which can unmask higher-order [[optical aberrations]] (e.g., [[spherical aberration]], [[coma]]) and degrade retinal image quality.
- Patients who have undergone [[refractive surgery]] (e.g., [[LASIK]], [[PRK]]) with small optical zones may experience significant glare and haloes when the pupil dilates beyond the treated zone, particularly in scotopic (low-light) conditions.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of mydriasis is entirely dependent on its etiology:

- **Pharmacological mydriasis:** Excellent prognosis; fully reversible upon clearance of the agent.
- **Adie's tonic pupil:** Generally benign; the pupil tends to become progressively miotic over years ("little old Adie's") due to aberrant reinnervation, though accommodation may remain impaired.
- **Compressive CN III palsy:** Prognosis depends on the underlying lesion and the timeliness of intervention. Pupillary function may recover partially or completely after successful treatment.
- **Traumatic mydriasis:** Variable; mild cases may recover, but severe sphincter damage often results in permanent mydriasis.
- **Drug-induced systemic mydriasis:** Resolves with treatment of the underlying condition.

---

## Summary

Mydriasis is a multifaceted clinical finding that spans the spectrum from benign physiological response to life-threatening neurological emergency. The eye care practitioner must be adept at recognizing the various presentations of mydriasis, systematically evaluating the pupil using clinical and pharmacological testing, and initiating appropriate management or referral. The ability to distinguish pharmacological mydriasis from a compressive [[oculomotor nerve]] palsy—using the simple but powerful pilocarpine test—can be literally life-saving.
