

# Thyroid Eye Disease

---

**Thyroid Eye Disease** (TED), also known as **Graves' orbitopathy** (GO), **Graves' ophthalmopathy**, or **thyroid-associated ophthalmopathy** (TAO), is a complex, sight-threatening, disfiguring autoimmune inflammatory disorder of the [[orbit]] and periorbital tissues. It is the most common extrathyroidal manifestation of [[Graves' disease]] and represents the most frequent cause of both unilateral and bilateral [[proptosis]] in adults. TED is characterized by an active inflammatory phase followed by a fibrotic, quiescent phase, and its clinical severity ranges from mild, self-limiting disease to severe, vision-threatening [[optic neuropathy]] and [[corneal]] decompensation. The condition profoundly impacts patients' quality of life, affecting visual function, ocular comfort, facial appearance, and psychosocial well-being.

---

## Epidemiology

TED occurs in approximately **25–50%** of patients with [[Graves' disease]], although subclinical orbital involvement detectable on imaging may be present in up to 70% of Graves' patients. The annual incidence is estimated at **16 per 100,000 women** and **2.9 per 100,000 men**. The female-to-male ratio is approximately **5:1** for mild disease, but this ratio narrows to approximately **1.4:1** for moderate-to-severe disease, suggesting that males are more likely to develop severe manifestations.

| **Epidemiological Parameter** | **Value** |
|---|---|
| Prevalence in Graves' disease | 25–50% (clinical); up to 70% (subclinical on imaging) |
| Annual incidence (females) | ~16 per 100,000 |
| Annual incidence (males) | ~2.9 per 100,000 |
| Female-to-male ratio (mild) | ~5:1 |
| Female-to-male ratio (severe) | ~1.4:1 |
| Peak age of onset (females) | 40–44 years and 60–64 years (bimodal) |
| Peak age of onset (males) | 45–49 years and 65–69 years (bimodal) |
| Bilateral involvement | ~85–95% of cases |
| Sight-threatening disease | ~3–5% of TED patients |

TED demonstrates a bimodal age distribution, with peaks in the fifth and seventh decades of life. While the vast majority of TED cases (approximately 80–90%) occur in the setting of [[hyperthyroidism]] due to Graves' disease, it can also occur in patients who are [[euthyroid]] (approximately 5–10%) or even [[hypothyroid]] (approximately 1–3%), including those with [[Hashimoto's thyroiditis]]. This underscores that TED is fundamentally an orbital autoimmune process sharing antigenic targets with the thyroid gland, rather than a direct consequence of thyroid hormone excess.

## Risk Factors

Several modifiable and non-modifiable risk factors have been identified for the development and progression of TED:

- **Cigarette smoking**: The single most significant modifiable risk factor. Smoking increases the risk of developing TED by **7–8-fold**, worsens disease severity, reduces the efficacy of treatment (including [[immunosuppression]] and [[radiotherapy]]), and increases the likelihood of disease progression after [[radioactive iodine]] (RAI) therapy. The mechanism involves increased orbital [[hypoxia]], generation of [[reactive oxygen species]], enhanced [[adipogenesis]], upregulation of [[glycosaminoglycan]] (GAG) production, and stimulation of [[fibroblast]] proliferation.
- **Radioactive iodine (RAI) therapy**: RAI treatment for hyperthyroidism can exacerbate or precipitate TED in approximately 15–20% of patients, particularly in smokers and those with pre-existing active eye disease. This is thought to be mediated by the release of thyroid antigens following RAI-induced thyroid destruction, which amplifies the autoimmune response. Concurrent prophylactic [[glucocorticoid]] therapy can mitigate this risk.
- **Thyroid dysfunction**: Both uncontrolled hyperthyroidism and post-treatment hypothyroidism are associated with worsening of TED. Rapid restoration and maintenance of a stable [[euthyroid]] state is critical.
- **High serum [[TSH receptor antibody]] (TRAb) levels**: Elevated TRAb titers correlate with disease activity, severity, and risk of progression.
- **Age**: Older patients tend to develop more severe disease with greater [[extraocular muscle]] involvement and higher risk of [[dysthyroid optic neuropathy]].
- **Sex**: Males are at higher risk for severe disease.
- **Genetic predisposition**: Associations with [[HLA-DR3]], [[CTLA-4]] polymorphisms, and other immune-regulatory gene variants have been identified.

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of TED is a multifaceted autoimmune process centered on the [[TSH receptor]] (TSHR) as the primary shared autoantigen between the [[thyroid gland]] and orbital tissues. The understanding of TED pathogenesis has evolved significantly, and the current model involves a complex interplay of autoimmune activation, orbital fibroblast biology, and tissue remodeling.

### Autoimmune Initiation and the Role of the TSH Receptor

In Graves' disease, loss of [[immune tolerance]] to the TSHR leads to the generation of stimulating [[autoantibodies]] (thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins, or TSI) directed against the TSHR. These antibodies activate the TSHR on [[thyroid follicular cells]], causing hyperthyroidism. Critically, the TSHR is also expressed on [[orbital fibroblasts]], [[orbital adipocytes]], and, to a lesser extent, on [[orbital connective tissue]] cells. Activation of the TSHR on orbital fibroblasts by TRAb initiates the inflammatory cascade within the orbit.

### The Central Role of Orbital Fibroblasts

[[Orbital fibroblasts]] are the primary effector cells in TED and are phenotypically distinct from fibroblasts in other anatomical locations. Key features include:

- **Overexpression of TSHR**: Orbital fibroblasts express significantly higher levels of TSHR compared to dermal fibroblasts, making them uniquely susceptible to autoimmune stimulation.
- **Expression of [[IGF-1 receptor]] (IGF-1R)**: Orbital fibroblasts overexpress IGF-1R, which forms a physical and functional complex with TSHR on the cell surface. IGF-1R signaling synergizes with TSHR activation to amplify downstream inflammatory and remodeling pathways. This TSHR/IGF-1R crosstalk is a critical pathogenic mechanism and a key therapeutic target.
- **Subpopulation heterogeneity**: Orbital fibroblasts comprise at least two functionally distinct subpopulations defined by the surface marker [[Thy-1]] (CD90):
  - **Thy-1(+) fibroblasts**: Differentiate into [[myofibroblasts]], contributing to [[fibrosis]] and tissue scarring.
  - **Thy-1(−) fibroblasts**: Differentiate into mature [[adipocytes]], contributing to orbital fat expansion ([[adipogenesis]]).
- **Hyaluronan (HA) production**: Stimulated orbital fibroblasts produce massive quantities of [[hyaluronan]] (a hydrophilic [[glycosaminoglycan]]), which absorbs water and causes dramatic tissue edema and expansion within the confined bony [[orbit]].

### Inflammatory Cascade

Activation of orbital fibroblasts triggers a robust inflammatory response:

1. **T-cell infiltration**: [[CD4+ T helper cells]] (both Th1 and Th2 subtypes) and [[CD8+ cytotoxic T cells]] infiltrate the orbital tissues, recognizing TSHR-derived peptides presented by orbital fibroblasts acting as [[antigen-presenting cells]].
2. **Cytokine release**: Infiltrating T cells and activated fibroblasts release a cascade of pro-inflammatory [[cytokines]] including [[interleukin-1]] (IL-1), [[interleukin-6]] (IL-6), [[interleukin-8]] (IL-8), [[interleukin-16]] (IL-16), [[tumor necrosis factor-alpha]] (TNF-α), [[interferon-gamma]] (IFN-γ), and [[RANTES]].
3. **B-cell involvement**: [[B lymphocytes]] and [[plasma cells]] infiltrate the orbit and produce autoantibodies locally. B cells also serve as antigen-presenting cells and produce cytokines, further amplifying the immune response.
4. **Mast cell degranulation**: [[Mast cells]] are present in increased numbers in TED orbital tissue and contribute to inflammation through release of [[histamine]], [[prostaglandins]], and cytokines.
5. **Oxidative stress**: Increased generation of [[reactive oxygen species]] (ROS) and [[superoxide radicals]] within orbital tissues stimulates fibroblast proliferation and GAG synthesis, creating a self-perpetuating cycle of inflammation.

### Tissue Remodeling and Expansion

The combined effects of inflammation, GAG accumulation, adipogenesis, and edema lead to a dramatic increase in the volume of orbital soft tissues (both [[extraocular muscles]] and [[orbital fat]]) within the rigid bony orbit. This volume expansion is the fundamental mechanism underlying the cardinal clinical features of TED:

- **[[Proptosis]]**: Forward displacement of the globe due to increased retrobulbar tissue volume.
- **[[Extraocular muscle]] enlargement**: Inflammatory infiltration and subsequent fibrosis of the extraocular muscles, particularly the [[inferior rectus]], [[medial rectus]], [[superior rectus]]/[[levator palpebrae superioris]] complex, and [[lateral rectus]] (mnemonic: **I'M SLow**).
- **[[Compressive optic neuropathy]]**: Enlarged extraocular muscles, particularly at the [[orbital apex]], can compress the [[optic nerve]], threatening vision.
- **Venous congestion**: Increased orbital pressure impedes venous outflow, contributing to [[periorbital edema]], [[chemosis]], and elevated [[episcleral venous pressure]].

### Rundle's Curve: The Natural History of TED

The natural history of TED is classically described by **Rundle's curve**, which illustrates a biphasic disease course:

1. **Active (inflammatory) phase**: Lasting approximately **6 months to 3 years** (average 18–24 months), characterized by progressive inflammation, worsening signs and symptoms, and tissue expansion.
2. **Plateau phase**: A period of stabilization.
3. **Inactive (fibrotic/quiescent) phase**: Inflammation subsides, but residual fibrosis, fat expansion, and structural changes persist. Spontaneous improvement may occur but is often incomplete, leaving patients with residual proptosis, [[strabismus]], [[eyelid retraction]], and disfigurement.

Understanding where a patient falls on Rundle's curve is essential for guiding treatment decisions, as immunomodulatory therapies are most effective during the active phase, while surgical rehabilitation is generally reserved for the stable, inactive phase.

## Clinical Presentation

The clinical manifestations of TED are diverse and range from subtle periorbital changes to severe, sight-threatening complications. Symptoms and signs may be asymmetric, and unilateral presentation occurs in approximately 5–15% of cases (though imaging often reveals subclinical contralateral involvement).

### Symptoms

Patients with TED may present with a wide spectrum of symptoms:

| **Symptom Category** | **Specific Symptoms** |
|---|---|
| **Ocular surface** | Grittiness, foreign body sensation, tearing ([[epiphora]]), photophobia, burning, dryness |
| **Periorbital** | Periorbital swelling (worse in the morning), puffiness of eyelids, sensation of pressure or fullness behind the eyes |
| **Visual** | Blurred vision, decreased [[visual acuity]], [[diplopia]] (binocular), color desaturation, visual field defects |
| **Cosmetic/Psychosocial** | Prominent or bulging eyes, staring appearance, facial disfigurement, depression, anxiety, social withdrawal |
| **Pain** | Retrobulbar aching or pain (especially with eye movement), headache |

### Signs

Clinical examination reveals a constellation of characteristic findings:

#### Eyelid Signs
- **[[Eyelid retraction]]**: The most common sign of TED, present in approximately 90% of patients. Upper eyelid retraction (Dalrymple's sign) results from sympathetic overstimulation of [[Müller's muscle]], inflammation/fibrosis of the [[levator palpebrae superioris]], and/or increased levator tone secondary to inferior rectus restriction. Lower eyelid retraction also occurs.
- **[[Lid lag]]** (von Graefe's sign): The upper eyelid lags behind the globe on downgaze due to fibrosis of the levator complex.
- **Incomplete lid closure** ([[lagophthalmos]]): Results from a combination of proptosis and eyelid retraction, leading to [[exposure keratopathy]].
- **Lateral flare**: Temporal flare of the upper eyelid.
- **Fullness of the eyelids**: Due to orbital fat prolapse and periorbital edema.

#### Orbital Signs
- **[[Proptosis]]** (exophthalmos): Measured with a [[Hertel exophthalmometer]]. Normal values vary by ethnicity (typically 12–21 mm in Caucasians). Asymmetry of >2 mm or absolute values exceeding population norms are significant. Proptosis is present in approximately 60% of TED patients.
- **Resistance to retropulsion**: Increased resistance when gentle posterior pressure is applied to the globe through closed eyelids, indicating increased orbital tissue volume.

#### Extraocular Motility Signs
- **[[Restrictive strabismus]]**: Fibrosis and inflammation of the [[extraocular muscles]] cause restriction of ocular motility, most commonly affecting elevation (due to [[inferior rectus]] involvement) and abduction (due to [[medial rectus]] involvement). This produces [[diplopia]], which may be intermittent initially and become constant as fibrosis progresses.
- **Pattern of muscle involvement**: The classic pattern follows the mnemonic **I'M SLow** (Inferior rectus > Medial rectus > Superior rectus > Lateral rectus), reflecting the relative frequency of involvement.

#### Ocular Surface Signs
- **[[Conjunctival]] injection and [[chemosis]]**: Particularly over the insertions of the [[rectus muscles]], due to venous congestion and inflammation.
- **[[Superior limbic keratoconjunctivitis]]** (SLK): Inflammation of the superior [[bulbar conjunctiva]] and adjacent [[cornea]], associated with TED.
- **[[Exposure keratopathy]]**: Punctate epithelial erosions, [[corneal]] desiccation, and in severe cases, [[corneal ulceration]], [[corneal perforation]], or [[microbial keratitis]] secondary to lagophthalmos and inadequate [[Bell's phenomenon]].
- **Increased [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP)**: Particularly in upgaze, due to compression of the globe by fibrotic inferior rectus and increased [[episcleral venous pressure]].

#### Optic Nerve Signs (Sight-Threatening)
- **[[Dysthyroid optic neuropathy]]** (DON): The most serious complication of TED, occurring in approximately 3–5% of patients. It results from compression of the [[optic nerve]] at the [[orbital apex]] by enlarged extraocular muscles. Signs include:
  - Decreased [[visual acuity]]
  - [[Relative afferent pupillary defect]] (RAPD) if asymmetric
  - [[Color vision]] deficiency (particularly red desaturation)
  - [[Visual field]] defects (central scotoma, generalized constriction, arcuate defects)
  - [[Optic disc]] edema or pallor
  - Choroidal folds

**Important clinical pearl**: DON can occur even in the absence of significant proptosis. In fact, patients with less proptosis may be at *higher* risk because the tight orbital septum prevents forward decompression of the expanding orbital contents, directing pressure posteriorly toward the optic nerve apex.

## Classification and Grading Systems

Several classification and grading systems have been developed to standardize the assessment of TED severity and activity:

### NOSPECS Classification (Werner)

A historical severity grading system:

| **Class** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **0** | No signs or symptoms |
| **1** | Only signs (lid retraction, lid lag, proptosis) |
| **2** | Soft tissue involvement (edema, chemosis, injection) |
| **3** | Proptosis (≥3 mm above normal) |
| **4** | Extraocular muscle involvement (restriction, diplopia) |
| **5** | Corneal involvement (exposure keratopathy) |
| **6** | Sight loss (optic neuropathy) |

### Clinical Activity Score (CAS)

The **Clinical Activity Score** is the most widely used tool for assessing disease activity and guiding treatment decisions. It evaluates seven parameters (10 in the modified version for follow-up):

| **CAS Parameter** | **Score** |
|---|---|
| Spontaneous retrobulbar pain | 1 |
| Pain on attempted up or downgaze | 1 |
| Redness of the eyelids | 1 |
| Redness of the conjunctiva | 1 |
| Swelling of the eyelids | 1 |
| Inflammation of the caruncle and/or plica | 1 |
| Chemosis | 1 |
| **Total (initial assessment)** | **/7** |

*Additional parameters for follow-up (modified CAS /10):*
- Increase in proptosis ≥2 mm
- Decrease in motility ≥8° in any direction
- Decrease in visual acuity ≥1 Snellen line

A **CAS ≥ 3/7** indicates **active disease** and suggests the patient is likely to respond to immunosuppressive therapy.

### EUGOGO Severity Classification

The **European Group on Graves' Orbitopathy (EUGOGO)** classifies TED severity into three categories to guide management:

| **Severity** | **Criteria** |
|---|---|
| **Mild** | Minor lid retraction (<2 mm), mild soft tissue involvement, proptosis <3 mm above normal, transient or no diplopia, corneal exposure responsive to lubricants |
| **Moderate-to-Severe** | Lid retraction ≥2 mm, moderate-severe soft tissue involvement, proptosis ≥3 mm above normal, inconstant or constant diplopia |
| **Sight-Threatening** | Dysthyroid optic neuropathy (DON) and/or corneal breakdown/perforation |

## Diagnosis

The diagnosis of TED is primarily clinical, based on the characteristic constellation of signs and symptoms in the context of known or suspected thyroid autoimmune disease. However, laboratory and imaging studies play important confirmatory and prognostic roles.

### Clinical Assessment

A comprehensive ophthalmic examination should include:

- **[[Visual acuity]]** (best-corrected)
- **[[Color vision]]** testing (Ishihara plates or Hardy-Rand-Rittler)
- **[[Pupillary examination]]** (assessment for [[RAPD]])
- **[[Hertel exophthalmometry]]** (measurement of proptosis)
- **[[Eyelid measurements]]** (margin-reflex distance 1 and 2, palpebral fissure height, upper lid crease height)
- **Assessment of [[lagophthalmos]]** and [[Bell's phenomenon]]
- **[[Extraocular motility]]** assessment (versions, ductions, forced duction testing if indicated)
- **[[Diplopia]] assessment** (Hess chart, Lancaster red-green test, or prism cover test)
- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]** (conjunctival injection, chemosis, corneal staining with [[fluorescein]], [[rose bengal]], or [[lissamine green]])
- **[[Intraocular pressure]]** measurement in primary gaze and upgaze
- **[[Fundoscopy]]** (optic disc assessment, choroidal folds)
- **[[Visual field]]** testing (if optic neuropathy suspected)
- **[[Optical coherence tomography]]** (OCT) of the [[retinal nerve fiber layer]] (RNFL) and [[macula]] to detect subclinical optic neuropathy
- **Clinical Activity Score** calculation
- **Clinical photography** for documentation and monitoring

### Laboratory Investigations

| **Test** | **Clinical Significance** |
|---|---|
| **[[Thyroid-stimulating hormone]]** (TSH) | Screening for thyroid dysfunction; suppressed in hyperthyroidism |
| **Free [[thyroxine]]** (FT4) | Elevated in hyperthyroidism |
| **Free [[triiodothyronine]]** (FT3) | Elevated in hyperthyroidism; may be elevated when FT4 is normal (T3 thyrotoxicosis) |
| **[[TSH receptor antibodies]]** (TRAb) | Elevated in >95% of Graves' disease patients; correlates with TED activity and severity; useful for diagnosis in euthyroid TED |
| **Thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulin** (TSI) | Stimulating subtype of TRAb; more specific for Graves' disease and TED |
| **[[Anti-thyroid peroxidase antibodies]]** (anti-TPO) | Present in Graves' disease and Hashimoto's thyroiditis; less specific for TED |

### Imaging

Orbital imaging is invaluable for confirming the diagnosis, assessing disease extent, evaluating for optic nerve compression, and planning surgical intervention.

#### Computed Tomography (CT)
- **Gold standard** for bony orbital anatomy and surgical planning for [[orbital decompression]].
- Demonstrates characteristic **fusiform enlargement of the extraocular muscle bellies with sparing of the tendinous insertions** (distinguishing TED from [[orbital myositis]]/[[idiopathic orbital inflammatory disease]], where the tendon is typically involved).
- Reveals increased orbital fat volume.
- Demonstrates apical crowding (compression of the optic nerve by enlarged muscles at the orbital apex).
- Axial and coronal views are essential.

#### Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
- Superior soft tissue contrast compared to CT.
- **T2-weighted and STIR (Short Tau Inversion Recovery) sequences** can differentiate active inflammation (high signal intensity indicating edema) from chronic fibrosis (low signal intensity), helping to assess disease activity and predict response to immunosuppressive therapy.
- Useful for evaluating the optic nerve and detecting subtle compressive changes.
- No ionizing radiation.

#### Orbital Ultrasonography
- Can demonstrate extraocular muscle thickening and increased reflectivity.
- Less commonly used than CT or MRI but may be useful for serial monitoring.

### Differential Diagnosis

While TED is the most common cause of unilateral and bilateral proptosis in adults, other conditions must be considered, particularly in atypical presentations:

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| [[Idiopathic orbital inflammatory disease]] (orbital pseudotumor) | Tendon involvement on imaging; typically unilateral; no thyroid association; rapid response to corticosteroids |
| [[Orbital lymphoma]] | Typically painless; molding to orbital structures on imaging; biopsy required |
| [[Orbital cellulitis]] | Acute onset; fever; sinusitis history; restricted motility with pain |
| [[Cavernous sinus fistula]] / [[Carotid-cavernous fistula]] | Pulsatile proptosis; arterialized conjunctival vessels; orbital bruit |
| [[IgG4-related orbital disease]] | Bilateral lacrimal gland enlargement; elevated serum IgG4; biopsy diagnostic |
| [[Orbital metastasis]] | History of primary malignancy; rapid progression; bone destruction on imaging |
| [[Orbital meningioma]] | Slowly progressive; optic nerve sheath enhancement on MRI |

## Management

The management of TED requires a multidisciplinary approach involving [[optometry]], [[ophthalmology]] (particularly [[oculoplastic surgery]] and [[neuro-ophthalmology]]), [[endocrinology]], and in some cases [[radiation oncology]]. Treatment strategy is guided by both **disease activity** (active vs. inactive) and **disease severity** (mild, moderate-to-severe, sight-threatening).

### General Measures (All Patients)

- **Smoking cessation**: Absolutely essential. Smoking cessation is the single most impactful modifiable intervention. Patients should be counseled emphatically and referred to cessation programs.
- **Restoration and maintenance of euthyroidism**: Close collaboration with [[endocrinology]] to achieve stable thyroid function as rapidly as possible. Both hyperthyroidism and hypothyroidism exacerbate TED.
- **Selenium supplementation**: The EUGOGO trial demonstrated that **sodium selenite 100 μg twice daily for 6 months** significantly improved quality of life, reduced soft tissue involvement, and slowed disease progression in patients with mild, active TED. Selenium is an antioxidant that may reduce oxidative stress in orbital tissues.
- **Ocular surface management**: Preservative-free [[artificial tears]], [[lubricating ointments]] (especially at night), moisture chamber glasses, and taping of eyelids during sleep for lagophthalmos.
- **Elevation of the head of bed**: Reduces periorbital edema and morning swelling.
- **Prism correction**: Temporary [[Fresnel prisms]] or ground-in prisms for symptomatic diplopia.
- **Psychosocial support**: TED significantly impacts quality of life. Referral for psychological support, counseling, or patient support groups should be considered.

### Management of Mild TED

- Observation with regular monitoring (every 3–6 months).
- Selenium supplementation (as above).
- Aggressive ocular surface lubrication.
- Prism therapy for diplopia if present.
- Reassurance regarding the natural history (Rundle's curve).

### Management of Moderate-to-Severe Active TED

#### Intravenous Glucocorticoids

**Intravenous [[methylprednisolone]]** pulse therapy is the first-line treatment for moderate-to-severe active TED, as established by EUGOGO consensus guidelines. It is significantly more effective and better tolerated than oral glucocorticoids.

**Standard EUGOGO protocol:**
- **Cumulative dose: 4.5 g of methylprednisolone** over 12 weeks.
- Weeks 1–6: 500 mg IV methylprednisolone once weekly.
- Weeks 7–12: 250 mg IV methylprednisolone once weekly.

**Important considerations:**
- Cumulative dose should **not exceed 8 g** due to risk of acute hepatotoxicity and liver failure.
- Liver function tests (LFTs) must be monitored before and during treatment.
- Contraindications include active hepatitis, significant hepatic dysfunction, uncontrolled diabetes, severe cardiovascular disease, and uncontrolled hypertension.
- Side effects include insomnia, weight gain, hyperglycemia, mood changes, Cushingoid features, and rarely, hepatotoxicity.

#### Oral Glucocorticoids

Oral [[prednisone]] (starting dose typically 60–80 mg/day with gradual taper over 3–6 months) may be used when IV therapy is unavailable or contraindicated, but is associated with a higher rate of side effects and lower efficacy compared to IV pulse therapy.

#### Teprotumumab (Tepezza®)

**[[Teprotumumab]]** is a fully human monoclonal antibody that inhibits the **[[insulin-like growth factor-1 receptor]]** (IGF-1R), blocking the critical TSHR/IGF-1R signaling axis in orbital fibroblasts. It was approved by the **U.S. FDA in January 2020** and represents a paradigm shift in TED treatment.

| **Parameter** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| **Mechanism** | IGF-1R inhibitor; blocks downstream signaling of both IGF-1R and TSHR/IGF-1R complex |
| **Administration** | IV infusion every 3 weeks for 8 infusions (24-week course) |
| **Dosing** | 10 mg/kg (first infusion), then 20 mg/kg (infusions 2–8) |
| **Key trial results (OPTIC trial)** | 83% proptosis responder rate (≥2 mm reduction) vs. 10% placebo; 67% diplopia responder rate vs. 29% placebo |
| **Adverse effects** | Hyperglycemia (especially in diabetics), muscle spasms, hearing impairment/tinnitus (sometimes irreversible), alopecia, nausea, diarrhea, fatigue, dry skin, dysgeusia, headache |
| **Contraindications** | Pregnancy (teratogenic); active inflammatory bowel disease (relative) |
| **Monitoring** | Blood glucose, audiometry (baseline and during treatment) |

Teprotumumab has demonstrated remarkable efficacy in reducing proptosis, diplopia, and CAS in both active and, to some extent, chronic/stable TED, and is increasingly considered a first-line option for moderate-to-severe TED where available.

#### Orbital Radiotherapy

**[[Orbital radiotherapy]]** (typically 20 Gy in 10 fractions over 2 weeks) has anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive effects on orbital lymphocytes and fibroblasts. It is most effective when combined with glucocorticoids and is generally reserved for patients with active disease, particularly those with prominent extraocular muscle involvement and diplopia. It is contraindicated in patients under 35 years of age (due to theoretical risk of radiation-induced malignancy) and in patients with [[diabetic retinopathy]] (risk of exacerbation).

#### Other Immunosuppressive Agents

- **[[Mycophenolate mofetil]]**: Used as a steroid-sparing agent or in combination with IV glucocorticoids. The MINGO trial demonstrated that combination therapy (IV methylprednisolone + mycophenolate) was superior to IV methylprednisolone alone.
- **[[Tocilizumab]]** (anti-[[IL-6 receptor]] antibody): Emerging evidence supports its use in glucocorticoid-resistant active TED.
- **[[Rituximab]]** (anti-[[CD20]] antibody): Targets B lymphocytes; some evidence of efficacy in active TED, but results have been mixed and it carries risk of serious adverse effects.
- **[[Azathioprine]]**: May be used as maintenance therapy following IV glucocorticoids to reduce relapse rates.
- **[[Cyclosporine A]]**: Occasionally used in combination with glucocorticoids.

### Management of Sight-Threatening TED (Dysthyroid Optic Neuropathy)

DON is an **ophthalmic emergency** requiring urgent intervention:

1. **High-dose IV methylprednisolone**: 1 g IV daily for 3 consecutive days, followed by reassessment. If improvement occurs, transition to standard weekly pulse protocol. If no improvement after 1–2 weeks of high-dose IV steroids:
2. **Urgent [[orbital decompression]] surgery**: Surgical removal of one or more orbital walls to expand the orbital volume and relieve pressure on the optic nerve. This is the definitive treatment for steroid-refractory DON.
3. **Teprotumumab**: Emerging evidence suggests potential utility in DON, though urgent cases typically still require steroids and/or surgery as first-line.

### Surgical Rehabilitation (Inactive/Stable Disease)

Surgical intervention is generally reserved for the **inactive, stable phase** of TED (typically after at least 6 months of clinical stability) and follows a specific sequential approach:

| **Surgical Step** | **Procedure** | **Indication** |
|---|---|---|
| **Step 1** | [[Orbital decompression]] | Proptosis, exposure keratopathy, cosmetic rehabilitation, DON (urgent) |
| **Step 2** | [[Strabismus surgery]] | Persistent diplopia, restrictive strabismus (after decompression if performed) |
| **Step 3** | [[Eyelid surgery]] (lid lengthening, blepharoplasty) | Eyelid retraction, lagophthalmos, cosmetic rehabilitation (after strabismus surgery if performed) |

**This sequential approach is critical** because orbital decompression can alter ocular alignment, and strabismus surgery can affect eyelid position. Performing procedures out of sequence may necessitate repeat surgeries.

#### Orbital Decompression Techniques

| **Approach** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Medial wall decompression** | Removal of the [[lamina papyracea]]; accessed via transcaruncular or endoscopic approach |
| **Lateral wall decompression** | Removal of the lateral orbital wall (deep lateral wall/sphenoid bone); accessed via lateral canthotomy or coronal approach |
| **Floor decompression** | Removal of the orbital floor (with preservation of the infraorbital nerve strut); risk of new-onset diplopia and [[infraorbital nerve]] hypoesthesia |
| **Balanced decompression** | Combination of medial and lateral wall removal; reduces risk of new-onset diplopia compared to floor decompression |
| **Fat decompression** | Removal of orbital fat (intraconal and/or extraconal); may be combined with bony decompression |

## Role of the Optometrist

Optometrists play a critical role in the detection, monitoring, and co-management of TED:

- **Early detection**: Recognizing subtle signs of TED (eyelid retraction, mild proptosis, ocular surface disease) in patients with known or suspected thyroid disease.
- **Comprehensive examination**: Performing thorough assessments including Hertel exophthalmometry, motility evaluation, CAS scoring, IOP measurement in primary and upgaze, and optic nerve assessment.
- **Monitoring disease activity and progression**: Serial documentation with clinical photography, exophthalmometry, motility measurements, and visual field testing.
- **Ocular surface management**: Prescribing and managing lubricant therapy, punctal plugs, moisture chambers, and scleral contact lenses for exposure keratopathy.
- **Diplopia management**: Prescribing prism correction (Fresnel or ground-in).
- **Referral**: Timely referral to ophthalmology (oculoplastics/neuro-ophthalmology) for moderate-to-severe or sight-threatening disease, and to endocrinology for thyroid management.
- **Patient education**: Counseling on smoking cessation, the importance of thyroid control, disease natural history, and available treatments.
- **Post-surgical co-management**: Monitoring patients following orbital decompression, strabismus surgery, or eyelid surgery.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of TED is variable and depends on disease severity, activity, and the presence of risk factors. Key prognostic considerations include:

- **Mild disease**: Approximately 60–70% of patients with mild TED experience spontaneous improvement without specific immunosuppressive treatment.
- **Moderate-to-severe disease**: Requires active treatment; outcomes have improved significantly with the advent of teprotumumab and optimized glucocorticoid protocols.
- **Sight-threatening disease (DON)**: With prompt treatment (IV steroids ± urgent decompression), visual outcomes are generally favorable, though some patients may have residual visual deficits.
- **Quality of life**: Even after successful treatment, many patients report persistent impairment in quality of life related to residual cosmetic changes, diplopia, and dry eye symptoms. The Graves' Ophthalmopathy Quality of Life (GO-QoL) questionnaire is a validated tool for assessing patient-reported outcomes.
- **Recurrence**: Disease reactivation can occur, particularly in patients who continue to smoke, have poorly controlled thyroid function, or have persistently elevated TRAb levels.

---

## Emerging Therapies and Future Directions

The therapeutic landscape for TED is rapidly evolving. Several novel agents and approaches are under investigation:

- **[[Batoclimab]]**: An anti-[[FcRn]] (neonatal Fc receptor) antibody that reduces circulating IgG levels, including pathogenic TRAb. Phase 2/3 trials are underway.
- **[[Linsitinib]]**: An oral IGF-1R inhibitor being investigated as a potential alternative to IV teprotumumab.
- **Anti-TSHR antibodies**: Monoclonal antibodies specifically targeting the TSH receptor to block stimulating autoantibodies.
- **[[JAK inhibitors]]** (e.g., [[baricitinib]]): Targeting intracellular signaling pathways involved in orbital inflammation.
- **Combination therapies**: Investigating synergistic combinations of teprotumumab with glucocorticoids or other immunomodulators.
- **Biomarkers**: Development of improved biomarkers (e.g., specific TRAb subtypes, orbital imaging signatures, serum cytokine profiles) for predicting disease course and treatment response.

---

## Summary

Thyroid Eye Disease is a complex autoimmune orbital disorder that demands a thorough understanding of its pathophysiology, natural history, and evolving treatment paradigm. The optometrist serves as a frontline clinician in detecting early disease, monitoring progression, managing ocular surface complications, and coordinating multidisciplinary care. The introduction of teprotumumab has fundamentally transformed the treatment landscape, offering unprecedented reductions in proptosis and diplopia. Nonetheless, comprehensive management requires attention to modifiable risk factors (especially smoking cessation and thyroid control), accurate assessment of disease activity and severity, and timely intervention to prevent irreversible sight-threatening complications.
