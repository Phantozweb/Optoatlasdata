

# Ophthalmic Division (V₁) of the Trigeminal Nerve

---

## Overview

The **Ophthalmic Division** (also designated **V₁**) is the first and smallest of the three major branches of the [[Trigeminal Nerve]] (Cranial Nerve V). It is a purely **sensory nerve** that provides afferent innervation to the upper face, [[Orbit]], [[Globe]], [[Cornea]], [[Conjunctiva]], frontal and ethmoidal sinuses, nasal dorsum, upper eyelid, and forehead. Unlike the [[Maxillary Division]] (V₂) and [[Mandibular Division]] (V₃), the ophthalmic division carries no motor fibers. Its clinical significance in optometry and ophthalmology is immense: it mediates the [[Corneal Reflex]], governs trophic maintenance of the [[Corneal Epithelium]], and serves as a critical diagnostic landmark in conditions ranging from [[Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus]] to [[Trigeminal Neuralgia]] and [[Cavernous Sinus Thrombosis]]. A thorough understanding of V₁ anatomy, physiology, and pathology is indispensable for every eye care practitioner.

---

## Embryology

The ophthalmic division derives from the [[Neural Crest]] cells of the first (ophthalmic) placode associated with the [[Trigeminal Ganglion]] (also known as the [[Gasserian Ganglion]] or [[Semilunar Ganglion]]). During the fourth to sixth weeks of embryonic development, neural crest cells migrate anteriorly to populate the periorbital mesenchyme. The sensory neurons of V₁ establish their cell bodies within the trigeminal ganglion, located in [[Meckel's Cave]] (the trigeminal impression on the petrous apex of the [[Temporal Bone]]). Peripheral axons extend toward the developing orbit and face, while central axons project into the [[Brainstem]], specifically the [[Principal Sensory Nucleus]] and the [[Spinal Nucleus of the Trigeminal Nerve]]. Aberrations in neural crest migration can result in congenital anomalies of corneal innervation, contributing to conditions such as [[Congenital Corneal Anesthesia]] and certain forms of [[Neurotrophic Keratopathy]].

## Gross Anatomy

### Origin and Intracranial Course

The ophthalmic division arises from the anteromedial aspect of the [[Trigeminal Ganglion]] within [[Meckel's Cave]] in the [[Middle Cranial Fossa]]. From its origin, V₁ courses anteriorly within the lateral wall of the [[Cavernous Sinus]], where it lies inferior to the [[Trochlear Nerve]] (CN IV) and lateral to the [[Internal Carotid Artery]]. Within the cavernous sinus, V₁ is in close proximity to the [[Oculomotor Nerve]] (CN III), [[Trochlear Nerve]] (CN IV), and [[Abducens Nerve]] (CN VI)—a relationship of paramount clinical importance in cavernous sinus pathology.

Just before entering the orbit, V₁ divides into its three terminal branches:

1. **[[Frontal Nerve]]**
2. **[[Lacrimal Nerve]]**
3. **[[Nasociliary Nerve]]**

All three branches pass through the [[Superior Orbital Fissure]] to enter the [[Orbit]].

### Passage Through the Superior Orbital Fissure

The [[Superior Orbital Fissure]] is a cleft between the greater and lesser wings of the [[Sphenoid Bone]]. The three branches of V₁ traverse this fissure, but their precise positions differ:

| Structure | Position Within the Superior Orbital Fissure |
|---|---|
| [[Frontal Nerve]] | Passes **outside** (superior to) the [[Annulus of Zinn]] (common tendinous ring) |
| [[Lacrimal Nerve]] | Passes **outside** (lateral to) the [[Annulus of Zinn]] |
| [[Nasociliary Nerve]] | Passes **through** the [[Annulus of Zinn]], between the two divisions of the [[Oculomotor Nerve]] |

This anatomical distinction is clinically relevant because lesions within the [[Annulus of Zinn]] (e.g., [[Orbital Apex Syndrome]]) may preferentially affect the nasociliary nerve while sparing the frontal and lacrimal nerves, and vice versa.

### Terminal Branches in Detail

#### 1. Frontal Nerve

The [[Frontal Nerve]] is the largest branch of V₁. It enters the orbit through the superior orbital fissure, outside the annulus of Zinn, and courses anteriorly along the roof of the orbit beneath the [[Periorbita]] and superior to the [[Levator Palpebrae Superioris]]. It divides into two terminal branches:

- **[[Supratrochlear Nerve]]**: Exits the orbit medially, above the [[Trochlea]] of the [[Superior Oblique Muscle]]. It supplies sensation to the medial forehead, medial upper [[Eyelid]], and the skin of the root of the [[Nose]].
- **[[Supraorbital Nerve]]**: Exits the orbit through the [[Supraorbital Notch]] (or foramen). It provides sensory innervation to the forehead, scalp (as far posteriorly as the [[Vertex]]), and the central and lateral upper eyelid.

#### 2. Lacrimal Nerve

The [[Lacrimal Nerve]] is the smallest branch of V₁. It enters the orbit through the superior orbital fissure, outside the annulus of Zinn, and courses along the lateral orbital wall above the [[Lateral Rectus Muscle]]. It provides sensory innervation to the [[Lacrimal Gland]] and the lateral portion of the upper eyelid and [[Conjunctiva]].

Importantly, the lacrimal nerve also carries **parasympathetic secretomotor fibers** to the [[Lacrimal Gland]], but these fibers do not originate from V₁ itself. They arise from the [[Facial Nerve]] (CN VII) via the [[Greater Petrosal Nerve]], synapse in the [[Pterygopalatine Ganglion]], and reach the lacrimal nerve via a communicating branch from the [[Zygomatic Nerve]] (a branch of V₂). The lacrimal nerve thus serves as a conduit for these postganglionic parasympathetic fibers.

#### 3. Nasociliary Nerve

The [[Nasociliary Nerve]] is the most clinically significant branch of V₁ for eye care professionals. It is the only branch that passes through the [[Annulus of Zinn]], entering the orbit between the superior and inferior divisions of the [[Oculomotor Nerve]]. It crosses the orbit medially, passing over the [[Optic Nerve]] (CN II).

The nasociliary nerve gives off the following branches:

| Branch | Course and Distribution |
|---|---|
| **[[Long Ciliary Nerves]]** (2–3) | Pierce the [[Sclera]] near the [[Optic Nerve]] and travel between the sclera and [[Choroid]] to reach the [[Ciliary Body]], [[Iris]], and [[Cornea]]. Carry sensory fibers from the globe and sympathetic fibers to the [[Dilator Pupillae]] muscle. |
| **[[Communicating Branch to the Ciliary Ganglion]]** (sensory root) | Provides the sensory root to the [[Ciliary Ganglion]]. These fibers pass through the ganglion **without synapsing** and continue as [[Short Ciliary Nerves]] to innervate the globe. |
| **[[Posterior Ethmoidal Nerve]]** | Exits the orbit through the [[Posterior Ethmoidal Foramen]] to supply the [[Sphenoid Sinus]] and posterior [[Ethmoidal Air Cells]]. |
| **[[Anterior Ethmoidal Nerve]]** | Exits the orbit through the [[Anterior Ethmoidal Foramen]], enters the [[Anterior Cranial Fossa]], then descends through the [[Cribriform Plate]] into the nasal cavity. Gives off **internal nasal branches** and terminates as the **[[External Nasal Nerve]]**, supplying the skin of the nasal tip and ala. |
| **[[Infratrochlear Nerve]]** | Terminal branch; passes below the trochlea to supply the medial eyelids, medial conjunctiva, lacrimal sac, caruncle, and skin of the lateral nose. |

The **[[Hutchinson's Sign]]** — vesicular eruption on the tip of the nose — indicates involvement of the external nasal branch of the anterior ethmoidal nerve (a terminal branch of the nasociliary nerve) in [[Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus]], and is a strong predictor of ocular involvement because the nasociliary nerve also supplies the globe.

## Connections to the Ciliary Ganglion

The [[Ciliary Ganglion]] is a small parasympathetic ganglion located in the posterior orbit, lateral to the [[Optic Nerve]]. It receives three roots:

1. **Sensory root** — from the [[Nasociliary Nerve]] (V₁)
2. **Parasympathetic root** — from the [[Inferior Division of the Oculomotor Nerve]] (CN III), carrying preganglionic fibers from the [[Edinger-Westphal Nucleus]]
3. **Sympathetic root** — from the [[Internal Carotid Plexus]]

Only the parasympathetic fibers synapse within the ciliary ganglion. The sensory (V₁) and sympathetic fibers pass through without synapsing. All three fiber types exit the ganglion as the [[Short Ciliary Nerves]] (10–20 in number), which pierce the sclera to innervate the [[Cornea]], [[Ciliary Body]], [[Iris]], and [[Choroid]].

## Physiology and Pathophysiology

### Sensory Function

The ophthalmic division transmits **touch, pain, temperature, and proprioceptive** information from its territory. Peripheral receptors in the [[Cornea]] are among the most densely innervated sensory endings in the human body, with an estimated density of 300–600 times that of skin. These are predominantly **free nerve endings** (nociceptors, mechanoreceptors, and cold thermoreceptors) derived from the [[Long Ciliary Nerves]] and [[Short Ciliary Nerves]].

Sensory impulses travel along V₁ axons to cell bodies in the [[Trigeminal Ganglion]], then centrally to:

- **[[Principal Sensory Nucleus]]** (in the pons) — mediating fine touch and pressure
- **[[Spinal Nucleus of the Trigeminal Nerve]]** (extending from the pons to the upper cervical spinal cord) — mediating pain and temperature

From these nuclei, second-order neurons decussate and ascend via the [[Trigeminothalamic Tract]] (ventral and dorsal) to the [[Ventral Posteromedial Nucleus]] (VPM) of the [[Thalamus]], and thence to the [[Primary Somatosensory Cortex]] (postcentral gyrus).

### The Corneal Reflex Arc

The [[Corneal Reflex]] (blink reflex) is a critical protective mechanism:

- **Afferent limb**: [[Nasociliary Nerve]] → V₁ → [[Trigeminal Ganglion]] → [[Spinal Nucleus of the Trigeminal Nerve]]
- **Efferent limb**: Interneurons project to the [[Facial Motor Nucleus]] → [[Facial Nerve]] (CN VII) → [[Orbicularis Oculi]] muscle → eyelid closure

Loss of the corneal reflex afferent (V₁ lesion) results in absent blink response when the ipsilateral cornea is stimulated, but a **consensual** blink may still occur when the contralateral cornea is stimulated (since the efferent CN VII is intact bilaterally).

### Trophic Function of Corneal Nerves

Beyond sensory transmission, corneal nerves derived from V₁ release **neurotrophic factors** including [[Substance P]], [[Calcitonin Gene-Related Peptide]] (CGRP), and [[Nerve Growth Factor]] (NGF) that are essential for maintaining the health, proliferation, and wound healing of the [[Corneal Epithelium]]. Disruption of V₁ innervation leads to **[[Neurotrophic Keratopathy]]** (NK), a degenerative condition characterized by epithelial breakdown, impaired healing, stromal melting, and potential [[Corneal Perforation]].

### Autonomic Fibers Carried by V₁

Although V₁ is classified as purely sensory, it serves as a conduit for autonomic fibers:

- **Sympathetic fibers**: Travel with the [[Long Ciliary Nerves]] to the [[Dilator Pupillae]] muscle and orbital blood vessels.
- **Parasympathetic fibers**: Travel via the communicating branch from V₂ (zygomatic nerve) to the [[Lacrimal Nerve]], providing secretomotor innervation to the [[Lacrimal Gland]].

## Clinical Conditions Involving the Ophthalmic Division

### Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO)

[[Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus]] results from reactivation of [[Varicella-Zoster Virus]] (VZV) latent in the [[Trigeminal Ganglion]], with subsequent spread along V₁. It accounts for approximately 10–20% of all herpes zoster cases. Clinical features include:

- Dermatomal vesicular rash strictly respecting the midline, involving the forehead, upper eyelid, and nose
- **[[Hutchinson's Sign]]**: Vesicles on the nasal tip (external nasal nerve involvement), associated with a 76% risk of ocular complications
- Ocular manifestations: [[Keratitis]] (pseudodendrites, stromal keratitis, neurotrophic keratopathy), [[Anterior Uveitis]], [[Episcleritis]], [[Scleritis]], [[Secondary Glaucoma]], [[Cranial Nerve Palsies]] (CN III, IV, VI)
- Post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN) in the V₁ distribution

### Neurotrophic Keratopathy

[[Neurotrophic Keratopathy]] is classified into three stages (Mackie classification):

| Stage | Clinical Features |
|---|---|
| **Stage 1** | Punctate epithelial erosions, corneal edema, superficial neovascularization, decreased [[Tear Break-Up Time]] |
| **Stage 2** | Persistent epithelial defect (PED), typically oval, located in the superior or central cornea, with smooth rolled edges and surrounding loose epithelium |
| **Stage 3** | Stromal melting, [[Descemetocele]] formation, and potential [[Corneal Perforation]] |

Etiologies include HZO, surgical damage to V₁ (e.g., during treatment of [[Trigeminal Neuralgia]], [[Acoustic Neuroma]] surgery), [[Diabetes Mellitus]] (corneal neuropathy), chronic [[Contact Lens]] wear, and topical anesthetic abuse.

### Trigeminal Neuralgia

[[Trigeminal Neuralgia]] (tic douloureux) most commonly affects V₂ and V₃, but isolated V₁ involvement occurs in approximately 4–5% of cases. Patients experience paroxysmal, lancinating, electric shock-like pain in the V₁ distribution, often triggered by light touch to the forehead or upper eyelid. Causes include [[Neurovascular Compression]] (typically by the [[Superior Cerebellar Artery]]), [[Multiple Sclerosis]], and [[Cerebellopontine Angle Tumors]].

### Cavernous Sinus Syndrome

Lesions within the [[Cavernous Sinus]] (e.g., [[Cavernous Sinus Thrombosis]], [[Carotid-Cavernous Fistula]], [[Pituitary Adenoma]], [[Meningioma]], [[Tolosa-Hunt Syndrome]]) may affect V₁ along with CN III, IV, and VI, producing:

- Ophthalmoplegia
- V₁ sensory loss (forehead numbness, absent corneal reflex)
- Proptosis and chemosis (in thrombosis/fistula)
- Horner syndrome (sympathetic plexus involvement)

### Superior Orbital Fissure Syndrome

Lesions at the [[Superior Orbital Fissure]] (trauma, tumors, inflammation) affect all structures traversing it: CN III, IV, VI, and V₁ (all three branches). This produces ophthalmoplegia, ptosis, a fixed dilated pupil, and V₁ sensory loss, but **without** optic nerve involvement (distinguishing it from [[Orbital Apex Syndrome]], which additionally involves CN II).

### Orbital Apex Syndrome

[[Orbital Apex Syndrome]] involves all structures of the superior orbital fissure **plus** the [[Optic Nerve]], resulting in the features of superior orbital fissure syndrome combined with visual loss.

## Symptoms of Ophthalmic Division Pathology

Patients with V₁ dysfunction may present with:

- **Numbness or hypoesthesia** of the forehead, upper eyelid, and/or nose
- **Absent or diminished corneal reflex** (a critical early sign)
- **Neuropathic pain**: Burning, stabbing, or electric shock-like pain in the V₁ dermatome
- **Dry eye symptoms**: Due to impaired reflex tearing (afferent limb disruption) or lacrimal nerve involvement
- **Recurrent corneal erosions** or non-healing epithelial defects ([[Neurotrophic Keratopathy]])
- **Photophobia** and **foreign body sensation** (in inflammatory or infectious conditions)
- **Vesicular skin eruption** (in [[Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus]])

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

| Test | Method | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Corneal Sensitivity Testing]]** | Cotton wisp, Cochet-Bonnet [[Esthesiometer]], or Belmonte non-contact gas esthesiometer | Quantifies corneal nerve function; reduced sensitivity indicates V₁ (nasociliary) dysfunction |
| **[[Corneal Reflex Testing]]** | Light touch to cornea with cotton wisp; observe bilateral blink | Absent ipsilateral and consensual blink = afferent (V₁) lesion; absent ipsilateral blink only = efferent (CN VII) lesion |
| **Dermatome Sensory Mapping** | Light touch and pinprick testing across V₁, V₂, V₃ territories | Localizes the division involved |
| **[[Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy]]** | Examination of cornea with [[Fluorescein]] staining | Detects punctate erosions, persistent epithelial defects, stromal thinning, neurotrophic ulcers |
| **[[Schirmer's Test]]** | Filter paper strip in inferior fornix | Evaluates reflex and basal tear secretion; reduced in V₁ afferent disruption |
| **[[Tear Break-Up Time]] (TBUT)** | Fluorescein instillation, observation under cobalt blue light | Reduced in neurotrophic states |

### Imaging

- **[[Magnetic Resonance Imaging]] (MRI)** with gadolinium contrast: Gold standard for evaluating the trigeminal nerve, cavernous sinus, superior orbital fissure, and orbital apex. High-resolution sequences (e.g., CISS/FIESTA) can visualize individual nerve branches and detect neurovascular compression, tumors, or inflammatory lesions.
- **[[Computed Tomography]] (CT)**: Useful for bony anatomy of the superior orbital fissure, orbital apex, and detecting fractures involving the orbital roof or supraorbital notch.
- **[[Magnetic Resonance Angiography]] (MRA)** or **[[CT Angiography]] (CTA)**: Indicated when vascular pathology (e.g., carotid-cavernous fistula, aneurysm) is suspected.

### Electrophysiological Testing

- **[[Blink Reflex Electrophysiology]]**: Electrical stimulation of the [[Supraorbital Nerve]] with EMG recording of the [[Orbicularis Oculi]]. Measures R1 (oligosynaptic, ipsilateral) and R2 (polysynaptic, bilateral) responses. Prolonged latencies or absent responses localize lesions to V₁ (afferent), CN VII (efferent), or brainstem interneurons.

### In Vivo Confocal Microscopy (IVCM)

**[[In Vivo Confocal Microscopy]]** (e.g., Heidelberg Retina Tomograph with Rostock Corneal Module) allows direct visualization and quantification of the [[Sub-basal Nerve Plexus]] of the cornea. Parameters measured include:

- Corneal nerve fiber density (CNFD)
- Corneal nerve branch density (CNBD)
- Corneal nerve fiber length (CNFL)

Reduced values indicate corneal denervation and are used to diagnose and monitor [[Neurotrophic Keratopathy]], [[Diabetic Corneal Neuropathy]], and post-surgical nerve damage.

## Treatment and Management

### Management of Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus

| Intervention | Details |
|---|---|
| **Systemic antivirals** | [[Acyclovir]] 800 mg PO 5×/day, [[Valacyclovir]] 1 g PO TID, or [[Famciclovir]] 500 mg PO TID for 7–10 days; most effective if initiated within 72 hours of rash onset |
| **Topical antivirals** | [[Ganciclovir]] 0.15% gel for epithelial keratitis |
| **Topical corticosteroids** | [[Prednisolone Acetate]] 1% for stromal keratitis, anterior uveitis |
| **Cycloplegics** | [[Cyclopentolate]] 1% or [[Atropine]] 1% for ciliary spasm and uveitis |
| **IOP management** | [[Timolol]] 0.5%, [[Brimonidine]] 0.2% if secondary glaucoma develops |
| **Post-herpetic neuralgia** | [[Gabapentin]], [[Pregabalin]], [[Amitriptyline]], [[Capsaicin]] cream, or [[Lidocaine]] patches |
| **Vaccination** | [[Shingrix]] (recombinant zoster vaccine) for prevention in adults ≥50 years |

### Management of Neurotrophic Keratopathy

| Stage | Treatment |
|---|---|
| **Stage 1** | Preservative-free [[Artificial Tears]], [[Lubricating Ointments]], discontinuation of toxic topical medications |
| **Stage 2** | [[Autologous Serum Tears]] (20%), [[Therapeutic Contact Lens]] (bandage lens), [[Tarsorrhaphy]] (temporary or permanent), [[Amniotic Membrane Transplantation]] |
| **Stage 3** | [[Cenegermin]] ([[Recombinant Human Nerve Growth Factor]], Oxervate™) 0.002% ophthalmic solution — the first FDA-approved treatment specifically for neurotrophic keratopathy; [[Conjunctival Flap]], [[Corneal Transplantation]] ([[Penetrating Keratoplasty]] or [[Deep Anterior Lamellar Keratoplasty]]) for perforation or impending perforation |

**[[Cenegermin]]** (rhNGF) represents a landmark pharmacological advance. In pivotal clinical trials (NGF0212/REPARO and NGF0214), cenegermin achieved complete corneal healing in approximately 70% of patients with moderate-to-severe neurotrophic keratopathy, compared to ~30% with vehicle alone.

### Corneal Neurotization

**[[Corneal Neurotization]]** is an emerging surgical technique in which a donor sensory nerve (typically the [[Supratrochlear Nerve]] from the contralateral side, or a [[Sural Nerve]] graft) is transferred to the denervated cornea to restore innervation. Indications include severe, refractory neurotrophic keratopathy. Early results demonstrate improved corneal sensation, epithelial healing, and reduced recurrence of epithelial defects.

### Management of Trigeminal Neuralgia (V₁)

- **First-line pharmacotherapy**: [[Carbamazepine]] (200–1200 mg/day) or [[Oxcarbazepine]] (600–1800 mg/day)
- **Second-line agents**: [[Baclofen]], [[Lamotrigine]], [[Gabapentin]]
- **Surgical options** (for refractory cases):
  - [[Microvascular Decompression]] (Jannetta procedure)
  - [[Percutaneous Rhizotomy]] (radiofrequency, balloon compression, or glycerol injection)
  - [[Gamma Knife Radiosurgery]]

> **Caution**: Ablative procedures targeting the trigeminal ganglion or root carry a significant risk of inducing corneal anesthesia and subsequent [[Neurotrophic Keratopathy]]. Patients must be counseled and monitored closely by their eye care provider.

### Nerve Blocks and Regional Anesthesia

Knowledge of V₁ anatomy is essential for performing regional nerve blocks in ophthalmic practice:

| Block | Target | Clinical Use |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Supraorbital Nerve Block]]** | Supraorbital notch/foramen | Anesthesia of forehead and upper eyelid for laceration repair, biopsy, or neuralgia management |
| **[[Supratrochlear Nerve Block]]** | Medial orbital rim, above trochlea | Anesthesia of medial forehead and upper eyelid |
| **[[Infratrochlear Nerve Block]]** | Medial orbital rim, below trochlea | Anesthesia of medial canthus, lacrimal sac area (e.g., for [[Dacryocystorhinostomy]]) |
| **[[Nasociliary Nerve Block]]** | Medial orbital wall | Anesthesia of the globe, cornea, and nasal bridge |

## Anatomical Variants

Anatomical variations of V₁ and its branches are well-documented and clinically relevant:

- The [[Supraorbital Notch]] is present as a foramen (completely enclosed in bone) in approximately 25–35% of individuals, which may complicate nerve block procedures.
- Accessory branches of the frontal nerve may exist, providing dual innervation to portions of the forehead.
- The number of [[Long Ciliary Nerves]] varies from 1 to 5.
- Rarely, the nasociliary nerve may pass outside the annulus of Zinn, altering the clinical presentation of orbital apex lesions.

## Differential Diagnosis of V₁ Sensory Loss

When a patient presents with sensory loss in the V₁ distribution, the differential diagnosis includes:

- **[[Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus]]** (most common infectious cause)
- **[[Trigeminal Schwannoma]]** or **[[Meningioma]]** involving the trigeminal ganglion or V₁
- **[[Cavernous Sinus Lesions]]** (thrombosis, fistula, tumor, Tolosa-Hunt syndrome)
- **[[Multiple Sclerosis]]** (demyelinating plaque in the brainstem trigeminal pathways)
- **[[Diabetes Mellitus]]** (trigeminal neuropathy)
- **Iatrogenic** (post-surgical: [[LASIK]], [[PRK]], trigeminal rhizotomy, acoustic neuroma surgery)
- **[[Orbital Tumors]]** compressing V₁ branches
- **[[Trauma]]** (orbital roof fracture, supraorbital rim fracture)
- **[[Sarcoidosis]]** or other granulomatous diseases involving the nerve

## Prognosis

The prognosis of V₁-related conditions depends on the underlying etiology:

- **HZO**: With prompt antiviral therapy, most patients recover well, though 10–20% develop chronic post-herpetic neuralgia and some degree of permanent corneal hypoesthesia.
- **Neurotrophic Keratopathy**: Stage 1 and 2 disease generally responds to conservative and medical management (especially with cenegermin). Stage 3 carries a guarded prognosis with risk of permanent visual loss.
- **Trigeminal Neuralgia**: Pharmacotherapy controls pain in ~70% of patients initially; surgical options offer high success rates (>80% for microvascular decompression) but carry risks of corneal denervation.
- **Corneal Neurotization**: Emerging data suggest meaningful recovery of corneal sensation in 70–90% of appropriately selected patients.
