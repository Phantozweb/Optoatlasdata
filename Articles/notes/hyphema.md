

# Hyphema

**Hyphema** is defined as the accumulation of [[red blood cells]] (erythrocytes) within the [[anterior chamber]] of the eye, the fluid-filled space bounded anteriorly by the [[cornea]] and posteriorly by the [[iris]] and [[crystalline lens]]. It represents a significant ocular emergency in clinical [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] practice, as it carries the risk of serious secondary complications including [[secondary glaucoma]], [[corneal blood staining]], [[optic atrophy]], and permanent vision loss. Hyphema most commonly results from blunt or penetrating [[ocular trauma]], but may also arise spontaneously from neovascularization, coagulopathies, or intraocular tumors. Prompt recognition, accurate grading, and appropriate management are essential to preserving visual function and preventing sight-threatening sequelae.

---

## Epidemiology

Hyphema is predominantly a condition of younger populations, with the highest incidence observed in males between the ages of 10 and 20 years, largely attributable to sports-related and recreational [[ocular trauma]]. The annual incidence of traumatic hyphema in the United States is estimated at approximately 12–20 per 100,000 population. Males are affected approximately three to five times more frequently than females. Common mechanisms of injury include projectile sports (baseball, basketball, racquet sports), air gun or BB gun injuries, motor vehicle accidents, falls, and interpersonal violence (assault). Occupational injuries also contribute, particularly in settings where protective [[eyewear]] is not utilized.

Spontaneous (non-traumatic) hyphema is considerably less common and tends to occur in older adults or in patients with predisposing conditions such as [[diabetes mellitus]], [[sickle cell disease]] or [[sickle cell trait]], [[iris neovascularization]] (rubeosis iridis), [[juvenile xanthogranuloma]], [[retinoblastoma]], [[iris melanoma]], blood dyscrasias, or anticoagulant therapy. Patients of African, Mediterranean, or Middle Eastern descent should be screened for [[hemoglobin S]] variants, as sickle cell hemoglobinopathies dramatically increase the risk of complications from hyphema, even at relatively low [[intraocular pressure]] (IOP) elevations.

## Anatomy and Pathophysiology

### Relevant Anatomy

The [[anterior chamber]] is the space between the posterior surface of the [[cornea]] (the [[corneal endothelium]]) and the anterior surface of the [[iris]] and [[pupil]]. It is filled with [[aqueous humor]], a clear fluid produced by the [[ciliary body]] that nourishes avascular structures and maintains [[intraocular pressure]]. The [[anterior chamber angle]], located at the junction of the [[cornea]], [[trabecular meshwork]], and iris root, is the primary site of aqueous outflow and is critically relevant to the pathophysiology of hyphema-related complications.

The blood supply to the anterior segment is derived from the [[major arterial circle of the iris]], which is formed by anastomoses of the [[long posterior ciliary arteries]] and the [[anterior ciliary arteries]]. The iris stroma is richly vascularized, and the [[ciliary body]] contains a dense capillary network. The [[trabecular meshwork]] and [[Schlemm's canal]] constitute the conventional aqueous outflow pathway and are highly susceptible to obstruction by blood, fibrin, and inflammatory debris.

### Mechanism of Hemorrhage

In **traumatic hyphema**, the most common mechanism involves a sudden compressive-decompressive force applied to the globe. Blunt trauma causes rapid anteroposterior compression of the eye, followed by equatorial expansion. This mechanical deformation generates shearing forces at the [[iris]] root and [[ciliary body]], resulting in tears of the blood vessels within the [[iris stroma]], the [[ciliary body face]], or the [[anterior chamber angle]] structures. The torn vessels release blood into the anterior chamber, where it mixes with [[aqueous humor]] and, under the influence of gravity, may layer inferiorly to form the characteristic hyphema.

Specifically, the vessels most commonly disrupted are branches of the [[major arterial circle of the iris]] and the arterioles and venules of the [[ciliary body]]. [[Angle recession]]—a traumatic separation of the longitudinal and circular fibers of the [[ciliary muscle]]—is frequently associated with hyphema and may predispose to chronic [[open-angle glaucoma]] years or decades after the initial injury.

In **spontaneous hyphema**, the pathophysiology differs. [[Neovascularization]] of the iris ([[rubeosis iridis]]), which occurs in conditions such as [[proliferative diabetic retinopathy]], [[central retinal vein occlusion]] (CRVO), [[ocular ischemic syndrome]], and chronic [[uveitis]], produces fragile, abnormal blood vessels on the iris surface and within the anterior chamber angle. These neovascular vessels lack the structural integrity of normal vasculature and are prone to spontaneous rupture. Intraocular tumors such as [[iris melanoma]], [[retinoblastoma]], [[medulloepithelioma]], and [[juvenile xanthogranuloma]] may also erode into vascular structures or produce their own abnormal vasculature, leading to spontaneous hemorrhage.

Iatrogenic hyphema may occur following intraocular surgery, including [[cataract surgery]], [[glaucoma surgery]] (trabeculectomy, tube shunt implantation), [[laser iridotomy]], [[laser trabeculoplasty]], and [[YAG laser capsulotomy]].

### Secondary Pathophysiological Processes

Once blood enters the anterior chamber, several secondary processes ensue:

1. **Clot Formation and Fibrinolysis**: Erythrocytes aggregate and form a clot, which adheres to anterior segment structures. The [[fibrinolytic system]] within the aqueous humor gradually lyses the clot, releasing free erythrocytes and cellular debris. This fibrinolytic activity is responsible for the phenomenon of **rebleeding** (secondary hemorrhage), which typically occurs 2–5 days after the initial injury when the original clot retracts and lyses before the damaged vessels have fully healed.

2. **Trabecular Meshwork Obstruction**: Free erythrocytes, ghost cells (degenerated erythrocytes that have lost their hemoglobin), hemoglobin-laden [[macrophages]], fibrin, and inflammatory cells can obstruct the [[trabecular meshwork]], impeding aqueous outflow and causing elevation of [[intraocular pressure]]. This mechanism underlies several forms of secondary glaucoma associated with hyphema, including [[ghost cell glaucoma]], [[hemolytic glaucoma]], and [[hemosiderotic glaucoma]].

3. **Corneal Blood Staining**: Prolonged contact of blood products with the [[corneal endothelium]], particularly in the setting of elevated IOP or endothelial compromise, can result in the deposition of hemoglobin and hemosiderin within the [[corneal stroma]]. This produces a characteristic yellow-brown discoloration known as [[corneal blood staining]], which may persist for months to years and can significantly impair vision.

4. **Sickle Cell Complications**: In patients with [[sickle cell disease]] or [[sickle cell trait]] (hemoglobin AS, SC, or SS), erythrocytes within the anterior chamber are prone to sickling in the relatively hypoxic, acidotic, and hypercarbic environment of the aqueous humor. Sickled cells are rigid and cannot pass through the [[trabecular meshwork]], leading to disproportionately severe IOP elevation even with small hyphemas. Furthermore, elevated IOP in these patients can compromise [[optic nerve]] perfusion and cause [[central retinal artery occlusion]] at lower pressures than in the general population.

## Classification and Grading

Hyphema is clinically graded based on the volume of blood visible in the anterior chamber. The most widely used classification system is as follows:

| **Grade** | **Description** | **Anterior Chamber Involvement** |
|-----------|----------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|
| **Microhyphema** | Circulating red blood cells in the anterior chamber without visible layering; RBCs suspended in aqueous | No visible blood level; cells seen on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] |
| **Grade I** | Blood occupying less than one-third of the anterior chamber | < 33% of anterior chamber height |
| **Grade II** | Blood occupying one-third to one-half of the anterior chamber | 33%–50% of anterior chamber height |
| **Grade III** | Blood occupying more than one-half but less than total filling of the anterior chamber | 50%–< 100% of anterior chamber height |
| **Grade IV (Total / "Eight-ball" Hyphema)** | Total filling of the anterior chamber with blood; may appear dark red or black ("eight-ball") | 100% of anterior chamber |

The grade of hyphema is a critical determinant of prognosis and management. Higher-grade hyphemas are associated with increased risk of secondary complications, including elevated IOP, rebleeding, [[corneal blood staining]], [[synechiae]] formation, and [[optic atrophy]]. A Grade IV or "eight-ball" hyphema, in which the entire anterior chamber is filled with dark, deoxygenated blood, represents the most severe presentation and carries the highest risk of permanent visual impairment.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Symptoms

Patients with hyphema typically present with the following symptoms:

- **Decreased visual acuity**: Ranges from mild blurring (microhyphema) to severe vision loss or light perception only (Grade IV). Visual acuity is directly correlated with the volume of blood in the anterior chamber and the degree of IOP elevation.
- **Ocular pain**: Varies from mild aching to severe pain, particularly if IOP is elevated. Pain may be exacerbated by [[photophobia]] and [[ciliary spasm]].
- **Photophobia**: Sensitivity to light due to associated [[traumatic iritis]] and [[ciliary spasm]].
- **Epiphora**: Reflex tearing secondary to pain and irritation.
- **History of trauma**: In traumatic cases, patients typically report a recent blow to the eye or periorbital region. In spontaneous cases, there may be no antecedent trauma.

### Signs

On clinical examination, the following signs may be observed:

- **Visible blood in the anterior chamber**: In upright patients, blood layers inferiorly due to gravity, forming a meniscus. In supine patients, blood may distribute diffusely.
- **Red blood cells in the aqueous humor**: In microhyphema, circulating erythrocytes are visible on [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] as suspended red cells in the anterior chamber, without a visible fluid level.
- **Elevated [[intraocular pressure]]**: IOP may be elevated due to trabecular meshwork obstruction by blood, fibrin, and inflammatory debris. IOP should be measured by [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]] or [[Tono-Pen]] (avoiding excessive pressure on the globe in cases of suspected [[open globe injury]]).
- **Traumatic [[mydriasis]] or [[miosis]]**: Pupillary abnormalities may be present depending on the mechanism and severity of trauma.
- **[[Iridodialysis]]**: Separation of the iris root from the [[ciliary body]], visible as a dark, D-shaped defect at the iris periphery.
- **[[Angle recession]]**: Widening of the [[ciliary body band]] on [[gonioscopy]], indicating traumatic separation of the ciliary muscle fibers.
- **[[Lens subluxation]] or [[lens dislocation]]**: Disruption of [[zonular fibers]] may be evident as [[phacodonesis]] (lens wobble) or [[iridodonesis]] (iris wobble).
- **[[Corneal edema]]**: May result from elevated IOP or direct endothelial trauma.
- **[[Conjunctival]] injection or [[subconjunctival hemorrhage]]**: Common in traumatic cases.
- **[[Posterior segment]] findings**: [[Vitreous hemorrhage]], [[retinal detachment]], [[commotio retinae]] (Berlin's edema), [[choroidal rupture]], or [[optic nerve]] damage may coexist, particularly in severe trauma.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

The diagnosis of hyphema is primarily clinical, established through a thorough history and comprehensive [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] examination. The following diagnostic steps are essential:

1. **Detailed History**: Mechanism and timing of injury, use of [[anticoagulants]] or [[antiplatelet agents]], history of [[sickle cell disease]] or trait, prior ocular surgery, and history of bleeding disorders.

2. **Visual Acuity Assessment**: Best-corrected [[visual acuity]] (BCVA) should be documented at presentation and at each follow-up visit.

3. **[[Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy]]**: Careful examination of the [[cornea]] (for abrasions, lacerations, blood staining), [[anterior chamber]] (depth, blood level, cell and flare, fibrin), [[iris]] (tears, iridodialysis, neovascularization, sphincter tears), [[crystalline lens]] (subluxation, cataract, capsular rupture), and [[anterior chamber angle]].

4. **[[Intraocular Pressure]] Measurement**: IOP should be measured at each visit. In cases of suspected [[open globe injury]], IOP measurement should be deferred until the globe integrity is confirmed. [[Goldmann applanation tonometry]] is the gold standard; [[Tono-Pen]] or [[iCare]] rebound tonometry may be used as alternatives.

5. **[[Gonioscopy]]**: Should be performed once the hyphema has cleared (typically 4–6 weeks post-injury) to evaluate for [[angle recession]], [[peripheral anterior synechiae]] (PAS), [[cyclodialysis cleft]], or neovascularization of the angle. Gonioscopy should be performed on both eyes for comparison.

6. **[[Dilated Fundus Examination]]**: When the view permits, a thorough examination of the [[posterior segment]] should be performed to rule out [[retinal detachment]], [[vitreous hemorrhage]], [[commotio retinae]], [[choroidal rupture]], and [[optic nerve]] damage. If the view is obscured by blood, [[B-scan ultrasonography]] should be performed.

### Ancillary Testing

| **Test** | **Purpose** |
|----------|-------------|
| **[[B-scan ultrasonography]]** | Evaluate posterior segment when direct visualization is obscured; rule out [[retinal detachment]], [[vitreous hemorrhage]], intraocular foreign body, or intraocular tumor |
| **[[Anterior segment OCT]] (AS-OCT)** | Quantify hyphema volume, assess anterior chamber angle structures, and monitor resolution |
| **[[Ultrasound biomicroscopy]] (UBM)** | Evaluate [[ciliary body]], [[angle recession]], [[cyclodialysis cleft]], and [[zonular integrity]] |
| **[[CT scan of the orbits]]** | Rule out [[orbital fracture]], [[intraocular foreign body]], or [[open globe injury]] in cases of significant trauma |
| **[[Sickle cell screening]] (Sickledex, hemoglobin electrophoresis)** | Mandatory in all African American patients and other at-risk populations; identifies hemoglobin S variants (AS, SS, SC) |
| **[[Complete blood count]] (CBC), [[PT/INR]], [[PTT]]** | Evaluate for coagulopathies, thrombocytopenia, or anticoagulant effects |
| **[[Fluorescein angiography]]** | May be indicated in spontaneous hyphema to evaluate for [[iris neovascularization]] or intraocular tumor vasculature |

### Differential Diagnosis

While the diagnosis of hyphema is usually straightforward, the clinician must consider the underlying etiology, particularly in cases of spontaneous hyphema:

- [[Traumatic iritis]] (without hyphema—cells without RBCs)
- [[Iris neovascularization]] ([[rubeosis iridis]]) secondary to [[proliferative diabetic retinopathy]], [[CRVO]], or [[ocular ischemic syndrome]]
- [[Iris melanoma]] or other intraocular tumors
- [[Juvenile xanthogranuloma]]
- [[Retinoblastoma]] (in pediatric patients)
- [[Blood dyscrasias]] (leukemia, hemophilia, von Willebrand disease)
- [[Anticoagulant]]-related hemorrhage
- Post-surgical hemorrhage (following [[cataract surgery]], [[glaucoma surgery]], or laser procedures)
- [[Herpes simplex]] or [[herpes zoster]] keratouveitis with associated hemorrhagic inflammation
- [[Fuchs heterochromic iridocyclitis]] (rarely associated with spontaneous hyphema)

## Management

The management of hyphema is directed toward preventing rebleeding, controlling [[intraocular pressure]], minimizing the risk of [[corneal blood staining]], and treating the underlying cause. Management strategies vary based on the grade of hyphema, the presence of complications, the patient's sickle cell status, and the clinical setting.

### Conservative (Medical) Management

The majority of hyphemas, particularly Grade I and microhyphema, can be managed conservatively with close outpatient follow-up. Key elements of conservative management include:

#### Activity Restriction and Positioning

- **Bed rest or limited activity**: Patients should be instructed to minimize physical activity, avoid bending, straining, or Valsalva maneuvers, and refrain from contact sports or strenuous exercise. Strict bed rest with bathroom privileges was historically recommended, but current evidence supports limited activity with head-of-bed elevation.
- **Head elevation**: The head of the bed should be elevated to 30–45 degrees at all times to promote gravitational settling of blood inferiorly, away from the [[visual axis]] and the [[trabecular meshwork]], and to facilitate reabsorption.
- **Eye protection**: A rigid [[eye shield]] (Fox shield) should be worn at all times to prevent inadvertent trauma to the affected eye. An eye patch is generally not recommended as it prevents monitoring of the eye and does not provide structural protection.

#### Pharmacological Interventions

| **Medication** | **Class** | **Purpose** | **Dosing** | **Notes** |
|----------------|-----------|-------------|------------|-----------|
| **[[Atropine]] 1% ophthalmic** | [[Cycloplegic]] / [[Mydriatic]] | Stabilize the [[blood-aqueous barrier]], reduce pain from [[ciliary spasm]], prevent [[posterior synechiae]] | 1 drop BID–TID | Preferred cycloplegic; long duration of action provides sustained comfort |
| **[[Prednisolone acetate]] 1%** | Topical [[corticosteroid]] | Reduce intraocular inflammation, stabilize the [[blood-aqueous barrier]], reduce risk of rebleeding | 1 drop Q1H–Q4H while awake, tapered over 2–4 weeks | Avoid in cases of suspected infectious etiology |
| **[[Aminocaproic acid]] (ACA)** | [[Antifibrinolytic]] agent | Inhibit clot lysis to reduce the risk of rebleeding | Oral: 50 mg/kg every 4 hours (max 30 g/day) for 5 days; Topical: 30% solution | Reduces rebleeding rate from ~25% to ~5%; side effects include nausea, vomiting, hypotension, and rarely thromboembolism |
| **[[Tranexamic acid]] (TXA)** | [[Antifibrinolytic]] agent | Alternative to aminocaproic acid for rebleed prevention | Oral: 25 mg/kg TID for 5–7 days | Better tolerated than ACA; increasingly used as first-line antifibrinolytic |
| **[[Timolol]] 0.5%** | Topical [[beta-blocker]] | Reduce [[intraocular pressure]] | 1 drop BID | First-line IOP-lowering agent; avoid in patients with asthma, COPD, or bradycardia |
| **[[Brimonidine]] 0.2%** | Topical [[alpha-2 agonist]] | Reduce IOP | 1 drop BID–TID | Avoid in children < 2 years (risk of CNS depression) |
| **[[Dorzolamide]] 2%** | Topical [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitor]] (CAI) | Reduce IOP | 1 drop BID–TID | Useful adjunct for IOP control |
| **[[Acetazolamide]]** | Oral [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitor]] | Reduce IOP in cases refractory to topical therapy | 250–500 mg PO BID–QID | **CONTRAINDICATED in sickle cell patients** (promotes sickling in anterior chamber by lowering aqueous pH) |
| **[[Methazolamide]]** | Oral [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitor]] | Alternative to acetazolamide | 25–50 mg PO BID–TID | **Also CONTRAINDICATED in sickle cell patients** |
| **[[Mannitol]] 20%** | Osmotic agent | Emergency IOP reduction | 1–2 g/kg IV over 45 minutes | Reserved for acute IOP crises; monitor renal function and electrolytes |
| **Analgesics ([[Acetaminophen]])** | Analgesic | Pain management | 500–1000 mg PO Q4–6H PRN | **Avoid [[aspirin]] and [[NSAIDs]]** due to antiplatelet effects and increased bleeding risk |

> **Critical Note on Sickle Cell Disease**: In patients with known or suspected [[sickle cell disease]] or [[sickle cell trait]], systemic [[carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]] (acetazolamide, methazolamide) are **absolutely contraindicated** because they lower the pH of the aqueous humor, promoting sickling of erythrocytes within the anterior chamber and trabecular meshwork. Topical CAIs (dorzolamide, brinzolamide) are generally considered safer but should be used with caution. IOP thresholds for intervention are lower in sickle cell patients: surgical intervention should be considered if IOP exceeds 24 mmHg for more than 24 hours (compared to 50–60 mmHg in non-sickle cell patients) to prevent [[optic nerve]] damage and [[central retinal artery occlusion]].

#### Medications to Avoid

- **[[Aspirin]]** and **[[NSAIDs]]** (ibuprofen, naproxen): Inhibit [[platelet aggregation]] and increase the risk of rebleeding.
- **Topical [[prostaglandin analogs]]** (latanoprost, travoprost, bimatoprost): May exacerbate intraocular inflammation.
- **[[Pilocarpine]]** and other [[miotics]]: May increase inflammation and promote [[posterior synechiae]].

### Follow-Up Schedule

Patients with hyphema require close follow-up to monitor for rebleeding, IOP elevation, and other complications:

| **Time Point** | **Assessment** |
|----------------|----------------|
| **Day 1 (24 hours)** | Visual acuity, IOP, slit-lamp examination, hyphema grading |
| **Daily for first 5–7 days** | Monitor for rebleeding (peak risk days 2–5), IOP, hyphema resolution |
| **Weekly until resolution** | Visual acuity, IOP, anterior segment examination |
| **4–6 weeks post-injury** | [[Gonioscopy]] (both eyes) to assess for [[angle recession]] |
| **Annually (lifelong)** | IOP measurement and [[optic nerve]] evaluation to screen for [[angle recession glaucoma]] |

### Rebleeding (Secondary Hemorrhage)

Rebleeding is the most feared early complication of hyphema, occurring in approximately 15–25% of untreated patients, typically between days 2 and 5 after the initial injury. Rebleeding occurs when the fibrin clot sealing the damaged vessels undergoes lysis before the vessels have fully healed. Secondary hemorrhage is often more severe than the initial bleed and is associated with significantly worse visual outcomes.

**Risk factors for rebleeding** include:
- Large initial hyphema (Grade II or higher)
- Use of [[aspirin]], [[NSAIDs]], or [[anticoagulants]]
- [[Sickle cell disease]] or trait
- Poor compliance with activity restriction
- Delayed presentation
- Young age

**Prevention of rebleeding** is a primary goal of management and is achieved through:
- Activity restriction and head elevation
- [[Antifibrinolytic agents]] (aminocaproic acid or tranexamic acid)
- Avoidance of antiplatelet and anticoagulant medications
- Topical [[corticosteroids]] and [[cycloplegics]]

### Surgical Management

Surgical intervention is indicated when medical management fails to control complications. Indications for surgical evacuation of hyphema include:

| **Indication** | **Details** |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Persistent IOP elevation** | IOP > 50 mmHg for > 5 days (risk of [[optic atrophy]]); IOP > 35 mmHg for > 7 days; IOP > 25 mmHg with [[sickle cell disease/trait]] for > 24 hours |
| **Corneal blood staining** | Early or progressive [[corneal blood staining]], particularly in Grade III–IV hyphema |
| **Total (Grade IV) hyphema** | Failure to resolve within 5–7 days; "eight-ball" hyphema with dark, deoxygenated blood |
| **Rebleeding with uncontrolled IOP** | Secondary hemorrhage with IOP refractory to maximum medical therapy |
| **Pupillary block** | Blood clot obstructing the [[pupil]] and causing [[iris bombé]] and secondary [[angle-closure glaucoma]] |

**Surgical techniques** include:

1. **[[Anterior chamber washout]] (irrigation and aspiration)**: The most common surgical approach. A paracentesis is performed, and the anterior chamber is irrigated with balanced salt solution (BSS) to evacuate blood and clot. Automated irrigation-aspiration systems or manual techniques may be used.

2. **[[Trabeculectomy]]**: May be performed in conjunction with anterior chamber washout if there is significant trabecular meshwork damage or persistent IOP elevation.

3. **Clot expression**: A large clot may be expressed through a limbal incision using viscoelastic or gentle irrigation.

4. **[[Peripheral iridectomy]]**: May be performed to relieve [[pupillary block]] caused by a blood clot.

5. **[[Intracameral tissue plasminogen activator]] (tPA)**: Injection of 25 μg of tPA into the anterior chamber may be used to lyse an organized clot that is resistant to irrigation. This is typically reserved for cases where the clot is well-organized and cannot be mechanically removed.

## Complications

| **Complication** | **Mechanism** | **Time Course** | **Management** |
|------------------|---------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Rebleeding** | Clot lysis before vessel healing | Days 2–5 | Antifibrinolytics, activity restriction, possible surgical evacuation |
| **[[Secondary glaucoma]]** | Trabecular meshwork obstruction by RBCs, ghost cells, hemoglobin-laden macrophages, or fibrin | Acute (days) to chronic (weeks–months) | IOP-lowering medications, surgical washout if refractory |
| **[[Corneal blood staining]]** | Hemoglobin/hemosiderin deposition in corneal stroma | Days to weeks (especially with elevated IOP and endothelial compromise) | Surgical evacuation of blood; may require [[penetrating keratoplasty]] if persistent |
| **[[Peripheral anterior synechiae]] (PAS)** | Inflammatory adhesions between iris and trabecular meshwork | Weeks | Cycloplegics, corticosteroids, gonioscopy monitoring |
| **[[Posterior synechiae]]** | Adhesions between iris and lens capsule | Days to weeks | Cycloplegics (atropine), corticosteroids |
| **[[Optic atrophy]]** | Prolonged IOP elevation causing [[optic nerve]] ischemia | Weeks to months | Aggressive IOP control, surgical intervention |
| **[[Angle recession glaucoma]]** | Chronic open-angle glaucoma from traumatic damage to trabecular meshwork | Months to decades post-injury | Lifelong IOP monitoring; medical or surgical glaucoma management |
| **[[Amblyopia]]** | Visual deprivation in pediatric patients with prolonged hyphema or corneal blood staining | Weeks to months | Prompt treatment of hyphema; [[amblyopia therapy]] (patching, atropine penalization) |

## Prognosis

The prognosis of hyphema depends on the grade, the occurrence of rebleeding, the presence of associated ocular injuries, the patient's sickle cell status, and the adequacy of management. Overall visual outcomes are as follows:

- **Microhyphema and Grade I**: Excellent prognosis; the majority of patients achieve visual acuity of 20/40 or better. Spontaneous resolution typically occurs within 5–7 days.
- **Grade II**: Good prognosis with appropriate management; resolution within 1–2 weeks.
- **Grade III**: Guarded prognosis; higher risk of complications including IOP elevation, corneal blood staining, and rebleeding.
- **Grade IV ("Eight-ball")**: Poor prognosis without surgical intervention; high risk of corneal blood staining, optic atrophy, and permanent vision loss.
- **Rebleeding**: Associated with significantly worse visual outcomes compared to initial hyphema alone.
- **Sickle cell patients**: Worse prognosis at all grades due to increased susceptibility to IOP-related optic nerve and retinal damage.

Long-term follow-up is essential for all patients with traumatic hyphema, as [[angle recession glaucoma]] may develop months to decades after the initial injury. Studies have demonstrated that angle recession involving greater than 180 degrees of the angle circumference carries a significantly elevated risk of late-onset glaucoma. Annual comprehensive eye examinations with [[gonioscopy]] and [[optic nerve]] assessment are recommended for life.

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Patients

Hyphema in children presents unique challenges. Children may be unable to articulate symptoms, and compliance with activity restriction and medication regimens may be poor. Additionally, prolonged visual deprivation from hyphema or corneal blood staining in young children (particularly those under age 7–8) carries a significant risk of [[deprivation amblyopia]]. Hospitalization may be considered more readily in pediatric patients to ensure compliance and close monitoring. Non-accidental trauma ([[child abuse]]) must be considered in any pediatric patient presenting with hyphema, particularly when the history is inconsistent with the clinical findings.

### Patients on Anticoagulation

Patients taking [[warfarin]], [[direct oral anticoagulants]] (DOACs such as [[apixaban]], [[rivarelbaan]], [[dabigatran]]), or [[antiplatelet agents]] ([[clopidogrel]], [[aspirin]]) present a management dilemma. The decision to discontinue anticoagulation must be made in consultation with the patient's primary care physician or cardiologist, weighing the risk of rebleeding against the risk of thromboembolic events. In many cases, temporary discontinuation or dose reduction may be appropriate.

### Patients with Sickle Cell Hemoglobinopathy

As discussed above, patients with [[sickle cell disease]] or [[sickle cell trait]] require modified management protocols, including avoidance of systemic carbonic anhydrase inhibitors, lower IOP thresholds for surgical intervention, and heightened vigilance for complications. All patients of African, Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, South Asian, or Hispanic descent presenting with hyphema should undergo [[sickle cell screening]] if their hemoglobin status is unknown.

---

## Prevention

Prevention of traumatic hyphema centers on the use of appropriate protective [[eyewear]] during sports, recreational activities, and occupational tasks. [[Polycarbonate]] lenses and sports goggles meeting ASTM F803 standards provide excellent impact resistance and are recommended for all participants in high-risk sports. Public health education regarding eye safety, particularly in pediatric and adolescent populations, is essential.
