

# Divergence Excess

**Divergence Excess** is a specific subtype of [[intermittent exotropia]] (X(T)) characterized by a significantly greater [[exodeviation]] at distance fixation than at near fixation, with the distance-near disparity typically being ≥10 [[prism diopters]] (Δ). It represents one of the most clinically important and debated classifications within the broader spectrum of [[exotropia]], demanding careful diagnostic evaluation to distinguish **true divergence excess** from **simulated (pseudo) divergence excess**. This distinction carries profound implications for clinical management, surgical planning, and long-term prognosis. Divergence excess accounts for a substantial proportion of intermittent exotropia cases seen in pediatric and adult [[strabismus]] clinics and remains a subject of ongoing research regarding its underlying neurophysiological mechanisms and optimal treatment strategies.

---

## Epidemiology

[[Intermittent exotropia]] is the most common form of [[exotropia]] worldwide, and divergence excess constitutes a significant subtype. Epidemiological data suggest the following:

- **Prevalence**: Intermittent exotropia affects approximately 0.5–1.0% of the general population. Divergence excess type accounts for roughly **25–35%** of all intermittent exotropia cases, though reported prevalence varies across studies depending on the diagnostic criteria employed and whether the simulated form is reclassified after occlusion testing.
- **Age of Onset**: Divergence excess typically manifests in **early childhood**, often between the ages of 2 and 6 years, though it may not be formally diagnosed until later when parents notice an intermittent outward deviation of one or both eyes, particularly during distance gaze or periods of fatigue, inattention, or bright sunlight exposure.
- **Sex Distribution**: Most large series report a roughly **equal sex distribution** or a slight female preponderance, consistent with the broader intermittent exotropia population.
- **Racial and Geographic Variation**: Intermittent exotropia, including the divergence excess subtype, is more commonly reported in Asian populations compared to Caucasian populations, where [[esotropia]] tends to predominate. This geographic variation may reflect both genetic and environmental factors.

---

## Classification

The classification of intermittent exotropia was originally described by **Burian (1966)** and later refined by multiple authors. The Burian classification divides intermittent exotropia into three primary subtypes based on the distance-near relationship of the deviation:

| **Subtype** | **Distance Deviation** | **Near Deviation** | **Distance-Near Disparity** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Divergence Excess** | Large | Small or absent | ≥10Δ greater at distance |
| **Basic Type** | Moderate to large | Approximately equal | <10Δ difference |
| **Convergence Insufficiency** | Small or absent | Large | ≥10Δ greater at near |

### True Divergence Excess vs. Simulated (Pseudo) Divergence Excess

This distinction is arguably the most critical diagnostic consideration in the evaluation of divergence excess and was elucidated through the work of **Kushner (1988)** and others.

- **True Divergence Excess**: The distance deviation remains significantly larger than the near deviation even after prolonged monocular [[occlusion]] (typically 30–60 minutes). This suggests a genuine increase in [[divergence]] tone or innervation at distance fixation. True divergence excess is relatively **uncommon**, accounting for only a minority of cases initially classified as divergence excess.

- **Simulated (Pseudo) Divergence Excess**: After prolonged monocular occlusion disrupts [[fusional convergence]] at near, the near deviation increases to approximate the distance deviation, effectively reclassifying the patient as having a **basic type** intermittent exotropia. The initial apparent distance-near disparity was caused by strong [[tonic convergence]] or high [[fusional convergence amplitudes]] at near that masked the full near deviation. This is the **more common** presentation, accounting for approximately **60–70%** of cases initially diagnosed as divergence excess.

A third mechanism involves a **high accommodative convergence to accommodation ratio ([[AC/A ratio]])**, where robust [[accommodation]]-driven convergence at near reduces the manifest near deviation. This subgroup may respond to different treatment strategies.

---

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of divergence excess is multifactorial and not completely understood. Several neurophysiological mechanisms have been proposed:

### 1. Excessive Divergence Innervation

The classical theory posits that true divergence excess results from an **overaction of the divergence mechanism** during distance fixation. The concept of an active [[divergence]] center, as opposed to divergence being merely a relaxation of [[convergence]], has been supported by neurophysiological studies identifying neurons in the **[[brainstem]]** (particularly the [[nucleus reticularis tegmenti pontis]]** and the **[[abducens nucleus]]** region) that fire specifically during divergence movements. In true divergence excess, these divergence neurons may exhibit excessive tonic activity or an exaggerated response to distance fixation stimuli, resulting in a disproportionately large exodeviation at distance.

### 2. Deficient Tonic Convergence at Distance

An alternative but complementary hypothesis suggests that the distance exodeviation reflects **inadequate tonic convergence** to counterbalance the natural anatomical divergence of the [[orbits]]. While fusional convergence mechanisms adequately compensate at near (where robust convergence stimuli exist), the reduced convergence demand at distance unmasks the underlying exodeviation.

### 3. Tenacious Proximal Convergence (Simulated Form)

In simulated divergence excess, the near deviation is artificially reduced by **tenacious proximal convergence**—a strong near-convergence response driven by [[proximal convergence]] (awareness of nearness), [[accommodative convergence]], and robust [[fusional vergence]] reserves. This mechanism is disrupted by prolonged monocular occlusion, which eliminates the binocular fusion stimulus and allows the full near deviation to manifest.

### 4. High AC/A Ratio Mechanism

In a subset of divergence excess patients, a **high [[AC/A ratio]]** plays a significant role. These patients generate excessive accommodative convergence per unit of accommodation at near, effectively reducing the near exodeviation. The distance deviation, where accommodative convergence plays a lesser role, remains large. This subgroup can be identified by measuring the near deviation with **+3.00 D lenses** (which relax accommodation and reduce accommodative convergence), causing the near deviation to increase toward the distance measurement.

### 5. Anatomical and Mechanical Factors

The resting position of the eyes is determined by the balance of passive orbital forces, including the elasticity of the [[extraocular muscles]], [[orbital connective tissue]], and [[Tenon's capsule]]. Variations in orbital anatomy, including the position and geometry of the [[muscle pulleys]] (connective tissue sleeves that serve as functional origins for the [[rectus muscles]]), may predispose certain individuals to a divergent resting position that becomes manifest primarily at distance.

### 6. Supranuclear Control Mechanisms

Higher cortical and subcortical centers, including the [[frontal eye fields]], [[parietal cortex]], and [[cerebellum]], modulate vergence tone. Dysfunction or imbalance in these supranuclear pathways may contribute to the distance-near disparity observed in divergence excess. The [[cerebellum]], in particular, plays a role in vergence adaptation and calibration, and cerebellar dysfunction has been implicated in some forms of vergence abnormalities.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

Patients with divergence excess may present with a range of symptoms, though many, particularly young children, are remarkably **asymptomatic** due to the intermittent nature of the deviation and the preservation of [[binocular vision]] at near:

| **Symptom** | **Description** | **Frequency** |
|---|---|---|
| **Intermittent distance exotropia** | Outward deviation of one eye noticed by parents or observers, especially during distance gaze | Very common |
| **Squinting or closing one eye in bright light** | Photosensitive closure of the deviating eye, possibly to eliminate [[diplopia]] or [[visual confusion]] | Common |
| **Asthenopia** | Eye strain, headache, or fatigue, particularly with prolonged distance viewing | Moderate |
| **Diplopia** | Intermittent [[double vision]] at distance; rare in young children due to [[suppression]] | Variable |
| **Difficulty with distance tasks** | Trouble with driving, watching movies, or viewing distant objects | Moderate (older patients) |
| **Cosmetic concern** | Noticeable outward eye turn, often more apparent when fatigued, ill, or inattentive | Common |

### Signs

- **Intermittent manifest [[exotropia]]** at distance fixation, with orthophoria or a small [[exophoria]] at near
- **Distance deviation typically ≥15–20Δ**, often ranging from 20–40Δ
- **Near deviation typically <10Δ** (before occlusion testing)
- **Normal or near-normal [[stereopsis]]** at near (e.g., 40–60 arc seconds on [[Titmus stereo test]] or [[Randot stereotest]])
- **Reduced or absent [[distance stereopsis]]** during manifest deviation
- **[[Suppression]]** of the deviating eye at distance, demonstrable with [[Worth 4-dot test]] or [[Bagolini striated lenses]]
- **Good [[fusional convergence]] amplitudes** at near
- **Possible [[amblyopia]]** in cases with a strongly preferred fixating eye, though this is less common than in constant strabismus

### Natural History

The natural history of divergence excess intermittent exotropia is variable:

- Some patients maintain excellent control for years or decades with minimal progression
- Others experience gradual **deterioration of fusional control**, with increasing frequency and duration of the manifest deviation
- Progression from intermittent to **constant exotropia** may occur, particularly during periods of illness, fatigue, or growth
- Loss of [[binocular vision]] and development of [[suppression scotoma]] may occur with prolonged manifest deviation
- Spontaneous resolution is **uncommon** but has been reported in a small percentage of cases

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination

A systematic approach to the diagnosis of divergence excess includes:

#### 1. History
- Age of onset and duration of the deviation
- Frequency and triggers (fatigue, illness, bright light, inattention)
- Presence of diplopia, asthenopia, or cosmetic concern
- Family history of [[strabismus]]
- Previous treatment (glasses, patching, surgery)

#### 2. Visual Acuity
- Best-corrected [[visual acuity]] at distance and near in each eye
- Assessment for [[amblyopia]]

#### 3. Cover Testing
The cornerstone of diagnosis:

| **Test** | **Fixation Distance** | **Expected Finding** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Cover-uncover test]] | Distance (6 m) | Intermittent or constant exotropia |
| [[Cover-uncover test]] | Near (33–40 cm) | Orthophoria or small exophoria |
| [[Alternate prism cover test]] | Distance (6 m) | Large exodeviation (typically ≥20Δ) |
| [[Alternate prism cover test]] | Near (33–40 cm) | Small or no exodeviation (<10Δ) |

#### 4. Prolonged Monocular Occlusion Test (Marlow Test)
- **Critical diagnostic step** to differentiate true from simulated divergence excess
- One eye is occluded (patched) for **30–60 minutes**
- After occlusion, the near deviation is immediately remeasured with the [[alternate prism cover test]]
- **Interpretation**:
  - If the near deviation **increases to within 10Δ of the distance deviation** → **Simulated divergence excess** (reclassified as basic type)
  - If the near deviation **remains significantly smaller** than the distance deviation (≥10Δ disparity persists) → **True divergence excess**

#### 5. +3.00 D Lens Test (Gradient AC/A Method)
- Near deviation is measured with **+3.00 D lenses** placed before both eyes during near fixation
- This relaxes accommodation and reduces [[accommodative convergence]]
- If the near deviation **increases significantly** (≥10Δ) with plus lenses → Suggests a **high AC/A ratio** component contributing to the distance-near disparity

#### 6. Sensory Testing

| **Test** | **Purpose** | **Expected Finding** |
|---|---|---|
| [[Worth 4-dot test]] (distance and near) | Assess fusion and suppression | Fusion at near; suppression or diplopia at distance |
| [[Bagolini striated lenses]] | Assess sensory status | Normal binocular response at near; suppression at distance |
| [[Stereopsis]] testing (near) | Quantify binocular function | Often normal or near-normal (40–100 arc seconds) |
| [[Distance stereopsis]] | Assess binocular function at distance | Often reduced or absent during manifest deviation |

#### 7. Assessment of Control
The **Newcastle Control Score** or **Office Control Score** may be used to grade the degree of control of the intermittent exotropia:

| **Score** | **Control Level** | **Description** |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Excellent | Exophoria only; deviation seen only after cover test disruption |
| 1 | Good | Deviation recovers within 1 blink after cover test |
| 2 | Moderate | Deviation recovers after >1 blink or refixation |
| 3 | Poor | Manifest exotropia present >50% of the time |
| 4 | None | Constant manifest exotropia |

Scores are assigned separately for distance and near, yielding a combined score (0–8 for home and clinic).

#### 8. Cycloplegic Refraction
- Essential to identify any underlying [[refractive error]]
- [[Myopia]] is common in intermittent exotropia and may influence management
- [[Hyperopia]], if significant and uncorrected, may reduce accommodative convergence and worsen the deviation

#### 9. Fundus Examination and Ocular Health Assessment
- Rule out organic causes of reduced vision or strabismus
- Assess for [[optic nerve]] abnormalities, [[retinal]] pathology, or other conditions

## Differential Diagnosis

Divergence excess must be differentiated from other conditions that may present with a distance exodeviation:

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| **Basic type [[intermittent exotropia]]** | Similar deviation at distance and near; no significant distance-near disparity |
| **[[Convergence insufficiency]]** | Greater deviation at near than distance; reduced [[near point of convergence]] |
| **[[Duane retraction syndrome]] (Type 2)** | Limitation of adduction; globe retraction and palpebral fissure narrowing on attempted adduction |
| **[[Cranial nerve III palsy]]** (partial) | May present with exotropia; associated ptosis, pupil involvement, or limitation of adduction/elevation/depression |
| **[[Cranial nerve VI palsy]]** (bilateral, recovering) | History of acute onset; esotropia converting to exotropia during recovery |
| **Sensory exotropia** | Unilateral poor vision from organic cause; constant deviation |
| **[[Decompensated exophoria]]** | Previously well-controlled exophoria that becomes intermittently manifest |
| **Consecutive exotropia** | History of prior esotropia surgery |

## Management

The management of divergence excess depends on the subtype (true vs. simulated), the degree of control, the magnitude of the deviation, the patient's age, symptoms, and the impact on binocular function. A stepwise approach is generally recommended.

### 1. Observation and Monitoring

- Appropriate for patients with **good control**, infrequent manifest deviation, preserved [[stereopsis]], and minimal symptoms
- Regular follow-up every **4–6 months** to monitor for deterioration
- Parents should be educated about signs of worsening (increasing frequency of deviation, squinting, head posture changes)

### 2. Non-Surgical Treatment

#### a. Optical Correction

- **Full correction of [[myopia]]**: Myopic correction improves distance visual acuity and may stimulate accommodative convergence through clearer retinal imagery, potentially reducing the distance exodeviation
- **Overcorrecting minus lenses**: The use of **-1.00 to -3.00 D overcorrection** has been employed to stimulate accommodative convergence and reduce the exodeviation. This approach is more effective in patients with a **high AC/A ratio** component. However, long-term efficacy is debated, and there is concern about inducing or worsening myopia
- **Undercorrection of [[hyperopia]]**: In hyperopic patients, prescribing less than the full hyperopic correction may stimulate accommodation and accommodative convergence

#### b. Orthoptic Exercises (Vision Therapy)

- **[[Fusional convergence]] training**: Exercises to improve convergence amplitudes and fusional vergence reserves
- **Anti-suppression therapy**: Techniques to reduce suppression and improve binocular awareness
- **Efficacy in divergence excess**: Orthoptic exercises are generally **less effective** for divergence excess than for convergence insufficiency, as the primary deviation occurs at distance where convergence training has limited applicability. However, they may be beneficial as an adjunct in patients with reduced fusional convergence reserves

#### c. Prism Therapy

- **[[Base-in prisms]]**: May be prescribed for distance wear to reduce the demand on fusional convergence
- **[[Fresnel prisms]]**: Useful as a temporary measure or diagnostic tool
- Limited long-term utility due to the large magnitude of most divergence excess deviations and cosmetic concerns with high-power prisms
- **Prism adaptation**: Some clinicians use preoperative prism adaptation to unmask the full deviation before surgical planning

#### d. Occlusion Therapy

- **Part-time [[occlusion]]** of the dominant or preferred eye may be used to:
  - Treat associated [[amblyopia]]
  - Disrupt fusion and potentially reveal the full deviation (diagnostic purpose)
  - Reduce suppression
- Not typically a primary long-term treatment for the deviation itself

### 3. Surgical Treatment

Surgery is the **primary definitive treatment** for divergence excess that is poorly controlled, cosmetically significant, or associated with deteriorating binocular function.

#### Indications for Surgery

- Poor control of the deviation (increasing frequency of manifest exotropia)
- Large deviation (typically ≥15–20Δ at distance)
- Deteriorating [[stereopsis]] or binocular function
- Significant cosmetic concern
- Failure of non-surgical management
- Development of [[suppression]] or [[amblyopia]]

#### Surgical Approaches

The choice of surgical procedure depends critically on whether the patient has **true divergence excess** or **simulated divergence excess**:

| **Subtype** | **Recommended Procedure** | **Rationale** |
|---|---|---|
| **True Divergence Excess** | **Bilateral [[lateral rectus]] recession (BLR recession)** | Targets the distance deviation; weakens the divergence mechanism bilaterally |
| **Simulated Divergence Excess (Basic Type)** | **Unilateral [[lateral rectus]] recession with [[medial rectus]] resection (R&R)** or **BLR recession with augmented amounts** | Addresses both distance and near deviation; recess-resect provides more balanced correction |
| **High AC/A Ratio Subtype** | **BLR recession** (possibly with augmented near correction) or **BLR recession with medial rectus resection** | May require additional near correction if AC/A-driven convergence is eliminated postoperatively |

#### Surgical Dosing

Surgical dosing tables for bilateral lateral rectus recession in divergence excess typically follow established guidelines:

| **Distance Deviation (Δ)** | **Bilateral Lateral Rectus Recession (mm each)** |
|---|---|
| 15Δ | 5.0 mm |
| 20Δ | 5.5–6.0 mm |
| 25Δ | 6.5–7.0 mm |
| 30Δ | 7.0–7.5 mm |
| 35Δ | 7.5–8.0 mm |
| 40Δ | 8.0–9.0 mm |

*Note: Dosing varies by surgeon preference and institutional protocols. Some surgeons use augmented recession amounts for divergence excess compared to basic type exotropia.*

#### Surgical Outcomes

- **Success rates** (defined as residual deviation ≤10Δ) for bilateral lateral rectus recession in true divergence excess range from **60–85%** in various series
- **Overcorrection** (consecutive [[esotropia]]) occurs in approximately **5–20%** of cases and may be transient or persistent
- **Undercorrection** (residual exotropia) is the most common cause of surgical failure and may require reoperation
- **Late recurrence** of exotropia is a well-recognized phenomenon, occurring in **20–40%** of patients over long-term follow-up (>5 years)
- Patients with **simulated divergence excess** who undergo surgery based only on the distance deviation (without recognizing the full near deviation) are at higher risk for **undercorrection** at near

#### Adjustable Sutures

- **[[Adjustable suture technique]]** may be employed, particularly in adult patients, to allow postoperative titration of the surgical effect
- Useful in cases where the target alignment is uncertain or when there is concern about overcorrection

#### Reoperation Considerations

- Reoperation may be necessary for significant undercorrection or overcorrection
- Options include additional lateral rectus recession, medial rectus resection, or revision of the original surgery
- Careful reassessment of the distance-near relationship is essential before reoperation

### 4. Botulinum Toxin Injection

- **[[Botulinum toxin]] (Botox)** injection into the [[lateral rectus muscle]](s) has been used as an alternative or adjunct to surgery
- May be considered in patients who are poor surgical candidates or as a diagnostic/therapeutic trial
- Effects are **temporary** (typically lasting 3–6 months), and repeat injections may be necessary
- Less predictable outcomes compared to incisional surgery for divergence excess

## Special Considerations

### Divergence Excess in Children

- Young children may not report symptoms; parental observation is critical
- [[Amblyopia]] screening is essential, as unilateral preference may lead to amblyopia in the non-preferred eye
- Surgical timing is debated: some advocate early surgery to preserve binocular function, while others prefer observation if control is good
- The **Pediatric Eye Disease Investigator Group (PEDIG)** has conducted studies on the management of intermittent exotropia, providing evidence-based guidance

### Divergence Excess and Myopia

- There is a well-documented association between [[intermittent exotropia]] and [[myopia]]
- Myopic progression may worsen the exodeviation by reducing accommodative demand
- Conversely, myopic correction may improve control of the deviation
- The use of overcorrecting minus lenses must be balanced against the risk of myopic progression

### Divergence Excess and A/V Patterns

- Some patients with divergence excess may exhibit an associated **[[A-pattern]]** or **[[V-pattern]]** deviation
- **V-pattern exotropia** (greater exodeviation in upgaze) is particularly common and may be associated with [[inferior oblique]] overaction
- The presence of an A or V pattern may influence surgical planning (e.g., vertical transposition of horizontal rectus muscles)

### Postoperative Management

- Close follow-up is essential in the early postoperative period (1 day, 1 week, 1 month, 3 months, 6 months, then annually)
- Small consecutive esotropia in the early postoperative period is often desirable and may resolve spontaneously
- Persistent overcorrection may require prism therapy, orthoptic exercises, or reoperation
- Long-term monitoring for late recurrence is recommended

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis for divergence excess depends on multiple factors:

- **True divergence excess** generally has a **better surgical prognosis** with bilateral lateral rectus recession, as the surgery directly targets the primary mechanism
- **Simulated divergence excess** requires accurate identification to ensure appropriate surgical dosing; failure to recognize the full near deviation may lead to suboptimal outcomes
- Patients with **good preoperative [[stereopsis]]** and **intermittent** (rather than constant) deviation tend to have better postoperative binocular outcomes
- **Younger age at surgery** may be associated with higher recurrence rates, though this is debated
- Long-term follow-up studies demonstrate that while initial surgical success rates are high, **late recurrence** remains a significant concern, necessitating ongoing monitoring

---

## Summary

Divergence excess is a clinically significant subtype of intermittent exotropia characterized by a distance exodeviation substantially exceeding the near deviation. The critical diagnostic distinction between true and simulated (pseudo) divergence excess—established through prolonged monocular occlusion testing and the +3.00 D lens test—has profound implications for treatment planning. True divergence excess, reflecting genuine overactivity of the divergence mechanism, responds well to bilateral lateral rectus recession targeted to the distance deviation. Simulated divergence excess, which is far more common, represents a basic type exotropia masked by strong near convergence and requires surgical dosing that accounts for the full near deviation. A comprehensive evaluation including detailed cover testing, sensory assessment, occlusion testing, and AC/A ratio determination is essential for accurate classification and optimal management. Long-term follow-up is necessary given the propensity for late recurrence.
