

# Retinitis Pigmentosa

---

**Retinitis Pigmentosa (RP)** is a clinically and genetically heterogeneous group of inherited [[retinal dystrophies]] characterized by the progressive degeneration of [[photoreceptor]] cells, primarily [[rod photoreceptors]] followed by [[cone photoreceptors]], leading to progressive visual field loss and, in many cases, eventual legal blindness. It is the most common inherited retinal degeneration worldwide, affecting approximately 1 in 3,000 to 1 in 5,000 individuals globally, with an estimated 1.5 to 2 million people affected worldwide. The hallmark clinical triad includes **bone-spicule pigmentation** of the mid-peripheral [[retina]], **arteriolar attenuation**, and **waxy pallor of the [[optic disc]]**. Retinitis Pigmentosa represents a significant cause of irreversible vision loss and remains a major focus of ophthalmic genetics, translational research, and emerging gene therapy.

---

## Etymology and Historical Background

The condition was first described by the Dutch ophthalmologist **Franciscus Cornelis Donders** in 1857, who coined the term "retinitis pigmentosa." The term "retinitis" is technically a misnomer, as the disease is fundamentally a **degeneration** (dystrophy) rather than an **inflammation** of the [[retina]]. Despite this, the historical nomenclature has persisted in clinical usage. Some authors have proposed the alternative term **"rod-cone dystrophy"** to more accurately reflect the pathological process, but "retinitis pigmentosa" remains the universally accepted clinical designation.

Early descriptions by **Helmholtz** (following his invention of the [[ophthalmoscope]] in 1851) and subsequent work by **Leber**, **Usher**, and **Bardet** helped delineate the clinical spectrum and associated syndromic forms of the disease. The 20th and 21st centuries have witnessed an explosion in understanding of the molecular genetics underlying RP, culminating in the landmark approval of [[voretigene neparvovec]] (Luxturna) in 2017 as the first FDA-approved gene therapy for an inherited retinal disease.

## Epidemiology

| Parameter | Data |
|---|---|
| **Worldwide prevalence** | ~1 in 3,000 to 1 in 5,000 |
| **Estimated affected individuals globally** | 1.5–2 million |
| **Carrier frequency (autosomal recessive)** | ~1 in 30 to 1 in 80 |
| **Male-to-female ratio** | Approximately equal (except X-linked forms, which predominantly affect males) |
| **Age of onset** | Variable; typically childhood to early adulthood |
| **Proportion of all inherited retinal dystrophies** | ~40–50% |

RP accounts for the largest proportion of inherited [[retinal dystrophies]] and is a leading cause of blindness in working-age adults. The prevalence is relatively consistent across ethnic groups, though certain founder mutations may be enriched in specific populations (e.g., *USH2A* mutations in European populations, *EYS* mutations in Japanese populations).

## Genetics and Inheritance Patterns

Retinitis Pigmentosa is one of the most genetically heterogeneous conditions in all of medicine. Over **100 genes and loci** have been implicated in non-syndromic RP, and additional genes are associated with syndromic forms. The three principal Mendelian inheritance patterns are observed, along with rarer modes:

### Inheritance Patterns

| Inheritance Pattern | Frequency (% of RP cases) | Characteristics | Key Genes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Autosomal Dominant]] (adRP)** | 15–25% | Variable expressivity; often milder phenotype; later onset | *[[RHO]]* (rhodopsin), *[[RP1]]*, *[[PRPH2]]* (peripherin 2), *[[PRPF31]]*, *[[PRPF8]]*, *[[SNRNP200]]*, *[[NR2E3]]*, *[[IMPDH1]]*, *[[TOPORS]]* |
| **[[Autosomal Recessive]] (arRP)** | 20–30% | Often earlier onset; more severe; consanguinity a risk factor | *[[USH2A]]*, *[[EYS]]*, *[[RPE65]]*, *[[PDE6A]]*, *[[PDE6B]]*, *[[CNGA1]]*, *[[CNGB1]]*, *[[ABCA4]]*, *[[CRB1]]*, *[[TULP1]]*, *[[RLBP1]]* |
| **[[X-linked]] (xlRP)** | 5–15% | Most severe form; early onset; rapid progression; carrier females may show mild signs | *[[RPGR]]* (RP GTPase regulator), *[[RP2]]* |
| **Simplex / Sporadic** | 30–40% | No family history; may represent de novo mutations, autosomal recessive, or X-linked with incomplete penetrance | Various |
| **Digenic / Mitochondrial** | Rare (<1%) | Requires mutations in two genes simultaneously (e.g., *ROM1* + *PRPH2*) | *[[ROM1]]*, *[[PRPH2]]* |

### Key Genetic Considerations

- **[[Rhodopsin]] (*RHO*) mutations** account for approximately 25–30% of all autosomal dominant RP cases and are the single most common cause of adRP. The **Pro23His (P23H)** mutation in *RHO* is the most prevalent RP-causing mutation in North America.
- **[[RPGR]] mutations** account for approximately 70–80% of X-linked RP and represent the most severe form of the disease, with many affected males becoming legally blind by the third or fourth decade of life.
- **[[RPE65]] mutations** cause a severe, early-onset form of autosomal recessive RP (sometimes classified as [[Leber Congenital Amaurosis]] type 2) and are the target of the gene therapy [[voretigene neparvovec]].
- **[[USH2A]]** is the most commonly mutated gene in autosomal recessive RP and is also the gene responsible for [[Usher syndrome]] type 2A.
- Genetic testing has become an essential component of RP management, both for prognostication and for determining eligibility for emerging gene-specific therapies.

## Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology of Retinitis Pigmentosa is fundamentally rooted in the **progressive dysfunction and death of [[photoreceptor]] cells**, beginning with [[rod photoreceptors]] and subsequently involving [[cone photoreceptors]]. The molecular mechanisms vary depending on the causative gene but converge on several common pathological pathways.

### Molecular Mechanisms

#### 1. Phototransduction Cascade Defects
Many RP-causing genes encode proteins essential to the [[phototransduction]] cascade—the biochemical process by which light is converted into electrical signals in photoreceptors. Mutations in genes such as *[[RHO]]* (encoding [[rhodopsin]], the rod visual pigment), *[[PDE6A]]* and *[[PDE6B]]* (encoding subunits of rod [[phosphodiesterase]]), and *[[CNGA1]]* and *[[CNGB1]]* (encoding subunits of the rod [[cyclic nucleotide-gated channel]]) directly impair the ability of rods to respond to light stimuli. Misfolded rhodopsin (as in the P23H mutation) accumulates in the [[endoplasmic reticulum]], triggering the [[unfolded protein response]] (UPR) and ultimately [[apoptosis]].

#### 2. Visual Cycle Defects
The [[visual cycle]] (retinoid cycle) is the enzymatic pathway that regenerates the visual chromophore [[11-cis-retinal]] after photoisomerization. Mutations in *[[RPE65]]* (encoding retinal pigment epithelium-specific 65 kDa protein, an isomerohydrolase critical to the visual cycle) and *[[RLBP1]]* (encoding [[cellular retinaldehyde-binding protein]]) disrupt chromophore regeneration, leading to photoreceptor dysfunction and accumulation of toxic retinoid byproducts.

#### 3. Ciliary Transport and Structural Defects
Photoreceptors are highly specialized [[ciliated cells]], and the [[connecting cilium]] serves as the sole conduit between the [[inner segment]] and [[outer segment]]. Mutations in genes involved in [[intraflagellar transport]] and ciliary structure—such as *[[RPGR]]*, *[[RP2]]*, *[[RP1]]*, *[[TOPORS]]*, and *[[BBS]]* genes—impair the trafficking of essential proteins (including opsins and phototransduction components) to the outer segment, leading to outer segment disorganization and photoreceptor death. This mechanism underlies the classification of many RP forms as [[ciliopathies]].

#### 4. RNA Splicing Defects
A surprising finding in RP genetics is that mutations in ubiquitously expressed [[pre-mRNA splicing factors]]—including *[[PRPF31]]*, *[[PRPF8]]*, *[[PRPF3]]*, and *[[SNRNP200]]*—cause autosomal dominant RP. The precise mechanism by which defects in a universal cellular process lead to a photoreceptor-specific disease remains an area of active investigation, but it is hypothesized that the extraordinarily high transcriptional demands of photoreceptors render them uniquely vulnerable to splicing inefficiency.

#### 5. Retinal Pigment Epithelium (RPE) Dysfunction
The [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE) plays a critical role in photoreceptor homeostasis, including [[phagocytosis]] of shed outer segment discs, retinoid recycling, nutrient transport, and maintenance of the [[outer blood-retinal barrier]]. Mutations in genes such as *[[RPE65]]*, *[[MERTK]]* (encoding the phagocytic receptor MER tyrosine kinase), and *[[LRAT]]* primarily affect RPE function, leading to secondary photoreceptor degeneration.

### Cellular Death Pathways

The final common pathway in RP is photoreceptor cell death, which occurs through multiple mechanisms:

- **[[Apoptosis]]**: The predominant mode of rod photoreceptor death, involving caspase-dependent and caspase-independent pathways.
- **[[Necroptosis]]** and **[[pyroptosis]]**: Emerging evidence suggests that programmed necrotic pathways also contribute to photoreceptor loss.
- **Oxidative stress**: Following the loss of rods (which consume the majority of retinal oxygen), the remaining cones are exposed to hyperoxic conditions, generating [[reactive oxygen species]] (ROS) that accelerate cone degeneration. This "**bystander effect**" or "**oxygen toxicity hypothesis**" is a leading explanation for why cones die secondarily in a disease that primarily affects rods.
- **Metabolic starvation**: Rods produce trophic factors, including [[rod-derived cone viability factor]] (RdCVF), that are essential for cone survival. Loss of rods deprives cones of these survival signals.
- **[[Müller cell]] gliosis and retinal remodeling**: As photoreceptors degenerate, [[Müller cells]] undergo reactive gliosis, and the inner retina undergoes progressive remodeling, including neuronal rewiring, migration, and eventual inner retinal degeneration.

### Bone-Spicule Pigment Formation

The characteristic **bone-spicule pigmentation** seen on [[fundoscopy]] results from the migration of [[retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE) cells into the neurosensory retina following photoreceptor death. These displaced RPE cells accumulate around retinal blood vessels, forming the distinctive perivascular pigment clumps that resemble bone spicules. This process is a secondary reactive phenomenon rather than a primary pathological event.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Symptoms

The clinical presentation of RP is typically insidious, with symptoms developing gradually over years to decades. The classic symptom progression reflects the rod-then-cone pattern of degeneration:

| Symptom | Pathological Basis | Typical Onset |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Nyctalopia]] (night blindness)** | Rod photoreceptor dysfunction/loss | Often the earliest symptom; childhood to adolescence |
| **Progressive peripheral visual field loss** | Mid-peripheral rod degeneration expanding centripetally | Adolescence to early adulthood |
| **"Tunnel vision"** | Advanced peripheral field loss with preserved central island | Third to fifth decade |
| **Difficulty with dark adaptation** | Impaired rod-mediated [[scotopic vision]] | Early in disease course |
| **Photopsia (flashes of light)** | Aberrant photoreceptor signaling during degeneration | Variable |
| **Photophobia / glare sensitivity** | Cone dysfunction; loss of rod-cone balance | Mid to late stages |
| **Decreased [[visual acuity]]** | Cone photoreceptor involvement; [[cystoid macular edema]]; [[epiretinal membrane]] | Late stages (often preserved until advanced disease) |
| **Dyschromatopsia (color vision defects)** | Cone dysfunction | Late stages; typically blue-yellow (tritan) axis initially |
| **Central vision loss** | Foveal cone degeneration | End-stage disease |

### Natural History

- **X-linked RP (RPGR)**: Most severe; nyctalopia in first decade; legal blindness often by 30–40 years of age.
- **Autosomal recessive RP**: Intermediate severity; onset typically in first or second decade; significant visual impairment by 40–50 years.
- **Autosomal dominant RP**: Often mildest; later onset (second to third decade); slower progression; some patients retain useful vision into the sixth or seventh decade.
- **Sector RP**: A variant in which degeneration is confined to one or two quadrants (often inferior or nasal), with relative preservation of the remaining retina. Often autosomal dominant; associated with *RHO* mutations.
- **Pericentral RP**: Degeneration concentrated in the perimacular region rather than the mid-periphery, leading to paracentral scotomas with relative preservation of far peripheral vision.

### Associated Ocular Findings

- **[[Posterior subcapsular cataract]] (PSC)**: Occurs in approximately 40–70% of RP patients. The mechanism is thought to involve chronic low-grade inflammation and altered lens metabolism.
- **[[Cystoid macular edema]] (CME)**: Present in approximately 10–50% of RP patients (depending on the imaging modality used). CME is a significant and potentially treatable cause of central visual acuity loss in RP.
- **[[Epiretinal membrane]] (ERM)**: May develop secondary to chronic retinal remodeling.
- **[[Vitreous cells]]**: Fine, dust-like vitreous opacities are commonly observed and reflect low-grade breakdown of the blood-retinal barrier.
- **[[Optic disc drusen]]**: Reported at increased frequency in RP patients.
- **[[Myopia]]**: Refractive error, particularly myopia, is common in RP, especially in X-linked forms.
- **[[Keratoconus]]**: An association with RP has been reported, though the mechanism is unclear.

## Syndromic Forms of Retinitis Pigmentosa

RP may occur as an isolated ocular finding (non-syndromic RP) or as part of a multisystem syndrome. Approximately **20–30%** of RP cases are syndromic.

| Syndrome | Gene(s) | Associated Features |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Usher Syndrome]]** (Types 1, 2, 3) | *[[MYO7A]]*, *[[USH2A]]*, *[[CDH23]]*, *[[CLRN1]]*, others | RP + [[sensorineural hearing loss]] ± vestibular dysfunction. Most common syndromic form (~10–15% of all RP). |
| **[[Bardet-Biedl Syndrome]]** | *[[BBS1]]* through *[[BBS21]]* | RP + obesity + polydactyly + renal anomalies + hypogonadism + cognitive impairment |
| **[[Kearns-Sayre Syndrome]]** | Mitochondrial DNA deletions | RP (atypical, salt-and-pepper or pigmentary retinopathy) + [[progressive external ophthalmoplegia]] + cardiac conduction defects |
| **[[Refsum Disease]]** | *[[PHYH]]*, *[[PEX7]]* | RP + peripheral neuropathy + cerebellar ataxia + elevated phytanic acid |
| **[[Cockayne Syndrome]]** | *[[ERCC6]]*, *[[ERCC8]]* | RP (pigmentary retinopathy) + dwarfism + photosensitivity + neurological degeneration |
| **[[Abetalipoproteinemia]] (Bassen-Kornzweig)** | *[[MTTP]]* | RP + acanthocytosis + fat malabsorption + neurological dysfunction |
| **[[Senior-Løken Syndrome]]** | *[[NPHP]]* genes | RP + [[nephronophthisis]] (renal cystic disease) |

## Diagnostic Modalities

A comprehensive diagnostic evaluation for RP involves a combination of clinical examination, electrophysiological testing, multimodal imaging, and genetic analysis.

### 1. Clinical Examination

- **[[Best-corrected visual acuity]] (BCVA)**: Often preserved until late stages; central acuity may be reduced earlier if CME or cataracts are present.
- **[[Dilated fundus examination]]**: Reveals the classic triad of bone-spicule pigmentation (mid-peripheral), arteriolar attenuation, and waxy pallor of the optic disc. Early disease may show minimal or no fundoscopic changes ("RP sine pigmento").
- **[[Slit-lamp biomicroscopy]]**: Evaluates for posterior subcapsular cataracts, vitreous cells, and anterior segment findings.
- **[[Goldmann visual field]] or [[Humphrey visual field]]**: Demonstrates progressive concentric constriction of the visual field, often with a ring scotoma in mid-peripheral regions. Goldmann kinetic perimetry is particularly useful for mapping the extent of peripheral field loss.

### 2. Electrophysiology

- **[[Full-field electroretinography]] (ffERG)**: The **gold standard** diagnostic test for RP. ERG findings include:
  - **Reduced or extinguished scotopic (rod-mediated) responses**: The earliest and most characteristic finding.
  - **Reduced photopic (cone-mediated) responses**: Occurs later in the disease course.
  - **Prolonged implicit times**: Both rod and cone b-wave implicit times are delayed.
  - **Non-recordable ERG**: In advanced disease, both scotopic and photopic responses may be completely extinguished.
  - ERG abnormalities typically precede fundoscopic changes and can detect subclinical disease in at-risk family members.

- **[[Multifocal electroretinography]] (mfERG)**: Provides topographic mapping of cone function across the central retina; useful for monitoring central retinal function and macular involvement.

- **[[Electrooculography]] (EOG)**: Typically shows a reduced Arden ratio (light peak to dark trough ratio), reflecting RPE dysfunction.

### 3. Multimodal Imaging

| Imaging Modality | Findings in RP |
|---|---|
| **[[Optical coherence tomography]] (OCT)** | Loss of the [[ellipsoid zone]] (EZ, formerly IS/OS junction) in affected areas; thinning of the [[outer nuclear layer]] (ONL); CME; ERM; preserved EZ band in the fovea correlates with residual central vision |
| **[[Fundus autofluorescence]] (FAF)** | **Hyperautofluorescent ring** surrounding the macula—a hallmark finding representing the boundary between functional and degenerating retina; the ring constricts over time as disease progresses; hypoautofluorescence in areas of RPE atrophy |
| **[[Fluorescein angiography]] (FA)** | CME (petaloid hyperfluorescence); window defects from RPE atrophy; arteriolar attenuation; optic disc staining |
| **[[OCT angiography]] (OCTA)** | Reduced perifoveal capillary density; enlarged [[foveal avascular zone]] (FAZ); choriocapillaris flow deficits |
| **[[Adaptive optics]] imaging** | Direct visualization of photoreceptor mosaic; cone spacing abnormalities; useful in research settings for monitoring individual photoreceptor survival |
| **[[Ultra-widefield imaging]]** | Captures peripheral retinal changes beyond the standard field of view; useful for documenting the full extent of pigmentary changes |

### 4. Genetic Testing

Genetic testing has become an integral component of the diagnostic workup for RP and is now considered **standard of care** by many expert panels. Approaches include:

- **Next-generation sequencing (NGS) panels**: Targeted panels covering known RP-associated genes (typically 100+ genes).
- **Whole exome sequencing (WES)** and **whole genome sequencing (WGS)**: For cases where panel testing is non-diagnostic.
- **Genetic counseling**: Essential for interpreting results, discussing inheritance patterns, recurrence risks, and implications for family members.
- **Clinical utility**: Identification of the causative mutation enables accurate genetic counseling, prognostication, and determination of eligibility for gene-specific therapies (e.g., *RPE65*-associated RP for voretigene neparvovec).

### 5. Additional Testing

- **[[Dark adaptometry]]**: Quantifies the rate and extent of dark adaptation; markedly prolonged in RP.
- **[[Color vision testing]]** (e.g., [[Farnsworth-Munsell 100 Hue]], [[Ishihara plates]]): May reveal tritan (blue-yellow) defects initially, progressing to more generalized dyschromatopsia.
- **[[Contrast sensitivity]]**: Often reduced even when Snellen acuity is relatively preserved.

## Differential Diagnosis

Several conditions may mimic the clinical appearance or symptoms of RP and must be considered in the differential diagnosis:

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|---|---|
| **[[Cone-rod dystrophy]]** | Central vision loss and dyschromatopsia precede peripheral field loss; photopic ERG more affected than scotopic |
| **[[Choroideremia]]** | X-linked; progressive RPE and choriocapillaris atrophy; scalloped areas of bare sclera; *[[CHM]]* gene |
| **[[Gyrate atrophy]]** | Autosomal recessive; sharply demarcated scalloped areas of chorioretinal atrophy; elevated serum ornithine; *[[OAT]]* gene |
| **Syphilitic retinitis** | Acquired; positive serological testing; may mimic pigmentary retinopathy |
| **Drug toxicity (e.g., [[thioridazine]], [[chloroquine]], [[deferoxamine]])** | History of drug exposure; characteristic patterns of pigmentary change |
| **[[Cancer-associated retinopathy]] (CAR)** | Rapid onset; anti-retinal antibodies (e.g., anti-recoverin); associated malignancy |
| **[[Vitamin A deficiency]] retinopathy** | Reversible with supplementation; associated with malnutrition or malabsorption |
| **Post-inflammatory pigmentary retinopathy** | History of uveitis, infection (e.g., rubella, syphilis), or trauma |
| **[[Leber Congenital Amaurosis]] (LCA)** | Severe visual impairment from birth or early infancy; overlapping genetic causes with RP (e.g., *RPE65*, *CRB1*) |

## Management and Treatment

There is currently **no cure** for Retinitis Pigmentosa. Management is multifaceted and includes monitoring disease progression, treating complications, genetic counseling, low vision rehabilitation, and emerging therapeutic interventions.

### 1. Monitoring and Surveillance

- **Regular comprehensive eye examinations**: Including BCVA, dilated fundoscopy, visual field testing, OCT, and FAF.
- **Serial ERG**: To document rate of functional decline (though ERG may become non-recordable in advanced disease).
- **FAF ring monitoring**: The diameter of the hyperautofluorescent ring on FAF correlates with the area of preserved photoreceptor function and can be tracked over time as a biomarker of progression.
- **OCT ellipsoid zone width**: The horizontal extent of the preserved EZ band on OCT is a validated structural biomarker that correlates with visual field area and can be used to monitor progression.

### 2. Nutritional Supplementation

| Supplement | Evidence | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Vitamin A]] palmitate (15,000 IU/day)** | The landmark **Berson et al. (1993)** randomized controlled trial demonstrated a modest slowing of ERG decline in adults with typical RP. | Considered by some clinicians; requires monitoring of liver function and serum vitamin A levels. **Contraindicated in pregnancy** (teratogenic risk). Not recommended for children without specialist guidance. |
| **[[Docosahexaenoic acid]] (DHA)** | An omega-3 fatty acid concentrated in photoreceptor outer segment membranes. Some evidence of additive benefit when combined with vitamin A, but results are mixed. | May be considered as adjunctive supplementation. |
| **[[Lutein]]** | A macular carotenoid with antioxidant properties. The **Berson et al. (2010)** trial suggested a slowing of midperipheral visual field loss in patients already taking vitamin A. | 12 mg/day may be considered as adjunctive therapy. |
| **Vitamin E (high dose)** | The Berson et al. (1993) trial found that high-dose vitamin E (400 IU/day) was associated with a **faster** rate of ERG decline. | **High-dose vitamin E supplementation should be avoided** in RP patients. |

### 3. Treatment of Complications

#### Cystoid Macular Edema (CME)
CME is a common and treatable cause of reduced central vision in RP:

- **[[Topical carbonic anhydrase inhibitors]]** (e.g., [[dorzolamide]] 2% TID): First-line treatment; effective in many RP-associated CME cases.
- **[[Oral acetazolamide]]** (250–500 mg/day): May be used for refractory cases; requires monitoring of electrolytes and renal function.
- **[[Intravitreal anti-VEGF]] agents**: Limited evidence in RP-associated CME; may be considered in select cases.
- **[[Intravitreal corticosteroids]]** (e.g., [[dexamethasone implant]], [[triamcinolone]]): May provide temporary benefit but carry risks of cataract progression and [[intraocular pressure]] elevation.

#### Posterior Subcapsular Cataract
- **[[Cataract surgery]]** ([[phacoemulsification]] with [[intraocular lens]] implantation): Indicated when cataract significantly contributes to visual impairment. Outcomes are generally favorable, though patients should be counseled that visual improvement may be limited by underlying retinal disease. Careful preoperative assessment of macular function (OCT, mfERG) is essential.

### 4. Gene Therapy

Gene therapy represents the most transformative therapeutic advance in RP to date.

#### Voretigene Neparvovec (Luxturna)
- **Indication**: Biallelic *[[RPE65]]*-associated retinal dystrophy (including RP and LCA) with viable retinal cells.
- **Mechanism**: Subretinal injection of an [[adeno-associated virus]] serotype 2 (AAV2) vector carrying a functional copy of the *RPE65* gene, restoring isomerohydrolase activity in the RPE and enabling visual cycle function.
- **Efficacy**: The pivotal Phase 3 trial demonstrated significant improvement in **multi-luminance mobility testing (MLMT)**, full-field stimulus testing, and visual field sensitivity compared to controls.
- **Approval**: FDA-approved December 2017; EMA-approved November 2018.
- **Limitations**: Applicable only to the small subset of RP patients with biallelic *RPE65* mutations (~1–2% of all RP); requires sufficient viable retinal cells; long-term durability of effect is under ongoing study.

#### Emerging Gene Therapies
Numerous gene therapy programs are in clinical trials for other genetic forms of RP, including:
- *[[RPGR]]*-associated X-linked RP (multiple Phase 2/3 trials)
- *[[CNGA3]]* and *[[CNGB3]]*-associated achromatopsia (related cone dysfunction)
- *[[PDE6B]]*-associated RP
- **Gene-agnostic approaches**: Optogenetic therapy (e.g., delivery of light-sensitive channelrhodopsins to surviving inner retinal neurons) aims to restore light sensitivity independent of the causative mutation.

### 5. Retinal Prostheses

- **[[Argus II Retinal Prosthesis System]]**: An epiretinal implant that converts images captured by a camera mounted on glasses into electrical stimulation of surviving [[retinal ganglion cells]]. FDA-approved in 2013 for severe RP (bare or no light perception). Provides rudimentary form vision (light localization, motion detection, shape recognition). The manufacturer (Second Sight Medical Products) ceased operations in 2020, limiting ongoing support.
- **Next-generation devices**: Subretinal implants (e.g., **Alpha AMS/IMS**, **PRIMA**), cortical visual prostheses (e.g., **Orion**), and photovoltaic subretinal chips are in various stages of development.

### 6. Neuroprotective and Pharmacological Strategies

| Strategy | Mechanism | Status |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Ciliary neurotrophic factor]] (CNTF)** | Neurotrophic factor delivered via encapsulated cell technology (NT-501 implant) | Phase 2/3 trials showed slowing of photoreceptor loss on OCT but no significant visual function benefit |
| **[[Rod-derived cone viability factor]] (RdCVF)** | Trophic factor secreted by rods that supports cone survival | Preclinical; promising results in animal models |
| **[[N-acetylcysteine]] (NAC)** | Antioxidant targeting oxidative stress in cones | Phase 1/2 trials ongoing |
| **[[Valproic acid]]** | Proposed neuroprotective effects via HDAC inhibition | Controversial; initial reports of benefit not confirmed; **not currently recommended** |
| **Oral [[minocycline]]** | Anti-inflammatory and anti-apoptotic properties | Early-phase clinical trials |

### 7. Cell-Based Therapies

- **[[Retinal pigment epithelium]] (RPE) cell transplantation**: Stem cell-derived RPE cells (from [[induced pluripotent stem cells]] or [[embryonic stem cells]]) are being investigated for transplantation to replace dysfunctional RPE.
- **Photoreceptor transplantation**: Transplantation of stem cell-derived photoreceptor precursors is in early-stage research; challenges include integration, synapse formation, and immune rejection.
- **Retinal organoids**: Three-dimensional retinal tissue derived from stem cells offers potential for both transplantation and disease modeling.

### 8. Low Vision Rehabilitation

Low vision rehabilitation is a critical component of RP management at all stages:

- **[[Low vision aids]]**: Magnifiers (handheld, stand, electronic), telescopic devices, closed-circuit television (CCTV) systems.
- **Orientation and mobility training**: Instruction in the use of a [[white cane]], guide dog, and environmental awareness strategies.
- **Assistive technology**: Screen readers, text-to-speech software, smartphone accessibility features, GPS navigation aids.
- **Lighting optimization**: Task lighting, glare reduction, contrast enhancement.
- **Tinted lenses and filters**: Amber, orange, or red-tinted lenses may improve contrast and reduce photophobia.
- **Psychosocial support**: Counseling, support groups (e.g., Foundation Fighting Blindness, Retina International), and vocational rehabilitation.
- **Driving assessment**: Patients must be counseled regarding legal visual requirements for driving and referred for formal driving assessment when appropriate.

### 9. Genetic Counseling

- All patients with RP should be offered **[[genetic counseling]]** and **genetic testing**.
- Counseling should address inheritance pattern, recurrence risk, carrier testing for family members, reproductive options (including [[preimplantation genetic diagnosis]]), and implications for eligibility for gene-specific therapies.
- Identification of the causative gene enables enrollment in gene-specific clinical trials.

## Prognosis

The prognosis of RP is highly variable and depends on the inheritance pattern, causative gene, and specific mutation:

- **Visual acuity**: Many patients retain central visual acuity of 20/40 or better until the fifth or sixth decade, though this varies widely. Approximately 50% of patients retain visual acuity of 20/60 or better by age 50.
- **Visual field**: Progressive concentric constriction is the rule; the rate of visual field loss averages approximately 4–10% per year of remaining field area.
- **Legal blindness**: A significant proportion of patients meet criteria for [[legal blindness]] (visual acuity ≤20/200 or visual field ≤20 degrees in the better eye) by the fourth to sixth decade, depending on the genetic subtype.
- **Complete blindness**: Total loss of light perception occurs in a minority of patients, typically in the most severe genetic forms (e.g., X-linked RPGR).

## Emerging Research and Future Directions

The field of RP therapeutics is rapidly evolving. Key areas of active research include:

1. **Expanded gene therapy**: Development of AAV-based and non-viral gene therapies for additional RP genotypes beyond *RPE65*.
2. **[[CRISPR-Cas9]] gene editing**: Correction of specific mutations at the genomic level; in preclinical and early clinical development for dominant-negative mutations (e.g., *RHO* P23H).
3. **Antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs)**: Targeting specific splice-site mutations (e.g., *[[CEP290]]* c.2991+1655A>G intronic mutation in LCA10; *USH2A* exon 13 skipping).
4. **Optogenetics**: Rendering surviving inner retinal neurons (e.g., [[retinal ganglion cells]], [[bipolar cells]]) light-sensitive through expression of microbial opsins; gene-agnostic approach applicable to end-stage disease.
5. **Stem cell therapies**: RPE and photoreceptor replacement using iPSC-derived or ESC-derived cells.
6. **Artificial intelligence (AI)**: Machine learning algorithms for automated analysis of OCT, FAF, and visual field data to improve monitoring and prognostication.
7. **Combination therapies**: Integrating gene therapy, neuroprotection, and cell replacement for synergistic benefit.

## Summary

Retinitis Pigmentosa is a genetically and clinically heterogeneous group of inherited [[retinal dystrophies]] that remains a leading cause of inherited blindness worldwide. The disease is characterized by progressive [[photoreceptor]] degeneration, beginning with [[rod photoreceptors]] and subsequently involving [[cone photoreceptors]], leading to nyctalopia, progressive visual field constriction, and eventual central vision loss. Diagnosis relies on clinical examination, [[electroretinography]], multimodal imaging, and genetic testing. While no cure currently exists, the approval of [[voretigene neparvovec]] for *RPE65*-associated disease has ushered in a new era of gene-based therapeutics, and numerous additional gene therapies, neuroprotective agents, retinal prostheses, and cell-based therapies are in active development. Comprehensive management includes monitoring, treatment of complications (particularly [[cystoid macular edema]] and [[posterior subcapsular cataract]]), nutritional supplementation, genetic counseling, and low vision rehabilitation. The future holds significant promise for patients with RP as the convergence of molecular genetics, gene therapy, stem cell biology, and bioengineering continues to advance.
