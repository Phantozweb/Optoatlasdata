

# Pterygium

---

**Pterygium** (plural: *pterygia*; from the Greek *pterygion*, meaning "small wing") is a benign, wedge-shaped fibrovascular proliferative growth of the [[bulbar conjunctiva]] that encroaches onto the [[cornea]]. It is one of the most common ocular surface disorders encountered in clinical [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] practice worldwide. Pterygium is characterized by elastotic degeneration of [[subconjunctival connective tissue]], which proliferates as vascularized granulation tissue to invade the cornea, destroying [[Bowman's layer]] and the superficial [[corneal stroma]]. Although histologically benign, pterygium can cause significant visual morbidity through induced [[astigmatism]], visual axis obstruction, chronic ocular surface inflammation, cosmetic disfigurement, and restriction of [[extraocular motility]] in advanced cases.

Pterygium is strongly associated with chronic [[ultraviolet (UV) radiation]] exposure, particularly UV-B (280–320 nm), and is consequently most prevalent in populations residing within the "pterygium belt" — a geographic band extending approximately 37 degrees north and south of the equator. The condition must be distinguished from [[pinguecula]], a related but non-cornea-invading degenerative lesion of the conjunctiva, and from [[pseudopterygium]], which arises secondary to inflammatory or traumatic insult and lacks true adhesion to the underlying corneal tissue.

---

## Epidemiology

The global prevalence of pterygium varies dramatically based on geographic latitude, altitude, occupational UV exposure, and ethnic background. Pooled epidemiological data suggest a worldwide prevalence ranging from **1.2% to over 33%**, with the highest rates observed in equatorial and tropical regions.

| **Epidemiological Factor** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| **Global Prevalence** | 1.2%–33%, depending on geographic region |
| **Pterygium Belt** | Latitudes 37°N to 37°S of the equator |
| **Peak Prevalence Regions** | Southeast Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, Central America, Middle East, Australia, Pacific Islands |
| **Sex Predilection** | Males > Females (approximately 2:1), likely due to greater occupational outdoor exposure |
| **Age of Onset** | Most commonly presents in the 3rd–4th decade of life; rare before age 20 |
| **Laterality** | Bilateral in 33%–60% of cases in high-prevalence regions; nasal predominance (~90%) |
| **Occupational Risk** | Farmers, fishermen, construction workers, outdoor laborers, surfers, welders |

Key risk factors include:

- **Chronic [[UV-B radiation]] exposure** — the single most significant etiological factor
- **Geographic latitude** — proximity to the equator
- **Outdoor occupation** — prolonged unprotected sun exposure
- **Dry, dusty, and windy environments** — chronic ocular surface irritation
- **Male sex** — likely related to occupational exposure patterns
- **Genetic predisposition** — familial clustering has been documented; associations with [[HLA antigens]] and specific [[p53 gene]] mutations have been reported
- **Low socioeconomic status** — reduced access to UV-protective eyewear
- **Smoking** — some studies suggest a paradoxically protective effect, though data remain controversial
- **[[Human papillomavirus (HPV)]]** — HPV DNA has been detected in some pterygium specimens, though a definitive causal role remains unestablished

## Anatomy and Morphology

A pterygium is classically described as having three distinct anatomical zones:

| **Anatomical Zone** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Cap (Leading Edge)** | A flat, avascular zone at the corneal apex of the lesion, composed of fibroblasts that actively invade and destroy [[Bowman's layer]]. This zone is responsible for progressive corneal encroachment. |
| **Head** | The portion of the pterygium adherent to the [[cornea]], immediately behind the cap. It is slightly elevated and may contain iron deposition at its leading edge ([[Stocker's line]]). |
| **Body** | The fleshy, vascularized portion overlying the [[sclera]] and [[bulbar conjunctiva]], extending toward the [[caruncle]] or [[plica semilunaris]]. It is composed of fibrovascular tissue with elastotic degeneration. |

### Nasal Predominance

Approximately **85–95%** of pterygia arise on the **nasal** (medial) side of the [[interpalpebral fissure]]. Several theories explain this predilection:

1. **Coroneo's Peripheral Light-Focusing Effect**: UV light entering the eye temporally is refracted and focused by the [[cornea]] onto the nasal [[limbus]], resulting in a concentration of UV irradiance up to **20 times** greater at the nasal limbal region than at the temporal limbus. This phenomenon, described by Professor Minas Coroneo, is considered the most compelling explanation for nasal predominance.
2. **Nasal tear film dynamics**: The nasal conjunctiva is more exposed to environmental irritants due to tear flow patterns.
3. **Nasal vascular anatomy**: Greater vascular density at the nasal limbus may facilitate fibrovascular proliferation.

Temporal pterygia do occur, particularly in bilateral cases, and double-headed pterygia (simultaneous nasal and temporal involvement in the same eye) have been reported, though they are uncommon.

## Pathophysiology

The pathogenesis of pterygium is multifactorial and involves a complex interplay of UV-induced cellular damage, [[limbal stem cell]] dysfunction, chronic inflammation, extracellular matrix remodeling, angiogenesis, and anti-apoptotic mechanisms. Current understanding positions pterygium not merely as a degenerative condition but as a disorder with features of **uncontrolled cell proliferation**, sharing some molecular characteristics with neoplastic processes.

### 1. Ultraviolet Radiation–Induced Damage

Chronic UV-B exposure is the primary inciting factor. UV-B radiation induces:

- **Direct DNA damage**: Formation of [[cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers]] and 6-4 photoproducts in [[limbal epithelial stem cells]] and conjunctival epithelial cells.
- **[[Oxidative stress]]**: Generation of [[reactive oxygen species (ROS)]], leading to lipid peroxidation, protein oxidation, and mitochondrial damage.
- **[[p53 tumor suppressor gene]] mutations**: UV-induced mutations in p53 have been identified in pterygium epithelial cells, leading to loss of normal apoptotic regulation and enabling abnormal cell survival and proliferation. This finding supports the concept of pterygium as a **UV-related neoplastic-like disorder**.
- **Microsatellite instability and [[loss of heterozygosity (LOH)]]**: Genetic alterations consistent with clonal expansion have been demonstrated in pterygium tissue.

### 2. Limbal Stem Cell Dysfunction

The [[limbal stem cells]], located in the [[Palisades of Vogt]] at the [[corneoscleral limbus]], serve as the barrier between conjunctival and corneal epithelium. UV-induced damage to these cells results in:

- **Focal limbal stem cell deficiency (LSCD)**: Breakdown of the limbal barrier allows conjunctival epithelial cells, goblet cells, and fibrovascular tissue to migrate onto the corneal surface — a process termed **[[conjunctivalization]]** of the cornea.
- **Altered limbal stem cell phenotype**: Pterygium basal epithelial cells express markers of both corneal and conjunctival differentiation, suggesting a disordered stem cell population.

### 3. Extracellular Matrix Remodeling

Pterygium tissue demonstrates profound alterations in the [[extracellular matrix (ECM)]]:

- **Elastotic degeneration**: Accumulation of abnormal, amorphous elastin-like material (solar elastosis) in the substantia propria, a hallmark histopathological finding.
- **Upregulation of [[matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs)]]**: Particularly MMP-1, MMP-2, MMP-3, MMP-7, and MMP-9, which degrade [[collagen]], [[fibronectin]], and other ECM components, facilitating tissue invasion and destruction of [[Bowman's layer]].
- **Tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases (TIMPs)**: Relative downregulation of TIMPs creates an imbalance favoring ECM degradation.

### 4. Angiogenesis and Lymphangiogenesis

Pterygium is a highly vascularized lesion. Key angiogenic mediators include:

- **[[Vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF)]]**: Markedly overexpressed in pterygium epithelium and fibroblasts; VEGF-A is the predominant isoform.
- **[[Basic fibroblast growth factor (bFGF)]]**
- **[[Platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF)]]**
- **[[Interleukin-6 (IL-6)]] and [[Interleukin-8 (IL-8)]]**: Pro-inflammatory cytokines that also promote angiogenesis.

Lymphangiogenesis, mediated by VEGF-C and VEGF-D acting on [[VEGFR-3]], has also been demonstrated in pterygium tissue and may contribute to immune evasion and recurrence.

### 5. Inflammation and Immune Dysregulation

Chronic inflammation is a central feature of pterygium pathogenesis:

- Infiltration by [[T-lymphocytes]], [[macrophages]], [[mast cells]], [[plasma cells]], and [[Langerhans cells]].
- Overexpression of pro-inflammatory cytokines: [[TNF-α]], [[IL-1β]], IL-6, IL-8.
- Upregulation of [[cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2)]] and [[nuclear factor-kappa B (NF-κB)]] signaling pathways.
- Increased expression of [[intercellular adhesion molecule-1 (ICAM-1)]] on pterygium epithelium.

### 6. Anti-Apoptotic Mechanisms

Pterygium cells demonstrate resistance to programmed cell death:

- Overexpression of anti-apoptotic proteins such as [[Bcl-2]] and [[survivin]].
- Downregulation of pro-apoptotic proteins such as [[Bax]].
- Mutant p53 accumulation, impairing normal apoptotic pathways.

### 7. Viral Association

[[Human papillomavirus (HPV)]] DNA, particularly types 16 and 18, has been detected in a subset of pterygium specimens using [[polymerase chain reaction (PCR)]] techniques. The HPV E6 and E7 oncoproteins can inactivate p53 and [[retinoblastoma protein (Rb)]], respectively, potentially contributing to the proliferative phenotype. However, detection rates vary widely across studies (0%–100%), and a definitive etiological role for HPV in pterygium remains debated.

## Histopathology

Histopathological examination of excised pterygium tissue reveals characteristic findings:

| **Layer** | **Histopathological Findings** |
|---|---|
| **Epithelium** | Acanthosis, dyskeratosis, goblet cell hyperplasia; may show varying degrees of [[squamous metaplasia]] or [[conjunctival intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN)]]-like changes |
| **Bowman's Layer** | Destruction and fragmentation at the head and cap region |
| **Substantia Propria (Stroma)** | Elastotic (basophilic) degeneration of collagen; accumulation of amorphous, acellular, eosinophilic material (solar elastosis); fibrovascular proliferation |
| **Vasculature** | Prominent neovascularization with dilated, tortuous blood vessels |
| **Inflammatory Infiltrate** | Chronic inflammatory cells: lymphocytes, plasma cells, macrophages, mast cells |

It is important to note that pterygium specimens should always undergo histopathological examination after excision, as [[ocular surface squamous neoplasia (OSSN)]] can occasionally masquerade as or coexist with pterygium.

## Clinical Presentation and Symptoms

### Symptoms

The symptomatology of pterygium ranges from asymptomatic to significantly visually debilitating:

| **Symptom** | **Mechanism** |
|---|---|
| **Asymptomatic** | Small, quiescent pterygia may be incidental findings |
| **Foreign body sensation** | Elevated tissue disrupts the [[tear film]] and irritates the [[palpebral conjunctiva]] |
| **Ocular surface dryness** | Irregular ocular surface leads to [[tear film instability]] and localized [[dry eye]] |
| **Burning and tearing** | Chronic surface irritation and reflex [[epiphora]] |
| **Redness (hyperemia)** | Vascular engorgement of the pterygium body, especially during inflammation |
| **Cosmetic concern** | Visible fleshy growth; significant psychosocial impact in some patients |
| **Blurred vision** | Induced [[irregular astigmatism]] (typically with-the-rule or oblique); direct visual axis obstruction in advanced cases |
| **Diplopia** | Rare; occurs with large pterygia restricting [[extraocular muscle]] function, particularly [[lateral rectus]] limitation |
| **Recurrent inflammation** | Episodic redness, swelling, and discomfort |

### Clinical Grading

Several grading systems have been proposed. The most commonly used clinical classification is based on the **degree of corneal involvement** and **tissue morphology**:

#### Grading by Corneal Encroachment (Tan Classification)

| **Grade** | **Extent of Corneal Involvement** |
|---|---|
| **T1 (Atrophic)** | Episcleral vessels clearly visible beneath the pterygium body; thin, translucent tissue |
| **T2 (Intermediate)** | Episcleral vessels partially visible; moderately thick tissue |
| **T3 (Fleshy/Opaque)** | Episcleral vessels completely obscured; thick, opaque, fleshy tissue |

#### Grading by Corneal Extension

| **Grade** | **Description** |
|---|---|
| **Grade 1** | Crossing the [[limbus]] but not reaching the [[pupillary margin]] by >2 mm |
| **Grade 2** | Approaching the pupillary margin (within 2 mm) |
| **Grade 3** | Involving the [[pupillary axis]] |
| **Grade 4** | Extensive involvement beyond the pupillary axis, potentially bilateral or double-headed |

Fleshy (T3) pterygia and those with greater corneal extension are associated with higher recurrence rates following surgical excision.

### Stocker's Line

A [[Stocker's line]] is a brownish iron deposition line ([[hemosiderin]]) in the [[corneal epithelium]] at the leading edge (cap) of the pterygium. It is analogous to other corneal iron lines such as the [[Hudson-Stähli line]], [[Fleischer ring]], and [[Ferry's line]]. The presence of a Stocker's line generally indicates a slowly progressive or relatively stationary pterygium.

## Diagnosis

The diagnosis of pterygium is primarily **clinical**, based on characteristic [[slit-lamp biomicroscopy]] findings. However, ancillary investigations may be employed to assess the impact on visual function, document progression, and exclude differential diagnoses.

### Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

The gold standard for diagnosis. Key findings include:

- Triangular or wing-shaped fibrovascular tissue originating from the [[bulbar conjunctiva]] (typically nasal) and extending across the [[limbus]] onto the [[cornea]]
- Identifiable cap, head, and body
- Vascular engorgement (active/inflamed pterygium) vs. flat, atrophic appearance (quiescent pterygium)
- Presence or absence of [[Stocker's line]]
- Assessment of corneal involvement relative to the [[visual axis]]
- Evaluation for [[Dellen]] (localized corneal thinning adjacent to the elevated pterygium)

### Corneal Topography

[[Corneal topography]] (e.g., [[Placido disc-based topography]], [[Scheimpflug imaging]]) is invaluable for:

- Quantifying pterygium-induced [[irregular astigmatism]]
- Documenting flattening of the cornea in the axis of the pterygium (typically nasal flattening with steepening of the perpendicular meridian)
- Monitoring progression over time
- Assessing post-surgical corneal regularization

Topographic patterns typically show:

- **With-the-rule or oblique astigmatism** induced by mechanical traction
- **Asymmetric bow-tie pattern** with nasal flattening
- Significant improvement in corneal regularity following successful pterygium excision

### Anterior Segment Optical Coherence Tomography (AS-OCT)

[[Anterior segment OCT]] provides high-resolution cross-sectional imaging of the pterygium and underlying cornea:

- Measurement of pterygium thickness and extent of corneal invasion
- Assessment of Bowman's layer integrity
- Differentiation from [[pseudopterygium]] (true pterygium shows adherence to underlying tissue without a probe-passable plane)
- Evaluation of corneal stromal scarring

### Refraction and Visual Acuity

- [[Manifest refraction]] to quantify induced astigmatism
- [[Best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA)]] assessment
- Significant astigmatism (>1.00 D) or reduced BCVA may constitute an indication for surgical intervention

### Differential Diagnosis

| **Condition** | **Distinguishing Features** |
|---|---|
| **[[Pinguecula]]** | Yellow-white, slightly elevated conjunctival lesion; does NOT cross the limbus onto the cornea |
| **[[Pseudopterygium]]** | Arises secondary to inflammation, chemical burn, or trauma; NOT adherent to underlying cornea (probe can be passed beneath); can occur at any location, not limited to interpalpebral fissure |
| **[[Ocular surface squamous neoplasia (OSSN)]]** | Gelatinous, papillomatous, or leukoplakic lesion; may have feeder vessels; can mimic pterygium; histopathological examination is essential for differentiation |
| **[[Conjunctival lymphoma]]** | Salmon-pink, smooth, subconjunctival mass; typically non-vascularized surface |
| **[[Pannus]]** | Superficial corneal vascularization; associated with [[trachoma]], [[contact lens]] overwear, or other inflammatory conditions; lacks the triangular fibrovascular morphology |
| **[[Dermoid]]** | Congenital, solid, well-circumscribed limbal mass; may contain hair follicles or sebaceous glands |

## Management

Management of pterygium ranges from conservative observation and medical therapy to surgical excision with adjunctive measures to prevent recurrence. The decision to intervene is guided by symptom severity, rate of progression, degree of visual impairment, cosmetic concern, and recurrence risk.

### Conservative (Non-Surgical) Management

Indicated for small, asymptomatic, or minimally symptomatic pterygia that are not threatening the visual axis:

| **Intervention** | **Purpose** |
|---|---|
| **Observation** | Serial monitoring with slit-lamp photography and/or corneal topography every 6–12 months |
| **UV protection** | Wraparound [[sunglasses]] with UV-A and UV-B blocking; wide-brimmed hats; critical for prevention of progression and recurrence |
| **[[Artificial tears]] and lubricants** | Alleviate foreign body sensation, dryness, and irritation; preservative-free formulations preferred |
| **Topical [[vasoconstrictors]]** | Short-term cosmetic relief of redness (e.g., [[naphazoline]], [[brimonidine]]); not for chronic use |
| **Topical [[NSAIDs]]** | (e.g., [[diclofenac 0.1%]], [[ketorolac 0.5%]]) — may reduce inflammation and discomfort in inflamed pterygia |
| **Topical [[corticosteroids]]** | Short courses (e.g., [[fluorometholone 0.1%]], [[loteprednol 0.5%]]) for acute inflammatory flares; monitor [[intraocular pressure (IOP)]] for steroid response |
| **Topical [[mast cell stabilizers]] / [[antihistamines]]** | (e.g., [[olopatadine]], [[ketotifen]]) — may help with associated allergic component |

### Indications for Surgical Excision

Surgery is indicated when:

1. The pterygium encroaches on or threatens the [[visual axis]] (within 3–4 mm of the pupillary center)
2. Significant induced [[astigmatism]] (>2.00 D) reducing [[BCVA]]
3. Chronic, recurrent inflammation unresponsive to medical therapy
4. Restriction of [[ocular motility]]
5. Significant cosmetic concern affecting quality of life
6. Suspicion of [[OSSN]] or other malignancy (excisional biopsy)
7. Interference with [[contact lens]] fitting

## Surgical Approaches

### 1. Bare Sclera Technique

The pterygium is excised, and the underlying [[sclera]] is left exposed without conjunctival coverage. This technique is **largely abandoned** as a standalone procedure due to unacceptably high recurrence rates of **24–89%**. It may still be used in combination with adjunctive antimetabolite therapy.

### 2. Conjunctival Autograft (CAG)

Currently considered the **gold standard** surgical technique:

- After pterygium excision, a free graft of healthy [[bulbar conjunctiva]] (typically harvested from the superotemporal quadrant of the same eye) is transplanted to cover the bare scleral bed.
- The graft is secured using **[[fibrin glue]]** (e.g., [[Tisseel]]) or **sutures** (e.g., 8-0 or 10-0 [[Vicryl]], [[nylon]]).
- **Fibrin glue** is increasingly preferred over sutures due to:
  - Reduced operative time
  - Less postoperative discomfort
  - Lower recurrence rates (some studies suggest fibrin glue is associated with recurrence rates of **3–5.3%** vs. **9.1–13.5%** with sutures)
  - Superior cosmetic outcome
- Overall recurrence rate with CAG: **2–15%** (significantly lower than bare sclera technique)

### 3. Limbal-Conjunctival Autograft (LCAG)

A modification of CAG that includes [[limbal stem cells]] in the graft:

- The graft is harvested to include a strip of limbal tissue, theoretically restoring the limbal barrier and reducing conjunctivalization
- May offer additional benefit in reducing recurrence, particularly in cases with significant limbal involvement
- Recurrence rates comparable to or lower than standard CAG

### 4. Amniotic Membrane Transplantation (AMT)

[[Amniotic membrane]] (derived from human placenta) is used to cover the bare scleral bed:

- Amniotic membrane possesses anti-inflammatory, anti-fibrotic, anti-angiogenic, and epithelialization-promoting properties
- Available as cryopreserved or dehydrated grafts
- Recurrence rates: **3–40.9%** (variable; generally higher than CAG in most comparative studies)
- Particularly useful when conjunctival autograft tissue is unavailable (e.g., prior surgery, [[glaucoma filtering surgery]] planned, bilateral extensive pterygia)
- May be combined with other adjunctive therapies

### 5. Adjunctive Therapies to Reduce Recurrence

| **Adjunctive Therapy** | **Mechanism** | **Application** | **Recurrence Rate** | **Complications** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Mitomycin C (MMC)]]** | Alkylating agent; inhibits fibroblast proliferation and DNA synthesis | Intraoperative application (0.02–0.04% for 1–5 minutes on bare sclera) or postoperative topical drops | Reduces recurrence to **2–12%** when combined with bare sclera; lower with CAG | [[Scleral necrosis]], [[scleral melting]], delayed [[epithelial healing]], [[corneal edema]], secondary [[glaucoma]], [[cataract]], [[endophthalmitis]], calcific plaque |
| **[[5-Fluorouracil (5-FU)]]** | Antimetabolite; inhibits thymidylate synthase and DNA synthesis | Subconjunctival injection or topical application | Variable; generally less effective than MMC | Less toxic than MMC; may cause punctate epitheliopathy |
| **[[Anti-VEGF agents]]** (e.g., [[bevacizumab]], [[ranibizumab]]) | Inhibit [[VEGF]]-mediated angiogenesis | Subconjunctival injection (preoperative, intraoperative, or postoperative) or topical application | Promising results in reducing vascularity and recurrence; data still evolving | Generally well-tolerated; rare reports of scleral thinning |
| **[[Beta-irradiation]]** (Strontium-90) | Ionizing radiation inhibits fibroblast proliferation | Postoperative application to the surgical bed | Reduces recurrence to **2–12%** | [[Scleral necrosis]] (delayed), [[cataract]], [[endophthalmitis]]; largely replaced by MMC and CAG |
| **[[Cyclosporine A]] topical** | Immunomodulator; inhibits T-cell activation and inflammatory cytokine production | Postoperative topical drops (0.05%–0.1%) | May reduce recurrence as adjunctive therapy; evidence is mixed | Burning, stinging on instillation |

### Surgical Technique: Key Principles

Regardless of the specific technique employed, the following principles optimize outcomes:

1. **Complete excision**: Meticulous removal of all pterygium tissue, including the cap, head, body, and underlying Tenon's capsule (subconjunctival fibrovascular tissue). Incomplete excision is the most significant risk factor for recurrence.
2. **Minimal cautery**: Excessive cautery promotes inflammation and fibrosis, increasing recurrence risk.
3. **Graft orientation**: The conjunctival autograft must be oriented correctly (limbal edge toward the limbus, epithelial side up) to ensure proper healing and limbal barrier restoration.
4. **Adequate graft size**: The graft should fully cover the bare scleral defect without tension.
5. **Postoperative anti-inflammatory therapy**: Topical [[corticosteroids]] (e.g., [[prednisolone acetate 1%]], [[dexamethasone 0.1%]]) tapered over 4–8 weeks; topical [[antibiotics]] for infection prophylaxis.

## Recurrence

Recurrence is the most significant challenge in pterygium management. A recurrent pterygium is often more aggressive, more vascularized, and more difficult to manage than the primary lesion due to increased subconjunctival fibrosis and scarring.

### Risk Factors for Recurrence

| **Factor** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| **Surgical technique** | Bare sclera >> AMT > CAG (bare sclera has highest recurrence) |
| **Young age** | Patients <40 years have significantly higher recurrence rates due to more robust wound healing and fibroblast activity |
| **Fleshy (T3) morphology** | Thick, vascularized pterygia recur more frequently than atrophic (T1) lesions |
| **Geographic location** | Higher recurrence in equatorial/tropical regions with intense UV exposure |
| **Incomplete excision** | Residual tissue serves as a nidus for regrowth |
| **Excessive intraoperative cautery** | Promotes inflammation and fibrosis |
| **Lack of adjunctive therapy** | Absence of MMC, anti-VEGF, or other adjuncts increases recurrence risk |
| **Postoperative UV exposure** | Continued unprotected sun exposure promotes recurrence |

### Management of Recurrent Pterygium

Recurrent pterygia are managed with:

- Repeat excision with **conjunctival autograft** (if donor tissue is available) or **amniotic membrane transplantation**
- **Intraoperative MMC** application (with careful attention to concentration and duration to minimize complications)
- **Subconjunctival anti-VEGF injection** (bevacizumab 2.5 mg/0.1 mL)
- **Lamellar keratoplasty** or **[[corneal transplantation]]** in cases with significant corneal scarring or thinning
- Extensive [[symblepharon]] lysis if cicatricial changes are present

## Complications of Pterygium

### Complications of the Pterygium Itself

- **Induced [[irregular astigmatism]]** — most common cause of visual impairment
- **Visual axis obstruction** — in advanced (Grade 3–4) cases
- **[[Corneal scarring]]** — persistent even after excision
- **[[Dellen]] formation** — localized corneal thinning adjacent to the elevated lesion
- **[[Tear film]] instability** and secondary [[dry eye disease]]
- **Restriction of [[ocular motility]]** — particularly medial rectus restriction mimicking [[abduction deficit]]
- **Cosmetic disfigurement** — psychosocial impact

### Complications of Surgical Treatment

| **Complication** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| **Recurrence** | Most common complication; rates depend on technique and adjunctive therapy |
| **Graft dehiscence or displacement** | More common with fibrin glue than sutures in some series; requires repositioning |
| **Graft retraction or necrosis** | Inadequate vascularization of the graft |
| **Corneal scarring** | Residual scarring at the site of Bowman's layer destruction |
| **Persistent or induced astigmatism** | May require spectacle or [[contact lens]] correction; usually improves post-excision |
| **[[Scleral necrosis/melting]]** | Particularly with MMC or beta-irradiation; potentially sight-threatening |
| **[[Infectious scleritis]] or [[endophthalmitis]]** | Rare but devastating; associated with scleral necrosis |
| **[[Corneoscleral dellen]]** | Localized thinning due to tear film disruption |
| **Granuloma formation** | Foreign body reaction to suture material |
| **[[Symblepharon]]** | Adhesion between palpebral and bulbar conjunctiva; more common in recurrent cases |
| **Diplopia** | Rare; due to scarring involving extraocular muscles |

## Emerging and Investigational Therapies

Several novel approaches are under investigation for pterygium prevention and treatment:

- **Topical [[anti-VEGF]] therapy**: Topical bevacizumab (1.0–2.5%) eye drops have shown promise in reducing pterygium vascularity and size in early-phase studies, though long-term efficacy and safety data are limited.
- **[[Photodynamic therapy (PDT)]]**: Selective vascular ablation using photosensitizing agents; early-stage investigation.
- **[[Argon laser photocoagulation]]**: Targeted coagulation of feeder vessels; limited evidence.
- **Topical [[interferon alpha-2b]]**: Has shown efficacy in treating [[OSSN]] and may have a role in pterygium management; further studies needed.
- **[[Pirfenidone]]**: An anti-fibrotic agent that inhibits [[TGF-β]] signaling; under investigation for reducing postoperative fibrosis and recurrence.
- **Gene therapy and targeted molecular therapy**: Targeting specific pathways (e.g., MMPs, VEGF, NF-κB) represents a future direction.
- **[[Cross-linking]]**: Corneal collagen cross-linking applied to the pterygium bed has been explored as an adjunctive measure to reduce recurrence.

## Prevention

Primary prevention strategies focus on reducing UV exposure:

- **UV-blocking sunglasses**: Wraparound styles providing lateral protection are most effective
- **Wide-brimmed hats**: Reduce UV exposure by up to 50%
- **Avoidance of peak UV hours**: 10:00 AM – 2:00 PM
- **Occupational protective eyewear**: Essential for outdoor workers
- **Patient education**: Particularly in high-prevalence regions

Secondary prevention (preventing recurrence after surgery) includes:

- Postoperative UV protection
- Appropriate surgical technique (CAG preferred)
- Adjunctive antimetabolite or anti-VEGF therapy when indicated
- Regular postoperative follow-up

---

## Prognosis

The prognosis for pterygium is generally favorable with appropriate management:

- Small, atrophic pterygia may remain stable for years with conservative management
- Surgical excision with conjunctival autograft and adjunctive therapy achieves cure rates of **85–98%**
- Residual corneal scarring may persist even after successful excision, potentially limiting visual recovery
- Corneal topographic irregularity typically improves significantly within 3–6 months postoperatively
- Recurrence, when it occurs, usually manifests within the **first 6–12 months** after surgery
- Long-term follow-up (minimum 12 months) is recommended to detect recurrence

---

## Summary

Pterygium is a prevalent, UV-driven, fibrovascular ocular surface disorder with significant implications for visual function and quality of life. Its pathogenesis involves a complex interplay of UV-induced genetic damage, limbal stem cell dysfunction, chronic inflammation, angiogenesis, and extracellular matrix remodeling. While conservative management suffices for early, asymptomatic lesions, surgical excision — preferably with conjunctival autograft and adjunctive therapy — remains the definitive treatment for progressive or visually significant pterygia. Ongoing research into anti-VEGF agents, anti-fibrotic therapies, and molecular-targeted approaches holds promise for further reducing recurrence rates and improving outcomes.
