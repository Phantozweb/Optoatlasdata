

# Negative Relative Accommodation (NRA)

---

**Negative Relative Accommodation (NRA)** is a clinical measurement in [[optometry]] and [[ophthalmology]] that quantifies the maximum amount by which a patient can **relax** (decrease) [[accommodation]] while maintaining clear, single [[binocular vision]] at a fixed near working distance. It is measured in [[diopters]] (D) and represents the ability of the [[accommodative system]] to reduce its tonic level of focus in response to plus (convex) lens power added over the patient's existing near correction. NRA is a fundamental component of the comprehensive [[binocular vision]] assessment and plays a critical role in determining the appropriate near-point [[refractive correction]], evaluating [[accommodative dysfunction]], and guiding the prescription of [[progressive addition lenses]], [[bifocal lenses]], and [[reading glasses]].

NRA is always assessed in conjunction with its counterpart, [[Positive Relative Accommodation (PRA)]], which measures the maximum ability to *increase* accommodation at the same near distance. Together, NRA and PRA define the total range of relative accommodation available to the patient and provide essential data for balanced lens prescribing, particularly for the [[add power]] in [[presbyopic]] patients and for the management of various [[accommodative]] and [[vergence]] anomalies.

---

## Definition and Conceptual Framework

Negative Relative Accommodation is formally defined as the maximum amount of accommodation that can be **relaxed** while the patient views a near target at a fixed distance (typically 40 cm, corresponding to a 2.50 D accommodative demand) through their best-corrected distance [[refraction]] or habitual near correction, without the target becoming blurred or diplopic. The term "negative" refers to the *reduction* or *relaxation* of the accommodative response—that is, the accommodative effort is moved in the negative (minus) direction relative to the baseline demand.

### Relationship to Accommodation and Vergence

The measurement of NRA is inextricably linked to the interplay between the [[accommodative system]] and the [[vergence system]]. When plus lenses are introduced at near, the [[accommodative demand]] is reduced. However, because the patient is still fixating at the same near distance, the [[convergence]] demand remains unchanged. This creates a dissociation between accommodation and convergence, requiring the patient to maintain [[fusional vergence]] (specifically, [[negative fusional vergence]] or divergence) while relaxing accommodation. The endpoint of NRA is reached when the patient can no longer relax accommodation further without experiencing sustained blur, which typically occurs because:

1. The [[ciliary muscle]] has reached its maximum state of relaxation for the given vergence posture.
2. The [[accommodative convergence]] (AC) component driven by the [[AC/A ratio]] causes a divergence demand that exceeds the patient's [[negative fusional vergence]] reserves, potentially leading to [[diplopia]] before blur.
3. The patient's [[depth of focus]] has been exceeded.

### Distinction from Other Accommodative Measurements

NRA should be carefully distinguished from several related but distinct clinical measurements:

| Measurement | What It Assesses | How It Differs from NRA |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Positive Relative Accommodation (PRA)]]** | Maximum ability to *stimulate* accommodation at a fixed near distance | PRA uses minus lenses; NRA uses plus lenses |
| **[[Amplitude of Accommodation]]** | Total range of accommodation from far point to near point (monocular) | Measured monocularly; NRA is binocular and relative to a fixed distance |
| **[[Accommodative Facility]]** | Speed of accommodative change (measured with ±2.00 D flippers) | Assesses dynamic accommodative response rate, not maximum range |
| **[[Binocular Cross-Cylinder (BCC) Test]]** | Accommodative lag or lead at near | Determines the accommodative response accuracy, not the relaxation range |
| **[[Negative Fusional Vergence (NFV)]]** | Ability to diverge the eyes | Vergence measurement; NRA involves both accommodation and vergence |

## Pathophysiology and Physiological Basis

### The Accommodative Mechanism

To understand NRA, one must first appreciate the physiology of [[accommodation]]. The [[crystalline lens]] of the eye changes shape to focus on objects at varying distances through a process mediated by the [[ciliary muscle]], the [[zonular fibers]] (zonules of Zinn), and the inherent elasticity of the [[lens capsule]]. This mechanism, described by the [[Helmholtz theory of accommodation]], operates as follows:

- **For near viewing:** The [[ciliary muscle]] contracts (primarily the circular fibers of Müller), causing the [[ciliary body]] to move anteriorly and inward. This releases tension on the [[zonular fibers]], allowing the elastic [[lens capsule]] to mold the [[crystalline lens]] into a more convex shape, increasing its [[refractive power]].
- **For distance viewing:** The [[ciliary muscle]] relaxes, the [[ciliary body]] moves posteriorly, tension on the [[zonular fibers]] increases, and the [[crystalline lens]] is pulled into a flatter configuration with reduced refractive power.

During NRA testing, the addition of plus lenses effectively reduces the optical demand for accommodation. The patient must therefore **relax** the [[ciliary muscle]] to maintain a clear retinal image. The NRA value reflects how much the ciliary muscle can relax from its current tonic state while the patient maintains [[binocular fusion]] at the test distance.

### Neural Control of Accommodative Relaxation

The [[ciliary muscle]] is primarily innervated by the [[parasympathetic nervous system]] via the [[oculomotor nerve]] (cranial nerve III), specifically through the [[Edinger-Westphal nucleus]], the [[ciliary ganglion]], and the [[short ciliary nerves]]. Accommodative relaxation involves a reduction in parasympathetic tone to the ciliary muscle, with some contribution from [[sympathetic innervation]] (via the [[superior cervical ganglion]] and [[long ciliary nerves]]) that may actively facilitate relaxation.

Conditions that affect the neural pathways controlling accommodation—such as [[Adie's tonic pupil]], [[cranial nerve III palsy]], pharmacological agents ([[cycloplegics]], [[parasympatholytics]]), or central nervous system lesions—can alter NRA findings.

### The AC/A Ratio and Its Role in NRA

The [[AC/A ratio]] (accommodative convergence to accommodation ratio) is a critical determinant of NRA performance. When plus lenses are added during NRA testing, the reduction in accommodation also reduces the [[accommodative convergence]] component. This means the eyes tend to diverge (exophoric shift). To maintain single binocular vision, the patient must exert compensatory [[positive fusional vergence]] (convergence). However, if the patient has a high [[AC/A ratio]], even a small reduction in accommodation produces a large exophoric shift, which may exceed the patient's [[positive fusional vergence]] reserves, causing the endpoint to be reached prematurely—not due to accommodative limitation but due to vergence limitation.

Conversely, a patient with a low AC/A ratio will experience minimal vergence disruption during NRA testing, and the endpoint will more purely reflect accommodative relaxation capacity.

### Age-Related Changes and Presbyopia

With advancing age, the [[crystalline lens]] undergoes progressive sclerosis and loss of elasticity—a process known as [[lenticular sclerosis]]. The [[lens capsule]] also becomes less pliable. These changes reduce the total [[amplitude of accommodation]] ([[presbyopia]]). In the context of NRA:

- **Pre-presbyopic patients** typically have a large NRA because the accommodative system has substantial reserve to relax from the near demand.
- **Presbyopic patients** already require a plus [[add power]] to meet the near demand, and their NRA is measured over this add. The NRA in presbyopes helps determine whether the prescribed add is appropriate: an NRA that is significantly higher than the PRA suggests the add may be too weak, while a very low NRA relative to PRA suggests the add may be too strong.

## Clinical Measurement Technique

### Equipment Required

- Standard [[phoropter]] or [[trial frame]] with trial lenses
- Near-point card with reduced [[Snellen acuity]] letters or equivalent (e.g., 20/20 to 20/30 equivalent at 40 cm)
- Adequate [[illumination]] of the near target
- Patient's best-corrected distance [[refraction]] in place (or habitual near correction for presbyopes)

### Step-by-Step Procedure

1. **Set the distance correction:** Place the patient's best-corrected distance [[subjective refraction]] in the [[phoropter]] or [[trial frame]]. For [[presbyopic]] patients, the tentative [[add power]] (determined by methods such as the [[binocular cross-cylinder test]], [[fused cross-cylinder]], or age-expected add) is placed over the distance correction.

2. **Position the near target:** Place the near-point card at the standard testing distance of **40 cm** (16 inches) from the patient's [[spectacle plane]]. The target should consist of a line of letters one or two lines larger than the patient's best near [[visual acuity]] (commonly 20/25 or 20/30 equivalent).

3. **Instruct the patient:** Ask the patient to keep the letters clear and single. Emphasize that they should report the **first sustained blur**—not a momentary blur that clears.

4. **Add plus power binocularly:** Slowly add plus sphere power in **+0.25 D steps** simultaneously to both eyes (binocularly). After each addition, allow the patient 2–3 seconds to attempt to clear the target.

5. **Record the endpoint:** The NRA value is the **total amount of plus power added** at the point where the patient reports the first **sustained blur** that cannot be cleared. For example, if +2.25 D of plus power is added before sustained blur occurs, the NRA is recorded as **+2.25 D**.

6. **Remove the added lenses:** Return the phoropter to the starting correction before proceeding to the PRA measurement.

### Expected Normal Values

| Patient Population | Expected NRA Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Young adults (pre-presbyopic)** | **+2.00 D to +2.50 D** | Reflects robust accommodative relaxation capacity |
| **Children and adolescents** | **+2.00 D to +2.50 D** | Similar to young adults; may be slightly higher |
| **Early presbyopes (40–50 years)** | **+1.50 D to +2.25 D** | Measured over tentative add; decreases with age |
| **Advanced presbyopes (>55 years)** | **+1.50 D to +2.00 D** | Measured over add; NRA and PRA should be approximately equal |

The widely accepted **expected norm** for NRA is **+2.00 D ± 0.50 D** in the general clinical population. A finding significantly outside this range warrants further investigation.

### Recording Convention

NRA is recorded as a **positive value** (e.g., +2.00 D) because plus lenses are added. This can occasionally cause confusion; it is important to remember that the plus sign refers to the lens power added, not to an increase in accommodation. The accommodation is actually being *relaxed* (decreased).

## Clinical Significance and Interpretation

### Balanced NRA and PRA

In an ideally corrected patient, the NRA and PRA should be approximately **equal in magnitude** (though opposite in sign convention). This balance indicates that the patient's near correction places the accommodative demand at the midpoint of the relative accommodative range, providing equal reserve for both relaxation and stimulation. The clinical significance of this balance is paramount in:

- **Determining the final add power for presbyopes:** The tentative add is adjusted until NRA ≈ PRA. If NRA >> PRA, the add is likely too low (undercorrected); if PRA >> NRA, the add is likely too high (overcorrected).
- **Verifying the binocular balance of the distance refraction:** An imbalance between NRA and PRA in a pre-presbyopic patient may indicate that the distance refraction is slightly over-minused or under-plussed (if NRA is low) or over-plussed (if NRA is high).

### Abnormal NRA Findings

#### Low NRA (Below +1.50 D)

A reduced NRA indicates that the patient has limited ability to relax accommodation at near. Possible causes include:

| Cause | Mechanism | Associated Findings |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Accommodative excess]] (accommodative spasm)** | Tonic over-accommodation; ciliary muscle cannot relax | High PRA, low NRA, [[esophoria]] at near, variable [[myopia]], headaches |
| **[[Latent hyperopia]]** | Habitual accommodative tone masks full hyperopic error; limited relaxation reserve | Low NRA, improved NRA after [[cycloplegic refraction]] correction |
| **Over-minused distance refraction** | Excessive minus power forces sustained accommodation | Low NRA, artificially high PRA, asthenopic symptoms |
| **High [[AC/A ratio]] with [[esophoria]]** | Relaxing accommodation causes excessive divergence demand | Diplopia may occur before blur; reduced NRA limited by vergence |
| **[[Convergence excess]]** | High near esophoria limits ability to relax accommodation without disrupting fusion | Low NRA, high near [[esophoria]], reduced [[negative fusional vergence]] |

#### High NRA (Above +2.50 D)

An elevated NRA indicates excessive ability to relax accommodation, which may suggest:

| Cause | Mechanism | Associated Findings |
|---|---|---|
| **Under-plussed hyperopic correction** | Patient is accommodating excessively through distance Rx; large relaxation reserve | High NRA, low PRA, [[asthenopia]] |
| **[[Accommodative insufficiency]]** | Weak accommodative response; system is already relatively relaxed | Low PRA, high NRA, receded [[near point of accommodation]] |
| **Excessive add power (presbyopes)** | Add is too strong; patient has excessive plus at near | High NRA, very low PRA, complaints of blur at intermediate distances |
| **Under-corrected myopia** | Insufficient minus in distance Rx; less accommodation needed at near | High NRA, may have distance blur complaints |

## Symptoms Associated with Abnormal NRA

Patients with abnormal NRA values—whether high or low—may present with a constellation of symptoms related to [[accommodative dysfunction]] and [[binocular vision]] imbalance. These symptoms are not caused by the NRA value itself but rather by the underlying condition that produces the abnormal finding.

### Symptoms of Low NRA (Accommodative Excess / Over-Accommodation)

- **[[Asthenopia]]** (eye strain), particularly with prolonged near work
- **Headaches** (frontal or temporal), often worsening through the day
- **Blurred vision at distance** after sustained near tasks (pseudomyopia)
- **Difficulty shifting focus** between near and far (accommodative inflexibility)
- **Intermittent [[diplopia]]** at near
- **Print appears to "swim" or move** on the page
- **Photophobia** and brow ache
- **Reduced reading stamina** and concentration difficulties

### Symptoms of High NRA (Accommodative Insufficiency / Under-Accommodation)

- **Blurred vision at near**, especially with prolonged reading
- **Difficulty sustaining near focus** (words "fade in and out")
- **Holding reading material at an increased distance**
- **Avoidance of near tasks** (particularly in children)
- **Slow reading speed** and loss of place
- **[[Asthenopia]]** with near work
- **Fatigue and drowsiness** when reading

## Diagnosis: NRA in the Context of the Comprehensive Binocular Vision Assessment

NRA is never interpreted in isolation. It forms part of a comprehensive diagnostic battery that evaluates the accommodative and vergence systems. The following tests are typically performed alongside NRA to arrive at a definitive diagnosis:

### Accommodative Testing Battery

| Test | Purpose | Relationship to NRA |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Positive Relative Accommodation (PRA)]]** | Maximum accommodative stimulation at near | Counterpart to NRA; balance between NRA and PRA guides prescribing |
| **[[Amplitude of Accommodation]]** (push-up, pull-away, or minus lens method) | Total accommodative range | Low amplitude may explain high NRA (insufficient accommodation) |
| **[[Accommodative Facility]]** (±2.00 D flippers, monocular and binocular) | Speed of accommodative response | Difficulty clearing plus lenses correlates with low NRA |
| **[[Monocular Estimation Method (MEM) Retinoscopy]]** | Objective measure of [[accommodative lag]] or lead | High lag may correlate with accommodative insufficiency (high NRA) |
| **[[Binocular Cross-Cylinder (BCC) / Fused Cross-Cylinder Test]]** | Determines tentative add power based on accommodative response | NRA/PRA balance verifies the BCC-derived add |
| **[[Near Point of Accommodation (NPA)]]** | Closest point of clear vision | Receded NPA with high NRA suggests accommodative insufficiency |

### Vergence Testing Battery

| Test | Purpose | Relationship to NRA |
|---|---|---|
| **[[Cover Test]] (distance and near)** | Detects [[heterophoria]] or [[heterotropia]] | Near esophoria may limit NRA; near exophoria may inflate NRA |
| **[[Near Point of Convergence (NPC)]]** | Closest point of maintained single binocular vision | Receded NPC with vergence-limited NRA suggests [[convergence insufficiency]] |
| **[[Positive Fusional Vergence (PFV)]] and [[Negative Fusional Vergence (NFV)]]** | Vergence reserves | Low PFV may limit NRA if AC/A ratio is high |
| **[[AC/A Ratio]] (calculated or gradient method)** | Relationship between accommodation and convergence | High AC/A ratio may cause vergence-limited NRA endpoint |
| **[[Vergence Facility]]** | Speed of vergence response | Complements NRA in assessing dynamic binocular function |

### Diagnostic Algorithms

The integration of NRA with other findings allows classification of specific [[binocular vision]] and [[accommodative]] disorders according to established diagnostic criteria (e.g., those outlined by Scheiman and Wick, or the [[Optometric Extension Program (OEP)]] analytical system):

- **[[Accommodative Excess]]:** Low NRA, high PRA, lead on MEM, difficulty clearing plus on facility testing, esophoria at near, variable refraction.
- **[[Accommodative Insufficiency]]:** High NRA, low PRA, high lag on MEM, difficulty clearing minus on facility testing, low amplitude of accommodation, receded NPA.
- **[[Accommodative Infacility]]:** NRA and PRA may both be reduced or normal, but accommodative facility is significantly reduced in both directions.
- **[[Convergence Excess]]:** Low NRA (vergence-limited), high near esophoria, high AC/A ratio, reduced NFV at near.

## Treatment and Management

Management of abnormal NRA findings is directed at the **underlying etiology** rather than the NRA value itself. Treatment strategies fall into several categories:

### 1. Refractive Correction Optimization

The most immediate clinical application of NRA is in refining the [[refractive correction]]:

- **Balancing the add power:** For [[presbyopic]] patients, the add is adjusted so that NRA ≈ PRA. The formula commonly used is:

  > **Final Add = Tentative Add + (NRA + PRA) / 2**

  For example, if the tentative add is +2.00 D, NRA is +2.50 D, and PRA is −2.00 D:
  Final Add = +2.00 + (+2.50 + (−2.00)) / 2 = +2.00 + 0.25 = **+2.25 D**

- **Correcting latent hyperopia:** If NRA is low due to [[latent hyperopia]], prescribing the full [[cycloplegic refraction]] (or maximum plus accepted) can restore NRA to normal levels.

- **Reducing over-minus:** If the distance refraction is over-minused, reducing the minus power (or increasing plus) will improve NRA.

### 2. Lens Therapy

- **Plus lenses at near (reading adds for non-presbyopes):** In cases of [[accommodative excess]] with low NRA, prescribing a low plus add (+0.50 to +1.00 D) for near work can relieve symptoms by reducing the accommodative demand and allowing the ciliary muscle to relax.

- **[[Progressive addition lenses (PALs)]]:** Provide a gradient of plus power for patients who need accommodative support at multiple distances.

- **[[Prism]] correction:** In cases where NRA is limited by vergence dysfunction (e.g., [[convergence excess]]), [[base-out prism]] may be prescribed to reduce the convergence demand and indirectly improve NRA.

### 3. Vision Therapy / Orthoptics

[[Vision therapy]] is the primary treatment for accommodative and vergence dysfunctions that produce abnormal NRA findings. Specific therapeutic approaches include:

#### For Low NRA (Accommodative Excess)

- **Accommodative relaxation training:** Using plus lenses of increasing power while maintaining clear near vision; [[Hart chart]] exercises at near with plus lenses.
- **Distance-near accommodative rock with emphasis on plus acceptance:** Binocular facility training with ±2.00 D flippers, focusing on improving the speed and ease of clearing the plus lens.
- **Near-far shifting exercises:** Alternating fixation between near and distant targets to improve accommodative flexibility.
- **[[Brock string]]** and **[[Vectogram]]** exercises: To improve fusional vergence ranges that may be limiting NRA.
- **Relaxation techniques:** General stress reduction, as accommodative spasm can be exacerbated by psychological stress and anxiety.

#### For High NRA (Accommodative Insufficiency)

- **Accommodative stimulation training:** Using minus lenses of increasing power while maintaining clear near vision.
- **Push-up accommodative exercises:** Bringing a near target progressively closer while maintaining clarity.
- **Minus lens facility training:** Emphasizing the ability to clear minus lenses during binocular facility testing.
- **[[Computer-based vision therapy]]:** Programs such as HTS (Home Therapy System) or VTS4 that provide structured accommodative training protocols.

### 4. Pharmacological Interventions

Pharmacological agents are rarely the primary treatment for NRA abnormalities but may be used in specific circumstances:

| Agent | Indication | Mechanism | Relevance to NRA |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[[Atropine]] (low-dose, 0.01%)** | [[Myopia control]] in children with accommodative excess | [[Cycloplegic]]; relaxes [[ciliary muscle]] via [[muscarinic receptor]] blockade | May improve NRA by reducing tonic accommodation; used cautiously |
| **[[Cyclopentolate]]** | Diagnostic cycloplegia to reveal [[latent hyperopia]] | Short-acting cycloplegic | Used diagnostically, not therapeutically; helps identify cause of low NRA |
| **[[Tropicamide]]** | Mild cycloplegia for diagnostic purposes | Weak, short-acting cycloplegic | Limited therapeutic role; diagnostic adjunct |
| **[[Pilocarpine]]** | Rarely used; [[accommodative insufficiency]] in specific cases | [[Parasympathomimetic]]; stimulates ciliary muscle contraction | Could theoretically reduce NRA; not standard treatment |

### 5. Environmental and Ergonomic Modifications

- **Proper working distance:** Ensuring the patient maintains an appropriate near working distance (typically 40 cm or the [[Harmon distance]]) reduces excessive accommodative demand.
- **Adequate lighting:** Poor illumination increases accommodative effort and may reduce NRA.
- **The 20-20-20 rule:** Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds. This periodic accommodative relaxation can benefit patients with low NRA due to sustained near work.
- **Screen positioning and ergonomics:** For digital device users, positioning screens at an appropriate distance and angle reduces accommodative and vergence stress.

### 6. Monitoring and Follow-Up

Patients with abnormal NRA findings should be monitored at regular intervals:

- **Vision therapy patients:** Re-evaluation every 4–8 weeks to assess progress and modify the therapy program.
- **New refractive corrections:** Follow-up at 2–4 weeks to verify symptom resolution and re-measure NRA/PRA balance.
- **Presbyopic patients:** Annual re-evaluation as the add power typically needs to be increased with age, and NRA/PRA balance should be reassessed.
- **Children:** More frequent monitoring (every 6–12 months) as the visual system is still developing and accommodative/vergence parameters may change.

## Special Populations and Considerations

### Pediatric Patients

NRA testing in children requires special consideration:

- Children may have difficulty understanding the concept of "first sustained blur" and may report blur inconsistently.
- High NRA values in children may indicate [[accommodative insufficiency]], which can contribute to reading difficulties and may be misdiagnosed as learning disabilities.
- Low NRA in children may be associated with [[accommodative spasm]] or [[pseudomyopia]], which can lead to unnecessary myopic corrections if not identified.

### Patients with Strabismus

In patients with [[strabismus]] (particularly [[intermittent esotropia]] or [[accommodative esotropia]]), NRA testing may be unreliable because binocular fusion—a prerequisite for valid NRA measurement—may not be consistently maintained. In these cases, the clinician should note whether the patient maintained fusion throughout the test.

### Post-Surgical Patients

Patients who have undergone [[refractive surgery]] (e.g., [[LASIK]], [[PRK]]) or [[cataract surgery]] with [[intraocular lens (IOL)]] implantation may have altered NRA findings:

- **Monofocal IOL patients** have no accommodative ability and NRA/PRA testing is not applicable in the traditional sense.
- **[[Accommodating IOL]]** or **[[multifocal IOL]]** patients may have measurable but altered NRA values.
- Post-LASIK patients may have residual refractive error affecting NRA balance.

### Patients on Systemic Medications

Various systemic medications can affect accommodation and therefore NRA:

- **[[Anticholinergic]] medications** (e.g., antihistamines, tricyclic antidepressants, antipsychotics): May reduce accommodative ability, potentially increasing NRA.
- **[[Cholinergic]] medications** (e.g., donepezil for Alzheimer's disease): May stimulate accommodation, potentially decreasing NRA.
- **[[Sympathomimetic]] agents** (e.g., pseudoephedrine): May have mild effects on accommodative relaxation.

---

## Relationship to Digital Eye Strain

In the modern era of pervasive digital device use, NRA has gained renewed clinical relevance. [[Digital eye strain]] (also known as [[computer vision syndrome]]) is frequently associated with sustained accommodative effort at near, which can lead to:

- Reduced NRA due to accommodative fatigue or spasm
- Imbalance between NRA and PRA
- Symptoms of [[asthenopia]], blurred vision, and headaches

Measuring NRA in symptomatic digital device users can help identify accommodative dysfunction as a contributing factor and guide the prescription of appropriate near-point lenses or vision therapy.
